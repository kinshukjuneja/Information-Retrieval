href="/wiki/Penwortham_Priory" title="Penwortham Priory">Penwortham Priory</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Roger Norreis</b><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> (died between 1223 and 1225) was <a href="/wiki/Abbot_of_Evesham" title="Abbot of Evesham">Abbot of Evesham</a> in England. He was a controversial figure, installed in several offices against opposition. In his appointment to Evesham he was accused of immoral behaviour and failing to follow monastic rules. In 1202 Norreis became embroiled in a dispute with his monks and his episcopal superior the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishop of Worcester</a>; litigation and argumentation lasted until his deposition in 1213. He then was appointed <a href="/wiki/Prior" title="Prior">prior</a> of a subsidiary monastic house of <a href="/wiki/Evesham" title="Evesham">Evesham</a> but was deposed within months, then re-appointed to the office five years later.\n</p><p>Norreis has been described by modern historians as being unsuited for the religious positions to which he was appointed and by one of being completely unsuitable to hold any kind of spiritual role. Nevertheless, even his most severe contemporary critic, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_of_Marlborough" title="Thomas of Marlborough">Thomas of Marlborough</a>, one of his own monks at Evesham, conceded that Norreis was energetic, entertaining, and enterprising; during his time as abbot of <a href="/wiki/Evesham_Abbey" title="Evesham Abbey">Evesham Abbey</a> he managed to complete the <a href="/wiki/Crossing_tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Crossing tower">crossing tower</a> of the monastic church. \n</p><p>Roger Norreis died between 1223 and 1225.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_early_career">Background and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Norreis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Norreis was a native of northern England and his family was probably of Norse origin.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup> He was a monk at <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church_Priory" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ Church Priory">Christ Church Priory</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_chapter" title="Cathedral chapter">cathedral chapter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Heads48_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads48-5">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> when he became a monk is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1187 he was appointed treasurer of the <a href="/wiki/Priory" title="Priory">priory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic332_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic332-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and in that role was sent by the cathedral chapter to King <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a> to plead their case against <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_of_Forde" title="Baldwin of Forde">Baldwin of Forde</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The archbishop and his monks were in dispute over Baldwin\'s plan to found a <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_church" title="Collegiate church">collegiate church</a> at Hackington in honour of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a> which most of the monks opposed because they feared it would diminish the prestige of their priory.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic319_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic319-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Once Norreis reached the king he was converted to Baldwin\'s side, perhaps because of the latter\'s appointment of Norreis as the <a href="/wiki/Cellarer" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellarer">cellarer</a> of Christ Church while the pair were at the king\'s court. The monks resisted this appointment, and in September they appealed to the papacy, arguing that the appointment was against the <a href="/wiki/Benedictine_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine Rule">Benedictine Rule</a>. They also captured and held Norreis in custody, telling anyone who enquired after him that he was sick. Norreis escaped in early 1188 by travelling through the sewer and fled to the safety of the archbishop, who was then at <a href="/wiki/Otford" title="Otford">Otford</a>. In a mocking reference to his escape route, Norreis was occasionally known as Roger Cloacarius or "Roger the Drain-Cleaner".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> Baldwin then tried to have Norreis installed as prior at Christ Church\'s <a href="/wiki/Priory" title="Priory">dependent priory</a> of <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s,_Dover" class="mw-redirect" title="St Martin&#39;s, Dover">St Martin\'s, Dover</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but the appointment was never confirmed.<sup id="cite_ref-Heads88_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads88-9">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_and_abbot">Prior and abbot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Norreis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prior and abbot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>King Henry died on 6 July 1189 and his son <a href="/wiki/Richard_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard I">Richard I</a> was crowned on 3 September.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbook36_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook36-10">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The succession of a new monarch eventually allowed a truce in the dispute between the Canterbury monks and their archbishop,<sup id="cite_ref-Gillingham110_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gillingham110-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> as Henry had been a supporter of the archbishop\'s scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic319_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic319-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Before the truce could be hammered out, Baldwin appointed Norreis as prior of Christ Church in October 1189 as another move in the quarrel over the Hackington project.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic319_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic319-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In November the cathedral chapter secured a compromise that included the abandonment of the scheme and Norreis\'s dismissal from office.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic319_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic319-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The agreement came at a council held by Richard in late November 1189 at which the monks agreed to let the king settle the dispute over the Hackington project if the archbishop would withdraw Norreis\'s appointment.<sup id="cite_ref-Richard94_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richard94-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Norreis\'s fellow monks considered him a traitor to their cause, and his reputation was that of someone with few morals. He was also known for ingratiating himself with those in power,<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic332_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic332-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> including the <a href="/wiki/Justiciar" title="Justiciar">justiciar</a> <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Fitz_Peter,_1st_Earl_of_Essex" title="Geoffrey Fitz Peter, 1st Earl of Essex">Geoffrey fitzPeter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic333_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic333-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Many of the details concerning Norreis\'s career at Christ Church come from the works of <a href="/wiki/Gervase_of_Canterbury" title="Gervase of Canterbury">Gervase of Canterbury</a>, as well as the collection of letters known as the <i>Epistolae Cantuarienses</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Norreis was appointed to the abbacy of <a href="/wiki/Evesham_Abbey" title="Evesham Abbey">Evesham Abbey</a> in 1190 and was blessed as abbot on 13 January 1190.<sup id="cite_ref-Heads48_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads48-5">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He owed his appointment to King Richard<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and to Baldwin\'s attempts to make the dismissal from Christ Church look less like a defeat for the archbishop. Norreis also claimed that he received the office in return for unspecified services he rendered to Richard. There was no attempt at an election by the monks, a lack which went against <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic332_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic332-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During his tenure of office, his monks accused him of fornication, excessive drinking, gluttony, setting up favourites, starving the monks, and appropriating monastic property for his own use.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic333_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic333-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A further offence was his habit of wearing secular clothing rather than the monastic habit prescribed by the Benedictine Rule.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> All these charges come from one of his monks, the historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_of_Marlborough" title="Thomas of Marlborough">Thomas of Marlborough</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Law43_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law43-14">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> who wrote much of the history of Evesham known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicon_Abbatiae_de_Evesham" title="Chronicon Abbatiae de Evesham">Chronicon Abbatiae de Evesham</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Law29_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law29-15">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Thomas\'s account of Norreis\'s rule is biased against the abbot<sup id="cite_ref-Law43_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law43-14">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and is a self-aggrandising account of how Thomas single-handedly triumphed over both his and his abbey\'s foes.<sup id="cite_ref-Law34_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law34-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Norreis did manage some good for the abbey, as it was while he was abbot that the <a href="/wiki/Crossing_tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Crossing tower">crossing tower</a> of the monastic church was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-Cox24_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cox24-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Early in his tenure Norreis appears to have been somewhat circumspect and did not greatly annoy his monks. The change appears to have happened around 1195, when the monks are first recorded as appealing to higher authority. Because Evesham had traditionally claimed to be exempt from episcopal oversight by the see of Worcester, in which it was located, the monks could only appeal to the <a href="/wiki/Papacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Papacy">papacy</a> or to a <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">papal legate</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Walter" title="Hubert Walter">Hubert Walter</a>, the Archbishop of Canterbury after Baldwin, held a legateship for England until July 1195 and so the monks appealed to him. Walter forced Norreis to make some compromises with his subordinates, but the truce did not last long, and the monks once again complained of their treatment. They were visited by the new <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishop of Worcester</a> <a href="/wiki/John_of_Coutances" title="John of Coutances">John of Coutances</a> some time between 1196 and 1198, but Norreis succeeded in avoiding any severe sanctions by offering gifts to the bishop. The monks again appealed to Walter in about 1200, but between the archbishop\'s preoccupation with political affairs and Norreis\'s promises to reform, no solid changes occurred.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic333_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic333-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dispute_with_Mauger_and_his_monks">Dispute with Mauger and his monks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Norreis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dispute with Mauger and his monks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1202 the new Bishop of Worcester <a href="/wiki/Mauger_of_Worcester" title="Mauger of Worcester">Mauger</a> attempted to investigate Norreis\'s conduct, but under the leadership of Thomas of Marlborough the monastic community refused to allow the bishop to visit, claiming that Evesham was exempt from <a href="/wiki/Canonical_visitation" title="Canonical visitation">episcopal visitation</a> and oversight.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> According to Thomas, Norreis offered to renounce the abbey\'s claim to exemption in return for money, but the bishop refused. Norreis then fled the monastery, while the monks continued to refuse the bishop and were excommunicated, a sentence that Norreis was spared.<sup id="cite_ref-Law45_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law45-19">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Between early September and mid-October the dispute was heard by three ecclesiastical tribunals; Mauger was supported by <a href="/wiki/Eustace_(bishop_of_Ely)" title="Eustace (bishop of Ely)">Eustace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Ely" title="Bishop of Ely">Bishop of Ely</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Giles_de_Braose" title="Giles de Braose">Giles de Braose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Hereford" title="Bishop of Hereford">Bishop of Hereford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The monks and Norreis then made common cause, each vowing to stand by the other in resisting Mauger.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As well as these two cases, there was a dispute between the monks and the bishop over the ownership of some properties.<sup id="cite_ref-Law34_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law34-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The monks and Norreis then obtained the right for their case to be heard by <a href="/wiki/Papal_judge-delegate" title="Papal judge-delegate">papal judge-delegates</a>, but Mauger objected to the fact that the appointed judges were all <a href="/wiki/Benedictine_monk" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine monk">Benedictine monks</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup> and travelled to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> to appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1203 Norreis, secure in his alliance with his own monks, once again began to exploit the monastery for his own profit and that of his family and friends. His monks became upset at this renewal of their exploitation and sent Thomas, along with other monks, to appropriate the harvest from some lands that had been given by Norreis to a non-member of the community. Norreis protested to King <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">John</a> and to Hubert Walter. Both the king and Walter summoned Thomas to appear before them separately and explain the actions of the monks. Thomas failed to persuade either man and nothing was done about Norreis\'s exactions and abuse of his monks.<sup id="cite_ref-Law53_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law53-21">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Norreis went to Rome in the company of Thomas of Marlborough shortly before 1205 to lay the abbey\'s case before the papacy. Thomas remained in Italy, but Norreis returned to England in the middle of 1205.<sup id="cite_ref-Law34_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law34-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> While in Rome, Norreis and Thomas borrowed 400 <a href="/wiki/Mark_(money)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark (money)">marks</a> to finance the litigation at the <a href="/wiki/Papal_curia" class="mw-redirect" title="Papal curia">papal curia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Law52_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law52-22">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;e&#93;</a></sup> Norreis could not repay this sum and for a while was in danger of being imprisoned, but he managed to leave Rome without suffering the fate of one of his companions who was detained for the debt and died while imprisoned.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> When Norreis made it back to England, Mauger excommunicated the abbot in April 1205.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The monks won a victory on the question of the abbey\'s exemption from episcopal visitation in 1206, but other elements of the quarrel remained unresolved, including that of the disputed churches.<sup id="cite_ref-Law34_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law34-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Later that year the monks became so annoyed by Norreis\'s abuse that they abandoned the abbey and did not return until they secured the abbot\'s confirmation of their rights.<sup id="cite_ref-Grandsen389_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grandsen389-24">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the question of the episcopal exemption decided in Evesham\'s favour, the alliance between Norreis and his monks dissolved. The monks again complained of their abbot\'s behaviour to the new papal legate, John of Sancta Maria.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The legate ordered an investigation, the result being a written agreement between the two parties.<sup id="cite_ref-Democratic27_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Democratic27-25">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Norreis gave gifts to the legate\'s nephew,<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> refused to sign the agreement,<sup id="cite_ref-Democratic27_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Democratic27-25">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and took revenge on his opponents by expelling Thomas of Marlborough and his allies from the monastery.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Thirty monks followed Thomas into exile, and Norreis pursued them with soldiers who were defeated by the monks.<sup id="cite_ref-Democratic27_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Democratic27-25">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A compromise was eventually reached, Norreis agreeing that the revenues of the abbey would be split with the monks, that officials of the abbey would be appointed by the abbot but with the advice and consent of the monks, and that the abbot would not admit or expel monks without taking the counsel of his monks nor dispose of the monastic property without the consent of his subordinates.<sup id="cite_ref-Democratic27_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Democratic27-25">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;f&#93;</a></sup> Although the issue of the monks\' support was temporarily solved, the issue of the disputed properties between Mauger and the monastery continued. Norreis appears to have been willing to compromise with the bishop, but the monks refused.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;g&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The dispute between Norreis and his monks dragged on for years. The <a href="/wiki/Interdict" title="Interdict">interdict</a> of <a href="/wiki/John,_King_of_England#Dispute_with_the_Pope" title="John, King of England">1207\xe2\x80\x931213</a> caused further delays, but when it was lifted in 1213 the case against Norreis was finally heard.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">papal legate</a> visited the abbey and found that the monks were lacking in food,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;h&#93;</a></sup> clothes, and other necessities. The <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgy</a> was not being performed according to the <a href="/wiki/Monastic_rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Monastic rule">monastic rule</a> because the monks lacked the proper clothing. Charity to the poor had ceased, the monastic buildings were dilapidated,<sup id="cite_ref-Moorman251_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moorman251-31">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and the monks were forced to beg for their needs.<sup id="cite_ref-Moorman305_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moorman305-32">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The legate found that the abbot was living well, wearing non-monastic clothes, and enjoying the company of young women in his dwelling, while his monks suffered.<sup id="cite_ref-Moorman251_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moorman251-31">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Norreis was deposed from the abbacy<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> by the papal legate in 1213.<sup id="cite_ref-Moorman251_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moorman251-31">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Norreis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 27 November 1213 Norreis was appointed the prior of <a href="/wiki/Penwortham_Priory" title="Penwortham Priory">Penwortham Priory</a>, a dependent priory of Evesham,<sup id="cite_ref-Heads94_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads94-33">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> partly to compensate him for the loss of the abbacy<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic335_18-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic335-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and partly to keep him from abandoning monastic life entirely.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Norreis was deposed five months later owing to his continued bad morals and behaviour<sup id="cite_ref-Heads94_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads94-33">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and again went to Rome in a bid to be restored to office. On this occasion he was unable to secure his reinstatement,<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but in 1218 or 1219 he was restored to Penwortham<sup id="cite_ref-Heads94_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads94-33">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> through the influence of the papal legate <a href="/wiki/Pandulf_Verraccio" title="Pandulf Verraccio">Pandulf Verraccio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Norreis died on 16 July<sup id="cite_ref-Heads261_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads261-34">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> or 19 July,<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but there is disagreement over the year of his death.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Heads261_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads261-34">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_Dictionary_of_National_Biography" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford Dictionary of National Biography">Oxford Dictionary of National Biography</a></i> states that he died in 1223,<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but the editors of <i>Heads of Religious Houses: England and Wales, 940\xe2\x80\x931216</i> state he died in either 1224 or 1225.<sup id="cite_ref-Heads261_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heads261-34">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> He was buried at Penwortham.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_2-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thomas of Marlborough, who knew Norreis, wrote that he was "everywhere condemned as the manifest enemy of God".<sup id="cite_ref-QDNB_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QDNB-7">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> More modern writers echo the sentiment. <a href="/wiki/John_Moorman" title="John Moorman">John Moorman</a> described Norreis as a man "totally unsuited to the delicate and responsible task of ruling over a company of men and directing the affairs of a wealthy corporation".<sup id="cite_ref-Moorman251_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moorman251-31">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Knowles_(scholar)" title="David Knowles (scholar)">David Knowles</a>, a historian of English monasticism, wrote that Norreis was "utterly unworthy to hold spiritual office of any kind", and that Baldwin\'s appointment of him as prior at Christ Church "must always remain a dark stain on the archbishop\'s reputation".<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic332_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic332-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Knowles also noted that he was "a man of great practical ability".<sup id="cite_ref-MonasticQ333_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MonasticQ333-35">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Even Thomas of Marlborough noted that Norreis was energetic, entertaining, and full of enterprise.<sup id="cite_ref-Monastic333_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monastic333-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
