class="mw-redirect" title="Harris, Outer Hebrides">Harris</a>, have both <a href="/wiki/Beaker_culture" title="Beaker culture">Beaker</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> dwelling houses, the oldest in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>, attesting to the settlement.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Celtic Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The earliest written mention of the Outer Hebrides was by the Greek historian Diodorus Siculus in 55 BC.  He wrote that there was an island called Hyperborea (which means "Far to the North") where a round temple stood from which the moon appeared only a little distance above the earth every 19 years, an apparent reference to the stone circle at Callanish.  Pomponius Mela, a Roman-Spanish writer of the first century, refers to a group of seven islands to which he gave the name Haemodae (Hebrides???).  Other ancient writers such as <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, the Egyptian astronomer <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, and Solinus (3 AD) all seem to mention the Hebrides, attesting to some contact of the peoples there with the Roman world.\n</p><p>Little is known of the history of the peoples of the Hebrides before the 6th century as they, like the rest of Scotland, were in the depths of what centuries later became known as the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>. The first written records of the islands come with the arrival of <a href="/wiki/St._Columba" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Columba">St. Columba</a> in the 6th century. It was this Irish-Scottish saint who first brought Christianity to the islands, founding several churches.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingdom_of_the_Isles">Kingdom of the Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kingdom of the Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Isles" title="Kingdom of the Isles">Kingdom of the Isles</a></div>\n<p>The Kingdom of the Isles (also known as the Kingdom of Mann and the Isles) was a <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">kingdom</a> that existed in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> from 1079 until 1266.\n</p><p>The kingdom was formed by <a href="/wiki/Godred_Crovan" title="Godred Crovan">Godred Crovan</a> when he seized the Isle of Man from other Vikings, probably from Dublin in 1079. In the first two attempts at capturing the island Godred was defeated; it was only with his third try that he was victorious near <a href="/wiki/Ramsey,_Isle_of_Man" title="Ramsey, Isle of Man">Ramsey</a>. Previously, the islands had been taken between c.700-900 AD, during the Viking invasions of the British Isles. Up until the arrival of Godred the islands had been administered by the Norse Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>. The later Kingdom of Mann was centred around the Isle of Man but also contained the Outer Hebrides, the Inner Hebrides forming the Kingdom of the Hebrides.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hebrides_under_Norse_control">The Hebrides under Norse control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Hebrides under Norse control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Known as <i>Su\xc3\xb0reyar</i> or southern islands in <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a>. <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> control of the Hebrides was formalised in 1098 when <a href="/wiki/Edgar_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Edgar of Scotland">Edgar of Scotland</a> recognised the claim of <a href="/wiki/Magnus_III_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus III of Norway">Magnus III of Norway</a>.  The Scottish acceptance of Magnus III as King of the Isles came after the Norwegian king had conquered the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a>, the Hebrides and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> in a swift campaign earlier the same year, directed against the local Norwegian leaders of the various islands.  By capturing the islands Magnus III subdued the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a> who had seized the islands centuries earlier and imposed a more direct royal control.\n</p><p>The Norwegian control of both the Inner and Outer Hebrides would see almost constant warfare until being ultimately resolved by the partitioning of the Western Isles in 1156. The Outer Hebrides would remain under the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Mann and the Isles">Kingdom of Mann and the Isles</a> while the Inner Hebrides broke out under <a href="/wiki/Somerled" title="Somerled">Somerled</a>, the Norse-Celtic kinsman of both <a href="/wiki/Lulach" title="Lulach">Lulach</a> and the Manx royal house. Although the Inner Hebrides, from 1156 known as the Kingdom of the Hebrides, was still nominally under the sovereignty of Norway, the leaders were Scottish in language and Gaelic in culture rather than Norse.\n</p><p>Two years after his victory of 1156 Somerled went on to seize control over the Isle of Man itself and become the last King of the Isle of Man and the Isles to rule over all the islands that the kingdom had once included. After Somerled\'s death in 1164 the rulers of Mann would no longer be in control of the Inner Hebrides.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_Control">Scottish Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scottish Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg/220px-HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg/330px-HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg/440px-HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg 2x" data-file-width="782" data-file-height="584" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HakonTheOldAndSkule-Flateyjarbok.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An illustration of H\xc3\xa1kon, King of Norway, and <a href="/wiki/Skule_B%C3%A5rdsson" title="Skule B\xc3\xa5rdsson">Skule B\xc3\xa5rdsson</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k">Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k</a></div></div></div><p>In 1262 there was a Scottish raid on Skye, causing <a href="/wiki/Haakon_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Haakon IV">Haakon IV</a>, King of Norway, to set sail for Scotland to settle the issue. Late in 1263 Haakon headed for Scotland with a large invasion force consisting of 200 ships and 15,000 men. The storms around the coast of Scotland took their toll on the Norwegian fleet, which at one point meant dragging forty ships overland to Loch Lomond. In the end a minor skirmish took place at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Largs" title="Battle of Largs">Battle of Largs</a> where the Norwegians and their Manx allies under <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Olafsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus Olafsson">Magnus Olafsson of Mann and the Isles</a> failed to achieve anything more than a minor tactical victory against the Scots led by Alexander Stewart. After the battle, bad weather forced the Norwegian-Manx fleet to sail back to the Orkney Islands. After arriving in <a href="/wiki/Kirkwall" title="Kirkwall">Kirkwall</a>, Haakon decided to winter in <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Palace,_Kirkwall" title="Bishop&#39;s Palace, Kirkwall">Bishop\'s Palace</a> before resuming his campaign the following summer. This failed to occur as the king was struck by illness and died in his palace in December that same year. The death of Haakon left the crown to his son <a href="/wiki/Magnus_VI_of_Norway" title="Magnus VI of Norway">Magnus the Lawmaker</a>, who considered peace with the Scots more important than holding on to the Norwegian possessions off western Scotland and in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Perth" title="Treaty of Perth">Treaty of Perth</a> of 1266 left the Hebrides and the Isle of Man to Scotland for 4,000 marks and an annual payment of 100 marks. The treaty also included that Scotland confirmed Norwegian sovereignty over Shetland and Orkney. Still, Scottish rule over the Isle of Man was finally confirmed only after the Manx under <a href="/wiki/Gu%C3%B0r%C3%B8%C3%B0r_Magn%C3%BAsson" title="Gu\xc3\xb0r\xc3\xb8\xc3\xb0r Magn\xc3\xbasson">Gu\xc3\xb0r\xc3\xb8\xc3\xb0r Magn\xc3\xbasson</a> were decisively defeated in the 1275 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ronaldsway" title="Battle of Ronaldsway">Battle of Ronaldsway</a>.\n</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Specific_Islands">History of Specific Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History of Specific Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barra">Barra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Barra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Barra" title="Barra">Barra</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kisimul_Castle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Kisimul_Castle.jpg/220px-Kisimul_Castle.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Kisimul_Castle.jpg/330px-Kisimul_Castle.jpg 1.5x, class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kisimul_Castle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kisimul_Castle" title="Kisimul Castle">Kisimul Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barra" title="Barra">Barra</a></div></div></div><p>The <a href="/wiki/Clan_MacNeil" title="Clan MacNeil">Clan MacNeil</a> has strong ties to the Isle of <a href="/wiki/Barra" title="Barra">Barra</a> and can trace its lineage back to the <a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_dynasty" title="O&#39;Neill dynasty">O\'Neills</a> of Ulster who came to Barra from Ireland around the year 1000. <a href="/wiki/Kisimul_Castle" title="Kisimul Castle">Kisimul Castle</a> at Castlebay is the hereditary seat of the MacNeils. It is located on an island in the bay, so giving the village its name. Other places of interest on the island include a <a href="/wiki/Blackhouse" title="Blackhouse">blackhouse</a> museum, a ruined church and museum at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cille_Bharra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cille Bharra (page does not exist)">Cille Bharra</a>, a number of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">brochs</a> such as those at D\xc3\xb9n Chuidhir and An D\xc3\xb9n B\xc3\xa0n, and a whole range of other Iron Age and later structures which have recently been excavated and recorded.\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flannan_Isles">Flannan Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Flannan Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flannan_Isles" title="Flannan Isles">Flannan Isles</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg/220px-The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg/330px-The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg/440px-The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="542" data-file-height="331" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_lighthouse_on_Eilean_Mor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The lighthouse on Eilean M\xc3\xb2r. The Chapel of St Flannan can be seen on the slope to the right of the lighthouse.</div></div></div>\n<p>As the name implies, Eilean Taighe, in the <a href="/wiki/Flannan_Isles" title="Flannan Isles">Flannan Isles</a>, hosts a ruined stone shelter. Eilean M\xc3\xb2r is home to the lighthouse and a ruined <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">chapel</a> dedicated to St Flannan, which the lighthouse keepers refer to as the \'dog kennel\' because of its very small size. These ruined <a href="/wiki/Bothies" class="mw-redirect" title="Bothies">bothies</a> were collectively described by the Ancient Monuments Commission as The Bothies of the <a href="/wiki/Chattan_Confederation" class="mw-redirect" title="Chattan Confederation">Clan McPhail</a><sup id="cite_ref-NLB1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NLB1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> or Bothain Chlann \xe2\x80\x98ic Phaill.<sup id="cite_ref-Tait_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tait-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is not entirely clear which St. Flannan the chapel honours. It is likely that he was either the 7th century Abbot of <a href="/wiki/Killaloe,_County_Clare" title="Killaloe, County Clare">Killaloe</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a> or alternatively the half brother of the 8th century <a href="/wiki/St_Ronan" class="mw-redirect" title="St Ronan">St Ronan</a> who gave his name to the nearby island of <a href="/wiki/North_Rona" title="North Rona">North Rona</a>. There was also a certain Flann, son of an Abbot of <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a> called Maol-duine who died in 890, and who may have loaned his name to these isolated isles.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> is also known as \'The Seven Hunters\', and in the Middle Ages they may also have been called the \'Seven Haley (Holy) Isles\'.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martin_Martin" title="Martin Martin">Martin Martin</a> (1703) lists a number of unusual customs associated with regular pilgrimages to Eilean M\xc3\xb2r such as removing one\'s hat and making a <a href="/wiki/Sunwise" title="Sunwise">sunwise</a> turn when reaching the plateau.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is possible that the saint or his acolytes lived on Eilean M\xc3\xb2r and perhaps Eilean Taighe as well. However, it is unlikely that there were permanent residents on the islands once the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic Church">Celtic Church</a> fell into decline in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> (as a result of 9th century <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> invasions), until the construction of the lighthouse and its occupation very shortly before the dawn of the 20th century.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Bernera">Great Bernera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span megalithic monument on <a href="/wiki/Great_Bernera" title="Great Bernera">Great Bernera</a> overlooks the bridge from Lewis</div></div></div>\n<p>The main settlement on the island is <a Lewis</a></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Sites">Historical Sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Callanish_standing_stones_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Callanish_standing_stones_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Callanish_Stones" title="Callanish href="/wiki/Garenin" title="Garenin">Garenin</a> (<i>Na Gearannan</i>), near Carloway and the Black House at <a href="/wiki/Arnol" title="Arnol">Arnol</a>;</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/St._Columba" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Columba">St. Columba</a>\'s church in <a href="/wiki/Aignish" title="Aignish">Aignish</a>;</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Teampull_Mholuaidh" class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%B9n_%C3%88istean" title="D\xc3\xb9n \xc3\x88istean">D\xc3\xb9n \xc3\x88istean</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>There are also numerous \'lesser\' stone circles and remains of brochs.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Events">Historical Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Historical Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg/220px-UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg/330px-UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg/440px-UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="506" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UigChessmen_SelectionOfKings.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two kings and two queens from the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_chessmen" title="Lewis chessmen">Lewis chessmen</a> at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a></div></div></div>\n<ul><li>The first evidence of human habitation of the islands is found in <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> samples which indicate that about 8,000 years ago, much of the native woodland was torched to make way for grassland to allow <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> to graze.</li>\n<li>The earliest <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> remains in Lewis date from about 5,000 years ago. At that time, people began to settle in permanent farms rather than following their herds.  The small houses of these people have been found throughout the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>, in particular, at <a href="/wiki/Dail_Mor" class="mw-redirect" title="Dail Mor">Dail Mor</a> in Lewis. The more striking great monuments of this period are the temples and communal burial cairns at places like <a href="/wiki/Calanais" class="mw-redirect" title="Calanais">Calanais</a>.</li>\n<li>About 500 BC, island society moved into the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. The buildings became larger and more prominent, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">brochs</a> which were circular dry-stone towers belonging to the local chieftains, the best example of which in Lewis is at <a href="/wiki/Dun_Carloway" title="Dun Carloway">Dun Charlabhagh</a>.</li>\n<li>7th and 8th centuries AD: Christianity began to spread throughout the islands.</li>\n<li>9th century AD: The <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> arrived, intermarrying with local families and abandoning their pagan beliefs. At this time, most buildings changed their forms from round to rectangular, following the Scandinavian style. At this time, Lewis was part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Mann and the Isles">Kingdom of Mann and the Isles</a> and officially part of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Lewis_chessmen" title="Lewis chessmen">Lewis chessmen</a>, which were found on the island in 1831, date from the time of Viking rule. The people were called the Gall-Ghaidheil, the \xe2\x80\x98Foreigner Gaels\', reflecting their mixed Scandinavian/Gaelic background and probably their bilingual speech.<sup id="cite_ref-http&#58;//www.cne-siar.gov.uk/factfile/cultureheritage/history/index.htm_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.cne-siar.gov.uk/factfile/cultureheritage/history/index.htm-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>1263 - The Battle of <a href="/wiki/Largs" title="Largs">Largs</a>: While not taking place in Lewis, it had a significant effect on <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>\'s defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Vikings</a>, leading to Lewis, and the rest of the Islands, being ceded to Scotland in 1266.</li>\n<li>Amie MacRuari, wife of <a href="/wiki/John_of_Islay" class="mw-redirect" title="John of Islay">John of Islay</a>, built <a href="/wiki/Borve_Castle,_Benbecula" title="Borve Castle, Benbecula">Borve Castle</a> on <a href="/wiki/Benbecula" title="Benbecula">Benbecula</a> Island sometime between 1344 and 1363<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>14th century: The <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Isles" title="Lord of the Isles">Lordship of the Isles</a> emerged as the most important power in northwestern Scotland. The Lords of the Isles were based on <a href="/wiki/Islay" title="Islay">Islay</a>, but controlled all of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>. They were descended from <a href="/wiki/Somerled" title="Somerled">Somerled</a> (Somhairle) Mac Gillibride, a Gall-Gaidheil lord who had held the Hebrides and West Coast two hundred years earlier.</li>\n<li>18th century:  Following the 1745 rebellion, and <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Prince_Charlie" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonnie Prince Charlie">Prince Charles Edward Stewart</a>\'s flight to France, the use of Gaelic was discouraged, rents were demanded in cash rather than kind, and the wearing of folk dress was made illegal. Emigration to the New World increasingly became an escape for those who could afford it during the latter half of the century.</li>\n<li>19th century: Clearances by landlords forced vast numbers of people off their lands, and increased again the flood of emigrants. Lewis was the site of numerous \'land struggles\' which have recently been commemorated in modern cairn-style monuments in various villages.</li>\n<li>1914-1918: During the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, thousands of islanders served in the forces, many losing their lives.</li>\n<li>1919: The Isle of Lewis suffered a terrible blow with the <a href="/wiki/Iolaire" class="mw-redirect" title="Iolaire">sinking of the <i>Iolaire</i></a> on New Year\'s Day at the close of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. The Admiralty yacht "HMY Iolaire" sank within sight of Stornoway\'s harbour, killing over 200 naval reservists from the island who were returning home.</li>\n<li>1939-1945: Again, Lewis contributed to the forces during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> with most serving in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merchant_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Merchant Navy">Merchant Navy</a>. Again, many lives were lost. Following the war, many more inhabitants emigrated to the Americas and mainland Scotland.</li>\n<li>Medieval development of Stornoway was spurred on by the construction of the original castle in the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a> by the Nicolson (or MacNicol) family of Viking descent. Infighting between rival clans continued throughout the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a> and resisted an attempt by <a href="/wiki/James_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI">James VI</a> to colonise Lewis in 1597.  The castle was destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>\'s forces in the mid 17th century, and the ownership of Lewis passed from the MacKenzies of Kintail through the Seaforth family and <a href="/wiki/Sir_James_Matheson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir James Matheson">Sir James Matheson</a> (and his descendants) to <a href="/wiki/William_Lever,_1st_Viscount_Leverhulme" title="William Lever, 1st Viscount Leverhulme">William Lever, 1st Viscount Leverhulme</a>.</li>\n<li>Lord Leverhulme finally gifted the town\'s parish to the Stornoway Trust, whose ownership remains to this day.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Uist">North Uist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: North Uist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/North_Uist" title="North Uist">North Uist</a></div>\n<p>North Uist has many prehistoric structures, including the <a href="/wiki/Barpa_Langass" title="Barpa Langass">Barpa Langass</a> <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pobull_Fhinn" title="Pobull Fhinn">Pobull Fhinn</a> <a href="/wiki/Stone_circle" title="Stone circle">stone circle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fir_Bhreige" class="mw-redirect" title="Fir Bhreige">Fir Bhreige</a> <a href="/wiki/Standing_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing stone">standing stones</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scalpay">Scalpay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scalpay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scalpay,_Outer_Hebrides" title="Scalpay, Outer Hebrides">Scalpay, Outer Hebrides</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Eilean_Glas,_Scalpay" title="Eilean Glas, Scalpay">Eilean Glas</a> a tiny peninsula on Scalpay\'s eastern shore is home to the first <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a> to be built in the Outer Hebrides.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Uist">South Uist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: South Uist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/South_Uist" title="South Uist">South Uist</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:South_Uist.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/South_Uist.jpg/220px-South_Uist.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/South_Uist.jpg/330px-South_Uist.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/South_Uist.jpg/440px-South_Uist.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:South_Uist.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Looking west to Nicolson\'s Leap.  In the background are <a href="/wiki/Beinn_Mh%C3%B2r_(South_Uist)" title="Beinn Mh\xc3\xb2r (South Uist)">Beinn Mh\xc3\xb3r</a> on the left, and <a href="/wiki/Hecla_(South_Uist)" title="Hecla (South Uist)">Hecla</a> on the right.</div></div></div><p>South Uist is home to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kildonan_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kildonan Museum (page does not exist)">Kildonan Museum</a> housing the 16th century <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clanranald_Stone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Clanranald Stone (page does not exist)">Clanranald Stone</a> and the ruins of the house where <a href="/wiki/Flora_MacDonald_(Scottish_Jacobite)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flora MacDonald (Scottish Jacobite)">Flora MacDonald</a> was born.\n</p><p>The SEARCH project (Sheffield Environmental and Archaeological Research Campaign in the Hebrides) on South Uist has been developing a long-term perspective on changes in settlement and house form from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> to the 19th century. Organisation within <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Roundhouse_(dwelling)" title="Roundhouse (dwelling)">roundhouses</a> appears to have been very different from 19th century <i>blackhouses</i> in which the dwelling was shared with stock.  Stock sharing living space with people is often regarded as a traditional Hebridean arrangement reflecting <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> influence.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An excavation at Bornais on the Isle of South Uist revealed what is probably the largest Viking settlement in Scotland.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> site of <a href="/wiki/Cladh_Hallan" title="Cladh Hallan">Cladh Hallan</a> on South Uist is the only site in the British Isles where prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummies</a> have been found.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="St_Kilda">St Kilda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: St Kilda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St Kilda, Scotland</a></div>\n<p>St Kilda was continuously inhabited for two millennia or more, from the Bronze Age to the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> However, little is known of the early history, the first written record of which dates from the late 14th century. The islands were historically part of the domain of the <a href="/wiki/Clan_MacLeod" title="Clan MacLeod">MacLeods</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harris,_Outer_Hebrides" class="mw-redirect" title="Harris, Outer Hebrides">Harris</a> whose steward was responsible for the collection of rents in kind and other duties. The first report of a visit to the islands dates from 1549.\n</p><p>Macaulay (1764) reports the existence of five druidic altars including a large circle of stones fixed perpendicularly in the ground, by the Stallir House on <a href="/wiki/Boreray,_St_Kilda" title="Boreray, St Kilda">Boreray</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stkildachurch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Stkildachurch.jpg/220px-Stkildachurch.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Stkildachurch.jpg/330px-Stkildachurch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Stkildachurch.jpg/440px-Stkildachurch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stkildachurch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The schoolroom (on the right of the photo) was added to the side of the church in 1884.</div></div></div>\n<p>Visiting ships in the 18th century brought <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> and in 1727 the loss of life was so high that there were not enough men to man the boats and new families were brought in from <a href="/wiki/Harris,_Outer_Hebrides" class="mw-redirect" title="Harris, Outer Hebrides">Harris</a> to replace them. By 1758 the population had risen to 88 and reached just under 100 by the end of the century. This figure remained fairly constant until 1851 when 36 islanders emigrated to Australia on board the <i>Priscilla</i>, a loss from which the island never fully recovered.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Insidestkildachurch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Insidestkildachurch.jpg/200px-Insidestkildachurch.jpg" width="200" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Insidestkildachurch.jpg/300px-Insidestkildachurch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Insidestkildachurch.jpg/400px-Insidestkildachurch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Insidestkildachurch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The interior of the church at Oiseabhal, St Kilda</div></div></div>\n<p>Tourism had a destabilising impact on St Kilda. During the 19th century steamers began to visit Hirta, enabling the islanders to earn money from the sale of <a href="/wiki/Tweed_(fabric)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tweed (fabric)">tweeds</a> and birds\' eggs but at the expense of their self-esteem as the tourists clearly regarded them as curiosities.\n</p><p>By the turn of the 20th century formal schooling had become a feature of the islands and in 1906 the church was extended to make a schoolhouse. All the children now learned English in addition to their native Gaelic. Improved <a href="/wiki/Midwifery" title="Midwifery">midwifery</a> skills reduced the problems of childhood tetanus. There had been some talk of an evacuation in 1875 but despite occasional food shortages and a \'flu epidemic in 1913 the population was stable at 75 to 80 people, and there was no obvious sign that within a few years the millennia-old occupation of the island was to end.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_One">World War One</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: World War One">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Early in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> erected a signal station on Hirta and daily communications with the mainland were established for the first time in St Kilda\'s history. In a belated response, a German submarine arrived in Village Bay on the morning of 15 May 1918 and after issuing a warning, started shelling the island. Seventy two shells in all were fired and the wireless station was destroyed. The manse, church, and jetty storehouse were also damaged.\n</p><p>As a result of this attack a Mark II QF gun was erected on a promontory overlooking Village Bay, but it was never <a href="/wiki/Fired_in_anger" class="mw-redirect" title="Fired in anger">fired in anger</a>. Of greater long-term significance to the islanders was the introduction of regular contact with the outside world and the slow development of a money-based economy, both of which made life easier but less self-reliant. These were both factors in the evacuation of the island only a little more than a decade later.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Evacuation">Evacuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Evacuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg/220px-Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg/330px-Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg/440px-Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="581" data-file-height="451" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boreray_and_the_Stacs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Boreray,_St_Kilda" title="Boreray, St Kilda">Boreray</a>, Stac Lee, and Stac an Armin (left) from the heights of Conachair</div></div></div>\n<p>The advent of tourism and the presence of the military in World War One had enabled the islanders to understand that there were alternatives to the privations they had routinely suffered. Despite the provision of a small jetty in 1902 the islands remained at the mercy of the weather. The authorities were unable to do much to assist them, although reliable radios and other infrastructure denied to the civilian islanders were later to be provided for the military base at a cost of millions of pounds.\n</p><p>After World War One most of the young men left the island and the population fell from 73 in 1920 to 37 in 1928. After the death of four men from <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">influenza</a> in 1926, and a succession of crop failures in the 1920s, the last straw came with the death from <a href="/wiki/Appendicitis" title="Appendicitis">appendicitis</a> of a young woman, Mary Gillies, in January 1930. On 29 August 1930, the last 36 inhabitants were evacuated to <a href="/wiki/Morvern" title="Morvern">Morvern</a> on the Scottish mainland at their own request.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_military_events">Later military events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Outer_Hebrides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Later military events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1955 the British government decided to incorporate St Kilda into a missile tracking range based in <a href="/wiki/Benbecula" title="Benbecula">Benbecula</a>, where test firings and flights are carried out. Thus in 1957 St Kilda became permanently inhabited once again. A variety of new military buildings and masts have since been erected, including the island\'s first licensed premises, the \'Puff Inn\'. The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> leases St Kilda from the National Trust for Scotland for a nominal fee. The main island of Hirta is still occupied all year round by a small number of civilians working in the military base there.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
