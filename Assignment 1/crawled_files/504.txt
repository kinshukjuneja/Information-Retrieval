England, Wales or Northern Ireland</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:England_and_Wales_AONBs_map.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/England_and_Wales_AONBs_map.svg/300px-England_and_Wales_AONBs_map.svg.png" width="300" height="364" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/England_and_Wales_AONBs_map.svg/450px-England_and_Wales_AONBs_map.svg.png 1.5x, class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:England_and_Wales_AONBs_map.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty in England and Wales</div></div></div>\n<p>An <b>Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</b> (<b>AONB</b>) is an area of <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">countryside</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> or <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> which has been designated for <a href="/wiki/Protected_area" title="Protected area">conservation</a> due to its significant landscape value.  Areas are designated in recognition of their national importance, by the relevant public body: <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Wales" title="Natural Resources Wales">Natural Resources Wales</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Environment_Agency" title="Northern Ireland Environment Agency">Northern Ireland Environment Agency</a>. In place of AONB, Scotland uses the similar <a href="/wiki/National_Scenic_Area_(Scotland)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Scenic Area (Scotland)">National Scenic Area</a> (NSA) designation.  Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty enjoy levels of protection from development similar to those of UK <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="National parks of the United Kingdom">national parks</a>, but unlike with national parks the responsible bodies do not have their own <a href="/wiki/Town_and_country_planning_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Town and country planning in the United Kingdom">planning powers</a>. They also differ from national parks in their more limited opportunities for extensive outdoor recreation.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div tocsection-12"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The idea for what would eventually become the AONB designation was first put forward by <a href="/wiki/John_Gordon_Dower" title="John Gordon Dower">John Dower</a> in his 1945 <i>Report to the Government on National Parks in England and Wales</i>.  Dower suggested there was need for protection of certain naturally beautiful landscapes which were unsuitable as national parks due to their small size and lack of wildness. Dower\'s recommendation for the designation of these "other amenity areas" was eventually embodied in the <a href="/wiki/National_Parks_and_Access_to_the_Countryside_Act_1949" title="National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949">National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949</a> as the AONB designation.<sup id="cite_ref-AONB_story_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AONB_story-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The purpose of an AONB designation is to <a href="/wiki/Landscape-scale_conservation" title="Landscape-scale conservation">conserve</a> and enhance the natural beauty of the designated landscape by placing it under the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gov_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gov-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There two secondary aims: meeting the need for quiet enjoyment of the countryside and having regard for the interests of those who live and work there. To achieve these aims, AONBs rely on <a href="/wiki/Town_and_country_planning_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Town and country planning in the United Kingdom">planning controls</a> and practical countryside management. As they have the same landscape quality, AONBs may be compared to the <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">national parks of England and Wales</a>. National parks are well known to many inhabitants of the UK; by contrast, there is evidence to indicate many residents in AONBs may be unaware of the status. However, the National Association of AONBs is working to increase awareness of AONBs in local communities,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and in 2014 successfully negotiated to have the boundaries of AONBs in England shown on <a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">Google Maps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_overview">Statistical overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Statistical overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg/220px-ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg/330px-ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg/440px-ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="375" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ThreeCliffsBay.jos.500pix.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View from the Gower peninsula, the first AONB to be designated.</div></div></div>\n<p>There are 46 AONBs in Britain (33 wholly in England, four wholly in Wales, one which straddles the Anglo-Welsh border and eight in Northern Ireland). The first AONB was designated in 1956 in the <a href="/wiki/Gower_Peninsula" title="Gower Peninsula">Gower Peninsula</a>, South Wales. The most recently confirmed is the <a href="/wiki/Tamar_Valley_AONB" title="Tamar Valley AONB">Tamar Valley AONB</a> in 1995,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> although the existing <a href="/wiki/Clwydian_Range" title="Clwydian Range">Clwydian Range</a> AONB was extended in 2012 to form the Clwydian Range and Dee Valley AONB, and the <a href="/wiki/Strangford_Lough" title="Strangford Lough">Strangford Lough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lecale_Coast_AONB" title="Lecale Coast AONB">Lecale Coast</a> AONBs were merged and redesignated as a single AONB in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-strangfordandlecale_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strangfordandlecale-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>AONBs vary greatly in terms of size, type and use of land, and whether they are partly or wholly open to the public. The smallest AONB is the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> (1976), 16&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (6.2&#160;sq&#160;mi), and the largest is the <a href="/wiki/Cotswolds" title="Cotswolds">Cotswolds</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> (1966, extended 1990<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>), 2,038&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (787&#160;sq&#160;mi). The AONBs of England and Wales together cover around 18% of the countryside in the two countries. The AONBs of Northern Ireland together cover about 70% of Northern Ireland\'s coastline.<sup id="cite_ref-AONB_story_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AONB_story-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status_and_organization">Legal status and organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal status and organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>AONBs in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> were originally created under the same legislation as the <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Parks_and_Access_to_the_Countryside_Act_1949" title="National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949">National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949</a>. Unlike AONBs, national parks have special legal powers to prevent unsympathetic development. AONBs in general remain the responsibility of their local authorities by means of special committees which include members appointed by the Minister and by parishes, and only very limited statutory duties were imposed on local authorities within an AONB by the original 1949 Act. However, further regulation and protection of AONBs in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> was added by the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">Countryside and Rights of Way (CRoW) Act 2000</a>, under which new designations are now made,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and the Government has recently in the <a href="/wiki/National_Planning_Policy_Framework" title="National Planning Policy Framework">National Planning Policy Framework</a> (March 2012) stated that AONBs and national parks have equal status when it comes to planning decisions on landscape issues.  Two of the AONBs (the Cotswolds and the Chilterns), which extend into a large number of local authority areas, have their own statutory bodies, known as Conservation Boards. \n</p><p>All English and Welsh AONBs have a dedicated AONB officer and other staff. As required by the CRoW Act, each AONB has a management plan that sets out the characteristics and special qualities of the landscape and how they will be conserved and enhanced. The AONBs are collectively represented by the National Association for AONBs, an independent organization acting on behalf of AONBs and their partners.\n</p><p>AONBs in Northern Ireland was designated originally under the Amenity Lands (NI) Act 1965; subsequently under the Nature Conservation and Amenity Lands (NI) Order 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Amexstadjuly10.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Amexstadjuly10.jpg/220px-Amexstadjuly10.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Amexstadjuly10.jpg/330px-Amexstadjuly10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Amexstadjuly10.jpg/440px-Amexstadjuly10.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1300" data-file-height="867" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amexstadjuly10.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Falmer stadium under construction in 2010 in the former Sussex Downs AONB</div></div></div>\n<p>There are growing concerns among environmental and countryside groups that AONB status is increasingly under threat from development. The <a href="/wiki/Campaign_to_Protect_Rural_England" title="Campaign to Protect Rural England">Campaign to Protect Rural England</a> said in July 2006 that many AONBs were under greater threat than ever before.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Three particular sites were cited: the <a href="/wiki/Dorset_AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorset AONB">Dorset AONB</a> threatened by a road plan, the threat of a football stadium in the <a href="/wiki/Sussex_Downs_AONB" title="Sussex Downs AONB">Sussex Downs AONB</a>, and, larger than any other, a \xc2\xa31&#160;billion plan by <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a> to build thousands of houses and offices on hundreds of acres of AONB land on the <a href="/wiki/Kent_Downs" title="Kent Downs">Kent Downs</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wye,_Kent" title="Wye, Kent">Wye</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In September 2007 government approval was finally given for the development of a new football ground for <a href="/wiki/Brighton_and_Hove_Albion" class="mw-redirect" title="Brighton and Hove Albion">Brighton and Hove Albion</a> within the boundaries of the Sussex Downs AONB, after a fierce fight by conservationists. The subsequent development, known as <a href="/wiki/Falmer_Stadium" title="Falmer Stadium">Falmer Stadium</a>, was officially opened in July 2011. The Weymouth Relief Road in Dorset was constructed between 2008 and 2011, after environmental groups lost a High Court challenge to prevent its construction.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Writing in 2006, Professor Adrian Phillips listed threats facing AONBs. He wrote that the apparent big threats were uncertainty over future support for land management, increasing development pressures, the impacts of globalization, and climate change. More subtle threats include creeping sub-urbanization and "<a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_equestrian_terms#H" title="Glossary of equestrian terms">Horsiculture</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-AONB_story_2-2"
