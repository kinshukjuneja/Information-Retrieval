id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Golden Age of Baseball</b>, or <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">Baseball</a>\'s Golden Era, is the period from about <a href="/wiki/1920_in_baseball" title="1920 in baseball">1920</a> to <a href="/wiki/1960_in_baseball" title="1960 in baseball">1960</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The golden era is the time period immediately following the <a href="/wiki/Dead-ball_era" title="Dead-ball era">dead-ball era</a> (before World War I) but prior to what is now called the modern era. There is no exact timeframe in any of these eras.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  MLB considers the post World War II era to be the beginning of the modern age, which places the golden era between the end of World War I and the end of World War II.\n</p><p>\nMuch of baseball\'s golden age was captured in black and white film, adding to the mystique and <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> of the game.  The first baseball game broadcast in color was in 1951,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and by the mid-1960s all baseball games were broadcast in color, which could be viewed as the end of the golden age.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruth1921.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ruth1921.jpg/150px-Ruth1921.jpg" width="150" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ruth1921.jpg/225px-Ruth1921.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ruth1921.jpg/300px-Ruth1921.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3970" data-file-height="5944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruth1921.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Babe Ruth was the single most dominant player in the golden age of baseball.</div></div></div>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Players"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Players</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Teams"><span class="mw-headline" id="Players">Players</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_age_of_baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Players">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The golden age was dominated by stars such as <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Lou Gehrig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hank_Greenberg" title="Hank Greenberg">Hank Greenberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Hornsby" title="Rogers Hornsby">Rogers Hornsby</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth%27s_called_shot" title="Babe Ruth&#39;s called shot">called shot</a> was one of the defining moments of the era.  Another defining moment of the golden era was <a href="/wiki/Gabby_Hartnett" title="Gabby Hartnett">Gabby Hartnett</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Homer_in_the_Gloamin%27" title="Homer in the Gloamin&#39;">Homer in the Gloamin\'</a>.  By 1919, when Ruth hit a then-league record 29 home runs, a spectacular feat at that time, the dead-ball era had officially come to an end, ushering in the <a href="/wiki/Live-ball_era" title="Live-ball era">Live-ball era</a>.\n</p><p>While the most popular icons of the golden era are hitters, and Ruth is generally thought of as one of the premier sports icons in history,<sup id="cite_ref-answers.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> there were also several pitchers who dominated hitters on the mound during that same time, and two of the best of them were <a href="/wiki/Lefty_Grove" title="Lefty Grove">Lefty Grove</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dizzy_Dean" title="Dizzy Dean">Dizzy Dean</a>, both of whom won over 30 games in the early 1930s.  Later in the era, players such as <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mickey Mantle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Banks" title="Ernie Banks">Ernie Banks</a>, and pitchers <a href="/wiki/Bob_Gibson" title="Bob Gibson">Bob Gibson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warren_Spahn" title="Warren Spahn">Warren Spahn</a>, among others, established themselves as <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Major League Baseball Hall of Fame">Hall of Famers</a> and ultimately played into the late 1960s and 1970s, providing a bridge between the current era and the golden age.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teams">Teams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_age_of_baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Teams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From a team perspective, the golden age of baseball (using the years 1918-<a href="/wiki/1964_in_baseball" title="1964 in baseball">1964</a> as a guideline) was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> who won 29 <a href="/wiki/Pennant_(sports)" title="Pennant (sports)">pennants</a> and 20 World Series titles between 1918 and 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  To expound on that figure, in the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>, it took three clubs combined to win 27 pennants (nine each) during that same timeframe, those teams being the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a>, all of whom were dominant in their own right.  In addition, the N.L.\'s <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> tallied 6 N.L. pennants (none until <a href="/wiki/2016_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="2016 Chicago Cubs season">2016</a>) and the A.L.\'s <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> snared four A.L. pennants during that same time, the second highest total in the junior circuit after the Bronx Bombers\' historic 29.\n</p><p>Teams travelled primarily by train during the period, occasionally stopping off at <a href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bar (establishment)">saloons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speakeasies" class="mw-redirect" title="Speakeasies">speakeasies</a> in between games, mingling with fans and adding to the mystique of the era, as this is unlikely to happen often today.  Many players also worked other "primary" jobs in the offseason, and others stepped away in the middle of their careers to serve in the military.\n</p><p>Entire franchises were worth hundreds of thousands of dollars, at the time, with the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> becoming the first team to be sold for over $1,000,000 when <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope" title="Bob Hope">Bob Hope</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Veeck" title="Bill Veeck">Bill Veeck</a> bought the club for $1.6 million.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
