colspan="2">\n</td></tr>\n\n\n\n</tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>South Downs National Park</b> is <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>\'s newest <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">national park</a>, having become fully operational on 1 April 2011. The park, covering an area of 1,627 square kilometres (628&#160;sq&#160;mi) in southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, stretches for 140 kilometres (87&#160;mi) from Winchester in the west to Eastbourne in the east through the counties of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Sussex" title="East Sussex">East Sussex</a>. The national park covers the chalk hills of the <a href="/wiki/South_Downs" title="South Downs">South Downs</a> (which on the English Channel coast form the white cliffs of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Sisters,_Sussex" title="Seven Sisters, Sussex">Seven Sisters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beachy_Head" title="Beachy Head">Beachy Head</a>) and a substantial part of a separate physiographic region, the <a href="/wiki/Western_Weald" title="Western Weald">western Weald</a>, with its heavily wooded sandstone and clay hills and vales. The <a href="/wiki/South_Downs_Way" title="South Downs Way">South Downs Way</a> spans the entire length of the park and is the only <a href="/wiki/National_Trail" class="mw-redirect" title="National Trail">National Trail</a> that lies wholly within a national park.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Administration"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Administration</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Geography"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Geography</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Geology"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Geology</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Downs_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The idea of a South Downs National Park goes back to the 1920s,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> when public concern was mounting about increasing threats to the beautiful downland environment, particularly the impact of indiscriminate speculative housing development on the eastern Sussex Downs (<a href="/wiki/Peacehaven" title="Peacehaven">Peacehaven</a> was a notorious example of this). In 1929, the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_the_Protection_of_Rural_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Campaign for the Protection of Rural England">Council for the Preservation of Rural England</a>, led by campaigners including the geographer <a href="/wiki/Vaughan_Cornish" title="Vaughan Cornish">Vaughan Cornish</a>, submitted a memorandum to the Prime Minister urging the case for national parks, including a national park on part of the South Downs. When however, towards the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, John Dower was asked to report on how a system of <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">national parks in England and Wales</a> might be established, his 1945 report, <i>National Parks in England and Wales</i>, did not identify the South Downs for national park status, but rather included it in a list of "other amenity areas". <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hobhouse" title="Arthur Hobhouse">Sir Arthur Hobhouse</a>\'s 1947 <i>Report of the National Parks Committee</i> took a different view, and he included the South Downs in his list of twelve areas recommended for designation as a national park, defined by John Dower as an "extensive area of beautiful and relatively wild country in which, for the nation\'s benefit...the characteristic landscape beauty is strictly preserved".\n</p><p>The South Downs was the last of the original twelve recommended national parks to be designated. Extensive damage to the chalk <a href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">downland</a> from 1940 onwards through arable farming, and a resulting decline in sheep grazing, militated at an early stage against further work on designation. When in 1956 the National Parks Commission came to consider the case for the South Downs as a national park, it found designation no longer appropriate, noting that the value of the South Downs as a potential national park had been reduced by cultivation. It did however recognise the "great natural beauty" of the area, and proposed it be designated as an <a href="/wiki/AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="AONB">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>. In due course two AONBs were designated, split along the county boundary, namely the <a href="/wiki/East_Hampshire_AONB" title="East Hampshire AONB">East Hampshire AONB</a> in 1962 and the <a href="/wiki/Sussex_Downs_AONB" title="Sussex Downs AONB">Sussex Downs AONB</a> in 1966.  These were later to form the basis of the South Downs National Park.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 1999 the government, following a review of national parks policy, declared support for a South Downs National Park, and announced a consultation on its creation. In January 2003 the then <a href="/wiki/Countryside_Agency" title="Countryside Agency">Countryside Agency</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>) made an Order to designate the proposed park in 2003 which was submitted to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for the Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Secretary of State for the Environment</a> on 27 January 2003.\n</p><p>As a result of objections and representations received on the proposed Order, a <a href="/wiki/Public_inquiry" title="Public inquiry">public inquiry</a> was conducted between 10 November 2003 and 23 March 2005,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> with the aim of recommending to ministers whether a national park should be confirmed and, if so, where its boundaries should be. The results of the inquiry were expected by the end of 2005, but were delayed pending a legal issue arising from a <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> case challenging part of the Order designating the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="New Forest National Park">New Forest National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following an appeal on the High Court case and new legislation included in the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> the South Downs Inquiry report was published on 31 March 2006. It recommended a 23% reduction in the size of the originally proposed national park, focussing it more narrowly on the chalk downland and excluding from it a large part of the existing East Hampshire and Sussex Downs AONBs. This proved highly controversial, leading to calls from the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_the_Protection_of_Rural_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Campaign for the Protection of Rural England">Campaign for the Protection of Rural England</a> and others for the inclusion of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Western_Weald" title="Western Weald">western Weald</a>, a region within the two AONBs possessing a geology, ecology and landscape quite different from the chalk hills of the South Downs, within the park boundary to ensure that it remained protected from development. The Secretary of State invited objections and representations on new issues relating to the proposed national park in a consultation that ran from 2 July to 13 August 2007. In the light of the responses received, the Secretary of State decided that it was appropriate to re-open the 2003\xe2\x80\x9305 public inquiry. The inquiry re-opened on 12 February 2008 and was closed on 4 July 2008 after 27 sitting days. The Inspector\'s report was submitted on 28 November 2008.\n</p><p>On 31 March 2009 the result of the inquiry was published. The <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Secretary of State</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hilary_Benn" title="Hilary Benn">Hilary Benn</a>, announced that the South Downs would be designated a national park,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and on 12 November 2009 he signed the order confirming the designation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Importantly, he confirmed that a number of hotly disputed areas, including the western Weald, the town of <a href="/wiki/Lewes" title="Lewes">Lewes</a> and the village of <a href="/wiki/Ditchling" title="Ditchling">Ditchling</a>, would be included within the national park.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The new national park came into full operation on 1 April 2011 when the new South Downs National Park Authority assumed statutory responsibility  for it. The occasion was marked by an opening ceremony which took place in the market square of <a href="/wiki/Petersfield" title="Petersfield">Petersfield</a>, a town situated in the western Weald just 4&#160;km (2.5&#160;mi) north of the chalk escarpment of the South Downs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Downs_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beachy_Head,_Sussex,_UK.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Beachy_Head%2C_Sussex%2C_UK.jpg/220px-Beachy_Head%2C_Sussex%2C_UK.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Beachy_Head%2C_Sussex%2C_UK.jpg/330px-Beachy_Head%2C_Sussex%2C_UK.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Beachy_Head%2C_Sussex%2C_UK.jpg/440px-Beachy_Head%2C_Sussex%2C_UK.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beachy_Head,_Sussex,_UK.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The cape of <a href="/wiki/Beachy_Head" title="Beachy Head">Beachy Head</a> lies within the national park</div></div></div>\n<p>The national park is administered by the <b>South Downs National Park Authority</b> (SDNPA). The SDNPA was established on 1 April 2010, and became fully functioning, including becoming the planning authority for the national park, on 1 April 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is responsible for promoting the statutory purposes of the national park and the interests of the people who live and work within it. The statutory purposes of the SDNPA, as a national park authority, are specified by the <a href="/wiki/Environment_Act_1995" title="Environment Act 1995">Environment Act 1995</a>; these are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>To conserve and enhance the natural beauty, wildlife and cultural heritage of the area</li>\n<li>To promote opportunities for the understanding and enjoyment of the Park\xe2\x80\x99s special qualities by the public.</li></ul>\n<p>It must also fulfill the following duty:\n</p>\n<ul><li>In carrying out its role, the authority has a duty to seek to foster the economic and social well-being of the communities living within the national park.</li></ul>\n<p>The SDNPA is a public body, funded by central government, and run by a board of twenty seven members. The board consists of seven national members, appointed by the environment secretary by means of an open recruitment process; fourteen local authority nominees drawn from the fifteen local authorities covering the park area with Adur and Worthing opting to share a place; and six parish council representatives, two for each county.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The chair of SDNPA is Margaret Paren, a former civil servant who after retirement became involved in campaigning for the national park.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Downs_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:South_Downs_View.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/South_Downs_View.jpg/220px-South_Downs_View.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/South_Downs_View.jpg/330px-South_Downs_View.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/South_Downs_View.jpg/440px-South_Downs_View.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:South_Downs_View.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A view west from <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Dyke,_Sussex" title="Devil&#39;s Dyke, Sussex">Devil\'s Dyke</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The South Downs National Park stretches for 140&#160;km (87&#160;mi) across southern England from <a href="/wiki/St._Catherine%27s_Hill,_Hampshire" title="St. Catherine&#39;s Hill, Hampshire">St Catherine\'s Hill</a> near <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Hampshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Winchester, Hampshire">Winchester</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> in the west to <a href="/wiki/Beachy_Head" title="Beachy Head">Beachy Head</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Eastbourne" title="Eastbourne">Eastbourne</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Sussex" title="East Sussex">East Sussex</a> in the east.  In its western half, the southern boundary of the park lies up to 10&#160;km (6.2&#160;mi) inland from the south coast; it thus excludes the major coastal towns and cities of <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chichester" title="Chichester">Chichester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bognor_Regis" title="Bognor Regis">Bognor Regis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Littlehampton" title="Littlehampton">Littlehampton</a>. Further east, where the park\'s southern boundary lies much closer to the coast, it has been carefully drawn to exclude the urban areas of <a href="/wiki/Worthing" title="Worthing">Worthing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brighton_and_Hove" title="Brighton and Hove">Brighton and Hove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newhaven,_East_Sussex" title="Newhaven, East Sussex">Newhaven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seaford,_East_Sussex" title="Seaford, East Sussex">Seaford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastbourne" title="Eastbourne">Eastbourne</a>, which had all made substantial encroachments onto the Downs during the 19th and 20th centuries. By contrast, the park includes a number of towns situated in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Weald" title="Western Weald">western Weald</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Petersfield,_Hampshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Petersfield, Hampshire">Petersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liss" title="Liss">Liss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midhurst" title="Midhurst">Midhurst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petworth" title="Petworth">Petworth</a>, and the two historic Sussex towns of <a href="/wiki/Arundel" title="Arundel">Arundel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lewes" title="Lewes">Lewes</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sussex_Downs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Sussex Downs.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Sussex_Downs.jpg/220px-Sussex_Downs.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Sussex_Downs.jpg/330px-Sussex_Downs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Sussex_Downs.jpg/440px-Sussex_Downs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4128" data-file-height="3096" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sussex_Downs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>The total population living within the national park is approximately 108,000. Of these 42,000 live in Hampshire, 40,000 in West Sussex and 25,000 in East Sussex. East Hampshire District Council area and Chichester District each have around 30,000 residents in the area and Lewes District 22,000. Winchester has 11,500 residents in the park, with much smaller numbers for the other districts and boroughs.<sup id="cite_ref-consult_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-consult-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The area receives about 39 million visits each year, which is thought likely to increase as a result of the creation of the national park.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The national park has an area of 1,625&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (627&#160;sq&#160;mi), of which 544&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (210&#160;sq&#160;mi) is in Hampshire, 807&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (312&#160;sq&#160;mi) in West Sussex and 237&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (92&#160;sq&#160;mi) in East Sussex. Among the district council areas, Chichester District has the largest area at 544&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (210&#160;sq&#160;mi), followed by East Hampshire District with 279&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (108&#160;sq&#160;mi), Winchester with 265&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (102&#160;sq&#160;mi), Lewes District with 159&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (61&#160;sq&#160;mi) and Arun 102&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (39&#160;sq&#160;mi). 93&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (36&#160;sq&#160;mi) are in Horsham District and 60&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (23&#160;sq&#160;mi) in Wealden District.<sup id="cite_ref-consult_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-consult-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Apart from a number of boundary revisions, the park incorporates two areas previously designated as <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>, the <a href="/wiki/East_Hampshire_AONB" title="East Hampshire AONB">East Hampshire AONB</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sussex_Downs_AONB" title="Sussex Downs AONB">Sussex Downs AONB</a>. The park also includes the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_Country_Park" title="Queen Elizabeth Country Park">Queen Elizabeth Country Park</a> near Petersfield.\n</p><p>The South Downs National Park\'s <a href="/wiki/Chalk_downland" class="mw-redirect" title="Chalk downland">chalk downland</a> is a feature that sets it apart from other national parks in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, almost a quarter (23%) of the national park consists of a quite different and strongly contrasting physiographic region, the <a href="/wiki/Western_Weald" title="Western Weald">western Weald</a>, whose densely wooded hills and vales are based on an older Wealden geology of resistant sandstones and softer clays. The highest point in the national park, <a href="/wiki/Blackdown,_West_Sussex" title="Blackdown, West Sussex">Blackdown</a>, at 280&#160;m (919&#160;ft) above sea level, is in fact situated in the Weald, on the <a href="/wiki/Greensand_Ridge" title="Greensand Ridge">Greensand Ridge</a>, whereas the highest point on the chalk escarpment of the South Downs, <a href="/wiki/Butser_Hill" title="Butser Hill">Butser Hill</a>, has an elevation of  271&#160;m (889&#160;ft) above sea level.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Downs_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seven_Sisters_cliffs_and_the_coastguard_cottages,_from_Seaford_Head_showing_Cuckmere_Haven_(looking_east_-_2003-05-26).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" 1.5x, Sisters cliffs and the coastguard cottages, from Seaford Head across the <a href="/wiki/River_Cuckmere" title="River Cuckmere">River Cuckmere</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Most of the national park consists of <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> <a href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">downland</a>, although a significant part includes the sandstones and clays of the western <a href="/wiki/Weald" title="Weald">Weald</a>, a strongly contrasting and distinctive landscape of densely wooded hills and vales.\n</p><p>The chalk was formed in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a>, between 100 million and 66 million years ago, when the area was under the sea. During the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> era the chalk was uplifted as part of the Weald uplift which created the great <a href="/wiki/Weald-Artois_Anticline" class="mw-redirect" title="Weald-Artois Anticline">Weald-Artois Anticline</a>, caused by the same <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogenic</a> movements that created the Alps. The relatively resistant chalk rock has, through <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>, resulted in a classic <a href="/wiki/Cuesta" title="Cuesta">cuesta</a> landform, with a northward-facing chalk <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpment</a> that rises dramatically above the low-lying vales of the Low Weald.\n</p><p>The chalk escarpment reaches the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> west of <a href="/wiki/Eastbourne" title="Eastbourne">Eastbourne</a>, where it forms the dramatic white cliffs of <a href="/wiki/Beachy_Head" title="Beachy Head">Beachy Head</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Sisters,_Sussex" title="Seven Sisters, Sussex">Seven Sisters</a> and Seaford Head. These cliffs were formed after the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>, when sea levels rose and the English Channel was formed, resulting in under-cutting of the chalk by the sea.\n</p><p>The South Downs run linearly west-north-westwards from the Eastbourne area through southern Sussex to the Hampshire downs, separating the south coastal plain from the clays and sandstones of the <a href="/wiki/Weald" title="Weald">Weald</a>. Behind the escarpment, on the dip slope, are the characteristic high, smooth, rolling downland hills interrupted by dry valleys and wind gaps, and the major river gaps of the <a href="/wiki/Cuckmere" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuckmere">Cuckmere</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Ouse,_Sussex" title="River Ouse, Sussex">Ouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Adur" title="River Adur">Adur</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Arun" title="River Arun">Arun</a>.\n</p><p>The chalk is a white <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> rock, notably homogeneous and fine-grained, and very permeable. It consists of minute <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> plates (<a href="/wiki/Coccoliths" class="mw-redirect" title="Coccoliths">coccoliths</a>) shed from micro-organisms called <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophores" class="mw-redirect" title="Coccolithophores">coccolithophores</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> include numerous layers of <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> <a href="/wiki/Nodule_(geology)" title="Nodule (geology)">nodules</a>, which have been widely exploited as a material for manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tools</a> as well as a <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building material</a> for dwellings. Similar areas in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> include the <a href="/wiki/North_Downs" title="North Downs">North Downs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chilterns" class="mw-redirect" title="Chilterns">Chilterns</a>.\n</p><p>In its western section, the national park extends north beyond the chalk escarpment of the South Downs into a quite different and strongly contrasting physiographic region, the western <a href="/wiki/Weald" title="Weald">Weald</a>, taking in the valley of the western <a href="/wiki/River_Rother_(Western)" class="mw-redirect" title="River Rother (Western)">River Rother</a>, incised into <a href="/wiki/Lower_Greensand" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower Greensand">Lower Greensand</a> bedrock, and the densely wooded hills and valleys of the <a href="/wiki/Greensand_Ridge" title="Greensand Ridge">Greensand Ridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weald_Clay" title="Weald Clay">Weald Clay</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Haslemere" title="Haslemere">Haslemere</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Downs_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In fact, the idea may go back further, according to Peter Brandon in his book, <i>The Shaping of the Sussex Landscape</i> (Snake River Press, 2009).
