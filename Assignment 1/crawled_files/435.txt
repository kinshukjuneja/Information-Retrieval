<small><i>(June 2018)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The term <b>Other White</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_ethnicity_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Classification of ethnicity in the United Kingdom">classification of ethnicity in the United Kingdom</a> and has been used in documents such as the <a href="/wiki/2011_UK_Census" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 UK Census">2011 UK Census</a> to describe people who self-identify as <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">white</a> persons who are not of the <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> or <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> ethnic groupings. The category  does not comprise a single ethnic group but is instead a method of identification for white people who are not represented by other white census categories. This means that the Other White group contains a diverse collection of people with different countries of birth, languages and religions. Along with <a href="/wiki/White_British" title="White British">White British</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Briton" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Briton">White Irish</a>, the category does not appear in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, where only one single "White" classification was presented to respondents.<sup id="cite_ref-Harmonised_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harmonised-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The categorisation was primarily intended to cover people with ancestry from <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental Europe</a>, with the largest of these ethnic groups in the country being <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Other_White&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner noresize" style=""><div class="thumbimage" style=""><div class="mw-graph" style="min-width:125px;min-height:125px"><img class="mw-graph-img" src="/api/rest_v1/page/graph/png/Other_White/0/dde86e8c14972a47c820abeba8eb7847471d892c.png" /></div></div><div class="thumbcaption">Birthplace of "Other White" in England and Wales as of the 2001 Census<sup id="cite_ref-ONS_who_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONS_who-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>In the 2001 UK Census, the majority of people living in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> ticking the \'Other White\' ethnic group specified their ethnicity as European.<sup id="cite_ref-ONS_who_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONS_who-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Four out of five of the \'Other White\' category (i.e. not British or Irish) were born overseas. A\nthird were born in a Western European country other than the UK, and one in seven were born in an Eastern European country.<sup id="cite_ref-ONS_who_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONS_who-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since January 2012">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup> The Other White group is largely of working age, with only one in ten aged over 65 and one in seven under 16 at the time of the 2001 census. This does vary according to the stated country of birth, with people born in the UK being disproportionately young. Polish and Italian respondents had a larger proportion of over 65s,<sup id="cite_ref-ONS_who_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONS_who-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since January 2012">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup> which reflects the migration of <a href="/wiki/Poles_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Poles in the United Kingdom">Poles</a> and Italians to Britain after the Second World War.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A wide number of religions are represented in the Other White group. In the census, the largest faith group, 63 per cent, identified themselves as Christian, with 16 per cent defining themselves as without religion, nine per cent as Muslims, and two per cent as Jewish.<sup id="cite_ref-ONS_who_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONS_who-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
