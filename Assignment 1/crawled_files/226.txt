class="mw-headline" id="Ireland_in_Classical_literature">Ireland in Classical literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ireland in Classical literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Roman_relations" title="Hiberno-Roman relations">Hiberno-Roman relations</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_references">Early references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Classical writers occasionally refer to Ireland under a variety of names, but these references contain little reliable information. For example, <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> claims that the <i>Prettanoi</i> of the island of <i>Iris</i> eat human flesh.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, who calls the island <i>Ierne</i>, repeats the accusation, adding that they consider it honourable to eat their dead fathers, and openly have sex with their mothers and sisters (although he is sceptical about his sources).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a> calls it <i>Iuverna</i> and says that, although the climate is unfavourable for grain, grass grows so richly that cattle burst if unrestrained from eating it.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico" title="Commentarii de Bello Gallico">Commentarii de Bello Gallico</a></i>, is the first to call the island <i><a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a></i>, describes it as about half the size of Britain, and correctly places it to the west of Britain<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> &#8211; unlike Strabo, who places it to the north.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tacitus">Tacitus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tacitus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> says that his father-in-law <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gnaeus Julius Agricola</a>, while governor of Britain (AD 78-85), considered conquering Ireland, believing it could be held with one <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">legion</a> plus <a href="/wiki/Auxiliaries_(Roman_military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliaries (Roman military)">auxiliaries</a>, and entertained an exiled Irish petty king with the intention of making him the pretext for conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-ta24_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ta24-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Parallels have been drawn with the Irish legend of <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BAathal_Techtmar" title="T\xc3\xbaathal Techtmar">T\xc3\xbaathal Techtmar</a>, who is said to have been exiled to Britain as a child and returned with an army to claim the kingship of <a href="/wiki/Hill_of_Tara" title="Hill of Tara">Tara</a> in the 1st century.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Tacitus also says that most of the harbours and approaches to Ireland were known through commerce, but inaccurately locates the island between Britain and the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberian peninsula">Iberian peninsula</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ta24_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ta24-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Juvenal">Juvenal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Juvenal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 2nd-century poet <a href="/wiki/Juvenal" title="Juvenal">Juvenal</a>, in his second <i><a href="/wiki/Satires_of_Juvenal" class="mw-redirect" title="Satires of Juvenal">Satire</a></i>, contrasting the victories of the Roman army with the low morals of the people at home in Rome, says that, as well as conquering Britain and the <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> islands, "we have advanced arms beyond the shores of <i>Iuverna</i>". Although Juvenal is not writing history, it is possible that he is referring to a genuine Roman military expedition to Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ptolemy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 2nd-century <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandrian</a> Greek writer <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, one of the most important geographers, mathematicians and astronomers in the ancient world, refers to Ireland in two of his works. In the astronomical treatise known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i> he gives the latitudes of an island he calls <i>Mikra Brettania</i> (\xce\x9c\xce\xb9\xce\xba\xcf\x81\xe1\xbd\xb0 \xce\x92\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1) or "Little Britain" (the south of the island at 58 degrees, the north at 61 degrees). In his <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i>, at the same latitudes, he places the Prettanic island <i>Iwernia</i>, next to its neighbour, the Prettanic island <i><a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>). The <i>Geography</i> contains the most detailed account of Ireland in classical literature, giving the latitude and longitude of six promontories, fifteen river mouths, ten settlements and nine islands, and naming sixteen population groups.<sup id="cite_ref-freeman_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freeman-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="float:left; position: relative;border: solid #CCCCCC 1px;padding:5px;margin:5px;">\n<p><a href="/wiki/File:Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png" class="image"><img alt="Island of Ireland NASA.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png/300px-Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png" width="300" height="380" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png/450px-Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png/600px-Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png 2x" data-file-width="746" data-file-height="945" /></a>\n</p>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:99px; top:352px"><b>Iwernoi</b></div>\n<p>Peoples of Ireland according to <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-freeman_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freeman-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_coast">North coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Ptolemy describes the northern coast of Ireland, from the Northern Promontory (possibly <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Foreland" class="mw-redirect" title="Bloody Foreland">Bloody Foreland</a> or Rossan Point in <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a>) in the west, to the <i>Wenniknion</i> promontory (probably <a href="/wiki/Malin_Head" title="Malin Head">Malin Head</a>), the mouth of the river <i>Widwa</i> (probably the <a href="/wiki/River_Foyle" title="River Foyle">Foyle</a>), the mouth of the river <i>Argita</i> (perhaps the <a href="/wiki/River_Bann" title="River Bann">Bann</a>) and the <i>Rhobogdion</i> promontory (<a href="/wiki/Fair_Head" title="Fair Head">Fair Head</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a>) in the east. The peoples who inhabit the north coast are the <i>Wenniknioi</i> in the west and the <i>Rhobogdioi</i> in the east.<sup id="cite_ref-freeman_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freeman-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="West_coast">West coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: West coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The west coast is poorly represented compared to the other three, and identification of the names Ptolemy gives is speculative. He begins with the Northern Promontory (see above), and working north to south names the river <i>Rhawiu</i> (possibly the <a href="/wiki/River_Erne" title="River Erne">Erne</a>); the town <i><a href="/wiki/Nagnata" title="Nagnata">Magnata</a></i> (a settlement of the <i>Magnatai</i> people, possibly somewhere in <a href="/wiki/County_Sligo" title="County Sligo">County Sligo</a>); the mouth of the rivers <i>Libniu</i> (possiby <a href="/wiki/Clew_Bay" title="Clew Bay">Clew Bay</a>), <i>Ausoba</i> (perhaps <a href="/wiki/Galway_Bay" title="Galway Bay">Galway Bay</a>) <i>Senu</i> (probably the <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">Shannon</a>, although placed too far to the north), <i>Dur</i> (possibly <a href="/wiki/Dingle_Bay" title="Dingle Bay">Dingle Bay</a>) and <i>Iernu</i> (possibly the <a href="/wiki/Kenmare" title="Kenmare">Kenmare</a>), and the Southern Promontory (any one of <a href="/wiki/Slea_Head" title="Slea Head">Slea Head</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bray_Head" title="Bray Head">Bray Head</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dursey_Head&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dursey Head (page does not exist)">Dursey Head</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mizen_Head" title="Mizen Head">Mizen Head</a>). Peoples of this coast are: the <i><a href="/wiki/Erdini" title="Erdini">Erdinoi</a></i> near Donegal Bay; the <i><a href="/wiki/Nagnatae" title="Nagnatae">Magnatai</a></i> or <i>Nagnatai</i> of County Mayo and Sligo; the <i>Auteinoi</i> between County Galway and the Shannon, identifiable with the early medieval <a href="/wiki/Uaithni" title="Uaithni">Uaithni</a>; the <i><a href="/wiki/Gangani" title="Gangani">Ganganoi</a></i>, also known in north <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and the <i>Wellaboroi</i> in the far south-west.<sup id="cite_ref-freeman_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freeman-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-or_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-or-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="South_coast">South coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: South coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The south coast stretches from the Southern Promontory to the mouths of the rivers <i>Dabrona</i> (possibly the <a href="/wiki/River_Lee_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="River Lee (Ireland)">Lee</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Munster_Blackwater" title="Munster Blackwater">Blackwater</a>) and <i>Birgu</i> (probably the <a href="/wiki/River_Barrow" title="River Barrow">Barrow</a>) and the Sacred Promontory (<a href="/wiki/Carnsore_Point" title="Carnsore Point">Carnsore Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>). Peoples of the south coast are the <i><a href="/wiki/Iverni" title="Iverni">Iwernoi</a></i> in the west, who share their name with the island, <i>Iwernia</i>, and can be identified with the early medieval \xc3\x89rainn; the <i>Usdiai</i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Brigantes" title="Brigantes">Brigantes</a></i> in the east, who share their name with a people of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-freeman_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freeman-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-or_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-or-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="East_coast">East coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: East coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>From the Sacred Promontory in the south to the <i>Rhobogdion</i> promontory in the north, Ptolemy names the river <i>Modonnu</i> (possibly the <a href="/wiki/River_Slaney" title="River Slaney">Slaney</a>, but more likely the <a href="/wiki/River_Avoca" title="River Avoca">Avoca</a>), the town of <i>Manapia</i> (a settlement of the <i>Manapii</i>), the river <i>Oboka</i> (perhaps the <a href="/wiki/River_Liffey" title="River Liffey">Liffey</a>; the river Avoca takes its modern name from a misinterpretation of Ptolemy\'s <i>Oboka</i>), the town of <i>Eblana</i> (a settlement of the <i>Eblanoi</i>, formerly mistakenly identified with <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>), the river <i>Buwinda</i> (the <a href="/wiki/River_Boyne" title="River Boyne">Boyne</a>), the promontory <i>Isamnion</i>, the river <i>Winderios</i> (possibly <a href="/wiki/Carlingford_Lough" title="Carlingford Lough">Carlingford Lough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundrum_Bay" title="Dundrum Bay">Dundrum Bay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Strangford_Lough" title="Strangford Lough">Strangford Lough</a>), and the river <i>Logia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Belfast_Lough" title="Belfast Lough">Belfast Lough</a>, <i>Loch Laoigh</i> in Irish). Peoples named, from south to north, are: the <i><a href="/wiki/Coriondi" title="Coriondi">Koriondoi</a></i>; the <i>Manapioi</i>, possibly related to the <a href="/wiki/Menapii" title="Menapii">Menapii</a> of Gaul; the <i><a href="/wiki/Cauci" title="Cauci">Kaukoi</a></i>, probably not related to the Germanic <a href="/wiki/Chauci" title="Chauci">Chauci</a> of the Low Countries; the <i><a href="/wiki/Eblani" title="Eblani">Eblanoi</a></i>; the <i>Woluntioi</i>, identifiable with the early medieval <a href="/wiki/Ulaid" title="Ulaid">Ulaid</a>; and the <i><a href="/wiki/Darini" title="Darini">Darinoi</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-freeman_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freeman-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_references">Later references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protohistory_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Later references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Roman sources mention raids on Britain by <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxons</a> of north-west <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> from <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and by two groups of people usually associated with Ireland, the <a href="/wiki/Scoti" title="Scoti">Scotti</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atacotti" class="mw-redirect" title="Atacotti">Atacotti</a>. The origins and meanings of Scotti and Atacotti is uncertain. Atacotti disappears with the Romans. Scotti means <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Adomn%C3%A1n" title="Adomn\xc3\xa1n">Adomn\xc3\xa1n</a> in the late seventh century, but not to Columbanus in the early sixth century, who uses the older term Iberi instead. The Scotti are perhaps a confederation of tribes in Ulster, and the Atacotti one in Leinster, but this is not certain.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
