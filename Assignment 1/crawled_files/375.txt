Education</h1>\t\t\t<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"><span For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/GCSE_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="GCSE (disambiguation)">GCSE (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p><p>The <b>General Certificate of Secondary Education</b> (<b>GCSE</b>) is an academic qualification, generally taken in a number of subjects by pupils in <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a> in England, Wales and Northern Ireland. Each GCSE qualification is in a particular subject, and stands alone, but a suite of such qualifications (or their equivalents) are generally accepted as the record of achievement at the age of 16, in place of a leaving certificate or baccalaureate qualification in other territories.\n</p><p>Studies for GCSE examinations generally take place over a period of two or three academic years (depending upon the subject, school, and exam board), starting in <a href="/wiki/Year_9" class="mw-redirect" title="Year 9">Year 9</a> or <a href="/wiki/Year_10" class="mw-redirect" title="Year 10">Year 10</a> for the majority of students, with examinations being sat at the end of <a href="/wiki/Year_11" class="mw-redirect" title="Year 11">Year 11</a>. The GCSE was introduced as a replacement for the former <a href="/wiki/GCE_Ordinary_Level_(United_Kingdom)" title="GCE Ordinary Level (United Kingdom)">O-Level (GCE Ordinary Level)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="Certificate of Secondary Education">CSE (Certificate of Secondary Education)</a> qualifications.\n</p>\n<div class="toclimit-3"><div id="toc" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_qualifications">Previous qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Previous qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Before the introduction of GCSEs, students took CSE (Certificate of Secondary Education) or the more academically challenging O-Level (General Certificate of Education (GCE) Ordinary Level) exams, or a combination of the two, in various subjects. The CSE broadly covered GCSE grades C-G or 4-1, and the O-Level covered grades A*-C or 9-4, but the two were independent qualifications, with different grading systems. The separate qualifications were criticised for disadvantaging the bottom 42% of O-Level entrants who failed to receive a qualification, and the highest-achieving CSE entrants who had no opportunity to demonstrate their true ability.\n</p><p>In its later years, O-Levels were graded on a scale from A to E, with a U (ungraded) grade below that. Before 1975, the grading scheme varied between examination boards, but typically there were "pass" grades of 1 to 6 and "fail" grades of 7 to 9. However the grades were not displayed on certificates. \n</p><p>The CSE was graded on a numerical scale from 1 to 5, with 1 being the highest, and 5 being the lowest passing grade. Below 5 there was a U (ungraded) grade. The highest grade, 1, was considered equivalent to an O-Level C grade or above, and achievement of this grade often indicated that the student could have taken an O-Level course in the subject to achieve a higher qualification. As the two were independent qualifications with separate syllabi, a separate course of study would have to be taken to "convert" a CSE to an O-Level in order to progress to <a href="/wiki/GCE_Advanced_Level" title="GCE Advanced Level">A-Level</a>.\n</p><p>There was a previous attempt to unite these two disparate qualifications in the 1980s, with a trial "16+" examination in some subjects, awarding both a CSE and an O-Level certificate, before the GCSE was introduced.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_the_GCSE">Introduction of the GCSE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Introduction of the GCSE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>GCSEs were introduced in 1988 <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> to establish a national qualification for those who decided to leave school at 16, without pursuing further academic study towards qualifications such as <a href="/wiki/GCE_Advanced_Level" title="GCE Advanced Level">A-Levels</a> or university degrees. They replaced the former CSE and O-Level qualifications, uniting the two qualifications to allow access to the full range of grades for more students. However the exam papers sometimes had a choice of questions designed for the more able and the less able candidates.\n</p><p>Upon introduction, the GCSEs were graded on a letter scale, from A to G, with a C being set as roughly equivalent to an O-Level Grade C, or a CSE Grade 1, and thus achievable by roughly the top 25% of each cohort.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_since_initial_introduction">Changes since initial introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Changes since initial introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Over time, the range of subjects offered, the format of the examinations, the regulations, the content, and the grading of GCSE examinations has altered considerably. Numerous subjects have been added and changed, and various new subjects are offered in the modern languages, ancient languages, vocational fields, and expressive arts, as well as Citizenship courses.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Introduction_of_the_A.2A_grade"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_the_A*_grade">Introduction of the A* grade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Introduction of the A* grade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1994, the A* grade was added above the grade A, to further differentiate attainment at the very highest end of the qualification. This remained the highest grade available until 2017. The youngest pupil to gain an A* grade was Thomas Barnes, who earned an A* in GCSE Mathematics at the age of 7.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s_reforms">2000s reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000s reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Between 2005 and 2010, a variety of reforms were made to GCSE qualifications, including increasing modularity and a change to the administration of non-examination assessment.\n</p><p>From the first assessment series in 2010, controlled assessment replaced coursework in various subjects, requiring more rigorous exam-like conditions for much of the non-examination assessed work, and reducing the opportunity for outside help in coursework.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s_reforms">2010s reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010s reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Under the Conservative government of David Cameron, and Education Secretary Michael Gove, various changes were made to GCSE qualifications. Before a wide range of reforms, interim changes were made to existing qualifications, removing the January series of examinations as an option in most subjects, and requiring that 100% of the assessment in subjects from the 2014 examination series is taken at the end of the course. These were a precursor to the later reforms.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 2015, a large-scale programme of reform began, changing the marking criteria and syllabi for most subjects, as well as the format of qualifications, and the grading system.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the new scheme, all GCSE subjects were revised between 2015 and 2018, and all new awards will be on the new scheme by summer 2020. The new qualifications are designed such that most exams will be taken at the end of a full 2-year course, with no interim modular assessment, coursework, or controlled assessment, except where necessary (such as in the arts). Some subjects will retain coursework on a non-assessed basis, with the completion of certain experiments in science subjects being assumed in examinations, and teacher reporting of spoken language participation for English GCSEs as a separate report.\n</p><p>Other changes include the move to a numerical grading system, to differentiate the new qualifications from the old-style letter-graded GCSEs, publication of core content requirements for all subjects, and an increase in longer, essay-style questions to challenge students more. Alongside this, a variety of low-uptake qualifications and qualifications with significant overlap will cease, with their content being removed from the GCSE options, or incorporated into similar qualifications.\n</p><p>GCSE examinations in English and mathematics were reformed with the 2015 syllabus publications, with these first examinations taking places in 2017. The remainder will be reformed with the 2016 and 2017 syllabus publications, leading to first awards in 2018 and 2019, respectively.\n</p><p>Qualifications that are not reformed will cease to be available. The science reforms, in particular, mean that single-award "science" and "additional science" options are no longer available, being replaced with a double award "combined science" option (graded on the scale 9-9 to 1-1 and equivalent to 2 GCSEs). Alternatively, students can take separate qualifications in chemistry, biology, and physics. Other removed qualifications include a variety of design technology subjects, which are reformed into a single "design and technology" subject with multiple options, and various catering and nutrition qualifications, which are folded into "food technology". Finally, several "umbrella" GCSEs such as "humanities", "performing arts", and "expressive arts" are dissolved, with those wishing to study those subjects needing to take separate qualifications in the incorporated subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>These reforms do not directly apply in Wales and Northern Ireland, where GCSEs will continue to be available on the A*-G grading system. However, due to legislative requirements for comparability between GCSEs in the three countries, and allowances for certain subjects and qualifications to be available in Wales and Northern Ireland, some 9-1 qualifications will be available, and the other changes are mostly adopted in these countries as well.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examination_boards">Examination boards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Examination boards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Historically, there were a variety of regional examination boards, or awarding organisations (AOs), who set examinations in their area. Over time, as deregulation allowed schools to choose which boards to use, mergers and closures led to only 5 examination boards remaining today.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Assessment_and_Qualifications_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Assessment and Qualifications Alliance">Assessment and Qualifications Alliance</a> (AQA), which absorbed the following boards: <a href="/wiki/Associated_Examining_Board" title="Associated Examining Board">AEB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joint_Matriculation_Board" title="Joint Matriculation Board">JMB</a>, <a href="/wiki/NEAB" title="NEAB">NEAB</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Examining_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Southern Examining Group (page does not exist)">SEG</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Oxford,_Cambridge_and_RSA_Examinations" title="Oxford, Cambridge and RSA Examinations">Oxford, Cambridge and RSA Examinations</a> (OCR), which absorbed the Oxford and Cambridge, <a href="/wiki/Midland_Examining_Group" title="Midland Examining Group">MEG</a>, and RSA exam boards.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Edexcel" title="Edexcel">Pearson Edexcel</a>, which absorbed the LREB, BTEC, and ULEAC boards.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Welsh_Joint_Education_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Joint Education Committee">Welsh Joint Education Committee</a> (WJEC or CBAC), the main examining board in Wales.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Council_for_the_Curriculum,_Examinations_%26_Assessment" title="Council for the Curriculum, Examinations &amp; Assessment">Council for the Curriculum, Examinations &amp; Assessment</a> (CCEA), the examining board and regulator in Northern Ireland.</li></ul>\n<p>The examination boards operate under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Ofqual" title="Ofqual">Ofqual</a> (The Office of Qualifications and Examinations Regulation) in England, <a href="/wiki/Qualifications_Wales" title="Qualifications Wales">Qualifications Wales</a> in Wales, and the CCEA in Northern Ireland.\n</p><p>In England, AQA, OCR, and Pearson operate under their respective brands. Additionally, WJEC operate the brand Eduqas, which develops qualifications in England. CCEA qualifications are not available in England.\n</p><p>In Wales, WJEC is the only accredited awarding body for GCSEs in the public sector, and thus no other board formally operates in Wales. However, some qualifications from the English boards are available as designated qualifications in some circumstances, due to not being available from WJEC.\n</p><p>In Northern Ireland, CCEA operates as both a board and a regulator. Most qualifications from the English boards are also available, with the exception of English language and the sciences, due to requirements for speaking and practical assessment, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_format">Structure and format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Structure and format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Students usually take at least 5 GCSEs in <a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_4" title="Key Stage 4">Key Stage 4</a>, in order to satisfy the long-standing headline measure of achieving 5 A*-C grades, including English and mathematics. The exact qualifications taken by students vary from school to school and student to student, but schools are encouraged to offer at least one pathway that leads to qualification for the <a href="/wiki/English_Baccalaureate" title="English Baccalaureate">English Baccalaureate</a>, requiring GCSEs in English language, English literature, mathematics, 2 science GCSEs, a modern or ancient language, and either history or geography.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subjects">Subjects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Subjects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The list of currently available GCSE subjects is much shorter than before the reforms, as the new qualifications in England all have core requirements set by the regulator, Ofqual, for each subject. These two levels roughly correspond, respectively, to foundation and higher tier in tiered GCSE qualifications. Level 1 qualifications constitute GCSEs at grades G, F, E, and D or 1, 2, and 3. Level 2 qualifications are those at grades C, B, A, and A* or 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, and 9.\n</p><p>The tiering of qualifications allows a subset of grades to be reached in a specific tier\'s paper. Formerly, many subjects were tiered, but with the mid-2010s reform, the number of tiered subjects reduced dramatically, including the removal of tiering from the GCSE English specifications. Untiered papers allow any grade to be achieved. Coursework and controlled assessment tasks are always untiered.\n</p><p>In the past, mathematics qualifications offered a different set of tiers, with three. These were foundation tier at grades G, F, E, and D; intermediate tier at grades E, D, C, and B; and higher tier at grades C, B, A, and A*. X indicates that a course was not completed in full, and therefore an appropriate grade cannot be calculated. The Q (query) grade is a temporary grade that requires the school to contact the examining body. These latter two grades are both usually provisional, and are replaced with a regular grade once any issues have been resolved. X grades are also sometimes used for other purposes, on rare occasions, such as to indicate that an examiner found offensive material or hate speech within a student\'s responses. In some cases, this may lead to the student losing all marks for that paper or course. These grades are most common in subjects which discuss ethical issues, such as biology, religious studies, and citizenship.\n</p><p>In 1994, an A* grade was added above the initial A grade to indicate exceptional achievement, above the level required for the A grade.\n</p><p>Under the letter grade scheme, foundation tier papers assess content at grades C to G, while higher tier papers assess content at grades A* to C. In foundation tier papers, the student can obtain a maximum grade of a C, while in a higher tier paper, they can achieve a minimum grade of a D. If a higher tier candidate misses the D grade by a small margin, they are awarded an E. Otherwise, the grade below E in these papers is U. In untiered papers, students can achieve any grade in the scheme.\n</p><p>This scheme is being phased out in England, but remains in Wales and Northern Ireland. In Northern Ireland, the A* grade has been adjusted upwards with the introduction of the numerical scheme in England, such that an A* is equivalent to a new English grade 9. They also added a C* grade to line up with the grade 5 in the English scheme, for comparison purposes.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Numerical_grades_.282017_onwards.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerical_grades_(2017_onwards)">Numerical grades (2017 onwards)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Numerical grades (2017 onwards)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>From 2017 in England (and in Wales and Northern Ireland on qualifications from the English boards), some GCSEs are now assessed on a 9-point scale, using numbers from 9 to 1, and, like before, a U (unclassified) grade for achievement below the minimum pass mark. Under this system, 9 is the highest grade, and is set above the former A* classification, equivalent to the new Northern Irish A* grade. The former C grade is set at grade 4 and the lower end of grade 5, with grade 5 being considered a "good pass" under the new scheme.\n</p><p>Although fewer qualifications have tiered examinations than before, the tiering system still exists. At foundation tier, the grades 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 are available, while at higher tier, the grades 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, and 9 are targeted. Once again, if a higher tier student misses the grade 4 mark by a small margin, they are awarded a grade 3, and controlled assessment and coursework tasks are untiered.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GCSE_results_2016.pdf" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/GCSE_results_2016.pdf/page1-220px-GCSE_results_2016.pdf.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/GCSE_results_2016.pdf/page1-330px-GCSE_results_2016.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/GCSE_results_2016.pdf/page1-440px-GCSE_results_2016.pdf.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1753" data-file-height="1240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GCSE_results_2016.pdf" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Infographic" title="Infographic">Infographic</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ofqual" title="Ofqual">Ofqual</a> showing statistics about GCSE entries in 2016. 5.24 million people took a GCSE in that year.</div></div></div>\n<p>GCSE results are published by the examination board in August, for the previous exam series in April to June of the same year. class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Assessment types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modular_and_linear_GCSEs">Modular and linear GCSEs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Modular and linear GCSEs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the past, many GCSE qualifications used a modular system, where some assessment (up to 60% under the \'terminal rule\') could be submitted prior to the final examination series. This allowed for students to take some units of a GCSE before the final examination series, and thus gave indication of progress and ability at various stages, as well as allowing for students to resit exams in which they did not score as highly, in order to boost their grade, before receiving the qualification.\n</p><p>Various qualifications were available as both modular and linear schemes, and schools could choose whichever fit best for them.\n</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> government of David Cameron, and <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Education" title="Secretary of State for Education">Education Secretary</a> Michael Gove, reforms were initiated which converted all GCSEs from 2012 (for assessment from 2014) to de facto linear schemes, in advance of the introduction of new specifications between 2015 and 2018 (for first assessment from 2017 to 2020). These new rules required that 100% of the assessment in a GCSE be submitted in the final examination series, at the same time as applying for certification of the full qualification.<sup id="cite_ref-edexcel.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edexcel.com-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ocr.org.uk_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocr.org.uk-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The examination boards complied by modifying the syllabi of the remaining GCSE qualifications to remove modular components.<sup id="cite_ref-edexcel.com_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edexcel.com-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ocr.org.uk_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocr.org.uk-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Both modular and linear assessment have been politically contentious, and the opposition <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_UK" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour Party UK">Labour Party UK</a>, and particularly the former <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament (UK)">MP</a> <a href="/wiki/Tristram_Hunt" title="Tristram Hunt">Tristram Hunt</a> stated that it was their policy that such reforms be halted and reversed, maintaining modular assessment in both GCSEs and A-Levels.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The modular scheme is supported by the University of Oxford and the University of Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coursework_and_controlled_assessment">Coursework and controlled assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Coursework and controlled assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In some subjects, one or more controlled assessment or coursework assignments may also be completed. These may contribute either a small or large proportion of the final grade. In practical and performance subjects, they generally have a heavier weighting to reflect the difficulty and potential unfairness of conducting examinations in these areas.\n</p><p>In the past, these were available in a variety of subjects, including extended writing in English, the sciences, business, and foreign languages; practical assessment in the sciences and technology subjects; and speaking assessments in languages. Since the 2010s reform, the availability has been cut back, with mostly only design and technology subjects and performing arts retaining their controlled assessment contributions. In English, the spoken language assessment has been downgraded to an endorsement which is reported separately on the English certificate, not contributing to the grade. In the sciences, practical exercises are a required part of the qualification, but are not directly assessed; they are only endorsed by a teacher\'s statement.\n</p><p>The balance between controlled assessment and examinations is contentious, with the time needing to be set aside for coursework sessions being seen as a burden on the school timetable. However, the use of controlled assessment allows for the marking of some work outside of examination season, and can ease the burden on students to perform well on the day of the examination.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptional_and_mitigating_circumstances">Exceptional and mitigating circumstances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Exceptional and mitigating circumstances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For pupils with learning difficulties, an injury/repetitive strain injury (RSI) or a disability, help is offered in these forms:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Extra time (the amount depends on the severity of the learning difficulty, such as <a href="/wiki/Dyslexia" title="Dyslexia">dyslexia</a>, disability, injury or learning in English as a second language provided that the pupil has been studying in the UK for no more than 2 years)</li>\n<li>Amanuensis (somebody types or handwrites as the pupil dictates; this is normally used when the pupil cannot write due to an injury or disability)</li>\n<li>A word processor (without any spell-checking tools) can be used by pupils who have trouble writing legibly or who are unable to write quickly enough to complete the exam</li>\n<li>A different format exam paper (large print, <a href="/wiki/Braille" title="Braille">Braille</a>, printed on coloured paper, etc.)</li>\n<li>A \'reader\' (a teacher or <a href="/wiki/Exam_invigilator" title="Exam invigilator">exam invigilator</a> can read out the words in the exam paper, but they cannot explain their meaning)</li>\n<li>A different room (sometimes due to a disability a pupil can be placed in a room by themselves or with selected others; this also happens when an amanuensis is used, so as not to disturb the other candidates. All exam rooms are covered by separate dedicated invigilators.)</li></ul>\n<p>Any of the above must be approved by the examination board. Other forms of help are available with the agreement of the examination board, but the above are the most common.\n</p><p>If a student is ill or an unforeseen circumstance occurs that may affect their performance in an examination, they can apply for special consideration from the examination board. The procedures vary depending on how much the student has completed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, but in the case of sitting an examination, they may receive a percentage increase on their grade<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to reflect this, or a consideration of their coursework and other assessment alongside their predicted grades, to calculate a fair grade based on their other attainment.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Progression">Progression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Progression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>GCSEs, BTECs or other Level 2 qualifications are generally required in order to pursue Level 3 qualifications such as A-Levels or <a href="/wiki/BTEC_Extended_Diploma" title="BTEC Extended Diploma">BTEC Extended Diplomas</a> beyond the age of 16.\n</p><p>The requirement of 5 or more A*\xe2\x80\x93C or 9-4 grades, including English and mathematics, is often a requirement for post-16 qualifications in <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form_college" title="Sixth form college">sixth form colleges</a> or <a href="/wiki/Further_education" title="Further education">further education</a> colleges after leaving secondary school. Where the subject taken post-16 has also been taken at GCSE, it is often required that the student achieved a grade C, 4, or 5 as a minimum at GCSE.\n</p><p>Most universities, in addition to their post-16 requirements, seek that their candidates have grades of C or 4 or higher in GCSE English and mathematics. Many of those who achieve below this standard will later retake GCSE English and mathematics to improve their grade. The November examination series exists for this purpose, to allow a faster path to gain these grades than waiting until the following summer\'s main series. Leading universities often take into account performance at GCSE level, sometimes expecting applicants to have a high proportion of A and A* grades.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_other_qualifications">Comparison with other qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Comparison with other qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Within_the_UK">Within the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Within the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>GCSEs in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland are part of the <a href="/wiki/Regulated_Qualifications_Framework" class="mw-redirect" title="Regulated Qualifications Framework">Regulated Qualifications Framework</a>. A GCSE at grades G, F, E, D, 1, 2, or 3 is a Level 1 qualification. A GCSE at C, B, A, A*, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9 is a Level 2 qualification. Qualifications are not awarded to grades U, X or Q. Level 2 qualifications are much more sought-after, and generally form minimum requirements for jobs and further study expectations.\n</p><p>The BTEC is another Level 1/2 qualification available in the same territories as the GCSE, and is graded at 5 levels. At Level 2, comparable to A*, A, B, and C respectively are the Distinction*, Distinction, Merit, and Pass. A BTEC at Level 1 is simply marked as "Level 1", with no subdivision. Below that level, a U is awarded, as in GCSEs.\n</p><p>Other qualifications at this level include Cambridge Nationals, Key Skills, and Functional Skills.\n</p><p>The comparable qualifications in Scotland are the National 4 and National 5 awards (formerly Standard Grades and/or Intermediates).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_territories">In other territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: In other territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Current and former British territories</b>:\n</p><p>The education systems of current and former British territories, such as <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and Nigeria, also offer the qualification, as supplied by the same examination boards. Other former British colonies, such as <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, continue to use the <a href="/wiki/O-level" class="mw-redirect" title="O-level">O-Level</a> qualification.<sup id="cite_ref-SEAB_O-Level_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEAB_O-Level-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The international version of the GCSE is the <a href="/wiki/IGCSE" class="mw-redirect" title="IGCSE">IGCSE</a>, which can be taken anywhere in the world and includes additional options relating to coursework and the language the qualification is pursued in. All subjects completed in the fifth of the <a href="/wiki/European_Baccalaureate" title="European Baccalaureate">European Baccalaureate</a> are generally equivalent to the GCSEs subjects.\n</p><p><b>Ireland</b>:\n</p><p>In the Republic of Ireland, the Junior Certificate is a comparable qualification.\n</p><p><b>United States</b>\n</p><p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/High_school_diploma" title="High school diploma">high school diploma</a> is required for entry to college. In the UK, this is considered to be at the level of the GCSE, awarded at Year 11.<sup id="cite_ref-UCASIntQuals_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCASIntQuals-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ChavegnesUSInfo_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChavegnesUSInfo-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form" title="Sixth form">college</a> and <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> admissions, the high school diploma may be accepted in lieu of the GCSE if an average grade better than D+ is obtained in subjects with a GCSE counterpart.<sup id="cite_ref-UCASIntQuals_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCASIntQuals-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As A-Levels are generally expected for university admission, the high school diploma is not considered enough for university entry in the UK. <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Placement" title="Advanced Placement">Advanced Placement</a> programmes or <a href="/wiki/International_Baccalaureate" title="International Baccalaureate">International Baccalaureate</a> are considered equal to the A-Level, earn points on the UCAS tariff, and may therefore be accepted in lieu of A-Levels for university entry in the UK by US students. The <a href="/wiki/SAT_Reasoning_Test" class="mw-redirect" title="SAT Reasoning Test">SAT Reasoning Test</a> and <a href="/wiki/SAT_Subject_Test" class="mw-redirect" title="SAT Subject Test">SAT Subject Tests</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/ACT_(test)" title="ACT (test)">ACT</a> may also be considered in an offer for university entry.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_controversy">Criticism and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Criticism and controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grade_disparity">Grade disparity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Grade disparity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Statistics released by London\xe2\x80\x99s Poverty Profile found overall GCSE attainment in London to be greater than the rest of England. 39% of pupils in Inner London and 37% in Outer London did not get five GCSEs at A* to C, compared with 42% in the rest of England.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Also, according to an ITV News report, UK students tend to outperform Jersey students on GCSE examinations.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gender bias is another area of concern. Department of Education data shows that the relative performance gap between girls and boys widened significantly under GCSEs, compared with O-Levels.<sup id="cite_ref-National_Archives_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Archives-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subject_decline">Subject decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Subject decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The declining number of pupils studying foreign languages in the UK has been a major concern of educational experts for many years. Paul Steer, the Exam Board Chief of the British exam board <a href="/wiki/Oxford,_Cambridge_and_RSA_Examinations" title="Oxford, Cambridge and RSA Examinations">OCR</a> recently expressed that "unless we act soon, even GCSE French and German could face the chop".<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Moreover, the publication of "soft" subjects (e.g. Critical Thinking, General Studies etc.) and "academic" subjects (e.g. Mathematics, Sciences, Languages) for GCSEs and A-Levels by the universities of Oxford and Cambridge has created an ongoing educational debate where, on the one hand, many educational experts would support this "division of importance" whereas, on the other hand, many head teachers would not only disagree but actually "oppose a move to solely traditional academic GCSE (and A-Level) subjects".<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grade_inflation">Grade inflation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Grade inflation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the GCSE system was introduced, there were comments that it was a <a href="/wiki/Dumbing_down" title="Dumbing down">dumbing down</a> from the previous <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_Level" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordinary Level">GCE O-Level</a> system (as it took the focus away from the theoretical side of many subjects, and taught pupils about real-world implications and issues relating to <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">ICT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition, the proportions of candidates awarded high grades at GCSE have been rising for many years, which critics attribute to <a href="/wiki/Grade_inflation" title="Grade inflation">grade inflation</a>. By comparing pupils\' scores in the YELLIS ability test with their GCSE results over a period of about 20 years, Robert Coe found a general improvement in grades awarded which ranges from 0.2 (science) to 0.8 (maths) of a GCSE grade.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Only slightly more than half of pupils sitting GCSE exams achieve the 5 A* to C grades required for most forms of academic further education.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the important differences between previous educational qualifications (and the earlier grading of A-Levels) and the later GCSE qualifications was supposed to be a move from norm-referenced marking to criterion-referenced marking.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In a norm-referenced grading system, fixed percentages of candidates achieve each grade. With criterion-referenced grades, in theory, all candidates who achieve the criteria can achieve the grade. A comparison of a clearly norm-referenced assessment, such as the NFER Cognitive Ability Test or CAT, with GCSE grading seems to show an unexpected correlation, which challenges the idea that the GCSE is a properly criterion-based assessment.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The incorporation of GCSE awards into school league tables, and the setting of targets at school level at above national average levels of attainment, has been criticised. At the time of introduction, the <b>E</b> grade was intended to be equivalent to the CSE grade 4, and so obtainable by a candidate of average/median ability.<sup id="cite_ref-times3_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times3-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Sir Keith Joseph set schools a target of 90% of their pupils obtaining at least a grade F (which was the "average" grade achieved in the past). This target was reached nationally about 20 years later. David Blunkett went further and set schools the goal of 50% of 16-year-olds gaining 5 GCSEs or equivalent at grade C and above, although these grades were previously only obtained by the top 30%. This was achieved with the help of equivalent and largely vocational qualifications.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Labelling schools as failing if 40% of their pupils do not achieve at least 5 Cs, including English and Maths at GCSE, has also been criticised, as it essentially requires 40% of each intake to achieve the grades only obtained by the top 20% when GCSE was introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In recent years, concerns about standards has led some <a href="/wiki/Public_school_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Public school (UK)">public schools</a> to complement GCSEs with IGCSEs within their curriculum, and to take their pupils straight<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2011)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Level_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Level (UK)">A-Level</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Business_and_Technology_Education_Council" title="Business and Technology Education Council">BTEC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Other schools, such as <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Grammar_School" title="Manchester Grammar School">Manchester Grammar School</a>, are replacing the GCSEs with <a href="/wiki/IGCSE" class="mw-redirect" title="IGCSE">IGCSEs</a> in which there is an option to do no coursework.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The new science syllabus has led to many public schools switching to the IGCSE Double Award syllabus.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Errors_and_mistakes">Errors and mistakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Errors and mistakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Teachers and pupils have the option to question exam results and have the scripts marked again if they feel the grade awarded does not reflect the pupil\'s ability and expectations; or if they review a copy of the script and notice a marking error. In recent years, there have been complaints that GCSEs and GCE A-Levels were marked unfairly. (In 2012, for the first time in the history of the exams, the proportion of all GCSEs awarded an A*-C grade fell.)<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In one incident, there were complaints about a question in a GCSE maths exam, which became known as the "Hannah\'s sweets" question. This question was said to be very difficult, and this was reported on several media websites. However several teachers, experts, and students posted the solution to the question on the media.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In another case, concerning the 2016 GCSE biology exam, there were complaints about the apparent lack of biology content in the exam.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The May 2017 English literature exam (under the regulation of OCR) wrongly implied that <a href="/wiki/Tybalt" title="Tybalt">Tybalt</a>, a character in <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i> was not a <a href="/wiki/Capulet" class="mw-redirect" title="Capulet">Capulet</a>. This serious flaw in the question confused many of the students. OCR accepted responsibility and claimed no pupil would be disadvantaged. The question was worth 40 marks.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/International_General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="International General Certificate of Secondary Education">International General Certificate of Secondary Education</a> (IGCSE), which is offered internationally as well as in some schools in the UK</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/GCE_Advanced_Level" title="GCE Advanced Level">GCE Advanced Level</a>; commonly referred to as "A-Levels", a set of exams that many pupils take after completing GCSEs that are more academically rigorous</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Business_and_Technology_Education_Council" title="Business and Technology Education Council">Business and Technology Education Council</a>; referred to as "BTECs", another set of exams many pupils take after completing GCSEs, often in vocational subjects</li>\n<li>Predecessor qualifications to the GCSE:\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/GCE_Ordinary_Level_(United_Kingdom)" title="GCE Ordinary Level (United Kingdom)">GCE Ordinary Level</a> (O-Levels)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="Certificate of Secondary Education">Certificate of Secondary Education</a> (CSE)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/School_Certificate_(United_Kingdom)" title="School Certificate (United Kingdom)">School Certificate</a></li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/General_Certificate_of_Education" title="General Certificate of Education">General Certificate of Education</a> (GCE), which comprises O-Levels and A-Levels</li></ul>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Brooks, Ron (2014) [First published 1991].
