style="display:none"><th colspan="2">\n</th></tr><tr style="display:none"><th colspan="2">\n</th></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>New Forest</b> is one of the largest remaining tracts of unenclosed <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> land, <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath (habitat)">heathland</a> and forest in the south-east of England, covering southwest <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> and southeast <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>.\n</p><p>It was proclaimed a royal forest by <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>, featuring in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a>. Pre-existing rights of common pasture are still recognised today, being enforced by official <a href="/wiki/Verderer" title="Verderer">verderers</a>. In the 18th century, The New Forest became a source of timber for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. It remains a habitat for many rare birds and mammals.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Like much of England, the site of the New Forest was once <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> woodland, recolonised by <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> and eventually <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> after the withdrawal of the ice sheets starting around 12,000 years ago. Some areas were cleared for cultivation from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> onwards; the poor quality of the soil in the New Forest meant that the cleared areas turned into heathland "waste", which may have been used even then as grazing land for horses.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was still a significant amount of woodland in this part of Britain, but this was gradually reduced, particularly towards the end of the Middle <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> around 250\xe2\x80\x93100 BC, and most importantly the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">12th and 13th centuries</a>, and of this essentially all that remains today is the New Forest.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Forest_Handbook_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Forest_Handbook-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are around 250 <a href="/wiki/Round_barrow" title="Round barrow">round barrows</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> within its boundaries, and scattered <a href="/wiki/Boiling_mound" class="mw-redirect" title="Boiling mound">boiling mounds</a>, and it also includes about 150 <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_ancient_monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled ancient monument">scheduled ancient monuments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> One such barrow in particular may represent the only known <a href="/wiki/Inhumation" class="mw-redirect" title="Inhumation">inhumation</a> burial of the Early Iron Age and the only known <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> burial in Britain; however, the acidity of the soil means that bone very rarely survives.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg/300px-Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg" width="300" height="378" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg/450px-Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg/600px-Royal.Forests.1327.1336.selected.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2324" data-file-height="2931" /></a></div>\n<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement">Anglo-Saxon settlement</a> in Britain, according to <a href="/wiki/Florence_of_Worcester" title="Florence of Worcester">Florence of Worcester</a> (d. 1118), the area became the site of the <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutish</a> kingdom of <i>Ytene</i>; this name was the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_plural" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive plural">genitive plural</a> of <i>Yt</i> meaning "Jute", i.e. "of the Jutes".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutes</a> were one of the early <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> tribal groups who colonised this area of southern Hampshire. The word ytene (or <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/ettin" class="extiw" title="wikt:Special:Search/ettin">ettin</a>) is also found locally as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Giant (mythology)">giant</a>, and features heavily in local folklore.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-heslop_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heslop-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>, the New Forest was proclaimed a <a href="/wiki/Royal_forest" title="Royal forest">royal forest</a>, in about 1079, by <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>. It was used for royal hunts, mainly of <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It was created at the expense of more than 20 small <a href="/wiki/Hamlet_(place)" title="Hamlet (place)">hamlets</a> and isolated <a href="/wiki/Farmstead" class="mw-redirect" title="Farmstead">farmsteads</a>; hence it was then \'new\' as a single compact area.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The New Forest was first recorded as <i>Nova Foresta</i> in <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> in 1086, where a section devoted to it is interpolated between lands of the king\'s <a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">thegns</a> and the town of <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>; it is the only forest that the book describes in detail. Twelfth-century chroniclers alleged that William had created the forest by evicting the inhabitants of 36 parishes, reducing a flourishing district to a wasteland; however, this account is thought dubious by most <a href="/wiki/Revisionist_historians" class="mw-redirect" title="Revisionist historians">historians</a>, as the poor soil in much of the area is believed to have been incapable of supporting large-scale agriculture, and significant areas appear to have always been uninhabited.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Young7_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young7-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two of William\'s sons died in the forest: <a href="/wiki/Richard,_Duke_of_Bernay" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard, Duke of Bernay">Prince Richard</a> sometime between 1069 and 1075, and <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_England" title="William II of England">King William II (William Rufus)</a> in 1100. Local <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> asserted that this was punishment for the crimes committed by William when he created his New Forest; 17th-century writer Richard Blome provides exquisite detail:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>In this County [Hantshire] is New-Forest, formerly called Ytene, being about 30 miles in compass; in which said tract William the Conqueror (for the making of the said Forest a harbour for Wild-beasts for his Game) caused 36 Parish Churches, with all the Houses thereto belonging, to be pulled down, and the poor Inhabitants left succourless of house or home. But this wicked act did not long go unpunished, for his Sons felt the smart thereof; Richard being blasted with a pestilent Air; Rufus shot through with an Arrow; and Henry his Grand-child, by Robert his eldest son, as he pursued his Game, was hanged among the boughs, and so dyed. This Forest at present affordeth great variety of Game, where his Majesty oft-times withdraws himself for his divertisement.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> </p></blockquote>\n<p>The reputed spot of Rufus\'s death is marked with a stone known as the <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Rufus Stone">Rufus Stone</a>.\n<a href="/wiki/John_White_(bishop)" title="John White (bishop)">John White</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Winchester" title="Bishop of Winchester">Bishop of Winchester</a>, said of the forest:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>From God and Saint King Rufus did Churches take, From Citizens town-court, and mercate place, From Farmer lands: New Forrest for to make, In Beaulew tract, where whiles the King in chase Pursues the hart, just vengeance comes apace, And King pursues. Tirrell him seing not, Unwares him flew with dint of arrow shot.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> </p></blockquote>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common</a> rights were confirmed by statute in 1698. The New Forest became a source of timber for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, and plantations were created in the 18th century for this purpose. In the <a href="/wiki/Great_Storm_of_1703" title="Great Storm of 1703">Great Storm of 1703</a>, about 4000 oak trees were lost.\n</p><p>The naval plantations encroached on the rights of the Commoners, but the Forest gained new protection under the New Forest Act 1877, which confirmed the historic rights of the Commoners and entrenched that the total of enclosures was henceforth not to exceed 65&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (25&#160;sq&#160;mi) at any time. It also reconstituted the Court of Verderers as representatives of the Commoners (rather than the Crown).\n</p><p>As of  2005<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, roughly 90% of the New Forest is still owned by the Crown. The Crown lands have been managed by the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> since 1923 and most of the Crown lands now fall inside the new National Park.\n</p><p>Felling of broadleaved trees, and their replacement by <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifers</a>, began during the First World War to meet the wartime demand for wood. Further encroachments were made during the Second World War. This process is today being reversed in places, with some plantations being returned to heathland or broadleaved woodland. <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron_ponticum" title="Rhododendron ponticum">Rhododendron</a> remains a problem.\n</p><p>During the Second World War, an area of the forest, <a href="/wiki/Ashley_Range" title="Ashley Range">Ashley Range</a>, was used as a bombing range.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Further New Forest Acts followed in 1949, 1964 and 1970. The New Forest became a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> in 1971, and was granted special status as the New Forest Heritage Area in 1985, with additional planning controls added in 1992. The New Forest was proposed as a UNESCO <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in June 1999,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and it became a National Park in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Common rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Forest_Pony_England.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Forest_Pony_England.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/New_Forest_pony" title="New Forest pony">New Forest pony</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cow_eating_straw_new_forest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Cow_eating_straw_new_forest.jpg/220px-Cow_eating_straw_new_forest.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cow_eating_straw_new_forest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cow eating winter feed, Longdown Inclosure</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Royal_forest#Forest_law" title="Royal forest">Forest laws</a> were enacted to preserve the New Forest as a location for royal <a href="/wiki/Deer_hunting" title="Deer hunting">deer hunting</a>, and interference with the king\'s deer and its forage was punished. But the inhabitants of the area (<i><a href="/wiki/Commons" title="Commons">Commoners</a></i>) had pre-existing <i>rights of common</i>: to turn horses and cattle (but only rarely sheep) out into the Forest to graze (<i>common pasture</i>), to gather fuel wood (<i><a href="/wiki/Estovers" title="Estovers">estovers</a></i>), to cut peat for fuel (<i><a href="/wiki/Turbary" title="Turbary">turbary</a></i>), to dig clay (<i><a href="/wiki/Marl" title="Marl">marl</a></i>), and to turn out pigs between September and November to eat fallen <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorns</a> and beechnuts (<i><a href="/wiki/Pannage" title="Pannage">pannage</a></i> or <i>mast</i>). There were also licences granted to gather <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a> after <a href="/wiki/Michaelmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Michaelmas Day">Michaelmas Day</a> (29 September) as litter for animals (<i>fern</i>).\n</p><p>Along with grazing, pannage is still an important part of the Forest\'s ecology. Pigs can eat acorns without a problem, but for ponies and cattle large quantities of acorns can be poisonous. Pannage always lasts 60 days, but the start date varies according to the weather \xe2\x80\x93 and when the acorns fall. The <a href="/wiki/Verderer" title="Verderer">Verderers</a> decide when pannage will start each year. At other times the pigs must be taken in and kept on the owner\'s land, with the exception that pregnant sows, known as <i>privileged sows</i>, are always allowed out providing they are not a nuisance and return to the Commoner\'s holding at night (they must not be "<i>levant</i> and <i>couchant</i>" in the Forest, that is, they may not consecutively feed <i>and</i> sleep there). This last is an established practice rather than a formal right.\n</p><p>The principle of levancy and couchancy applies generally to the right of pasture.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Commoners must have backup land, outside the Forest, to accommodate these depastured animals when necessary, for example during a <a href="/wiki/Foot-and-mouth_disease" title="Foot-and-mouth disease">foot-and-mouth disease</a> epidemic.\n</p><p>Commons rights are attached to particular plots of land (or in the case of turbary, to particular <a href="/wiki/Hearth" title="Hearth">hearths</a>), and different land has different rights \xe2\x80\x93 and some of this land is some distance from the Forest itself. Rights to graze ponies and cattle are not for a fixed number of animals, as is often the case on other commons. Instead a "marking fee" is paid for each animal each year by the owner. The marked animal\'s tail is trimmed by the local <i><a href="/wiki/Agister" class="mw-redirect" title="Agister">agister</a></i> (Verderers\' official), with each of the four or five forest agisters using a different trimming pattern. Ponies are branded with the owner\'s brand mark; cattle may be branded, or nowadays may have the brand mark on an ear tag. Grazing of Commoners\' ponies and cattle is an essential part of the management of the forest, helping to maintain the heathland, bog, grassland and wood-pasture habitats and their associated wildlife.\n</p><p>Recently this ancient practice has come under pressure as benefitting houses pass to owners with no interest in commmoning. Existing families with a new generation heavily rely on inheritance of, rather than mostly the expensive purchase of, a benefitting house with paddock or farm.\n</p><p>The Verderers and Commoners\' Defence Association has fought back these allied economic threats. The EU <a href="/wiki/Single_Payment_Scheme" title="Single Payment Scheme">Single Payment Scheme</a> helped some Commoners significantly. Commoners grazing animals can claim up to \xc2\xa3850 per cow per year, and \xc2\xa3900 for a pair of ponies. If registered for and participating in the stewardship scheme, greater. With 10 cattle and 40 ponies, a Commoner qualifying for both schemes would receive over \xc2\xa330,000 a year and more if they put out pigs: net of marking fees, feed and veterinary costs this part-time level of involvement across a family is calculated to give annual income in the thousands of pounds in most years. Added to this a small dairy farming income would accrue.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_New_Forest" title="Geology of the New Forest">Geology of the New Forest</a></i></dd></dl>\n<div /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alder_trees_beaulieu_river_fawley_ford.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Alder trees by the <a href="/wiki/Beaulieu_river" class="mw-redirect" title="Beaulieu river">Beaulieu river</a> at Fawley Ford, north of Beaulieu</div></div></div>\n<p>The New Forest National Park area covers 566&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (219&#160;sq&#160;mi),<sup id="cite_ref-ParkArea_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ParkArea-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and the New Forest <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">SSSI</a> covers almost 300&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (120&#160;sq&#160;mi), making it the largest contiguous area of unsown vegetation in lowland Britain. It includes roughly:\n</p>\n<ul><li>146&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (56&#160;sq&#160;mi) of broadleaved woodland</li>\n<li>118&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (46&#160;sq&#160;mi) of heathland and grassland</li>\n<li>33&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (13&#160;sq&#160;mi) of wet heathland</li>\n<li>84&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (32&#160;sq&#160;mi) of tree plantations (<i>woodland inclosures</i>) established since the 18th century, including 80&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (31&#160;sq&#160;mi) planted by the Forestry Commission since the 1920s.</li></ul>\n<p>The New Forest has also been classed as <a href="/wiki/National_Character_Area" title="National Character Area">National Character Area</a> No. 131 by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>. The NCA covers an area of 738&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (285&#160;sq&#160;mi) and is bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Dorset_Heaths" title="Dorset Heaths">Dorset Heaths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorset_Downs" title="Dorset Downs">Dorset Downs</a> to the west, the <a href="/wiki/West_Wiltshire_Downs" title="West Wiltshire Downs">West Wiltshire Downs</a> to the north and the <a href="/wiki/South_Hampshire_Lowlands" title="South Hampshire Lowlands">South Hampshire Lowlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Coast_Plain" title="South Coast Plain">South Coast Plain</a> to the east.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The New Forest is drained to the south by three rivers, <a href="/wiki/Lymington_River" title="Lymington River">Lymington River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beaulieu_River" title="Beaulieu River">Beaulieu River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avon_Water_(Hampshire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Avon Water (Hampshire)">Avon Water</a>, and to the west by the <a href="/wiki/Latchmore_Brook" title="Latchmore Brook">Latchmore Brook</a>, Dockens Water, Linford Brook and other streams.\n</p><p>The highest point in the New Forest is Pipers Wait, near <a href="/wiki/Nomansland,_Wiltshire" title="Nomansland, Wiltshire">Nomansland</a>. Its summit is 129 metres (423 feet) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_New_Forest" title="Geology of the New Forest">Geology of the New Forest</a> consists mainly of sedimentary rock, in the centre of a sedimentary basin known as the Hampshire Basin.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife">Wildlife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wildlife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The ecological value of the New Forest is enhanced by the relatively large areas of lowland habitats, lost elsewhere, which have survived. There are several kinds of important lowland habitat including <a href="/wiki/Valley_bog" class="mw-redirect" title="Valley bog">valley bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alder_carr" class="mw-redirect" title="Alder carr">alder carr</a>, wet <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heaths</a>, dry heaths and deciduous <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>. The area contains a profusion of rare wildlife, including the New Forest <a href="/wiki/Cicada" title="Cicada">cicada</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Cicadetta_montana" title="Cicadetta montana">Cicadetta montana</a></i>, the only cicada native to Great Britain, although the last unconfirmed sighting was in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The wet heaths are important for rare plants, such as marsh gentian (<i><a href="/wiki/Gentiana_pneumonanthe" title="Gentiana pneumonanthe">Gentiana pneumonanthe</a></i>) and marsh clubmoss (<i><a href="/wiki/Lycopodiella_inundata" title="Lycopodiella inundata">Lycopodiella inundata</a></i>) and other important species include the wild gladiolus (<i>Gladiolus illyricus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several species of <a href="/wiki/Sundew" class="mw-redirect" title="Sundew">sundew</a> are found, as well as many unusual insect species, including the southern damselfly (<i><a href="/wiki/Coenagrion_mercuriale" title="Coenagrion mercuriale">Coenagrion mercuriale</a></i>) and the mole cricket (<i><a href="/wiki/Gryllotalpa_gryllotalpa" title="Gryllotalpa gryllotalpa">Gryllotalpa gryllotalpa</a></i>), both rare in Britain. In 2009, 500 adult southern damselflies were captured and released in the Venn Ottery nature reserve in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, which is owned and managed by the <a href="/wiki/Devon_Wildlife_Trust" title="Devon Wildlife Trust">Devon Wildlife Trust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The Forest is an important stronghold for a rich variety of <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>, and although these have been heavily gathered in the past, there are control measures now in place to manage this.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birds">Birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Specialist heathland birds are widespread, including <a href="/wiki/Dartford_warbler" title="Dartford warbler">Dartford warbler</a> (<i>Silvia undata</i>), <a href="/wiki/Woodlark" title="Woodlark">woodlark</a> (<i>Lullula arborea</i>), <a href="/wiki/Northern_lapwing" title="Northern lapwing">northern lapwing</a> (<i>Vanellus vanellus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_curlew" title="Eurasian curlew">Eurasian curlew</a> (<i>Numenius arquata</i>), <a href="/wiki/European_nightjar" title="European nightjar">European nightjar</a> (<i>Caprimulgus europaeus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_hobby" title="Eurasian hobby">Eurasian hobby</a> (<i>Falco subbuteo</i>), <a href="/wiki/European_stonechat" title="European stonechat">European stonechat</a> (<i>Saxicola rubecola</i>), <a href="/wiki/Common_redstart" title="Common redstart">common redstart</a> (<i>Phoenicurus phoenicurus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Tree_pipit" title="Tree pipit">tree pipit</a> (<i>Anthus sylvestris</i>). As in much of Britain <a href="/wiki/Common_snipe" title="Common snipe">common snipe</a> (<i>Gallinago gallinago</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Meadow_pipit" title="Meadow pipit">meadow pipit</a> (<i>Anthus trivialis</i>) are common as wintering birds, but in the Forest they still also breed in many of the bogs and heaths respectively.\n</p><p>Woodland birds include <a href="/wiki/Wood_warbler" title="Wood warbler">wood warbler</a> (<i>Phylloscopus sibilatrix</i>), <a href="/wiki/Stock_dove" title="Stock dove">stock dove</a> (<i>Columba oenas</i>), <a href="/wiki/European_honey_buzzard" title="European honey buzzard">European honey buzzard</a> (<i>Pernis apivorus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawk</a> (<i>Accipiter gentilis</i>). <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">Common buzzard</a> (<i>Buteo buteo</i>) is very common and <a href="/wiki/Common_raven" title="Common raven">common raven</a> (<i>Corvus corax</i>) is spreading. Birds seen more rarely include <a href="/wiki/Red_kite" title="Red kite">red kite</a> (<i>Milvus milvus</i>), wintering <a href="/wiki/Great_grey_shrike" title="Great grey shrike">great grey shrike</a> (<i>Lanius exubitor</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harrier</a> (<i>Circus cyaneus</i>) and migrating <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzel</a> (<i>Turdus torquatus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a> (<i>Oenanthe oenanthe</i>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reptiles_and_amphibians">Reptiles and amphibians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reptiles and amphibians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>All three British native species of snake inhabit the Forest. The adder (<i><a href="/wiki/Vipera_berus" title="Vipera berus">Vipera berus</a></i>) is the most common, being found on open heath and grassland. The <a href="/wiki/Grass_snake" title="Grass snake">grass snake</a> (<i>Natrix natrix</i>) prefers the damper environment of the valley mires. The rare smooth snake (<i><a href="/wiki/Coronella_austriaca" class="mw-redirect" title="Coronella austriaca">Coronella austriaca</a></i>) occurs on sandy hillsides with <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">heather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gorse" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorse">gorse</a>. It was mainly adders which were caught by <a href="/wiki/Brusher_Mills" title="Brusher Mills">Brusher Mills</a> (1840\xe2\x80\x931905), the "New Forest Snake Catcher". He caught many thousands in his lifetime, sending some to <a href="/wiki/London_Zoo" title="London Zoo">London Zoo</a> as food for their animals.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  A <a href="/wiki/Public_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Public House">pub</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brockenhurst" title="Brockenhurst">Brockenhurst</a> is named <i>The Snakecatcher</i> in his memory. All British snakes are now <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_and_Countryside_Act_1981" title="Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981">legally protected</a>, and so the New Forest snakes are no longer caught.\n</p><p>A programme to reintroduce the <a href="/wiki/Sand_lizard" title="Sand lizard">sand lizard</a> (<i>Lacerta agilis</i>) started in 1989<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_crested_newt" class="mw-redirect" title="Great crested newt">great crested newt</a> (<i>Triturus cristatus</i>) already breeds in many locations.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Ponies.2C_cattle.2C_pigs"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ponies,_cattle,_pigs">Ponies, cattle, pigs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ponies, cattle, pigs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ShetlandPony.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/ShetlandPony.jpg/220px-ShetlandPony.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/ShetlandPony.jpg/330px-ShetlandPony.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/ShetlandPony.jpg/440px-ShetlandPony.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ShetlandPony.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shetland pony with foal in New Forest District, Hampshire</div></div></div><p> Commoners\' cattle, ponies and <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">donkeys</a> roam throughout the open heath and much of the woodland, and it is largely their grazing that maintains the open character of the Forest. They are also frequently seen in the Forest villages, where home and shop owners must take care to keep them out of gardens and shops. The <a href="/wiki/New_Forest_pony" title="New Forest pony">New Forest pony</a> is one of the indigenous horse breeds of the British Isles, and is one of the New Forest\'s most famous attractions \xe2\x80\x93 most of the Forest ponies are of this breed, but there are also some <a href="/wiki/Shetland_pony" title="Shetland pony">Shetlands</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Crossbreed" title="Crossbreed">crossbreeds</a>.\n</p><p>Cattle are of various breeds, most commonly <a href="/wiki/Galloway_(cattle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Galloway (cattle)">Galloways</a> and their crossbreeds, but also various other hardy types such as <a href="/wiki/Highland_cattle" title="Highland cattle">Highlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hereford_(cattle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hereford (cattle)">Herefords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dexter_cattle" title="Dexter cattle">Dexters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerry_cattle" title="Kerry cattle">Kerries</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_White_cattle" title="British White cattle">British whites</a>. The pigs used for <a href="/wiki/Pannage" title="Pannage">pannage</a> are now of various breeds, but the New Forest was the original home of the <a href="/wiki/Wessex_Saddleback" title="Wessex Saddleback">Wessex Saddleback</a>, now extinct in Britain.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deer">Deer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Deer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Numerous deer live in the Forest; they are usually rather shy and tend to stay out of sight when people are around, but are surprisingly bold at night, even when a car drives past. <a href="/wiki/Fallow_deer" title="Fallow deer">Fallow deer</a> (<i>Dama dama</i>) are the most common, followed by <a href="/wiki/Roe_deer" title="Roe deer">roe deer</a> (<i>Capreolus capreolus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> (<i>Cervus elephas</i>). There are also smaller populations of the introduced <a href="/wiki/Sika_deer" title="Sika deer">sika deer</a> (<i>Cervus nippon</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Reeves%27s_muntjac" title="Reeves&#39;s muntjac">muntjac</a> (<i>Muntiacus reevesii</i>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_mammals">Other mammals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other mammals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Red_squirrel" title="Red squirrel">red squirrel</a> (<i>Sciurus vulgaris</i>) survived in the Forest until the 1970s \xe2\x80\x93 longer than most places in lowland Britain (though it still occurs on The Isle of Wight and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Brownsea_Island" title="Brownsea Island">Brownsea Island</a>). It is now fully supplanted in the Forest by the introduced North American <a href="/wiki/Eastern_gray_squirrel" title="Eastern gray squirrel">grey squirrel</a> (<i>Sciurus carolinensis</i>). The <a href="/wiki/European_polecat" title="European polecat">European polecat</a> (<i>Mustela putorius</i>) has recolonised the western edge of the Forest in recent years. <a href="/wiki/European_otter" class="mw-redirect" title="European otter">European otter</a> (<i>Lutra lutra</i>) occurs along watercourses, as well as the introduced <a href="/wiki/American_mink" title="American mink">American mink</a> (<i>Neovison vison</i>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_measures">Conservation measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Conservation measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The New Forest is designated as a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI), an EU <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> (SAC),<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Special_Protection_Area" title="Special Protection Area">Special Protection Area</a> for birds (SPA),<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Ramsar_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="Ramsar Site">Ramsar Site</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> it also has its own <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plan</a> (BAP).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlements">Settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg/220px-New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg/330px-New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg/440px-New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Forest_ponies_in_Lyndhurst.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ponies walking the streets in <a href="/wiki/Burley,_Hampshire" title="Burley, Hampshire">Burley</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The New Forest itself gives its name to the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest_(district)" class="mw-redirect" title="New Forest (district)">New Forest district</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, and the National Park area, of which it forms the core.\n</p><p>The Forest itself is dominated by three large villages, <a href="/wiki/Lyndhurst,_Hampshire" title="Lyndhurst, Hampshire">Lyndhurst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brockenhurst" title="Brockenhurst">Brockenhurst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burley,_Hampshire" title="Burley, Hampshire">Burley</a>, with several smaller villages such as <a href="/wiki/Beaulieu,_Hampshire" title="Beaulieu, Hampshire">Beaulieu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Godshill,_Hampshire" title="Godshill, Hampshire">Godshill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blissford" title="Blissford">Blissford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fritham" title="Fritham">Fritham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nomansland,_Wiltshire" title="Nomansland, Wiltshire">Nomansland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minstead" title="Minstead">Minstead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sway,_Hampshire" title="Sway, Hampshire">Sway</a> also lying within or immediately adjacent. Outside of the National Park in New Forest District, several clusters of larger towns frame the area - <a href="/wiki/Totton" class="mw-redirect" title="Totton">Totton</a> and the Waterside settlements (<a href="/wiki/Marchwood" title="Marchwood">Marchwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dibden" title="Dibden">Dibden</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Hythe,_Hampshire" title="Hythe, Hampshire">Hythe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fawley,_Hampshire" title="Fawley, Hampshire">Fawley</a>) to the East, <a href="/wiki/Christchurch,_Dorset" title="Christchurch, Dorset">Christchurch</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Milton" title="New Milton">New Milton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milford_on_Sea" title="Milford on Sea">Milford on Sea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lymington" title="Lymington">Lymington</a> to the South, and <a href="/wiki/Fordingbridge" title="Fordingbridge">Fordingbridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ringwood" title="Ringwood">Ringwood</a> to the West.\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_locations_in_the_New_Forest" title="List of locations in the New Forest">List of locations in the New Forest</a></div>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Forest_National_Park">New Forest National Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: New Forest National Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg/250px-New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg.png" width="250" height="247" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg/375px-New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg/500px-New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1244" data-file-height="1227" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Forest_National_Park_map.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Location of the National Park</div></div></div>\n<p>Consultations on the possible designation of a <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">National Park</a> in the New Forest were commenced by the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_Agency" title="Countryside Agency">Countryside Agency</a> in 1999. An order to create the park was made by the Agency on 24 January 2002 and submitted to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Secretary of State</a> for confirmation in February 2002. Following objections from seven local authorities and others, a <a href="/wiki/Public_inquiry" title="Public inquiry">public inquiry</a> was held from 8 October 2002 to 10 April 2003, and concluded by endorsing the proposal with some detailed changes to the boundary of the area to be designated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="seems like it needs citing (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On 28 June 2004, Rural Affairs Minister <a href="/wiki/Alun_Michael" title="Alun Michael">Alun Michael</a> confirmed the government\'s intention to designate the area as a National Park, with further detailed boundary adjustments. The area was formally designated as such on 1 March 2005. A <a href="/wiki/National_park_authority" title="National park authority">national park authority</a> for the New Forest was established on 1 April 2005 and assumed its full statutory powers on 1 April 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> retain their powers to manage the Crown land within the Park. The Verderers under the New Forest Acts also retain their responsibilities, and the park authority is expected to co-operate with these bodies, the local authorities, <a href="/wiki/English_Nature" title="English Nature">English Nature</a> and other interested parties.\nThe designated area of the National Park covers 566&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (219&#160;sq&#160;mi)<sup id="cite_ref-ParkArea_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ParkArea-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and includes many existing <a href="/wiki/SSSI" class="mw-redirect" title="SSSI">SSSIs</a>. It has a population of about 38,000 (it excludes most of the 170,256 people who live in the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest_(district)" class="mw-redirect" title="New Forest (district)">New Forest</a> local government district). As well as most of the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest_(district)" class="mw-redirect" title="New Forest (district)">New Forest district</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, it takes in the <a href="/wiki/South_Hampshire_Coast" title="South Hampshire Coast">South Hampshire Coast</a> <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>, a small corner of <a href="/wiki/Test_Valley" title="Test Valley">Test Valley</a> district around the village of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canada,_Hampshire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Canada, Hampshire (page does not exist)">Canada</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> south-east of <a href="/wiki/Redlynch,_Wiltshire" title="Redlynch, Wiltshire">Redlynch</a>.\n</p><p>However, the area covered by the Park does not include all the areas initially proposed: it excludes most of the valley of the <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Hampshire" title="River Avon, Hampshire">River Avon</a> to the west of the Forest and <a href="/wiki/Dibden" title="Dibden">Dibden Bay</a> to the east. Two challenges were made to the designation order, by Meyrick Estate Management Ltd in relation to the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Hinton_Admiral" title="Hinton Admiral">Hinton Admiral Park</a>, and by <a href="/wiki/RWE" title="RWE">RWE</a> <a href="/wiki/Npower_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Npower (UK)">NPower</a> Plc in relation to the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Fawley_Power_Station" title="Fawley Power Station">Fawley Power Station</a>. The second challenge was settled out of court, with the power station being excluded.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> upheld the first challenge;<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> but an appeal against the decision was then heard by the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a> in Autumn 2006. The final ruling, published on 15 February 2007, found in favour of the challenge by Meyrick Estate Management Ltd,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and the land at Hinton Admiral Park is therefore excluded from the New Forest National Park. The total area of land initially proposed for inclusion but ultimately left out of the Park is around 120&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (46&#160;sq&#160;mi).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" fiction">historical fiction</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Forest_(novel)" title="The Forest (novel)">The Forest</a></i> is based in the New Forest in the period from 1099 to 2000. The Forest is also a setting of the <a href="/wiki/Warriors_(novel_series)" title="Warriors (novel series)"><i>Warriors</i></a> novel series, in which the \'Forest Territories\' was initially based on New Forest.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The New Forest and southeast England, around the 12th century, is a prominent setting in <a href="/wiki/Ken_Follett" title="Ken Follett">Ken Follett</a>\'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pillars_of_the_Earth" title="The Pillars of the Earth">The Pillars of the Earth</a></i>. It is also a prominent setting in <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_George" title="Elizabeth George">Elizabeth George</a>\'s novel <i>This Body of Death</i>. Oberon, Titania and the other Shakespearean fairies live in a rapidly diminishing Sherwood Forest whittled away by urban development in the fantasy novel <a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer%27s_Nightmare_(novel)" title="A Midsummer&#39;s Nightmare (novel)">A Midsummer\'s Nightmare</a> by Garry Kilworth.
