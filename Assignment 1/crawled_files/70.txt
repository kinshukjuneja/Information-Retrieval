class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RedRiverCornwallAtGodrevyBeach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Red River at Godrevy beach</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Red River</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Dowr Koner</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a small river in north-west <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> which issues into <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_Bay" title="St Ives Bay">St Ives Bay</a> at <a href="/wiki/Godrevy" title="Godrevy">Godrevy</a> on Cornwall\'s <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Red River is about 8 miles (13&#160;km) long and was given its name from the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> deposits associated with <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Mining in Cornwall">tin mining</a> which formerly coloured its water red. The river\'s gradient is relatively steep; the stream falls 170 metres (560&#160;ft) from source to sea.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Geography"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Geography</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Tin_streaming"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Tin streaming</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Wildlife_and_ecology"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Wildlife and ecology</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_River_(Koner)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Red River rises from springs near <a href="/wiki/Bolenowe" title="Bolenowe">Bolenowe</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Carnmenellis" title="Carnmenellis">Carnmenellis</a> granite <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">batholith</a>, an upland plateau. The river flows north, passing through a gorge in the granite ridge west of <a href="/wiki/Carn_Brea,_Redruth" title="Carn Brea, Redruth">Carn Brea</a>. Beyond the gorge, the river passes <a href="/wiki/Tuckingmill,_Camborne,_Cornwall" title="Tuckingmill, Camborne, Cornwall">Tuckingmill</a>, and Tuckingmill Valley Park, once a centre of mining and associated industries. At the hamlet of Combe, the <a href="/wiki/Tehidy_Country_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Tehidy Country Park">Tehidy</a> stream joins the Red River which then turns west towards Godrevy.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tin_streaming">Tin streaming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_River_(Koner)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tin streaming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Red River\'s catchment area includes the major mining areas of Tuckingmill, <a href="/wiki/Pool,_Cornwall" title="Pool, Cornwall">Pool</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a>. Thus:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The Red River catchment has been subjected to mining and mineral working for many centuries, particularly during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. It has been extensively tin streamed and its water used for mineral processing, both for use in the mineral separation processes and as a source of power. As a result of past mining activities the river has undergone many modifications and for significant parts of its course the river has been diverted, canalised, and, in places, embanked. Very little, if any, of the river can be considered to be in a truly natural condition.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/The_Mining_Journal_(trade_magazine)" title="The Mining Journal (trade magazine)">The Mining Journal</a> (1872), the author, Mr Watson, asked \xe2\x80\xb3if any stream or gully in Australia, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> or <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> had proved to be so continuously rich and productive as the Red River.\xe2\x80\xb3 The river was fed by tributary streams that flowed from the dressing-floors and slime pits of the principal mines, and the \xe2\x80\xb3squatters\xe2\x80\xb3 could earn \xc2\xa320,000 per year working on the slimy river-bed. Mr Watson remarked on the inefficiency of the mines in allowing so much tin to be lost. By 1879, Captain James of South Frances mine, estimated \xc2\xa330,000s worth of tin was lost by the mines.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 25 June 1879 the <a href="/wiki/Lease" title="Lease">lease</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Tenement_(law)" title="Tenement (law)">tenement</a> of Rosewarne Mill was auctioned at Abraham\'s Hotel, Camborne. The sale included the "valuable" tin stream works" including a 12-inch cylinder engine, three waterwheels, stamps and all the minerals found in the mill leat and the main river within certain defined boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Magor Coombe streamworks were bought for \xc2\xa3750 by Captain Rabling in 1879<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Within Tuckingmill Valley Park is a small island containing the remains of a chimney stack, brick scrubber building and collapsed flue. The buildings (circa 1905) are associated with the production of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> which was a valuable resource for Cornish tin and copper mines when production of the metals was declining and the mines were closing. More efficient calcining furnaces were built and the gases fed through convoluted labyrinths where they cooled and condensed on the flue walls. The walls were scraped and the deposits further refined to make 100% pure arsenic. Arsenic was used as a <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Boll_weevil" title="Boll weevil">boll weevil</a> (<i>Anthonomus grandis</i>) in the cotton fields of the United States, as well as in the paint and dye industry producing new colours such as canary yellow and emerald green.<sup id="cite_ref-plan2009_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plan2009-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>High stone-faced banks, are the remains of ten sluices which controlled the flow of water, to enable the recovery of tailings. The sluices were part of the Tolgarrick tin treatment operations which closed in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-plan2009_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plan2009-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the closure of the last mine, <a href="/wiki/South_Crofty" title="South Crofty">South Crofty</a>, in 1998, the Red River has lost its distinctive colour, and natural ecology and biodiversity are being re-established. Tn the 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Environment_Agency" title="Environment Agency">Environment Agency</a> granted permission for the new mine owner to discharge effluent from the mine to prepare for future reopening. <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The river gives its name to the Red River Inn public house (formerly The Pendarves Arms) in nearby <a href="/wiki/Gwithian" title="Gwithian">Gwithian</a>. \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_and_ecology">Wildlife and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_River_(Koner)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wildlife and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Red River is one of the most modified streams in the UK due to centuries of mining which has led to heavy metal contamination and the realignment of the course. The river is gradually re-establishing a more natural look as banks erode and some of the sediment deposited to form bars and small islands.\n</p><p>The river and ponds of the <a href="/wiki/Tuckingmill,_Camborne,_Cornwall#Tuckingmill_Valley_Park" title="Tuckingmill, Camborne, Cornwall">Tuckingmill Valley Park</a> provide valuable habitat within an urban area, for two nationally scarce insects; <a href="/wiki/Scarce_blue-tailed_damselfly" title="Scarce blue-tailed damselfly">scarce blue-tailed damselfly</a>  (<i>Ischnura pumilio</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Small_red_damselfly" title="Small red damselfly">small red damselfly</a> (<i>Ceriagrion tenellum</i>). The larvae of the <a href="/wiki/Golden-ringed_dragonfly" title="Golden-ringed dragonfly">golden-ringed dragonfly</a> (<i>Ceriagrion tenellum</i>) have been found and the adults can be seen through the summer and autumn. Other dragonflies include the <a href="/wiki/Common_darter" title="Common darter">common darter</a>  (<i>Sympetrum striolatum</i>) and the once rare <a href="/wiki/Migrant_hawker" title="Migrant hawker">migrant hawker</a> (<i>Aeshna mixta</i>) which usually fly in August and September.\n</p><p>Both <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frog</a> (<i>Rana temporaria temporaria</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Common_toad" title="Common toad">common toad</a> (<i>Bufo bufo</i>) <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawn</a> are found along the edges of the ponds in the early part of the year and the <a href="/wiki/European_otter" class="mw-redirect" title="European otter">otter</a> (<i>Lutra lutra</i>) has returned following the improvement in water quality. The <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myriophyllum_aquaticum" title="Myriophyllum aquaticum">parrot feather</a> (<i>Myriophyllum aquaticum</i>) is regularly removed by hand to prevent its spread, and the growth of  <a href="/wiki/Fallopia_japonica" title="Fallopia japonica">Japanese knotweed</a> (<i>Fallopia japonica</i>) has been restricted by chemical treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-plan2009_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plan2009-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
