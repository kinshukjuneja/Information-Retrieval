<b>heather</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is the sole species in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Calluna</b></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a> family <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">Ericaceae</a>.  It is a low-growing <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> growing to 20 to 50 centimetres (7.9 to 19.7&#160;in) tall, or rarely to 1 metre (39&#160;in) and taller,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and is found widely in Europe and Asia Minor on <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> soils in open sunny situations and in moderate shade. It is the dominant plant in most <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath (habitat)">heathland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a> in Europe, and in some <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a> vegetation and acidic <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> woodland.  It is tolerant of grazing and regenerates following occasional burning, and is often managed in nature reserves and grouse <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moors</a> by sheep or cattle grazing, and also by light burning.\n</p><p><i>Calluna</i> was separated from the closely related genus <i>Erica</i> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Anthony_Salisbury" title="Richard Anthony Salisbury">Richard Anthony Salisbury</a>, who devised the generic name <i>Calluna</i> probably from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Greek</a> <i>Kallyno (\xce\xba\xce\xb1\xce\xbb\xce\xbb\xcf\x8d\xce\xbd\xcf\x89)</i>, "beautify, sweep clean", in reference to its traditional use in <a href="/wiki/Besom" title="Besom">besoms</a>. The specific <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">epithet</a> <i>vulgaris</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for \'common\'. <i>Calluna</i> is differentiated from <i>Erica</i> by its <a href="/wiki/Corolla_(flower)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corolla (flower)">corolla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calyx_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Calyx (botany)">calyx</a> each being in four parts instead of five.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Calluna</i> has small scale-leaves (less than 2\xe2\x80\x933&#160;mm long) borne in opposite and <a href="/wiki/Decussation" title="Decussation">decussate</a> pairs, whereas those of <i>Erica</i> are generally larger and in whorls of 3-4, sometimes 5.<sup id="cite_ref-Stace_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stace-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The flowers emerge in late summer; in wild plants these are normally mauve, but white-<a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowered</a> plants also occur occasionally.  They are terminal in racemes with sepal-like bracts at the base with a superior ovary, the fruit a capsule.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Unlike <i>Erica</i>, <i>Calluna</i> sometimes sports double flowers. <i>Calluna</i> is sometimes referred to as Summer (or Autumn) heather to distinguish it from winter or spring flowering species of <i>Erica</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Calluna</i> is native to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DVF_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVF-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It has been introduced into many other places worldwide with suitable climates, including <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-issg_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-issg-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Calluna-vulgaris-closup.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Calluna</i> flower close-up</div></div></div>\n<p>Despised until the 19th century for its associations with the most rugged rural poverty, heather\'s growth in popularity may be paralleled with the vogue for <a href="/wiki/Alpine_plant" title="Alpine plant">alpine plants</a>. It is a very popular <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a> and for landscaping, in lime-free areas where it will thrive, but has defeated many a gardener on less acid soil.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There are many named <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a>, selected for variation in flower colour and for different foliage colour and growing habits.\n</p><p>Different cultivars have flower colours ranging from white, through pink and a wide range of purples, and including reds. The flowering season with different cultivars extends from late July to November in the northern hemisphere. The flowers may turn brown but still remain on the plants over winter, and this can lead to interesting decorative effects.\n</p><p>Cultivars with ornamental foliage are usually selected for reddish and golden leaf colour. A few forms can be silvery grey. Many of the ornamental foliage forms change colour with the onset of winter weather, usually increasing in intensity of colour. Some forms are grown for distinctive young spring foliage. <a href="/wiki/Cultivars" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultivars">Cultivars</a> include \xe2\x80\x98Beoley Crimson\xe2\x80\x99 (Crimson red), class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(December 2010)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Heather is an important food source for various sheep and deer which can graze the tips of the plants when snow covers low-growing vegetation. <a href="/wiki/Willow_Grouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Willow Grouse">Willow Grouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Grouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Grouse">Red Grouse</a> feed on the young shoots and seeds of this plant.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Both adult and <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larva</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lochmaea_suturalis" title="Lochmaea suturalis">Heather Beetle <i>Lochmaea suturalis</i></a> feed on it, and can cause extensive mortality in some instances. The larvae of a number of <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> species also feed on the plant, notably the small emperor moth <i><a href="/wiki/Saturnia_pavonia" title="Saturnia pavonia">Saturnia pavonia</a></i>.\n</p><p>Formerly heather was used to dye wool yellow and to tan leather. With <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a>, heather is an ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Gruit" title="Gruit">gruit</a>, a mixture of flavourings used in the brewing of <a href="/wiki/Beer_in_Scotland" title="Beer in Scotland">heather-beer</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> before the use of <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Thomas Pennant</a> wrote in <i>A Tour in Scotland</i> (1769) that on the Scottish island of <a href="/wiki/Islay" title="Islay">Islay</a> "ale is frequently made of the young tops of heath, mixing two thirds of that plant with one of malt, sometimes adding hops".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The use of heather in the brewing of modern heather beer is carefully regulated. By law<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (December 2010)">specify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, the heather must be cleaned carefully before brewing, as the undersides of the leaves may contain a dusting of an <a href="/wiki/Ergot" title="Ergot">ergot</a>-like fungus, which is a hallucinogenic intoxicant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>From time immemorial heather has been used for making <a href="/wiki/Besom" title="Besom">besoms</a>, a practice recorded in <a href="/wiki/Broom_Buzzems" title="Broom Buzzems">Buy Broom Buzzems</a> a song probably written by <a href="/wiki/William_Purvis_(Blind_Willie)" title="William Purvis (Blind Willie)">William Purvis (Blind Willie)</a> (1752\xe2\x80\x931832) from <a href="/wiki/Newcastle-upon-Tyne" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle-upon-Tyne">Newcastle-upon-Tyne</a>, England.\n</p><p>Heather <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> is a highly valued product in moorland and heathland areas, with many <a href="/wiki/Beehives" class="mw-redirect" title="Beehives">beehives</a> being moved there in late summer.  Not always as valued as it is today,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> it was dismissed as <i>mel improbum</i> by <a href="/wiki/Dioscurides" class="mw-redirect" title="Dioscurides">Dioscurides</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Heather honey has a characteristic strong taste, and an unusual texture, for it is <a href="/wiki/Thixotropic" class="mw-redirect" title="Thixotropic">thixotropic</a>, being a <a href="/wiki/Gel" title="Gel">jelly</a> until stirred, when it becomes a syrup like other honey, but then sets again to a jelly.  This makes the <a href="/wiki/Honey_extraction" title="Honey extraction">extraction</a> of the honey from the comb difficult, and it is therefore often sold as <a href="/wiki/Comb_honey" title="Comb honey">comb honey</a>.\n</p><p>White heather is regarded in Scotland as being lucky,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> a tradition brought from <a href="/wiki/Balmoral_Castle" title="Balmoral Castle">Balmoral</a> to England by <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and sprigs of it are often sold as a charm and worked into bridal <a href="/wiki/Flower_bouquet" title="Flower bouquet">bouquets</a>.\n</p><p>Heather stalks are used by a small industry in Scotland as a raw material for sentimental jewelry. The stalks are stripped of bark, dyed in bright colors and then compressed with resin.\n</p><p><i>Calluna vulgaris</i> herb has been used in the traditional Austrian medicine internally as tea for treatment of disorders of the kidneys and urinary tract.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_species">Invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The plant was introduced to New Zealand and has become an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive weed</a> in some areas, notably the <a href="/wiki/Tongariro_National_Park" title="Tongariro National Park">Tongariro National Park</a> in the North Island and the Wilderness Reserve (Te Anau) in the South Island, overgrowing native plants. <a href="/wiki/Lochmaea_suturalis" title="Lochmaea suturalis">Heather beetles</a> have been released to stop the heather, with preliminary trials successful to date.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The shoots of <i>Calluna vulgaris</i> contain the phenolic compounds <a href="/wiki/Chlorogenic_acid" title="Chlorogenic acid">chlorogenic acid</a>, its 3-O-glucoside, 3-O-galactoside and 3-O-arabinoside.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Heather is seen as iconic of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, where the plant grows widely. When poems like <i>Bonnie Auld Scotland</i> speak of "fragrant hills of purple heather\', when the hero of <a href="/wiki/Kidnapped_(novel)" title="Kidnapped (novel)"><i>Kidnapped</i></a> flees through the heather, when heather and Scotland are linked in the same sentence, the heather talked about is <i>Calluna vulgaris</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scottish writer <a href="/wiki/Nan_Shepherd" title="Nan Shepherd">Nan Shepherd</a> speaks of the heather in her book <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Living_Mountain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Living Mountain (page does not exist)">The Living Mountain</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Howard" title="Robert E. Howard">Robert E. Howard</a> story "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Kings_of_the_Night&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kings of the Night (page does not exist)">Kings of the Night</a>" frequently references heather when describing a portion of what would later become Great Britain.\n</p><p>The English folksong, <a href="/wiki/Scarborough_Fair_(ballad)" title="Scarborough Fair (ballad)">Scarborough Fair</a>, has the line, "Gather it all in a bunch of heather".\n</p><p>The Joy of Living, written by folksinger-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Ewan_MacColl" title="Ewan MacColl">Ewan MacColl</a> refers to his growing old and his saying goodbye to the hills, mountains and his loved ones, the last verse containing his dying wishes in the lines: "Take me to some high place of heather, rock and ling, Scatter my dust and ashes, feed me to the wind...".\nMacColl\'s most famous song, <a href="/wiki/The_Manchester_Rambler" title="The Manchester Rambler">The Manchester Rambler</a>, relates to the <a href="/wiki/Mass_Trespass_of_Kinder_Scout" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass Trespass of Kinder Scout">Mass Trespass of Kinder Scout</a> in 1932, in which he retorts to an angry gamekeeper: "My rucksack has oft been my pillow, The heather has oft been my bed, And sooner than part from the mountains, I think I would rather be dead".\n</p><p>A poem written by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heather_Ale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Heather Ale (page does not exist)">Heather Ale</a>: A Galloway Legend, tells a story of a long-forgotten drink of heather ale.\n</p><p>Purple heather is one of the two <a href="/wiki/Floral_emblem" title="Floral emblem">national flowers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>.\n</p><p>Adam Duritz of Counting Crows sang about Calluna in his song "Rain King" off the 1993 release "August and Everything After":  "Lay me down in a field of flame and heather."\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a> word for heather is <i>wrzos</i> [vzhos], from which the month name <i>wrzesie\xc5\x84</i> (September) is derived, which means quite <i>the month when heather blooms</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, heather fields are commonly associated with the Baltic Sea shore and are a popular motive in both poetry and prose that has sea shore as a background.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a>, the Norse god <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> suggests it\'s uses as an treatment for intestinal worms in stanza 137 of the poem <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A1vam%C3%A1l" title="H\xc3\xa1vam\xc3\xa1l">H\xc3\xa1vam\xc3\xa1l</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/List_of_Lepidoptera_that_feed_on_Calluna" title="List of Lepidoptera that feed on Calluna">List of Lepidoptera that feed on <i>Calluna</i></a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath (habitat)">Heath (habitat)</a>.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Erica" title="Erica">Erica</a></i></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calluna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Matveev, Vladimir. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.biology-online.org/dictionary/Ling">"Ling \xe2\x80\x93 definition from"</a>.
