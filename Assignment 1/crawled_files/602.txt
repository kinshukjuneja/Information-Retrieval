England">southwest</a> of England, began in the early <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, around 2150 BC, and ended (at least temporarily) with the closure of <a href="/wiki/South_Crofty" title="South Crofty">South Crofty</a> <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> mine in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in 1998. Tin, and later <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, were the most commonly extracted metals. Some tin mining continued long after the mining of other metals had become unprofitable.\n</p><p>Historically, tin and copper as well as a few other metals (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>) have been mined in Cornwall and <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>. As of  2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> there are no active <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metalliferous" class="extiw" title="wikt:metalliferous">metalliferous</a> mines remaining. However, tin deposits still exist in Cornwall, and there has been talk of reopening the South Crofty tin mine.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In addition, work has begun on re-opening the Hemerdon <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> and tin mine in south-west Devon.<sup id="cite_ref-Plymouth_Herald_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plymouth_Herald-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In view of the economic importance of mines and quarries, geological studies have been conducted; about forty distinct minerals have been identified from <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(geology)" title="Type locality (geology)">type localities</a> in Cornwall (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Endellionite" class="mw-redirect" title="Endellionite">endellionite</a> from <a href="/wiki/St_Endellion" title="St Endellion">St Endellion</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Quarrying of the <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rocks</a> has also been a significant industry. href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As a result, Cornwall was one of the most important mining areas in Europe until the early 20th century. It is thought that tin ore (<a href="/wiki/Cassiterite" title="Cassiterite">cassiterite</a>) was mined in Cornwall as early as the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Over the years, many other metals (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>) have been mined in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Alquifou (based, along with the word <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>, on the Arabic word "al-kuhl") is a <a href="/wiki/Lead_ore" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead ore">lead ore</a> found in Cornwall, and used by potters to give pottery a green glaze.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cornwall and Devon provided most of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>\'s tin, copper, and arsenic until the 20th century. Originally tin was found as <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_deposit" class="mw-redirect" title="Alluvial deposit">alluvial deposits</a> of cassiterite in the gravels of stream beds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Eventually tin was mined underground; underground mines sprang up as early as the 16th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Tin <a href="/wiki/Lode" title="Lode">lodes</a> were also found in outcroppings of cliffs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric_period">Prehistoric period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prehistoric period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stone_Age_and_early_Bronze_Age">Stone Age and early Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stone Age and early Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Tin is one of the earliest metals to have been exploited in Britain. <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a> metal workers discovered that by putting a small proportion of tin (5 \xe2\x80\x93 20%) in molten copper, the alloy <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> was produced. The alloy is harder than copper. The oldest production of tin-bronze is in Turkey about 3500 BC, but exploitation of the tin resources in Britain is believed to have started before 2000 BC,<sup id="cite_ref-French_1999_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-French_1999-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> with a thriving tin trade developing with the civilisations of the Mediterranean. The strategic importance of tin in forging bronze weapons brought the south west of Britain into the Mediterranean economy at an early date. Later tin was also used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Pewter" title="Pewter">pewter</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age Britain</a> around 2150 BC.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Cornwall was traditionally thought to have been visited by <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> metal traders from the eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cunliffe_1982_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunliffe_1982-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> but this view changed during the 20th century, and Timothy Champion observed in 2001 that "The direct archaeological evidence for the presence of Phoenician or Carthaginian traders as far north as Britain is non-existent".<sup id="cite_ref-Champion_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Champion-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">Britain</a> is one of the places proposed for the <i><a href="/wiki/Cassiterides" title="Cassiterides">Cassiterides</a></i>, that is "Tin Islands", first mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The tin content of the bronze from the <a href="/wiki/Nebra_Sky_Disc" class="mw-redirect" title="Nebra Sky Disc">Nebra Sky Disc</a> dating from 1600 BCE, was found to be from Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Originally it is likely that alluvial deposits in the gravels of streams were exploited, but later underground mining took root. Shallow cuttings were then used to extract ore.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_of_trade">Expansion of trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion of trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>As demand for bronze grew in the Middle East, the accessible local supplies of tin ore (cassiterite) were exhausted and searches for new supplies were made over all the known world, including Britain. Control of the tin trade seems to have been in <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> hands, and they kept their sources secret. The Greeks understood that tin came from the <a href="/wiki/Cassiterides" title="Cassiterides">Cassiterides</a>, the "tin islands", of which the geographical identity is debated. By 500 BC <a href="/wiki/Hecataeus_of_Miletus" title="Hecataeus of Miletus">Hecataeus</a> knew of islands beyond Gaul where tin was obtained. <a href="/wiki/Pytheas_of_Massalia" class="mw-redirect" title="Pytheas of Massalia">Pytheas of Massalia</a> travelled to Britain in about 325 BC where he found a flourishing tin trade, according to the later report of his voyage. <a href="/wiki/Posidonius" title="Posidonius">Posidonius</a> referred to the tin trade with Britain around 90 BC but <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> in about 18 AD did not list tin as one of Britain\'s exports. This is likely to be because Rome was obtaining its tin from Spain at the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>William Camden, in his <i><a href="/wiki/William_Camden#Britannia" title="William Camden">Britannia</a></i> of 1607, identified the Cassiterides with the <a href="/wiki/Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Scilly Isles">Scilly Isles</a> and first gave currency to the belief that the Phoenicians traded to Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, there is no evidence of tin mining on the Scilly Isles apart from minor exploratory excavations. Timothy Champion found it likely that the trade of the Phoenicians with Britain was indirect and under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Veneti_(Gaul)" title="Veneti (Gaul)">Veneti</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Champion_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Champion-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Champion, discussing Diodorus Siculus\'s comments on the tin trade, states that "Diodorus never actually says that the Phoenicians sailed to Cornwall. In fact, he says quite the opposite: the production of Cornish tin was in the hands of the natives of Cornwall, and its transport to the Mediterranean was organised by local merchants, by sea and then over land through France, well outside Phoenician control."<sup id="cite_ref-Champion_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Champion-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Diodorus_Siculus.27s_account"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Diodorus_Siculus\'s_account">Diodorus Siculus\'s account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diodorus Siculus&#039;s account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ictis" title="Ictis">Ictis</a></div>\n<p>In his <i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_historica" title="Bibliotheca historica">Bibliotheca historica</a></i>, written in the 1st century BC, <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> described ancient tin mining in Britain. "They that inhabit the British promontory of Belerion by reason of their converse with strangers are more civilised and courteous to strangers than the rest are. These are the people that prepare the tin, which with a great deal of care and labour, they dig out of the ground, and that being done the metal is mixed with some veins of earth out of which they melt the metal and refine it. Then they cast it into regular blocks and carry it to a certain island near at hand called Ictis for at low tide, all being dry between there and the island, tin in large quantities is brought over in carts." Pliny, whose text has survived in eroded condition, quotes <a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(historian)" title="Timaeus (historian)">Timaeus of Taormina</a> in referring to "<i>insulam Mictim</i>", "the island of Mictim" [sic], where the <i>m</i> of <i>insulam</i> has been repeated.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Several locations for "Ictin" or "Ictis", signifying "tin port"<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> have been suggested, including <a href="/wiki/St._Michael%27s_Mount" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Michael&#39;s Mount">St. Michael\'s Mount</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but, as a result of excavations, Barry Cunliffe has proposed that this was <a href="/wiki/Mount_Batten" title="Mount Batten">Mount Batten</a> near Plymouth. A shipwreck site with ingots of tin was found at the <a href="/wiki/Erme_Mouth" class="mw-redirect" title="Erme Mouth">mouth of the River Erme</a> not far away,<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> which may represent trade along this coast during the Bronze Age, although dating the site is very difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Strabo reported that British tin was shipped from Marseille.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Legend_of_Joseph_of_Arimathea">Legend of Joseph of Arimathea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legend of Joseph of Arimathea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ding_Dong_mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Ding Dong mine">Ding Dong mine</a>, reputedly one of the oldest in Cornwall, in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Gulval" title="Gulval">Gulval</a> is said in local legend to have been visited by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_of_Arimathea" title="Joseph of Arimathea">Joseph of Arimathea</a>, a tin trader, and that he <a href="/wiki/And_did_those_feet_in_ancient_time" title="And did those feet in ancient time">brought a young Jesus</a> to address the miners, although there is no evidence to support this.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age_archaeology">Iron Age archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Iron Age archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>There are few remains of prehistoric tin mining in Cornwall or Devon, probably because later workings have destroyed early ones. However, shallow cuttings used for extracting ore can be seen in some places such as Challacombe Down, Dartmoor. There are a few stone hammers, such as those in the Zennor Wayside Museum.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It may well be that mining was mostly undertaken with shovels, antler picks, and wooden wedges. An excavation at Dean Moor on Dartmoor, at a site dated at 1400 \xe2\x80\x93 900 BC from pottery, yielded a pebble of tin ore and tin slag.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Rocks were used for crushing the ore and stones for this were found at Crift Farm.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> There have been finds of tin slag on the floors of Bronze Age houses,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> for example at Trevisker. Tin slag was found at Caerloges with a dagger of the Camerton-Snowhill type.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> bronze continued to be used for ornaments though not for tools and weapons, so tin extraction seems to have continued. An ingot from Castle Dore is probably of Iron Age date.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_and_Post-Roman_periods">Roman and Post-Roman periods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Roman and Post-Roman periods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The tin resources are said to have been a reason the Romans invaded Britain,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> but they had control of mines in Spain and Brittany in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD. Later production in Spain was curtailed, probably by raiding. Production in Britain increased in the 3rd century, for use in coinage, and there was extensive use of tin in pewter manufacture, at Camerton in Somerset for example. Cornwall and West Devon are less Romanised than many other parts of Britain, and tin mining may have been in local hands, with tin purchased by the imperial authority. A possible official stamp has been identified on the Carnington tin ingot.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> A number of tin ingots have been found in Roman contexts, such as 42 found in a wreck at <a href="/wiki/Bigbury-on-Sea" title="Bigbury-on-Sea">Bigbury Bay</a> in 1991\xe2\x80\x9392.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A site in the <a href="/wiki/River_Erme" title="River Erme">Erme Valley</a>, Devon, shows sediment aggregation in late Roman and Post-Roman times due to tin mining on Dartmoor.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> There is a peak in activity between the 4th and 7th centuries. Tin slag at Week Ford in Devon has been dated to 570 \xe2\x80\x93 890 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/St_Piran" class="mw-redirect" title="St Piran">St Piran</a> (patron saint of tinners) is said to have landed at <a href="/wiki/Perranporth" title="Perranporth">Perranporth</a> from Ireland about 420 AD.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_and_modern_mining">Medieval and modern mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medieval and modern mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>There is no record of tin mining in <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a>, possibly because the rights were Crown property. During the first half of the 12th century Dartmoor provided most of the tin for Europe, exceeding the production of Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pipe_Roll" class="mw-redirect" title="Pipe Roll">Pipe Roll</a> of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> gives the annual tin production of Dartmoor as about 60 tons. In 1198 he agreed that "all the diggers and buyers of black tin, and all the smelters of tin, and traders of tin in the first smelting shall have the just and ancient customs and liberties established in Devon and Cornwall." This shows that mining had been going on for a long time. A charter confirming the miners\' rights was granted by <a href="/wiki/John,_King_of_England" title="John, King of England">King John</a> in 1201. The alluvial silt record in the Erme Valley, Devon, shows a build-up of tin waste between 1288 and 1389.\n</p><p>Following the transfer of power to the Norman lord <a href="/wiki/Robert,_Count_of_Mortain" title="Robert, Count of Mortain">Robert, Count of Mortain</a>, who held the manor of <a href="/wiki/Trematon_Castle" title="Trematon Castle">Trematon</a>, silver mining became a major industry, particularly in the Tamar valley around <a href="/wiki/Bere_Ferrers" title="Bere Ferrers">Bere Ferrers</a> in Devon. Established in 1292 by the Crown under <a href="/wiki/Edward_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward I">Edward I</a>, skilled labour was initially imported from <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a>, with specialist expertise from Germany and capital from Italy .<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rippon_etal_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rippon_etal-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Profits from rights to the silver mines for the Crown led to the rise of the ancient Cornish <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Mount_Edgcumbe" title="Earl of Mount Edgcumbe">Edgcumbe</a> family at <a href="/wiki/Cotehele" title="Cotehele">Cotehele</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Mount_Edgcumbe_Country_Park" title="Mount Edgcumbe Country Park">Mount Edgcumbe</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" of the Great Court</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg/220px-Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg/330px-Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg/440px-Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bendigo_Miners%27_statue.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A statue commemorating Cornish and German miners in <a href="/wiki/Bendigo" title="Bendigo">Bendigo</a>, Victoria, Australia</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1305 King Edward I established separate Stannaries for Devon and Cornwall. Water was used to operate <a href="/wiki/Stamp_mill" title="Stamp mill">stamps</a> to crush the ore, the lighter waste being washed away. The mineral "black tin" was placed in furnaces and layered with peat. The molten metal was poured into granite moulds which produced ingots of tin. These were taken on pack horses to the Stannary towns for assaying. Usable deposits in Devon became worked out, and so Cornwall became the centre of tin production. In 1337 Cornish tin production was 650 tons, but in 1335<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> it had been reduced to 250 tons by the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>. In 1400 Cornish production rose to 800 tons. The production in Devon was only 25% of that of Cornwall in 1450\xe2\x80\x9370.\n</p><p>The tin works of Devon and Cornwall were of such importance that the medieval kings established <a href="/wiki/Stannary_Courts_and_Parliaments" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary Courts and Parliaments">Stannary Courts and Parliaments</a> to administer the law in Cornwall and part of Devon. Up to the mid 16th century, Devon produced about 25\xe2\x80\x9340% of the amount of tin that Cornwall did, but the total amount of tin production from both Cornwall and Devon during this period was relatively small.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Rebellion_of_1497" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Rebellion of 1497">Cornish Rebellion of 1497</a> originated among Cornish tin miners who opposed the raising of taxes by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a> to make war on <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. This levy was resented for the economic hardship it would cause; it also intruded on a special Cornish tax exemption. The rebels marched on London, gaining supporters as they went, but were defeated at the Battle of Deptford Bridge.\n</p><p>Quarrying was of very limited importance in medieval Cornwall. Stone for church building was very rarely imported from outside the county: they used whatever stone could be found within a short distance. For some ornamental features such as doorways, pillars and fonts good use was made of varieties of <a href="/wiki/Elvan" title="Elvan">elvan</a> (e.g. Polyphant and Catacleuze). The granite was not quarried but collected from the moorlands and worked on site. Quarrying of slate developed in north Cornwall in the later Middle Ages and later developed in early modern times into larger undertakings.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>After the 1540s, Cornwall\'s production increased rapidly and Devon\'s production was only about 10% - 11% of that of Cornwall. From the mid-16th century the Devon Stannaries generated very little income for the Crown, and they were sidelined under the Supremacy of Parliament Act 1512, an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Supremacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Supremacy">Act of Supremacy</a> (this did not apply to the Stannaries of Cornwall). The first <a href="/wiki/Crockern_Tor" title="Crockern Tor">Crockern Tor</a> <a href="/wiki/Stannary_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary Parliament">Stannary Parliament</a> in Devon was held in 1494 and the last in 1748. At <a href="/wiki/Combe_Martin" title="Combe Martin">Combe Martin</a> several disused silver mines are located on the eastern ridge and evidence of tunnels can still be seen, as well as the remains of a wheelhouse used to lift ore from the mine. There are items in the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Jewels" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Jewels">Crown Jewels</a> made from Combe Martin silver.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A second tin boom came around the 16th century when open cast mining was used. German miners who had knowledge of the techniques were employed. In 1689, Thomas Epsley, a Somerset man, developed a method to blast the very hard granite rock loose, using gunpowder with quill fuses. It revolutionised hard rock mining. Six days\' work with a pick could be accomplished with one blast.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> There was a third boom in the 18th century when shafts were dug to extract the ore.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_modern_period">Later modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Later modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg/220px-Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg/330px-Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg/440px-Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Richard_Trevithick" title="Richard Trevithick">Richard Trevithick</a>\'s steam engine</div></div></div>\n<p>In the 19th century Cornish mining reached its zenith, before foreign competition depressed the price of copper, and later tin, to a level that made the extraction of Cornish ore unprofitable. The areas of Cornwall around <a href="/wiki/Gwennap" title="Gwennap">Gwennap</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Day" title="St Day">St Day</a> and on the coast around <a href="/wiki/Porthtowan" title="Porthtowan">Porthtowan</a> were among the richest mining areas in the world. At its height the Cornish tin mining industry had around 600 <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> working to pump out the mines (many mines reached under the sea and some went down to great depths). Adventurers put up the capital, hoping that the mine would return them a profit, but the outcomes were very uncertain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Caradon_Hill" title="Caradon Hill">Caradon Hill</a> had the most productive mine in east Cornwall. The South Caradon Copper Mine, 1&#160;km to the southwest of the <a href="/wiki/Caradon_Hill_transmitting_station" title="Caradon Hill transmitting station">Caradon Hill transmitting station</a>, was the largest copper mine in the UK in its heyday in the second half of the 19th century. Other disused copper and tin mines are scattered around the base of the hill. By the mid-19th century Looe had become a major port, one of Cornwall\'s largest, exporting local tin, arsenic, and granite, as well as hosting thriving fishing and boatbuilding industries. At <a href="/wiki/Callington,_Cornwall" title="Callington, Cornwall">Callington</a> arsenic was found with copper ores and was processed by crushing and condensing; the poisonous nature of dust containing arsenic made the work very hazardous. Numerous precautions were taken but the workers tended to die in middleage.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Menheniot" title="Menheniot">Menheniot</a> was a centre of lead mining and is now surrounded by disused shafts and engine houses. Lead seams were discovered in the 1840s and Menheniot became the centre of a mining boom which lasted until the 1870s. During this period the population doubled.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kit_Hill_Country_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Kit Hill Country Park">Kit Hill Country Park</a> is steeped in mining history. Metals extracted included, tin, silver, copper, and tungsten. The main mines were Kit Hill Summit Mines (which included a windmill near the present stack) (started about 1826; Kit Hill United closed in 1864); East Kit Hill Mine, worked from 1855 to 1909; <a href="/wiki/Hingston_Down" title="Hingston Down">Hingston Down</a> mine (which worked westwards towards Kit Hill, may have started in the 17th century, it closed in 1885; and South Kit Hill Mine, worked from 1856 to 1884.\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg/450px-Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg" width="450" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg/675px-Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg/900px-Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1283" data-file-height="579" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>View from Dolcoath Mine towards Redruth, c. 1890</center></div></div></div></div>\n<p>The last Cornish Stannary Parliament was held at Hingston Down in 1753, and the Devon Stannary Parliament last met in 1748. The Stannary Courts of Devon and Cornwall were combined in 1855 and their powers transferred to local authorities in 1896.\n</p><p>By the middle and late 19th century, Cornish mining was in decline, and many <a href="/wiki/Cornish_diaspora" title="Cornish diaspora">Cornish miners emigrated</a> to developing mining districts overseas, where their skills were in demand: these included South Africa, Australia and North America. Cornish miners became dominant in the 1850s in the iron and <a href="/wiki/Copper_Country" title="Copper Country">copper districts</a> of northern <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> in the United States, as well as in many other mining districts. In the first six months of 1875, over 10,000 miners left Cornwall to find work overseas.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_and_after">20th century and after</span><span href="/wiki/Dolcoath_mine" title="Dolcoath mine">Dolcoath mine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> for <i>Old Ground</i>)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, the \'Queen of Cornish Mines\', was 3500 feet (1067 m) deep, and was for many years the deepest mine in the world<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, not to mention one of the oldest before its closure in 1921. Indeed, the last working tin mine in Europe was <a href="/wiki/South_Crofty" title="South Crofty">South Crofty</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a>, until its closure in March 1998. After an attempt was made to reopen it, it was abandoned. There were local media reports in September 2006 that South Crofty was being considered for re-opening as the price of tin had soared, but the site was subject to a <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_Purchase_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Compulsory Purchase Order">Compulsory Purchase Order</a> (October 2006).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On the wall outside the gate is some graffiti dating from 1999:\n</p><p>"Cornish lads are fishermen and Cornish lads are miners too. / But when the fish and tin are gone, what are the Cornish boys to do?"\n</p><p>The collapse of the world tin <a href="/wiki/Cartel" title="Cartel">cartel</a> in 1986 was the end for Cornish and Devonian tin mining. The most recent mine in Devon to produce tin ore was Hemerdon Mine near <a href="/wiki/Plympton" title="Plympton">Plympton</a> in the 1980s. The last Cornish tin mine in production at <a href="/wiki/South_Crofty" title="South Crofty">South Crofty</a> closed in 1998. The <a href="/wiki/Hemerdon_Mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemerdon Mine">Hemerdon</a> tungsten and tin mine in south-west Devon re-opened as <a href="/wiki/Drakelands_Mine" title="Drakelands Mine">Drakelands Mine</a> in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Plymouth_Herald_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plymouth_Herald-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1992, <a href="/wiki/Geevor_Tin_Mine" title="Geevor Tin Mine">Geevor mine</a> was acquired by Cornwall County Council as a Heritage Museum, which is now run by Pendeen Community Heritage. Both Geevor Tin Mine and <a href="/wiki/Morwellham_Quay" title="Morwellham Quay">Morwellham Quay</a> have been selected as "anchor points" on the <a href="/wiki/European_Route_of_Industrial_Heritage" title="European Route of Industrial Heritage">European Route of Industrial Heritage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The extraction of <a href="/wiki/Kaolin" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaolin">china clay</a> (kaolin) continues to be of considerable importance: the larger works are in the <a href="/wiki/St_Austell" title="St Austell">St Austell</a> district. The amount of waste in proportion to kaolin is so great that huge waste mounds were created whose whiteness in the early years means that they can be seen from afar. The <a href="/wiki/Eden_Project" title="Eden Project">Eden Project</a> has been developed on the site of a former china clay quarry. Extraction of slate and roadstone by quarrying still continues on a reduced scale: it was formerly an important industry, and has been carried on in Cornwall ever since the Middle Ages.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Several quarries have been productive enough to need their own mineral railways. <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">Granite</a> of high quality has been extracted from many Cornish quarries such as De Lank and <a href="/wiki/Porthoustock" title="Porthoustock">Porthoustock</a>. Some granite has been taken very long distances for use in building. There are also some important quarries in Devon, such as <a href="/wiki/Meldon_Quarry" title="Meldon Quarry">Meldon</a> (a source of railway ballast for the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern Railway (Great Britain)">Southern Railway</a>) and granite quarries on Dartmoor such as <a href="/wiki/Merrivale,_Devon" title="Merrivale, Devon">Merrivale</a>.\n</p><p>In 2017, plans were reported to extract lithium reserves from beneath Cornwall by Cornish Lithium, who had signed agreements to develop potential deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Disasters</dt></dl>\n<p>In the <a It continues to teach mining as well as many other earth-related subjects (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Engineering_geology" title="Engineering geology">engineering geology</a>) relevant to the Cornish economy. CSM now forms part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Exeter" title="University of Exeter">University of Exeter</a>, and has relocated to the University\'s <a href="/wiki/Tremough" class="mw-redirect" title="Tremough">Tremough</a> campus in <a href="/wiki/Penryn,_Cornwall" title="Penryn, Cornwall">Penryn</a>. Despite this move, the School continues the use of "Camborne" in its name. CSM graduates are to be found working in the mining industry all over the world.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_and_symbolism">Terminology and symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Terminology and symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Several Cornish mining words are still in use in English language mining terminology, such as <a href="/wiki/Costean" title="Costean">costean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gunnies" title="Gunnies">gunnies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vug" title="Vug">vug</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bucket_(machine_part)#Mining" title="Bucket (machine part)">kibbal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gossan" title="Gossan">gossan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Keeve_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Keeve (disambiguation)">kieve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Fish, tin, and copper together are sometimes used as a symbol of Cornwall because they show the three main traditional industries of Cornwall. Tin has a special place in the Cornish culture, the Stannary Parliament, and \'<a href="/wiki/Cornish_currency" title="Cornish currency">Cornish pennies</a>\' are a testament to the former power of the Cornish tin industry. Cornish tin is highly prized for jewellery, often of mine engines or <a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic</a> designs.\n</p><p>The houses at <a href="/wiki/Penair_School" title="Penair School">Penair School</a> are named after four notable tin mines. The pub sign at Zennor pictures a tin miner at work, testimony to its origins.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The Jolly Tinners building at St Hilary was at one time used to accommodate the <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Walke#St_Hilary_Children&#39;s_Home" title="Bernard Walke">St Hilary Children\'s Home</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WSHH_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSHH-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Three_hares">Three hares</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Three hares">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Three_hares" title="Three hares">three hares</a></b> is a circular <a href="/wiki/Motif_(visual_arts)" title="Motif (visual arts)">motif</a> which appears in <a href="/wiki/Sacred_sites" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred sites">sacred sites</a> from the Middle and Far East to the churches of south west England (where it is often referred to as the "Tinners\' Rabbits").<sup id="cite_ref-Chapman_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> It occurs with the greatest frequency in the churches of the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> of England. The motif appears in architectural <a href="/wiki/Wood_carving" title="Wood carving">wood carving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stone_carving" title="Stone carving">stone carving</a>, window <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a>. In South Western England there are nearly thirty recorded examples of the Three Hares appearing on \'<a href="/wiki/Roof_boss" class="mw-redirect" title="Roof boss">roof bosses</a>\' (carved wooden knobs) on the ceilings in <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> churches in Devon, (particularly <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a>). There is a good example of a roof boss of the Three hares at <a href="/wiki/Widecombe-in-the-Moor" title="Widecombe-in-the-Moor">Widecombe-in-the-Moor</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Widecombe_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Widecombe-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Another exemplary roof boss can be found in the town of <a href="/wiki/Tavistock,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Tavistock, Devon">Tavistock</a>, in Dartmoor, on the edge of the moor.\n</p><p>Tinners\' Rabbits is the name of a dance of many forms involving use of sticks and rotation of three, six or nine Mine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Hemerdon Mine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hemerdon_Mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemerdon Mine">Hemerdon Mine</a>, alternatively known as the <a href="/wiki/Drakelands_Mine" title="Drakelands Mine">Drakelands Mine</a> or Hemerdon Ball or Hemerdon Bal Mine,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> is a historic tungsten and tin mine, 11&#160;km (7 miles) NE of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Plympton" title="Plympton">Plympton</a>, in Devon. It lies to the north of the villages of <a href="/wiki/Sparkwell" title="Sparkwell">Sparkwell</a> and Hemerdon and adjacent to the large <a href="/wiki/China_clay" class="mw-redirect" title="China clay">china clay</a> pits near Lee Moor. The mine, which had been out of operation since 1944, except for the brief operation of a trial mine in the 1980s, hosts one of the largest tungsten and tin deposits in the world. It restarted production in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC_7126273_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_7126273-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Crofty_Mine">South Crofty Mine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: South Crofty Mine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/South_Crofty" title="South Crofty">South Crofty</a></div>\n<p>In November 2007 it was announced that <a href="/wiki/South_Crofty" title="South Crofty">South Crofty</a> mine, near <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a>, may restart production in 2009. When it closed in 1998 it was Europe\'s last tin mine. Its owners Baseresult Holdings Ltd, which bought the mine in 2001, have created a new company, Western United Mines Limited (WUM), to operate it and has said it will be spending in excess of \xc2\xa350m on restarting the mine. The company claims that rising tin prices had given the mine, first opened in the late 16th century, another 80 years of life. More than \xc2\xa33.5m will be spent during the next seven months on continuing the mine development. Crofty Developments, a partner of the new company, still has to resolve a row with the <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West</a> <a href="/wiki/Regional_Development_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Development Agency">Regional Development Agency</a> (RDA) over use of more than 30 acres (120,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) of land surrounding the site. The RDA wants to make a compulsory purchase order on the site for leisure, housing and industry, but Crofty Developments has been fighting in the High Court to retain the site. The Cornish mining industry, started in 2000 BC, reached its peak in the 19th century, when thousands of workers were employed in up to 2,000 mines, before the industry collapsed when ores began to be
