href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Bosvena</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> and historic town in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, England, United Kingdom. It is situated south-west of <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Moor" title="Bodmin Moor">Bodmin Moor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The extent of the civil parish corresponds fairly closely to that of the town so is mostly urban in character. It is bordered to the east by <a href="/wiki/Cardinham" title="Cardinham">Cardinham</a> parish, to the southeast by <a href="/wiki/Lanhydrock" title="Lanhydrock">Lanhydrock</a> parish, to the southwest and west by <a href="/wiki/Lanivet" title="Lanivet">Lanivet</a> parish, and to the north by <a href="/wiki/Helland" title="Helland">Helland</a> parish.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bodmin had a population of 14,736 as of the 2011 Census.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  It was formerly the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> of Cornwall until the Crown Courts moved to <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a> which is also the administrative centre (before 1835 the county town was <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Cornwall" title="Launceston, Cornwall">Launceston</a>). Bodmin was in the administrative <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall" title="North Cornwall">North Cornwall</a> District until local government reorganisation in 2009 abolished the District (<i>see also <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Cornwall" title="Politics of Cornwall">Cornwall Council</a></i>). The town is part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="North Cornwall (UK Parliament constituency)">North Cornwall</a> parliamentary constituency, which is represented by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Mann_(politician)" title="Scott Mann (politician)">Scott Mann</a> MP.\n</p><p>Bodmin Town Council is made up of sixteen councillors who are elected to serve a term of four years. Each year, the Council elects one of its number as Mayor to serve as the town\'s civic leader and to chair council meetings.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Situation_and_origin_of_the_name">Situation and origin of the name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Situation and origin of the name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Bodmin lies in the east of Cornwall, south-west of <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Moor" title="Bodmin Moor">Bodmin Moor</a>. It has been suggested that the town\'s name comes from an archaic word in the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> "bod" (meaning a dwelling; the later word is "bos") and a contraction of "menegh" (monks). The "monks\' dwelling" may refer to an early monastic settlement instituted by St. Guron, which <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petroc" title="Saint Petroc">St. Petroc</a> took as his site. Guron is said to have departed to <a href="/wiki/St_Goran" title="St Goran">St Goran</a> on the arrival of Petroc.\n</p><p>The hamlets of Cooksland, <a href="/wiki/Dunmere,_Cornwall" title="Dunmere, Cornwall">Dunmere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turfdown" title="Turfdown">Turfdown</a> are in the parish.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Saint_Petroc" title="Saint Petroc">St. Petroc</a> founded a monastery in Bodmin in the 6th century<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and gave the town its alternative name of <i>Petrockstow</i>. The monastery was deprived of some of its lands at the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest</a> but at the time of <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday</a> still held eighteen manors, including Bodmin, Padstow and Rialton.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Bodmin is one of the oldest towns in Cornwall, and the only large Cornish settlement recorded in the Domesday Book in 1086.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the 15th century the Norman church of St Petroc was largely rebuilt and stands as one of the largest churches in Cornwall (the largest after the cathedral at Truro). Also built at that time was an abbey of canons regular, now mostly ruined. For most of Bodmin\'s history, the <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> industry was a mainstay of the economy.\n</p><p>The name of the town probably derives from the Cornish "Bod-meneghy", meaning "dwelling of or by the sanctuary of monks".<sup id="cite_ref-HoB_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HoB-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Variant spellings recorded include <i>Botmenei</i> in 1100, <i>Bodmen</i> in 1253, <i>Bodman</i> in 1377 and <i>Bodmyn</i> in 1522.<sup id="cite_ref-HoB_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HoB-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Bodman</i> spelling also appears in sources and maps from the 16th and 17th centuries,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> most notably in the celebrated map of Cornwall produced by <a href="/wiki/John_Speed" title="John Speed">John Speed</a> but actually engraved by the Dutch cartographer <a href="/wiki/Jodocus_Hondius" title="Jodocus Hondius">Jodocus Hondius</a> the Elder (1563\xe2\x80\x931612) in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> in 1610 (published in London by Sudbury and Humble in 1626). It is unclear whether the Bodman spelling signifies any historical or monastic connection with the equally ancient settlement of Bodman at the western end of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Bodensee</a> in the German province of <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>An inscription on a stone built into the wall of a summer house in Lancarffe furnishes proof of a settlement in Bodmin in the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. It is a memorial to one "Duno[.]atus son of Me[.]cagnus" and has been dated from the 6th to 8th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Stone_Cross_on_Old_Callywith_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_757140.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Ancient_Stone_Cross_on_Old_Callywith_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_757140.jpg/220px-Ancient_Stone_Cross_on_Old_Callywith_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_757140.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Ancient_Stone_Cross_on_Old_Callywith_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_757140.jpg/330px-Ancient_Stone_Cross_on_Old_Callywith_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_757140.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Ancient_Stone_Cross_on_Old_Callywith_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_757140.jpg 2x" data-file-width="427" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Stone_Cross_on_Old_Callywith_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_757140.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Cornish cross on Old Callywith Road</div></div></div>\n<p>Arthur Langdon (1896) records three Cornish crosses at Bodmin; one was near the Berry Tower, one was outside Bodmin Gaol and another was in a field near Castle Street Hill.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There is also <a href="/wiki/Carminow_Cross" title="Carminow Cross">Carminow Cross</a> at a road junction southeast of the town.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> killed half of Bodmin\'s population in the mid 14th century (1,500 people).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebellions">Rebellions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rebellions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Bodmin was the centre of three Cornish uprisings. The first was the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Rebellion_of_1497" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Rebellion of 1497">Cornish Rebellion of 1497</a> when a Cornish army, led by <a href="/wiki/Michael_An_Gof" title="Michael An Gof">Michael An Gof</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmith</a> from <a href="/wiki/St._Keverne" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Keverne">St. Keverne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Flamank" title="Thomas Flamank">Thomas Flamank</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a> from Bodmin, marched to <a href="/wiki/Blackheath,_London" title="Blackheath, London">Blackheath</a> in London where they were eventually defeated by 10,000 men of the King\'s army under Baron Daubeny. Then, in the autumn of 1497, <a href="/wiki/Perkin_Warbeck" title="Perkin Warbeck">Perkin Warbeck</a> tried to usurp the throne from <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a>. Warbeck was proclaimed King Richard IV in Bodmin but Henry had little difficulty crushing the uprising. In 1549, Cornishmen, allied with other rebels in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, rose once again in rebellion when the staunchly Protestant <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">Edward VI</a> tried to impose a new <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Prayer Book</a>. The lower classes of Cornwall and Devon were still strongly attached to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> religion and again a Cornish army was formed in Bodmin which marched across the border into Devon to lay siege to <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>. This became known as the <a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a>. Proposals to translate the Prayer Book into Cornish were suppressed and in total 4,000 people were killed in the rebellion.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bodmin_Borough_Police">Bodmin Borough Police</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bodmin Borough Police">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Borough of Bodmin was one of the 178 municipal boroughs which under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Corporations_Act_1835" title="Municipal Corporations Act 1835">Municipal Corporations Act 1835</a> was mandated to create an electable council and a Police Watch Committee responsible for overseeing a police force in the town. The new system directly replaced the Parish Constables that had policed the borough since time immemorial and brought paid, uniformed and accountable law enforcement for the first time. Bodmin Borough Police was the municipal police force for the Borough of Bodmin from 1836 to 1866. The creation of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Constabulary" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornwall Constabulary">Cornwall Constabulary</a> in 1857 put pressure on smaller municipal police forces to merge with the county. The two-man force of Bodmin came under threat almost immediately, but it would take until 1866 for the Mayor of Bodmin and the Chairman of the Police Watch Committee to agree on the terms of amalgamation. After a public enquiry, the force was disbanded in January 1866 and policing of the borough was deferred to the county from thereon.\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".22Bodmin_Town.22"></span><span href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a The holy well of St Guron is a small stone building at the churchyard gate. The Berry Tower is all that remains of the former church of the Holy Rood and there are even fewer remains from the substantial Franciscan Friary established ca. 1240: a gateway in Fore Street and two pillars elsewhere in the town. The Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St_Mary_and_St_Petroc" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbey of St Mary and St Petroc">Abbey of St Mary and St Petroc</a>, formerly belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Canons_Regular" class="mw-redirect" title="Canons Regular">Canons Regular</a> of the Lateran was built in 1965 next to the already existing seminary.<sup id="cite_ref-pevsner1970_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner1970-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The Roman Catholic parish of Bodmin includes a large area of North Cornwall and there are churches also at Wadebridge, Padstow and Tintagel.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1881 the Roman Catholic mass was celebrated in Bodmin for the first time since 1539. A church was planned in the 1930s but delayed by the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>: the Church of St Mary and St Petroc was eventually consecrated in 1965:<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> it was built next to the already existing seminary.<sup id="cite_ref-pevsner1970_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner1970-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There are also five other churches in Bodmin, including a Methodist church.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archdeaconry_of_Bodmin">Archdeaconry of Bodmin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Archdeaconry of Bodmin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Archdeacon_of_Bodmin" title="Archdeacon of Bodmin">Archdeacon of Bodmin</a></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sites_of_interest">Sites of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sites of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutions">Institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Jail" title="Bodmin Jail">Bodmin Jail</a>, operational for over 150 years but now a semi-ruin, was built in the late 18th century, and was the first British prison to hold prisoners in separate cells (though often up to ten at a time) rather than communally. Over fifty prisoners condemned at the Bodmin <a href="/wiki/Assize_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Assize Court">Assize Court</a> were hanged at the prison. It was also used for temporarily holding prisoners sentenced to transportation, awaiting transfer to the prison hulks lying in the highest navigable reaches of the <a href="/wiki/River_Fowey" title="River Fowey">River Fowey</a>. and &#039;hurling&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1865\xe2\x80\x9366 <a href="/wiki/William_Robert_Hicks" title="William Robert Hicks">William Robert Hicks</a> was mayor of Bodmin, when he revived the custom of <a href="/wiki/Beating_the_bounds" title="Beating the bounds">Beating the bounds</a> of the town. He was \xe2\x80\x94 according to the <a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_National_Biography" title="Dictionary of National Biography">Dictionary of National Biography</a> \xe2\x80\x94 a very good man of business. This still takes place more or less every five years and concludes with a game of <a href="/wiki/Cornish_hurling" title="Cornish hurling">Cornish hurling</a>. Hurling survives as a traditional part of beating the bounds at Bodmin, commencing at the close of the \'Beat\'. The game is organised by the <a href="/wiki/Rotary_club" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotary club">Rotary club</a> of Bodmin and was last played in 2015. The game is started by the Mayor of Bodmin by throwing a silver ball into a body of water known as the "Salting Pool". There are no teams and the hurl follows a set route. The aim is to carry the ball from the "Salting Pool" via the old <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30</a>, along Callywith Road, then through Castle Street, Church Square and Honey Street to finish at the Turret Clock in Fore Street. The participant carrying the ball when it reaches the turret clock will receive a \xc2\xa310 reward from the mayor.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> \nIn 2015, beating of the bounds and Cornish hurling took place at Bodmin 8 April organised by the <a href="/wiki/Rotary_club" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotary club">Rotary club</a> of Bodmin.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/List_of_topics_related_to_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="List of topics related to Cornwall">List of topics related to Cornwall</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bodmin_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Bodmin (UK Parliament constituency)">List of Bodmin MPs</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bodmin_NHS_Treatment_Centre_(Bodmin_Hospital)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bodmin NHS Treatment Centre (Bodmin Hospital)">Bodmin NHS Treatment Centre (Bodmin Hospital)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bodmin_manumissions" title="Bodmin manumissions">Bodmin manumissions</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Beast_of_Bodmin" class="mw-redirect" title="Beast of Bodmin">Beast of Bodmin</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bodmin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
