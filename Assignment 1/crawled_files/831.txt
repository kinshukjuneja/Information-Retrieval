pennants</b>  <b>0</b></li></ul>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Louisville Grays</b> were a 19th-century United States <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> team and charter member of the <a href="/wiki/19th_century_National_League_teams" class="mw-redirect" title="19th century National League teams">National League</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>. They played two seasons, 1876 and 1877, and compiled a record of 65&#8211;61.  Their home games were at the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Baseball_Park" title="Louisville Baseball Park">Louisville Baseball Park</a>. The Grays were owned by businessman <a href="/wiki/Walter_Newman_Haldeman" title="Walter Newman Haldeman">Walter Newman Haldeman</a>, owner and publisher of the <a href="/wiki/The_Courier-Journal" title="The Courier-Journal">Louisville Courier-Journal</a> newspaper.\n</p><p>The Grays were undone by <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>\'s first <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a> scandal. The team was in first place in August 1877, then suddenly lost seven games and tied one against the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Boston_Braves" title="History of the Boston Braves">Boston Red Stockings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hartford_Dark_Blues" title="Hartford Dark Blues">Hartford Dark Blues</a>. Boston ended up winning the pennant, seven games ahead of the second-place Grays. A <i>Courier-Journal</i> story questioning the team\'s conduct was written by <a href="/wiki/John_Haldeman" title="John Haldeman">John Haldeman</a>, the owner\'s son.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Team president Charles Chase received two anonymous <a href="/wiki/Telegram" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegram">telegrams</a>. One noted that gamblers were favoring the less talented Hartford team in an upcoming series. The second telegram predicted Louisville would <a href="/wiki/Match_fixing" title="Match fixing">throw</a> the next game versus Hartford on August 21. The Grays committed a number of suspicious errors and lost that game 7&#8211;0. League president <a href="/wiki/William_Hulbert" title="William Hulbert">William Hulbert</a> investigated and ordered players to authorize <a href="/wiki/Western_Union" title="Western Union">Western Union</a> to release all <a href="/wiki/Telegram" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegram">telegrams</a> sent or received during the 1877 season. All players complied except <a href="/wiki/Shortstop" title="Shortstop">shortstop</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Craver" title="Bill Craver">Bill Craver</a>, the team\'s captain.\n</p><p>The telegrams indicated that <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Jim_Devlin" title="Jim Devlin">Jim Devlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Left_fielder" title="Left fielder">left fielder</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Hall_(baseball)" title="George Hall (baseball)">George Hall</a>, and utility player <a href="/wiki/Al_Nichols" title="Al Nichols">Al Nichols</a> intentionally lost games in exchange for money. No direct evidence was found implicating Craver. All four were banned from baseball for life, Craver for refusing to comply with the investigation.\n</p><p>Devlin pitched every inning for the 1877 Grays, leading the league in games and innings pitched. Hall played every inning in left field; he was a good batter, the 1876 home run "champion" with five. The original <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Brown_Stockings" title="St. Louis Brown Stockings">St. Louis Brown Stockings</a> had signed Devlin and Hall for 1878 and went out of business with the Grays after the investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
