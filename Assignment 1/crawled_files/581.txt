Field</a> in 1913</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, the <b>dead-ball era</b> was the period between around 1900 and the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> as a power hitter in 1919. That year, Ruth hit a then-league record 29 home runs, a spectacular feat at that time.\n</p><p>This era was characterized by low-scoring games and a lack of <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a>. The lowest league run average in history was in 1908, when teams averaged only 3.4 runs per game.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" dead-ball era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead-ball_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Baseball 1900\xe2\x80\x931918 inclusive)</div></div></div>\n<p>During the dead-ball era, baseball was much more of a strategy-driven game, using a style of play now known as <a href="/wiki/Small_ball_(baseball)" title="Small ball (baseball)">small ball</a> or <a href="/wiki/Inside_Baseball_(strategy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inside Baseball (strategy)">inside baseball</a>. It relied much more on <a href="/wiki/Stolen_base" title="Stolen base">stolen bases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hit_and_run_(baseball)" title="Hit and run (baseball)">hit-and-run</a> types of plays than on <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> These strategies emphasized speed, perhaps by necessity.\n</p><p>Teams played in spacious ball parks that limited hitting for power, and, compared to modern baseballs, the ball used then was "dead" both by design and from overuse. Low-power hits like the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Chop" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Chop">Baltimore Chop</a>, developed in the 1890s by the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(19th_century)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Orioles (19th century)">Baltimore Orioles</a>, were used to get on base.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Once on base, a runner would often steal or be bunted over to second base and move to third base or score on a hit-and-run play. In no other era have teams stolen as many bases as in the dead-ball era.\n</p><p>On 13 occasions between <a href="/wiki/1900_Major_League_Baseball_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1900 Major League Baseball season">1900</a> and <a href="/wiki/1920_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1920 Major League Baseball season">1920</a>, the league leader in home runs had fewer than 10 home runs for the season, as opposed to just 4 where the league leaders had 20 or more homers. Meanwhile, there were 20 instances where the league leader in <a href="/wiki/Triple_(baseball)" title="Triple (baseball)">triples</a> had 20 or more.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> outfielder <a href="/wiki/Chief_Wilson" title="Chief Wilson">Owen "Chief" Wilson</a> set a record of 36 triples in 1912, a little-known record that is likely one of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Major_League_Baseball_records_considered_unbreakable#Most_triples_in_a_season_\xe2\x80\x93_36" title="List of Major League Baseball records considered unbreakable">baseball\'s unbreakable records</a>, as is that of the 309 career triples of <a href="/wiki/Sam_Crawford" title="Sam Crawford">Sam Crawford</a> set during this time.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite their speed, teams struggled to score during the dead-ball era. Major league cumulative batting averages ranged between .239 and .279 in the National League and between .239 and .283 in the American League. The lack of power in the game also meant lower <a href="/wiki/Slugging_average" class="mw-redirect" title="Slugging average">slugging averages</a> and on-base percentages, as pitchers could challenge hitters more without the threat of the long ball. The nadir of the dead-ball era was around <a href="/wiki/1907_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1907 Major League Baseball season">1907</a> and <a href="/wiki/1908_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1908 Major League Baseball season">1908</a>, with a league-wide batting average of .239, slugging average of .306, and ERA under 2.40. In the latter year, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> hit three home runs for the entire season, yet they finished 88\xe2\x80\x9364, just a couple of games from winning the pennant.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:365px;&#32;; background-color: lightgray;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9c ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9d ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><big>"This should prove that leather is mightier than wood".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></big></div>\n<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">\xe2\x80\x94White Sox Manager <a href="/wiki/Fielder_Jones" title="Fielder Jones">Fielder Jones</a>, after his 1906 "Hitless Wonders" won the World Series with a .230 club batting average</cite>\n</p>\n</div>\n<p>Some players and fans complained about the low-scoring games, and baseball sought to remedy the situation. In <a href="/wiki/1909_in_baseball" title="1909 in baseball">1909</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Shibe" title="Ben Shibe">Ben Shibe</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a>-centered ball, which the Reach Company\xe2\x80\x94official ball supplier to the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> (AL)\xe2\x80\x94began marketing.<sup id="cite_ref-Evolution_of_the_Ball_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evolution_of_the_Ball-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spalding_(sports_equipment)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spalding (sports equipment)">Spalding</a>, who supplied the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (NL), followed with its own cork-center ball.\n</p><p>The change in the ball dramatically affected play in both leagues.<sup id="cite_ref-Evolution_of_the_Ball_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evolution_of_the_Ball-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1910_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1910 Major League Baseball season">1910</a>, the American League batting average was .243; in 1911, it rose to .273. The National League saw a jump in the league batting average from .256 in 1910 to .272 in <a href="/wiki/1912_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1912 Major League Baseball season">1912</a>. <a href="/wiki/1911_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1911 Major League Baseball season">1911</a> happened to be the best season of <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a>\xe2\x80\x99s career; Cobb batted .420 with 248 hits. <a href="/wiki/Shoeless_Joe_Jackson" title="Shoeless Joe Jackson">Joe Jackson</a> hit .408 in 1911, and the next year Cobb batted .410. These were the only .400 averages between <a href="/wiki/1902_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1902 Major League Baseball season">1902</a> and <a href="/wiki/1919_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1919 Major League Baseball season">1919</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1913_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1913 Major League Baseball season">1913</a>, however, pitchers started to regain control, helped by a serendipitous invention by minor league pitcher <a href="/wiki/Russ_Ford" title="Russ Ford">Russ Ford</a>. Ford accidentally scuffed a baseball against a concrete wall, and after he threw it, noticed the pitch quickly dived as it reached the batter. The <a href="/wiki/Emery_pitch" class="mw-redirect" title="Emery pitch">emery pitch</a> was born. Soon pitchers not only had the dominating <a href="/wiki/Spitball" title="Spitball">spitball</a>; they had another pitch in their arsenal to control the batter, aided by the fact that the same ball was used throughout the game and almost never replaced. As play continued, the ball became increasingly scuffed. This made it harder to hit as it moved more during the pitch, and more difficult to see as it became dirtier. By <a href="/wiki/1914_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1914 Major League Baseball season">1914</a> run scoring was essentially back to the pre-1911 years and remained so until 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Such a lack of power in the game led to one of the more unusual player nicknames in history. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Baker" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Baker">Frank Baker</a>, one of the best players of the dead-ball era, earned the nickname of "Home Run" Baker merely for hitting two home runs in the <a href="/wiki/1911_World_Series" title="1911 World Series">1911 World Series</a>. Although Baker led the American League in home runs four times (1911\xe2\x80\x931914), his highest home run season was 1913, when he hit 12 home runs,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and he finished with 96 home runs for his career.\n</p><p>The best homerun hitter of the dead-ball era was <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> outfielder "Cactus" <a href="/wiki/Gavvy_Cravath" title="Gavvy Cravath">Gavvy Cravath</a>. Cravath led the National League in home runs six times, with a high total of 24 for the pennant-winning Phillies in <a href="/wiki/1915_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1915 Major League Baseball season">1915</a> and seasons of 19 home runs each in 1913 and 1914. Cravath, however, was aided by batting in the <a href="/wiki/Baker_Bowl" title="Baker Bowl">Baker Bowl</a>, a notoriously hitter-friendly park with only a short 280-foot (85&#160;m) distance from the plate to the right field wall.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributing_factors">Contributing factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead-ball_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contributing factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The following factors contributed to the dramatic decline in runs scored during the dead-ball era:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foul_strike_rule">Foul strike rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead-ball_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Foul strike rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The foul strike rule was a major rule change that, in just a few years, sent baseball from a high-scoring game to a game where scoring any runs was a struggle. Prior to this rule, foul balls did not count as strikes. Thus, a batter could foul off a countless number of pitches with no strikes counted against him\xe2\x80\x94except for bunt attempts. This gave the batter an enormous advantage. In 1901, the National League adopted the foul strike rule, and the American League followed suit in 1903.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ball_itself">Ball itself</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead-ball_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ball itself">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Before 1921, it was common for a baseball to be in play for over 100 pitches. Players used the same ball until it started to unravel. Early baseball leagues were very cost-conscious, so fans had to throw back balls that had been hit into the stands. The longer the ball was in play, the softer it became\xe2\x80\x94and hitting a heavily used, softer ball for distance is much more difficult than hitting a new, harder one. The ball itself was softer to begin with, probably making home runs less likely.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spitball">Spitball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead-ball_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spitball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The ball was also hard to hit because pitchers could manipulate it before a pitch. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Spitball" title="Spitball">spitball</a> pitch was permitted in baseball until 1921. Pitchers often marked the ball, scuffed it, spat on it\xe2\x80\x94anything they could to influence the ball\'s motion. This made the ball "dance" and curve much more than it does now, making it more difficult to hit. Tobacco juice was often added to the ball as well, which discolored it.  This made the ball difficult to see, especially since baseball parks did not have lights until the late 1930s. This made both hitting and fielding more difficult.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ballpark_size">Ballpark size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead-ball_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ballpark size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many ballparks were large by modern standards, such as the <a href="/wiki/West_Side_Grounds" class="mw-redirect" title="West Side Grounds">West Side Grounds</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>, which was 560 feet to the center field fence, and the <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Avenue_Grounds" title="Huntington Avenue Grounds">Huntington Avenue Grounds</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a>, which was 635 feet to the center field fence. The dimensions of <a href="/wiki/Braves_Field" title="Braves Field">Braves Field</a> prompted <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a> to say that no one would ever hit the ball out of it.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_era">End of the era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dead-ball_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: End of the era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The dead-ball era ended suddenly. By 1921, offenses were scoring 40% more runs and hitting four times as many home runs as they had in 1918. The abruptness of this change causes widespread debate among baseball historians, with no consensus on its cause.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Six popular theories have been advanced:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Changes in the ball:</i> This theory claims that owners replaced the ball with a newer, livelier ball (sometimes referred to as the "jackrabbit" ball), presumably with the intention of boosting offense and, by extension, ticket sales. The theory has been rebutted by Major League Baseball. The yarn used to wrap the core of the ball was changed prior to the 1920 season, although testing by the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">United States Bureau of Standards</a> found no difference in the physical properties of the two different types of balls. The so-called "livelier" ball was actually introduced in 1911, when the league began using a cork-centered ball, as opposed to rubber.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This led to an increase in hitting. The Major League runs per game jumped from 3.83 to 4.51. Major League batting average increased from .249 to .266, with two hitters, Ty Cobb and Shoeless Joe Jackson, hitting over .400 (.420 and .408, respectively). Most notably, the number of total home runs in 1911 went to 514, up from 361 the previous season.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Schulte" title="Frank Schulte">Frank Schulte</a> became the first player of the 20th century to reach twenty home runs in a season.</li>\n<li><i>Outlawing certain pitches:</i> Pitches now considered illegal, per MLB Rule 6.02(c), were outlawed.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This included the shine ball, emery ball, and <a href="/wiki/Spitball" title="Spitball">spitball</a> (a very effective pitch throughout the dead-ball era). This theory states that without such effective pitches in the pitcher\'s arsenal, batters gained an advantage. When the spitball was outlawed in 1920, MLB recognized seventeen pitchers who had nearly built their careers specializing in the spitball. They were permitted to continue using it for the rest of their careers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>More baseballs per game:</i> The fatal <a href="/wiki/Beanball" title="Beanball">beaning</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ray_Chapman" title="Ray Chapman">Ray Chapman</a> during the 1920 season led to a rule that the baseball must be replaced every time that it got dirty. With a clean ball in play at all times, players no longer had to contend with a ball that "traveled through the air erratically, tended to soften in the later innings, and as it came over the plate, was very hard to see."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>Stat keeping and rule changes:</i> In 1920, Major League Baseball adopted writer <a href="/wiki/Fred_Lieb" title="Fred Lieb">Fred Lieb</a>\'s proposal that a game-winning home run with men on base count as a home run, even if its run is not needed to win the game. Owners tried unsuccessfully to eliminate the <a href="/wiki/Intentional_walk" class="mw-redirect" title="Intentional walk">intentional walk</a>. They succeeded only in changing the rules to require that the catcher be within the catcher\'s box when the pitcher throws\xe2\x80\x9a and that everything that happened in a protested game was added to the game record. (From 1910 to 1919\xe2\x80\x9a records in protested games were excluded.)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>:</i> One theory is that the prolific success of Babe Ruth at hitting home runs led players around the league to forsake their old methods of hitting (described above) and adopt a "free-swinging" style designed to hit the ball hard and with an uppercut stroke, with the intention of hitting more home runs. Critics of this theory claim that it doesn\'t account for the improvement in batting averages from 1918 to 1921, over which time the league average improved from .254 to .291.</li>\n<li><i>Ballpark dimensions:</i> This theory contends that offensive success came from changes in the dimensions of the ballparks. Accurate estimates of ballpark sizes of the era can be difficult to find, however, so there is disagreement over whether the dimensions changed at all, let alone whether the change led to improved offense. A 1920 season rule change stated that balls hit over the fence in fair territory but landing foul were fair, and hence home runs rather than foul balls. This rule change greatly pleased hitters for both New York City teams,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> whose many "hooking" home runs were called foul in the <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
