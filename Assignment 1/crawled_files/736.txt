He is being greeted by the <i>ollamh r\xc3\xacgh</i>, the royal poet, who is addressing him with the proclamation "Benach De Re Albanne" (= <i>Beannachd do R\xc3\xacgh Albann</i>, "Blessings to the King of Scotland"); the poet goes on to recite Alexander\'s genealogy.</div></div></div>\n<p><b><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">Alba</i></b> is the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> name (<small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA/Scottish Gaelic">[\xcb\x88al\xcc\xaa\xcb\xa0ap\xc9\x99]</a></span>) for <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> with the Irish term <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Alba</i></i> (gen. <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Albann</i></i>, dat. <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Albainn</i></i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a> term <i><i lang="gv" title="Manx language text">Nalbin</i></i>, the two other <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Goidelic</a> <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic languages</a>, as well as contemporary words used in <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> (<i><i lang="kw" title="Cornish language text">Alban</i></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> (<i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Yr Alban</i></i>), both of which are <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic</a> Insular Celtic languages. (The third surviving Brythonic language, <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>, instead uses <i><i lang="br" title="Breton language text">Bro-Skos</i></i>, meaning \'country of the Scots\'.) In the past these terms were names for <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> as a whole, related to the Brythonic name <a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term first appears in classical texts as <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbb\xce\xb2\xce\xaf\xcf\x89\xce\xbd</span> <i><i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek-language transliteration">Alb\xc3\xadon</i></i>  or <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbb\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\x90\xcf\x89\xce\xbd</span> <i><i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek-language transliteration">Alou\xc3\xadon</i></i> (in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s writings in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>), and later as <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a></i></i> in Latin documents. Historically, the term refers to Britain as a whole and is ultimately based on the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Indo-European</a> root for "white".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It later came to be used by Gaelic speakers in the form of <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">Alba</i></i> (<a href="/wiki/Dative" class="mw-redirect" title="Dative">dative</a> <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Albainn</i></i>, <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a> <i><i lang="mga" title="Middle Irish language text">Albann</i></i>, now obsolete) as the name given to the former kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> which when first used in this sense (around the time of king <a href="/wiki/Causant%C3%ADn_mac_%C3%81eda" class="mw-redirect" title="Causant\xc3\xadn mac \xc3\x81eda">Causant\xc3\xadn mac \xc3\x81eda</a> (Constantine II, 943\xe2\x80\x93952)) had expanded. The region <a href="/wiki/Breadalbane,_Scotland" title="Breadalbane, Scotland">Breadalbane</a> (<i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">Br\xc3\xa0ghad Albann</i></i>, the upper part of "Alba") takes its name from it as well. The Pictish, and later Scottish, Kings were crowned at the seat on Moot Hill Scone. It was this stone which was taken to Westminster Abbey and used in Coronations for the monarchs of the United Kingdom.  \n</p><p>As time passed that kingdom incorporated others to the southern territories. It became re-<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinized</a> in the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Medieval</a> period as "Albania" (it is unclear whether it may ultimately share the same <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymon</a> as the modern <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>). This latter word was employed mainly by Celto-Latin writers, and most famously by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>. It was this word which passed into <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> as <a href="/wiki/Albany_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Albany (disambiguation)">Albany</a>, although very rarely was this used for the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a>, but rather for the notional <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Albany" title="Duke of Albany">Duchy of Albany</a>. It is from the latter that <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany</a>, the capital of the US state of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Albany,_Western_Australia" title="Albany, Western Australia">Albany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> take their names.\n</p><p>It also appears in the anglicised literary form of <i>Albyn</i>, as in <a href="/wiki/Byron" class="mw-redirect" title="Byron">Byron</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Childe_Harold" class="mw-redirect" title="Childe Harold">Childe Harold</a></i>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>And wild and high the \'Cameron\'s gathering\' rose,</i>\n<dl><dd><i>The war-note of Lochiel, which Albyn\'s hills</i></dd></dl></dd>\n<dd><i>Have heard, and heard, too, have her Saxon foes</i></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_uses">Modern uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Britain)">A7</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/F%C3%A1ilte" title="F\xc3\xa1ilte">F\xc3\xa0ilte</a> gu Alba</i></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Belgian" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian">Belgian</a> <a A new version of <a href="/wiki/Runrig" title="Runrig">Runrig</a>\'s song <i>Alba</i> (originally on their album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cutter_and_the_Clan" title="The Cutter and the Clan">The Cutter and the Clan</a></i>) was featured on the channel\'s launch.\n</p><p>In the mid-1990s, the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_League_(political_organisation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic League (political organisation)">Celtic League</a> started a campaign to have the word "Alba" on the Scottish <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby</a> tops. Since 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Football_Association" title="Scottish Football Association">SFA</a> have supported the use of Scottish Gaelic by adding <i>Alba</i> on the back of the official team strip.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Rugby_Union" title="Scottish Rugby Union">SRU</a> is still being lobbied to have <i>Alba</i> added to the national rugby union strip.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2007, the then <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Executive">Scottish Executive</a> re-branded itself as "The Scottish Government" and started to use a bilingual logo with the Gaelic name <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">Riaghaltas na h-Alba</i></i>. However, the Gaelic version from the outset had always been <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">Riaghaltas na h-Alba</i></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, likewise, uses the Gaelic name <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">P\xc3\xa0rlamaid na h-Alba</i></i>.\n</p><p>A new welcome sign on the historic <a href="/wiki/A7_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A7 road (Great Britain)">A7</a> route into Scotland was erected in 2009, with the text <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text"><a href="/wiki/F%C3%A0ilte" class="mw-redirect" title="F\xc3\xa0ilte">F\xc3\xa0ilte</a> gu Alba</i></i>.\n</p><p>Phrases such as <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text"><a href="/wiki/Alba_gu_br%C3%A0th" title="Alba gu br\xc3\xa0th">Alba gu br\xc3\xa0th</a></i></i> may be used as a catch-phrase or rallying cry. It was used in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Braveheart" title="Braveheart">Braveheart</a></i> as <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace" title="William Wallace">William Wallace</a> encouraged the troops at the
