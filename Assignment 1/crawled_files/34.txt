Tidenham</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Wye Valley</b> <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB; <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Dyffryn Gwy</i>) is an internationally important protected landscape straddling the border between <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. It is one of the most dramatic and scenic landscapes in Britain.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">River Wye</a> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Afon Gwy</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Rivers_of_Great_Britain#Longest_rivers_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Rivers of Great Britain">fifth-longest river</a> in the United Kingdom. The upper part passes through <a href="/wiki/Rhayader" title="Rhayader">Rhayader</a>, <a href="/wiki/Builth_Wells" title="Builth Wells">Builth Wells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hay-on-Wye" title="Hay-on-Wye">Hay-on-Wye</a>, but the area designated as an AONB covers 326 square kilometres (126&#160;sq&#160;mi) surrounding a 72-kilometre (45&#160;mi) stretch lower down the river, from just south of <a href="/wiki/Hereford" title="Hereford">Hereford</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chepstow" title="Chepstow">Chepstow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This area covers parts of the counties of <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire" title="Monmouthshire">Monmouthshire</a>, and is recognised in particular for its <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> <a href="/wiki/Gorge" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorge">gorge</a> scenery and dense native woodlands, as well as its wildlife, archaeological and industrial remains. It is also historically important as one of the birthplaces of the modern tourism industry. The area is predominantly rural, and many people make a living from tourism, agriculture or forestry. <a href="/wiki/Ross-on-Wye" title="Ross-on-Wye">Ross-on-Wye</a> is the only town within the AONB itself, but Hereford, <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coleford,_Gloucestershire" title="Coleford, Gloucestershire">Coleford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chepstow" title="Chepstow">Chepstow</a> lie just outside its boundaries.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The varied landscapes of the Wye Valley can be explained by underlying rocks and structures, and how ice and then the river and tributary streams have acted upon them through time.\n</p><p>Close to Hereford, the geology of the area around the village of <a href="/wiki/Woolhope" title="Woolhope">Woolhope</a> is largely made up of <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a>. To the south of this, the Herefordshire lowlands are largely underlain by red <a href="/wiki/Mudstone" title="Mudstone">mudstones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a>, producing a redder soil. These rocks are softer than the limestones elsewhere, so the river created more <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meanders</a>, a wider <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplain</a>, and a gentler and more rolling landscape. Around <a href="/wiki/Symonds_Yat" title="Symonds Yat">Symonds Yat</a>, limestones and red sandstones meet. This leads to a landscape of hills and plains, as well as substantial meanders which have formed impressive river cliffs.\n</p><p>The Lower Wye landscape was formed by the river acting on a series of layers of rock that dip towards the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a>. Here the river has incised into the margins of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a> plateau to form a gorge with substantial river cliffs. The steepest parts of the Wye gorge are cut through the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous_Limestone" title="Carboniferous Limestone">Carboniferous Limestone</a>. Here the combined action of the river, natural joints in the rocks and quarrying have exposed many vertical faces, particularly between <a href="/wiki/Tintern" title="Tintern">Tintern</a> and Chepstow.\n</p><p>Geological interest extends underground, and there are many <a href="/wiki/Rock_shelters" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock shelters">rock shelters</a> and solution <a href="/wiki/Caves" class="mw-redirect" title="Caves">caves</a> in the area. These include King Arthur\'s Cave and many others in the area of Symonds Yat and <a href="/wiki/Slaughter_Stream_Cave" title="Slaughter Stream Cave">Slaughter Stream Cave</a> near Berry Hill. At <a href="/wiki/St_Arvans" class="mw-redirect" title="St Arvans">St Arvans</a>, near Chepstow, the underground watercourses have carved out long cave systems, which exit at <a href="/wiki/Otter_Hole" title="Otter Hole">Otter Hole</a> at the base of Piercefield cliffs&#160;&#8211;&#32;the only cave system in England or Wales which can only be reached through a tidal sump, making it a <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">mecca</a> for experienced cavers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_and_nature_reserves">Wildlife and nature reserves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wildlife and nature reserves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Lancaut" title="Lancaut">Lancaut</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and Ban-y-Gor<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> are <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_Wildlife_Trust" title="Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust">Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust</a> nature reserves. They both have <a href="/wiki/SSSI" class="mw-redirect" title="SSSI">SSSI</a> status being part of the nationally important <a href="/wiki/Lower_Wye_Gorge_SSSI" title="Lower Wye Gorge SSSI">Lower Wye Gorge SSSI</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and within the Wye Valley <a href="/wiki/AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="AONB">AONB</a>. The Wye Valley is important for its rich wildlife habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The area has three sites of international importance, designated as <a href="/wiki/Special_Areas_of_Conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Areas of Conservation">Special Areas of Conservation</a> (SACs) under the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Habitats_Directive" title="Habitats Directive">Habitats Directive</a>. These are the <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">River Wye</a> (<i>Afon Gwy</i>), the Wye Valley and Forest of Dean Bat Sites (<i>Safleoedd Ystlumod Dyffryn Gwy a Fforest y Ddena</i>) and the Wye Valley Woodlands (<i>Coetiroedd Dyffryn Gwy</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It supports a population of <a href="/wiki/Lesser_horseshoe_bat" title="Lesser horseshoe bat">lesser horseshoe bats</a>, a growing population of <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcons" class="mw-redirect" title="Peregrine falcons">peregrine falcons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Goshawk">goshawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raven" title="Raven">ravens</a>, rare <a href="/wiki/Whitebeam" title="Whitebeam">whitebeam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nightjar" title="Nightjar">nightjar</a> and lesser known fish like the <a href="/wiki/Shad" class="mw-redirect" title="Shad">shad</a> and twaite. The main Welsh populations of the small but colourful <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moth</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Oecophora_bractella" title="Oecophora bractella">Oecophora bractella</a></i> are found here. \n</p><p>In September 2006 it was reported that one colony of lesser horseshoe bats in the area had reached record numbers, with some 890 bats in a small stone barn (599 adults and 291 babies recorded).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; overflow:hidden; width:auto; max-width:708px;">\n<div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;"><a href="/wiki/File:River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg" class="image" title="River Wye at Lancaut looking towards Wintour&#39;s Leap"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg/700px-River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg" width="700" height="225" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg/1050px-River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg/1400px-River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg 2x" data-file-width="9717" data-file-height="3128" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="text-align:center"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:River_Wye_Lancat_and_Ban_y_Gore_Nature_Reserve.jpg" title="File:River Wye Lancat and Ban y Gore Nature Reserve.jpg"> </a></div>River Wye at Lancaut looking towards Wintour\'s Leap</div>\n</div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The valley has been inhabited for at least 12,000 years. Caves near <a href="/wiki/Symonds_Yat" title="Symonds Yat">Symonds Yat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chepstow" title="Chepstow">Chepstow</a> provide evidence of settlement dating from <a href="/wiki/Palaeolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeolithic">Palaeolithic</a> times, and finds from later stone ages such as the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> have also been found. These have yielded evidence of how <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> human populations lived as nomadic hunters and traders.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Standing_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing stones">Standing stones</a> at <a href="/wiki/Huntsham" title="Huntsham">Huntsham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staunton,_near_Coleford,_Gloucestershire" title="Staunton, near Coleford, Gloucestershire">Staunton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trellech" title="Trellech">Trellech</a> all have origins dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. Later, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> forts along the lower Wye Valley, and in the Woolhope area, took advantage of the natural hilltops and promontories to form well-defended settlements. It is likely that many of these marked the edges of disputed <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">tribal pre-Roman</a> territories.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Watling_Street" title="Watling Street">Watling Street</a> ran through the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> settlements of <a href="/wiki/Ariconium" title="Ariconium">Ariconium</a> (just north of modern <a href="/wiki/Ross-on-Wye" title="Ross-on-Wye">Ross-on-Wye</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Blestium" title="Blestium">Blestium</a> (Monmouth), and a number of other small Roman settlements are known. The first evidence of the exploitation of iron and coal in the valley is found in the Roman period, with iron working known from sites at <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a>, Trellech and elsewhere, as well as in the adjoining <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a>. The medieval boroughs of <a href="/wiki/Goodrich,_Herefordshire" title="Goodrich, Herefordshire">Goodrich</a> and Chepstow, at each end of the Wye Gorge, may have originally been established at this time.\n</p><p>Closely following the River Wye, <a href="/wiki/Offa%27s_Dyke" title="Offa&#39;s Dyke">Offa\'s Dyke</a> was built in the 8th century under <a href="/wiki/King_Offa" class="mw-redirect" title="King Offa">King Offa</a> to mark out the boundary between England and Wales and is, today, the longest archaeological monument in Britain. <a href="/wiki/Offa%27s_Dyke_Path" title="Offa&#39;s Dyke Path">Offa\'s Dyke Path</a> <a href="/wiki/Long_distance_footpath" class="mw-redirect" title="Long distance footpath">long distance footpath</a> today traces the route through the Wye valley on the English bank, while the <a href="/wiki/Wye_Valley_Walk" title="Wye Valley Walk">Wye Valley Walk</a> follows the Welsh bank.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_medieval_period">The medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> conquered the region in the 11th century they immediately built major <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castles</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chepstow_Castle" title="Chepstow Castle">Chepstow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a> to defend the territory. Smaller castles were built at <a href="/wiki/St_Briavels" title="St Briavels">St Briavels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruardean" title="Ruardean">Ruardean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goodrich_Castle" title="Goodrich Castle">Goodrich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilton_Castle" title="Wilton Castle">Wilton Castle</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tintern_Abbey" title="Tintern Abbey">Tintern Abbey</a> was founded in 1131 by <a href="/wiki/Cistercian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> monks, and largely rebuilt in the 13th century. It is the best-preserved <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbey</a> in Wales and an outstanding example of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic architecture</a>.\n</p><p>Many of the smaller villages in the area probably date from the Middle Ages, and much of this expansion was probably associated with the early iron industry. The medieval iron industry consumed large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> and much of the woodland was <a href="/wiki/Coppice" class="mw-redirect" title="Coppice">coppiced</a> for this purpose. Trellech was one of the largest communities in Wales during this period.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_development_of_industry">The development of industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The development of industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Iron has been made in the Wye Valley since <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times, using the ready supply of timber, good quality ore and abundant <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a>. The river provided transport for the raw materials and finished product, and with the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> in the 16th century, its tributaries began to be used for water power.\n</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> made in Britain was founded at <a href="/wiki/Tintern" title="Tintern">Tintern</a> in 1566. <a href="/wiki/Wire" title="Wire">Wire</a>-making followed, with water mills situated on all the tributaries of the lower Wye. The area resounded to the noise and smoke of heavy industry for the next 400 years and gave rise to many pioneering industries. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Whitebrook" title="Whitebrook">Whitebrook</a> became famous for paper milling, when <a href="/wiki/Wallpaper" title="Wallpaper">wallpaper</a> became a fashionable way to decorate houses. At <a href="/wiki/Redbrook" title="Redbrook">Redbrook</a>, copper works were established by 1691, and a century later the village became one of the world\'s major <a href="/wiki/Tinplate" title="Tinplate">tinplate</a> manufacturing centres. This industry survived until the 1960s and was renowned for producing the thinnest, highest quality plate in the world. The <a href="/wiki/Lydbrook" title="Lydbrook">Lydbrook</a> valley was also a thriving centre for metal industries, such as the manufacture of telegraph cables.\n</p><p>The valley woodlands were carefully managed to produce mature trees for <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">shipbuilding</a>, or by coppicing for <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, and to provide bark for <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tanning</a>. The valley industries were also massive consumers of timber. A ship of 150 tons, for example, required 3,000 wagonloads of timber to complete \xe2\x80\x93 and in 1824, 13 ships were launched at <a href="/wiki/Brockweir" title="Brockweir">Brockweir</a> alone.\n</p><p>The river was the economic backbone of the region, providing an important means of transport, trade and communication. In late medieval times, <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> weirs hindered free passage on the river, but the Wye Navigation Act in 1662 enabled the river\'s potential to be developed. By 1727 shallow draught boats could get upstream beyond Hereford, and a significant shipbuilding industry developed at Monmouth, <a href="/wiki/Llandogo" title="Llandogo">Llandogo</a>, Brockweir and Chepstow. However, by 1835 it was stated that the Wye <i>"can scarcely be considered a commercial highway"</i> above Monmouth, and by the 1880s Brockweir bridge was the effective upper limit of navigation.\n</p><p>As the 19th century progressed, the valley\'s industries gradually declined, and management of the woodlands lessened when there was no longer a ready market for their products.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_origins_of_British_tourism">The origins of British tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The origins of British tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wye_Tour" title="Wye Tour">Wye Tour</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turner_Tintern1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Turner_Tintern1.jpg/250px-Turner_Tintern1.jpg" width="250" height="349" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Turner_Tintern1.jpg/375px-Turner_Tintern1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Turner_Tintern1.jpg/500px-Turner_Tintern1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1099" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turner_Tintern1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Chancel and Crossing of <a href="/wiki/Tintern_Abbey" title="Tintern Abbey">Tintern Abbey</a>, Looking towards the East Window</i> by <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a>, 1794</div></div></div>\n<p>The Wye Valley witnessed the birth of British tourism in the 18th century. The earliest known appreciation of the area\'s spectacular beauty can be dated to the beginning of the century, when <a href="/wiki/John_Kyrle" title="John Kyrle">John Kyrle</a> developed the \'Prospect\' at <a href="/wiki/Ross-on-Wye" title="Ross-on-Wye">Ross-on-Wye</a>, and it was later mentioned in verse by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>.\n</p><p>In 1745, <a href="/wiki/John_Egerton_(bishop)" title="John Egerton (bishop)">John Egerton</a>, later <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a>, started taking friends on boat trips down the valley from the rectory at Ross. The area became more widely known following the publication of works by the poet <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gray" title="Thomas Gray">Thomas Gray</a>, and, in particular, <i>Observations on the River Wye</i> by the Reverend <a href="/wiki/William_Gilpin_(clergyman)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Gilpin (clergyman)">William Gilpin</a>, published in 1782. The first illustrated tour guide to be published in Britain, it helped travellers locate and enjoy the most "<a href="/wiki/Picturesque" title="Picturesque">Picturesque</a>" aspects of the countryside. Regular excursions began to be established from Ross, the boat journey to <a href="/wiki/Chepstow" title="Chepstow">Chepstow</a> taking two days.\n</p><p>Some of the most famous poets, writers and artists of the day made the pilgrimage to the great sights of Goodrich, <a href="/wiki/Tintern" title="Tintern">Tintern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chepstow" title="Chepstow">Chepstow</a>&#160;&#8211;&#32;among them <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Coleridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Makepeace_Thackeray" title="William Makepeace Thackeray">Thackeray</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">Turner</a>. <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">Wordsworth</a> was also captivated by the area, writing <i><a href="/wiki/Lines_written_a_few_miles_above_Tintern_Abbey" title="Lines written a few miles above Tintern Abbey">Lines written a few miles above Tintern Abbey</a></i> in 1798. Poetic influence continued to be felt in the next century, as in 1811, popular \'peasant poet\', <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bloomfield" title="Robert Bloomfield">Robert Bloomfield</a> wrote \'The Banks of Wye; a Poem in Four Books\' providing account of an 1807 trip made by him and a party of friends down the <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">River Wye</a> and surrounding areas.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first of Britain\'s great landscapes to be \'discovered\', the Wye Valley\'s particular attraction was its river scenery, and the many guidebooks, engravings and paintings ensured a continuing steady stream of visitors. Viewpoints were specially constructed, including <a href="/wiki/The_Kymin" title="The Kymin">the Kymin</a> above <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a>, with its round house giving panoramic views across the town. Another highlight for travellers was the cliff ascent and walks at <a href="/wiki/Piercefield_House" title="Piercefield House">Piercefield</a>. However, most of the truly \'Picturesque\' scenes were sketched from river level, with the shimmering water as the foreground for the forests and cliffs behind, and the castle and abbey ruins.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Tramroad linked <a href="/wiki/Coalway" title="Coalway">Coalway</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Coleford,_Gloucestershire" title="Coleford, Gloucestershire">Coleford</a>), <a href="/wiki/Redbrook" This was the world\'s first railed-way to make specific powers in its Act of Parliament to allow for the charging of fares to passengers.\n</p><p>The standard gauge <a href="/wiki/Wye_Valley_Railway" title="Wye Valley Railway">Wye Valley Railway</a> line between Chepstow, Monmouth and Ross opened much later, in 1876. This made the valley more accessible and popular to tourists. In the early 20th century, crowds of up to 1300 would travel on a special train journey to see Tintern Abbey on the night of the harvest moon. The line closed to passengers in 1959, although sections remain as bridleways and footbridges.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> There are now main line railway stations at <a href="/wiki/Hereford_railway_station" title="Hereford railway station">Hereford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chepstow_railway_station" title="Chepstow railway station">Chepstow</a>.\n</p><p>The road network in the lower Wye valley remained essentially undeveloped during the rise of the Valley\'s industrialisation, until a series of <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Turnpike trust">Turnpike trusts</a> were authorised during the 18th century. It was not until 1828 that the current Wye Valley road, the <a href="/wiki/A466_road" title="A466 road">A466</a>, was first constructed.  The area became more accessible by road to much of the country with the building of the <a href="/wiki/M50_motorway_(Great_Britain)" title="M50 motorway (Great Britain)">M50</a> between the <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5</a> and Ross-on-Wye, and the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Bridge" title="Severn Bridge">Severn Bridge</a> (now part of the <a href="/wiki/M48_motorway" title="M48 motorway">M48</a>) in 1966.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_of_the_area">Management of the area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Management of the area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div id="mwe_player_0" class="PopUpMediaTransform" style="width:220px;" videopayload="&lt;div class=&quot;mediaContainer&quot; The designation indicates the importance of recognising and preserving the area\'s distinctive qualities, for the benefit of present and future generations.\nAdministratively the area is very complex, being the only protected landscape to straddle the border between England and Wales. The Counties of Herefordshire, Monmouthshire and Gloucestershire are each in a different government region.\nCo-ordination of conservation across these political boundaries is undertaken by an AONB unit and Joint Advisory Committee. A Management Plan for the AONB enlists a range of partners in conserving and enhancing its beauty for the benefit of present and future generations.\n</p><p>The navigation of the tidal part of the Wye (below <a href="/wiki/Bigsweir_Bridge" title="Bigsweir Bridge">Bigsweir</a>) comes under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Harbour_Trustees" title="Gloucester Harbour Trustees">Gloucester Harbour Trustees</a> as Competent Harbour Authority.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Beer_in_Wales" title="Beer in Wales">Kingstone Brewery</a>, Meadow Farm, Tintern</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Wye_Valley_Brewery" title="Wye Valley Brewery">Wye Valley Brewery</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Wye_Valley_Walk" title="Wye Valley Walk">Wye Valley Walk</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_places_in_the_Wye_Valley" title="List of places in the Wye Valley">List of places in the Wye Valley</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wye_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
