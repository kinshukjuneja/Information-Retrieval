Its position on the geographical periphery of the island of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> is also a factor.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> shares some of the political issues of the other <a href="/wiki/Celtic_nations" title="Celtic nations">Celtic nations</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and a notable movement exists seeking <a href="/wiki/Cornish_self-government_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish self-government movement">greater powers of self-government</a> within the UK, similar to that achieved in Wales.  Cornish politics is also defined by its historical relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> (and formerly the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cornwall\'s politics have partly been dictated by its geography and history. It is a peninsula, relatively distant from London, and its main industries \xe2\x80\x93 fishing, farming and various kinds of mining, have been in decline for a long time.  Cornwall\'s GDP still remains low.  However, Cornwall is attractive to tourists, and to people seeking to move into the area to live.  There are therefore tensions in the housing market between the demands of inward migrants to the area, and the needs of local people.\n</p><p>Historically, Cornwall was a <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">Brittonic</a>-speaking area separate from the rest of England until about the 10th century, and retained much of its cultural distinctiveness in later centuries. Religious non-conformism was strong in Cornwall, and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> was less well supported than some areas to the east. This has continued to inform later Cornish politics, in the form of Liberalism, now represented mainly by the Liberal Democrats can be traced to historical associations with Liberalism and non-conformist religion, particularly <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a>, in the 19th century and similarly land ownership and the Conservative party in the same period. The Conservative Party is also fairly strong in Cornwall, but for slightly different reasons. They suffered a particularly bad setback in the 1990s. However they regained three of the six Cornish seats in the 2010 general election. The Labour Party is traditionally much weaker in Cornwall than many other parts of the UK, although it has had some representation locally. This may be partly because there is no major urban centre in Cornwall \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> tends to fulfil that role. Cornwall also traditionally elects a number of independent councillors, and is a centre for the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK,_1989)" title="Liberal Party (UK, 1989)">rump Liberal Party</a> in the UK.\n</p><p>The distinctive nature of Cornish politics has led to a significant number of office holders from both the Liberal Democrats and the Conservative Party being supportive of greater Cornish autonomy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, with both parties this has never been official national policy. 1986 saw the death of the charismatic MP <a href="/wiki/David_Penhaligon" title="David Penhaligon">David Penhaligon</a> of the (then) <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>. Penhaligon\'s career looked promising, with some tipping him as a future leader. In a speech he made in support of the Cornish miners at <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a>, he said:\n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">\xe2\x80\x9c\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">You need more in an economy than just tourism, icecream and deckchairs. Our mining industry is not a figment of the last decade or the last two decades. It has occupied Cornishmen and it has produced wealth for this century, the previous century and probably the last two thousand years; and what we\xe2\x80\x99re asking the government to do is to recognise the great contribution we have made for the wealth of Britain, and in this time of great trial and tribulation to come to our assistance \xe2\x80\x93 that\'s what we\xe2\x80\x99re asking our government to do.\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">\xe2\x80\x9d\n</td></tr>\n\n</tbody></table>\n<p>Cornwall\'s distinctiveness as a <i>national</i>, as opposed to regional, minority has been periodically recognised by major British papers. For example, a <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">Guardian</a></i> editorial in 1990 pointed to these differences, and warned that they should be constitutionally recognised:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"<i>Smaller minorities also have equally proud visions of themselves as irreducibly <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a>, Irish, Manx or Cornish. These identities are </i>distinctly national<i> in ways which </i>proud people from <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a><i>, much less proud people from <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> will never know. Any new constitutional settlement </i>which ignores these factors<i> will be built on uneven ground.</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>At a local level the tradition of Independent candidates and councillors is far stronger than most other areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a> was formed in 1951, initially as a pressure group. Some of its members and supporters were politicians (councillors and MPs) from the three main British political parties, but later on, it became a party in its own right and members of other parties left. Its most famous supporter of the time was the novelist <a href="/wiki/Daphne_du_Maurier" title="Daphne du Maurier">Daphne du Maurier</a>. One of Mebyon Kernow\'s main campaigns is for a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Assembly" title="Cornish Assembly">Cornish Assembly</a> and in 2001 it presented a petition to <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a> with 50,000 signatures in favour of the Assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This was backed up by Cornwall Council\'s Feb 2003 <a href="/wiki/MORI" class="mw-redirect" title="MORI">MORI</a> poll which showed 55% in favour of an elected, fully devolved regional assembly for Cornwall and 13% against.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (Previous result 46% in favour in 2002).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Growing dissatisfaction with <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> fishing policy including the <a href="/wiki/Common_Fisheries_Policy" title="Common Fisheries Policy">Common Fisheries Policy</a> has recently led to a growth of support for <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Independence_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Independence Party">United Kingdom Independence Party</a> (UKIP) within fishing ports such as <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a>. On 26 July 2007 the Conservative party appointed <a href="/wiki/Mark_Prisk" title="Mark Prisk">Mark Prisk</a> (Member of Parliament for <a href="/wiki/Hertford_and_Stortford_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Hertford and Stortford (UK Parliament constituency)">Hertford and Stortford</a>) "<a href="/wiki/Shadow_Minister_for_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Shadow Minister for Cornwall">Shadow Minister for Cornwall</a>". This appointment was called "the fictional minister for Cornwall," by a Liberal Democrat MP, as there was no government minister to shadow.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The post was not continued following the 2010 election, and no longer exists. This was to put the Duchy\'s concerns "at the heart of Conservative thinking", according to a party statement. Conservative Party leader <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> said he wholeheartedly endorsed the appointment and it would ensure that the voice of Cornwall is heard.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, as the post has been discontinued, this concept remains unproven.\n</p><p>In the June 2016 EU referendum, Cornwall voted decisively to leave the EU, 43.5per cent (140,540) of Cornwall\'s voters wanted to remain in the EU, while 56.5per cent (182,665) voted to leave.<sup id="cite_ref-5" Before April 2009, Cornwall was a non-metropolitan county that followed the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Administrative divisions of England">three tier system</a> typical of English <a href="/wiki/Shire_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Shire county">shire counties</a>, and had five <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">districts</a>: <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerrier" title="Kerrier">Kerrier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carrick,_Cornwall" title="Carrick, Cornwall">Carrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caradon" title="Caradon">Caradon</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall" title="North Cornwall">North Cornwall</a>, and one <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">borough</a> <a href="/wiki/Restormel" title="Restormel">Restormel</a>. Cornwall Council provides a wide range of services to more than half a million residents, has an annual budget of more than \xc2\xa31 billion, and is the biggest employer in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, which are part of the <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial county</a> of Cornwall, are not part of the Cornwall unitary authority, as they have their own unitary council.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Cornwall_Council_election_results.2C_4_June_2009"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornwall_Council_election_results,_4_June_2009">Cornwall Council election results, 4 June 2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cornwall Council election results, 4 June 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council_election,_2009" title="Cornwall Council election, 2009">Cornwall Council election, 2009</a></div>\n<p>Elections for the new unitary Cornwall Council were held on 4 June 2009, and 123 members were elected, replacing the previous 82 councillors of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_County_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornwall County Council">Cornwall County Council</a> and also another 249 (some of whom were also county councillors) on the six district councils.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At the 2009 elections, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> lost overall control of Cornwall, with no single party gaining overall control of the new council. Although the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> now have the largest number of elected members, they do not have a majority.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Conservatives received 34% of the vote and won 50 seats, followed by the Liberal Democrats on 28% winning 38 seats, the <a href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent (politician)">Independents</a> 23% and 32 seats, and <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a> 4% and three seats. The turnout was 41%. Labour, the Green Party, UKIP and the BNP failed to secure any seats in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Cornwall_Council_election_results.2C_May_2013"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornwall_Council_election_results,_May_2013">Cornwall Council election results, May 2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cornwall Council election results, May 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council_election,_2013" title="Cornwall Council election, 2013">Cornwall Council election, 2013</a></div>\n<p>The council remained as "no overall control", with the Independent politicians becoming the largest grouping on the council through a modest gain of councillors from the previous election. The Liberal Democrats remained the second largest party after losing 2 councillors and the Conservatives slipped to third after losing over a third of their councillors. The Labour Party (+8), UKIP (+6), and the Green Party (+1) all gained seats, with UKIP and the Greens entering Cornwall Council for the first time. Mebyon Kernow had 6 councillors prior to the election, having added 2 since the 2009 election, their total following the election was reduced to 4.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Cornwall_Council_election_results.2C_May_2017"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornwall_Council_election_results,_May_2017">Cornwall Council election results, May 2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cornwall Council election results, May 2017">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council_election,_2017" title="Cornwall Council election, 2017">Cornwall Council election, 2017</a></div>\n<p>The Conservative Party became the largest group on Cornwall Council, with 46 seats achieving 35% of the overall vote. The Liberal Democrats became the second largest party, with 37 seats, with the Independents in third place with 30.  (Conservative\t46 seats (+18) Liberal Democrat 38 seats (-7) Independent 30 seats (-4) Labour 5 seats  (-2) Mebyon Kernow 4 seats (NC) Green 0 seats (-1) UKIP 0 seats\t(-1) Other 0 seats (-5) )<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_nationalism">Cornish nationalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cornish nationalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_nationalism" title="Cornish nationalism">Cornish nationalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall" title="Constitutional status of Cornwall">Constitutional status of Cornwall</a></div>\n<p>Two of the major factors in Cornish nationalism are the disputed position of Cornwall as separate constitutional entity within the UK and the rights of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a> as a minority.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> These issues affect all of those involved in Cornish politics, even those who are at odds with these ideas.  Three UK political parties recognise the cause of Cornish self-determination, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK,_1989)" title="Liberal Party (UK, 1989)">Liberal Party</a>, the Cornish section of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Britain" title="Communist Party of Britain">Communist Party of Britain</a>. However, in the 2015 general election, the Liberal party in Cornwall withdrew its candidates, and encouraged its supporters to vote <a href="/wiki/UKIP" class="mw-redirect" title="UKIP">UKIP</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/An_Gof" title="An Gof">An Gof</a> have also expressed Cornish nationalism, though not in political ways as much as violent ones.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Cornish_nationalist_and.2For_regionalist_organisations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_nationalist_and/or_regionalist_organisations">Cornish nationalist and/or regionalist organisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cornish nationalist and/or regionalist organisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a></div>\n<p>The principal political party in the Cornish Nationalist movement is <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a> which labels itself as a \'centre-left, green and decentralist party\' and has close association with its sister party <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> in Wales. Mebyon Kernow\'s membership is calculated at around 1,000 members across Cornwall. In 1979, in the first elections to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, Mebyon Kernow won almost 10% (over 10,000 votes) of the vote in the Cornwall seat. This reflected a decade of steady growth for the party. MK continues to contest parliamentary seats and also local government seats.\n</p><p>In the 2015 general election, Mebyon Kernow fielded candidates in each of the constituencies in Cornwall.\n</p>\n<ul><li>North Cornwall:  6th, (out of 7) 1.3% of votes, +0.2%</li>\n<li>South Cornwall: 6th (out of 7) 2% of votes, +0.7%</li>\n<li>Truro and Falmouth: 7th (out of 9) 1.1% of votes, \xe2\x88\x921%</li>\n<li>Camborne and Redruth: (last place)  2% of votes +0.1%</li>\n<li>St Ives 7th (last place) 1.1% of votes, +0.2%</li>\n<li>St.Austell and Newquay (last place) 4.1% -0.2%</li></ul>\n<p>All Mebyon Kernow candidates lost their deposits.\n<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mebyon Kernow declined to stand candidates in the 2017 general election, stating; <i>\xe2\x80\x9cThe timing of this snap election, so close to the local polls, makes it impractical for a party with our level of resources to put together and finance a meaningful campaign for the General Election."</i><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the district elections of 2007 seven Mebyon Kernow district councillors were elected. MK lost one district seat and gained two, a net gain of one. This gave them seven of the 249 seats (2.8% of seats) up for election.  Mebyon Kernow got around 5 percent of the total vote in these district elections, putting the party in third position behind the Liberal Democrats and the Conservative Party and ahead of Labour in several seats including <a href="/wiki/Kerrier" title="Kerrier">Kerrier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restormel" title="Restormel">Restormel</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall" title="North Cornwall">North Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caradon" title="Caradon">Caradon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council_election,_2013" title="Cornwall Council election, 2013">2013 local elections</a>  Mebyon Kernow held six seats on the council, having gained two due to defections from other parties, and winning one in a by-election. Keeping the seat won in the by-election, and a gain of one seat elsewhere, left them with four in total. This dropped them to being the sixth largest group on the council, from the position of fourth largest prior to the election, being overtaken by UKIP and The Labour party.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Nationalist_Party" title="Cornish Nationalist Party">Cornish Nationalist Party</a> was founded as a splinter group from Mebyon Kernow in the 1970s following a split over the ideological path of the Cornish National Movement, the members of the Cornish Nationalist party favouring a more \'right of centre approach\' to attracting support. Initially led by Dr <a href="/wiki/James_Whetter" title="James Whetter">James Whetter</a>, the Cornish Nationalist Party are not a registered political party under the <a href="/wiki/Registration_of_Political_Parties_Act_1998" title="Registration of Political Parties Act 1998">Registration of Political Parties Act 1998</a> and therefore cannot stand for local or Westminster elections.\n</p><p>In addition to political parties, other independent organisations promote the autonomy movement. The <a href="/wiki/Revived_Cornish_Stannary_Parliament" title="Revived Cornish Stannary Parliament">Revived Cornish Stannary Parliament</a> was a human rights pressure group which claimed to be a revival of the mediaeval <a href="/wiki/Stannary_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary Parliament">Stannary Parliaments</a>, local legislative organisations in the mining regions. It was established in 1974 and had campaigned against the government of the United Kingdom\'s position on the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall" title="Constitutional status of Cornwall">constitutional status of Cornwall</a>. The group has not been active since 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Other groups include TGG (Tyr-Gwyr-Gweryn), <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_2000" title="Cornwall 2000">Cornwall 2000</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Solidarity" title="Cornish Solidarity">Cornish Solidarity</a> who were constitutional and Cornish human rights groups, but are no longer active,  and the Cornish Constitutional Convention which campaigns for a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Assembly" title="Cornish Assembly">Cornish Assembly</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_issues_and_lobby_groups">Other issues and lobby groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other issues and lobby groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Within Cornwall there are a growing number of pressure groups/lobbying groups devoted to Cornish issues other than the national question.  Local environmental issues feature prominently, notably the <a href="/wiki/Surfers_against_Sewage" class="mw-redirect" title="Surfers against Sewage">Surfers against Sewage</a> group, formed in this region, heavily dependent on the tourist industry.  The <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a> (CND) has a local presence, with a branch in <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Some east Cornwall CND activists are members of the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> branch. There is a Cornish branch of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> have a local branch.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
