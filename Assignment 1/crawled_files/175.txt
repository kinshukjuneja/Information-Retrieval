class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg/170px-DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg/255px-DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg/340px-DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1224" data-file-height="1632" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DSCN1948PenrynPrayerBookRebellionMemorial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a> Memorial, near the site of <a href="/wiki/Glasney_College" title="Glasney College">Glasney College</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Penryn is one of Cornwall\'s most ancient towns and boasts a wealth of history. The ancient town first appears in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> under the name of "Trelivel", and was since founded and named Penryn in 1216 by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Exeter" title="Bishop of Exeter">Bishop of Exeter</a>. The borough was enfranchised and its Charter of Incorporation was made in 1236. The contents of this Charter were embodied in a confirmation by Bishop <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bronescombe" class="mw-redirect" title="Walter Bronescombe">Walter Bronescombe</a> in the year 1259.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1265, a religious college, called <a href="/wiki/Glasney_College" title="Glasney College">Glasney College</a>, was built in Penryn for the Bishop of Exeter to develop the church\'s influence in the far west of the diocese. In 1374, the chapel of St Thomas (sometimes called St Mary\'s) was opened. Standing at the head of the Penryn River, Penryn occupies a sheltered position and was a port of some significance in the 15th century. After the Dissolution of the Monasteries by King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Established_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Established church">disestablishing</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> church, Glasney College was dissolved and demolished in 1548 during the brief reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VI">Edward VI</a>, the first Protestant <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a> and afterwards King of England. The dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Glasney_College" title="Glasney College">Glasney College</a> helped trigger the <a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a> of 1549. The loss of Glasney and the defeat of the 1549 rebellion proved to be a turning point in the history of the town from which Penryn has never recovered.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg/220px-Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg/330px-Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg/440px-Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jubilee_Wharf_Penryn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_764174.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jubilee Wharf</div></div></div>\n<p>From 1554, Penryn held a <a href="/wiki/Penryn_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Penryn (UK Parliament constituency)">parliamentary constituency</a>, which became <a href="/wiki/Penryn_and_Falmouth_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Penryn and Falmouth (UK Parliament constituency)">Penryn and Falmouth</a> in 1832. The constituency was abolished in 1950, with Penryn becoming part of the <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_and_Camborne_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Falmouth and Camborne (UK Parliament constituency)">Falmouth and Camborne</a> constituency. It received a royal charter as a borough in 1621, mainly in a bid by the crown to cure the town of piracy. At least three mayors of Penryn were convicted of piracy between 1550 and 1650. The arms of the borough of Penryn were a Saracen\'s head Or in a bordure of eight bezants.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\nThe merchant traveller and writer <a href="/wiki/Peter_Mundy" title="Peter Mundy">Peter Mundy</a> (c1600-1667) was son of a Penryn <a href="/wiki/Pilchard" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilchard">pilchard</a> trader and travelled extensively throughout his life in Asia (where he was one of the first Europeans to taste <i><a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">Chaa</a></i>), Russia and Europe before returning to Penryn to write his <i>Itinerarium Mundi</i> (\'World Itinerary\'); one of the earliest travel guides in English.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the mid 17th century the port was thriving with the trade in Cornish fish, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. However, Penryn lost its custom house and market rights to the new town of <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> as a direct result of supporting the Parliamentary side in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> (1642\xe2\x80\x9348). The Killigrews of Arwenack were more skilful turncoats, and as their new town grew so the older port of Penryn declined from the 17th century right up to today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In the early 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite works</a> were established by the river and large quantities of the stone were shipped from its quays for construction projects both within the UK and abroad.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/A39_road" title="A39 road">A39 road</a>, which begins in <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a> and is about 200 miles (320&#160;km) long, once passed through Penryn towards the end of its route in nearby Falmouth, but in 1994 was diverted around the town when the Penryn Bypass was opened, incorporating a stretch of new road along with upgrading to an existing road.\n</p><p>The town is the setting of the play <i>The Penryn Tragedy</i>, which tells of a young man unwittingly murdered by his parents after disguising himself as a rich stranger.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a is a quiet town and has retained a large amount of its heritage. A large proportion of its buildings date from <a href="/wiki/Tudor_architecture" title="Tudor architecture">Tudor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_architecture" title="Jacobean architecture">Jacobean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian</a> times; the town has therefore been designated as an important conservation area. The local museum is housed in the Town Hall. The Town Hall building is partly 18th-century and partly 19th-century in date; its clock tower is dated 1839.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Penryn has a small but active Rotary Club.\n</p><p>Penryn is <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twinned</a> with <a href="/wiki/Audierne" title="Audierne">Audierne</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, France.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Penryn_railway_station" title="Penryn railway station">Penryn railway station</a> was opened by the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway" title="Cornwall Railway">Cornwall Railway</a> on 24 August 1863. It is towards the north west end of the town and is served by regular trains from <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a> to <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Line" title="Maritime Line">Maritime Line</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_education">Higher education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Higher education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Penryn_Campus" title="Penryn Campus">Penryn Campus</a></div>\n<p>In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Penryn_Campus" title="Penryn Campus">Penryn Campus</a> was completed, creating the hub of the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Universities_in_Cornwall" title="Combined Universities in Cornwall">Combined Universities in Cornwall</a> (CUC) project. It includes the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Cornish_Studies" title="Institute of Cornish Studies">Institute of Cornish Studies</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Exeter" title="University of Exeter">University of Exeter</a>\'s world-renowned <a href="/wiki/Camborne_School_of_Mines" title="Camborne School of Mines">Camborne School of Mines</a>, which has moved from <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a>, where it has been for over a century. The Campus also houses departments of <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_University" title="Falmouth University">Falmouth University</a>, which is based in the centre of <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>. In 2007, phase two was completed, which includes increased student accommodation and new teaching areas.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools">Schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are currently two schools in Penryn:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Penryn Primary Academy (a merging of Penryn Infants and Junior Schools)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Penryn_College" title="Penryn College">Penryn College</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_and_leisure">Sport and leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sport and leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Penryn_RFC" title="Penryn RFC">Penryn RFC</a>, founded in 1872 is a <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> club which plays in the <a href="/wiki/Tribute_Western_Counties_West" title="Tribute Western Counties West">Tribute Western Counties West</a> league; the seventh tier of the <a href="/wiki/English_rugby_union_system" title="English rugby union system">English rugby union league system</a>. They are nicknamed "The Borough" and are the oldest rugby club in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Penryn_Athletic_F.C." title="Penryn Athletic F.C.">Penryn Athletic</a> (founded 1963) is a <a href="/wiki/Non-League_football" title="Non-League football">non-League football</a> club who play at the 1,500 capacity Kernick Road ground. The club is a member of the <a href="/wiki/2014%E2%80%9315_South_West_Peninsula_League#Division_One_West" title="2014\xe2\x80\x9315 South West Peninsula League">South West Peninsula League Division One West</a>, which is a step 7 league in the <a href="/wiki/National_league_system" class="mw-redirect" title="National league system">national league system</a>. Also known as "The Borough".\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/English_Shinty_Association" title="English Shinty Association">English Shinty Association</a> is based in Penryn.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policing">Policing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Policing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The policing of the area is the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Devon_and_Cornwall_Police" title="Devon and Cornwall Police">Devon and Cornwall Police</a> who have a dedicated team to cover the area; The Penryn &amp; Mylor local Policing Team.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_residents">Notable residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Notable residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Emma_Hosken" title="Emma Hosken">Emma Hosken</a>, novelist, was born in Penryn.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Vic_Roberts" title="Vic Roberts">Vic Roberts</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_and_Irish_Lions" title="British and Irish Lions">British Lion</a> and England rugby union international born in and played for Penryn RFC.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/William_Harris_Rule" title="William Harris Rule">William Harris Rule</a>, Methodist missionary was born here in 1802.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/John_Tucker_Williams" title="John Tucker Williams">John Tucker Williams</a>, Canadian soldier and politician, was born in Penryn.</li></ul>\n<p>Penryn was the home of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pellow" title="Thomas Pellow">Thomas Pellow</a> (born circa 1704) who spent 23 years as a white slave in Morocco. Pellow\'s story is told in his autobiography, <i>The History of the Long Captivity and Adventures of Thomas Pellow</i> (1740) and in <a href="/wiki/Giles_Milton" title="Giles Milton">Giles Milton</a>\'s 2007 book <i>White Gold: the extraordinary story of Thomas Pellow and Islam\'s one million white slaves</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Footnotes">Footnotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Footnotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://neighbourhood.statistics.gov.uk/dissemination/LeadTableView.do?a=3&amp;b=792767&amp;c=Penryn&amp;d=16&amp;e=15&amp;g=430100&amp;i=1001x1003x1004&amp;m=0&amp;enc=1&amp;dsFamilyId=779">"Local statistics - Office for National Statistics"</a>. <i>neighbourhood.statistics.gov.uk</i>. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160303193634/http://neighbourhood.statistics.gov.uk/dissemination/LeadTableView.do?a=3&amp;b=792767&amp;c=Penryn&amp;d=16&amp;e=15&amp;g=430100&amp;i=1001x1003x1004&amp;m=0&amp;enc=1&amp;dsFamilyId=779">Archived</a> from the original on 3 March 2016<span class="reference-accessdate">.
