href="/w/index.php?title=Cantre%27r_Gwaelod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cantre\'r Gwaelod was an area of land which, according to legend, was located in an area west of present-day Wales which is now under the waters of Cardigan Bay. Accounts variously suggest the tract of land extended from <a href="/wiki/Bardsey_Island" title="Bardsey Island">Bardsey Island</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cardigan,_Ceredigion" title="Cardigan, Ceredigion">Cardigan</a> or as far south as <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Island" title="Ramsey Island">Ramsey Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gwyndaf_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gwyndaf-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Legends of the land suggest that it may have extended 20 miles west of the present coast.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc-legacies_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-legacies-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are several versions of the myth. The earliest known form of the legend is usually said to appear in the <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Book_of_Carmarthen" title="Black Book of Carmarthen">Black Book of Carmarthen</a></i>, in which the land is referred to as <i>Maes Gwyddno</i> (English: <span lang="en">the Plain of Gwyddno</span>). In this version, the land was lost to floods when a well-maiden named Mererid neglected her duties and allowed the well to overflow.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc-legacies_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-legacies-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rachel_Bromwich" title="Rachel Bromwich">Rachel Bromwich</a> questions this identification, saying that "There is no certainty, however, that in twelfth century tradition Maes Gwyddneu did represent the submerged land in Cardigan Bay." She also links <a href="/wiki/Gwyddno_Garanhir" title="Gwyddno Garanhir">Gwyddno Garanhir</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Hen_Ogledd" title="Hen Ogledd">Hen Ogledd</a>, not Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-Rachel_Bromwich_1950_231_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rachel_Bromwich_1950_231-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The popular version known today is thought to have been formed from the <a href="/wiki/17th_century" title="17th century">17th century</a> onwards. Cantre\'r Gwaelod is described as a low-lying land fortified against the sea by a <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">dyke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarn_Badrig" title="Sarn Badrig">Sarn Badrig</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a>\'s causeway"), with a series of <a href="/wiki/Sluice" title="Sluice">sluice</a> gates that were opened at low tide to drain the land.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc-legacies_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-legacies-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cantre\'r Gwaelod\'s capital was <i><a href="/wiki/Caer" title="Caer">Caer</a> Wyddno</i>, seat of the ruler Gwyddno Garanhir. Two princes of the realm held charge over the dyke. One of these princes, called <a href="/wiki/Seithenyn" title="Seithenyn">Seithenyn</a>, is described in one version as a notorious drunkard and carouser, and it was through his negligence that the sea swept through the open floodgates, ruining the land.\n</p><p>The church bells of Cantre\'r Gwaelod are said to ring out in times of danger.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_myth_of_Llys_Helig">Relationship to myth of Llys Helig</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantre%27r_Gwaelod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship to myth of Llys Helig">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Rachel Bromwich discusses a similar tale, that of the submergence of the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Helig_ap_Glanawg" title="Helig ap Glanawg">Helig ap Glanawg</a> in the <a href="/wiki/River_Conwy" title="River Conwy">Conwy estuary</a>. As with Cantre\'r Gwaelod, there are tales of remains being seen of the sunken kingdom (<a href="/wiki/Llys_Helig" title="Llys Helig">Llys Helig</a>).  Bromwich believes that the two stories influenced each other, and that "The widespread parallels to this inundation theme would suggest that the two stories are in fact one in origin, and were localized separately in Cardiganshire and in the Conway estuary, around two traditional figures of the sixth century. She also notes that the <a href="/wiki/Masonic_manuscripts" title="Masonic manuscripts">Halliwell Manuscript</a> gives Helig the title "Lord of Cantre\'r Gwaelod".<sup id="cite_ref-Rachel_Bromwich_1950_231_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rachel_Bromwich_1950_231-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the book <i>New Directions In Celtic Studies</i> Antone Minard wrote that "The Welsh legends of Cantre\'r Gwaelod and Llys Helig (Helig\'s Court) contain the same details of audible bells beneath the waves and ruins which are visible at the equinoctial tides, which are the anchors of credulity in the story".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_evidence">Physical evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantre%27r_Gwaelod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CardiganBay.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/CardiganBay.svg/220px-CardiganBay.svg.png" width="220" height="338" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/CardiganBay.svg/440px-CardiganBay.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2030" data-file-height="3120" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CardiganBay.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cardigan Bay, supposed location of Cantre\'r Gwaelod</div></div></div>\n<p>There is no reliable physical evidence of the substantial community that legend promises lies under the sea, although several reports exist of remains being sighted.\n</p><p>In 1770, Welsh antiquarian scholar <a href="/wiki/William_Owen_Pughe" title="William Owen Pughe">William Owen Pughe</a> reported seeing sunken human habitations about four miles (6.4&#160;km) off the <a href="/wiki/Ceredigion" title="Ceredigion">Ceredigion</a> coast, between the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Ystwyth" title="River Ystwyth">Ystwyth</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Teifi" title="River Teifi">Teifi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-haughton_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haughton-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1846 edition of <i>The Topographical Dictionary of Wales</i>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Lewis_(publisher)" title="Samuel Lewis (publisher)">Samuel Lewis</a> described a feature of stone walls and causeways beneath the shallow waters of Cardigan Bay:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In the sea, about seven miles west of Aberystwyth in Cardiganshire, is a collection of loose stones, termed Caer Wyddno, "the fort or palace of Gwyddno;" and adjoining it are vestiges of one of the more southern causeways or embankments of Catrev Gwaelod. The depth of water over the whole extent of the bay of Cardigan is not great; and on the recess of the tide, stones bearing Latin inscriptions, and Roman coins of various emperors, have been found below high-water mark: in different places in the water, also, are observed prostrate trees."</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Lewis_(publisher)" title="Samuel Lewis (publisher)">Samuel Lewis</a>, The Topographical Dictionary of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-haughton_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haughton-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Lewis takes the view that maps by the <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> marked the coastline of Cardigan Bay in the same location as it appears in modern times, suggesting that the date of the flood occurred before the second century AD.\n</p>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 300px; Known as <i>Sarnau</i>, these ridges stretch several miles into the sea at right angles to the coast, and are located between each of the four river mouths in the north of Cardigan Bay. Modern geologists surmise that these formations of clay, gravel and rocks are <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> formed by the action of <a href="/wiki/Glacial_landform" title="Glacial landform">melting glaciers</a> end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. In a 2006 episode of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Coast_(TV_series)" title="Coast (TV series)">Coast</a></i>, presenter <a href="/wiki/Neil_Oliver" title="Neil Oliver">Neil Oliver</a> visited <i><a href="/wiki/Sarn_Gynfelyn" title="Sarn Gynfelyn">Sarn Gynfelyn</a></i> at <a href="/wiki/Wallog" title="Wallog">Wallog</a>. The programme also featured the remains of the submerged forest at <a href="/wiki/Ynyslas" title="Ynyslas">Ynyslas</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Borth" title="Borth">Borth</a> which is associated with the lost land of Cantre\'r Gwaelod. The vista of dead oak, pine, birch, willow and hazel tree stumps preserved by the acid <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anaerobic</a> conditions in the soil is revealed at <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">low tide</a> and is estimated to be about 5000 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-coast_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coast-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-submerged_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-submerged-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_the_myth">Origins of the myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantre%27r_Gwaelod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origins of the myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Satellite view of the Celtic Sea" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg/150px-Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg" width="150" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg/225px-Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg/300px-Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1362" data-file-height="1509" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coccoliths_in_the_Celtic_Sea-NASA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparable Celtic myths describe a submerged kingdom near Brittany or Cornwall</div></div></div>\n<p>The myth, like so many others, may be a folk memory of gradually rising sea levels at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. The physical remains of the preserved sunken forest at <a href="/wiki/Borth#Geography" title="Borth">Borth</a>, and of Sarn Badrig nearby, could have suggested that some great tragedy had overcome a community there long ago, and so the myth may have grown from that.<sup id="cite_ref-submerged_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-submerged-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogies_in_other_legends">Analogies in other legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantre%27r_Gwaelod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Analogies in other legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The legend of Cantre\'r Gwaelod is comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Flood_myth" title="Flood myth">deluge myth</a> found in nearly every ancient culture, and it has been likened to the story of <a href="/wiki/Atlantis" title="Atlantis">Atlantis</a>.\n</p><p>Several similar legends exist in <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic mythology</a> which refer to a lost land beneath the waves. Both the <a href="/wiki/Breton_mythology" title="Breton mythology">Breton legend</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ys" title="Ys">Ker-Ys</a> and the <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">Arthurian</a> tale of <a href="/wiki/Lyonesse" title="Lyonesse">Lyonesse</a> refer to a kingdom submerged somewhere in the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Sea" title="Celtic Sea">Celtic Sea</a>, off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> respectively.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantre%27r_Gwaelod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantre%27r_Gwaelod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The legend has inspired many poems and songs throughout the ages. The earliest mention of Cantre\'r Gwaelod is thought to be in the thirteenth-century <a href="/wiki/Black_Book_of_Carmarthen" title="Black Book of Carmarthen">Black Book of Carmarthen</a> in a poem called <i>"Boddi Maes Gwyddno"</i> ("The Drowning of the Land of Gwyddno") which relates the tale of Mererid and the well.\n</p><p>The story inspired a <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>-novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Misfortunes_of_Elphin" title="The Misfortunes of Elphin">The Misfortunes of Elphin</a></i> (1829), by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Love_Peacock" title="Thomas Love Peacock">Thomas Love Peacock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  At the 1925 <a href="/wiki/National_Eisteddfod_of_Wales" title="National Eisteddfod of Wales">National Eisteddfod of Wales</a>, held in <a href="/wiki/Pwllheli" title="Pwllheli">Pwllheli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dewi_Morgan" title="Dewi Morgan">Dewi Morgan</a> (\'Dewi Teifi\') won the <a href="/wiki/Chairing_of_the_Bard" title="Chairing of the Bard">Bardic Chair</a> with his <a href="/wiki/Awdl" title="Awdl">Awdl</a> recounting the legend, adopting Thomas Love Peacock\'s version as the basis for his poetic rendition.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ptolemy_Cosmographia_1467_-_Great_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ptolemy\'s map of Great Britain and Ireland (1467 copy)</div></div></div>\n<p>Geologist William Ashton\'s 1920 book, <i>The Evolution of a Coast-Line, Barrow to Aberystwyth and the Isle of Man, with Notes on Lost Towns, Submarine Discoveries, &amp;C</i>, discusses the legend and takes Ptolemy\'s map as evidence of the existence of an area of lost land in Cardigan Bay. Ashton also includes a conjectural map of Cantre\'r Gwaelod within the bay.<sup id="cite_ref-ashton_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ashton-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cantre\'r Gwaelod is also featured in modern <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_literature" title="Children&#39;s literature">children\'s literature</a>. Cantre\'r Gwaelod is central to the setting of the 1977 <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">Newbery Honor</a> Book <i><a href="/wiki/A_String_in_the_Harp" title="A String in the Harp">A String in the Harp</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Bond" title="Nancy Bond">Nancy Bond</a>.  The kingdom also plays a major role in <i>Silver on the Tree</i>, the last book of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Is_Rising_Sequence" title="The Dark Is Rising Sequence">The Dark Is Rising</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Susan_Cooper" title="Susan Cooper">Susan Cooper</a>, parts of which are set in <a href="/wiki/Aberdyfi" title="Aberdyfi">Aberdyfi</a>. Si\xc3\xa2n Lewis and Jackie Morris\'s book <i>Cities in the Sea</i> (2002) retells the legend for children,<sup id="cite_ref-lewis_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and Welsh musician <a href="/wiki/Cerys_Matthews" title="Cerys Matthews">Cerys Matthews</a>\'s first children\'s book <i>Tales from the Deep</i> (2011) features a story, <i>The Ghost Bells of the Lowlands</i>, which was adapted from the legend of Cantre\'r Gwaelod.<sup id="cite_ref-matthews_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matthews-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_art">Music and art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Aberdyfi</i></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk song</a> "Clychau Aberdyfi" ("<a href="/wiki/The_Bells_of_Aberdovey_(song)" title="The Bells of Aberdovey This song inspired cultural projects in the town involving bells; a new <a href="/wiki/Chime_(bell_instrument)" title="Chime (bell instrument)">chime of bells</a> was installed in September 1936 in the tower St Peter\'s Church, Aberdyfi, specifically designed to allow the playing of <i>The Bells of Aberdovey</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-stpeters_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stpeters-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  An <a href="/wiki/Installation_art" title="Installation art">art installation</a> by the sculptor Marcus Vergette, a bronze "Time and Tide Bell", was mounted beneath the jetty in Aberdyfi Harbour in July 2011 as a homage to the folk song.
