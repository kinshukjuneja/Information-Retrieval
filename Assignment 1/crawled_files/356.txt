one of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>\'s national radio <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">stations</a> and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_most-listened-to_radio_programs" title="List of most-listened-to radio programs">most popular station</a> in the United Kingdom with over 15 million weekly listeners.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Much of its daytime <a href="/wiki/Playlist" title="Playlist">playlist</a>-based programming is <a href="/wiki/Adult_contemporary_music" title="Adult contemporary music">adult contemporary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Album-oriented_rock" title="Album-oriented rock">AOR</a>, although the station also broadcasts other specialist musical genres. Radio 2 broadcasts throughout the UK on <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a> between 88.1 and 90.2<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">MHz</a> from studios in <a href="/wiki/Wogan_House" title="Wogan House">Wogan House</a>, adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting_House" title="Broadcasting House">Broadcasting House</a> in central London. Programmes are relayed on <a href="/wiki/Digital_radio_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Digital radio in the United Kingdom">digital radio</a> via <a href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Broadcasting" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Audio Broadcasting">DAB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sky_(UK_and_Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sky (UK and Ireland)">Sky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">Cable TV</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPTV" title="IPTV">IPTV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freeview_(UK)" title="Freeview (UK)">Freeview</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freesat" title="Freesat">Freesat</a> and the Internet.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_BBC_Radio_2" title="Timeline of BBC Radio 2">Timeline of BBC Radio 2</a></div>\n<h3><span id="1967.E2.80.931986"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1967\xe2\x80\x931986">1967\xe2\x80\x931986</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1967\xe2\x80\x931986">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The station was launched at 5:30<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> on 30 September 1967, and evolved from the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Light_Programme" title="BBC Light Programme">Light Programme</a>, with some of the Light Programme\'s music shows transferring to the newly launched <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a>. The first show had started at 5:30<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am (on the Light Programme) but continued with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Radio_2_Breakfast_Show" title="The Radio 2 Breakfast Show">Breakfast Special</a></i> from Paul Hollingdale as Radio 1 split.\n</p><p>In early years, much programming and music was common to both stations, particularly on the shared FM frequency. Radio 1 was targeted at the audience of <a href="/wiki/Pirate_radio_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Pirate radio in the United Kingdom">pirate radio</a> stations whereas Radio 2 settled down as a <a href="/wiki/Middle_of_the_road_(music)" title="Middle of the road (music)">middle-of-the-road</a> station playing laid-back pop/rock, folk and country, jazz and big-band music, <a href="/wiki/Easy_listening" title="Easy listening">easy listening</a>, light classics, and oldies, with significant amounts of comedy and sport. Notable broadcasters on Radio 2 in the 70s and 80s were <a href="/wiki/Tom_Edwards_(broadcaster)" title="Tom Edwards (broadcaster)">Tom Edwards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Moore_(broadcaster)" title="Ray Moore (broadcaster)">Ray Moore</a> who both singly presented the early breakfast show, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Wogan" title="Terry Wogan">Terry Wogan</a> on breakfast, replaced by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Bruce" title="Ken Bruce">Ken Bruce</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Derek_Jameson" title="Derek Jameson">Derek Jameson</a>; <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Young_(broadcaster)" title="Jimmy Young (broadcaster)">Jimmy Young</a> and his lunchtime news and current affairs show; <a href="/wiki/David_Hamilton_(broadcaster)" title="David Hamilton (broadcaster)">\'Diddy\' David Hamilton</a> on mid-afternoons, <a href="/wiki/John_Churchill_Dunn" title="John Churchill Dunn">John Dunn</a> at what became known as drivetime.\n</p><p>On Monday 6 January 1975, the broadcasting hours for BBC Radio 2 were reduced due to budget cuts at the BBC.  The former 5<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am&#160;&#8211;&#32;2<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am schedule was reduced to a 6<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am start up Mondays to Saturdays with a 6:55<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am start up on Sundays. The station closed down at around 12:33<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am each day. However from Monday 29 September 1975 the closedown hours were scaled back even more, with BBC Radio 2 concluding their day at around 12:10<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am Mondays to Fridays, and at 12:33<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am on Saturdays and Sundays.  There were exceptions to the rule, especially over Christmas and New Year periods when hours would be temporarily extended.  Broadcasting hours would be extended to the 5<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am&#160;&#8211;&#32;2<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am schedule on Saturday 1 April 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>BBC Radio 2 became the first national 24-hour radio station in the UK in 1979.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Frances_Line:_1986.E2.80.931996"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Frances_Line:_1986\xe2\x80\x931996">Frances Line: 1986\xe2\x80\x931996</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Frances Line: 1986\xe2\x80\x931996">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The station\'s policy remained stable with only minor changes until April 1986 when <a href="/wiki/Frances_Line" title="Frances Line">Frances Line</a>, head of music, repositioned the station. She would become Controller in 1990. An ageing <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">Radio 1</a> audience which had grown up with the station was sticking with it into their 40s and beyond; Line repositioned Radio 2 to appeal exclusively to the over-fifties and introduced older presenters and based the playlist around nostalgia, <a href="/wiki/Easy_listening" title="Easy listening">easy listening</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light_music" title="Light music">light music</a>. As a result, <a href="/wiki/David_Hamilton_(broadcaster)" title="David Hamilton (broadcaster)">David Hamilton</a> quit the station at the end of 1986, claiming the music policy had become "geriatric"; <a href="/wiki/Terry_Wogan" title="Terry Wogan">Terry Wogan</a>\'s replacement <a href="/wiki/Derek_Jameson" title="Derek Jameson">Derek Jameson</a> also appealed to an older, down-market demographic. Although popular with its target audience, the policy alienated many younger listeners who had listened to both Radio 1 and Radio 2 and the station\'s audience fell.\n</p><p>It took another hit in 1990 when it lost its <a href="/wiki/Medium_wave" title="Medium wave">medium wave</a> frequencies to a new network, <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_5_(former)" title="BBC Radio 5 (former)">BBC Radio 5</a> and BBC radio\'s sports coverage moved from Radio 2 to the new station at the same time. Further blows were struck by the rise of album-rock commercial stations (particularly <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Radio" title="Virgin Radio">Virgin Radio</a>) and \'gold\' spinoffs from <a href="/wiki/Independent_Local_Radio" title="Independent Local Radio">Independent Local Radio</a> stations playing classic pop and rock. With the station\'s audience in decline a change of emphasis was needed.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="James_Moir_.22The_Nation.27s_Favourite.22_.E2.80.93_1996_onwards"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="James_Moir_&quot;The_Nation\'s_Favourite&quot;_\xe2\x80\x93_1996_onwards">James Moir "The Nation\'s Favourite" \xe2\x80\x93 1996 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: James Moir &quot;The Nation&#039;s Favourite&quot; \xe2\x80\x93 1996 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Line was replaced by <a href="/wiki/James_Moir" title="James Moir">James Moir</a> in 1996. Moir repositioned Radio 2 with a largely <a href="/wiki/Album-orientated_rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Album-orientated rock">AOR</a>/contemporary playlist by day, aimed at a more mature audience than Radio 1 (which, post-<a href="/wiki/Britpop" title="Britpop">Britpop</a>, was again starting to focus on a young audience) but still embracing new music, and more specialist broadcasting by recognised genre experts in the evenings. Unlike the early-1990s repositioning of Radio 1 in which the BBC lost many well-known names, many former Radio 1 presenters stayed with the BBC and moved across to Radio 2.\n</p><p>Radio 2 is now termed "the nation\'s favourite", a title the BBC formerly used for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a>. It is the most listened-to station in the UK, its schedule filled with broadcasters such as: <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wright_(DJ)" title="Steve Wright (DJ)">Steve Wright</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Evans_(presenter)" title="Chris Evans (presenter)">Chris Evans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Mayo" title="Simon Mayo">Simon Mayo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blackburn" title="Tony Blackburn">Tony Blackburn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Bruce" title="Ken Bruce">Ken Bruce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Vine" title="Jeremy Vine">Jeremy Vine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Radcliffe_(radio_broadcaster)" title="Mark Radcliffe (radio broadcaster)">Mark Radcliffe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo_Whiley" title="Jo Whiley">Jo Whiley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gambaccini" title="Paul Gambaccini">Paul Gambaccini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johnnie_Walker_(DJ)" title="Johnnie Walker (DJ)">Johnnie Walker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Harris_(radio)" title="Bob Harris (radio)">Bob Harris</a>.\n</p><p>As well as having most listeners nationally, it ranks first in many regions above local radio stations. BBC Radio 2 played to 27% of the available audience in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2007, Radio 2 recruited Jeff Smith, director of UK and International programming at Napster and a former head of music at Radio 1, as its new head of music. Smith joined the network on 26 March.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The licence fee funding of Radio 2, alongside Radio 1, is often criticised by the commercial sector. In the first quarter of 2011, Radio 2 was part of an efficiency review conducted by <a href="/wiki/John_Myers_(radio_executive)" title="John Myers (radio executive)">John Myers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tel-nov2010_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tel-nov2010-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His role, according to Andrew Harrison, the chief executive of RadioCentre, was "to identify both areas of best practice and possible savings."<sup id="cite_ref-tel-nov2010_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tel-nov2010-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 29 July 2013, Radio 2 changed its "sonic logo" for the first time in 15 years, replacing the "heritage" logo composed by US jingle company <a href="/wiki/Groove_Worx" title="Groove Worx">Groove Worx</a> with a new seven note melody composed by British composer and producer <a href="/wiki/Jem_Godfrey" title="Jem Godfrey">Jem Godfrey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This coincided with the launch of a new jingle package produced by Godfrey in association with <a href="/wiki/Wise_Buddah_Productions" class="mw-redirect" title="Wise Buddah Productions">Wise Buddah Productions</a>, marking Radio 2\'s second new package in as many years.\n</p><p>Radio 2 has recently been running several "pop-up" DAB services to cover special events, the first being <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2_Eurovision" title="BBC Radio 2 Eurovision">BBC Radio 2 Eurovision</a>, providing coverage of the <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2014" title="Eurovision Song Contest 2014">Eurovision Song Contest 2014</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The station returned in 2015 for coverage of <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2015" title="Eurovision Song Contest 2015">that year\'s</a> contest. Others include <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2_Country" title="BBC Radio 2 Country">BBC Radio 2 Country</a> covering the <a href="/wiki/C2C:_Country_to_Country" title="C2C: Country to Country">C2C: Country to Country</a> festival and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2_50s" title="BBC Radio 2 50s">BBC Radio 2 50s</a>, a service dedicated to music programmes covering the 1950s.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_position">Current position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Current position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The station\'s audience is now mainly adults over the age of 35 (82% of listeners)<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> although in recent years it has attracted more younger listeners. Its daytime <a href="/wiki/Playlist" title="Playlist">playlist</a> features music from the 1960s to various current chart hits, album and <a href="/wiki/Indie_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Indie music">indie music</a>. The station\'s appeal is broad and deep, with accessible daytime programmes and specialist programmes of particular types or eras of music. In 2009, Radio 2 again won the Music Week Award for National Radio Station of the Year, an award it won for several consecutive years.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Weekday evenings feature specialist music, including <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_and_western" class="mw-redirect" title="Country and western">country and western</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Classic_rock" title="Classic rock">classic rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Show_tune" title="Show tune">showtunes</a> and biographies and documentaries on musical artists and genres. This specialist programming typically runs from 8<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>pm to midnight. Radio 2 hosts both the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Concert_Orchestra" title="BBC Concert Orchestra">BBC Concert Orchestra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Big_Band" title="BBC Big Band">BBC Big Band</a>.\n</p><p>"Sounds of the Sixties" remains a regular fixture on the Saturday schedule, as does <a href="/wiki/Johnnie_Walker_(DJ)" title="Johnnie Walker (DJ)">Johnnie Walker</a>\'s "Sounds of the Seventies" on a Sunday. On 5 October 2013, these two shows were joined by "Sounds of the Eighties", which was originally hosted by <a href="/wiki/Sara_Cox" title="Sara Cox">Sara Cox</a> and broadcast on Friday between 10pm and midnight,  in May 2018 Gary Davies took over this show with Cox hosting a live 10pm to midnight slot on Monday to Thursday.\n</p><p>On Sundays, the schedule reverts closer to its old style, with a focus on <a href="/wiki/Easy_listening" title="Easy listening">easy listening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">Jazz</a> and show music, with presenters like <a href="/wiki/Clare_Teal" title="Clare Teal">Clare Teal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Don_Black_(lyricist)" title="Don Black (lyricist)">Don Black</a> and long-standing programmes like <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Hour" title="The Sunday Hour">The Sunday Hour</a>.\n</p><p>Radio 2 does not broadcast complete works of <a href="/wiki/European_classical_music" class="mw-redirect" title="European classical music">classical music</a> (the domain of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_3" title="BBC Radio 3">Radio 3</a>) or offer in-depth discussion or drama and although some book readings, comedy and arts coverage still remains on the station this is the remit of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">Radio 4</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Vine" title="Jeremy Vine">Jeremy Vine</a>\'s weekday lunchtime show covers current and consumer affairs informally, a style pioneered by Jimmy Young. Until the launch of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_5_(former)" title="BBC Radio 5 (former)">Radio 5</a> in August 1990, Radio 2\'s medium wave frequencies carried the BBC\'s sports coverage.\n</p><p>Like all BBC radio stations broadcasting to UK audiences, Radio 2 is funded by the <a href="/wiki/Television_licence" title="Television licence">television licence</a> fee, and does not broadcast <a href="/wiki/Adverts" class="mw-redirect" title="Adverts">adverts</a>.\n</p><p>BBC Radio 2\'s last closedown was at 2:02<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am on 27 January 1979. <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Kennedy" title="Sarah Kennedy">Sarah Kennedy</a> (who later became a daily early-morning presenter from 1993 until her departure in August 2010) was at the Newsdesk after Brian Matthew finished "Round Midnight". From 2<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am to 5<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am the following night, listeners heard "You and the Night and the Music". Radio 2 has the longest period of continuous broadcasting of any national radio station in the UK.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/BBC_Pips" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC Pips">BBC Pips</a> are broadcast at 7<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am and 8<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am on weekdays, then again at 5<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>pm and 7<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>pm.\n</p><p>BBC Radio 2 moved its studios from <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting_House" title="Broadcasting House">Broadcasting House</a> to the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Western_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Western House">Wogan House</a> in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Although the majority of programming comes from London, some shows are broadcast from other cities around the UK, including <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>. For many years, the network\'s overnight presenters, such as <a href="/wiki/Janice_Long" title="Janice Long">Janice Long</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alex_Lester" title="Alex Lester">Alex Lester</a>, were based in Birmingham, but made the move to London in April 2008.\n</p><p>News bulletins on Radio 2 generally air on the hour every hour 24 hours a day from a studio in BBC Broadcasting House, with updates on the half hour during the Breakfast Show. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_presenters">Current presenters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Current presenters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Michael_Ball_(singer)" title="Michael Ball (singer)">Michael Ball</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Zo%C3%AB_Ball" title="Zo\xc3\xab Ball">Zo\xc3\xab Ball</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Don_Black_(lyricist)" title="Don Black (lyricist)">Don Black</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tony_Blackburn" title="Tony Blackburn">Tony Blackburn</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/O._J._Borg" title="O. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The presenter <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Kennedy" title="Sarah Kennedy">Sarah Kennedy</a> has sometimes attracted controversy. In May 1999, she gave a bizarre performance while standing in for <a href="/wiki/Terry_Wogan" title="Terry Wogan">Terry Wogan</a>, blaming the incident on a lack of sleep the previous night.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Her slurred speech throughout her show on 13 August 2007 also made the headlines. She blamed a sore throat and later took a month-long break.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It was later reported that Kennedy was recovering from pneumonia,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and she returned to work on 10 September. In October 2007, she was reprimanded after joking that she had almost run over a black pedestrian because she could not see him in the dark. The BBC later apologised for the comment.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> She was also "spoken to" by BBC bosses after praising <a href="/wiki/Enoch_Powell" title="Enoch Powell">Enoch Powell</a> during a show in July 2009, describing him as "the best prime minister this country never had".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Russell_Brand_Show_prank_telephone_calls_row" title="The Russell Brand Show prank telephone calls row">The Russell Brand Show prank telephone calls row</a></div>\n<p>On 16 October 2008, an episode of the <a href="/wiki/The_Russell_Brand_Show_(radio_show)" title="The Russell Brand Show (radio show)">Russell Brand Show</a>, co-hosted by fellow Radio 2 presenter <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Ross" title="Jonathan Ross">Jonathan Ross</a> was recorded for transmission at a later date. The show included Brand and Ross leaving four prank messages on actor <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Sachs" title="Andrew Sachs">Andrew Sachs</a>\' answerphone including offensive remarks about his granddaughter and use of foul language. The programme was subsequently broadcast on Saturday 18 October, partially censored, having passed the various pre-transmission checks from the programme\'s editors. Initially, the programme only received a negligible number of complaints regarding <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Ross" title="Jonathan Ross">Jonathan Ross</a>\' bad language; however, the incident was reported a week later by <a href="/wiki/The_Mail_on_Sunday" title="The Mail on Sunday">the Mail on Sunday</a> and a public outcry soon ensued. The case was referred to both <a href="/wiki/Ofcom" title="Ofcom">Ofcom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Trust" title="BBC Trust">BBC Trust</a>, and in the interim, Ross and Brand were both suspended for 12 weeks from all BBC programmes pending investigation. Soon after these announcements, <a href="/wiki/Russell_Brand" title="Russell Brand">Russell Brand</a> announced his resignation from the BBC shortly followed by the controller at the time, <a href="/wiki/Lesley_Douglas" title="Lesley Douglas">Lesley Douglas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Ross" title="Jonathan Ross">Jonathan Ross</a> was suspended from the BBC without pay for 12 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 2009, longtime presenter <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Laycock" title="Malcolm Laycock">Malcolm Laycock</a> announced his resignation live on air following a long running dispute over the content of his show, <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_Night_at_10" title="Sunday Night at 10">Sunday Night at 10</a></i>, and issues regarding his salary.<sup id="cite_ref-Departure_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Departure-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> He later criticised Radio 2 management for abandoning its older listeners and claimed to have been <a href="/wiki/Constructive_dismissal" title="Constructive dismissal">constructively dismissed</a> by the station, although Radio 2 denied this was the case.<sup id="cite_ref-Maily_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maily-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
