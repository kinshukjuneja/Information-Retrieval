href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">countries</a> in the British Isles</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> comprise two <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Sovereign states">sovereign states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>  and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">three dependencies of the British Crown</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-justicecommittee_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-justicecommittee-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.\n</p><p>Ireland is a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary state</a> and a republic. Since 1998, it shares certain common institutions with <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, in the United Kingdom, from which it was <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partitioned</a> in 1921. The United Kingdom is a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> and also a unitary state<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> comprising <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and Northern Ireland. Also since 1998, the United Kingdom has <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolved</a> significant domestic powers (though differing in extent) to administrations in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Wales">Wales</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> (including its two semi-autonomous territories of <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>), are collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a>; although not part of the United Kingdom, the UK is responsible for their defence and international relations on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/British_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="British Crown">British Crown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bradley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bradley-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-backgroundbrief_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-backgroundbrief-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1998, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>, Ireland and the United Kingdom established a number of organisations, including the <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British-Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Council">British-Irish Council</a>, the latter being a <a href="/wiki/Multilateralism" title="Multilateralism">multilateral</a> body in which both sovereign states, the three devolved administrations of the UK and the three Crown dependencies participate. Additionally, there are numerous relations between the countries in the British Isles, including formal and informal bilateral and multilateral relations between Ireland, the devolved administrations of the UK, and the crown dependencies, as well as shared cultural and economic links.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" Isles</a></div>\n<p>The British Isles contain two <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Sovereign states">sovereign states</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, also known as the Republic of Ireland</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, within which certain powers are devolved:\n<ul><li>to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a></li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>The archipelago also contains three <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, which contains the somewhat autonomous <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>The Crown dependencies are constitutionally linked to the United Kingdom but independent of it.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Politics of the Republic of Ireland">Politics of the Republic of Ireland</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_republic" title="Parliamentary republic">parliamentary republic</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> comprising approximately five sixths of the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Island of Ireland">island of Ireland</a> in the west of the archipelago.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Island of Ireland">island of Ireland</a> was partitioned in 1920 with the Government of Ireland Act into the 26 counties of Southern Ireland and the six counties of Northern Ireland. In 1921 Southern Ireland was renamed the Irish Free State when it gained independence from the United Kingdom following the Irish War of Independence. Since the passage in 1949 of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Ireland Act">Republic of Ireland Act</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> is no longer part of the commonwealth.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Politics of the United Kingdom">Politics of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> that covers the whole of the island of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> in the east of the archipelago, a north-eastern part of the island of Ireland, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_the_British_Isles" title="List of islands of the British Isles">many smaller surrounding islands</a> including <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>.\n</p><p>The United Kingdom comprises four parts: the countries of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and the province of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> All but Northern Ireland have been independent states at one point and each have their own history and sense of identity.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Devolution">Devolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Devolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">Devolution in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>Northern Ireland has had a degree of self-government for most of the time since 1921. In 1998, following referendums in Scotland, Wales, and both parts of Ireland, a range of powers were <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">transferred</a> to <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Wales</a> as well as restoring self-government in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a>. England does not have self-government.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_dependencies">Crown dependencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crown dependencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Guernsey" title="Politics of Guernsey">Politics of Guernsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Jersey" title="Politics of Jersey">Politics of Jersey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Politics of the Isle of Man">Politics of the Isle of Man</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, in the south of the archipelago (and geographically closer to France), and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, in the Irish Sea between Great Britain and Ireland, are self-governing states but independent. The Bailiwick of Guernsey furthermore contains two smaller islands, which also have a certain degree of autonomy: <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a>.\n</p><p>The Crown dependencies are not part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. They are administered independently by their own governments, legislatures and judiciaries, but are not sovereign. The United Kingdom, on behalf of the British Crown, is responsible for their international relations and defence, and the <a href="/wiki/British_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="British Crown">Crown</a> is ultimately responsible for their good governance.<sup id="cite_ref-royal-channel-islands_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royal-channel-islands-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They can be legislated for from the Parliament of the United Kingdom, although in practice the UK rarely exercises this power.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While the Crown dependencies are not part of the United Kingdom, residents of the Isle of Man, Jersey and Guernsey are nonetheless considered British citizens, and European Union citizens by extension (with some exceptions), and are given British passports (albeit with a special cover for each Crown dependency). Nevertheless, British citizens from outside of each Crown dependency and Irish citizens may require work permits in order to work on the islands<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> although usually this is merely a formality. There have been discussions within some of the Crown dependencies about greater independence from the Crown, but they have not yet gained strong popular or governmental support.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations">Relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Multilateral relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The main body for multilateral international relations in the islands, since 1998, is the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Council" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council</a>. The British-Irish Council (<b>BIC</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/International_organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="International organisation">international organisation</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> established under the <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Belfast Agreement">Belfast Agreement</a> in 1998. Its membership comprises representatives from:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The two <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign</a> governments of <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a></li>\n<li>The three <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolved administrations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">crown dependencies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>The Council formally came into being on 2 December 1999. Its stated aim is to "promote the harmonious and mutually beneficial development of the totality of relationships among the peoples of these islands". The BIC has a standing secretariat, located in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and meets in bi-annual summit session and regular <a href="/wiki/Government_minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Government minister">ministerial</a> meetings.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that the BIC provides a more equitable forum for participation of the devolved administrations than the UK-only Joint Ministerial Council, and that it has created a "novel political and administrative space for intergovernmental co-operation."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some researchers have compared the British-Irish council to similar multilateral bodies amongst the Nordic countries: the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Council" title="Nordic Council">Nordic Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Council_of_Ministers" class="mw-redirect" title="Nordic Council of Ministers">Nordic Council of Ministers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>British-Irish Parliamentary Assembly</dt></dl>\n<p>In addition to the council, there is also the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Parliamentary_Assembly" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly</a> (<b>BIPA</b>), a deliberative body consisting of members of legislative bodies in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, and the British <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependency" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown dependency">crown dependencies</a>. Its purpose is to foster common understanding between elected representatives from these jurisdictions.\n</p><p>The assembly consists of 25 members of each of the two sovereign parliaments: the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> (the Irish parliament). It also includes as five representatives from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, five from the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>, five from the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, and one each from the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Jersey" title="States of Jersey">States of Jersey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Guernsey" title="States of Guernsey">States of Guernsey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Common travel area</dt></dl>\n<p>Various arrangements over the years have led to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a>, a passport-free zone that comprises the islands of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>. The area\'s internal borders are subject to minimal or non-existent border controls and can normally be crossed by Irish and British citizens with only minimal <a href="/wiki/Identity_document" title="Identity document">identity documents</a>, if any.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Inter-island relations</dt></dl>\n<p>The three crown dependencies, while independent, share a relatively similar position with respect to the United Kingdom and with international bodies such as the EU or the OECD. As a result, the crown dependencies work together on areas of mutual interest. For example, in 2000, the three states cooperated on development of common policies for offshore banking.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 2003, they developed a joint approach to certain EU activities around tax information.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The heads of government of the crown dependencies, including Isle of Man, Guernsey, Alderney, Sark, and Jersey, meet at an annual <i>inter-island</i> summit, to discuss matters of common concern, such as financial regulation and relations with the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 24 January 2013 Jersey, Guernsey and the Isle of Man signed <a href="/wiki/Double_taxation" title="Double taxation">double taxation</a> agreements with each other (in the case of Jersey and Guernsey, updating the existing agreement) .<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  This was the first time all three Crown Dependencies had established such mutual agrrements which also included provision for exchange of tax information equivalent to <a href="/wiki/TIEA" class="mw-redirect" title="TIEA">TIEAs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bilateral_relations">Bilateral Indeed, fostering such bilateral and multilateral relations between the countries was an explicit goal of the British Irish council.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One important body is the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a> (NSMC) <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> a body established by the British and Irish governments under the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> to co-ordinate activity and exercise certain governmental powers across the whole island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. The Council takes the form of meetings between ministers from both the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and is responsible for twelve policy areas. Six of these areas are the responsibility of corresponding North/South Implementation Bodies.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> has also established bilateral relations with three <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Ireland-Isle_of_Man_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Ireland-Isle of Man relations">Isle of Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland%E2%80%93Jersey_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Ireland\xe2\x80\x93Jersey relations">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland%E2%80%93Jersey_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Ireland\xe2\x80\x93Jersey relations">Guernsey</a>, for example through the signing of various bilateral tax agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The crown dependencies are also pursuing direct linkages with other countries in the Isles. For example, Scotland\'s first minister was invited to the Isle of Man in 2008, the first such visit of a Scottish minister to the Island.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, the two governments had a dispute over Isle of Man government laws restricting scallop fishing in the Irish sea, which were seen as unfair to Scottish fishermen.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Since 2011 the government of Jersey has sent representatives to the main party conferences of the United Kingdom, its "most significant economic partner", as part of a commitment to enhancing political engagement with the UK. In 2012 the Assistant Chief Minister attended the conference of the UK Liberal Democrats, the Chief Minister attended the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour Party (UK) Conference">UK Labour Party conference</a>, and the Deputy Chief Minister and Treasury and Resources Minister were announced to attend the UK Conservative Party conference.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The Deputy Chief Minister of Guernsey also attended the UK Liberal Democrats conference in 2012 to communicate the message that "Guernsey and the Channel Islands are good neighbours to the UK".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The Chief Minister of Guernsey, accompanied by the Commerce and Employment Minister, has been announced to attend the UK Conservative Party conference 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Guernsey\'s Deputy Chief Minister and Jersey\'s Assistant Chief Minister travelled to Dublin in September 2012 as a first step in a more coordinated approach to international relations. The purpose of the visit was to meet Ireland\'s Minister for European Affairs ahead of Ireland\'s assumption of the European Union presidency in 2013 for mutual discussions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ireland has also established bilateral relationships with Wales and Scotland. In 1999, Ireland opened consulates in Edinburgh and Cardiff, although the Cardiff consulate was closed in 2009 in order to cut costs.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The Irish and Welsh governments are collaborating on various economic development projects through the auspices of the Ireland Wales Programme, funded by the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Scotland and Ireland are also pursuing various forms of bilateral cooperation, evidenced by visits of the Irish Prime Minister to address the Scottish Parliament, and public statements by Scottish leaders calling for closer bilateral ties between Ireland and Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-salmond_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salmond-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the creation of devolved administrations in the UK, there are also increasing bilateral and multilateral relations between the constituent countries of the UK, covered in more detail in <a href="/wiki/Politics_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Politics in the United Kingdom">Politics in the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">Devolution in the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-salmond_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salmond-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_projects">Joint projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Joint projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Several joint projects amongst the various countries in the Isles have been undertaken, often around infrastructure and energy. For example, the governments of Ireland, Scotland, and Northern Ireland are collaborating on the <a href="/wiki/ISLES" class="mw-redirect" title="ISLES">ISLES</a> project, which will facilitate the development of offshore <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_sources" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable energy sources">renewable energy sources</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal</a> energy, and renewable energy trade between <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mcwilliams_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcwilliams-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Through the auspices of the British-Irish council, Ministers from the UK, Ireland, Isle of Man and the Channel Islands have agreed to work on energy cooperation and development through what is called the All Islands Approach (AIA), facilitating closer links between the energy markets of the various countries.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Isle of Man and Ireland are also planning the development of renewable energy sources, including sharing costs for the development of a wind farm off the coast of the Isle of Man.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> A wider collaboration is also planned, with Jersey, Guernsey, Isle of Man, and Ireland, to leverage the strong tidal currents around the Channel Islands.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An intergovernmental collaboration platform called the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Sea_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Irish Sea Region (page does not exist)">Irish Sea Region</a> has also been set up, managed by the Dublin regional authority. The platform links the governments of Ireland, Isle of Man, the UK, and various local jurisdictions, in order to collaborate on planning for development of the Irish sea and bordering areas.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2004, a natural gas interconnection agreement was signed between Ireland and the UK, linking Ireland with Scotland via the Isle of Man.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are three <a href="/wiki/General_lighthouse_authority" title="General lighthouse authority">lighthouse authorities</a> in the British Isles: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Lighthouse_Board" title="Northern Lighthouse Board">Northern Lighthouse Board</a> - responsible for Scotland and the Isle of Man; The <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House Lighthouse Service</a> - responsible for England, Wales, the Channel Islands and Gibraltar; <a href="/wiki/Commissioners_of_Irish_Lights" title="Commissioners of Irish Lights">Commissioners of Irish Lights</a> - responsible for Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland. These three authorities, responsible for provision of <a href="/wiki/Aid_to_navigation" class="mw-redirect" title="Aid to navigation">navigational aids</a> around the coasts of the British Isles, collaborate closely, and all draw on a single fund administered by the UK <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Department for Transport</a> and funded through <a href="/wiki/Light_dues" title="Light dues">light dues</a> levied on ships calling at UK and Irish ports.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Although this broad arrangement will continue, from 2015-16 the work of the Commissioners of Irish Lights in the Republic of Ireland will be funded entirely from domestic sources.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Citizenship_and_citizens_rights">Citizenship and citizens rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Citizenship and citizens rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British nationality law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationality_law" title="Irish nationality law">Irish nationality law</a>, and <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law_and_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="British nationality law and the Republic of Ireland">British nationality law and the Republic of Ireland</a></div><p>\nHistorically, citizens of Ireland were <a href="/wiki/British_subjects" class="mw-redirect" title="British subjects">British subjects</a>. Currently, people born in Northern Ireland are deemed by UK law to be <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">citizens of the United Kingdom</a> (unless neither parent is either a British or Irish citizen). They are also, with similar exceptions, entitled to be <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationality_law" title="Irish nationality law">citizens of Ireland</a>. This dual entitlement was reaffirmed in the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> between the British and Irish governments, which provides that:</p><blockquote><p>"...it is the birthright of all the people of Northern Ireland to identify themselves and be accepted as Irish or British, or both, as they may so choose, and accordingly [the two governments] confirm that their right to hold both British and Irish citizenship is accepted by both Governments and would not be affected by any future change in the status of Northern Ireland."</p></blockquote><p> Ireland and the United Kingdom provide reciprocal recognition to each other\'s citizens. Irish citizens resident in the UK can vote and stand in any UK elections. United Kingdom citizens resident in Ireland can vote or stand in European and local elections, and vote in parliamentary elections, but cannot vote or stand in Presidential elections or referendums. British and Irish citizens can avail of public services such as health care and <a href="/wiki/Social_welfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Social welfare">social welfare</a> in each other\'s jurisdiction on an equal basis, and are entitled to the right of abode, with deportation only in the most exceptional of circumstances.\n</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/British_Nationality_Act_1981" title="British Nationality Act 1981">British Nationality Act 1981</a> came into effect, the Crown dependencies have been treated as part of the United Kingdom for <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British nationality law</a> purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> However, each dependency maintains local controls over housing and employment, with special rules applying to British citizens without specified connections to that dependency (as well as to non-British citizens).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_movements">Political movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Unionism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionism in Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Unionism in the United Kingdom">Unionism in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>An important political movement in several countries in the Isles is <a href="/wiki/British_unionism" class="mw-redirect" title="British unionism">British unionism</a>, an ideology favouring the continued union of the United Kingdom. It is most prevalent in <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Scotland" title="Unionism in Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. <a href="/wiki/British_unionism" class="mw-redirect" title="British unionism">British unionism</a> has close ties to <a href="/wiki/British_nationalism" title="British nationalism">British nationalism</a>. Another movement is <a href="/wiki/Loyalism#Modern_loyalists_in_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="Loyalism">Loyalism</a>, which manifests itself as loyalism to the <a href="/wiki/British_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="British Crown">British Crown</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nationalism">Nationalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Nationalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The converse of unionism, <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>, is also an important factor for politics in the Isles. Nationalism can take the form of <a href="/wiki/Welsh_nationalism" title="Welsh nationalism">Welsh nationalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_nationalism" title="Cornish nationalism">Cornish nationalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_nationalism" title="English nationalism">English nationalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Irish nationalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_nationalism" title="Scottish nationalism">Scottish nationalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulster_nationalism" title="Ulster nationalism">Ulster nationalism</a> or independence movements in the Isle of Man or Channel Islands.<sup id="cite_ref-jerseyindependence_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerseyindependence-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">Identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Identity is intertwined with politics, especially in the case of nationalism and independence movements. Details on identity formation in the British Isles can be found at <a href="/wiki/Britishness" title="Britishness">Britishness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_identity" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish identity">Scottish identity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_people#Irish_Identity" title="Irish people">Irish people</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pan-Celticism" title="Pan-Celticism">Pan-Celticism</a> is also a movement which is present in several of the countries which have a Celtic heritage.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_parties">Political parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Political parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are no major political parties that are present in all of the countries, but several Irish parties such as <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna F\xc3\xa1il">Fianna F\xc3\xa1il</a> have won seats in both the Republic of Ireland and Northern Ireland, and both of these parties have established offices in Britain in order to raise funds and win additional supporters.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarship_of_identity_and_politics_in_the_British_Isles">Scholarship of identity and politics in the British Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_in_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Scholarship of identity and politics in the British Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Several academic perspectives are important in the politics and relations in the British isles. Important strands of scholarship include research on identity, especially <a href="/wiki/Britishness" title="Britishness">Britishness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_people#Irish_Identity" title="Irish people">Irish identity</a>, and studies of the major political movements, such as separatism, <a href="/wiki/British_unionism" class="mw-redirect" title="British unionism">unionism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Post-nationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-nationalism">post-nationalism</a> is also a contemporary trend in studies of history, culture and politics in the isles.\n</p><p>The recent trend of using an <i>archipelago</i> perspective in scholarship of history, politics and identity was initiated by historian <a href="/wiki/J._G._A._Pocock" title="J. G. A. Pocock">J. G. A. Pocock</a> in the 1970s. He coined the term <i>Atlantic archipelago</i> as a replacement for <i>British Isles</i>, and he pressed his fellow historians to reconsider two issues linked to the future of British history. First, he urged historians of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> to move away from histories of the Three Kingdoms (Scotland, Ireland, England) as separate entities,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> and he called for studies implementing a bringing-together or conflation of these national narratives into truly integrated enterprises. Pocock proposed the term <i>Atlantic archipelago</i> to avoid the contested <i>British isles</i>. It has since become the commonplace preference of historians to treat British history in just this fashion (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Kearney" title="Hugh Kearney">Hugh Kearney</a>\'s <i>The British Isles: A History of Four Nations</i> or <a href="/wiki/Norman_Davies" title="Norman Davies">Norman Davies</a> <i>The Isles: A History</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In recent times, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Kearney" title="Richard Kearney">Richard Kearney</a> has been an important scholar in this space, through his works for example on a Postnationalist Archipelago.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> While Kearney\'s work has been noted by many as important for understanding of modern Irish politics and identity, some have also argued that his approach can be applied to the archipelago as a whole: "Scholars and critics have noted the importance of Kearney\'s work on post-nationalism for Irish studies and politics. However, less attention has been paid to its implications for discussions and debates beyond the Irish Sea. In this context, Kearney\'s writings can be viewed as part of a broader intellectual landscape in which national identity, nationalism, and possibly postnationalism are at the center of political and intellectual discussions in the Isles. I say the Isles here, rather than simply Britain, because re-imagining the component parts of Britain, or more precisely the United Kingdom, entails reconfiguring the relationships in the entire archipelago."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Kearney\'s ideas and thinking were important in the lead-up to the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>, and he was an early proponent of what eventually became the <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Council">British-Irish Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The University of Exeter in the UK and the Moore Institute at the National University of Ireland, Galway started in October 2010 the Atlantic Archipelago Research Project, which purports to "take an interdisciplinary view on how Britain\xe2\x80\x99s post-devolution state inflects the formation of post-split Welsh, Scottish and English identities in the context of Ireland\xe2\x80\x99s own experience of partition and self-rule; Consider the significance of this island grouping to the understanding of a Europe that exists in a range of configurations; from large scale political union, to provinces, dependencies, and micro-nationalist regions (such as Cornwall), each with their contribution and presence; Reconsider relations across our island grouping in light of issues regarding the management and use of the environment."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Although they are proud of their British associations, the Crown Dependencies are not part of the United Kingdom and are\nautonomous and self-governing, with their own, independent legal, administrative and fiscal systems. The Island parliaments legislate for themselves. UK legislation and international treaties are only extended to them with their consent. It has been argued that Westminster retains a residual legislative power over the Islands in order to avoid \xe2\x80\x9cthe impossible position of having responsibility without power\xe2\x80\x9d. Within HM Government, the Ministry of Justice is the point of contact for the Crown Dependencies, and communications in both directions are passed through its offices.
