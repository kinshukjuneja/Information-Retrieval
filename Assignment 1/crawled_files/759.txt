England</a> and one of the most famous English admirals of the 17th century, whose successes have "never been excelled, not even by <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Nelson</a>" according to one biographer.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Blake is recognised as the chief founder of England\'s <a href="/wiki/Naval_supremacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval supremacy">naval supremacy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-WestminsterAbbey_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestminsterAbbey-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hansard_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hansard-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> a dominance subsequently inherited by the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> into the early 20th century. Despite this, due to deliberate attempts to expunge the Parliamentarians from history following the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration</a>, Blake\'s achievements tend not to receive the full recognition that they deserve.<sup id="cite_ref-hansard_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hansard-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Blake family had a seat for several generations at (and were Lords of the Manor of) Tuxwell, in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Bishops_Lydeard" title="Bishops Lydeard">Bishops Lydeard</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Bridgwater" title="Bridgwater">Bridgwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>. The earliest member of the family located in records was Humphrey Blake, who lived in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a>. Admiral Blake\'s grandfather, also named Robert, was the first of the family to strike out on his own from country life as a merchant, hoping to become rich from Spanish trade. He served as chief magistrate of Bridgwater several times, in recognition of the esteem in which the townspeople held him. His son, Humphrey, succeeded him in business, and in addition to his father\'s estates at Puriton (of which he held the lordship), Catcote, Bawdrippe, and Wollamington, came into the estate at Plansfield held by the family of his wife, Sara Williams, since the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Robert Blake was the first son of thirteen children born to Humphrey and Sara.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He attended Bridgwater Grammar School for Boys, then went up to <a href="/wiki/Wadham_College,_Oxford" title="Wadham College, Oxford">Wadham College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a>. He had hoped to follow an academic career, but failed to secure a fellowship to <a href="/wiki/Merton_College,_Oxford" title="Merton College, Oxford">Merton College</a>, probably in consideration of his political and religious views, but also because the warden of Merton, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Savile_(Bible_translator)" title="Henry Savile (Bible translator)">Sir Henry Savile</a>, had \'an eccentric distaste for men of low stature\'. Blake, at five feet, six inches tall, thus failed to meet Savile\'s \'standard of manly beauty\'.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After his departure from university in 1625, it is believed that Blake was engaged in trade, and a Dutch writer subsequently claimed that he had lived for \'five of six years\' in <a href="/wiki/Schiedam" title="Schiedam">Schiedam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Having returned to Bridgwater, probably because of the death of his mother in 1638, he decided to stand for election to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1640 Blake was elected as the Member of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a> for <a href="/wiki/Bridgwater_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Bridgwater (UK Parliament constituency)">Bridgwater</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Short_Parliament" title="Short Parliament">Short Parliament</a>. When the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> broke out during the period of the <a href="/wiki/Long_Parliament" title="Long Parliament">Long Parliament</a>, and having failed to be re-elected, Blake began his military career on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">parliamentarians</a> despite having no substantial experience of military or naval matters.\n</p><p>He would later return to recover from an injury sustained in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Portland" title="Battle of Portland">Battle of Portland</a>. During that time he represented Bridgwater in the Barebone\'s Parliament of 1653 and First Protectorate Parliament of 1654 and <a href="/wiki/Taunton_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Taunton (UK Parliament constituency)">Taunton</a> in the Second Protectorate Parliament of 1656 before returning to sea.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="On_land">On land</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: On land">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After joining the <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Captain_(land)" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain (land)">captain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Popham" title="Alexander Popham">Alexander Popham\'s</a> regiment, Blake distinguished himself at the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Bristol_(1643)" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege of Bristol (1643)">Siege of Bristol</a> (July 1643) and was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel" title="Lieutenant colonel">lieutenant colonel</a>. After his leading role in holding Lyme Regis in the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Lyme_Regis" title="Siege of Lyme Regis">Siege of Lyme Regis</a> (April 1644) he was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a>. He went on to hold the Parliamentary enclave of Taunton during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Taunton" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege of Taunton">Siege of Taunton</a> (1645), which earned him national recognition and where he famously declared that he would eat three of his four pairs of boots before he would surrender. He subsequently succeeded in winning the Siege of <a href="/wiki/Dunster" title="Dunster">Dunster</a> (November 1645).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="At_sea">At sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: At sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Blake was appointed <a href="/wiki/General_at_sea" title="General at sea">general at sea</a> in 1649.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Although it is commonly used, Blake\'s name was never prefixed by \'admiral\', a rank which was not used in the Parliamentarian navy; his actual rank of general at sea combined the role of an <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">admiral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lords_Commissioners_of_the_Admiralty" class="mw-redirect" title="Lords Commissioners of the Admiralty">commissioner of the Navy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1651 he led a force to successfully remove the Royalist <a href="/wiki/John_Grenville,_1st_Earl_of_Bath" class="mw-redirect" title="John Grenville, 1st Earl of Bath">Sir John Grenville</a> from the Scilly Isles, where he had been appointed <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_the_Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of the Scilly Isles">Governor</a> by Charles II after a local rebellion.\n</p><p>Blake is often referred to as the \'Father of the Royal Navy\'. As well as being largely responsible for building the largest navy the country had then ever known, from a few tens of ships to well over a hundred, he was first to keep a fleet at sea over the winter. Blake also produced the navy\'s first ever set of rules and regulations, <i>The Laws of War and Ordinances of the Sea</i>, the first version of which, containing 20 provisions, was passed by the House of Commons on 5 March 1649,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> with a printed version published in 1652 as <i>The Laws of War and Ordinances of the Sea (Ordained and Established by the Parliament of the Commonwealth of England)</i>, listing 39 offences and their punishments&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 mostly death.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Instructions of the Admirals and generals of the Fleet for Councils of War</i>, issued in 1653 by Blake, George Monck, <a href="/wiki/John_Disbrowe" class="mw-redirect" title="John Disbrowe">John Disbrowe</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Penn_(Royal_Navy_officer)" title="William Penn (Royal Navy officer)">William Penn</a>, also instituted the first naval <a href="/wiki/Court-martial#United_Kingdom" title="Court-martial">courts-martial</a> in the English navy.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Blake developed new techniques to conduct blockades and landings; his <i>Sailing instructions</i> and <i>Fighting Instructions</i>, which were major overhauls of <a href="/wiki/Naval_tactics" title="Naval tactics">naval tactics</a> written while recovering from injury in 1653, were the foundation of English <a href="/wiki/Naval_tactics_in_the_Age_of_Sail" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval tactics in the Age of Sail">naval tactics in the Age of Sail</a>. Blake\'s <i>Fighting Instructions</i>, issued by the generals at sea on 29 March 1653, are the first known instructions to be written in any language to adopt the use of the <a href="/wiki/Line_of_battle" title="Line of battle">single line ahead</a> battle formation<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Blake was also the first to repeatedly successfully attack despite fire from shore forts.<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1656, the year before his death, Blake was appointed <a href="/wiki/Lord_Warden_of_the_Cinque_Ports" title="Lord Warden of the Cinque Ports">Lord Warden of the Cinque Ports</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Civil_War">English Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: English Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>On 11 January 1649 <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert_of_the_Rhine" title="Prince Rupert of the Rhine">Prince Rupert of the Rhine</a> led eight undermanned ships to Kinsale in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> in an attempt to prevent the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Parliamentarians</a> taking Ireland from the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalists</a>. Blake blockaded Rupert\'s fleet in Kinsale from 22 May, allowing <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> to land at Dublin on 15 August. Blake was driven off by a storm in October and Rupert escaped via Spain to Lisbon, where he had expanded his fleet to 13 ships. Blake put to sea with 12 ships in February 1650 and dropped anchor off Lisbon in an attempt to persuade the Portuguese king to expel Rupert. After two months the king decided to back Rupert. Blake was joined by another four warships commanded by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Popham" title="Edward Popham">Edward Popham</a>, who brought authority to go to war with <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.\n</p><p>Rupert twice failed to break the blockade, which was finally raised after Blake sailed for <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C\xc3\xa1diz">C\xc3\xa1diz</a> with seven ships he had captured after a three-hour engagement with 23 ships of the Portuguese fleet (during which the Portuguese vice-admiral was also sunk.) Blake re-engaged with Rupert, now with six ships, on 3 November near M\xc3\xa1laga, capturing one ship. Two days later Rupert\'s other ships in the area were driven ashore attempting to escape from Cartagena, securing Parliamentarian supremacy at sea, and the recognition of the Parliamentary government by many European states. Parliament voted Blake 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Pound_Sterling" class="mw-redirect" title="Pound Sterling">pounds</a> by way of thanks in February 1651. In June of the same year Blake captured the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, the last outpost of the Royalist navy, for which he again received Parliament\'s thanks. Soon afterwards he was made a member of the <a href="/wiki/English_Council_of_State" title="English Council of State">Council of State</a>.\n</p><p>Thanks to its command of the sea, the fleet was able to supply Cromwell\'s army with provisions as it successfully marched on Scotland. By the end of 1652 the various English colonies in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> had also been secured.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Anglo-Dutch_War">First Anglo-Dutch War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First Anglo-Dutch War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/First_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="First Anglo-Dutch War">First Anglo-Dutch War</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>Blake\'s next adventures were during the <a href="/wiki/First_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="First Anglo-Dutch War">First Anglo-Dutch War</a>. The war started prematurely with a skirmish between the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> fleet of <a href="/wiki/Maarten_Tromp" title="Maarten Tromp">Maarten Tromp</a> and Blake off Folkestone on 29 May 1652, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Goodwin_Sands" title="Battle of Goodwin Sands">Battle of Goodwin Sands</a>. The proper war started in June with an English campaign against the Dutch East Indies, Baltic and fishing trades by Blake, in command of around 60 ships. On 5 October 1652 Dutch Vice-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Witte_Corneliszoon_de_With" title="Witte Corneliszoon de With">Witte Corneliszoon de With</a>, underestimating the strength of the English, attempted to attack Blake, but due to the weather it was Blake who attacked on 8 October 1652 in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Kentish_Knock" title="Battle of the Kentish Knock">Battle of the Kentish Knock</a>, sending de With back to the Netherlands in defeat. The English government seemed to think that the war was over and sent ships away to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. Blake had only 42 warships when he was attacked and decisively defeated by 88 Dutch ships under Tromp on 9 December 1652 in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dungeness" title="Battle of Dungeness">Battle of Dungeness</a>, losing control of the English Channel to the Dutch. Meanwhile, the ships sent away had also been defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leghorn" title="Battle of Leghorn">Battle of Leghorn</a>.\n</p><p>Following the navy\'s poor performance at Dungeness, Blake demanded that the <a href="/wiki/Lords_Commissioners_of_the_Admiralty" class="mw-redirect" title="Lords Commissioners of the Admiralty">Lords Commissioners of the Admiralty</a> enact major reforms.  They complied by, among other things, enacting <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_War_(Royal_Navy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Articles of War (Royal Navy)">Articles of War</a> to reinforce the authority of an admiral over his captains.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Blake then sailed with around 75 ships to disrupt Channel shipping, engaging Tromp with a similar sized fleet in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Portland" title="Battle of Portland">Battle of Portland</a> from 28 February to 2 March 1653 when Tromp escaped with his convoy under cover of darkness.\n</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Gabbard" title="Battle of the Gabbard">Battle of the Gabbard</a> on 12 and 13 June 1653 Blake reinforced the ships of Generals <a href="/wiki/Richard_Deane_(regicide)" title="Richard Deane (regicide)">Richard Deane</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Monck" class="mw-redirect" title="George Monck">George Monck</a> and decisively defeated the Dutch fleet, sinking or capturing 17 ships without losing one. Now also the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> was brought under English control, and the Dutch fleet was blockaded in various ports until the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Scheveningen" title="Battle of Scheveningen">Battle of Scheveningen</a>, where Tromp was killed.\n</p><p>Peace with the Dutch achieved, Blake sailed in October 1654 with 24 warships to the Mediterranean, successfully deterring the <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Duke_of_Guise" title="Henry II, Duke of Guise">Duke of Guise</a> from conquering Naples.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bey_of_Tunis">Bey of Tunis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bey of Tunis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary pirate">Barbary pirate</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>In April 1655 Blake was sent to the Mediterranean again to extract compensation from the piratical states that had been attacking English shipping. The <a href="/wiki/Bey" title="Bey">Bey</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> alone refused compensation, and with 15 ships Blake destroyed the two shore batteries and nine Algerian ships in Porto Farina, the first time shore batteries had been taken out without landing men ashore.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Spanish_War">Anglo-Spanish War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Anglo-Spanish War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:246px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_George_at_Santa_Cruz_de_Tenerife.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f3/The_George_at_Santa_Cruz_de_Tenerife.jpg" width="244" height="186" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="244" data-file-height="186" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_George_at_Santa_Cruz_de_Tenerife.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robert Blake\'s flagship the <i>George</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Santa_Cruz_de_Tenerife_(1657)" title="Battle of Santa Cruz de Tenerife (1657)">Battle of Santa Cruz de Tenerife, 1657</a></div></div></div>\n<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1654)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1654)">Anglo-Spanish War (1654)</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>In February 1656 commercial rivalry with Spain was soon turned to war. In the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1654)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1654)">Anglo-Spanish War</a> Blake blockaded <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C\xc3\xa1diz">C\xc3\xa1diz</a>, during which one of his captains, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Stayner" title="Richard Stayner">Richard Stayner</a>, destroyed most of the Spanish plate fleet at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_C%C3%A1diz_(1656)" title="Battle of C\xc3\xa1diz (1656)">Battle of C\xc3\xa1diz</a>. A galleon of treasure was captured, and the overall loss to Spain was estimated at \xc2\xa32,000,000. Blake maintained the blockade throughout the winter, the first time the fleet had stayed at sea over winter.\n</p><p>On 20 April 1657 Blake totally destroyed another armed merchant convoy, the Spanish West Indian fleet, in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Santa_Cruz_de_Tenerife_(1657)" title="Battle of Santa Cruz de Tenerife (1657)">Battle of Santa Cruz de Tenerife</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 a port so well fortified that it was thought to be impregnable to attack from the sea<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 for the loss of just one ship. Although the silver had already been landed, Blake\'s victory delayed its arrival at the royal treasury of the Spanish government and earned the new English Navy respect throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> As a reward Blake was given an expensive diamond ring by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Cromwell</a>. The action also earned him respect 140 years later from <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Lord Nelson</a> who lost his arm there in a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Santa_Cruz_de_Tenerife_(1797)" title="Battle of Santa Cruz de Tenerife (1797)">failed attack</a>; in a letter written on 17 April 1797, to <a href="/wiki/John_Jervis,_1st_Earl_of_St_Vincent" title="John Jervis, 1st Earl of St Vincent">Admiral Sir John Jervis</a>, Nelson wrote "I do not reckon myself equal to Blake", before going on to outline the plans for his own attack.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Lord Nelson</a> ranked Robert Blake as one of the greatest naval generals ever known, even when compared with his own reputation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Blake_(admiral)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blake_statue_bridgwater.jpg" class="image"><img alt="In the foreground is a statue of a man on a plinth above steps, with person sitting on them. After <a href="/wiki/Lying_in_state" title="Lying in state">lying in state</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_House" title="Queen&#39;s House">Queen\'s House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a>, he was given a full <a href="/wiki/State_funerals_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="State funerals in the United Kingdom">state funeral</a> and was buried in <a buried at St Andrew\'s Church, Plymouth).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/The_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="The Restoration">restoration of the Monarchy</a> his body was exhumed in 1661 and dumped in a common grave in <a href="/wiki/St_Margaret%27s,_Westminster" title="St Margaret&#39;s, Westminster">St Margaret\'s churchyard</a>, adjoining the Abbey, on the orders of the new king, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WestminsterAbbey_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestminsterAbbey-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honouring_Blake">Honouring Blake</span><span /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parliamentarian-reburials-september-1661.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Memorial marking the reburial of title="Westminster Abbey Burials and Memorials">Westminster Abbey</a>, a stone memorial of Robert Blake, unveiled on 27 February 1945, can be found in the south choir aisle.<sup Westminster">St Margaret\'s Church</a>, where Blake was reburied, has a stained glass window depicting Blake\'s life, together with a brass plaque to his memory, unveiled on 18 December 1888.<sup house in Bridgwater where it is believed that Blake was born, was purchased and turned into the <a href="/wiki/Blake_Museum" title="Blake Museum">Blake Museum</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> where a room is devoted to him and his exploits.\n</p><p>Blake and his <a href="/wiki/Flagship" title="Flagship">flagship</a> <i>Triumph</i> featured on a second class <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamp</a> issued in 1982.\n</p><p>In 2007 various events took place in Bridgwater, Somerset, from April to September to commemorate the 350th anniversary of the death of Robert Blake. These included a civic ceremony on 8 July 2007 and a 17th-century market on 15 July 2007.\n</p><p>In the Royal Navy a series of ships have carried the name <a href="/wiki/HMS_Blake" title="HMS Blake">HMS <i>Blake</i></a> in honour of the general at sea. The bell of the last HMS <i>Blake</i>, scrapped in 1982, is on display in Saint Mary\'s Church, Bridgwater.\n</p><p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blake_oilfield&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blake oilfield (page does not exist)">Blake oilfield</a> in the United Kingdom Sector of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_oil" title="North Sea oil">North Sea</a> is named in honour of
