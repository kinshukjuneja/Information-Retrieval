href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a>.  He currently serves as a special assistant to the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> and is the manager of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_baseball_team" title="United States national baseball team">United States national baseball team</a>.\n</p><p>He led the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins" title="Miami Marlins">Florida Marlins</a> to a <a href="/wiki/1997_World_Series" title="1997 World Series">World Series</a> championship in 1997, and previously won three straight division titles (<a href="/wiki/1990_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1990 National League Championship Series">1990</a>, <a href="/wiki/1991_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1991 National League Championship Series">1991</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1992_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1992 National League Championship Series">1992</a>) with the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>. With the Tigers victory in the <a href="/wiki/2006_American_League_Championship_Series" title="2006 American League Championship Series">2006 American League Championship Series</a>, Leyland became the seventh manager in history to win pennants in both the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American Leagues</a>. He is a three-time <a href="/wiki/Manager_of_the_Year_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Manager of the Year Award">Manager of the Year Award</a> winner, twice in the National League (1990 and 1992), and once in the American League (2006).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toclevel-1 tocsection-10"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">10</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Leyland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Leyland began his baseball career with the Tigers when they signed him as a <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">catcher</a> on September 21, 1963. He spent seven seasons as a minor leaguer in the Tigers organization (1964\xe2\x80\x931970), but mainly served as a coach with the <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_Rebels" title="Montgomery Rebels">Montgomery Rebels</a> in 1970 while playing in just two games for the team. Leyland was a career .222 <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">hitter</a> in the minor leagues.\n</p><p>In 1972, Leyland became a minor league manager in the Tigers organization; beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Pilots" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinton Pilots">Clinton Pilots</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Midwest_League" title="Midwest League">Midwest League</a>; from 1979\xe2\x80\x931981, he was the manager of the <a href="/wiki/Evansville_Triplets" title="Evansville Triplets">Evansville Triplets</a> winning two divisions (1979, 1981) in the American Association.\n</p><p>Leyland left the Tigers organization for the first time in 1982 when he became <a href="/wiki/Tony_La_Russa" title="Tony La Russa">Tony La Russa</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Third_base_coach" class="mw-redirect" title="Third base coach">third base coach</a> for four seasons (1982\xe2\x80\x9385) with the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>, including the team\'s 1983 AL West division title, before being named the 33rd manager in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> history on November 20, 1985.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pittsburgh_Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Leyland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pittsburgh Pirates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Leyland was the manager of the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> from 1986 to 1996. He won two <a href="/wiki/Manager_of_the_Year_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Manager of the Year Award">Manager of the Year</a> trophies with the Pirates in 1990 and 1992, and finished as runner-up in 1988 and 1991.  Leyland helped develop such <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Stars</a> as <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay_Bell" title="Jay Bell">Jay Bell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Wakefield" title="Tim Wakefield">Tim Wakefield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Van_Slyke" title="Andy Van Slyke">Andy Van Slyke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Bonilla" title="Bobby Bonilla">Bobby Bonilla</a> in Pittsburgh before a <a href="/wiki/Fire_sale_(sports)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire sale (sports)">fire sale</a> in the mid-1990s soured him with new ownership. Under Leyland, the Pirates went to the <a href="/wiki/National_League_Championship_Series" title="National League Championship Series">National League Championship Series</a> in three straight seasons (<a href="/wiki/1990_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1990 National League Championship Series">1990</a>, <a href="/wiki/1991_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1991 National League Championship Series">1991</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1992_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1992 National League Championship Series">1992</a>). The Pirates lost all three of those NLCS, however, with the latter two going the full seven games against the Atlanta Braves.\n</p><p>Although he has moved on in his career, Leyland still keeps his home in the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> suburb of <a href="/wiki/Mt._Lebanon,_Pennsylvania" title="Mt. Lebanon, Pennsylvania">Mt. Lebanon, Pennsylvania</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> where he met his wife Katie<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and has raised two children, Pat<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and Kellie.\n</p><p>One member of Leyland\'s coaching staff while with the Pirates, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Collins" title="Terry Collins">Terry Collins</a>, who was most recently the manager of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a>, wore number 10 to honor Leyland.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida_Marlins_and_Colorado_Rockies">Florida Marlins and Colorado Rockies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Leyland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Florida Marlins and Colorado Rockies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1997, Leyland was hired by <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Huizenga" title="Wayne Huizenga">Wayne Huizenga</a> to manage the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins" title="Miami Marlins">Florida Marlins</a> and promptly led them to the franchise\'s first championship. The Marlins, in only their fifth year of existence, became the fastest expansion franchise to win a World Series. The <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> surpassed the Marlins when they won the World Series in 2001, their fourth season.\n</p><p>In the offseason, Huizenga dismantled the team in what became known as "the <a href="/wiki/Fire_sale_(sports)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire sale (sports)">fire sale</a>." After Game 7, when asked about rumors that he might retire if Huizenga sold the franchise, Leyland quipped, "My wife doesn\'t like me that much. I can\'t retire."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Leyland indeed remained as the manager, but resigned after the 1998 season, when the Marlins went 54\xe2\x80\x93108. At his press conference, Leyland remarked that he thought his job was to win championships, but that apparently was not what Huizenga wanted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Leyland was subsequently hired by the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Rockies" title="Colorado Rockies">Colorado Rockies</a> for the 1999 season, walking away from the final two years of his contract.\n</p><p>When he left managing after the 1999 season, he became a Pittsburgh-based <a href="/wiki/Scout_(sport)" title="Scout (sport)">scout</a> for the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detroit_Tigers">Detroit Tigers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Leyland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Detroit Tigers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Leyland_1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Leyland_1.JPG/220px-Leyland_1.JPG" width="220" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Leyland_1.JPG/330px-Leyland_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Leyland_1.JPG/440px-Leyland_1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="779" data-file-height="829" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Leyland_1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leyland hits balls to <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Cabrera" title="Miguel Cabrera">Miguel Cabrera</a> during pregame warmups at Kauffman Stadium, June 4, 2010</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the release of <a href="/wiki/Alan_Trammell" title="Alan Trammell">Alan Trammell</a> as the manager of the Tigers, Leyland was hired as new Tigers manager, returning to the franchise with which he spent the first 18 years of his professional baseball career.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It marked the first time Leyland managed in the American League.\n</p><p>In the 2006 regular season, Leyland guided the Tigers to a 95\xe2\x80\x9367 record, the Tigers best season since 1987. The Tigers entered the playoffs as a <a href="/wiki/Wild_card_(sports)" title="Wild card (sports)">wild card</a>, and went on to defeat the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> and sweep the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> to win the American League pennant before falling to the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2006_World_Series" title="2006 World Series">2006 World Series</a>. In leading the team to the AL pennant, he became the seventh manager to win pennants in both major leagues, joining <a href="/wiki/Joe_McCarthy_(manager)" title="Joe McCarthy (manager)">Joe McCarthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Berra" title="Yogi Berra">Yogi Berra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Dark" title="Alvin Dark">Alvin Dark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sparky_Anderson" title="Sparky Anderson">Sparky Anderson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dick_Williams" title="Dick Williams">Dick Williams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tony_La_Russa" title="Tony La Russa">Tony La Russa</a>. After the 2006 season ended, Leyland was recognized with the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Manager_of_the_Year_Award" title="Major League Baseball Manager of the Year Award">Manager of the Year</a> award for the third time in his career. He became the third person to win the award in both leagues, joining La Russa and <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Cox" title="Bobby Cox">Bobby Cox</a>. Leyland also won <a href="/wiki/The_Sporting_News_Manager_of_the_Year_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="The Sporting News Manager of the Year Award">The Sporting News Manager of the Year Award</a> for the American League in 2006.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg/220px-Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg/330px-Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg/440px-Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jim_Leyland_pregame_at_Dodger_Stadium.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leyland in Tigers dugout at <a href="/wiki/Dodger_Stadium" title="Dodger Stadium">Dodger Stadium</a>, June 22, 2011</div></div></div>\n<p>In 2011, Leyland led the Tigers to another 95\xe2\x80\x9367 regular season record, winning the American League Central Division. They went on to defeat the New York Yankees in the <a href="/wiki/2011_American_League_Division_Series" title="2011 American League Division Series">American League Division Series</a> before losing to the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2011_American_League_Championship_Series" title="2011 American League Championship Series">American League Championship Series</a>.\n</p><p>On May 1, 2012, Leyland gained his 1,600th victory as a major league manager, passing <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lasorda" title="Tommy Lasorda">Tommy Lasorda</a> on the all-time wins list.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, Leyland led the Tigers to an 88\xe2\x80\x9374 regular season record, winning the American League Central Division. On that team, Tiger third baseman <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Cabrera" title="Miguel Cabrera">Miguel Cabrera</a> was the American League Triple Crown winner that season. This was the first Triple Crown winner in Major League Baseball since <a href="/wiki/Carl_Yastrzemski" title="Carl Yastrzemski">Carl Yastrzemski</a> in 1967. When the Tigers won the AL Central for the second consecutive season, Leyland became the only Tigers manager besides <a href="/wiki/Hughie_Jennings" title="Hughie Jennings">Hughie Jennings</a> to lead Detroit to the postseason three times.\n</p><p>In the 2012 postseason, Detroit defeated the Athletics in a five-game <a href="/wiki/2012_ALDS" class="mw-redirect" title="2012 ALDS">ALDS</a> and returned to the World Series following a sweep of the New York Yankees in the <a href="/wiki/2012_American_League_Championship_Series" title="2012 American League Championship Series">American League Championship Series</a>. Detroit was swept in the <a href="/wiki/2012_World_Series" title="2012 World Series">2012 World Series</a> by the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a>. They were shut out twice, the same number as in the entire 162-game regular season, and had a team batting average of .159.\n</p><p>On September 25, 2013, Leyland won his 700th game with the Tigers. With the 1\xe2\x80\x930 victory over the Minnesota Twins, the Tigers clinched their third consecutive American League Central Division title.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Leyland joined former coach <a href="/wiki/Tony_La_Russa" title="Tony La Russa">Tony La Russa</a> as the only managers who have led two different MLB franchises to three consecutive division titles. He retired from managing following the 2013 season, remaining with the Tigers organization as a special assistant.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Brad_Ausmus" title="Brad Ausmus">Brad Ausmus</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Team_USA">Team USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Leyland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Team USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On April 15, 2016, Leyland was named the manager of <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_baseball_team" title="United States national baseball team">Team USA</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2017_World_Baseball_Classic" title="2017 World Baseball Classic">2017 World Baseball Classic</a>. The United States won the 2017 tournament under his leadership, making Leyland the only manager to win a World Series and a WBC title.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a
