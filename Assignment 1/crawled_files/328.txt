href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucestershire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Gloucestershire" title="History of Gloucestershire">History of Gloucestershire</a></div>\n<p>Gloucestershire is a historic county mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> in the 10th century, though the areas of <a href="/wiki/Winchcombe" title="Winchcombe">Winchcombe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> were not added until the late 11th century. Gloucestershire originally included Bristol, then a small town. The local rural community moved to the port city (as Bristol was to become), and Bristol\'s population growth accelerated during the industrial revolution. Bristol became a county in its own right, separate from Gloucestershire and Somerset in 1373. It later became part of the administrative <a href="/wiki/County_of_Avon" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Avon">County of Avon</a> from 1974 to 1996.\n</p><p>Upon the abolition of Avon in 1996, the region north of Bristol became a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a> area of <a href="/wiki/South_Gloucestershire" title="South Gloucestershire">South Gloucestershire</a> and is now part of the <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceremonial county">ceremonial county</a> of Gloucestershire.\n</p><p>The official former postal county abbreviation was "Glos.", rather than the frequently used but erroneous "Gloucs." or "Glouc.".\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/2007_United_Kingdom_floods" title="2007 United Kingdom floods">July 2007, Gloucestershire suffered the worst flooding</a> in recorded British history, with tens of thousands of residents affected. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a> conducted the largest peace time domestic operation in its history to rescue over 120 residents from flood affected areas. The damage was estimated at over \xc2\xa32&#160;billion.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The county recovered rapidly from the disaster, investing in attracting tourists to visit the many sites and diverse range of shops in the area.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_environment">Geography and environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucestershire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography and environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Gloucestershire has three main landscape areas, a large part of the Cotswolds, the Royal Forest of Dean and the Severn Vale. The Cotswolds take up a large portion of the east and south of the county, The Forest of Dean taking up the west, with the Severn and its valley running between these features. The Daffodil Way in the <a href="/wiki/River_Leadon" title="River Leadon">Leadon</a> Valley, on the border of Gloucestershire and Herefordshire surrounding the village of <a href="/wiki/Dymock" title="Dymock">Dymock</a>, is known for its many spring flowers, orchards, and woodland, which attracts many walkers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span with suburbs in Gloucestershire:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chepstow" title="Chepstow">Chepstow</a></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_belt">Green belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucestershire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Green belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Avon_Green_Belt" title="Avon Green Belt">Avon Green Belt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_and_Cheltenham_Green_Belt" title="Gloucester and Cheltenham Green Belt">Gloucester and Cheltenham Green Belt</a></div>\n<p>The county has two green belt areas, the first covers the southern area in the South Gloucestershire district, to protect outlying villages and towns between Thornbury and Chipping Sodbury from the urban sprawl of the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Built-up_Area" title="Bristol Built-up Area">Bristol conurbation</a>. The second belt lies around Gloucester, Cheltenham, and Bishop\'s Cleeve, to afford those areas and villages in between a protection from urban sprawl and further convergence. Both belts intersect with the boundaries of the Cotswolds <a href="/wiki/AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="AONB">AONB</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquities">Antiquities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucestershire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Antiquities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are a variety of religious buildings across the county, notably the cathedral of <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Cathedral" title="Gloucester Cathedral">Gloucester</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tewkesbury_Abbey" title="Tewkesbury Abbey">abbey church</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tewkesbury" title="Tewkesbury">Tewkesbury</a> (which is over 500 years old and has the tallest Norman tower in England), and the church of <a href="/wiki/Cirencester" title="Cirencester">Cirencester</a>. Of the abbey of <a href="/wiki/Hailes_Abbey" title="Hailes Abbey">Hailes</a> near <a href="/wiki/Winchcombe" title="Winchcombe">Winchcombe</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Richard,_Earl_of_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard, Earl of Cornwall">Richard, Earl of Cornwall</a>, in 1246, little more than the foundations are left, but these have been excavated and fragments have been brought to light.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:October_plenty.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/October_plenty.jpg/220px-October_plenty.jpg" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/October_plenty.jpg/330px-October_plenty.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/October_plenty.jpg/440px-October_plenty.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5003" data-file-height="4243" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:October_plenty.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Parish Church of St. Mary, Fairford</div></div></div>\n<p>Most of the old <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market towns</a> have <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish churches</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Deerhurst" title="Deerhurst">Deerhurst</a> near Tewkesbury and <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Cleeve" title="Bishop&#39;s Cleeve">Bishop\'s Cleeve</a> near <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>, there are churches of special interest on account of the pre-Norman work they retain. There is also a <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular_Period" class="mw-redirect" title="Perpendicular Period">Perpendicular</a> church in <a href="/wiki/Lechlade" title="Lechlade">Lechlade</a>, and that at <a href="/wiki/Fairford" title="Fairford">Fairford</a> was built (c. 1500), according to tradition, to contain a series of <a href="/wiki/Stained-glass_windows" class="mw-redirect" title="Stained-glass windows">stained-glass windows</a> which are said to have been brought from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. These are, however, adjudged to be of English workmanship.\n</p><p>Other notable buildings include <a href="/wiki/Calcot_Manor" title="Calcot Manor">Calcot Barn</a> in Calcot, a relic of <a href="/wiki/Kingswood_Abbey" title="Kingswood Abbey">Kingswood Abbey</a>. <a href="/wiki/Thornbury_Castle" title="Thornbury Castle">Thornbury Castle</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor</a> country house, the pretensions of which evoked the jealousy of <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">Cardinal</a> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wolsey" title="Thomas Wolsey">Wolsey</a> against its builder, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Stafford,_3rd_Duke_of_Buckingham" title="Edward Stafford, 3rd Duke of Buckingham">Edward Stafford, duke of Buckingham</a>, who was <a href="/wiki/Decapitation" title="Decapitation">beheaded</a> in 1521. Near Cheltenham is the 15th-century mansion of <a href="/wiki/Southam,_Gloucestershire" title="Southam, Gloucestershire">Southam de la Bere</a>, of timber and stone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Memorials of the de la Bere family appear in the church at Cleeve. The mansion contains a tiled floor from <a href="/wiki/Hailes_Abbey" title="Hailes Abbey">Hailes Abbey</a>. At Great Badminton is the mansion and vast domain of the Beauforts (formerly of the Botelers and others), on the south-eastern boundary of the county. <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Castle" title="Berkeley Castle">Berkeley Castle</a> at over 800 years old and the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Great_Witcombe_Roman_Villa" title="Great Witcombe Roman Villa">Witcombe Roman Villa</a> at Great Witcombe are also notable heritage features.\n</p><p>There are several <a href="/wiki/Royal_residence" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal residence">royal residences</a> in Gloucestershire, including <a href="/wiki/Highgrove_House" title="Highgrove House">Highgrove House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gatcombe_Park" title="Gatcombe Park">Gatcombe Park</a>, and (formerly) <a href="/wiki/Nether_Lypiatt_Manor" title="Nether Lypiatt Manor">Nether Lypiatt Manor</a>.\n</p><p>An annual "<a href="/wiki/Cooper%27s_Hill_Cheese-Rolling_and_Wake" title="Cooper&#39;s Hill Cheese-Rolling and Wake">cheese-rolling</a>" event takes place at Cooper\'s Hill, near <a href="/wiki/Brockworth,_Gloucestershire" title="Brockworth, Gloucestershire">Brockworth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_Games" class="mw-redirect" title="Cotswold Games">Cotswold Games</a> occurred within the county.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_of_interest">Places of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> They can be recognised by the white stripe that runs down the centre of their backs to the tip of their tails.
