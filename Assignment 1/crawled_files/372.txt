template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Local Government Act 1972 (c. 70)</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> that reformed <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local government</a> in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> on 1 April 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-lga1972_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lga1972-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Its pattern of two-tier <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England" title="Metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties of England">metropolitan and non-metropolitan county</a> and <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">district</a> councils remains in use today in large parts of England, although the metropolitan county councils were abolished in 1986, and both county and district councils were replaced with <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">unitary authorities</a> in many areas in the 1990s.\n</p><p>In Wales, too, the Act established a similar pattern of counties and <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Wales" title="Districts of Wales">districts</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-arnold_baker_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arnold_baker-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but these have since been entirely replaced with a <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Subdivisions of Wales">system of unitary authorities</a>.\n</p><p>It was one of the most significant Acts of Parliament to be passed by the <a href="/wiki/Heath_ministry" title="Heath ministry">Heath Government of 1970&#8211;74</a> and is surpassed only by the <a href="/wiki/European_Communities_Act_1972_(UK)" title="European Communities Act 1972 (UK)">European  Communities Act 1972</a> which took the United Kingdom into the <a href="/wiki/European_Communities" title="European Communities">European Communities</a>. \n</p><p><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_local_elections,_1973" title="United Kingdom local elections, 1973">Elections</a> were held to the new authorities in 1973, and they acted as "shadow authorities" until the handover date. Elections to county councils were held on 12 April, for metropolitan and Welsh districts on 10 May, and for non-metropolitan district councils on 7 June.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Elected <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> had been established in England and Wales for the first time in 1888, covering areas known as administrative counties. Some large towns, known as <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a>, were politically independent from the counties in which they were physically situated. The county areas were two-tier, with many <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban district</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rural_district" title="Rural district">rural districts</a> within them, each with its own council.<sup id="cite_ref-bryne_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bryne-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Apart from the creation of new county boroughs, the most significant change since 1899 (and the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a>) had been the establishment in 1965 of <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> and its thirty-two <a href="/wiki/London_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="London borough">London boroughs</a>, covering a much larger area than the previous <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">county of London</a>.  A <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1958_-_1967)" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Government Commission for England (1958 - 1967)">Local Government Commission for England</a> was set up in 1958 to review local government arrangements throughout the country, and made some changes, such as merging two pairs of small administrative counties to form <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_and_Isle_of_Ely" title="Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely">Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely</a>, and creating several contiguous county boroughs in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Country" title="Black Country">Black Country</a>.  However, most of the Commission\'s recommendations, such as its proposals to abolish <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a> or to reorganise <a href="/wiki/Tyneside" title="Tyneside">Tyneside</a>, were ignored in favour of the status quo.\n</p><p>It was generally agreed that there were significant problems with the structure of local government.<sup id="cite_ref-bryne_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bryne-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Despite mergers, there was still a proliferation of small district councils in rural areas, and in the major conurbations the borders had been set before the pattern of urban development had become clear. For example, in the area that was to become the seven boroughs of the metropolitan county of <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a>, local government was split between three administrative counties (<a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>), and eight county boroughs (<a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dudley" title="Dudley">Dudley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solihull" title="Solihull">Solihull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walsall" title="Walsall">Walsall</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Borough_of_Warley" title="County Borough of Warley">Warley</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Bromwich" title="West Bromwich">West Bromwich</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a>). Many county boundaries reflected traditions of the Middle Ages or even earlier; industrialisation had created new and very large urban areas like the West Midlands, Liverpool and Manchester which spanned traditional county boundaries and were now often bigger than and far from their traditional county towns.\n</p><p>The Local Government Commission was wound up in 1966, and replaced with a Royal Commission (known as the <a href="/wiki/Redcliffe-Maud_Report" title="Redcliffe-Maud Report">Redcliffe-Maud commission</a>). In 1969 it recommended a system of single-tier <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Unitary authorities">unitary authorities</a> for the whole of England, apart from three metropolitan areas of <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">SELNEC</a> (South East Lancashire and North East Cheshire, now known as Greater Manchester) and <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Country" title="Black Country">Black Country</a>), which were to have both a metropolitan council and district councils.\n</p><p>This report was accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> government of the time despite considerable opposition,<sup id="cite_ref-bryne_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bryne-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> won the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1970" title="United Kingdom general election, 1970">June 1970 general election</a> on a manifesto that committed it to a two-tier structure.  The new government made <a href="/wiki/Peter_Walker,_Baron_Walker_of_Worcester" title="Peter Walker, Baron Walker of Worcester">Peter Walker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Page" title="Graham Page">Graham Page</a> the ministers, and quickly dropped the Redcliffe-Maud report.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  They invited comments from interested parties regarding the previous government\'s proposals.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  The Association of Municipal Corporations put forward a scheme with 13 provincial councils and 132 main councils, about twice the number proposed by Redcliffe-Maud.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="White_Paper_and_Bill">White Paper and Bill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: White Paper and Bill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The incoming government\'s proposals for England were presented in a White Paper published in February 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-white_paper_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white_paper-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The White Paper substantially trimmed the metropolitan areas, and proposed a two-tier structure for the rest of the country. Many of the new boundaries proposed by the Redcliffe-Maud report were retained in the White Paper. The proposals were in large part based on ideas of the County Councils Association, the Urban District Councils Association and the Rural District Councils Association.<sup id="cite_ref-process_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-process-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The White Paper outlined principles, including an acceptance of the minimum population of 250,000 for education authorities in the Redcliffe-Maud report, and its findings that the division of functions between town and country had been harmful, but that some functions were better performed by smaller units. The White Paper set out the proposed division of functions between districts and counties, and also suggested a minimum population of 40,000 for districts. The government aimed to introduce a Bill in the 1971/72 session of Parliament for elections in 1973, so that the new authorities could start exercising full powers on 1 April 1974. The White Paper made no commitments on regional or provincial government, since the Conservative government preferred to wait for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Crowther Commission</a> to report.<sup id="cite_ref-white_paper_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white_paper-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The proposals were substantially changed with the introduction of the Bill into Parliament in November 1971:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Area 4 (Cleveland) would have had a border with area 2 (Tyne and Wear), cutting area 3 (Durham) off from the coast. Suffolk to unify the <a href="/wiki/River_Stour,_Suffolk" title="River Stour, Suffolk">Stour Estuary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  The keeping it in County Durham<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>removal of <a href="/wiki/Skelmersdale_and_Holland" title="Skelmersdale and district of Southport before Southport was included within Merseyside</li>\n<li>exclusion of <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a> and area from <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" href="#cite_note-tamarside_rejected-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-up_all_night_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-up_all_night-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>exclusion of <a href="/wiki/Newmarket,_Suffolk" title="Newmarket, Suffolk">Newmarket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haverhill,_Suffolk" title="Haverhill, Suffolk">Haverhill</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>, kept in Suffolk (despite protests of Newmarket UDC, which was happy to see the town transferred to Cambridgeshire)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a from Suffolk to <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a></li></ul>\n<p>In the Bill as published, the Dorset/Hampshire border was between Christchurch and Lymington.  On 6 July 1972, a government amendment added Lymington to Dorset, which would have had the effect of having the entire <a href="/wiki/South_East_Dorset_conurbation" title="South East Dorset conurbation">Bournemouth conurbation</a> in one county (although the town in Lymington itself does not form part of the built-up area, the borough was large and contained villages which do).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  The House of Lords reversed this amendment in September, with the government losing the division 81 to 65.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In October, the government brought up this issue again, proposing an amendment to put the western part of Lymington borough in Dorset. The amendment was withdrawn.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The government lost divisions in the House of Lords at Report Stage on the exclusion of <a href="/wiki/Wilmslow" title="Wilmslow">Wilmslow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poynton" title="Poynton">Poynton</a> from Greater Manchester and their retention in Cheshire, and also on whether Rothwell should form part of the Leeds or Wakefield districts.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> (Rothwell had been planned for Wakefield, but an amendment at report stage was proposed by local <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> <a href="/wiki/Albert_Roberts_(British_politician)" title="Albert Roberts (British politician)">Albert Roberts</a><sup id="cite_ref-up_all_night_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-up_all_night-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and accepted by the government.  This was overturned by the Lords.) Instead, the Wakefield district gained the town of <a href="/wiki/Ossett" title="Ossett">Ossett</a>, which was originally placed in the <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees</a> district, following an appeal by Ossett Labour Party.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The government barely won a division in the Lords on the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Weston-super-Mare" title="Weston-super-Mare">Weston-super-Mare</a> in Avon, by 42 to 41.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two more metropolitan districts were created than were originally in the Bill:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Rochdale" title="Metropolitan Borough of Rochdale">Rochdale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Bury" title="Metropolitan Borough of Bury">Bury</a> were originally planned to form a single district (dubbed "Botchdale" by local MP <a href="/wiki/Michael_Fidler" title="Michael Fidler">Michael Fidler</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  Rochdale took Middleton from Oldham in compensation.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Knowsley" title="Metropolitan Borough of Knowsley">Knowsley</a> was not originally planned, and was formed from the western part of the planned <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_St_Helens" title="Metropolitan Borough of St Helens">St Helens</a> district<sup id="cite_ref-up_all_night_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-up_all_night-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>As passed, the Act would have included <a href="/wiki/Charlwood" title="Charlwood">Charlwood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horley" title="Horley">Horley</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">Gatwick Airport</a>. This was reversed by the <a href="/wiki/Charlwood_and_Horley_Act_1974" title="Charlwood and Horley Act 1974">Charlwood and Horley Act 1974</a>, passed just before the Act came into force. Charlwood was made part of the <a href="/wiki/Mole_Valley" title="Mole Valley">Mole Valley</a> district and Horley part of <a href="/wiki/Reigate_and_Banstead" title="Reigate and Banstead">Reigate and Banstead</a>. Gatwick Airport was still transferred.\n</p><p>Although willing to compromise about exact boundaries, the government stood firm on the existence or abolition of county councils. The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> (originally scheduled to be merged back into <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> as a district) was the only local campaign to succeed, and also the only county council in England to violate the 250,000 limit for education authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-white_paper_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white_paper-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-reformed_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformed-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The government bowed to local demand for the island to retain its status in October 1972, moving an amendment in the Lords to remove it from Hampshire. Lord Sanford noting that "nowhere else is faced with problems of communication with its neighbours which are in any way comparable."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Protests from <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> failed, although Rutland was able to secure its treatment as a single district despite not even managing to meet the stated minimum population of 40,000 for districts.\n</p><p>Several metropolitan boroughs fell under the 250,000 limit, including three of <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a>\'s five boroughs (<a href="/wiki/North_Tyneside" title="North Tyneside">North Tyneside</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Tyneside" title="South Tyneside">South Tyneside</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Gateshead" title="Metropolitan Borough of Gateshead">Gateshead</a>), and the four metropolitan boroughs that had resulted from the splitting of the proposed <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Bury" title="Metropolitan Borough of Bury">Bury</a>/<a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Rochdale" title="Metropolitan Borough of Rochdale">Rochdale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Knowsley" title="Metropolitan Borough of Knowsley">Knowsley</a>/<a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_St_Helens" title="Metropolitan Borough of St Helens">St Helens</a> boroughs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Wales, the background was substantially different. The Redcliffe-Maud Commission had not considered Wales, which had been the subject of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> proposals in the 1960s. A White Paper was published in 1967 on the subject of Wales, based on the findings of the 1962 report of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_Wales" title="Local Government Commission for Wales">Local Government Commission for Wales</a>. The White Paper proposed five counties, and thirty-six districts. The county boroughs of <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Wales" title="Newport, Wales">Newport</a> would be retained, but the small county borough of <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a> would become a district. The proposed counties were as follows<sup id="cite_ref-process_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-process-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dyfed" title="Dyfed">Dyfed</a> \xe2\x80\x93 West Wales \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Cardiganshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardiganshire">Cardiganshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carmarthenshire" title="Carmarthenshire">Carmarthenshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Glamorgan" title="Glamorgan">Glamorgan</a> \xe2\x80\x93 South Wales</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gwent_(county)" title="Gwent (county)">Gwent</a> \xe2\x80\x93 South-East Wales \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire_(historic)" title="Monmouthshire (historic)">Monmouthshire</a> (also including <a href="/wiki/Rhymney_Valley" title="Rhymney Valley">Rhymney valley</a> from Glamorgan)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a> \xe2\x80\x93 North Wales \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caernarvonshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Caernarvonshire">Caernarvonshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denbighshire_(historic)" title="Denbighshire (historic)">Denbighshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flintshire_(historic)" title="Flintshire (historic)">Flintshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merionethshire" title="Merionethshire">Merionethshire</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Powys" title="Powys">Powys</a> \xe2\x80\x93 Mid Wales \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Montgomeryshire" title="Montgomeryshire">Montgomeryshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radnorshire" title="Radnorshire">Radnorshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breconshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Breconshire">Breconshire</a></li></ul>\n<p>Implementation of reform in Wales was not immediate, pending decisions on the situation in England, and a new Secretary of State, <a href="/wiki/George_Thomas,_1st_Viscount_Tonypandy" title="George Thomas, 1st Viscount Tonypandy">George Thomas</a>, announced changes to the proposals in November 1968. The large northern county of Gwynedd was to be split to form two counties (creating Gwynedd in the west and <a href="/wiki/Clwyd" title="Clwyd">Clwyd</a> in the east) with various alterations to the districts. The Redcliffe-Maud report led to a reconsideration of the plans, especially with respect to Glamorgan and Monmouthshire, and a March 1970 White Paper proposed three unitary authorities for South Wales, based on Cardiff, Swansea and Newport.<sup id="cite_ref-process_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-process-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1970" title="United Kingdom general election, 1970">1970 general election</a>, the new Conservative government published a Consultative Document in February 1971, at the same time as the English White Paper.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  The proposals were  similar to the Labour proposals of 1968, except that the county boroughs were instead two-tier districts, and that Glamorgan was to be subdivided into West Glamorgan and East Glamorgan, making 7 counties and 36 districts.<sup id="cite_ref-process_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-process-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the Bill as introduced Glamorgan had been split into three authorities: with East Glamorgan further subdivided into a Mid Glamorgan covering the valleys and South Glamorgan. The decision to split East Glamorgan further left South Glamorgan with only two districts (one of which was the Conservative-controlled <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, who had requested the split) and Mid Glamorgan one of the poorest areas in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-process_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-process-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  The Labour-controlled Glamorgan County Council strongly opposed this move, placing adverts in newspapers calling for Glamorgan to be saved from a "carve up", and demanding that the east/west split be retained.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>  The resulting <a href="/wiki/South_Glamorgan_County_Council" title="South Glamorgan County Council">South Glamorgan</a> was the only Welsh county council the Conservatives ever controlled (from 1977 to 1981).\n</p><p>One of the effects of the Act was to confirm the area of <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire_(historic)" title="Monmouthshire (historic)">Monmouthshire</a> as part of Wales. <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire_(historic)#Ambiguity_over_Welsh_status" title="Monmouthshire (historic)">Ambiguity</a> as to the status of Monmouthshire had been introduced by legislation in the 16th and 17th centuries, and by the gradual cultural <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicisation</a> of some eastern parts of the county. By the late 19th century the area was treated in legislation as one with Wales, using the terminology "Wales and Monmouthshire".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Apart from the new Glamorgan authorities, all the names of the new Welsh counties were in the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh language</a>, with no English equivalent. With the exception of <a href="/wiki/Clwyd" title="Clwyd">Clwyd</a> (which was named after the <a href="/wiki/River_Clwyd" title="River Clwyd">River Clwyd</a>) the names of the counties were taken from ancient British kingdoms.  Welsh names were also used for many of the Welsh districts.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> There were no metropolitan counties and, unlike in England, the Secretary of State could not create future metropolitan counties there under the Act.<sup id="cite_ref-arnold_baker_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arnold_baker-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Act">The Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After much comment, the proposals were introduced as the Local Government Bill into Parliament soon after the start of the 1971\xe2\x80\x931972 session.\n</p><p>In the Commons it passed through Standing Committee D, who debated the Bill in fifty-one sittings from 25 November 1971, to 20 March 1972.\n</p><p>The Act abolished previous existing local government structures, and created a two-tier system of counties and districts everywhere. Some of the new counties were designated <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan counties</a>, containing <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a> instead. The allocation of functions differed between the metropolitan and the non-metropolitan areas (the so-called \'<a href="/wiki/Shire_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Shire county">shire counties</a>\') \xe2\x80\x94 for example, <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_services" title="Social services">social services</a> were the responsibility of the shire counties, but in metropolitan areas was given to the districts. The distribution of powers was slightly different in Wales than in England, with libraries being a county responsibility in England\xe2\x80\x94but in Wales districts could opt to become library authorities themselves. One key principle was that education authorities (non-metropolitan counties and metropolitan districts), were deemed to need a population base of 250,000 in order to be viable.\n</p><p>Although called two-tier, the system was really three-tier, as it retained <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> councils, although in Wales they were renamed <a href="/wiki/Community_council" title="Community council">community councils</a>.\n</p><p>The Act introduced \'agency\', where one local authority (usually a district) could act as an <a href="/wiki/Agent_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Agent (law)">agent</a> for another authority. For example, since road maintenance was split depending upon the type of road, both types of council had to retain engineering departments. A county council could delegate its road maintenance to the district council if it was confident that the district was competent. Some powers were specifically excluded from agency, such as education.\n</p><p>The Act abolished various historic relics such as <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">aldermen</a>.  The office previously known as sheriff was retitled high sheriff.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Many existing boroughs that were too small to constitute a district, but too large to constitute a <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a>, were given <a href="/wiki/Charter_trustees" title="Charter trustees">charter trustees</a>.\n</p><p>Most provisions of the Act came into force at midnight on 1 April 1974. Elections to the new councils had already been held, in 1973, and the new authorities were already up and running as \'shadow authorities\', following the example set by the <a href="/wiki/London_Government_Act_1963" title="London Government Act 1963">London Government Act 1963</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_new_local_government_areas">The new local government areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The new local government areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Act specified the composition and names of the English and Welsh counties, and the composition of the metropolitan and Welsh districts.  It did not specify any names of districts, nor indeed the borders of the non-metropolitan districts in England \xe2\x80\x93 these were specified by <a href="/wiki/Statutory_Instrument" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutory Instrument">Statutory Instrument</a> after the passing of the Act.  A Boundary Commission, provided for in the Act, had already begun work on dividing England into districts whilst the Bill was still going through Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> there were 45 counties and 296 districts, in Wales there were 8 and 37. Six of the English counties were designated as metropolitan counties. The new English counties were based clearly on the traditional ones, albeit with several substantial changes.<sup id="cite_ref-Her_Majesty&#39;s_Stationery_Office_1996_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Her_Majesty&#39;s_Stationery_Office_1996-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The 13 <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_Wales" title="Historic counties of Wales">historic counties of Wales</a>, however, were abandoned entirely for administrative purposes, and 8 new ones instituted.\n</p><p>The Act substituted the new counties "for counties of any other description" for purposes of law.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  This realigned the boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial</a> and judicial counties used for <a href="/wiki/Lord-Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord-Lieutenant">lieutenancy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Custos_rotulorum" title="Custos rotulorum">custodes rotulorum</a>, <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="High Sheriff">shrievalty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commission_of_the_peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Commission of the peace">commissions of the peace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates\'</a> courts to the metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties.<sup id="cite_ref-Her_Majesty&#39;s_Stationery_Office_1996_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Her_Majesty&#39;s_Stationery_Office_1996-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  The Act also extended the rights of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a> to appoint Lord-Lieutenants for the shrunken <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> along with all of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In England before the passing of the Act there had been 1086 urban and rural districts and 79 county boroughs. The number of districts was reduced about fourfold.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England_2">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_local_elections,_1973" title="United Kingdom local elections, 1973">United Kingdom local elections, 1973</a></div>\n<p>Elections to the new authorities were held on three different Thursdays in 1973.  Each new county and district was divided into electoral divisions, known as <a href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (politics)">wards</a> in the districts.  For county councils, each electoral division elected one member; for metropolitan district councils, each ward elected three members; and wards in non-metropolitan districts could elect a varying number of members.  There was not sufficient time to conduct a full warding arrangement so a temporary system was used: in some county councils electoral divisions elected multiple councillors.<sup id="cite_ref-process_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-process-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>County councils were set on a four-year cycle of elections of all members, and the next elections were in 1977.  Metropolitan district councils elected one councillor for each seat in the three other years, starting in 1975.  Non-metropolitan districts had a general election again in 1976, and could subsequently either conduct elections of the whole council or by-thirds.<sup id="cite_ref-process_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-process-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-reformed_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformed-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>  Schedule 3 provided that for each metropolitan ward, the councillor for who obtained the least votes in the 1973 election would retire in 1975, the next least in 1976, and the others in 1978, setting up the cycle.  If equal numbers of votes were obtained, or ward elections in 1973 had been uncontested, the decision would be made by lot.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_of_functions">Division of both authorities had some powers, and certain Welsh districts were allowed greater powers by the Secretary of State.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The system established by the Act was the object of some criticism. One major controversy was the failure to reform local government finance. Having lost office at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_February_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, February 1974">general election of February 1974</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Page" title="Graham Page">Graham Page</a>, the minister who had piloted the Act through Parliament, condemned the existing system of rates and grants. His successor as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Environment" title="Secretary of State for the Environment">Minister for the Environment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Crosland" class="mw-redirect" title="Tony Crosland">Tony Crosland</a> said that he would be re-examining the rates system, while the Association of Metropolitan Authorities sought the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Royal_commission" title="Royal commission">royal commission</a> to consider the matter.<sup id="cite_ref-change_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-beg_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beg-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The two-tier structure established was also seen as problematic. In particular, the division of planning between districts and counties was a source of friction between the new councils.<sup id="cite_ref-change_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Thamesdown Borough Council called for a further reform and complete abolition of counties as they felt <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire_County_Council" title="Wiltshire County Council">Wiltshire County Council</a> was unable to respond to the needs of an expanding urban area.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Further complaints surrounded the loss of water supply and sewerage powers to regional water authorities created by the <a href="/wiki/Water_Act_1973" title="Water Act 1973">Water Act 1973</a>. This was felt to reduce the ability of district councils to plan new housing developments.<sup id="cite_ref-beg_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beg-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> It was also felt that the boundaries of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan counties</a> were too tightly drawn, leaving out much of the suburban areas of the conurbations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. The leading article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> on the day the Act came into effect noted that the <i>new arrangement is a compromise which seeks to reconcile familiar geography which commands a certain amount of affection and loyalty, with the scale of operations on which modern planning methods can work effectively</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-change_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was some criticism of county boundary changes. A campaign was mounted to return the <a href="/wiki/Uffington_White_Horse" title="Uffington White Horse">Uffington White Horse</a> to Berkshire, and a bonfire was lit at the site by protestors as the Act came into effect.<sup id="cite_ref-remote_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remote-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The campaigners claimed 10,000 signatures in favour of diverting the county boundary to include the "Berkshire White Horse".<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The calls were rejected by the local MP, <a href="/wiki/Airey_Neave" title="Airey Neave">Airey Neave</a>, who pointed out that the horse predated county boundaries, and by the chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_White_Horse" title="Vale of White Horse">Vale of White Horse</a> District Council.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Professor Anthony Fletcher, of the Department of Medieval History of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sheffield" title="University of Sheffield">University of Sheffield</a>, suggested that the new councils place signs at the boundaries of ancient counties.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The removal of Gatwick Airport and the surrounding area from Surrey into West Sussex met some fierce local opposition with the result that the parishes of <a href="/wiki/Horley" title="Horley">Horley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charlwood" title="Charlwood">Charlwood</a> were subsequently returned to Surrey in the <a href="/wiki/Eponymous" class="mw-redirect" title="Eponymous">eponymous</a> <a href="/wiki/Charlwood_and_Horley_Act_1974" title="Charlwood and Horley Act 1974">Charlwood and Horley Act 1974</a>, leaving the airport to stay in West Sussex.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some of the reaction against the Act was motivated by opposition to loss of local control.  The <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> councils regretted the loss of their independent status. Especially stung was the <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">City and County of Bristol</a>, which had had its own <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lieutenant">Lord Lieutenant</a> for centuries.  Criticism of the Act also centred on the size of the new districts. The new Minister, whose party had opposed the reforms in opposition, hoped that <i>"it will be more efficient \xe2\x80\x93 but it could easily become more remote"</i>. In order to combat this, Crosland was considering the creation of "neighbourhood councils" in unparished areas of the new districts.<sup id="cite_ref-remote_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remote-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The names of some of the new authorities also caused controversy.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amendment_and_adaptation">Amendment and adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1972&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Amendment and adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The system established by the Act was not to last. In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> a series of incremental measures amended it. First, the <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> of the metropolitan counties were abolished in 1986 by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>\'s government, effectively re-establishing county borough status for the metropolitan boroughs. Second, a review of local government outside the metropolitan counties was announced in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1992)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1992)">local government reform in the 1990s</a> led to the creation of many new <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authorities</a>, and the complete abolition of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Avon" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Avon">Avon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_England" title="Cleveland, England">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hereford_and_Worcester" title="Hereford and Worcester">Hereford and Worcester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humberside" title="Humberside">Humberside</a>. Names such as <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding of Yorkshire</a> reappeared as local government entities, although often with new boundaries. Several former county boroughs such as <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> regained unitary status. Additionally, another wave of <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">unitary authorities were formed in 2009</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> there was a more radical change in policy with the two-tier system entirely abolished in 1996, and replaced with the current <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Subdivisions of Wales">principal areas of Wales</a>. The 1974 counties have been retained as <a href="/wiki/Preserved_counties_of_Wales" title="Preserved counties of Wales">preserved counties</a> for various purposes, notably as ceremonial counties, albeit with substantive border revisions.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
