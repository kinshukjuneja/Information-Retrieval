For Nippon Professional Baseball interleague play, see <a href="/wiki/Interleague_play_(NPB)" title="Interleague play (NPB)">Interleague play (NPB)</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Interleague_Logo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/21/Interleague_Logo.png" width="250" height="220" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="220" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">Interleague play logo</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Interleague play</b> in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> refers to regular-season baseball games played between an <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> (AL) team and a <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (NL) team. Interleague play was first introduced in the <a href="/wiki/1997_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1997 Major League Baseball season">1997 Major League Baseball season</a>. Prior to that, matchups between AL teams and NL teams occurred only during <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">spring training</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Exhibition_game" title="Exhibition game">exhibition games</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Doubleday_Field#Hall_of_Fame_game" title="Doubleday Field">Hall of Fame Game</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cooperstown,_New_York" title="Cooperstown, New York">Cooperstown, New York</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>. Unlike modern interleague play, none of these contests, except for the World Series, counted toward official team or league records.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_discussions">Early discussions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early discussions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg/180px-Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg" width="180" height="247" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg/270px-Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg/360px-Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="674" data-file-height="925" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hank_Greenberg_1937_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hank_Greenberg" title="Hank Greenberg">Hank Greenberg</a>, Hall of Famer and 2-time MVP</div></div></div><p>Regular season interleague play was discussed for baseball\'s major leagues as early as 1903, when the two major leagues made peace and formed the National Commission as governing body. The first National Commission Chairman, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati</a> president <a href="/wiki/August_Herrmann" title="August Herrmann">August Herrmann</a> (who had already been a proponent of interleague play), proposed an ambitious scheme in late-1904. Herrmann\'s plan would have seen the two leagues ending their seasons earlier, after approximately 116 games, "and then have every National League team play two games in every American League city, and have every American League team play two games in every National League city."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-news.google.com_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.google.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Another interleague play idea was floated around the same time by <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Americans</a> owner <a href="/wiki/John_I._Taylor" title="John I. Taylor">John Taylor</a>, whose plan was for each league to play its full 154-game schedule, to be followed by not just a championship series between the two league winners, but also by series\' between the two second-place finishers, the two third-place teams, and all other corresponding finishers.<sup id="cite_ref-news.google.com_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.google.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In August 1933, several owners reacted favorably to a proposal by <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> president <a href="/wiki/William_Veeck_Sr." title="William Veeck Sr.">William Veeck</a> to have teams play four interleague games in the middle of the season, beginning in 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In December 1956, Major League owners considered a proposal by <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland</a> general manager and minority-owner <a href="/wiki/Hank_Greenberg" title="Hank Greenberg">Hank Greenberg</a> to implement limited interleague play beginning in 1958. Under Greenberg\'s proposal, each team would continue to play a 154-game season, with 126 within that team\'s league, and 28 against the eight clubs in the other league. The interleague games would be played immediately following the All-Star Game. Notably, under Greenberg\'s proposal, all results would count in regular season game standings and league statistics.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> While this proposal was not adopted, the current system shares many elements. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Veeck" title="Bill Veeck">Bill Veeck</a> predicted in 1963 that Major League Baseball would someday have interleague play.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> While the concept was again considered in the 1970s,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> it was not implemented until the 1990s, at least in part as an effort to renew the public\'s interest in MLB following the <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1994\xe2\x80\x9395 Major League Baseball strike">1994 players\' strike</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interleague_play_introduced">Interleague play introduced</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interleague play introduced">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>MLB\'s first regular-season interleague game took place on June 12, 1997, as the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> hosted the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_Ballpark_in_Arlington" class="mw-redirect" title="The Ballpark in Arlington">The Ballpark in Arlington</a>. There were four interleague games on the schedule that night, but the other three were played on the West Coast, so the Giants\xe2\x80\x93Rangers matchup started a few hours earlier than the others. Texas\'s <a href="/wiki/Darren_Oliver" title="Darren Oliver">Darren Oliver</a> threw the game\'s first pitch and San Francisco outfielder <a href="/wiki/Glenallen_Hill" title="Glenallen Hill">Glenallen Hill</a> was the first <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> used in a regular-season game by a <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> team. San Francisco\'s <a href="/wiki/Darryl_Hamilton" title="Darryl Hamilton">Darryl Hamilton</a> got the first base hit in interleague play, while <a href="/wiki/Stan_Javier" title="Stan Javier">Stan Javier</a> hit the first home run, leading the Giants to a 4\xe2\x80\x933 victory over the Rangers.\n</p><p>From 1997 to 2001, teams played against the same division from the other league; for example, the American League West played teams from the National League West, typically scheduled to alternate between home and away in consecutive years. In 2002, however, the league began alternating which divisions played which divisions, and thus in 2002 the American League East played the National League West, the American League Central played the National League East, and the American League West played the National League Central. Matchups which had been of particular interest prior to this format \xe2\x80\x94 mainly geographic rivals \xe2\x80\x94 were preserved. This is expected to be the continuing format of the interleague schedule. Corresponding divisions were skipped once when this rotation began, but were put back in the rotation in 2006.\n</p><p>From 2002 to 2012, all interleague games were played prior to the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a> (with the exception of games postponed by weather that were made up after the All-Star Game). Most games were played in June and early July, although beginning in 2005, interleague games were played during one weekend in mid-May.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> (DH) rule is applied in the same manner as in the World Series (and the All-Star Game prior to 2010). In an American League ballpark, both teams have the option to use a DH. In a National League ballpark, both teams\' pitchers must bat. Teams from both leagues have both benefited and have been at a disadvantage when it comes to the DH rule in interleague play. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a>, who spent his entire career in the National League and actually won eight <a href="/wiki/Rawlings_Gold_Glove_Award" title="Rawlings Gold Glove Award">Gold Gloves</a> earlier in his career, was used strictly as a DH later in his career when the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> played away interleague games due to his poor fielding.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> slugger <a href="/wiki/David_Ortiz" title="David Ortiz">David Ortiz</a>, who spent his entire career in the American League and was the Red Sox\'s regular DH, was forced to play <a href="/wiki/First_baseman" title="First baseman">first base</a> when the Red Sox had away interleague games, forcing the Sox to give up good defensive fielding in favor of retaining Ortiz\'s power hitting.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of the end of the <a href="/wiki/2017_MLB_season" class="mw-redirect" title="2017 MLB season">2017 MLB season</a>, the American League holds an all-time series advantage of 2,890\xe2\x80\x932,574 and has finished with the better record in interleague play for 14 straight seasons, dating back to <a href="/wiki/2004_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2004 Major League Baseball season">2004</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  2006 was the most lopsided season in interleague history, with American League teams posting a 154\xe2\x80\x9398 record against their National League counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-history_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  The team with the best all-time record in interleague play is the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> of the AL at 144\xe2\x80\x93102 (.585), followed by the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> at 143\xe2\x80\x93104 (.579). The <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins" title="Miami Marlins">Miami Marlins</a> holds the NL\'s best interleague record at 127\xe2\x80\x93107 (.543), followed by the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> at 109\xe2\x80\x9396 (.532).<sup id="cite_ref-history_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2007, two teams \xe2\x80\x94 the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> \xe2\x80\x94 played six games with more than one interleague opponent. The Dodgers played both the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim</a> while the Orioles played both the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a>.  This happened again in 2012 as the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> played both the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> for six games. The <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins" title="Miami Marlins">Miami Marlins</a> also did this, playing both the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Rays" title="Tampa Bay Rays">Tampa Bay Rays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> for six games each.\n</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Game" title="Civil Rights Game">Civil Rights Game</a> was an exhibition interleague game between the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> at <a href="/wiki/AutoZone_Park" title="AutoZone Park">AutoZone Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis</a> on March 31, 2007.  The first regular season <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Game" title="Civil Rights Game">Civil Rights Game</a> was an interleague game between the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> at <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Ball_Park" title="Great American Ball Park">Great American Ball Park</a> on June 20, 2009.\n</p><p>Since the introduction of interleague play, two teams have shifted leagues: the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a> from the American League to the National League in 1998, and the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> from the National League to the American League in 2013. As a result, a 2013 interleague series between the two teams made it the first time that two teams faced each other in an interleague series after both teams previously faced each other in an interleague series representing opposite leagues: the two teams met from September 1\xe2\x80\x933, 1997 (Houston in NL, Milwaukee in AL), then again from June 18\xe2\x80\x9320, 2013 (Houston in AL, Milwaukee in NL). In both instances, the series took place in Houston, with the team representing the American League winning 2-1.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> From 1998 to 2012, both teams were division opponents in the <a href="/wiki/National_League_Central" title="National League Central">National League Central</a>.\n</p><p>Interleague play has largely favored the American League in terms of win-loss records. In 22 years of play, the AL has won the season series 17 times, including 14 straight from 2004-2017. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interleague statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The following is the text of Major League Baseball\'s policy regarding the compilation of statistics as a result of Interleague Play:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"For the first time in the history of Major League Baseball, Interleague games are to be played during the regular season. Breaking tradition always brings about controversy and the matter of baseball records is no exception."</p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>"It is the opinion of Major League Baseball that there is no justification for compiling a new volume of records based on Interleague Play. On the contrary, the sovereignty of each league\'s records will be retained, and if a player or a team breaks a record against an Interleague opponent it will be considered a record in that league. In cases where two teams \xe2\x80\x93 as Interleague opponents \xe2\x80\x93 break a league or Major League record, that record will be annotated with the phrase \'Interleague game.\' Streaks by both teams and individual will continue (or be halted) when playing Interleague opponents in the same manner as if playing against an intraleague opponent. In essence, records will be defined by who made them rather than against whom they were made."</p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>"The official statistics of both leagues will be kept separately as they have in the past. This means statistics for each team and their individual players will reflect their performance in games within the league and also in Interleague games without differentiation."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_matchups_and_natural_rivals">Geographical matchups and natural rivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geographical matchups and natural rivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_rivalries" title="Major League Baseball rivalries">Major League Baseball rivalries</a></div>\n<p>Certain interleague matchups are highly anticipated each year, due to the geographic proximity of the teams involved. Many cities, metropolitan areas and states contain at least one team in each league. In each of these "natural rivalry" matchups, the two teams meet annually for four games, two in each ballpark. Prior to 2013, and in 2015, there were six games between the two teams, three per ballpark:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> \xe2\x80\x94 This interleague rivalry was introduced in 2017, replacing the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> rivalry.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a> \xe2\x80\x94 <b><a href="/wiki/Beltway_Series" title="Beltway Series">Beltway Series</a></b>, Battle of the Beltways, Parkway Series, or <a href="/wiki/MARC_Train" title="MARC Train">MARC</a> Madness<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Introduced in 2006, the Nationals\xe2\x80\xb2 second season in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, after their move from <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> \xe2\x80\x94 <b><a href="/wiki/Cubs%E2%80%93White_Sox_rivalry" title="Cubs\xe2\x80\x93White Sox rivalry">Windy City Series</a></b>, Crosstown Classic or <a href="/wiki/CTA_Red_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="CTA Red Line">Red Line</a> Series. The two teams played in the <a href="/wiki/1906_World_Series" title="1906 World Series">1906 World Series</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> \xe2\x80\x94 <b><a href="/wiki/Ohio_Cup" title="Ohio Cup">Ohio Cup</a></b></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> \xe2\x80\x94  While this series only became a rivalry because the other <a href="/wiki/American_League_Central" title="American League Central">AL</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_League_Central" title="National League Central">NL</a> Central teams were already paired up, it has become popular with fans of both teams, possibly due to the rivalry between the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">NHL</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Red_Wings" title="Detroit Red Wings">Detroit Red Wings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a>.  The two teams have several other connections as well. The Tigers\' AA Minor League affiliate, the <a href="/wiki/Erie_SeaWolves" title="Erie SeaWolves">Erie SeaWolves</a>, located near Pittsburgh, is a former affiliate of the Buccos, and has retained the logo of a wolf wearing a pirate bandanna and eye patch.  Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Leyland" title="Jim Leyland">Jim Leyland</a>, former manager of both the Pirates (1986\xe2\x80\x931996) and the Tigers (2005\xe2\x80\x932013), remains popular in Pittsburgh where he continues to reside. title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> \xe2\x80\x93 Played from 1997 to 2005. Although the Nationals moved to Washington in 2005, the schedule for that season was already established, so the Washington-Baltimore rivalry series could not start until the next season. Therefore, in 2005 the Orioles played the Phillies as their rival for the last time, while Washington played Montreal\xe2\x80\xb2s planned 2005 schedule, which included what would have been the Expos\xe2\x80\xb2s interleague rivalry games with the <a href="/wiki/2005_Toronto_Blue_Jays_season" title="2005 Toronto Blue Jays season">Toronto Blue Jays</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lone_Star_Series" title="Lone Star Series">Lone Star Series</a>) \xe2\x80\x93 Played from 2001 to 2012. Since then, the Diamondbacks have aligned with the Astros and <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> have been in a "split rivalry" with the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Rockies" title="Colorado Rockies">Colorado Rockies</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pearson_Cup" title="Pearson Cup">Pearson Cup</a> or The All-Canadian Series) \xe2\x80\x93 Played from 1997 to 2004 (and by Washington against Toronto in 2005). With the exception of the two NL teams playing each other, all teams were involved in interleague play at the same time (originally in June and July), playing only interleague opponents until the interleague schedule was complete for the year. The schedule was later changed to occur only in June; in 2005, it was changed again to allow for more weekend interleague games, with each team playing one series during the third weekend in May and the rest in mid-to-late June (occasionally stretching into early July).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_Astros_joining_AL_on_scheduling">Effect of Astros joining AL on scheduling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Effect of Astros joining AL on scheduling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> joined the American League, giving each league 15 teams and thereby necessitating that interleague games be played throughout the season, including on Opening Day and during key division races all the way to the end of the season. This did not require expanding the total number of interleague games, because the probability of an interleague game during the era in which the Astros played in the NL was 252/2430 or about 1 in 9.6 games (this number is not an integer because not all teams had the same number of interleague games). With an odd number of teams in each league, one team in each league would be the "odd man out" and have to play an interleague game to fill out the schedule, meaning as few as 1 in 15 games could be interleague (14 AL teams in 7 AL games, 14 NL teams in 7 NL games and 1 AL and 1 NL team in an interleague game). Despite this, there have been proposals to increase interleague play to 30 games.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> While the increase to 30 games is highly unlikely for the foreseeable future, a smaller increase took place immediately, having every team to play 20 interleague games.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the 2013 season, the 20 interleague games are played in eight series.  Each team plays one three-game series against four teams from one division in the other league, and two two-game series (one home, one away) against the remaining team in that division.  Since 2002, this has been on a rotating basis. The remaining four games are played against a team\'s "natural rival" in two back-to-back two-game series. Should a team\'s natural rival be a member of the division they are scheduled to play as part of the yearly rotation (this first occurred for all teams in 2015), the team will play home-and-home three game series against the natural rival, home-and-home two game series against two other opponents, and single three game series against the last two (one home, one away). For 2013, the natural rivalry games were played from May 27\xe2\x80\x9330.  Teams played in one city on May 27 and 28, then traveled to the other city for games on May 29 and 30. This dynamic was repeated from June 15\xe2\x80\x9318, 2015, except that no pairs of natural rivals played each other during this time. For 2014, however, these natural rivalry games were spread out over the season, between early May and early August.  They were spread out even further in 2016, ranging from the second week of the season in April through late August. Because the requirement for nearly daily interleague play (the only exception being if not all teams are playing) spreads out interleague play throughout the year, not every team will be in interleague play on the same day. Due to the new CBA lengthening the schedule by four days, 2018 was the first year during which no team was required to play back-to-back home-and-home two game series against any other team.\n</p><p>Most days, there will be either one or three interleague games as the average number of interleague games per day will be 1.68 [(20 interleague games per team x 30 teams in MLB)/(183 total days in baseball season (including off days and excluding the All-Star break))/2 teams per game]. With 15 teams in each league, the number of interleague games is almost always odd, with exceptions based on when teams from each of the AL and NL have the same off day. Doubleheaders and make-up games also apply should a rainout or other extended delay requires a game (or games) to be postponed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Match-ups">Match-ups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Match-ups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On April 1, 2013, for the first time in major league baseball history, an interleague game was played on <a href="/wiki/Opening_Day" title="Opening Day">Opening Day</a>, between the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> at <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Ball_Park" title="Great American Ball Park">Great American Ball Park</a>, with the Angels claiming the distinction of winning that game 3-1 in 13 innings. The Reds, however, would win the next two contests by scores of 5-4.<sup id="cite_ref-Reds_use_three_homers_to_beat_Angels_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reds_use_three_homers_to_beat_Angels-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Also, on September 29, 2013, for the first time in major league baseball history, an interleague game was played on the last day of the regular season, between the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins" title="Miami Marlins">Miami Marlins</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> at <a href="/wiki/Marlins_Park" title="Marlins Park">Marlins Park</a>. The Marlins not only claimed the distinction of winning that game 1-0 in walk-off fashion, but also saw their pitcher <a href="/wiki/Henderson_%C3%81lvarez" title="Henderson \xc3\x81lvarez">Henderson \xc3\x81lvarez</a> pitch a no-hitter, marking just the 7th time a no-hitter was tossed in an interleague contest.\n</p><p>On April 3, 2016, for the first time in MLB history, the previous year\'s World Series participants faced off on Opening Day the following year. The <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a> hosted the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kauffman_Stadium" title="Kauffman Stadium">Kauffman Stadium</a> in a nationally televised game and won 4-3. The Mets would not only return the favor two days later with a 2-0 win to split the two game series, but also sweep two more from the Royals in <a href="/wiki/Citi_Field" title="Citi Field">Citi Field</a> on June 21-22 that year.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> were the last two teams to play each other, from June 10-12, 2005, at Minute Maid Park. With the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>\xe2\x80\xb2 5-0 victory over the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> on July 10, 2013, every team has beaten every other team at least once; the A\'s had previously been 11-0 all time against the Pirates.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Every team has also hosted and visited every other team at least once. class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since its introduction, regular-season interleague play has continued to be a source of controversy among baseball fans and others involved with the sport. Among the arguments used in favor of and in opposition to interleague play are the following:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pros">Pros</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pros">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>Interleague matchplay increases attendance; however, these numbers may be skewed, as interleague games are primarily played on weekends, when attendance is higher across the board, and in June, when school is not in session and temperatures are higher than some of the other months of the season.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, season-long interleague play should mitigate this statistical bias.</li>\n<li>Fans can see players they might not otherwise get to see, especially those who have only ever played in one league.</li>\n<li>Certain geographic rivalries are played out during the regular season that otherwise might not happen for years at a time. The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">Yankees</a> now play four games against the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">Mets</a> each season whereas they would only have gone head to head in the <a href="/wiki/2000_World_Series" title="2000 World Series">2000 World Series</a> if not for interleague play. From 1962 until interleague play, the Mets and the Yankees played each other in a single game, the annual Mayor\'s Trophy Game, which was an exhibition game even though it was played during the regular season.</li>\n<li>It creates matchups that might not have been seen in generations. For example:\n<ul><li>During the <a href="/wiki/2004_in_baseball" title="2004 in baseball">2004 season</a>, the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">Giants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Red Sox</a> played each other for the first time since meeting in the <a href="/wiki/1912_World_Series" title="1912 World Series">1912 World Series</a>.</li>\n<li>The Red Sox had never played at <a href="/wiki/Wrigley_Field" title="Wrigley Field">Wrigley Field</a> (an NL park since 1916) until <a href="/wiki/2005_in_baseball" title="2005 in baseball">2005</a>. Conversely, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>\' trip to to <a href="/wiki/Fenway_Park" title="Fenway Park">Fenway Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/2011_in_baseball" title="2011 in baseball">2011</a> was their first appearance there since the teams met in the <a href="/wiki/1918_World_Series" title="1918 World Series">1918 World Series</a>. The Cubs played their home games in that series in the more expansive <a href="/wiki/Comiskey_Park" title="Comiskey Park">Comiskey Park</a>.</li>\n<li>In <a href="/wiki/2004_in_baseball" title="2004 in baseball">2004</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">Yankees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Dodgers</a> met for the first time since the <a href="/wiki/1981_World_Series" title="1981 World Series">1981 World Series</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dodger_Stadium" title="Dodger Stadium">Dodger Stadium</a>. They didn\'t meet at the Bronx until 2013, making the Dodgers the only team to never have played a regular season game at the <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium_(1923)" title="Yankee Stadium (1923)">old Yankee Stadium</a>.</li>\n<li>In <a href="/wiki/2008_in_baseball" title="2008 in baseball">2008</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> visited the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> for the first time since the <a href="/wiki/1960_World_Series" title="1960 World Series">1960 World Series</a>.</li></ul></li>\n<li>It allows for a rematch of the previous World Series. This has occurred in 12 of the 23 seasons of interleague play, most notably in 2016 when (<a href="/wiki/2015_World_Series" title="2015 World Series">2015 World Series</a> combatants <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a>) met in a four game home-and-home series, including on Opening Day in Kansas City. This is the only time in MLB history that a World Series rematch occurred, within the subsequent season, either on Opening Day or at the venues of both participants.</li>\n<li>It allows the relative strength of the two leagues to be measured against each other over 300 games per year, rather than just in the World Series once per year.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cons">Cons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interleague_play&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>Most <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitchers</a> do not like taking batting practice for the opportunity to bat in one or two games (unless they\'ve played in the National League before.) These pitchers are also unaccustomed to running the bases, which can lead to injury and premature fatigue. (For example, <a href="/wiki/Chien-Ming_Wang" title="Chien-Ming Wang">Chien-Ming Wang</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> suffered a season-ending <a href="/wiki/Lisfranc_ligament" title="Lisfranc ligament">lisfranc</a> sprain on his right foot when running the bases during an interleague game against the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> in 2008.)</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> and <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a> are robbed of the mystique that used to result from the two leagues playing completely exclusive schedules during the regular season: in the case of the World Series, the "best in the American League" playing the "best in the National League" for the only time that season.</li>\n<li>More games against interleague opponents means fewer games against same-league and division rivals \xe2\x80\x93 the latter of which may be more compelling. However, the leagues currently play an unbalanced schedule that favors divisional opponents rather than teams from other divisions (which is important due to the postseason qualifying structure \xe2\x80\x93 only the best team from a given division is guaranteed a berth in the postseason). Now, each divisional opponent meets each other 19 times as opposed to 18 in previous years, and each natural rivalry sees only 4 games as opposed to 6 in previous years.</li>\n<li>Some have argued that the AL possesses an unfair advantage over the NL because of the designated hitter rule in the AL, citing the overwhelming dominance of the AL in interleague play for more than a decade. When NL teams are on the road, they are forced to find a DH in place of their pitcher, who would normally bat ninth. Sometimes, the NL team will use one of their star hitters as the DH and use a bench player to fill in for the appointed DH, and other times, the NL team will simply use a bench player as the DH and have him bat later. In either case, however, the benefits of using a DH in place of the pitcher are minimal, especially considering that the AL designated hitters have seen more action in their positions and that AL teams still possess their full 9-man batting lineup. Even when the NL team is hosting, arguments have been made that there is no real benefit for the NL team either. For the most part, designated hitters are also passable fielders, meaning that they can still be used in the game. And although AL pitchers see less action than NL pitchers, stats have showed that AL pitcher batting average is not much lower than NL pitcher batting average, in large part due to the fact that several AL pitchers have played in the NL. For these reasons, some have argued that the NL should adopt the DH, while others have argued that the AL should drop the DH.</li>\n<li>These cons applied primarily to the 1997\xe2\x80\x932001 and/or 2002\xe2\x80\x932012 formats and not necessarily to the current format:\n<ul><li>The "rivalry" series that consist of six games a year for some teams leads to further scheduling inequities. For example, the AL West race can be skewed by Seattle getting six games per year against last-place San Diego and Oakland\'s six matchups against defending champion San Francisco.</li>\n<li>Some teams play a certain inter-league team more than a certain intra-league team. For example, the Washington Nationals played the Baltimore Orioles (an AL team) 6 times in the 2012 season, and the San Diego Padres and Pittsburgh Pirates (NL teams) only 5 times each. This will no longer be the case starting 2013, when every pair of intra-league foes are guaranteed six meetings against each other, three at each venue.</li>\n<li>Most notably, teams no longer play identical opponents as their divisional rivals, and even where they do, they don\'t always play them an identical number of times. This can lead to "<a href="/wiki/Strength_of_schedule" title="Strength of schedule">strength of schedule</a>" disparities like those the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a> has to deal with on a yearly basis. For example, in 2005, the San Diego Padres played every AL Central team except for Kansas City Royals, who had the worst record in the league; while their division rivals, the San Francisco Giants, played every AL Central team except for the Chicago White Sox, who had the best record in the AL and went on to win the World Series. In the current format these inequities are sharply reduced, since all teams within a division will have the same opponents from the rotating division each season, while the rivalry series is reduced to four games each season.</li></ul></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
