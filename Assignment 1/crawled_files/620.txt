href="/w/index.php?title=Cheltenham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg/220px-Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg" width="220" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg/330px-Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg/440px-Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1856" data-file-height="1552" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheltenhammap_1933.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cheltenham in 1933</div></div></div>\n<p>Cheltenham stands on the small <a href="/wiki/River_Chelt" title="River Chelt">River Chelt</a>, which rises nearby at <a href="/wiki/Dowdeswell" title="Dowdeswell">Dowdeswell</a> and runs through the town on its way to the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">Severn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It was first recorded in 803, as <i>Celtan hom</i>; the meaning has not been resolved with certainty, but latest scholarship concludes that the first element preserves a pre-British noun <i>cilta</i>, \'steep hill\', here referring to the Cotswold scarp; the second element may mean \'settlement\' or \'water-meadow\'.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  As a royal manor, it features in the earliest pages of the <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> where it is named <i>Chintenha[m]</i>. The town was awarded a market charter in 1226.\n</p><p>Though little remains of its pre-spa history, Cheltenham has been a health and holiday spa town resort since the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Mineral_spring" title="Mineral spring">mineral springs</a> there in 1716. Captain <a href="/wiki/Henry_Skillicorne" title="Henry Skillicorne">Henry Skillicorne</a> (1678\xe2\x80\x931763), is credited with being the first entrepreneur to recognise the opportunity to exploit the mineral springs.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The retired "master mariner" became co-owner of the property containing Cheltenham\'s first mineral spring upon his 1732<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> marriage to Elizabeth Mason.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Her father, William Mason, had done little in his lifetime to promote the healing properties of the mineral water apart from limited advertising and building a small enclosure over the spring.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Skillicorne\'s wide travels as a merchant had prepared him to see the potential lying dormant on this inherited property. After moving to Cheltenham in 1738, he immediately began improvements intended to attract visitors to his spa. He built a pump to regulate the flow of water and erected an elaborate well-house complete with a ballroom and upstairs billiard room to entertain his customers. The beginnings of Cheltenham\'s famous tree-lined promenades and gardens surrounding its spas were first designed by Captain Skillicorne with the help of "wealthy and traveled" friends who understood the value of relaxing avenues. The beautiful walks and gardens were naturally adorned with sweeping vistas of the countryside. Soon the gentry and nobility from across the county were enticed to come and investigate the beneficial waters of Cheltenham\'s market town spa.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The visit of <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="George III of Great Britain">George III</a> with the queen and royal princesses in 1788 set a stamp of fashion on the spa.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The spa waters can still be sampled at the <a href="/wiki/Pittville_Pump_Room" title="Pittville Pump Room">Pittville Pump Room</a>, built for this purpose and completed in 1830;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> it is a centrepiece of <a href="/wiki/Pittville" title="Pittville">Pittville</a>, a planned extension of Cheltenham to the north, undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pitt" title="Joseph Pitt">Joseph Pitt</a>, who laid the first stone 4 May 1825.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cheltenham\'s success as a spa town is reflected in the railway station, which is still called <i><a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Spa_railway_station" title="Cheltenham Spa railway station">Cheltenham Spa</a></i>, and spa facilities in other towns that were inspired by or named after it.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alice_Liddell" title="Alice Liddell">Alice Liddell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a> were regular visitors to a house in Cudnall Street, <a href="/wiki/Charlton_Kings" title="Charlton Kings">Charlton Kings</a> \xe2\x80\x93 a suburb of Cheltenham. This house was owned by Alice Liddell\'s grandparents, and still contains the mirror, or looking glass, that was purportedly the inspiration for Lewis Carroll\'s novel <a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a>, published in 1871.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Horse racing began in Cheltenham in 1815, and became a major national attraction after the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Festival" title="Cheltenham Festival">Festival</a> in 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Whilst the volume of tourists visiting the spa has declined, the racecourse attracts tens of thousands of visitors to each day of the festival each year,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> with such large numbers of visitors having a significant impact on the town.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the United States Army Service of Supply,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> European Theatre of Operations established its primary headquarters at Cheltenham under the direction of <a href="/wiki/John_C._H._Lee" title="John C. H. Lee">Lt. Gen. John C. H. Lee</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> with the flats of the Cheltenham Racecourse<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> becoming a giant storage depot for countless trucks, jeeps, tanks and artillery pieces. Most of this materiel was reshipped to the continent for and after the <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">D-Day invasion</a>. Lee and his primary staff had offices and took residence at Thirlestaine Hall in Cheltenham.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 1 April 1974, under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, the borough of Cheltenham was merged with <a href="/wiki/Charlton_Kings" title="Charlton Kings">Charlton Kings</a> <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban district</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">non-metropolitan district</a> of Cheltenham. Four parishes\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Swindon_Village" title="Swindon Village">Swindon Village</a>, <a href="/wiki/Up_Hatherley" title="Up Hatherley">Up Hatherley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leckhampton" title="Leckhampton">Leckhampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prestbury,_Gloucestershire" title="Prestbury, Gloucestershire">Prestbury</a>\xe2\x80\x94were added to the borough of Cheltenham from the borough of <a href="/wiki/Tewkesbury_(borough)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tewkesbury (borough)">Tewkesbury</a> in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first British jet aircraft prototype, the <a href="/wiki/Gloster_E.28/39" title="Gloster E.28/39">Gloster E.28/39</a>, was manufactured in Cheltenham. Manufacturing started in <a href="/wiki/Hucclecote" title="Hucclecote">Hucclecote</a> near Gloucester, but was later moved to Regent Motors in Cheltenham High Street (now the Regent Arcade), considered a location safer from bombing during the Second World War.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheltenham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cheltenham is on the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Cotswolds" title="Cotswolds">Cotswolds</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> in the South-West region of England. The small <a href="/wiki/River_Chelt" title="River Chelt">River Chelt</a> flows under and through the town.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cleeve_Hill" title="Cleeve Hill">Cleeve Hill</a> overlooks the town and is the highest point in the <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">county</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a> at 1,083 feet (330 m).\n</p><p>The town is near the northeastern edge of the <a href="/wiki/South_West_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="South West of England">South West of England</a> region being 88 miles (140&#160;km) west-northwest of London, 38 miles (61 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> and 41 miles (65&#160;km) south of Birmingham.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighbourhoods">Neighbourhoods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a affluent <a href="/wiki/Montpellier,_Cheltenham" title="Montpellier, Cheltenham">Montpellier</a> is known for its vibrant bars, restaurants and specialist shops. Surrounded by many grade one listed buildings, Montpellier Gardens are part of the Cheltenham Central Conservation Area.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><br />\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Lansdown Crescent</dt></dl>\n<p>Lansdown Crescent is a <a href="/wiki/Regency_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Regency period">Regency period</a> <a href="/wiki/Terraced_house" title="Terraced house">terrace</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Buonarotti_Papworth" title="John Buonarotti Papworth">John Buonarotti Papworth</a> for R.W. and C. Jearrad and constructed in the 1830s. The terrace is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Convex" class="extiw" title="wikt:Convex">convex</a>, and opposite the north-eastern part stands <b>Lansdown Court</b>, an <a href="/wiki/Italianate_architecture" title="Italianate architecture">Italianate villa</a> possibly designed by Papworth but more probably by the Jearrads and built about 1830.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Charlton Park</dt></dl>\n<p>Charlton Park is a former 72-acre (29&#160;ha) historic park with mansion house,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> about a mile south-east of the town centre. From 1935 the parkland gradually became a private residential area, the main housing development taking place between 1976 and 1983. The original mansion house dated from the 13th century; alterations throughout the centuries transformed it from a medieval, timber-framed hall-house into an 18th-century brick-faced mansion in the classical style. In the 1780s the estate was emparked for deer and had magnificent Dutch-style water gardens. After 1935 the old house became part of Charlton Park Convent, and since 1987 has been part of <a href="/wiki/St._Edward%27s_School,_Cheltenham" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Edward&#39;s School, Cheltenham">St Edward\'s School</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_belt">Green belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheltenham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Green belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_and_Cheltenham_Green_Belt" title="Gloucester and Cheltenham Green Belt">Gloucester and Cheltenham Green Belt</a></div>\n<p>Parts of the town has green belt along its fringes, and this extends into the surrounding Tewkesbury district, helping to maintain local green space, prevent further urban sprawl and unplanned expansion towards Gloucester and Bishop\'s Cleeve, as well as protecting smaller villages in between. along with St Peter Leckhampton parish church and Brizen Playing Fields/Haven and Greenmead parks along the south of the borough, are covered.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" In comparison to many other areas in Britain, Cheltenham has warm summers and mild winters. The town held the British maximum temperature record from 1990 to 2003\xe2\x80\x94temperatures reached 37.1&#160;\xc2\xb0C (98.8&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The absolute minimum is \xe2\x88\x9220.1&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x924.2&#160;\xc2\xb0F), set during December 1981. It has a <a href="/wiki/Michelin-starred" class="mw-redirect" title="Michelin-starred">Michelin two-star restaurant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Le_Champignon_Sauvage" title="Le Champignon Sauvage">Le Champignon Sauvage</a>.\n</p><p>In 2006, a house valuation web site rated Cheltenham the most desirable property location in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2014, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> newspaper named Cheltenham as the best place class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> found that, at the end of 2011, 41 multi-millionaires lived in Cheltenham, which was the fourth-highest rate in the UK of multi-millionaires per 100,000 people at 35.44.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a In 2014 many of the town\'s historic cultural and leisure buildings were put under the control of <a href="/wiki/The_Cheltenham_Trust" title="The Cheltenham Trust">The Cheltenham Trust</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> a charity set up to manage and develop the buildings on behalf of the town. Along with The Wilson, the Trust now manages the Town Hall, the Pittville Pump Room, the Prince of Wales Stadium and Leisure @, a large fitness and swimming complex. A volunteer board of Trustees, chaired by media entrepreneur Peter Harkness,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> controls the Trust. The Trust\'s CEO is Julie Finch<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> former director museums in Bristol.\n</p><p>Cheltenham features several sculptural artworks of note, including: \n</p>\n<ul><li>Neptune\'s Fountain in the Promenade, built in 1893 and designed by Joseph Hall<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The Hare and the Minotaur, also in the Promenade, created in 1995 by <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Ryder" title="Sophie Ryder">Sophie Ryder</a><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>A life-size bronze of an Emperor Penguin by Nick Bibby and placed in the foyer of The Wilson art gallery and museum in 2015<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The Wishing Fish Clock in the Regent Shopping Arcade, unveiled in 1987 and designed by <a href="/wiki/Kit_Williams" title="Kit Williams">Kit Williams</a></li></ul>\n<dl><dt>Music</dt></dl>\n<p>Cheltenham hosts the annual <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Music_Festival" title="Cheltenham Music Festival">Cheltenham Music Festival</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Jazz_Festival" title="Cheltenham Jazz Festival">Cheltenham Jazz Festival</a> and the Ukulele Festival of Great Britain.\n</p><p>In 2010, Cheltenham was named the UK\'s fifth "most musical" City by <a href="/wiki/PRS_for_Music" title="PRS for Music">PRS for Music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mirror.co.uk_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mirror.co.uk-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>History</dt></dl>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Art_Gallery_%26_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheltenham Art Gallery &amp; Museum">Cheltenham Art Gallery &amp; Museum</a> has a notable collection of decorative arts from the era of the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Arts and Crafts Movement">Arts and Crafts Movement</a>. Cheltenham\'s arts festivals, having been started in 1926.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greenbelt_festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenbelt festival">Greenbelt</a>, a Christian arts and music festival, The meeting is often very popular with Irish visitors,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> mostly because of that nation\'s affinity with horse racing, but also because it usually coincides with <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Patrick&#39;s Day">St. Patrick\'s Day</a>, a national holiday in celebration of the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.\n</p><p>Large amounts of money are bet during festival week, with hundreds of millions of <a href="/wiki/Pound_Sterling" class="mw-redirect" title="Pound Sterling">pounds</a> being gambled over the four days.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Cheltenham is often noted for its atmosphere, most notably the "Cheltenham roar", which refers to the enormous amount of noise that the crowd generates as One is at St. Christopher\'s (Warden Hill), the lightest ring of church bells in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The other is a ring of 12 bells hung in <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Minster,_St_Mary%27s" title="Cheltenham Minster, St Mary&#39;s">St. Mary\'s Church</a> (the Minster). These were the venue in 2008 for the eliminators of the National 12 Bell Striking contest, in which teams of <a href="/wiki/Campanology" title="Campanology">campanologists</a> from around the world compete to win the Taylor Trophy. In 2017 the old ring of 12 was completely replaced with <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.cheltminsterbells.org.uk/">new bells</a> cast by <a href="/wiki/John_Taylor_%26_Co" title="John Taylor &amp; Co">John Taylor &amp; Co</a>. The tenor bell is just over a ton in weight, and the new ring also includes a thirteenth bell, a sharp 2nd, to provide a lighter 8.
