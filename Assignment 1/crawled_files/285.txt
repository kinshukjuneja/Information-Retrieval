id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Independence_Party_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Independence Party (disambiguation)">Independence Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Independent_Party_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Independent Party (disambiguation)">Independent Party</a> or <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Party" title="America&#39;s Party">America\'s Independent Party</a>.</div>\n<p>An <b>independent</b> or <b>nonpartisan politician</b> is an individual <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> not affiliated with any <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a>. There are numerous reasons why someone may stand for office as an independent.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Independents may support policies which are different from those of the major political parties.</li>\n<li>In some parts of the world, electors may have a tradition of electing independents, so standing for a political party is a disadvantage.</li>\n<li>In some countries (such as <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>), a political party can only be registered if it has a large number of members in more than one region, but in certain regions only a minority of electors support the major parties.</li>\n<li>In some countries (including <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>), political parties are <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unlawful" class="extiw" title="wikt:unlawful">unlawful</a> and all candidates thus stand as independents.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In some countries where politics is otherwise traditionally partisan, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, subnational bodies and offices such as the <a href="/wiki/Nebraska_State_Legislature" class="mw-redirect" title="Nebraska State Legislature">Nebraska State Legislature</a> and various directly-elected judicial and executive positions are nonpartisan and require politicians to abstain from running for office as part of a political party, even if they may be a member of one.</li>\n<li>In some countries where politics is otherwise traditionally partisan, such as <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, the incumbent President must always be an independent and cannot run for reelection as a member of a political party.</li></ul>\n<p>Some independent politicians may be associated with a political party, perhaps as former members of it, or else have views that align with it, but choose not to stand in its name, or are unable to do so because the party in question has selected another candidate. Others may belong to or support a political party at the national level but believe they should not formally represent it (and thus be subject to its policies) at another level.\n</p><p>In running for public office, independents sometimes choose to form a party or alliance with other independents, and may formally register their party or alliance. Even where the word "independent" is used, such alliances have much in common with a political party, especially if there is an organization which needs to approve the "independent" candidates.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-39"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">30</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Independent_politicians_in_Australia" title="Independent politicians in Australia">Independent politicians in Australia</a></div>\n<p>Independents are a recurrent feature of the federal <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Australia" title="Parliament of Australia">Parliament of Australia</a>, and they are more commonly elected to state parliaments. There have been up to five independents in every federal parliament since 1990, and independents have won twenty-eight times during national elections in that time. A large proportion of independents are former members of one of Australia\'s four main parties, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Australian Labor Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Australia" title="Liberal Party of Australia">Liberal Party of Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Australian Greens</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/National_Party_of_Australia" title="National Party of Australia">National Party of Australia</a>. In 2013 a political party named the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Independents" title="Australian Independents">Australian Independents</a> was registered with the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Electoral_Commission" title="Australian Electoral Commission">Australian Electoral Commission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As at 2017, two independents sit in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_House_of_Representatives" title="Australian House of Representatives">Australian House of Representatives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Wilkie" title="Andrew Wilkie">Andrew Wilkie</a> from <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Denison" title="Division of Denison">Denison</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> (former <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Greens</a> candidate) and <a href="/wiki/Cathy_McGowan_(politician)" title="Cathy McGowan (politician)">Cathy McGowan</a> from <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Indi" title="Division of Indi">Indi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>.\n</p><p>Independent <a href="/wiki/Australian_Senate" title="Australian Senate">Senators</a> are quite rare. In modern politics, independent <a href="/wiki/Brian_Harradine" title="Brian Harradine">Brian Harradine</a> served from 1975 to 2005 with considerable influence at times. <a href="/wiki/Nick_Xenophon" title="Nick Xenophon">Nick Xenophon</a> has been the only elected independent Senator since his election to the Senate at the <a href="/wiki/2007_Australian_federal_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2007 Australian federal election">2007 federal election</a>. Xenophon was re-elected for another six-year term at the <a href="/wiki/Australian_federal_election,_2013" title="Australian federal election, 2013">2013 federal election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Labour_Party_(Australia)" title="Democratic Labour Party (Australia)">DLP</a> Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Madigan_(Australian_politician)" title="John Madigan (Australian politician)">John Madigan</a> became an independent Senator in September 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Palmer_United_Party" title="Palmer United Party">PUP</a> Senators <a href="/wiki/Jacqui_Lambie" title="Jacqui Lambie">Jacqui Lambie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Lazarus" title="Glenn Lazarus">Glenn Lazarus</a> became independent Senators in November 2014 and March 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The independent politicians are not allowed to run for offices in Brazil. The Constitution of 1988, in its Article 14, \xc2\xa73rd, item V, says that "Are conditions for eligibility: V - party affiliation."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, the Proposal Amendment to the Constitution (PEC) no. 6/2015, authored by senator <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Reguffe" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Reguffe">Jos\xc3\xa9 Reguffe</a>, would allow the independent candidacy of individuals who have the support of at least 1% of the electors able to vote in the region (city, state or country, depending on the election) in which the candidate is running.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Currently, members of the legislative and executive can leave their respective parties after elected, as is the case of senator Reguffe, who left the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Labour_Party_(Brazil)" title="Democratic Labour Party (Brazil)">Democratic Labour Party</a> (PDT) in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bulgaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Bulgaria" title="President of Bulgaria">President of Bulgaria</a> <a href="/wiki/Rumen_Radev" title="Rumen Radev">Rumen Radev</a> is an independent with support from the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Socialist_Party" title="Bulgarian Socialist Party">Bulgarian Socialist Party</a>. Radev was elected in the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_presidential_election,_2016" title="Bulgarian presidential election, 2016">Bulgarian presidential election of 2016</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Independent_Liberal" title="Independent Liberal">Independent Liberal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independent_Conservative" title="Independent Conservative">Independent Conservative</a></div>\n<p>Independent Members of Parliament were numerous in the last decades of the 19th century but diminished as the party system solidified. It remained common, however, to have a small number of <a href="/wiki/Independent_Liberal" title="Independent Liberal">Independent Liberal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Independent_Conservative" title="Independent Conservative">Independent Conservative</a> MPs into the 1950s.\n</p><p>Independent politicians have held considerable sway in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons of Canada</a> in recent years as Canada has been governed by successive <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority governments</a> with independent <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Members of Parliament</a> (MPs) sometimes sharing in the <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_(parliament)" title="Balance of power (parliament)">balance of power</a>.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2004" title="Canadian federal election, 2004">2004 federal election</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Cadman" title="Chuck Cadman">Chuck Cadman</a> was elected to federal parliament as an independent MP representing the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> <a href="/wiki/Riding_(division)" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding (division)">riding</a> of <a href="/wiki/Surrey_North" title="Surrey North">Surrey North</a>. Cadman had previously represented that riding on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Party_of_Canada" title="Reform Party of Canada">Reform Party of Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Alliance" title="Canadian Alliance">Canadian Alliance</a>, but after the Canadian Alliance merged with the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Canada">Progressive Conservative Party of Canada</a> to form the new <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Conservative Party of Canada">Conservative Party of Canada</a> in 2003, Cadman lost the nomination to represent the Conservative Party in that riding to <a href="/wiki/Jasbir_Singh_Cheema" title="Jasbir Singh Cheema">Jasbir Singh Cheema</a>. Cadman then stood in the subsequent election as an independent and defeated Cheema, as well as the candidates of other Canadian parties, by a significant margin.\n</p><p>In the spring of 2005, Cadman cast the tying vote in favour of a budget supported by the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal Party</a> government of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Martin" title="Paul Martin">Paul Martin</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party_of_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="New Democratic Party of Canada">New Democratic Party</a> (NDP), but opposed by the opposition Conservatives and <a href="/wiki/Bloc_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois" title="Bloc Qu\xc3\xa9b\xc3\xa9cois">Bloc Qu\xc3\xa9b\xc3\xa9cois</a>.  Two other independents also voted on that budget. <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Parrish" title="Carolyn Parrish">Carolyn Parrish</a>, independent MP for <a href="/wiki/Mississauga%E2%80%94Erindale" title="Mississauga\xe2\x80\x94Erindale">Mississauga\xe2\x80\x94Erindale</a>, had recently been kicked out of the Liberal Party for criticizing US president George W Bush, but nonetheless sided with the Liberals on the budget vote. <a href="/wiki/David_Kilgour" title="David Kilgour">David Kilgour</a> independent MP for <a href="/wiki/Edmonton%E2%80%94Mill_Woods%E2%80%94Beaumont" title="Edmonton\xe2\x80\x94Mill Woods\xe2\x80\x94Beaumont">Edmonton\xe2\x80\x94Mill Woods\xe2\x80\x94Beaumont</a>, had previously quit the Liberal caucus and voted with the opposition parties against the budget. The tie vote required the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="Speaker of the House of Commons of Canada">Speaker of the House</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Milliken" title="Peter Milliken">Peter Milliken</a> to cast the deciding vote, and he did so in favor of the budget, allowing the government to survive.\n</p><p>Cadman was terminally ill with <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> at the time he cast his crucial vote, and he died later in 2005. In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2006" title="Canadian federal election, 2006">2006 federal election</a>, his riding was won by NDP candidate <a href="/wiki/Penny_Priddy" title="Penny Priddy">Penny Priddy</a>. Neither Parrish nor Kilgour (nor <a href="/wiki/Pat_O%27Brien_(Canadian_politician)" title="Pat O&#39;Brien (Canadian politician)">Pat O\'Brien</a>, MP for <a href="/wiki/London%E2%80%94Fanshawe" title="London\xe2\x80\x94Fanshawe">London\xe2\x80\x94Fanshawe</a>, who quit the Liberal Party to sit as an independent after the 2005 budget vote) stood for re-election in 2006.\n</p><p>Another independent candidate, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Arthur" title="Andr\xc3\xa9 Arthur">Andr\xc3\xa9 Arthur</a>, was elected in the <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> riding of <a href="/wiki/Portneuf%E2%80%94Jacques-Cartier" title="Portneuf\xe2\x80\x94Jacques-Cartier">Portneuf\xe2\x80\x94Jacques-Cartier</a> in 2006, and was the only independent to win a seat in that election. He was re-elected in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">October 14, 2008 federal election</a>. Former <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Canada">Progressive Conservative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Conservative Party of Canada">Conservative</a> MP <a href="/wiki/Bill_Casey" title="Bill Casey">Bill Casey</a>, who was expelled from the Conservative Party for voting against the 2007 Federal Budget, also ran as an independent in the 2008 election, easily retaining his seat.\n</p><p>Candidates in federal elections who are not affiliated with a party have two options: independent or no affiliation. In the former case, they appear on the ballot with "Independent" following their name; in the second case, they appear with their name only. The two options are otherwise equivalent.\n</p><p>The territorial legislatures of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a> are <a href="/wiki/Consensus_government" title="Consensus government">consensus governments</a> with no political parties, so that all members sit as independents. There are a few independent members of the other subnational legislatures, which are similar in principle to the federal House of Commons; for example, in the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_general_election,_2009" title="British Columbia general election, 2009">2009 election</a> in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, independent candidate <a href="/wiki/Vicki_Huntington" title="Vicki Huntington">Vicki Huntington</a> narrowly defeated incumbent <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_British_Columbia" title="Attorney General of British Columbia">Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Wally_Oppal" title="Wally Oppal">Wally Oppal</a> as MLA for <a href="/wiki/Delta_South" title="Delta South">Delta South</a>.\n</p><p>True independents should not be confused with members of parties without <a href="/wiki/Official_party_status" title="Official party status">official party status</a> in a legislature. Most legislatures provide that a party must hold a certain number of seats to enjoy certain advantages in staffing, budget, ability to ask questions in <a href="/wiki/Question_Period" title="Question Period">Question Period</a>, and the like. Although members whose parties do not hold this status may have no more privileges than independent members, they remain representatives of political parties.\n</p><p>Also, members who are expelled from or choose to leave their party caucus may sit as "Independent" with some designation, e.g., "Independent Liberal" or "Independent Conservative", to indicate their affiliation to that party even if it is not officially recognized.\n</p><p>Election as an independent is far more common at the municipal level. Many municipalities have no tradition of political parties.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Croatia">Croatia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a politicians are frequently categorised as <i>sans \xc3\xa9tiquette</i> ("without label") in municipal or district elections. However, it is rare to have independent politicians at national level, notably <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Bov%C3%A9" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Bov\xc3\xa9">Jos\xc3\xa9 Bov\xc3\xa9</a> in the 2007 presidential election. <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron" title="Emmanuel Macron">Emmanuel Macron</a> was an independent politician as Minister, but formed his own party to stand in the 2016 presidential election.\n</p><p>In 1920, <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Millerand" title="Alexandre Millerand">Alexandre Millerand</a> was elected <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">president of the Republic</a> under the banner "without label".\n</p><p>From 2001 to 2008 "without label" was no longer used in the nomenclature of the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Interior_(France)" title="Minister of the Interior (France)">Ministry of the Interior</a>. Therefore, from 2008 onwards, the DIV (miscellaneous) or the LDIV code for the "miscellaneous" list has been created to group unclassifiable or categorical interests and, by default, mayors without a declared label claiming No political sensitivity, be it left, center or right. The AUT (other) grade replaces the DIV grade without changing its definition.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Georgia">Georgia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Georgia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 28 October <a href="/wiki/Salome_Zurabishvili" title="Salome Zurabishvili">Salome Zurabishvili</a> runs in <a href="/wiki/Georgian_presidential_election,_2018" title="Georgian presidential election, 2018">the 2018 presidential elections</a> as an independent candidate to become first-ever female president of Georgia.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Joachim_Gauck" title="Joachim Gauck">Joachim Gauck</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_of_Germany" title="President of Germany">President of Germany</a> from March 2012 to March 2017 and the first Federal President without party affiliation, was to date the most prominent Independent politician. In the <a href="/wiki/German_presidential_election,_2010" title="German presidential election, 2010">German presidential election of 2010</a> he was the candidate of the  <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance &#39;90/The Greens">Greens</a>, in <a href="/wiki/German_presidential_election,_2012" title="German presidential election, 2012">2012</a> the candidate of all major parties except <a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">The Left</a>. His presidency\xe2\x80\x94though his powers are limited\xe2\x80\x94constitutes an exception, as Independent politicians have rarely held high office in German history, at least not since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. It has nevertheless happened that a presidential candidate without any chances of election by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Convention_(Germany)" title="Federal Convention (Germany)">Federal Convention</a> was not a party member: for example, when in 1984 the Greens came up with the writer <a href="/wiki/Luise_Rinser" title="Luise Rinser">Luise Rinser</a>.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a> parliament nearly all deputies belong to a political party. The voting system of <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">personalized proportional representation</a> (since 1949) allows any individual holding the <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">passive right to vote</a> to stand for a direct <a href="/wiki/Mandate_(politics)" title="Mandate (politics)">mandate</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_districts" class="mw-redirect" title="Electoral districts">electoral districts</a>\xe2\x80\x94half of the seats in parliament are distributed by districts according to a <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">plurality voting system</a>. Such a candidate has to present 200 signatures in favor of his candidacy, the same as a candidate of a party that had no parliamentary presentation previously. The first <a href="/wiki/West_German_federal_election,_1949" title="West German federal election, 1949">Bundestag election in 1949</a> saw three independents elected; since then, no party-independent candidate has won a seat.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> level, the situation is more or less the same: only party members have a real chance to be elected to a <a href="/wiki/Landtag" title="Landtag">Landtag</a> legislature, and state ministers without party membership are just as rare as at the federal level. However, in <a href="/wiki/Local_election" title="Local election">local elections</a> it may occur that an independent politician is elected deputy to <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Germany" title="Districts of Germany">districts</a>\', cities\' and municipalities\' assemblies, as well as member of a <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">Northern Germany</a>. In recent years, independents have formed <a href="/wiki/Free_Voters" title="Free Voters">Free Voters</a> associations to enter Landtag parliaments, so far only successful in <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Bavaria" title="Landtag of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>.\n</p><p>An independent <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a>, who also is not a member of a voters\' association, holds the status of a <i><a href="/wiki/Non-inscrit" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-inscrit">non-inscrit</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">fraktionsloser Abgeordneter</i>) not affiliated to any <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_group" title="Parliamentary group">parliamentary group</a>. A representative who leaves his party (and his parliamentary group) and does not join another becomes an independent and <i>non-inscrit</i>. In 1989 the Bundestag MP <a href="/wiki/Thomas_W%C3%BCppesahl" title="Thomas W\xc3\xbcppesahl">Thomas W\xc3\xbcppesahl</a>, who had left the Green Party in 1987 and was excluded from the Green parliamentary group the next year, obtained more rights as a non-inscrit, for example more talking time and representation in a subcommittee, when the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Constitutional Court of Germany">Federal Constitutional Court</a> decided partially in his favor.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">German unification</a> of 1871, the first <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Reich Chancellors</a> (<a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">heads of government</a>) <i>de jure</i> served as <a href="/wiki/Executive_officer" title="Executive officer">executive officers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Imperial states</a> as non-partisans, usually recruited from the traditional bureaucratic, aristocratic and/or military elites. In the fierce political conflicts during the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar</a> period after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, several chancellors and Reich Ministers also had no party affiliation: these chancellors were <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Cuno" title="Wilhelm Cuno">Wilhelm Cuno</a> (1922\xe2\x80\x931923), <a href="/wiki/Hans_Luther" title="Hans Luther">Hans Luther</a> (1925\xe2\x80\x931926), the former <a href="/wiki/Centre_Party_(Germany)" title="Centre Party (Germany)">Centre</a> politician <a href="/wiki/Franz_von_Papen" title="Franz von Papen">Franz von Papen</a> (1932), and <a href="/wiki/Kurt_von_Schleicher" title="Kurt von Schleicher">Kurt von Schleicher</a> (1932\xe2\x80\x931933). The last two <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Germany" title="Cabinet of Germany">cabinets</a> appointed by Reich President <a href="/wiki/Paul_von_Hindenburg" title="Paul von Hindenburg">Paul von Hindenburg</a>, a non-partisan (though strongly <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_Germany" title="Conservatism in Germany">Conservative</a>) himself, were regarded as apolitical cabinets of experts with regard to the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a>; many of the ministers were not party members.\n</p><p>Since World War II, only two ministers of (West) German cabinets have not been party members, though "on the ticket" of the major party in the coalition, the Social Democrats: Education Minister <a href="/wiki/Hans_Leussink" title="Hans Leussink">Hans Leussink</a> (1969\xe2\x80\x931972), and Minister of Economy <a href="/wiki/Werner_M%C3%BCller_(politician)" title="Werner M\xc3\xbcller (politician)">Werner M\xc3\xbcller</a> (1998\xe2\x80\x932002). Minister of Justice <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Kinkel" title="Klaus Kinkel">Klaus Kinkel</a> only shortly after his appointment joined the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democrats</a> in 1991. A special case is the former Federal Minister and Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Erhard" title="Ludwig Erhard">Ludwig Erhard</a>, whose affiliation with the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a> (CDU) has not been conclusively established: although he served as Minister of Economics from 1949 to 1963 and as Federal Chancellor from 1963 to 1966, and was even elected CDU <a href="/wiki/Party_chair" title="Party chair">party chairman</a> in 1966, it seems that he never signed a membership form or paid contributions. Researches by <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Stern" class="mw-redirect" title="Der Stern">Der Stern</a></i> magazine have revealed a record at the CDU party archives created only in 1968, with the faked date of entry of early March 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>More than half of Hong Kong\'s <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Council_(Hong_Kong)" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative Council (Hong Kong)">Legislative Council</a> is made up of independents, or members whose political groups are represented by one sole member in the legislature. The foundation of formal political parties, starting at the end of the 19th century, considerably diminished the number of unaffiliated politicians, although a smaller number of independent candidates continued to be elected up until the 1940s. Since then, however, there have been relatively few independent politicians in Parliament. No independent candidate has won or held a seat in a general election since <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1943" title="New Zealand general election, 1943">1943</a>, although two independent candidates have been successful in <a href="/wiki/By-election" title="By-election">by-elections</a> (in all cases after having held the seats in question as partisan candidates up until that point). Other politicians have become independents in the course of a parliamentary term, but not been voted into office as such.\n</p><p>The last person to be directly elected to Parliament as an independent in New Zealand was <a href="/wiki/Winston_Peters" title="Winston Peters">Winston Peters</a>, who won the <a href="/wiki/Tauranga_by-election,_1993" title="Tauranga by-election, 1993">1993 by-election</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tauranga_(New_Zealand_electorate)" title="Tauranga (New Zealand electorate)">Tauranga</a> electorate as an independent after having previously held it a member of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party" title="New Zealand National Party">National Party</a>. By the time of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1993" title="New Zealand general election, 1993">next general election</a>, he had formed his own party (<a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a>), and thus was no longer standing as an independent. Since that time, the only independents in Parliament have been people who quit or were expelled from their original party  but retained their seats without going through a by-election. Some have gone on to found or co-found their own parties, with varying levels of success \xe2\x80\x94 examples include <a href="/wiki/Peter_Dunne" title="Peter Dunne">Peter Dunne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taito_Phillip_Field" title="Taito Phillip Field">Taito Phillip Field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Copeland" title="Gordon Copeland">Gordon Copeland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tau_Henare" title="Tau Henare">Tau Henare</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alamein_Kopu" title="Alamein Kopu">Alamein Kopu</a>. Others have joined parties which were then outside Parliament, such as <a href="/wiki/Frank_Grover" title="Frank Grover">Frank Grover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuariki_Delamere" title="Tuariki Delamere">Tuariki Delamere</a>.\n</p><p>There were two independent MPs in the <a href="/wiki/49th_New_Zealand_Parliament" title="49th New Zealand Parliament">last Parliament</a>; <a href="/wiki/Chris_Carter_(politician)" title="Chris Carter (politician)">Chris Carter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hone_Harawira" title="Hone Harawira">Hone Harawira</a>. Carter became an independent after his criticisms of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour Party</a>\'s leadership resulted in his being expelled from the Labour caucus, while Harawira resigned from the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_Party" title="M\xc4\x81ori Party">M\xc4\x81ori Party</a> and, after a short period as an independent, also resigned as an MP in order to force the <a href="/wiki/Te_Tai_Tokerau_by-election,_2011" title="Te Tai Tokerau by-election, 2011">2011 by-election</a> when he was re-elected as representative of his new political party, <a href="/wiki/Mana_Party_(New_Zealand)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mana Party (New Zealand)">Mana</a> and retained the seat in the 2011 General Election. There are also two parties other which have only a single MP  <a href="/wiki/United_Future" title="United Future">United Future</a> with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Dunne" title="Peter Dunne">Peter Dunne</a> and <a href="/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand" title="ACT New Zealand">ACT</a> with <a href="/wiki/David_Seymour_(politician)" title="David Seymour (politician)">David Seymour</a>. Neither Dunne nor Seymour are classed as independents \xe2\x80\x94 Dunne\'s presence in Parliament is due to personal votes in his home electorate, and Seymour\'s presence is as the sole elected MP of ACT because of a collapse in their support in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2011" title="New Zealand general election, 2011">2011 election</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/50th_New_Zealand_Parliament" title="50th New Zealand Parliament">50th New Zealand Parliament</a> there was one independent MP: <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Horan" title="Brendan Horan">Brendan Horan</a>, a former New Zealand First MP who was expelled from his party because of allegations of misappropriation of family assets.\n</p><p>Peter Dunne effectively became an Independent MP for a short period after his United Future political party was deregistered on 25 June 2013 by the Electoral Commission, as the party no longer had the required minimum of 500 members. Candidates standing for United Kingdom local elections and United Kingdom parliamentary elections, including the devolved assemblies, can use the name of a registered political party, or the term \'Independent\' (or its Welsh language equivalent <i>annibynol</i>) or no ballot paper description at all<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> (this latter choice was used, for example, by David Icke at the <a href="/wiki/Haltemprice_and_Howden_by-election,_2008" title="Haltemprice and Howden by-election, 2008">Haltemprice and Howden by-election, 2008</a>).\n</p><p>Some groups in the United Kingdom who are not affiliated to any national or regional party have registered locality-based political parties. Some English examples are the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Kidderminster_Hospital_and_Health_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent Kidderminster Hospital and Health Concern">Independent Kidderminster Hospital and Health Concern</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Epsom_and_Ewell_Residents_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Epsom and Ewell Residents Association">Epsom and Ewell Residents Association</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Devizes_Guardians" title="Devizes Guardians">Devizes Guardians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Derwentside_Independents" title="Derwentside Independents">Derwentside Independents</a>, and the East Yorkshire Independents.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="House_of_Commons">House of Commons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: House of Commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_UK_minor_party_and_independent_MPs_elected" title="List of UK minor party and independent MPs elected">List of UK minor party and independent MPs elected</a></div>\n<p>Before the twentieth century it was fairly common for independents to be elected to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons of the United Kingdom</a>, but there have been very few since 1945.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/S._O._Davies" title="S. O. Davies">S. O. Davies</a>, the veteran <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> MP held his <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a> seat in the General Election of 1970, standing as an independent, after he had been deselected by the Labour Party.\n</p><p>The journalist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Bell" title="Martin Bell">Martin Bell</a> was elected at <a href="/wiki/Tatton_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Tatton (UK Parliament constituency)">Tatton</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election_1997" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom general election 1997">General Election of 1997</a>, having stood on an anti-corruption platform. He was the first independent to be newly elected to the Commons since 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-GuardianMartinBell_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianMartinBell-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He stood unsuccessfully in a different constituency in 2001.\n</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001 General Election</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(UK_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Taylor (UK politician)">Dr Richard Taylor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Kidderminster_Hospital_and_Health_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent Kidderminster Hospital and Health Concern">Independent Kidderminster Hospital and Health Concern</a> party was elected for the constituency of <a href="/wiki/Wyre_Forest_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Wyre Forest (UK Parliament constituency)">Wyre Forest</a>.\n</p><p>Two independent (or local party) members of parliament were elected in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 election</a>, although both were defeated five years later.\n</p><p>At the same General Election, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Law" title="Peter Law">Peter Law</a> was elected as an independent at <a href="/wiki/Blaenau_Gwent_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Blaenau Gwent (UK Parliament constituency)">Blaenau Gwent</a>. Law died on 25 April 2006: the resulting <a href="/wiki/Blaenau_Gwent_by-elections,_2006" title="Blaenau Gwent by-elections, 2006">by-election</a> elected <a href="/wiki/Dai_Davies_(politician)" title="Dai Davies (politician)">Dai Davies</a> of the local party <a href="/wiki/Blaenau_Gwent_People%27s_Voice" title="Blaenau Gwent People&#39;s Voice">Blaenau Gwent People\'s Voice</a>. The by-election was unusual as it was the first time in over eighty years that an independent had held a seat previously occupied by another independent.\n</p><p>Also at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 General Election</a> Richard Taylor was re-elected for Wyre Forest. This was notable for the fact that he was the only independent in recent times to have been elected for a second term.\n</p><p>Only one independent was elected to the Commons in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017">2017 elections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Hermon" title="Sylvia Hermon">Sylvia Hermon</a>, the Member for <a href="/wiki/North_Down_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="North Down (UK Parliament constituency)">North Down</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionist</a> who left the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster Unionist Party</a> because of its links with the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a>.\n</p><p>There have also been several instances of politicians being elected to the Commons as representatives of a political party, then resigning the party\'s whip, or having it withdrawn. Examples in this in the 2010-2015 parliament included <a href="/wiki/Mike_Hancock_(British_politician)" title="Mike Hancock (British politician)">Mike Hancock</a> (formerly a Liberal Democrat), <a href="/wiki/Eric_Joyce" title="Eric Joyce">Eric Joyce</a> (formerly Labour) and <a href="/wiki/Nadine_Dorries" title="Nadine Dorries">Nadine Dorries</a>, a Conservative who had the whip withdrawn for part of the parliament and thus sat as an independent during that time.\n</p><p>Independent candidates often stand in British parliamentary elections, often with platforms about specific local issues, but usually without  success. An example from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001 general election</a> was <a href="/wiki/Aston_Villa" class="mw-redirect" title="Aston Villa">Aston Villa</a> supporter Ian Robinson, who stood as an independent in the <a href="/wiki/Sutton_Coldfield_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Sutton Coldfield (UK Parliament constituency)">Sutton Coldfield constituency</a> in protest at the way chairman <a href="/wiki/Doug_Ellis" title="Doug Ellis">Doug Ellis</a> ran the football club. Another example an independent candidate, in the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Salisbury (UK Parliament constituency)">Salisbury constituency</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Uther_Pendragon" title="Arthur Uther Pendragon">Arthur Uther Pendragon</a>, a local activist and self-declared reincarnation of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>.\n</p><p>Other independent candidates are associated with a political party and may be former members of it, but cannot stand under its label. For instance, after being expelled from the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> but before the <a href="/wiki/RESPECT_The_Unity_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="RESPECT The Unity Coalition">Respect Coalition</a> was founded, British <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP) <a href="/wiki/George_Galloway" title="George Galloway">George Galloway</a> described himself as "Independent Labour".\n</p><p>On 23 March 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Network" title="Independent Network">Independent Network</a> was set up to support independent candidates in the General Election.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Independent Network still supports Independent candidates in local, regional, national and European elections. It has an organic<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> set of principles which are known as the Bell Principles and are very closely related to <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Standards_in_Public_Life" title="Committee on Standards in Public Life">Lord Nolan\'s Standards of Public Life</a>. The Independent Network does not impose any <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a> or political influence on their candidates.\n</p><p>In March 2009, the multi-millionaire <a href="/wiki/Paul_Judge" title="Paul Judge">Paul Judge</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Jury_Team" title="Jury Team">Jury Team</a>, an umbrella organisation dedicated to increasing the number of independent candidates standing in Britain, in both national and European elections.<sup id="cite_ref-Independant8Mar09_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independant8Mar09-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_and_No_Description_candidates">Independent and No Description candidates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Independent and No Description candidates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Part II of the <a href="/wiki/Political_Parties,_Elections_and_Referendums_Act_2000" title="Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act 2000">Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act 2000</a> allows individuals who wish to stand as a candidate to all parliaments and assemblies in the UK, including the House of Commons, the right to use one of three ballot paper descriptions. Those descriptions are the name of a registered political party; the word "independent" or the Welsh equivalent; or no description at all, leaving the ballot paper description box blank.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unless a candidate stands as "independent" or as a "No Description" candidate leaving the ballot paper description box blank, their candidature must be confirmed by a signed certificate from the relevant officer from a registered political party, as set out in Section 52 of the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Administration_Act_2006" title="Electoral Administration Act 2006">Electoral Administration Act 2006</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="House_of_Lords">House of Lords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: House of Lords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Crossbencher" title="Crossbencher">Crossbencher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-affiliated_members_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Non-affiliated members of the House of Lords">Non-affiliated members of the House of Lords</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Lords Spiritual</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> includes a large number of peers independent from political parties. Some are simply <a href="/wiki/Non-affiliated_members_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Non-affiliated members of the House of Lords">not affiliated</a> with any grouping, whilst another, larger, grouping is given the official designation of <a href="/wiki/Crossbench#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Crossbench">crossbenchers</a>. Additionally the <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Lords Spiritual</a> (bishops of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>) do not have party affiliations.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Scottish_Parliament.2C_Welsh_Assembly_and_Northern_Irish_Assembly"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_Parliament,_Welsh_Assembly_and_Northern_Irish_Assembly">Scottish Parliament, Welsh Assembly and Northern Irish Assembly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Scottish Parliament, Welsh Assembly and Northern Irish Assembly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_election,_2003" title="Scottish Parliament election, 2003">2003 Scottish Parliamentary elections</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Members_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Members of the Scottish Parliament">MSPs</a> were elected as Independents: <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Canavan" title="Dennis Canavan">Dennis Canavan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Falkirk_West_(Scottish_Parliament_constituency)" title="Falkirk West (Scottish Parliament constituency)">Falkirk West</a>), <a href="/wiki/Jean_Turner" title="Jean Turner">Dr Jean Turner</a> (<a href="/wiki/Strathkelvin_and_Bearsden_(Scottish_Parliament_constituency)" title="Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Scottish Parliament constituency)">Strathkelvin and Bearsden</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Margo_MacDonald" title="Margo MacDonald">Margo MacDonald</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lothians_(Scottish_Parliament_region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lothians (Scottish Parliament region)">Lothians</a>). The first <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Livingstone" title="Ken Livingstone">Ken Livingstone</a>, was <a href="/wiki/London_mayoral_election,_2000" title="London mayoral election, 2000">first elected</a> as an independent, having run against the official Labour candidate <a href="/wiki/Frank_Dobson" title="Frank Dobson">Frank Dobson</a>. He was subsequently re-admitted to the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> in December 2003 before his first re-election campaign.\n</p><p>Independent candidates frequently stand and are elected to local councils. There is a special Independent group of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Association" title="Local Government Association">Local Government Association</a> to cater for them. A number of local authorities have been entirely or almost entirely composed of independent members, such as the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of and <a href="/wiki/Comhairle_nan_Eilean_Siar" title="Comhairle nan Eilean Siar">Comhairle nan Eilean Siar</a> (Western Isles Council) in the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a>.\n</p><p>Roughly a quarter of the <a href="/wiki/Police_and_crime_commissioner" title="Police and crime commissioner">police and crime commissioners</a> elected in England and Wales in class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> was the only president elected as an independent, as he was Weicker, Jr.">Lowell P. Weicker, Jr.</a> is sometimes mentioned as an independent governor, though this is not technically correct; he ran as an <a href="/wiki/A_Connecticut_Party" title="A Connecticut Party">A Connecticut Party</a> candidate (which gave him better ballot placement than an unaffiliated candidate would receive), defeating the Democratic and Republican party nominees.  Another former governor who is sometimes mentioned as an independent is <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Ventura" title="Jesse Ventura">Jesse Ventura</a>, who actually ran as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Party_of_the_United_States_of_America" title="Reform Party of the United States of America">Reform Party\'s</a> Minnesota affiliate, which later disaffiliated from the party and reverted to their original name the <a href="/wiki/Independence_Party_of_Minnesota" title="Independence Party of Minnesota">Independence Party of Minnesota</a>.\n</p><p>In 1971, State Senator <a href="/wiki/Henry_Howell" title="Henry Howell">Henry Howell</a> of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, a former Democrat, was elected <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Virginia" title="Lieutenant Governor of Virginia">lieutenant governor</a> as an independent. Two years later, he campaigned for <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Virginia" title="Governor of Virginia">governor</a> as an independent, but lost by 15,000 votes.\n</p><p>There were several unsuccessful independent gubernatorial candidates in 2006 who impacted their electoral races. In <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, state legislator <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Merrill" title="Barbara Merrill">Barbara Merrill</a> (formerly a Democrat) received 21% of the vote. In <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> singer and <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">mystery novelist</a> <a href="/wiki/Kinky_Friedman" title="Kinky Friedman">Kinky Friedman</a> received 12.43% of the vote, and State <a href="/wiki/Comptroller" title="Comptroller">Comptroller</a> <a href="/wiki/Carole_Keeton_Strayhorn" title="Carole Keeton Strayhorn">Carole Keeton Strayhorn</a> received 18.13%. Strayhorn and Friedman\'s presence in the race resulted in a splitting of the ballot four ways between themselves and the two major parties.\n</p><p>In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> governor <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Crist" title="Charlie Crist">Charlie Crist</a> left the Republican party and became Independent (He later became a Democrat.)<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> rather than face former <a href="/wiki/Florida_House_of_Representatives" title="Florida House of Representatives">state house</a> <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_Florida_House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Speaker of the Florida House of Representatives">Speaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Marco_Rubio" title="Marco Rubio">Marco Rubio</a> in the Republican primary (Rubio won, though Crist came in ahead of Democratic nominee <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Meek" title="Kendrick Meek">Kendrick Meek</a>).\n</p><p>In 2014, former <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a> mayor <a href="/wiki/Mufi_Hannemann" title="Mufi Hannemann">Mufi Hannemann</a> ran as an independent candidate for the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Hawaii" title="Governor of Hawaii">governorship</a> of the State of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> after previously campaigning in the state\'s Democratic primary. As a result, Democratic candidate <a href="/wiki/David_Ige" title="David Ige">David Ige</a> was elected as governor with a plurality of 49%.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Congress_.E2.80.93_House_of_Representatives_and_Senate"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Congress_\xe2\x80\x93_House_of_Representatives_and_Senate">Congress \xe2\x80\x93 House of Representatives and Senate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Congress \xe2\x80\x93 House of Representatives and Senate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There have been several independents elected to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> throughout history. Notable examples include <a href="/wiki/David_Davis_(Supreme_Court_justice)" title="David Davis (Supreme Court justice)">David Davis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> (a former <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a>) in the 19th century, and <a href="/wiki/Harry_F._Byrd,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Harry F. Byrd, Jr.">Harry F. Byrd, Jr.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> (who had been elected to his first term as a Democrat) in the 20th century.  Some officials have been elected as members of a party but became independent while in office (without being elected as such), such as <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Morse" title="Wayne Morse">Wayne Morse</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> senator <a href="/wiki/George_W._Norris" title="George W. Norris">George W. Norris</a> was elected for four terms as a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> before changing to an independent after the Republicans lost their majority in Congress in 1930. Norris won re-election as an independent in 1936, but later lost his final re-election attempt to Republican <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_S._Wherry" title="Kenneth S. Wherry">Kenneth S. Wherry</a> in 1942. <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jeffords" title="Jim Jeffords">Jim Jeffords</a> left the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> to become an independent in 2001. Jeffords\'s <a href="/wiki/Party_switching" title="Party switching">change of party status</a> was especially significant because it shifted the Senate composition from 50-50 between the Republicans and <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> (with a Republican <a href="/wiki/Vice_President" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice President">Vice President</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a>, who would presumably break all ties in favor of the Republicans), to 49 Republicans, 50 Democrats, and one Independent. Jeffords agreed to vote for Democratic control of the Senate in exchange for being appointed chairman of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate_Committee_on_Environment_and_Public_Works" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works">Senate Environment and Public Works Committee</a>, and the Democrats held control of the Senate until the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate_election,_2002" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate election, 2002">Congressional elections in 2002</a>, when the Republicans regained their majority. Jeffords retired at the end of his term in 2007. Wayne Morse after two years as an independent became a Democrat. <a href="/wiki/Dean_Barkley" title="Dean Barkley">Dean Barkley</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Independence_Party_of_Minnesota" title="Independence Party of Minnesota">Independence Party of Minnesota</a> was appointed a day before the 2002 elections to fill the senate seat of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wellstone" title="Paul Wellstone">Paul Wellstone</a> who, while running for re-election, died weeks prior. Barkley refused to caucus with either party.\n</p><p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> is the longest serving independent member of Congress in American history.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> He was an independent member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> for <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives,_Vermont_At_Large" class="mw-redirect" title="United States House of Representatives, Vermont At Large">Vermont-at-large</a> from 1991 to 2007. Sanders later won the open Senate seat of <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jeffords" title="Jim Jeffords">Jim Jeffords</a> as an independent. <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lieberman" title="Joe Lieberman">Joe Lieberman</a> is a former <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> who, like Lowell P. Weicker, Jr., ran under a third party (<a href="/wiki/Connecticut_for_Lieberman" title="Connecticut for Lieberman">Connecticut for Lieberman Party</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_United_States_Senate_election,_2006" class="mw-redirect" title="Connecticut United States Senate election, 2006">2006 election</a>. Though both representatives are technically independent politicians, they often caucus with the Democrats. In 2006, Sanders and Lieberman were the only two victorious independent candidates for Congress. In 2012 <a href="/wiki/Angus_King" title="Angus King">Angus King</a> was elected to the US Senate as an Independent from Maine.  As of 2016, he has typically caucused with the Democrats.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> has also seen a handful of <a href="/wiki/Third-party_members_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Third-party members of the United States House of Representatives">independent members</a>. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virgil_Goode" title="Virgil Goode">Virgil Goode</a> of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frazier_Reams" title="Frazier Reams">Frazier Reams</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Victor_Berger" class="mw-redirect" title="Victor Berger">Victor Berger</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_and_local_offices">State and local offices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_politician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: State and local offices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In August 2008, there were 12 independents who held offices in state legislatures. There were four state senators, one from <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Senate" title="Kentucky Senate">Kentucky</a>, one from <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="Oregon Senate">Oregon</a>, one from <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Senate" title="Tennessee Senate">Tennessee</a>, and one from <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico_Senate" title="New Mexico Senate">New Mexico</a>. The representatives came from the states of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_House_of_Representatives" title="Louisiana House of Representatives">Louisiana</a> (two), <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine</a> (two), <a href="/wiki/Vermont_House_of_Representatives" title="Vermont House of Representatives">Vermont</a> (two), and <a href="/wiki/Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="Virginia House of Delegates">Virginia</a> (two). In the 2008 general elections, Wisconsin State Assemblyman Jeffrey Wood left the Republican Party and won reelection as an independent. After the 2008 primary election, New Mexico State Senator <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Carraro" title="Joseph Carraro">Joseph Carraro</a> left the Republican Party and registered as an Independent. He did not run for reelection.\n</p><p>In November 2005, <a href="/wiki/Manny_Diaz_(Florida_politician)" title="Manny Diaz (Florida politician)">Manny Diaz</a> was elected Mayor of <a href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a> as an independent.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On June 19, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_New_York_City" title="Mayor of New York City">New York Mayor</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a> switched his party affiliation from Republican to independent. <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Goodman" title="Oscar Goodman">Oscar Goodman</a>, Mayor of <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" class="mw-redirect" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas, Nevada</a> switched his affiliation to Independent from Democrat in December 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dan_Hollingsworth" title="Dan Hollingsworth">Dan Hollingsworth</a> has won four consecutive elections as an Independent since 1998 for mayor of the small city of <a href="/wiki/Ruston,_Louisiana" title="Ruston, Louisiana">Ruston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, the home of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Tech_University" title="Louisiana Tech University">Louisiana Tech University</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nebraska_Legislature" title="Nebraska Legislature">Nebraska Legislature</a> is unique in that it is the only nonpartisan state legislature. In the Legislature (which is additionally unique in that it is also the only state legislature that is <a href="/wiki/Unicameral" class="mw-redirect" title="Unicameral">unicameral</a>), there are no formal party alignments or groups and the members are nominated in nonpartisan primary elections. Members are allowed to register with political parties but choose not to reveal their affiliation while seated, as a professional courtesy. However, the political affiliation of party-affiliated members are considered <a href="/wiki/Open_secret" title="Open secret">open secrets</a> and the parties exist in the legislature on an unofficial basis. Some members, such as <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Chambers" title="Ernie Chambers">Ernie Chambers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Omaha" class="mw-redirect" title="Omaha">Omaha</a>, are independent of party officially, while others have not publicly disclosed their affiliation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
