href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>, a nonphysical <a href="/wiki/Juridical_person" title="Juridical person">juridical entity</a> that is represented by one <a href="/wiki/Centralized_government" title="Centralized government">centralized government</a> that has <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> over a geographic area. International law defines sovereign states as having a permanent population,  defined territory, one <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, and the capacity to enter into <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">relations with other sovereign states</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is also normally understood that a sovereign state is neither dependent on nor subjected to any other power or <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While according to the declarative theory of statehood, a sovereign state can exist without being <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_recognition" title="Diplomatic recognition">recognised by other sovereign states</a>, unrecognised states will often find it hard to exercise full treaty-making powers and engage in <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomatic relations</a> with other sovereign states.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Westphalian sovereignty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Westphalian_sovereignty" title="Westphalian sovereignty">Westphalian sovereignty</a></div>\n<p>Westphalian sovereignty is the concept of <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation-state</a> sovereignty based on territoriality and the absence of a role for external agents in domestic structures. It is an international system of states, <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporations</a>, and organizations that began with the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> in 1648.\n</p><p>Sovereignty is a term that is frequently misused.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Up until the 19th century, the radicalised concept of a "standard of civilization" was routinely deployed to determine that certain peoples in the world were "uncivilised", and lacking organised societies. That position was reflected and constituted in the notion that their "sovereignty" was either completely lacking, or at least of an inferior character when compared to that of "civilised" people."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lassa_Oppenheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Lassa Oppenheim">Lassa Oppenheim</a> said, "There exists perhaps no conception the meaning of which is more controversial than that of sovereignty. It is an indisputable fact that this conception, from the moment when it was introduced into political science until the present day, has never had a meaning which was universally agreed upon."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the opinion of <a href="/wiki/H._V._Evatt" title="H. V. Evatt">H. V. Evatt</a> of the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court of Australia</a>, "sovereignty is neither a question of fact, nor a question of law, but a question that does not arise at all."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sovereignty has taken on a different meaning with the development of the principle of <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a> and the prohibition against the threat or use of force as <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_cogens" class="mw-redirect" title="Jus cogens">jus cogens</a></i> norms of modern <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">United Nations Charter</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Draft_Declaration_on_Rights_and_Duties_of_States&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Draft Declaration on Rights and Duties of States (page does not exist)">Draft Declaration on Rights and Duties of States</a>, and the charters of regional international organizations express the view that all states are juridically equal and enjoy the same rights and duties based upon the mere fact of their existence as persons under international law.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The right of nations to determine their own political status and exercise permanent sovereignty within the limits of their territorial jurisdictions is widely recognized.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In political science, sovereignty is usually defined as the most essential attribute of the state in the form of its complete self-sufficiency in the frames of a certain territory, that is its supremacy in the domestic policy and independence in the foreign one.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Named after the 1648 Treaty of Westphalia, the Westphalian System of state sovereignty, which according to Bryan Turner is "made a more or less clear separation between religion and state, and recognised the right of princes \'to confessionalise\' the state, that is, to determine the religious affiliation of their kingdoms on the pragmatic principle of cuius regio eius religio &#91;<a href="/wiki/Whose_realm,_his_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Whose realm, his religion">whose realm, his religion</a>&#93;."<sup id="cite_ref-MariahSutt_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MariahSutt-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Westphalian model of state sovereignty has increasingly come under fire from the "non-west" as a system imposed solely by Western Colonialism. What this model did was make religion a subordinate to politics,<sup id="cite_ref-MariahSutt_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MariahSutt-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> a problem that has caused some issues in the Islamic world. This system does not fit in the Islamic world because concepts such as "separation of church and state" are not recognised in the Islamic religion as valid.\n</p><p>In casual usage, the terms "<a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a>", "nation", and "state" are often used as if they were <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonymous</a>; but in stricter usage they can be distinguished:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">Country</a></i> denotes a region of land defined by geographical features or political boundaries.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">Nation</a></i> denotes a people who are believed to or deemed to share common customs, religion, language, origins, ancestry or history. However, the adjectives <i>national</i> and <i>international</i> are frequently used to refer to matters pertaining to what are strictly <i>sovereign states</i>, as in <i>national capital</i>, <i>international law</i>.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">State</a></i> refers to the set of governing and supportive institutions that have <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> over a definite territory and <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>. Sovereign states are <a href="/wiki/Legal_personality" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal personality">legal persons</a>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>State recognition signifies the decision of a sovereign state to treat another entity as also being a sovereign state.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Recognition can be either expressed or implied and is usually retroactive in its effects. It does not necessarily signify a desire to establish or maintain diplomatic relations.\n</p><p>There is no definition that is binding on all the members of the community of nations on the criteria for statehood. In actual practice, the criteria are mainly political, not legal.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> L.C. Green cited the recognition of the unborn <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovak</a> states in World War I and explained that "since recognition of statehood is a matter of discretion, it is open to any existing State to accept as a state any entity it wishes, regardless of the existence of territory or of an established government."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>, however, there are several theories of when a state should be recognised as sovereign.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutive_theory">Constitutive theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Constitutive theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <b>constitutive theory of statehood</b> defines a state as a person of international law if, and only if, it is recognised as sovereign by other states. This theory of recognition was developed in the 19th century. Under it, a state was sovereign if another sovereign state recognised it as such. Because of this, new states could not immediately become part of the international community or be bound by international law, and recognised nations did not have to respect international law in their dealings with them.<sup id="cite_ref-ctos_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctos-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1815, at the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> the <a href="/wiki/Final_Act_of_the_Congress_of_Vienna" class="mw-redirect" title="Final Act of the Congress of Vienna">Final Act</a> recognised only 39 sovereign states in the European diplomatic system, and as a result it was firmly established that in the future new states would have to be recognised by other states, and that meant in practice recognition by one or more of the <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great powers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the major criticisms of this law is the confusion caused when some states recognise a new entity, but other states do not. Hersch Lauterpacht, one of the theory\'s main proponents, suggested that it is a state\'s duty to grant recognition as a possible solution. However, a state may use any criteria when judging if they should give recognition and they have no obligation to use such criteria. Many states may only recognise another state if it is to their advantage.<sup id="cite_ref-ctos_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctos-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1912, <a href="/wiki/L._F._L._Oppenheim" title="L. F. L. Oppenheim">L. F. L. Oppenheim</a> said the following, regarding constitutive theory:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>International Law does not say that a State is not in existence as long as it isn\'t recognised, but it takes no notice of it before its recognition. Through recognition only and exclusively a State becomes an International Person and a subject of International Law.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Declarative_theory">Declarative theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Declarative theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Montevideo_Convention" title="Montevideo Convention">Montevideo Convention</a></div>\n<p>By contrast, the <b>declarative theory of statehood</b> defines a state as a <a href="/wiki/Public_international_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Public international law">person in international law</a> if it meets the following criteria: 1) a defined territory; 2) a permanent population; 3) a government and 4) a capacity to enter into relations with other states. According to declarative theory, an entity\'s statehood is independent of its recognition by other states, as long as the sovereignty was not gained by military force. The declarative model was most famously expressed in the 1933 <a href="/wiki/Montevideo_Convention" title="Montevideo Convention">Montevideo Convention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Article 3 of the Montevideo Convention declares that political statehood is independent of recognition by other states, and the state is not prohibited from defending itself.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, recognition is considered a requirement for statehood by the constitutive theory of statehood. An important part of the convention was Article 11 that prohibits using military force to gain sovereignty.\n</p><p>A similar opinion about "the conditions on which an entity constitutes a state" is expressed by the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> <i>Opinions of the <a href="/wiki/Badinter_Arbitration_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Badinter Arbitration Committee">Badinter Arbitration Committee</a></i>, which found that a state was defined by having a territory, a population, government, and capacity to enter into relations with other states.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_recognition">State recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: State recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition" title="List of states with limited recognition">List of states with limited recognition</a></div>\n<p>State practice relating to the recognition of states typically falls somewhere between the declaratory and constitutive approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> International law does not require a state to recognise other states.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Recognition is often withheld when a new state is seen as illegitimate or has come about in breach of international law. Almost universal non-recognition by the international community of <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Cyprus" title="Northern Cyprus">Northern Cyprus</a> are good examples of this, the former only having been recognized by <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and the latter only recognized by <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. In the case of Rhodesia, recognition was widely withheld when the white minority <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia%27s_Unilateral_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Rhodesia&#39;s Unilateral Declaration of Independence">seized power</a> and attempted to form a state along the lines of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa_under_apartheid" class="mw-redirect" title="South Africa under apartheid">Apartheid South Africa</a>, a move that the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> described as the creation of an "illegal racist minority r\xc3\xa9gime".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In the case of Northern Cyprus, recognition was withheld from a state created in Northern Cyprus.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> International law contains no prohibition on declarations of independence,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and the recognition of a country is a political issue.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> As a result, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Cypriots" title="Turkish Cypriots">Turkish Cypriots</a> gained "observer status" in the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe</a>, and their representatives are elected in the Assembly of Northern Cyprus;<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and Northern Cyprus became an observer member of the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation" title="Organisation of Islamic Cooperation">Organisation of Islamic Cooperation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Economic_Cooperation_Organization" title="Economic Cooperation Organization">Economic Cooperation Organization</a>.<br />\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="De_facto_and_de_jure_states"><i>De facto</i> and <i>de jure</i> states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: De facto and de jure states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most sovereign states are states <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> (i.e., they exist both in law and in reality). However, a state may be recognised only as a <i>de jure</i> state, in that it is recognised as being the legitimate government of a territory over which it has no actual control. For example, during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_in_exile" title="Government in exile">governments-in-exile</a> of a number of continental European states continued to enjoy diplomatic relations with the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a>, notwithstanding that their countries were under Nazi occupation. The <a href="/wiki/PLO" class="mw-redirect" title="PLO">PLO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Palestinian Authority">Palestinian Authority</a> claim that the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">State of Palestine</a> is a sovereign state, a claim which has been <a href="/wiki/International_recognition_of_the_State_of_Palestine" title="International recognition of the State of Palestine">recognised by most states</a>, though the territory it claims is under the <i>de facto</i> and "<a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a>" control of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-saeb_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saeb-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-israel_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-israel-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  \nOther entities may have <i>de facto</i> control over a territory but lack international recognition; these may be considered by the <a href="/wiki/International_community" title="International community">international community</a> to be only <i>de facto</i> states. They are considered <i>de jure</i> states only according to their own law and by states that recognise them. For example, <a href="/wiki/Somaliland" title="Somaliland">Somaliland</a> is commonly considered to be such a state.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> For a list of entities that wish to be universally recognised as sovereign states, but do not have complete worldwide <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_recognition" title="Diplomatic recognition">diplomatic recognition</a>, see the <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition" title="List of states with limited recognition">list of states with limited recognition</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_between_state_and_government">Relationship between state and government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship between state and government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although the terms "state" and "government" are often used interchangeably,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> international law distinguishes between a non-physical state and its government; and in fact, the concept of "<a href="/wiki/Government-in-exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Government-in-exile">government-in-exile</a>" is predicated upon that distinction.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford,_J._2006_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford,_J._2006-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> States are non-physical juridical entities, and not organisations of any kind.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> However, ordinarily, only the government of a state can obligate or bind the state, for example by treaty.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford,_J._2006_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford,_J._2006-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State_extinction">State extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: State extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Generally speaking, states are durable entities, though it is possible for them to become extinguished, either through voluntary means or outside forces, such as military conquest. Violent state abolition has virtually ceased since the end of World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Because states are non-physical juridical entities, it has been argued their extinction cannot be due to physical force alone.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Instead, the physical actions of the military must be associated with the correct social or judiciary actions in order to abolish a state.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontological_status_of_the_state">Ontological status of the state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ontological status of the state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontological</a> status of the state has been the subject of debate,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> specially, whether or not the state, being an object that no one can see, taste, touch, or otherwise detect,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> actually exists.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="The_state_as_.22quasi-abstract.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_state_as_&quot;quasi-abstract&quot;">The state as "quasi-abstract"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The state as &quot;quasi-abstract&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>It has been argued that one potential reason as to why the existence of states has been controversial is because states do not have a place in the traditional Platonist duality of the concrete and the abstract.<sup id="cite_ref-dl.dropboxusercontent.com_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dl.dropboxusercontent.com-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Characteristically, concrete objects are those that have position in time and space, which states do not have (though their territories have spatial position, but states are distinct from their territories), and abstract objects have position in neither time nor space, which does not fit the supposed characteristics of states either, since states do have temporal position (they can be created at certain times and then become extinct at a future time). Therefore, it has been argued that states belong to a third category, the quasi-abstract, that has recently begun to garner philosophical attention, especially in the area of <a href="/wiki/Documentality" title="Documentality">documentality</a>, an ontological theory that seeks to understand the role of documents in understanding all of social reality. Quasi-abstract objects, such as states, can be brought into being through document acts, and can also be used to manipulate them, such as by binding them by treaty or surrendering them as the result of a war.<sup id="cite_ref-dl.dropboxusercontent.com_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dl.dropboxusercontent.com-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scholars in international relations can be broken up into two different practices, realists and pluralists, of what they believe the ontological state of the state is. Realists believe that the world is one of only states and interstate relations and the identity of the state is defined before any international relations with other states. On the other hand, pluralists believe that the state is not the only actor in international relations and interactions between states and the state is competing against many other actors.<sup id="cite_ref-MariahSutt2_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MariahSutt2-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="The_state_as_.22spiritual_entity.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_state_as_&quot;spiritual_entity&quot;">The state as "spiritual entity"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The state as &quot;spiritual entity&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another theory of the ontology of the state is that the state is a spiritual,<sup id="cite_ref-Fundamentals71_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fundamentals71-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> or "mystical entity"<sup id="cite_ref-Fundamentals71_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fundamentals71-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> with its own being, distinct from the members of the state.<sup id="cite_ref-Fundamentals71_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fundamentals71-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The German <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">Idealist</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Georg_Hegel" class="mw-redirect" title="Georg Hegel">Georg Hegel</a> (1770\xe2\x80\x931831) was perhaps the greatest proponent of this theory.<sup id="cite_ref-Fundamentals71_60-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fundamentals71-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The Hegelian definition of the state is "the Divine Idea as it exists on Earth".<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends_in_the_number_of_states">Trends in the number of states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereign_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Trends in the number of states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since the end of World War II, the number of sovereign states in the international system has surged.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Some research suggests that the existence of international and regional organisations, the greater availability of economic aid, and greater acceptance of the norm of self-determination have increased the desire of political units to secede and can be credited for the increase in the number of states in the international system.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Harvard economist <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Alesina" title="Alberto Alesina">Alberto Alesina</a> and Tufts economist Enrico Spolaore argue in their book, <i>Size of Nations,</i> that the increase in the number of states can partly be credited to a more peaceful world, greater free trade and international economic integration, democratisation, and the presence of international organisations that co-ordinate economic and political policies.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
