The name <i>Hibernia</i> was taken from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> geographical accounts. During his exploration of <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Northwest Europe">northwest Europe</a> (c. 320 BC), <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas of Massilia</a> called the island <i>I\xc3\xa9rn\xc4\x93</i> (written <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\xb8\xce\xad\xcf\x81\xce\xbd\xce\xb7</span>). In his book <i>Geographia</i> (c. 150 AD), <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemaeus</a> ("Ptolemy") called the island <i>Iouern\xc3\xad\xc4\x81</i> (written <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\xb8\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\xb5\xcf\x81\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1</span>, where "\xce\xbf\xcf\x85"/<i>ou</i> stands for <i>w</i>). The Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>, in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Agricola_(book)" title="Agricola (book)">Agricola</a></i> (c. 98 AD), uses the name Hibernia. \n</p><p><span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\xb8\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\xb5\xcf\x81\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1</span> <i>Iouern\xc3\xad\xc4\x81</i> was a Greek rendering of the <a href="/wiki/P-Celtic_and_Q-Celtic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="P-Celtic and Q-Celtic languages">Q-Celtic</a> name *<i>\xc4\xaaweri\xc5\xab</i>, from which eventually arose the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> names <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89riu" title="\xc3\x89riu">\xc3\x89riu</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89ire" title="\xc3\x89ire">\xc3\x89ire</a></i>. The name was altered in Latin (influenced by the word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hibernus#Latin" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:hibernus">hibernus</a></i>) as though it meant "land of winter".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Roman_usage">Post-Roman usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hibernia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Post-Roman usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The High King <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru" title="Brian Boru">Brian Boru</a> (c. 941\xe2\x80\x931014) based his title on being <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru#Emperor_of_the_Irish" title="Brian Boru">emperor of the Scots people</a>, which was in Latin <i>Imperator Scottorum</i>, as distinct from claiming to be Emperor of the island of Ireland. From 1172 <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Ireland" title="Lordship of Ireland">Lordship of Ireland</a> gave the King of England the additional title <i>Dominus Hibernie</i> (<i>sic</i>, for <i>Hiberniae</i>; also <i>Dominus Hybernie</i>), \'Lord of Ireland\'. The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> created the title <i>Rex Hiberniae</i>, King of Ireland, for use in Latin texts. Gerard Mercator called Ireland "Hybernia" on his world map of 1541.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1642 the motto of the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Ireland" title="Confederate Ireland">Irish Confederates</a>, a Catholic-landlord administration that ruled much of Ireland until 1650 was <i>Pro Deo, Rege et Patria, Hibernia Unanimis</i> (English: \'For God, King and Fatherland, Ireland is United\').\n</p><p>However, unlike many Roman geographical names, the Latin <i>Hibernia</i> did not become the basis for the name for Ireland in any modern languages, with even Italian using <i>Irlanda</i>.  Apart from the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a> all modern languages use a local variant of the English "Ireland". This is presumably because direct medieval contacts between Ireland and continental Europe were at too low a level to embed use of the Hibernian root, or the original Irish \xc3\x89ire, in local <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernaculars</a>.  \n</p><p>By the classicising 18th century the use of Hibernia had revived in some contexts, just as had the use of <a href="/wiki/Caledonia" title="Caledonia">Caledonia</a>, one of the Latin terms for <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a> for Britain.  "Hibernia" was used on Irish coins and companies such as the <a href="/wiki/Hibernian_Insurance_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Hibernian Insurance Company">Hibernian Insurance Company</a> were established (later renamed the Hibernian Group). The name took on popularity with the success of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Patriot_Party" title="Irish Patriot Party">Irish Patriot Party</a>. At a time when <a href="/wiki/Palladian_architecture" title="Palladian architecture">Palladian classical architecture and design</a> were being adopted in northern Europe, Hibernia was a useful word to describe Ireland with overtones of classical style and civility, including by the prosperous <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Anglo-Irish Ascendancy</a> who were taught Latin at school. "Hibernian" was used as a term for people, and a general adjective. The <a href="/wiki/City_Hall,_Dublin" title="City Hall, Dublin">Royal Exchange</a> in Dublin was built in 1769\xe2\x80\x9379 with the carved inscription "SPQH" for <i>Senatus PopulusQue Hibernicus</i> \'the senate and people of Ireland\'.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Hibernian_Academy" title="Royal Hibernian Academy">Royal Hibernian Academy</a> dates from 1823.\n</p><p>The 18th Century Spanish Regiment composed of Irish exiles was known as the <a href="/wiki/Regiment_of_Hibernia" title="Regiment of Hibernia">Regiment of Hibernia</a>.\n</p><p>Hibernia is a word that is rarely used today with regard to Ireland, except in long-established names.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is occasionally used for names of organisations and various other things; for instance: <a href="/wiki/Hibernia_National_Bank" title="Hibernia National Bank">Hibernia National Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hibernian_Insurance_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Hibernian Insurance Group">Hibernian Insurance Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Order_of_Hibernians" title="Ancient Order of Hibernians">Ancient Order of Hibernians</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hibernian" title="The Hibernian">The Hibernian</a></i> magazine, <a href="/wiki/Hibernia_College" title="Hibernia College">Hibernia College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hibernian_Orchestra" title="Hibernian Orchestra">Hibernian Orchestra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hibernian_F.C." title="Hibernian F.C.">Hibernian Football Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Hibernia" title="HMS Hibernia">HMS&#160;<i>Hibernia</i></a>, and modern derivatives, from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> like <i>Respublica Hibernica</i> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_Republic" title="Irish Republic">Irish Republic</a>) and <i>Universitas Hiberniae Nationalis</i> (<a href="/wiki/National_University_of_Ireland" title="National University of Ireland">National University of Ireland</a>).  In Canada, Hibernia lends its name to the <a href="/wiki/Hibernia_oil_field" title="Hibernia oil field">Hibernia oil field</a> off <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, and to a large sea oil platform there, the <a href="/wiki/Hibernia_Gravity_Base_Structure" title="Hibernia Gravity Base Structure"><i>Hibernia</i></a>.\n</p><p>Another occurrence is in <a href="/wiki/TNF_receptor_associated_periodic_syndrome" title="TNF receptor associated periodic syndrome">familial Hibernian fever or TRAPS</a> (tumor necrosis factor receptor-associated periodic syndrome), a periodic fever first described in 1982 in a family of Irish and Scottish descent, but found in all ethnic groups.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The compound form \'Hiberno-\' remains more common, as \'<a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Norse" class="mw-redirect" title="Hiberno-Norse">Hiberno-Norse</a>\', \'<a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</a>\', \'Hiberno-Scottish\', \'<a href="/wiki/Hibernophile" title="Hibernophile">Hibernophile</a>\', etc.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> football club <a href="/wiki/Hibernian_FC" class="mw-redirect" title="Hibernian FC">Hibernian FC</a> has adopted the name.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
