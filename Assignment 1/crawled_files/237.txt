title="Italy">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerist</a> <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_School_(art)" href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and rivalling <a href="/wiki/Giambologna" title="Giambologna">Giambologna</a> as the foremost sculptors of the late 16th century in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Vittoria was born in the Italian city of Trent and was the son of a tailor. Vittoria was trained in the atelier of the architect-sculptor <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Sansovino" title="Jacopo Sansovino">Jacopo Sansovino</a>; he was a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> whose influence can be detected in his compositions. He was a virtuoso in terracotta, often presented with gilded surfaces, marble and bronze. Like all Italian sculptors of his generation, Vittoria was influenced also by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> and by the Florentine Mannerist, <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeo_Ammanati" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartolomeo Ammanati">Bartolomeo Ammanati</a>. The closeness of his associations in projects by architects <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Sansovino" title="Jacopo Sansovino">Sansovino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michele_Sanmicheli" title="Michele Sanmicheli">Sanmicheli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Palladio</a>, working with painters <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Veronese" title="Paolo Veronese">Veronese</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> placed him squarely among the protagonists of the Cinquecento art world in late 16th-century Venice.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span data-file-width="1342" data-file-height="1800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alessandro_Vittoria_-_Bust_of_Marino_Grimani_-_Walters_27225.jpg" href="/wiki/Walters_Art_Museum" title="Walters Art Museum">Walters Art Museum</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>Vittoria was first trained in his native city, <a href="/wiki/Trento" title="Trento">Trento</a>, then moved to <a href="/wiki/Venice,_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="Venice, Italy">Venice</a> in 1543, where he trained and worked with <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Sansovino" title="Jacopo Sansovino">Jacopo Sansovino</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His long artistic relationship with Sansovino was a stormy one. After one quarrel with Sansovino, he removed from Venice and worked in <a href="/wiki/Vicenza" title="Vicenza">Vicenza</a>, where he collaborated with Veronese on the decorations of the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Barbaro" title="Villa Barbaro">Villa Barbaro at Maser</a> (1560\xe2\x80\x9362) before returning. The two masters worked jointly on great sculptural commissions until Sansovino\'s death. Vittoria took up his studio and completed Sansovino\'s unfinished commissions. His pupils included <a href="/wiki/Camillo_Mariani" title="Camillo Mariani">Camillo Mariani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrea_di_Alessandro" title="Andrea di Alessandro">Andrea di Alessandro</a>.\n</p><p>He died at Venice in 1608. His tomb, with his self-portrait bust, is in the church of <a href="/wiki/San_Zaccaria" class="mw-redirect" title="San Zaccaria">San Zaccaria</a>.\n</p><p>Vittoria is known for his classicising portrait busts, a genre that scarcely existed in Venice before him,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and for medals as well as for his full-length figures, some of which surmount Sansovino\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Biblioteca_Marciana" title="Biblioteca Marciana">Biblioteca Marciana</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Vittoria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leithe-Jasper; his patrons, by Thomas Martin; his connections with Venetian painting, by Stefano Tumidei; and Vittoria\'s role as a collector, by Victoria Avery.\n</p>\n<div
