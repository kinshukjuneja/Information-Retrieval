times formed part of an area called <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a>. They gave their name to the modern city of <a href="/wiki/Vannes" title="Vannes">Vannes</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">Bibliography</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veneti_(Gaul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Other ancient <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> peoples historically attested in <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a> include the <a href="/wiki/Redones" class="mw-redirect" title="Redones">Redones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Curiosolitae" title="Curiosolitae">Curiosolitae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osismii" title="Osismii">Osismii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esubii" class="mw-redirect" title="Esubii">Esubii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namnetes" title="Namnetes">Namnetes</a>.\n</p><p>The Veneti inhabited southern <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a>, along the <a href="/wiki/Morbihan" title="Morbihan">Morbihan</a> bay. They built their strongholds on coastal eminences, which were islands when the tide was in, and peninsulas when the tide was out. Their most notable city, and probably their capital, was Darioritum (now known as Gwened in Breton or <a href="/wiki/Vannes" title="Vannes">Vannes</a> in French), mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <i>Geography</i>.\n</p><p>The Veneti built their ships of oak with large transoms fixed by iron nails of a thumb\'s thickness. They navigated and powered their ships through the use of leather sails. This made their ships strong, sturdy and structurally sound, capable of withstanding the harsh conditions of the Atlantic.\n</p><p>Judging by Caesar\'s <i>Bello Gallico</i> the Veneti evidently had close relations with <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Age Britain">Iron Age Britain</a>; he describes how the Veneti sail to Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They controlled the tin trade from <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon" title="Mining in Cornwall and Devon">mining in Cornwall and Devon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawkins_1811_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawkins_1811-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Caesar mentioned that they summoned military assistance from that island during the war of 56 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_coming_of_Caesar">The coming of Caesar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veneti_(Gaul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The coming of Caesar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>\'s victories in the <a href="/wiki/Gallic_Wars" title="Gallic Wars">Gallic Wars</a>, completed by 51&#160;BCE, extended Rome\'s territory to the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. Caesar became the first Roman general to cross both bodies of water when he built a <a href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_Rhine_bridges" title="Caesar&#39;s Rhine bridges">bridge across the Rhine</a> and conducted the first <a href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_invasions_of_Britain" title="Caesar&#39;s invasions of Britain">invasion of Britain</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Caesar" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesar">Caesar</a> reports in the <a href="/wiki/Bellum_Gallicum" class="mw-redirect" title="Bellum Gallicum">Bellum Gallicum</a> that in 57 BCE, the Gauls on the Atlantic coast, including the Veneti, were forced to submit to Caesar\'s authority as governor. They were obliged to sign treaties and yield hostages as a token of good faith. However, in 56 BCE, the Veneti captured some of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>\'s officers while they were foraging within their legions, intent on using them as bargaining chips to secure the release of the hostages Caesar had forced them to give him. Angered by what he considered a breach of law, Caesar prepared for war.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png/300px-Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png" width="300" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png/450px-Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png/600px-Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png 2x" data-file-width="1842" data-file-height="1125" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bataille_Morbihan_-56.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Battle of Morbihan</div></div></div>\n<p>Given the highly defensible nature of the Veneti strongholds, land attacks were frustrated by the incoming tide, and naval forces were left trapped on the rocks when the tide ebbed. Despite this, Caesar managed to engineer <a href="/wiki/Mole_(architecture)" title="Mole (architecture)">moles</a> and raised siegeworks that provided his legions with a base of operations. However, once the Veneti were threatened in one stronghold, they used their fleet to evacuate to another stronghold, obliging the Romans to repeat the same engineering feat elsewhere.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_Morbihan">Battle of Morbihan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veneti_(Gaul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Battle of Morbihan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since the destruction of the enemy fleet was the only permanent way to end this problem, Caesar directed his men to build ships. However, his <a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">galleys</a> were at a serious disadvantage compared to the far thicker Veneti ships. The thickness of their ships meant they were resistant to ramming, whilst their greater height meant they could shower the Roman ships with projectiles, and even command the wooden turrets which Caesar had added to his bulwarks. The Veneti manoeuvred so skilfully under sail that boarding was impossible. These factors, coupled with their intimate knowledge of the coast and tides, put the Romans at a disadvantage.\nHowever, these advantages would not stand in the face of Roman perseverance and ingenuity. Caesar\'s <a href="/wiki/Legatus" title="Legatus">legate</a> <a href="/wiki/Decimus_Junius_Brutus_Albinus" title="Decimus Junius Brutus Albinus">Decimus Junius Brutus Albinus</a> was given command of the Roman fleet, and in a decisive battle, succeeded in destroying the Gaulish fleet in <a href="/wiki/Quiberon_Bay" title="Quiberon Bay">Quiberon Bay</a>, with Caesar watching from the shore. Using long billhooks, the Romans struck at the enemy\'s <a href="/wiki/Halyard" title="Halyard">halyards</a> as they swept past (these must have been fastened out-board), having the effect of dropping the huge leathern mainsails to the deck, which hopelessly crippled the vessel whether for sailing or rowing. The Romans were at last able to board, and the whole Veneti fleet fell into their hands.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_end_of_the_Veneti">The end of the Veneti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veneti_(Gaul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The end of the Veneti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The strongholds on the coast were now stormed and the nobles were slaughtered and the rest sold into slavery. This served as a lesson to the rest of the confederacy of the fate in store for those who dared to stand against Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
