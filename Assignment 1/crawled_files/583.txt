<a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta</a>-shaped kite are not rigid</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>fixed-wing aircraft</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">flying machine</a>, such as an <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplane or aeroplane</a> (see <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences" title="American and British English spelling differences">spelling differences</a>), which is capable of <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> using <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> that generate <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> caused by the aircraft\'s forward <a href="/wiki/Airspeed" title="Airspeed">airspeed</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration" title="Wing configuration">shape of the wings</a>. Fixed-wing aircraft are distinct from <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotary-wing aircraft</a> (in which the wings form a <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_rotor" title="Helicopter rotor">rotor</a> mounted on a spinning shaft or "mast"), and <a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopters</a> (in which the wings <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight#Flapping_flight" title="Bird flight">flap</a> in a manner similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>). The wings of a fixed-wing aircraft are not necessarily rigid; kites, <a href="/wiki/Hang_glider" class="mw-redirect" title="Hang glider">hang gliders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Variable-sweep_wing" title="Variable-sweep wing">variable-sweep wing</a> aircraft and aeroplanes that use <a href="/wiki/Wing_morphing" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing morphing">wing morphing</a> are all examples of fixed-wing aircraft.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gliding_flight" title="Gliding flight">Gliding</a> fixed-wing aircraft, including free-flying <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">gliders</a> of various kinds and tethered <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kites</a>, can use moving air to gain <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>. <a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft" title="Powered aircraft">Powered</a> fixed-wing aircraft (<a href="/wiki/Aeroplanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Aeroplanes">aeroplanes</a>) that gain forward <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> from an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">engine</a> include <a href="/wiki/Powered_paraglider" class="mw-redirect" title="Powered paraglider">powered paragliders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powered_hang_glider" title="Powered hang glider">powered hang gliders</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Ground_effect_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground effect vehicle">ground effect vehicles</a>. Most fixed-wing aircraft are flown by a <a href="/wiki/Pilot_(aviator)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilot (aviator)">pilot</a> on board the craft, but some are specifically designed to be <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned</a> and controlled either <a href="/wiki/Radio_control" title="Radio control">remotely</a> or autonomously (using onboard computers).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" Some authors hold that leaf kites were being flown much earlier in what is now Sulawesi, based on their interpretation of cave paintings on <a href="/wiki/Muna_Island" title="Muna Island">Muna Island</a> off <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> By at least 549 AD paper kites were being flown, as it was recorded in that year a paper kite was used as a message for a rescue mission.<sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_1_127_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_1_127-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Ancient and medieval Chinese sources list other uses of kites for measuring distances, testing the wind, lifting men, signaling, and communication for military operations.<sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_1_127_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_1_127-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg/310px-Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg" width="310" height="146" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg/620px-Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="650" data-file-height="307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boys flying a kite in 1828 <a kites were brought to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> by <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> towards the end of the 13th century, and kites were brought back by sailors from <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> in the 16th and 17th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-G-Kites_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G-Kites-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Although they were initially regarded as mere curiosities, by the 18th and 19th centuries kites were being used as vehicles for scientific research.<sup id="cite_ref-G-Kites_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G-Kites-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gliders_and_powered_models">Gliders and powered models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gliders and powered models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Around <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">400 BC in Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archytas" title="Archytas">Archytas</a> was reputed to have designed and built the first artificial, self-propelled flying device, a bird-shaped model propelled by a jet of what was probably steam, said to have flown some 200&#160;m (660&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a aircraft">gliders</a> was by the 11th-century monk <a href="/wiki/Eilmer_of_Malmesbury" title="Eilmer of Malmesbury">Eilmer of Malmesbury</a>, which ended in failure. A 17th-century account states that the 9th-century poet <a href="/wiki/Abbas_Ibn_Firnas" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbas Ibn Firnas">Abbas Ibn Firnas</a> made a similar attempt, though no earlier His box kite designs were widely adopted. Although he also developed a type of rotary aircraft engine, he did not create and fly a powered fixed-wing aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powered_flight">Powered flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Powered flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Aviation_in_the_pioneer_era" title="Aviation in the pioneer era">Aviation in the pioneer era</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Sir_Hiram_Maxim" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Hiram Maxim">Sir Hiram Maxim</a> built a craft that weighed 3.5 tons, with a 110-foot (34-meter) wingspan that was powered by two 360-horsepower (270-kW) steam engines driving two propellers. In 1894, his machine was tested with overhead rails to prevent it from rising. The test showed that it had enough lift to take off. The craft was uncontrollable, which Maxim, it is presumed, realized, because he subsequently abandoned work on it.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a 1906, Brazilian inventor <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Santos_Dumont" class="mw-redirect" title="Alberto Santos Dumont">Alberto Santos Dumont</a> designed, <a href="/wiki/Santos-Dumont_14-bis" Aircraft demonstrated their potential as mobile observation platforms, then proved themselves to be machines of war capable of causing casualties to the enemy. The earliest known aerial victory with a synchronised machine gun-armed <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighter aircraft</a> occurred in 1915, by German <a href="/wiki/Luftstreitkr%C3%A4fte" title="Luftstreitkr\xc3\xa4fte">Luftstreitkr\xc3\xa4fte</a> <i>Leutnant</i> <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Wintgens" title="Kurt Wintgens">Kurt Wintgens</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aces" class="mw-redirect" title="Fighter aces">Fighter aces</a> appeared; the greatest (by number of air victories) was <a href="/wiki/Manfred_von_Richthofen" title="Manfred von Richthofen">Manfred von Richthofen</a>.\n</p><p>Following WWI, aircraft technology continued to develop. <a href="/wiki/Alcock_and_Brown" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcock and Brown">Alcock and Brown</a> crossed the Atlantic non-stop for the first time in 1919. The first commercial flights took place between the United States and Canada in 1919.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Interwar_aviation_in_the_.22Golden_Age.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Interwar_aviation_in_the_&quot;Golden_Age&quot;">Interwar aviation in the "Golden Age"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interwar aviation in the &quot;Golden Age&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aviation_between_the_World_Wars" title="Aviation between the World Wars">Aviation between the World Wars</a></div>\n<p>The so-called <b>Golden Age of Aviation</b> occurred between the two World Wars, during which both updated interpretations of earlier breakthroughs \xe2\x80\x94 as with <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Junkers" title="Hugo Junkers">Hugo Junkers</a>\' pioneering of all-metal airframes <a href="/wiki/Junkers_J_1" title="Junkers J 1">in 1915</a> leading to giant multi-engined aircraft <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_ANT-20" title="Tupolev ANT-20">of up to 60+ meter wingspan</a> sizes by the early 1930s, adoption of the mostly air-cooled <a href="/wiki/Radial_engine" title="Radial engine">radial engine</a> as a practical aircraft powerplant alongside powerful V-12 liquid-cooled aviation engines, and ever-greater instances of long-distance flight attempts - as with <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight_of_Alcock_and_Brown" title="Transatlantic flight of Alcock and Brown">a Vickers Vimy in 1919</a>, followed only months later by <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_NC-4#The_transatlantic_flight" title="Curtiss NC-4">the U.S. Navy\'s NC-4 transatlantic flight</a>; culminating in May 1927 with <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a>\'s solo trans-Atlantic flight in the <i><a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_St._Louis" title="Spirit of St. Louis">Spirit of St. Louis</a></i> spurring ever-longer flight attempts, pioneering the way for long-distance flights of the future to become commonplace.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Aeroplanes had a presence in all the major battles of World War II. They were an essential component of the military strategies of the period, such as the German <a href="/wiki/Blitzkrieg" title="Blitzkrieg">Blitzkrieg</a> or the American and Japanese aircraft carrier campaigns of the Pacific.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Military_glider" title="Military glider">Military gliders</a> were developed and used in several campaigns, but they did not become widely used due to the high casualty rate often encountered. The <a href="/wiki/Focke-Achgelis_Fa_330" title="Focke-Achgelis Fa 330">Focke-Achgelis Fa 330</a> <i>Bachstelze</i> (Wagtail) rotor kite of 1942 was notable for its use by German submarines.\n</p><p>Before and during the war, both British and German designers were developing <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a> to power aeroplanes. The first jet aircraft to fly, in 1939, was the German <a href="/wiki/Heinkel_He_178" title="Heinkel He 178">Heinkel He 178</a>. In 1943, the first operational jet fighter, the <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Me_262" title="Messerschmitt Me 262">Messerschmitt Me 262</a>, went into service with the German <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> and later in the war the British <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Meteor" title="Gloster Meteor">Gloster Meteor</a> entered service but never saw action \xe2\x80\x94 top airspeeds of aircraft for that era went as high as 1,130&#160;km/h (702&#160;mph), with the early July 1944 unofficial record flight of the German <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Me_163#Later_versions" class="mw-redirect" title="Messerschmitt Me 163">Me 163B V18</a> rocket fighter prototype.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar">Postwar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Postwar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In October 1947, the <a href="/wiki/Bell_X-1" title="Bell X-1">Bell X-1</a> was the first aircraft to exceed the speed of sound.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1948\xe2\x80\x9349, aircraft transported supplies during the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Blockade" title="Berlin Blockade">Berlin Blockade</a>. New aircraft types, such as the <a href="/wiki/B-52" class="mw-redirect" title="B-52">B-52</a>, were produced during the Cold War.\n</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Jet_airliner" title="Jet airliner">jet airliner</a>, the <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland Comet</a>, was introduced in 1952, followed by the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-104" title="Tupolev Tu-104">Tupolev Tu-104</a> in 1956. The <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a>, the first widely successful commercial jet, was in commercial service for more than 50 years, from 1958 to 2010. The <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a> was the world\'s biggest passenger aircraft from 1970 until it was surpassed by the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a> in 2005.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classes_of_fixed-wing_aircraft">Classes of fixed-wing aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Classes of fixed-wing aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Airplane.2Faeroplane"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Airplane/aeroplane">Airplane/aeroplane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Airplane/aeroplane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">Airplane</a></div>\n<p>An <b>aeroplane</b> (also known as an <b>airplane</b> or simply a <b>plane</b>) is a powered fixed-wing aircraft that is propelled forward by <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propeller</a>. Planes come in a variety of sizes, shapes, and wing configurations. The broad spectrum of uses for planes includes recreation, transportation of goods and people, military, and research.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seaplane">Seaplane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Seaplane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">Seaplane</a></div>\n<p>A <b>seaplane</b> is a fixed-wing aircraft capable of <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">taking off</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_landing" title="Water landing">landing</a> (alighting) on water. Seaplanes that can also operate from dry land are a subclass called <a href="/wiki/Amphibian_aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphibian aircraft">amphibian aircraft</a>. These aircraft were sometimes called <b>hydroplanes</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Seaplanes and amphibians are usually divided into two categories based on their technological characteristics: <a href="/wiki/Floatplane" title="Floatplane">floatplanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flying_boat" title="Flying boat">flying boats</a>.\n</p>\n<ul><li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Floatplane" title="Floatplane">floatplane</a></b> is similar in overall design to a land-based aeroplane, with a generally unmodified fuselage from as compared to its landplane version, except that the wheels at the base of the undercarriage are replaced by <a href="/wiki/Float_(nautical)" title="Float (nautical)">floats</a>, allowing the craft to operate from water rather than from dry land.</li>\n<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Flying_boat" title="Flying boat">flying boat</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">seaplane</a> with a watertight <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hull</a> forming the lower (ventral) areas of its fuselage, resting directly on the water\'s surface. It differs from a <a href="/wiki/Float_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Float plane">float plane</a> as it does not need additional floats for buoyancy, although it may have small underwing floats or fuselage-mount <a href="/wiki/Sponson" title="Sponson">sponsons</a> to stabilize it on the water. Large seaplanes are usually flying boats, with most classic amphibian aircraft designs using some form of flying-boat design for their fuselage/hull.</li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Powered_gliders">Powered gliders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Powered gliders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Many forms of glider (see below) may be modified by adding a small power plant. These include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Motor_glider" title="Motor glider">Motor glider</a> - a conventional <a href="/wiki/Glider_aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Glider aircraft">glider</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sailplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sailplane">sailplane</a> with an auxiliary power plant that may be used when in flight to increase performance.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Powered_hang_glider" title="Powered hang glider">Powered hang glider</a> - a <a href="/wiki/Hang_glider" class="mw-redirect" title="Hang glider">hang glider</a> with a power plant added.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Powered_parachute" title="Powered parachute">Powered parachute</a> - a <a href="/wiki/Paraglider" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraglider">paraglider</a> type of parachute with an integrated airframe, seat, undercarriage and power plant hung beneath.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Powered_paraglider" class="mw-redirect" title="Powered paraglider">Powered paraglider</a> or paramotor - a <a href="/wiki/Paraglider" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraglider">paraglider</a> with a power plant suspended behind the pilot.</li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_effect_vehicle">Ground effect vehicle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ground effect vehicle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ground_effect_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground effect vehicle">Ground effect vehicle</a></div>\n<p>A <b>ground effect vehicle</b> (GEV) is a craft that attains level flight near the surface of the earth, making use of the <a href="/wiki/Ground_effect_(aerodynamics)" title="Ground effect (aerodynamics)">ground effect</a> \xe2\x80\x93 an aerodynamic interaction between the wings and the earth\'s surface. Some GEVs are able to fly higher out of ground effect (OGE) when required \xe2\x80\x93 these are classed as powered fixed-wing aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glider">Glider</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Glider">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Schempp-Hirth_Ventus_2b_glider_being_launched_at_Lasham_Airfield_in_UK.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="487" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Schempp-Hirth_Ventus_2b_glider_being_launched_at_Lasham_Airfield_in_UK.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">glider (sailplane)</a> being winch-launched</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">Glider (aircraft)</a></div>\n<p>A <b>glider</b> is a heavier-than-air craft that is supported in flight by the dynamic reaction of the air against its lifting surfaces, and whose free flight does not depend on an engine. A <b>sailplane</b> is a fixed-wing glider designed for soaring - the ability to gain height in updrafts of air and to fly for long periods.\n</p><p>Gliders are mainly used for recreation, but have also been used for other purposes such as aerodynamics research, warfare and recovering spacecraft.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Motor_glider" title="Motor glider">motor glider</a> does have an engine for extending its performance and some have engines powerful enough to take off, but the engine is not used in normal flight.\n</p><p>As is the case with planes, there are a wide variety of glider types differing in the construction of their wings, aerodynamic efficiency, location of the pilot and controls. Perhaps the most familiar type is the toy <a href="/wiki/Paper_plane" title="Paper plane">paper plane</a>.\n</p><p>Large gliders are most commonly launched by a tow-plane or by a winch. <a href="/wiki/Military_glider" title="Military glider">Military gliders</a> have been used in war to deliver assault troops, and specialised gliders have been used in atmospheric and <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamic</a> research. <a href="/wiki/Rocket-powered_aircraft" title="Rocket-powered aircraft">Rocket-powered aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplanes</a> have also made unpowered landings.\n</p><p>Gliders and sailplanes that are used for the sport of <a href="/wiki/Gliding" title="Gliding">gliding</a> have high aerodynamic efficiency. The highest <a href="/wiki/Lift-to-drag_ratio" title="Lift-to-drag ratio">lift-to-drag ratio</a> is 70:1, though 50:1 is more common. After launch, further energy is obtained through the skillful exploitation of rising air in the atmosphere. Flights of thousands of kilometres at average speeds over 200&#160;km/h have been achieved.\n</p><p>The most numerous unpowered aircraft are <a href="/wiki/Paper_aeroplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Paper aeroplane">paper aeroplanes</a>, a handmade type of glider. Like hang gliders and paragliders, they are foot-launched and are in general slower, smaller, and less expensive than sailplanes. Hang gliders most often have flexible wings given shape by a frame, though some have rigid wings. Paragliders and paper aeroplanes have no frames in their wings.\n</p><p>Gliders and sailplanes can share a number of features in common with powered aircraft, including many of the same types of fuselage and wing structures. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Horten_H.IV" title="Horten H.IV">Horten H.IV</a> was a tailless flying wing glider, and the delta wing-shaped <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> orbiter flew much like a conventional glider in the lower atmosphere. Many gliders also use similar controls and instruments as powered craft.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_glider">Types of glider</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Types of glider">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:327px;"><div id="mwe_player_0" class="PopUpMediaTransform" style="width:325px;" videopayload="&lt;div class=&quot;mediaContainer&quot; As pilots began to understand how to use rising air, <a href="/wiki/Sailplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sailplane">sailplane</a> gliders were developed with a high <a href="/wiki/Lift-to-drag_ratio" title="Lift-to-drag ratio">lift-to-drag ratio</a>. These allowed longer glides to the next source of "<a href="/wiki/Lift_(soaring)" title="Lift (soaring)">lift</a>", and so increase their chances of flying long distances. This gave rise to the popular sport of <a href="/wiki/Gliding" title="Gliding">gliding</a>.\n</p><p>Early gliders were mainly built of wood and metal but the majority of sailplanes now use composite materials incorporating glass, carbon or <a href="/wiki/Aramid" title="Aramid">aramid</a> fibres. To minimise <a href="/wiki/Drag_(force)" class="mw-redirect" title="Drag (force)">drag</a>, these types have a streamlined <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> and long narrow wings having a <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration#Aspect_ratio" title="Wing configuration">high aspect ratio</a>. Both single-seat and two-seat gliders are available.\n</p><p>Initially training was done by short "hops" in <a href="/wiki/Primary_glider" title="Primary glider">primary gliders</a> which are very basic aircraft with no <a href="/wiki/Cockpit_(aviation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cockpit (aviation)">cockpit</a> and minimal instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-Schweizer_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schweizer-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Since shortly after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> training has always been done in two-seat dual control gliders, but high performance two-seaters are also used to share the workload and the enjoyment of long flights. Originally skids were used for landing, but the majority now land on wheels, often retractable. Some gliders, known as <a href="/wiki/Motor_glider" title="Motor glider">motor gliders</a>, are designed for unpowered flight, but can deploy <a href="/wiki/Piston_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Piston engine">piston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotary_engine" title="Rotary engine">rotary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric engines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-definition_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-definition-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Gliders are classified by the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale" title="F\xc3\xa9d\xc3\xa9ration A\xc3\xa9ronautique Internationale">FAI</a> for competitions into <a href="/wiki/Glider_Competition_Classes" class="mw-redirect" title="Glider Competition Classes">glider competition classes</a> mainly on the basis of span and flaps.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Goatglider.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Goatglider.jpg/220px-Goatglider.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Goatglider.jpg/330px-Goatglider.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Goatglider.jpg/440px-Goatglider.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="510" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Goatglider.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ultralight "airchair" <a href="/wiki/Sandlin_Goat" title="Sandlin Goat">Goat 1 glider</a></div></div></div>\n<p>A class of ultralight sailplanes, including some known as <a href="/wiki/Microlift_glider" title="Microlift glider">microlift gliders</a> and some known as "airchairs", has been defined by the FAI based on a maximum weight. They are light enough to be transported easily, and can be flown without licensing in some countries. Ultralight gliders have performance similar to <a href="/wiki/Hang_gliding" title="Hang gliding">hang gliders</a>, but offer some additional crash safety as the pilot can be strapped in an upright seat within a deformable structure. Landing is usually on one or two wheels which distinguishes these craft from hang gliders. Several commercial ultralight gliders have come and gone, but most current development is done by individual designers and home builders.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_gliders">Military gliders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Military gliders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG/220px-Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG/330px-Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG/440px-Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1338" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Waco_CG-4A_USAF.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1943 <a href="/wiki/USAAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAAF">USAAF</a> <a href="/wiki/Waco_CG-4" title="Waco CG-4">Waco CG-4</a>A</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Military_gliders" class="mw-redirect" title="Military gliders">Military gliders</a> were used during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> for carrying troops (<a href="/wiki/Glider_infantry" title="Glider infantry">glider infantry</a>) and heavy equipment to combat zones. The gliders were towed into the air and most of the way to their target by military transport planes, e.g. <a href="/wiki/C-47_Dakota" class="mw-redirect" title="C-47 Dakota">C-47 Dakota</a>, or by bombers that had been relegated to secondary activities, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Short_Stirling" title="Short Stirling">Short Stirling</a>. Once released from the tow near the target, they landed as close to the target as possible. The advantage over paratroopers were that heavy equipment could be landed and that the troops were quickly assembled rather than being dispersed over a drop zone. The gliders were treated as disposable, leading to construction from common and inexpensive materials such as wood, though a few were retrieved and re-used. By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, transport aircraft had also become larger and more efficient so that even light tanks could be dropped by parachute, causing gliders to fall out of favor.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_gliders">Research gliders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Research gliders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>Even after the development of powered aircraft, gliders continued to be used for <a href="/wiki/Aviation_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Aviation research">aviation research</a>. The <a href="/wiki/NASA_Paresev" title="NASA Paresev">NASA Paresev Rogallo flexible wing</a> was originally developed to investigate alternative methods of recovering spacecraft. Although this application was abandoned, publicity inspired hobbyists to adapt the flexible-wing airfoil for modern hang gliders.\n</p><p>Initial research into many types of fixed-wing craft, including <a href="/wiki/Flying_wing" title="Flying wing">flying wings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting bodies</a> was also carried out using unpowered prototypes.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Hang_glider">Hang glider</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Hang glider">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg/220px-Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg/330px-Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg/440px-Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hang_gliding_hyner.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hang gliding</div></div></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hang_glider" class="mw-redirect" title="Hang glider">hang glider</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Glider_aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Glider aircraft">glider aircraft</a> in which the pilot is ensconced in a harness suspended from the <a href="/wiki/Airframe" title="Airframe">airframe</a>, and exercises control by shifting body weight in opposition to a control frame. Most modern hang gliders are made of an <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_alloy" title="Aluminium alloy">aluminium alloy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite</a>-framed fabric wing. Pilots have the ability to <a href="/wiki/Lift_(soaring)" title="Lift (soaring)">soar</a> for hours, gain thousands of metres of altitude in <a href="/wiki/Thermal" title="Thermal">thermal</a> updrafts, perform aerobatics, and glide cross-country for hundreds of kilometres.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Paraglider">Paraglider</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Paraglider">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Paraglider" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraglider">paraglider</a> is a lightweight, free-flying, foot-launched glider aircraft with no rigid primary structure.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The pilot sits in a <a href="/wiki/Safety_harness" title="Safety harness">harness</a> suspended below a hollow fabric wing whose shape is formed by its suspension lines, the pressure of air entering vents in the front of the wing and the aerodynamic forces of the air flowing over the outside. Paragliding is most often a recreational activity.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unmanned_gliders">Unmanned gliders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Unmanned gliders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Paper_airplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Paper airplane">paper plane</a> is a toy aircraft (usually a glider) made out of paper or paperboard.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Model_glider" class="mw-redirect" title="Model glider">Model glider aircraft</a> are models of aircraft using lightweight materials such as <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balsa_wood" class="mw-redirect" title="Balsa wood">balsa wood</a>. This enables the carrying aircraft to attack a heavily defended target from a distance.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kite">Kite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Kite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chinese_Kite.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Chinese_Kite.jpg/220px-Chinese_Kite.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Chinese_Kite.jpg/330px-Chinese_Kite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Chinese_Kite.jpg/440px-Chinese_Kite.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chinese_Kite.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kite</a> in flight</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">Kite</a></div>\n<p>A <b>kite</b> is an aircraft tethered to a fixed point so that the wind blows over its wings.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">Lift</a> is generated when air flows over the kite\'s wing, producing low pressure above the wing and high pressure below it, and deflecting the airflow downwards. class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Airframe" title="Airframe">Airframe</a></div>\n<p>The structural parts of a fixed-wing aircraft are called the airframe. The parts present can vary according to the aircraft\'s type and purpose. Early types were usually made of wood with fabric wing surfaces, When engines became available for a powered flight around a hundred years ago, their mounts were made of metal. Then as speeds increased more and more parts became metal until by the end of WWII all-metal aircraft were common. In modern times, increasing use of <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite materials</a> has been made.\n</p><p>Typical structural parts include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>One or more large horizontal <i>wings</i>, often with an <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">airfoil</a> cross-section shape. The wing deflects air downward as the aircraft moves forward, generating <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lifting force</a> to support it in flight. The wing also provides stability in <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight dynamics (aircraft)">roll</a> to stop the aircraft from rolling to the left or right in steady flight.</li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antonov_225_(2010).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Antonov_225_%282010%29.jpg/220px-Antonov_225_%282010%29.jpg" width="220" height="54" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Antonov_225_%282010%29.jpg/330px-Antonov_225_%282010%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Antonov_225_%282010%29.jpg/440px-Antonov_225_%282010%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2855" data-file-height="705" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Antonov_225_(2010).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/An-225_Mriya" class="mw-redirect" title="An-225 Mriya">An-225 Mriya</a>, the largest airplane in the world, which can carry a 250-tonne payload, has two vertical stabilizers</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a></i>, a long, thin body, usually with tapered or rounded ends to make its shape <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamically" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerodynamically">aerodynamically</a> smooth. The fuselage joins the other parts of the airframe and usually contains important things such as the pilot, payload and flight systems.</li>\n<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Vertical_stabilizer" title="Vertical stabilizer">vertical stabilizer</a></i> or fin is a vertical wing-like surface mounted at the rear of the plane and typically protruding above it. The fin stabilizes the plane\'s <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight dynamics (aircraft)">yaw</a> (turn left or right) and mounts the <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudder</a> which controls its rotation along that axis.</li>\n<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Horizontal_stabilizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Horizontal stabilizer">horizontal stabilizer</a></i>, usually mounted at the tail near the vertical stabilizer. The horizontal stabilizer is used to stabilize the plane\'s <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight dynamics (aircraft)">pitch</a> (tilt up or down) and mounts the <a href="/wiki/Elevator_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Elevator (aircraft)">elevators</a> which provide pitch control.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">Landing gear</a>,</i> a set of wheels, skids, or floats that support the plane while it is on the surface. On seaplanes, the bottom of the fuselage or floats (pontoons) support it while on the water. On some planes, the landing gear retracts during the flight to reduce drag.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wings">Wings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Wings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The wings of a fixed-wing aircraft are static planes extending either side of the aircraft. When the aircraft travels forwards, \nair flows over the wings which are shaped to create lift.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wing_structure">Wing structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Wing structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Kites and some light weight gliders and aeroplanes have flexible wing surfaces which are stretched across a frame and made rigid by the lift forces exerted by the airflow over them. Larger aircraft have rigid wing surfaces which provide additional strength.\n</p><p>Whether flexible or rigid, most wings have a strong frame to give them their shape and to transfer lift from the wing surface to the rest of the aircraft. The main structural elements are one or more spars running from root to tip, and many ribs running from the leading (front) to the trailing (rear) edge.\n</p><p>Early aeroplane engines had little power and light weight was very important. Also, early aerofoil sections were very thin, and could not have strong frame installed within. So until the 1930s, most wings were too light weight to have enough strength and external bracing struts and wires were added. When the available engine power increased during the 1920s and 1930s, wings could be made heavy and strong enough that bracing was not needed any more. This type of unbraced wing is called a cantilever wing.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wing_configuration">Wing configuration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Wing configuration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration" title="Wing configuration">Wing configuration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">Wing</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg/220px-Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg/330px-Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg/440px-Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="334" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morane-Saulnier_Type_L_-_Captured_with_german_insigna.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Captured <a href="/wiki/Morane-Saulnier_L" title="Morane-Saulnier L">Morane-Saulnier L</a> wire-braced parasol monoplane</div></div></div>\n<p>The number and shape of the wings vary widely on different types. A given wing plane may be full-span or divided by a central <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> into port (left) and starboard (right) wings. Occasionally, even more, wings have been used, with the three-winged <a href="/wiki/Triplane" title="Triplane">triplane</a> achieving some fame in WWI. The four-winged <a href="/wiki/Quadruplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Quadruplane">quadruplane</a> and other <a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiplane_(Aeronautics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Multiplane (Aeronautics) (page does not exist)">Multiplane (Aeronautics)</a> designs have had little success.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Monoplane" title="Monoplane">monoplane</a>, which derives from the prefix, mono means one which means it has a single wing plane, a <a href="/wiki/Biplane" title="Biplane">biplane</a> has two stacked one above the other, a <a href="/wiki/Tandem_wing" title="Tandem wing">tandem wing</a> has two placed one behind the other. When the available engine power increased during the 1920s and 1930s and bracing was no longer needed, the unbraced or cantilever monoplane became the most common form of powered type.\n</p><p>The wing <a href="/wiki/Planform_(aeronautics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Planform (aeronautics)">planform</a> is the shape when seen from above. To be aerodynamically efficient, a wing should be straight with a long span from side to side but have a short chord (high <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a>). But to be structurally efficient, and hence lightweight, a wing must have a short span but still enough area to provide lift (low aspect ratio).\n</p><p>At transonic speeds, near the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>, it helps to sweep the wing backward or forwards to reduce drag from supersonic shock waves as they begin to form. The <a href="/wiki/Swept_wing" title="Swept wing">swept wing</a> is just a straight wing swept backward or forwards.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg/220px-Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg/330px-Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg/440px-Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="571" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dassault_Mirage_G8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two <a href="/wiki/Dassault_Mirage_G" title="Dassault Mirage G">Dassault Mirage G</a> prototypes, one with wings swept (top)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta wing</a> is a triangle shape which may be used for a number of reasons. As a flexible <a href="/wiki/Rogallo_wing" title="Rogallo wing">Rogallo wing</a> it allows a stable shape under aerodynamic forces, and so is often used for kites and other ultralight craft. As a supersonic wing, it combines high strength with low drag and so is often used for fast jets.\n</p><p>A variable geometry wing can be changed in flight to a different shape. The <a href="/wiki/Variable_sweep_wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Variable sweep wing">variable-sweep wing</a> transforms between an efficient straight configuration for <a href="/wiki/Takeoff_and_landing" title="Takeoff and landing">takeoff and landing</a>, to a low-drag swept configuration for high-speed flight. Other forms of variable planform have been flown, but none have gone beyond the research stage.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuselage">Fuselage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Fuselage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a></div>\n<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a></i> is a long, thin body, usually with tapered or rounded ends to make its shape <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamically" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerodynamically">aerodynamically</a> smooth. The fuselage may contain the <a href="/wiki/Flight_crew" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight crew">flight crew</a>, passengers, cargo or <a href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Payload (air and space craft)">payload</a>, fuel and engines. The pilots of manned aircraft operate them from a <i><a href="/wiki/Cockpit_(aviation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cockpit (aviation)">cockpit</a></i> located at the front or top of the fuselage and equipped with controls and usually windows and instruments. A plane may have more than one fuselage, or it may be fitted with booms with the tail located between the booms to allow the extreme rear of the fuselage to be useful for a variety of purposes.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wings_vs._bodies">Wings vs. bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Wings vs. bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flying_wing">Flying wing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Flying wing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flying_wing" title="Flying wing">Flying wing</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg/220px-USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg" width="220" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg/330px-USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg/440px-USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="711" data-file-height="695" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:USAF_B-2_Spirit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The US-produced <a href="/wiki/B-2_Spirit" class="mw-redirect" title="B-2 Spirit">B-2 Spirit</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bomber" title="Strategic bomber">strategic bomber</a> capable of intercontinental missions, has a flying wing configuration</div></div></div>\n<p>A flying wing is a <a href="/wiki/Tailless_aircraft" title="Tailless aircraft">tailless</a> aircraft which has no definite <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>, with most of the crew, payload and equipment being housed inside the main wing structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Crane_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crane-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>224</span></sup>\n</p><p>The flying wing configuration was studied extensively in the 1930s and 1940s, notably by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Northrop" title="Jack Northrop">Jack Northrop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheston_L._Eshelman" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheston L. Eshelman">Cheston L. Eshelman</a> in the United States, and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lippisch" title="Alexander Lippisch">Alexander Lippisch</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Horten_brothers" title="Horten brothers">Horten brothers</a> in Germany.\nAfter the war, a number of experimental designs were based on the flying wing concept. Some general interest continued until the early 1950s, but designs did not necessarily offer a great advantage in range and presented a number of technical problems, leading to the adoption of "conventional" solutions like the <a href="/wiki/Convair_B-36" class="mw-redirect" title="Convair B-36">Convair B-36</a> and the <a href="/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress" class="mw-redirect" title="B-52 Stratofortress">B-52 Stratofortress</a>. Due to the practical need for a deep wing, the flying wing concept is most practical for designs in the slow-to-medium speed range, and there has been continual interest in using it as a tactical <a href="/wiki/Airlift" title="Airlift">airlifter</a> design.\n</p><p>Interest in flying wings was renewed in the 1980s due to their potentially low <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> reflection cross-sections. <a href="/wiki/Stealth_technology" title="Stealth technology">Stealth technology</a> relies on shapes which only reflect radar waves in certain directions, thus making the aircraft hard to detect unless the radar receiver is at a specific position relative to the aircraft - a position that changes continuously as the aircraft moves. This approach eventually led to the Northrop <a href="/wiki/B-2_Spirit" class="mw-redirect" title="B-2 Spirit">B-2 Spirit</a> <a href="/wiki/Stealth_aircraft" title="Stealth aircraft">stealth</a> bomber. In this case the aerodynamic advantages of the flying wing are not the primary needs. However, modern computer-controlled <a href="/wiki/Fly-by-wire" title="Fly-by-wire">fly-by-wire</a> systems allowed for many of the aerodynamic drawbacks of the flying wing to be minimised, making for an efficient and stable long-range bomber.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Blended_wing_body">Blended wing body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Blended wing body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blended_wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Blended wing">Blended wing</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NASA_BWB.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/NASA_BWB.jpg/300px-NASA_BWB.jpg" width="300" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/NASA_BWB.jpg/450px-NASA_BWB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/NASA_BWB.jpg/600px-NASA_BWB.jpg 2x" data-file-width="795" data-file-height="361" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NASA_BWB.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Computer-generated model of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_X-48" title="Boeing X-48">Boeing X-48</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Blended wing body aircraft have a flattened and airfoil shaped body, which produces most of the lift to keep itself aloft, and distinct and separate wing structures, though the wings are smoothly blended in with the body.\n</p><p>Thus blended wing bodied aircraft incorporate design features from both a futuristic fuselage and flying wing design. The purported advantages of the blended wing body approach are efficient high-lift wings and a wide <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">airfoil</a>-shaped body. This enables the entire craft to contribute to <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> generation with the result of potentially increased fuel economy.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifting_body">Lifting body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Lifting body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:X24.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/X24.jpg/220px-X24.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/X24.jpg/330px-X24.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/X24.jpg/440px-X24.jpg 2x" data-file-width="771" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:X24.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Martin Aircraft Company <a href="/wiki/Martin-Marietta_X-24" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin-Marietta X-24">X-24</a> was built as part of a 1963\xe2\x80\x931975 experimental US military program</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">Lifting body</a></div>\n<p>A lifting body is a configuration in which the body itself produces <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a>. In contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Flying_wing" title="Flying wing">flying wing</a>, which is a wing with minimal or no conventional <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>, a lifting body can be thought of as a fuselage with little or no conventional wing. Whereas a flying wing seeks to maximize cruise efficiency at <a href="/wiki/Subsonic_flight" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsonic flight">subsonic</a> speeds by eliminating non-lifting surfaces, lifting bodies generally minimize the drag and structure of a wing for subsonic, <a href="/wiki/Supersonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hypersonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypersonic">hypersonic</a> flight, or, <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> <a href="/wiki/Re-entry" class="mw-redirect" title="Re-entry">re-entry</a>. All of these flight regimes pose challenges for proper flight stability.\n</p><p>Lifting bodies were a major area of research in the 1960s and 1970s as a means to build a small and lightweight manned spacecraft. The US built a number of famous lifting body rocket planes to test the concept, as well as several rocket-launched re-entry vehicles that were tested over the Pacific. Interest waned as the <a href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a> lost interest in the manned mission, and major development ended during the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_design_process" title="Space Shuttle design process">Space Shuttle design process</a> when it became clear that the highly shaped fuselages made it difficult to fit fuel tankage.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Empennage_and_foreplane">Empennage and foreplane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Empennage and foreplane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">Empennage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canard_(aeronautics)" title="Canard (aeronautics)">Canard (aeronautics)</a></div>\n<p>The classic aerofoil section wing is unstable in flight and difficult to control. Flexible-wing types often rely on an anchor line or the weight of a pilot hanging beneath to maintain the correct attitude. Some free-flying types use an adapted aerofoil that is stable, or other ingenious mechanisms including, most recently, electronic artificial stability.\n</p><p>But in order to achieve trim, stability and control, most fixed-wing types have an <a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a> comprising a fin and rudder which act horizontally and a tailplane and elevator which act vertically. This is so common that it is known as the conventional layout. Sometimes there may be two or more fins, spaced out along the tailplane.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SaabViggen_Canards.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SaabViggen_Canards.jpg/220px-SaabViggen_Canards.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SaabViggen_Canards.jpg/330px-SaabViggen_Canards.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SaabViggen_Canards.jpg/440px-SaabViggen_Canards.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SaabViggen_Canards.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Canards on the <a href="/wiki/Saab_Viggen" class="mw-redirect" title="Saab Viggen">Saab Viggen</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Some types have a horizontal "<a href="/wiki/Canard_(aeronautics)" title="Canard (aeronautics)">canard</a>" foreplane ahead of the main wing, instead of behind it.<sup id="cite_ref-Crane_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crane-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>86</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GroundUp_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GroundUp-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAR1.1_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAR1.1-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> This foreplane may contribute to the trim, stability or control of the aircraft, or to several of these.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft_controls">Aircraft controls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Aircraft controls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kite_control">Kite control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Kite control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Kites are controlled by wires running down to the ground. Typically each wire acts as a tether to the part of the kite it is attached to.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Free-flying_aircraft_controls">Free-flying aircraft controls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Free-flying aircraft controls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Gliders and aeroplanes have more complex control systems, especially if they are piloted.\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_flight_control_system" title="Aircraft flight control system">Aircraft flight control system</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG/220px-Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG/330px-Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG/440px-Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pilotska_kabina_zrakoplova.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Typical light aircraft (<a href="/wiki/Cessna_150" title="Cessna 150">Cessna 150</a>M) cockpit with control yokes</div></div></div>\n<p>The main controls allow the pilot to direct the aircraft in the air. Typically these are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Yoke_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yoke (aircraft)">yoke</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Joystick" title="Joystick">joystick</a></i> controls rotation of the plane about the pitch and roll axes. A <a href="/wiki/Yoke_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yoke (aircraft)">yoke</a> resembles a steering wheel, and a control stick is a joystick. The pilot can pitch the plane down by pushing on the yoke or stick, and pitch the plane up by pulling on it. Rolling the plane is accomplished by turning the yoke in the direction of the desired roll, or by tilting the control stick in that direction.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">Rudder</a> pedals</i> control rotation of the plane about the yaw axis. There are two pedals that pivot so that when one is pressed forward the other moves backward, and vice versa. The pilot presses on the right rudder pedal to make the plane yaw to the right, and pushes on the left pedal to make it yaw to the left. The rudder is used mainly to balance the plane in turns, or to compensate for winds or other effects that tend to turn the plane about the yaw axis.</li>\n<li>On powered types, an engine stop control ("fuel cutoff", for example) and, usually, a <i><a href="/wiki/Throttle" title="Throttle">Throttle</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Thrust_lever" title="Thrust lever">thrust lever</a></i> and other controls, such as a fuel-mixture control (to compensate for air density changes with altitude change).</li></ul>\n<p>Other common controls include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Flap_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flap (aircraft)">Flap</a> levers,</i> which are used to control the deflection position of flaps on the wings.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Spoiler_(aeronautics)" title="Spoiler (aeronautics)">Spoiler</a> levers,</i> which are used to control the position of spoilers on the wings, and to arm their automatic deployment in planes designed to deploy them upon landing. The spoilers reduce lift for landing.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Trim_tab" title="Trim tab">Trim</a> controls,</i> which usually take the form of knobs or wheels and are used to adjust pitch, roll, or yaw trim. These are often connected to small airfoils on the trail edge of the control surfaces called "trim tabs". Trim is used to reduce the amount of pressure on the control forces needed to maintain a steady course.</li>\n<li>On wheeled types, <i><a href="/wiki/Brake" title="Brake">Brakes</a></i> are used to slow and stop the plane on the ground, and sometimes for turns on the ground.</li></ul>\n<p>A craft may have two pilots\' seats with dual controls, allowing two pilots to take turns. This is often used for training or for longer flights.\n</p><p>The control system may allow full or partial automation of flight, such as an <a href="/wiki/Autopilot" title="Autopilot">autopilot</a>, a wing leveler, or a <a href="/wiki/Flight_management_system" title="Flight management system">flight management system</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aircraft</a> has no pilot but is controlled remotely or via means such as gyroscopes or other forms of autonomous control.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cockpit_instrumentation">Cockpit instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fixed-wing_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Cockpit instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On manned fixed-wing aircraft, instruments provide information to the pilots, including <a href="/wiki/Flight_instruments" title="Flight instruments">flight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">engines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_navigation" title="Air navigation">navigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aviation_communication" title="Aviation communication">communications</a>, and other aircraft systems that may be installed.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Six_flight_instruments.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Six_flight_instruments.JPG/240px-Six_flight_instruments.JPG" width="240" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Six_flight_instruments.JPG/360px-Six_flight_instruments.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Six_flight_instruments.JPG/480px-Six_flight_instruments.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1503" data-file-height="927" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Six_flight_instruments.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The six basic flight instruments.<br />Top row (left to right): airspeed indicator, attitude indicator, altimeter.<br />Bottom row (left to right): turn coordinator, heading indicator, vertical speed indicator.</div></div></div>\n<p>The six basic instruments, sometimes referred to as the "six pack", are as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-6pack_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6pack-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ol><li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Airspeed_indicator" title="Airspeed indicator">airspeed indicator</a> (ASI)</i> shows the speed at which the plane is moving through the surrounding air.</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Attitude_indicator" title="Attitude indicator">attitude indicator</a> (AI)</i>, sometimes called the <i>artificial horizon</i>, indicates the exact orientation of the aircraft about its <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes" title="Aircraft principal axes">pitch and roll axes</a>.</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeter</a></i> indicates the altitude or height of the plane <a href="/wiki/Metres_above_sea_level" title="Metres above sea level">above mean sea level</a> (AMSL).</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Vertical_speed_indicator" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertical speed indicator">vertical speed indicator</a> (VSI)</i>, or <i>variometer</i>, shows the rate at which the plane is <a href="/wiki/Climb_(aeronautics)" title="Climb (aeronautics)">climbing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Descent_(aeronautics)" title="Descent (aeronautics)">descending</a>.</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Heading_indicator" title="Heading indicator">heading indicator</a> (HI)</i>, sometimes called the <i>directional gyro (DG)</i>, shows the <a href="/wiki/Heading_(navigation)" title="Heading (navigation)">magnetic compass direction</a> that the plane\'s fuselage is pointing towards. The <a href="/wiki/Course_(navigation)" title="Course (navigation)">actual direction</a> the plane is flying towards is affected by the wind conditions.</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Turn_and_slip_indicator#turn_coordinator" title="Turn and slip indicator">turn coordinator</a> (TC)</i>, or <i>turn and bank indicator</i>, helps the pilot to control the plane in a coordinated <a href="/wiki/Orientation_(geometry)" title="Orientation (geometry)">attitude</a> while turning.</li></ol>\n<p>Other cockpit instruments might include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Two-way_radio" title="Two-way radio">two-way radio</a></i>, to enable communications with other planes and with <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a>. Airplanes built before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> may not have been equipped with a radio but they are practically essential now in case of emergency.</li>\n<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Horizontal_situation_indicator" title="Horizontal situation indicator">horizontal situation indicator</a> (HSI)</i>, to indicate the position and movement of the plane as seen from above with respect to the ground, including course/heading and other information.</li>\n<li>Instruments showing the status of each of the plane\'s engines (<a href="/wiki/Engine#Engine_speed" title="Engine">operating speed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engine#Thrust" title="Engine">thrust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">temperature</a>, and other variables).</li>\n<li>Combined display systems such as <i><a href="/wiki/Primary_flight_display" title="Primary flight display">primary flight displays</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Navigation_aid" class="mw-redirect" title="Navigation aid">navigation aids</a></i>.</li>\n<li>Information displays such as onboard <i><a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a></i> displays.</li>\n<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Radio_direction_finder" title="Radio direction finder">radio direction finder</a> (RDF)</i>, to indicate the direction to one or more radio beacons, which can be used to determine the plane\'s position.</li>\n<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation" title="Satellite navigation">satellite navigation</a> (satnav)</i> system, to provide an accurate position.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
