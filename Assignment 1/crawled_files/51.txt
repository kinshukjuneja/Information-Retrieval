killed (estimated)</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Great Chicago Fire</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Conflagration" title="Conflagration">conflagration</a> that burned from Sunday, October 8, to Tuesday, October 10, 1871. The fire killed up to 300 people, destroyed roughly 3.3 square miles (9&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, and left more than 100,000 residents homeless.<sup id="cite_ref-Rayfield1997_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rayfield1997-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Help flowed to the city from near and far after the fire. The City of Chicago improved building codes to stop the rapid spread of fire, and re-built rapidly to those higher standards. A donation from the United Kingdom spurred the establishment of the Chicago Public Library, a free public library system, a contrast to the private, fee for membership libraries common before the fire.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg/220px-1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg/330px-1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg/440px-1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6472" data-file-height="4616" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1871_Chicago_view_before_the_Great_Conflagration.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1871 Chicago view before the \'Great Conflagration\'</div></div></div>\n<p>The fire started at about 9:00&#160;p.m. on October&#160;8, in or around a small barn belonging to the O\'Leary family that bordered the alley behind 137 <a href="/wiki/DeKoven_Street_(Chicago)" title="DeKoven Street (Chicago)">DeKoven Street</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The shed next to the barn was the first building to be consumed by the fire, but city officials never determined the exact cause of the blaze,<sup id="cite_ref-owen_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-owen-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but the rapid spread of the fire due to a long drought in the prior summer, strong winds from the southwest, and the rapid destruction of the water pumping system explain the extensive damage of the mainly wooden city structures. There has been much speculation over the years on a single start to the fire. The most popular tale blames <a href="/wiki/Catherine_O%27Leary" title="Catherine O&#39;Leary">Mrs. O\'Leary\'s cow</a>, who allegedly knocked over a lantern; others state that a group of men were gambling inside the barn and knocked over a lantern.<sup id="cite_ref-JimMurphy_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JimMurphy-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Still other speculation suggests that the blaze was related to other fires in the Midwest that day.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fire\'s spread was aided by the city\'s use of wood as the predominant building material in a style called <a href="/wiki/Framing_(construction)#Balloon_framing" title="Framing (construction)">balloon frame</a>. More than two thirds of the structures in Chicago at the time of the fire were made entirely of wood, with most of the houses and buildings being topped with highly flammable <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Roof_shingle" title="Roof shingle">shingle</a> roofs. All of the city\'s sidewalks and many roads were also made of wood.<sup id="cite_ref-JimMurphy_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JimMurphy-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Compounding this problem, Chicago received only 1 inch (2.5&#160;cm) of rain from July&#160;4 to October&#160;9, causing severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> conditions before the fire, while strong southwest winds helped to carry flying embers toward the heart of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>144</span></sup>\n</p><p>In 1871, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Fire_Department" title="Chicago Fire Department">Chicago Fire Department</a> had 185 <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">firefighters</a> with just 17 horse-drawn steam engines to protect the entire city.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>146</span></sup> The initial response by the fire department was quick, but due to an error by the watchman, Matthias Schaffer, the firefighters were sent to the wrong place, allowing the fire to grow unchecked.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>146</span></sup> An alarm sent from the area near the fire also failed to register at the courthouse where the fire watchmen were, while the firefighters were tired from having fought numerous small fires and one large fire in the week before.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These factors combined to turn a small barn fire into a conflagration.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread_of_the_fire">Spread of the fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spread of the fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg/170px-Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg/255px-Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg/340px-Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3588" data-file-height="5382" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Chicago_Fire_map_with_starting_point.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1868 map of Chicago, highlighting the area destroyed by the fire (location of O\'Leary\'s barn indicated by red dot)</div></div></div>\n<p>When firefighters finally arrived at DeKoven Street, the fire had grown and spread to neighboring buildings and was progressing toward the central business district. Firefighters had hoped that the South Branch of the Chicago River and an area that had previously thoroughly burned would act as a natural <a href="/wiki/Firebreak" title="Firebreak">firebreak</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>147</span></sup> All along the river, however, were lumber yards, warehouses, and coal yards, and barges and numerous bridges across the river. As the fire grew, the southwest wind intensified and became superheated, causing structures to catch fire from the heat and from burning debris blown by the wind. Around 12:00&#160;p.m., flaming debris blew across the river and landed on roofs and the South Side Gas Works.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>148</span></sup>\n</p><p>With the fire across the river and moving rapidly toward the heart of the city, panic set in. About this time, Mayor <a href="/wiki/Roswell_B._Mason" title="Roswell B. Mason">Roswell B. Mason</a> sent messages to nearby towns asking for help. When the courthouse caught fire, he ordered the building to be evacuated and the prisoners jailed in the basement to be released. At 2:30&#160;a.m. on the 9th, the cupola of the courthouse collapsed, sending the great bell crashing down.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>148</span></sup> Some witnesses reported hearing the sound from a mile (1.6&#160;km) away.\n</p><p>As more buildings succumbed to the flames, a major contributing factor to the fire\xe2\x80\x99s spread was a meteorological phenomenon known as a <a href="/wiki/Fire_whirl" title="Fire whirl">fire whirl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> As overheated air rises, it comes into contact with cooler air and begins to spin creating a tornado-like effect. These fire whirls are likely what drove flaming debris so high and so far. Such debris was blown across the main branch of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_River" title="Chicago River">Chicago River</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Railroad_car" title="Railroad car">railroad car</a> carrying <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>152</span></sup> The fire had jumped the river a second time and was now raging across the city\xe2\x80\x99s north side.\n</p><p>Despite the fire spreading and growing rapidly, the city\'s firefighters continued to battle the blaze. A short time after the fire jumped the river, a burning piece of timber lodged on the roof of the city\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/Waterworks" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterworks">waterworks</a>. Within minutes, the interior of the building was engulfed in flames and the building was destroyed. With it, the city\xe2\x80\x99s water mains went dry and the city was helpless.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>152\xe2\x80\x933</span></sup> The fire burned unchecked from building to building, block to block.\n</p><p>Finally, late into the evening of the 9th, it started to rain, but the fire had already started to burn itself out. The fire had spread to the sparsely populated areas of the north side, having consumed the densely populated areas thoroughly.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>158</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chicago-fire2.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Chicago-fire2.jpeg/220px-Chicago-fire2.jpeg" width="220" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Chicago-fire2.jpeg/330px-Chicago-fire2.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6e/Chicago-fire2.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="432" data-file-height="420" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chicago-fire2.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aftermath of the fire, corner of Dearborn and Monroe Streets, 1871</div></div></div>\n<p>Once the fire had ended, the smoldering remains were still too hot for a survey of the damage to be completed for many days. Eventually, the city determined that the fire destroyed an area about 4 miles (6&#160;km) long and averaging <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> mile (1&#160;km) wide, encompassing an area of more than 2,000 acres (809&#160;ha).<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>159</span></sup> Destroyed were more than 73 miles (117&#160;km) of roads, 120 miles (190&#160;km) of sidewalk, 2,000 lampposts, 17,500 buildings, and $222 million in property,<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> which was about a third of the city\'s valuation in 1871.<sup id="cite_ref-Rayfield1997_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rayfield1997-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The value of the property destroyed by the fire is equivalent to $4.00 billion in 2016.\n</p><p>Of the <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Chicago" title="Demographics of Chicago">300,000</a> inhabitants of Chicago in 1871, 100,000 were left homeless, one-third of the population. 120 bodies were recovered, but the death toll may have been as high as 300. The county coroner speculated that an accurate count was impossible, as some victims may have drowned or had been incinerated, leaving no remains.\n</p><p>In the days and weeks following the fire, monetary donations flowed into Chicago from around the country and abroad, along with donations of food, clothing, and other goods. These donations came from individuals, corporations, and cities. <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> gave $450,000 along with clothing and provisions, <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> gave $300,000, and the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation#Court_of_Common_Council" title="City of London Corporation">Common Council of London</a> gave 1,000 guineas, as well as \xe2\x82\xa47,000 from private donations.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>, Scotland (pop.  40,000) a town meeting raised \xc2\xa3518 on the spot.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo</a>, all commercial rivals, donated hundreds and thousands of dollars. <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>, along with other nearby cities, helped by sending fire-fighting equipment.  Additionally, food, clothing and books were brought by train from all over the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Mayor Mason placed the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Relief_and_Aid_Society" title="Chicago Relief and Aid Society">Chicago Relief and Aid Society</a> in charge of the city\xe2\x80\x99s relief efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>162</span></sup>\n</p><p>Operating from the <a href="/wiki/First_Baptist_Congregational_Church" title="First Baptist Congregational Church">First Congregational Church</a>, city officials and <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">aldermen</a> began taking steps to preserve order in Chicago. <a href="/wiki/Price_gouging" title="Price gouging">Price gouging</a> was a key concern, and in one ordinance, the city set the price of bread at 8\xc2\xa2 for a 12-ounce (340&#160;g) loaf.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Public buildings were opened as places of refuge, and <a href="/wiki/Bar" title="Bar">saloons</a> closed at 9 in the evening for the week following the fire. Many people who were left homeless after the incident were never able to get their normal lives back since all their personal papers and belongings burned in the conflagration.\n</p><p>After the fire, A.H. Burgess of London proposed an \xe2\x80\x9cEnglish Book Donation,\xe2\x80\x9d to spur a free library in Chicago, in their sympathy with Chicago over the damages suffered.<sup id="cite_ref-CPL_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPL-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Libraries in Chicago had been private with membership fees. In April 1872, the City Council passed the ordinance to establish the free <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Public_Library" title="Chicago Public Library">Chicago Public Library</a>, starting with the donation from the United Kingdom of more than 8,000 volumes.<sup id="cite_ref-CPL_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPL-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fire also led to questions about development in the United States. Due to Chicago\xe2\x80\x99s rapid expansion at that time, the fire led to Americans reflecting on industrialization. Based on a religious point of view, some said that Americans should return to a more old-fashioned way of life, and that the fire was caused by people ignoring traditional morality. On the other hand, others believed that a lesson to be learned from the fire was that cities needed to improve their building techniques. <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted" title="Frederick Law Olmsted">Frederick Law Olmsted</a> observed that poor building practices in Chicago were a problem:\n</p><p><i>"Chicago had a weakness for "big things," and liked to think that it was outbuilding New York. It did a great deal of commercial advertising in its house-tops. The faults of construction as well as of art in its great showy buildings must have been numerous. Their walls were thin, and were overweighted with gross and coarse misornamentation."</i>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheer_up.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Cheer_up.jpg/220px-Cheer_up.jpg" width="220" height="118" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/Cheer_up.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="309" data-file-height="166" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheer_up.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Chicago Tribune</i> editorial</div></div></div>\n<p>Olmsted also believed that with brick walls, and disciplined firemen and police, the deaths and damage caused would have been much less.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Almost immediately, the city began to rewrite its fire standards, spurred by the efforts of leading insurance executives, and fire-prevention reformers such as <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Ducat" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur C. Ducat">Arthur C. Ducat</a>. Chicago soon developed one of the country\'s leading fire-fighting forces.\n</p><p>Business owners, and land speculators such as <a href="/wiki/Gurdon_Saltonstall_Hubbard" title="Gurdon Saltonstall Hubbard">Gurdon Saltonstall Hubbard</a>, quickly set about rebuilding the city. The first load of lumber for rebuilding was delivered the day the last burning building was extinguished. By the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World&#39;s Columbian Exposition">World\'s Columbian Exposition</a> 22 years later, Chicago hosted more than 21 million visitors. The <a href="/wiki/Palmer_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Palmer House">Palmer House</a> hotel burned to the ground in the fire 13 days after its grand opening. Its developer, Potter Palmer, secured a loan and rebuilt the hotel to higher standards across the street from the original, proclaiming it to be "The World\'s First Fireproof Building".\n</p><p>In 1956, the remaining structures on the original O\'Leary property at 558 W. <a href="/wiki/DeKoven_Street_(Chicago)" title="DeKoven Street (Chicago)">DeKoven Street</a> were torn down for construction of the Chicago Fire Academy, a training facility for Chicago firefighters, known as the Quinn Fire Academy or Chicago Fire Department Training Facility. A bronze <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a> of stylized flames, entitled <i>Pillar of Fire</i> by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Egon_Weiner" title="Egon Weiner">Egon Weiner</a>, was erected on the point of origin in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surviving_structures">Surviving href="/wiki/St._Michael%27s_Church,_Old_Town,_Chicago" title="St. Michael&#39;s Church, Old Town, Chicago">St. Michael\'s Church, Old Town</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Water_Tower" Hudson<sup id="cite_ref-wbez.org_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wbez.org-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>St. Michael\'s Church and the Pumping Station were both gutted in the fire, but their exteriors survived, and the buildings were rebuilt using the surviving walls. Additionally, though the inhabitable portions of the building were destroyed, the bell tower of <a href="/wiki/St._James_Cathedral_(Chicago)" title="St. James Cathedral (Chicago)">St. James Cathedral</a> survived the fire and was incorporated into the rebuilt church. The stones near the top of the tower are still blackened from the soot and smoke.\n</p><p>A couple of wooden cottages on North Cleveland Avenue also survived the blaze.<sup id="cite_ref-wbez.org_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wbez.org-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Panorama_of_Chicago_after_the_1871_Fire">Panorama of Chicago after the 1871 Fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Panorama of Chicago after the 1871 Fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; overflow:hidden; width: -moz-min-content; width: -webkit-min-content; width: min-content; max-width: 100%;"><div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize overflowbugx" style="overflow: auto;"><a href="/wiki/File:Attributed_to_George_N._Barnard_-_Untitled_(Chicago_after_the_Chicago_Fire)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image" title="Attributed to George N. Barnard</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rumors_about_the_precise_start_of_the_fire">Rumors about the precise start of the fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rumors about the precise start of the fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg/220px-Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg/330px-Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg/440px-Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="474" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mrs_OLeary%27s_cow.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1871 illustration from <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper&#39;s Magazine">Harper\'s Magazine</a></i> depicting Mrs. O\'Leary milking the cow</div></div></div>\n<p>Almost from the moment the fire broke out, various theories about its cause began to circulate.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BalesSchwartz_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BalesSchwartz-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>56, 90, 232</span></sup>\n</p><p>The most popular and enduring legend maintains that the fire began in the O\'Leary barn, as Mrs. O\xe2\x80\x99Leary was milking her cow. The cow kicked over a lantern (or an oil lamp in some versions), setting fire to the barn. The O\'Leary family denied this, stating that they were in bed before the fire started, but stories of the cow began to spread across the city. <a href="/wiki/Catherine_O%27Leary" title="Catherine O&#39;Leary">Catherine O\'Leary</a> seemed the perfect <a href="/wiki/Scapegoat" title="Scapegoat">scapegoat</a>: she was a poor, Irish Catholic immigrant. During the latter half of the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Anti-Irish_sentiment" title="Anti-Irish sentiment">anti-Irish sentiment</a> was strong throughout the United States and in Chicago. This was intensified as a result of the growing political power of the city\'s Irish population.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldMiller_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldMiller-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>442</span></sup> Furthermore, the United States had been distrustful of Catholics (or "<a href="/wiki/Papists" class="mw-redirect" title="Papists">papists</a>", as they were often called) since its beginning, carrying over attitudes in England in the 17th century<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <i>(main article: <a href="/wiki/Anti-Catholicism_in_the_United_States" title="Anti-Catholicism in the United States">Anti-Catholicism in the United States</a>)</i>; as an Irish Catholic, Mrs. O\'Leary was a target of both anti-Catholic and anti-Irish sentiment. This story was circulating in Chicago even before the flames had died out, and it was noted in the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a>\'</i>s first post-fire issue. In 1893 the reporter Michael Ahern retracted the "cow-and-lantern" story, admitting it was fabricated, but even his confession was unable to put the legend to rest.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Although the O\'Learys were never officially charged with starting the fire, the story became so engrained in local lore that Chicago\'s city council officially exonerated them\xe2\x80\x94and the cow\xe2\x80\x94in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-Mills1997_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mills1997-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Amateur historian Richard Bales has suggested the fire started when Daniel "Pegleg" Sullivan, who first reported the fire, ignited hay in the barn while trying to steal milk.<sup id="cite_ref-BalesSchwartz_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BalesSchwartz-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>127\xe2\x80\x93130</span></sup> Part of Bales\' evidence includes an account by Sullivan who claimed in an inquiry before the Fire Department of Chicago on November 25, 1871 that he saw the fire coming through the side of the barn and ran across <a href="/wiki/DeKoven_Street_(Chicago)" title="DeKoven Street (Chicago)">DeKoven Street</a> to free the animals from the barn, one of which included a cow owned by Sullivan\'s mother.<sup id="cite_ref-Bales2004_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bales2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Bales\' account does not have consensus. The Chicago Public Library staff criticized his account in their web page on the fire.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Despite this, the Chicago city council was convinced of Bales\' argument and stated that the actions of Sullivan on that day should be scrutinized after the O\'Leary family was exonerated in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-Mills1997_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mills1997-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Anthony DeBartolo reported evidence in two articles the <i>Chicago Tribune</i> (October 8, 1997 and March 3, 1998, reprinted in <i>Hyde Park Media</i>) suggesting that Louis M. Cohn may have started the fire during a craps game.<sup id="cite_ref-DeBartoloHydePark_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeBartoloHydePark-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DeBartoloOct1997_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeBartoloOct1997-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DeBartoloMar1998_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeBartoloMar1998-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> According to Cohn, on the night of the fire, he was gambling in the O\'Learys\' barn with <a href="/wiki/James_Patrick_O%27Leary" title="James Patrick O&#39;Leary">one of their sons</a> and some other neighborhood boys. When Mrs. O\'Leary came out to the barn to chase the gamblers away at around 9:00, they knocked over a lantern in their flight, although Cohn states that he paused long enough to scoop up the money. Following his death in 1942, Cohn bequeathed $35,000 which was assigned by his executors to the <a href="/wiki/Medill_School_of_Journalism" title="Medill School of Journalism">Medill School of Journalism</a> at <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a>. The bequest was given to the school on September 28, 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-DeBartoloOct1997_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeBartoloOct1997-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The argument is not universally accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An alternative theory, first suggested in 1882 by <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_L._Donnelly" title="Ignatius L. Donnelly">Ignatius L. Donnelly</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Ragnarok:_The_Age_of_Fire_and_Gravel" title="Ragnarok: The Age of Fire and Gravel">Ragnarok: The Age of Fire and Gravel</a></i>, is that the fire was caused by a <a href="/wiki/Meteor_shower" title="Meteor shower">meteor shower</a>. This was described as "A fringe theory" concering <a href="/wiki/Biela%27s_Comet#Meteoric_impacts" title="Biela&#39;s Comet">Biela\'s Comet</a>. At a 2004 conference of the Aerospace Corporation and the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Aeronautics_and_Astronautics" title="American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics">American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics</a>, engineer and physicist Robert Wood suggested that the fire began when <a href="/wiki/Biela%27s_Comet" title="Biela&#39;s Comet">Biela\'s Comet</a> broke up over the Midwest. That four large fires took place, all on the same day, all on the shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a> (see <a href="#Related_events">Related Events</a>), suggests a common root cause. Eyewitnesses reported sighting spontaneous ignitions, lack of smoke, "balls of fire" falling from the sky, and blue flames. According to Wood, these accounts suggest that the fires were caused by the <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> that is commonly found in comets.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">Meteorites</a> are not known to start or spread fires and are cool to the touch after reaching the ground, so this theory has not found favor in the scientific community.<sup id="cite_ref-Calfee_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Calfee-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NASA_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Methane-air mixtures become flammable only when the methane concentration exceeds 5%, at which point the mixtures also become explosive, a situation unlikely to occur from meteorites.<sup id="cite_ref-ToolBox_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ToolBox-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FactSheet_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FactSheet-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Methane gas is lighter than air and thus does not accumulate near the ground;<sup id="cite_ref-FactSheet_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FactSheet-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> any localized pockets of methane in the open air rapidly dissipate. Moreover, if a fragment of an icy comet were to strike the Earth, the most likely outcome, due to the low <a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" class="mw-redirect" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> of such bodies, would be for it to disintegrate in the upper atmosphere, leading to an <a href="/wiki/Air_burst" title="Air burst">air burst</a> explosion analogous to that of the <a href="/wiki/Tunguska_event" title="Tunguska event">Tunguska event</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Beech_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beech-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A common cause for the fires in the Midwest in the fall of 1871 is that the area had suffered through a tinder-dry summer, so that winds from the front that moved in that evening were capable of generating rapidly expanding blazes from available ignition sources, which were plentiful in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-Gess_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gess-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BalesSchwartz_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BalesSchwartz-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>111</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_events">Related events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Related events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On that hot, dry, and windy autumn day, three other major fires occurred along the shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a> at the same time as the Great Chicago Fire. Some 250 miles (400&#160;km) to the north, the <a href="/wiki/Peshtigo_Fire" title="Peshtigo Fire">Peshtigo Fire</a> consumed the town of <a href="/wiki/Peshtigo,_Wisconsin" title="Peshtigo, Wisconsin">Peshtigo, Wisconsin</a>, along with a dozen other villages. It killed 1,200 to 2,500 people and charred approximately 1.5 million acres (6,000&#160;km\xc2\xb2). The Peshtigo Fire remains the deadliest in American history<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> but the remoteness of the region meant it was little noticed at the time, due to the fact that one of the first things that burned were the telegraph lines to Green Bay.<sup id="cite_ref-sun_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sun-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Across the lake to the east, the town of <a href="/wiki/Holland,_Michigan" title="Holland, Michigan">Holland, Michigan</a>, and other nearby areas burned to the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilkins2012_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkins2012-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Some 100 miles (160&#160;km) to the north of Holland, the lumbering community of <a href="/wiki/Manistee,_Michigan" title="Manistee, Michigan">Manistee</a> also went up in flames<sup id="cite_ref-Co1882_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Co1882-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> in what became known as <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Michigan_Fire" class="mw-redirect" title="The Great Michigan Fire">The Great Michigan Fire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilkins2012_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkins2012-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Farther east, along the shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Port_Huron_Fire_of_1871" title="Port Huron Fire of 1871">Port Huron Fire</a> swept through <a href="/wiki/Port_Huron,_Michigan" title="Port Huron, Michigan">Port Huron, Michigan</a> and much of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/The_Thumb" title="The Thumb">"Thumb"</a>. On October 9, 1871, a fire swept through the city of <a href="/wiki/Urbana,_Illinois" title="Urbana, Illinois">Urbana, Illinois</a>, 140 miles (230&#160;km) south of Chicago, destroying portions of its downtown area.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Ontario" title="Windsor, Ontario">Windsor, Ontario</a>, likewise burned on October 12.<sup id="cite_ref-Timeline_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Singapore,_Michigan" title="Singapore, Michigan">Singapore, Michigan</a>, provided a large portion of the lumber to rebuild Chicago. As a result, the area was so heavily deforested that the land deteriorated into barren sand dunes that buried the town, and the town had to be abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Great_Chicago_Fire&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>.
