counties of <a href="/wiki/West_Berkshire" title="West Berkshire">West Berkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" The name <i>North Wessex Downs</i> is not a traditional one, the area covered being better known by various overlapping local names, including the <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Downs" title="Berkshire Downs">Berkshire Downs</a>, the North <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_Downs" title="Hampshire Downs">Hampshire Downs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White_Horse_Hills" class="mw-redirect" title="White Horse Hills">White Horse Hills</a>, the <b>Lambourn Downs</b>, the <b>Marlborough Downs</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Pewsey" title="Vale of Pewsey">Vale of Pewsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Savernake_Forest" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Wessex_Downs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The AONB covers an area of some 1,730&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (670&#160;sq&#160;mi). It takes the form of a horseshoe on its side, with the open end facing east, surrounding the town of <a href="/wiki/Newbury,_Berkshire" title="Newbury, Berkshire">Newbury</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Kennet" title="River Kennet">River Kennet</a> watershed. The northern arm reaches as far east as the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a> in mid-<a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> and as far north as <a href="/wiki/Didcot" title="Didcot">Didcot</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Oxfordshire" title="South Oxfordshire">South Oxfordshire</a>, whilst the southern arm similarly reaches <a href="/wiki/Basingstoke" title="Basingstoke">Basingstoke</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>. The western extreme of the AONB reaches as far as <a href="/wiki/Calne" title="Calne">Calne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devizes" title="Devizes">Devizes</a>. The highest points are the 297&#160;m (974&#160;ft) summit of <a href="/wiki/Walbury_Hill" title="Walbury Hill">Walbury Hill</a>, situated southeast of <a href="/wiki/Hungerford" title="Hungerford">Hungerford</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Berkshire" title="West Berkshire">West Berkshire</a> (and the highest point in southern England east of the <a href="/wiki/Mendip_Hills" title="Mendip Hills">Mendip Hills</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Milk_Hill" title="Milk Hill">Milk Hill</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tan_Hill,_Wiltshire" title="Tan Hill, Wiltshire">Tan Hill</a> plateau northeast of <a href="/wiki/Devizes" title="Devizes">Devizes</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, at 295&#160;m (968&#160;ft) above sea level.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb href="/wiki/Lardon_Chase" class="mw-redirect" title="Lardon Chase">Lardon Chase</a> within the North Wessex Downs AONB faces across the <a href="/wiki/Goring_Gap" title="Goring Gap">Goring Gap</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Chilterns_AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="Chilterns AONB">Chilterns AONB</a> on the other side of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>. From here working anti-clockwise around the horseshoe, the Berkshire Downs have a steep <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">scarp</a> slope facing north over the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_White_Horse" title="Vale of White Horse">Vale of White Horse</a> and a gentler <a href="/wiki/Dip_slope" title="Dip slope">dip slope</a> facing south into the valley of the Kennet. This area includes the horse-racing village of <a href="/wiki/Lambourn" title="Lambourn">Lambourn</a> and is hence sometimes known as the Lambourn Downs. Beyond the town of <a href="/wiki/Marlborough,_Wiltshire" title="Marlborough, Wiltshire">Marlborough</a> the downs (now called the Marlborough Downs) sweep in a semicircle to the south around the headwaters of the River Kennet, with the Vale of Pewsey cutting through these downs carrying the headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Hampshire" title="River Avon, Hampshire">Hampshire River Avon</a>. Here too can be found the wooded area of <a href="/wiki/Savernake_Forest" title="Savernake Forest">Savernake Forest</a>. Finally, the highest stretch of the Downs runs east along the Berkshire-Hampshire border on the opposite side of the River Kennet from the Berkshire Downs. Again the scarp slope is to the north (facing down in the valley of the Kennet) and the dip slope is to the south into Hampshire.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">downland</a> is part of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_England_Chalk_Formation" title="Southern England Chalk Formation">Southern England Chalk Formation</a> which runs from <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> in the west to <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> in the east and also includes the <a href="/wiki/Dorset_Downs" title="Dorset Hills">Chiltern Hills</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_Downs" title="North Downs">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Downs" title="South Downs">South Downs</a>.\n</p><p>The area is a site of scientific interest in numerous fields and has an internationally important habitat for early <a href="/wiki/Gentian" class="mw-redirect" title="Gentian">gentian</a>. <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geologically</a>, its chalk downs, <a href="/wiki/Dry_valley" title="Dry valley">dry valleys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarsen" title="Sarsen">sarsen</a> outcrops are of note, the last in the area around Marlborough providing material for many of the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> sites in the area such as <a href="/wiki/Avebury_Henge" class="mw-redirect" title="Avebury Henge">Avebury Henge</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Wessex_Downs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">Horse racing</a> forms a major industry in the area, largely because of the good quality turf that comes with the chalk underlay, and much of upland area is made over to gallops and other training areas. Several of the upland villages, and especially the large village of <a href="/wiki/Lambourn" title="Lambourn">Lambourn</a>, are home to major racing <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stables</a>. Other villages with strong horse racing connections include <a href="/wiki/Avebury,_Wiltshire" title="Avebury, Wiltshire">Beckhampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingsclere" title="Kingsclere">Kingsclere</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Ilsley" title="West Ilsley">West Ilsley</a>. The term <a href="/wiki/Steeplechase_(horse_racing)" title="Steeplechase (horse racing)">steeplechase</a> originated in this area,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> a steeplechase originally being a race between two villages, navigated by reference to the church <a href="/wiki/Steeple_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Steeple (architecture)">steeples</a> visible across the rolling downs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span
