Venice.</div></div></div>\n<p>From the latter part of the 15th century, <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> had a distinctive, thriving, and influential artistic environment. Beginning with the work of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Bellini" title="Giovanni Bellini">Giovanni Bellini</a> (c. 1430\xe2\x80\x931516) and his brother <a href="/wiki/Gentile_Bellini" title="Gentile Bellini">Gentile Bellini</a> (c. 1429\xe2\x80\x931507) and their workshops, the major artists of the <b>Venetian school</b> included <a href="/wiki/Giorgione" title="Giorgione">Giorgione</a> (c. 1477\xe2\x80\x931510), <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> (c. 1489\xe2\x80\x931576), <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a> (1518\xe2\x80\x931594), <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Veronese" title="Paolo Veronese">Paolo Veronese</a> (1528\xe2\x80\x931588) and <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Bassano" title="Jacopo Bassano">Jacopo Bassano</a> (1510\xe2\x80\x931592) and his brothers. Considered to give primacy of colour over line,<sup id="cite_ref-PradoGuide_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PradoGuide-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the tradition of the Venetian school contrasted with the <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a> prevalent in the rest of Italy. The Venetian style exerted great influence upon the subsequent development of <a href="/wiki/Western_painting" title="Western painting">Western painting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardner_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_school_(art)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the early-15th century, Venetian art was dominated by the earlier styles arising from its <a href="/wiki/Byzantine" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> links,<sup id="cite_ref-PradoGuide_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PradoGuide-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> as exemplified by the work of the <a href="/wiki/Vivarini" title="Vivarini">Vivarini</a> family. From the late-15th century, Venetian painting developed through links with <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Andrea Mantegna</a> (1431\xe2\x80\x931506) (from nearby <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a>) and of a visit by <a href="/wiki/Antonello_da_Messina" title="Antonello da Messina">Antonello da Messina</a> (c. 1430\xe2\x80\x931479), who introduced the <a href="/wiki/Oil_painting" title="Oil painting">oil painting</a> technique of <a href="/wiki/Early_Netherlandish_painting" title="Early Netherlandish painting">Early Netherlandish painting</a>, probably acquired through his training in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Another external factor was the visit by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, who was particularly influential on Giorgione.<sup id="cite_ref-PradoGuide_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PradoGuide-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During his long career, Bellini has been credited with creating the Venetian style.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardner_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> From his earlier works, such as his <i><a href="/wiki/Madonna_of_the_Small_Trees" title="Madonna of the Small Trees">Madonna of the Small Trees</a></i> (c. 1487) which largely reflect the linear approach of Mantegna, he later developed a softer style, where glowing colours are used to represent form and suggest an atmospheric haze. Applying this approach in his <i><a href="/wiki/San_Zaccaria_Altarpiece" title="San Zaccaria Altarpiece">San Zaccaria Altarpiece</a></i> (1505), the high viewpoint, the uncluttered and interconnected figures arranged in space, and the subtle gestures all combine to form a tranquil yet majestic image.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardner_a_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner_a-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> With such works he has been described as reaching the <a href="/wiki/High_Renaissance" title="High Renaissance">High Renaissance</a><sup id="cite_ref-Gardner_a_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner_a-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> ideals, and certainly expresses the key distinctive factors of the Venetian school.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Giorgione_and_Titian">Giorgione and Titian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_school_(art)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Giorgione and Titian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Frari_(Venice)_nave_left_-_Altar_of_Madona_di_Ca%27Pesaro.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" data-file-width="3928" data-file-height="6140" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Frari_(Venice)_nave_left_-_Altar_of_Madona_di_Ca%27Pesaro.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian\'s</a> early and dramatically composed <i><a href="/wiki/Pesaro_Madonna" title="Pesaro Madonna">Pesaro Madonna</a></i> (1526), with rich Venetian colouring.</div></div></div>\n<p>Giorgione and Titian were both apprentices at Bellini\'s workshop. They collaborated on numerous paintings, and their styles could be so similar that it is difficult to conclusively assign authorship. A speciality of Giorgione\'s were idyllic <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_(utopia)" title="Arcadia (utopia)">Arcadian</a> scenes, with an example being his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Philosophers" title="The Three Philosophers">Three Philosophers</a></i>, and this element was adopted by his master Bellini, who increased the landscape in his many Madonnas,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and by Titian in work like <i><a href="/wiki/Pastoral_Concert" title="Pastoral Concert">Pastoral Concert</a></i> (1508) and <i><a href="/wiki/Sacred_and_Profane_Love" title="Sacred and Profane Love">Sacred and Profane Love</a></i> (1515). This emphasis on nature as a setting was a major contribution of the Venetian School.\n</p><p>Titian, through his long and productive life, with a wide variety of themes and subjects was the most influential and greatest of all the Venetian School painters.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His early <i><a href="/wiki/Pesaro_Madonna" title="Pesaro Madonna">Pesaro Madonna</a></i> (1519\xe2\x80\x931528) shows a bold new composition for such a traditional religious subject,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> putting the focal point of the Madonna off from the centre and on a steep diagonal. Colours are used to enliven the painting, but also to unify the composition, such as by the large red flag on the left counterbalancing the red in the Madonna<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and such skilful and sumptuous use of colour became a hallmark of the Venetian style.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tizian_102.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Tizian_102.jpg/300px-Tizian_102.jpg" width="300" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Tizian_102.jpg/450px-Tizian_102.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Tizian_102.jpg/600px-Tizian_102.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1665" data-file-height="1196" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tizian_102.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian\'s</a> reclining <i><a href="/wiki/Venus_of_Urbino" title="Venus of Urbino">Venus</a></i> (1538) an erotic work, with a lapdog on the right, balancing the face on the left.</div></div></div>\n<p>Although pre-figured by the <i><a href="/wiki/Sleeping_Venus_(Giorgione)" title="Sleeping Venus (Giorgione)">Sleeping Venus</a></i> (completed by Titian after Giorgione\'s death in 1510) Titian is credited with establishing the reclining female <a href="/wiki/Art_nude" class="mw-redirect" title="Art nude">nude</a> as an important subgenre in art. Using mythological subjects, works such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Venus_of_Urbino" title="Venus of Urbino">Venus of Urbino</a></i> (1538) richly depict the fabrics and other textures, and use a composition that is carefully controlled by organising colours. As an example, in this painting the diagonal of the nude is matched by the opposite diagonal between the red of the cushions in the front with the red skirts of the woman in the background.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\nWith such paintings, readily transported by virtue of being oils on canvas, Titian became famous, and helped establish a reputation for Venetian art. Possession of such paintings symbolised luxurious wealth,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and for his skills in portraiture he was sought by powerful, rich individuals, such as in his long relationship working for Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> of Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_developments">Later developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Accademia_-_Miracle_of_the_Slave_by_Tintoretto.jpg/300px-Accademia_-_Miracle_of_the_Slave_by_Tintoretto.jpg" data-file-height="4912" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accademia_-_Miracle_of_the_Slave_by_Tintoretto.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Miracle_of_the_Slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Miracle of the Slave">Miracle of the Slave</a></i> (1548), one of a trio of Tintoretto works on St Mark, the patron saint of Venice.</div></div></div>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a> is sometimes classified as a <a href="/wiki/Mannerist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mannerist">Mannerist</a> artist,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> he also incorporates Venetian and individualistic aspects. \nIn his <i><a href="/wiki/Miracle_of_the_Slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Miracle of the Slave">Miracle of the Slave</a></i> (1548), the Mannerist features include the crowded scene, the twisting linking of figures (as in the central figures, from the foreshortened slave on the ground to the miraculous figure of St. Mark in the sky, through the turbaned, grey-robed figure), and the drama in the gestures and poses. But the colouring maintains the warm reds, golds and greens of the Venetian school, and the figures are arranged in real three-dimensional space, in contrast to the more compressed compositions of many Mannerist works, and with its  intensely theatrical, stage-like display his painting is a forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_painting" title="Baroque painting">Baroque</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following Tintoretto came <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Veronese" title="Paolo Veronese">Paolo Veronese</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Bassano" title="Jacopo Bassano">Bassano</a>. These are a few of the most outstanding in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_painters_and_architects_of_Venice" title="List of painters and architects of Venice">great number of artists in the Venetian tradition</a>, many originally from outside the Republic\'s territory.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venetian_school_(art)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Venetian school had a great influence of subsequent painting, and the history of later Western art has been described as a dialogue between the more intellectual and sculptural/linear approach of the <a href="/wiki/Florentine_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Florentine School">Florentine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Roman</a> traditions, and the more sensual, poetic, and pleasure-seeking of the colourful Venetian school.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Specifically through the presence of Titian in Spain, the Venetian style influenced later Spanish art, including that of <a href="/wiki/Vel%C3%A1zquez" class="mw-redirect" title="Vel\xc3\xa1zquez">Vel\xc3\xa1zquez</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and through <a href="/wiki/Rubens" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubens">Rubens</a> was more broadly transmitted through the rest of Europe.\n</p><p>In the 18th century Venetian painting had a final flowering in <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Tiepolo" title="Giovanni Battista Tiepolo">Tiepolo</a>\'s decorative painting and <a href="/wiki/Canaletto" title="Canaletto">Canaletto</a>\'s and <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Guardi" title="Francesco Guardi">Guardi\'s</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Veduta" title="Veduta">veduta</a></i> or panoramic views, mostly of the city itself. The extinction of the Republic by French Revolutionary armies in 1797 effectively brought the distinctive Venetian style to an end; it had at least arguably outlasted its rival <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> in that respect.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
