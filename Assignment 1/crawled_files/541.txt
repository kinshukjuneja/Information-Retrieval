colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><i><b>The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society</b></i> is the sixth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" class="mw-redirect" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by the English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> group <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">the Kinks</a>, released in November 1968. It was the last album by the original quartet (<a href="/wiki/Ray_Davies" title="Ray Davies">Ray Davies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Davies" title="Dave Davies">Dave Davies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Quaife" title="Pete Quaife">Pete Quaife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mick_Avory" title="Mick Avory">Mick Avory</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>), as bassist Quaife left the group in early 1969. A collection of vignettes of English life,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society</i> was assembled from songs written and recorded over the previous two years.<sup id="cite_ref-VGPS1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VGPS1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the record is widely considered one of the most influential and important works by the Kinks, it failed to chart upon release, selling about 100,000 copies. In 2003 the album was ranked number 255 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine\'s list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" class="mw-redirect" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" See <a href="/wiki/Help:Media" title="Help:Media">media help</a>.</i></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Comberton_village_green.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Comberton_village_green.jpg/200px-Comberton_village_green.jpg" width="200" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Comberton_village_green.jpg/300px-Comberton_village_green.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Comberton_village_green.jpg/400px-Comberton_village_green.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="938" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Comberton_village_green.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A village green, a communal open-air green park space usually located in a village or rural setting, used for relaxation and recreation (sports such as <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(sport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cricket (sport)">cricket</a> are common on the green); a meeting place for the people of a village during times of celebration or public ceremonies.</div></div></div>\n<p>The November 1966 track "<a href="/wiki/Village_Green_(song)" title="Village Green (song)">Village Green</a>" was inspired by a visit to <a href="/wiki/Devon,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Devon, England">Devon, England</a> in late 1966. Davies has also stated that <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">Dylan Thomas</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Under_Milk_Wood" title="Under Milk Wood">Under Milk Wood</a></i> was an influence. The song suggests a broad theme, "I miss the <a href="/wiki/Village_green" title="Village green">village green</a>, and all the simple people", a world which could be extended by adding an "<a href="/wiki/Animal_Farm_(song)" title="Animal Farm (song)">Animal Farm</a>", which is "a dirty old shack that we called our home", and "<a href="/wiki/Sitting_by_the_Riverside" title="Sitting by the Riverside">Sitting by the Riverside</a>". It was populated with <a href="/wiki/Character_sketch" title="Character sketch">character sketches</a> of the writer\'s childhood sweetheart Daisy and of Walter, once a close friend, both now married, of <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Thunder_(song)" title="Johnny Thunder (song)">Johnny Thunder</a>, the local hoodlum, and <a href="/wiki/Monica_(song)" title="Monica (song)">Monica</a>, a prostitute.<sup id="cite_ref-33_and_1/3_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33_and_1/3-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Davies did not compose the songs to fit a predetermined theme of the album but a certain commonality develops in his lyric interests of the time. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> described <i>Village Green</i> as a "<a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> lamenting the passing of old-fashioned English traditions."<sup id="cite_ref-VGPS1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VGPS1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The intentional Englishness of the lyrics\' imagery<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> may have partly been due to The Kinks\' exclusion from the USA.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>But the village green was also a metaphor, a "Walt Disney" fantasy of an ideal protected place of retreat<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> that, as Davies has since opined, may rather have belonged on a solo album or personal diary; "It\xe2\x80\x99s all in my head, probably.... Everybody\xe2\x80\x99s got their own village green, somewhere you go to when the world gets too much."<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Davies, who had suffered mental exhaustion himself, isolated and conscious of "the hole I was in" \xe2\x80\x93 to either be a hit machine or not to exist,<sup id="cite_ref-Savage,_Jon_1984,_p._102_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage,_Jon_1984,_p._102-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> sings; "This world is big and wild and half insane... It\'s a hard, hard world if it gets you down \xe2\x80\x93 Dreams often fade and die in a bad, bad world" with "Everybody pushing one another around... all the people who think they got problems.. don\'t let it get you down". He advises one friend ("<a href="/wiki/Starstruck_(The_Kinks_song)" title="Starstruck (The Kinks song)">Starstruck</a>"); "you\'re a victim of bright city lights and your mind is not right.... running around like you\'re crazy... out on your feet \xe2\x80\x93 It\'s gonna drive you insane because the world\'s not so tame".\n</p><p>The writer admits; "I sought fame, and so I left the village green." But he has somewhere to return to; "I\'ll take you where real animals are playing, and people are real people not just playing. It\'s a quiet, quiet life". The village green offers a place to be natural, a place of solitude, while the "city" offers only artifice, haste, competition and the dangers of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. The animal farm, Ray Davies said, "was just me thinking everybody else is mad and we are all animals anyway \xe2\x80\x93 which is really the idea of the whole album."<sup id="cite_ref-33_and_1/3_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33_and_1/3-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As well as to nature, the lyrics make many references to Davies\' own city childhood; "The real village green is a combination of north London places: the little green near my childhood home in <a href="/wiki/Fortis_Green" title="Fortis Green">Fortis Green</a>... That little green is where we played football, and where we stayed \xe2\x80\x99til it was dark. There was mystery there... The record\xe2\x80\x99s about lost childhood, but also being a kid." Similarly "<a href="/wiki/Do_You_Remember_Walter%3F" title="Do You Remember Walter?">Do You Remember Walter?</a>" is based upon a real-life childhood friend,<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> "Sitting By The Riverside" recalls how Davies "went fishing a lot when I was about eight",<sup id="cite_ref-33_and_1/3_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33_and_1/3-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the eventual title track, one of the last written and recorded (in August 1968), is full of references to children\'s entertainments of his youth; comic-strips, cinema, radio and theatre, and two lyrics, "<a href="/wiki/Phenomenal_Cat" title="Phenomenal Cat">Phenomenal Cat</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Wicked_Annabella" title="Wicked Annabella">Wicked Annabella</a>", resemble children\'s fantasy literature.\n</p><p>"Remember when the world was young" \xe2\x80\x93 childhood nostalgia brings with it a sense of "things about to be swept away, ideals that can never be kept".<sup id="cite_ref-Hasted_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The village green is an ideal past, a place of memory; Walter is "just an echo of a world I knew so long ago... but memories of people can remain". Two songs "<a href="/wiki/People_Take_Pictures_of_Each_Other" title="People Take Pictures of Each Other">People Take Pictures of Each Other</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Picture_Book_(song)" title="Picture Book (song)">Picture Book</a>") discuss photographs, "pictures of things as they used to be", that people keep as a "moment to last them for ever"; a "picture of me when I was just three, sucking my thumb by the old oak tree" by which he would first kiss Daisy. Davies notes the absurdity of those people\'s trying "to prove that they really existed", yet imagines a future in which to "picture yourself when you\'re getting old, sat by the fireside" with the family photo album.\n</p><p>But this sense of the past makes the village houses into "rare antiquities", he is "the last of the good old renegades. All my friends are all middle class and grey, but I live in a museum, so I\'m okay." Walter is "fat and married", Davies remembers "how we said we\'d fight the world so we\'d be free" and goes on believing that "one day we\'ll be free". Like Johnny Thunder he has "vowed that he would never, ever end up like the rest". His memory connects him to his ideals as well as his individuality, his creativity, his natural life, even if it leaves him "appallingly out of sync with the times",<sup id="cite_ref-Savage,_Jon_1984,_p._102_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage,_Jon_1984,_p._102-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> in which the trend was to speak of sweeping away the entire social system, taking a nuanced view, "preserving the old things", as well as "protecting the new", yet still condemning the worst of the new \xe2\x80\x93 skyscrapers and office blocks \xe2\x80\x93 as well as the old. This theme would be further developed on the following album, <i><a href="/wiki/Arthur_(Or_the_Decline_and_Fall_of_the_British_Empire)" title="Arthur (Or the Decline and Fall of the British Empire)">Arthur (Or the Decline and Fall of the British Empire)</a></i>.\n</p><p>The village green is a place of personal memories, ideals and allegiances. In "<a href="/wiki/Last_of_the_Steam-Powered_Trains" title="Last of the Steam-Powered Trains">Last of the Steam-Powered Trains</a>" Davies compares himself with the 1968 decommissioning of the last <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotives_of_British_Railways" title="Steam locomotives of British Railways">steam locomotives of British Railways</a>, asserting his personal loyalty to the temporarily out-of-fashion <a href="/wiki/British_blues_boom" class="mw-redirect" title="British blues boom">British blues boom</a> by basing the song on another steam-driven tune, <a href="/wiki/Howlin%27_Wolf" title="Howlin&#39; Wolf">Howlin\' Wolf</a>\'s "<a href="/wiki/Smokestack_Lightnin%27" class="mw-redirect" title="Smokestack Lightnin&#39;">Smokestack Lightnin\'</a>", a rhythm and blues standard performed by many groups including The Kinks and later commenting "it\'s really about not having anything in common with people".<sup id="cite_ref-33_and_1/3_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33_and_1/3-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>But the mystery of the village green also offers an eternity that belittles problems such as public embarrassment ("<a href="/wiki/All_of_My_Friends_Were_There" title="All of My Friends Were There">All of My Friends Were There</a>"), because "When I feel that the world is too much for me I think of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Sky_(song)" title="Big Sky (song)">Big Sky</a>, and nothing matters much to me". Elsewhere, he is "Spreading my arms to the open wide. Now I am free... I can close my eyes", and "the sky is wide" \xe2\x80\x93 of which last Davies has said; "to me, the whole record is the way I sing that line.".<sup id="cite_ref-God_Save_the_Kinks_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-God_Save_the_Kinks-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Kinks_Are_the_Village_Green_Preservation_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The sessions for <i>Village Green</i> took place in a rough period for the group. In early 1968 legal and family issues slowed Ray Davies\' songwriting and the song "<a href="/wiki/Wonderboy_(The_Kinks_song)" title="Wonderboy (The Kinks song)">Wonderboy</a>", released in April, was the first single since The Kinks\' success not to enter the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Musical_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="New Musical Express">NME</a></i> Top 30.<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_114_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_114-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The first songs recorded, in May<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_114_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_114-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> included "Misty Water" (an outtake), "Picture Book", and "Days", intended for the group\'s next single release.<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_114_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_114-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> After laying down versions of these the group departed for the Netherlands on 18 and 19 May<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_114_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_114-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> then returned to the studio to continue work on "Days".<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_118_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_118-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On 27 May a new song, "Pictures in the Sand", was recorded and "Days" was revised to the final version, which was released on 24 July, backed with "<a href="/wiki/She%27s_Got_Everything_(The_Kinks_song)" title="She&#39;s Got Everything (The Kinks song)">She\'s Got Everything</a>", an outtake from 1966 used due to a lack of newer finished material.<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_118_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_118-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The single quickly climbed to number 14 on the <i>NME</i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Shortly after, Dave Davies stated that the group\'s upcoming LP would be called <i>Village Green</i> and revolve around a town and its people. He added that it was to be "the best thing we\'ve ever done."<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_118_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_118-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In early August the band focused on completing the LP, tentatively titled "Village Green".<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_118_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_118-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the exception of the early "<a href="/wiki/Village_Green_(song)" title="Village Green (song)">Village Green</a>", strings and woodwind were played on the <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">Mellotron</a> by Ray Davies and <a href="/wiki/Nicky_Hopkins" title="Nicky Hopkins">Nicky Hopkins</a>. Hopkins contributed significantly to the album: he later stated that he provided "about seventy per cent of the work" on the keyboards, and developed a lifelong grudge when Davies apparently credited himself for the majority of the keyboard playing.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The last track was completed on 12 August and twelve mixed-down songs were delivered to Pye. Release was scheduled for 27 September.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_39-42_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller_39-42-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Ray Davies then asked the label to postpone the album\'s release in order to rework songs and add new material,<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_118_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_118-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> wishing Pye to expand <i>Village Green</i> into a double album. Pye refused, but allowed Davies to produce a new fifteen-track edition.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_39-42_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller_39-42-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Davies agreed and the band returned to the studio to lay down "Big Sky" and "Last of The Steam Powered Trains".<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_39-42_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller_39-42-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By October mixing was complete for the expanded 15-track album, now named <i>The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_118_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_118-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and it was released on 22 November (the same day <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> released their <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_(album)" title="The Beatles (album)">The Beatles</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_121_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_121-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Andy Miller commented on these changes in his book about the album;\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Davies was a perfectionist, and by this stage his perfectionism was verging on the neurotic, indulged by a management and record company who hoped the Kinks\' main man would soon recapture his hit making form. In a sense, he was also reluctant to finish the project, describing it as "a pet dream". The album was already highly personal; now it represented ... a decisive break in \'the hit machine\'.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Pye had already shipped tapes to some of its foreign subsidiaries before Davies\' revision so that a twelve-track LP was released in France, Italy, Sweden, Norway and New Zealand. These have since become rare and collectible.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_39-42_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller_39-42-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Upon its release <i>The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society</i> was greeted with almost unanimously positive reviews from both UK and US rock critics but failed to sell strongly, an estimated 100,000 copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-VGPS1_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VGPS1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Miller_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Despite this the album has become the Kinks\' best-selling original record.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The album did not have a popular single ("Starstruck" was released in North America and continental Europe, but failed to chart anywhere but the Netherlands).<sup id="cite_ref-VGPS1_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VGPS1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Although it was commercially unsuccessful <i>Village Green</i>, upon its US release in January 1969, was embraced by the new underground rock press, particularly in the United States where the Kinks\' status as a cult band began to grow.<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_125_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_125-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> called it "the best album of the year so far",<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_125_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_125-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Circus</i> magazine ran an article under the heading "Kinks \xe2\x80\x93 Unhip But Original", which stated: "The Kinks are backdated, cut off from the mainstream of pop progression. Just the same they\'re originals and now have a fine new album out".<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_125_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_125-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Boston\'s underground paper <i>Fusion</i>, a review stated, "The Kinks continue, despite the odds, the bad press and their demonstrated lot, to come across... Their persistence is dignified, their virtues are stoic. The Kinks are forever, only for now in modern dress".<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_125_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_125-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Paul Williams in <i>Rolling Stone</i> wrote a review that heaped praise on <i>Village Green</i>, saying "I\'ve played [<i>Village Green</i>] twice since it arrived here this afternoon, and already the songs are slipping into my mind, each new hearing is a combined joy of renewal and discovery. Such a joy, to make new friends! And each and every song Ray Davies has written is a different friend to me."<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_125_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_125-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The record was not without its critics, however. In the student paper <i>California Tech</i>, one writer commented that it was "schmaltz rock", and that it is "without imagination, poorly arranged, and a poor copy of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Hinman_125_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinman_125-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The LP went virtually unnoticed in the UK, receiving only a single review in <i>Disc</i>. The nameless reviewer commented that "[Davies has managed to bypass] everything <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelic</a> and electronic ... The Kinks may not be on the crest of the pop wave at these days, but Ray Davies will remain one of our finest composers for many years."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The record soon achieved a cult following, and remains popular today. Kinks fan and British musician <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> later said that "For me, <i>Village Green Preservation Society</i> was Ray\'s masterwork. It\'s his <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper" class="mw-redirect" title="Sgt. Pepper">Sgt. Pepper</a></i>, it\'s what makes him the definitive pop poet laureate."<sup id="cite_ref-God_Save_The_Kinks:_A_Biography_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-God_Save_The_Kinks:_A_Biography-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Davies\' timing with the album\'s nostalgic concept proved to be just out of step in the cultural turmoil of 1968, but it soon gained a much greater mainstream appeal. In 2003, the album was ranked number 255 on <i>Rolling Stone</i> magazine\'s list of <a href="/wiki/The_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" class="mw-redirect" title="The 500 Greatest Albums of All Time">the 500 greatest albums of all time</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequels_and_follow-ups">Sequels and follow-ups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Kinks_Are_the_Village_Green_Preservation_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sequels and follow-ups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From its inception, Davies considered the album for stage presentation and its general theme served to inspire the Kinks\' more ambitious, but less popular, two-part theatrical work <i><a href="/wiki/Preservation:_Acts_1_%26_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Preservation: Acts 1 &amp; 2">Preservation</a></i> in 1972\xe2\x80\x931974. In his autobiography <i><a href="/wiki/X-Ray_(book)" title="X-Ray (book)">X-Ray</a></i>, Davies would refer to the three albums as his "Preservation trilogy," confirming that <i>Preservation</i> is directly related to <i>Village Green Preservation Society</i>.\n</p><p>On 19 June 2011 Ray Davies performed the album live in its entirety for the first time. The concert was at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a> in London and was part of the <a href="/wiki/Meltdown_(festival)" title="Meltdown (festival)">Meltdown</a> festival. He was accompanied by the <a href="/wiki/London_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="London Philharmonic Orchestra">London Philharmonic Orchestra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crouch_End_Festival_Chorus" title="Crouch End Festival Chorus">Crouch End Festival Chorus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" two</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Kinks_Are_the_Village_Green_Preservation_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" market. It was released in France, Sweden and Norway in October 1968, New Zealand in December 1968 and Italy in January 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> However, before its release in the UK, Ray and Pye had it withdrawn before it was manufactured and a re-sequenced version, expanded to 15 songs, was released there (22 November 1968) and in the United States (January 1969).  Apart from sequencing, this early version differs by the absence of "Last of the Steam-Powered Trains", "Big Sky", "Sitting by the Riverside", "Animal Farm" and "All of My Friends Were There", and the inclusion of "Mr. Songbird" and "Days" (the latter a #12 UK hit single released in June 1968). The stereo mixes of "Do You Remember Walter" and "People Take Pictures of Each Other" on the 12-song version featured slightly different parts from the regular stereo mixes, with the former having the tambourine mixed out and the latter featuring an added big band ending that was removed from the final version.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, a 16-song version of the album was released, with the mono single mix of "<a href="/wiki/Days_(The_Kinks_song)" title="Days (The Kinks song)">Days</a>" being the eighth song on side one.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_unreleased_Four_More_Respected_Gentlemen">The unreleased <i>Four More Respected Gentlemen</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Kinks_Are_the_Village_Green_Preservation_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The unreleased Four More Respected Gentlemen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Four_More_Respected_Gentlemen" title="Four More Respected Gentlemen">Four More Respected Gentlemen</a></div>\n<p>In parallel with the 12-song European version, Ray and Reprise Records (the band\'s US label) intended to release an 11-song album (originally 15 songs before it was shortened) for the American market called <i>Four More Respected Gentlemen</i>. The unreleased album was even given a Reprise serial number (RS 6309). However, Reprise at some point decided that the 15-track UK "Village Green" album was suitable for the US market and cancelled plans for this album. The track listing would have consisted of:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Side_one_2">Side one</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a final master tape was compiled.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Song_and_album_notes">Song and album notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Kinks_Are_the_Village_Green_Preservation_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Song and album notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In late summer of 1968, the Kinks had hoped to release the album as a two-record set with 20 tracks, but Pye Records rejected this plan.  A twelve-track version of the album was released in September 1968 throughout certain European markets; these are now valuable collector\'s items.  Production of this version was quickly halted at Ray Davies\'s insistence and the final revamped fifteen-track version was released in the UK in November 1968.\n</p><p>U.S. record label Reprise had intended to release many of album\'s tracks on a separate Kinks album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Four_More_Respected_Gentlemen" title="Four More Respected Gentlemen">Four More Respected Gentlemen</a></i> sometime in mid-1968 to fulfil a contractual album obligation. This was in the final stages of pre-production when Reprise dropped all plans to issue it, based on the strength of the forthcoming <i>Village Green</i> album.\n</p><p>The photography used for the album art was shot in August 1968 on Parliament Hill, a part of <a href="/wiki/Hampstead_Heath" title="Hampstead Heath">Hampstead Heath</a>, North London.\n</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Starstruck_(The_Kinks_song)" title="Starstruck (The Kinks song)">Starstruck</a>" was released as a single in Europe and the United States, and charted in The Netherlands, peaking at #13.  This is the only appearance of any track from the album on the hit parade in any country. A promotional film was shot for "Starstruck" in late 1968 in Kenwood (another part of Hampstead Heath) for the overseas promotion of the single, and has since been re-used in various Kinks bios. The only other surviving contemporary footage of the band performing Village Green songs is their January 1969 spot on the TV music programme "<a href="/wiki/Once_More_with_Felix" class="mw-redirect" title="Once More with Felix">Once More with Felix</a>", on which they were seen performing "Last Of the Steam-Powered Trains" and "Picture Book" in colour. This clip, long believed lost, came to light in 2007.\n</p><p>Out of print on vinyl for years, although consistently available on US Reprise CD since 1990, the album is reported to be the best-selling non-compilation album in the Kinks\' catalogue. Ray Davies has recently referred to it as the <i>"most successful flop of all time"</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Picture_Book_(song)" title="Picture Book (song)">Picture Book</a>", although not one of the singles from the album, became popular after it was used in a 2004 television commercial for <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">Hewlett-Packard</a> digital imaging products.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/The_Village_Green_Preservation_Society" title="The Village Green Preservation Society">The Village Green Preservation Society</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Village_Green_(song)" title="Village Green (song)">Village Green</a>" were used in the 2007 British comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Fuzz" title="Hot
