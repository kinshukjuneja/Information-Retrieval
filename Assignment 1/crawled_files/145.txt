In politics the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> collapsed and the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> became the main challenger to the dominant <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> throughout the period. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> impacted Britain less severely economically and politically than other major nations, although there were severe pockets of <a href="/wiki/Interwar_unemployment_and_poverty_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Interwar unemployment and poverty in the United Kingdom">long -term unemployment and hardship,</a> especially in mining districts and in Scotland and North West England.\n</p><p>Historian <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Marwick" title="Arthur Marwick">Arthur Marwick</a> sees a radical transformation of British society resulting from the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">Great War</a>, a deluge that swept away many old attitudes and brought in a more egalitarian society. He sees the famous literary pessimism of the 1920s as misplaced, arguing there were major positive long-term consequences of the war to British society. He points to an energized self-consciousness among workers that quickly built up the Labour Party, the coming of partial women\'s suffrage, and an acceleration of social reform and state control of the economy. He sees a decline of deference toward the aristocracy and established authority in general, and the weakening among youth of traditional restraints on individual moral behaviour. The <a href="/wiki/Chaperone_(social)" title="Chaperone (social)">chaperone</a> faded away; village chemists sold contraceptives.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Marwick says that class distinctions softened, national cohesion increased, and British society became more equal.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_of_1920s">Politics of 1920s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Politics of 1920s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enlarging_democracy">Enlarging democracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Enlarging democracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Representation_of_the_People_Act_1918" title="Representation of the People Act 1918">Representation of the People Act 1918</a> finally gave Britain universal manhood suffrage at age 21, with no property qualifications. Even more dramatically it opened up woman suffrage for most women over the age of 30. In 1928, all women were covered on the same terms as men.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> With the emergence of revolutionary forces, most notably in Bolshevik Russia and Socialist Germany, but also in Hungary, Italy and elsewhere, revolution to overthrow established elites and aristocracies was in the air. The Labour Party largely controlled working-class politics, and it strongly supported the government in London and opposed violent revolution. Conservatives were especially worried about "<a href="/wiki/Red_Clydeside" title="Red Clydeside">Red Clydeside</a>" in industrial Scotland. Their fears were misplaced, for there was no organized attempt at any revolution. Indeed, the far left white working men in Red Clydeside were chiefly concerned with excluding blacks and women from good jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nevertheless, there were concerns about republicanism. The king and his top advisors were deeply concerned about the republican threat to the British monarchy, so much so that it was a factor in the king\'s decision not to rescue his cousin the overthrown Tsar <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas II">Nicholas II</a> of Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Nervous conservatives associated republicanism with the rise of socialism and the growing labour movement. Their concerns, although exaggerated, resulted in a redesign of the monarchy\'s social role to be more inclusive of the working class and its representatives, a dramatic change for George, who was most comfortable with naval officers and landed gentry. In fact the socialists by 1911 no longer believed in their anti-monarchy slogans and took a wait-and-see attitude toward George V. They were ready to come to terms with the monarchy if it took the first step.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> During the war George took that step; he made nearly 300 visits to shipyards and munitions factories, chatting with and congratulating ordinary workers on their hard work for the war effort.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBGeorgeV_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBGeorgeV-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He adopted a more democratic stance that crossed class lines and brought the monarchy closer to the public. The king also cultivated friendly relations with leading Labour party politicians and trade union officials. George V\'s abandonment of social aloofness conditioned the royal family\'s behaviour and enhanced its popularity during the economic crises of the 1920s and for over two generations thereafter. For example, in 1924 the king proved willing, in the absence of a clear majority for any one of the three parties, to replace Conservative Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ramsay_MacDonald" title="Ramsay MacDonald">Ramsay MacDonald</a>, the first Labour Party prime minister. King George\'s tactful and understanding reception of the MacDonald government allayed the suspicions of the party\'s supporters throughout the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An armed insurrection by Irish republicans known as the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> took place in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> during Easter Week, 1916. It was badly organized, and quickly suppressed by the Army. The government responded with harsh repression, 2000 arrests, and quick execution of 15 leaders. The Catholic Irish then underwent a dramatic change of mood, and shifted to demand vengeance and independence.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1917 Lloyd George called the 1917\xe2\x80\x9318 <a href="/wiki/Irish_Convention" title="Irish Convention">Irish Convention</a> in an attempt to settle the outstanding <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Home Rule for Ireland</a> issue. It had little support. The upsurge in republican sympathies in Ireland following the Easter Rising coupled with Lloyd George\'s disastrous attempt to extend <a href="/wiki/Conscription_Crisis_of_1918" title="Conscription Crisis of 1918">conscription to Ireland</a> in April 1918 led to the wipeout of the old <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Home Rule Party</a> at the December 1918 election. They had supported the British war effort and were now displaced by <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a>, which had mobilised grass-roots opposition to helping the British rule.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Sinn F\xc3\xa9in MPs did not take up their seats in the British Parliament, instead setting up their own new parliament in Dublin, and immediately declared an <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republic" title="Irish Republic">Irish Republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>British policy was confused and contradictory, as the cabinet could not decide on war or peace, sending in enough force to commit atrocities that angered Catholics in Ireland and America, and Liberals in Britain, but not enough to suppress the rebels outside the cities. Lloyd George waxed hot and cold, denouncing murderers one day, but eventually negotiating with them. He sent in 40,000 soldiers as well as newly formed para-military units -- the "<a href="/wiki/Black_and_Tans" title="Black and Tans">Black and Tans</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_Division" title="Auxiliary Division">Auxiliaries</a> -- to reinforce the professional police (the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Constabulary" title="Royal Irish Constabulary">Royal Irish Constabulary</a>). British firepower prevailed in the cities forcing the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA) (the paramilitary force of Sinn F\xc3\xa9in) into hiding. However the IRA controlled much of the countryside and set up an alternative local government.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The British units were poorly coordinated while <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(Irish_leader)" title="Michael Collins (Irish leader)">Michael Collins</a> designed a highly effective organization for the IRA that used informers to destroy the British intelligence system by assassinating its leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Although it was called "<a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>" historians generally agree that it was quite unlike the later <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a> that was fought in 1922\xe2\x80\x9323 between the forces of Collins and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a>. The 1919\xe2\x80\x9321 clash "was no war in any conventional sense of the term, but a highly contingent, very small-scale and low-intensity conflict in which assassination was as important as ambush or fixed battle."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Lloyd George finally solved the crisis with the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a> which partitioned Ireland into <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> in May 1921. Sinn F\xc3\xa9in won control of the south and agreed to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> in December 1921 with Irish leaders. Collins took power when de Valera refused to sign and led a breakaway faction.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Under the treaty southern Ireland seceded in 1922 to form the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>. Meanwhile, the Unionists under <a href="/wiki/Edward_Carson" title="Edward Carson">Edward Carson</a> controlled Ulster and Northern Ireland remained loyal to London.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> By 1922 the Irish situation had stabilized, and no longer played a major role in British politics. Nevertheless, <a href="/wiki/Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations" title="Ireland\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom relations">disputes sputtered for decades</a> regarding the exact relationship to the monarchy, <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Trade_War" title="Anglo-Irish Trade War">a trade war in the 1930s</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_Ports_(Ireland)" title="Treaty Ports (Ireland)">British use of naval ports</a>. The Irish Free State cut many of its ties to Britain in 1937. As the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="History of the Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> it was one of a handful of neutrals in Europe in the Second World War.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expanding_the_welfare_state">Expanding the welfare state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expanding the welfare state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Two major programmes dealing with unemployment and housing that permanently expanded the welfare state passed in 1919 and 1920 with surprisingly little debate, even as the Conservatives dominated parliament. \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_Insurance_Act_1920" title="Unemployment Insurance Act 1920">Unemployment Insurance Act 1920</a> expanded the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/National_Insurance_Act_1911" title="National Insurance Act 1911">National Insurance Act 1911</a>. It set up the dole system that provided 39 weeks of unemployment benefits to practically the entire civilian working population except domestic servants, farm workers, and civil servants. Funded in part by weekly contributions from both employers and employed, it provided weekly payments of 15s for unemployed men and 12s for unemployed women. It passed at a time of very low unemployment. Historian <a href="/wiki/C._L._Mowat" title="C. L. Mowat">C. L. Mowat</a> called these laws "Socialism by the back door," and notes how surprised politicians were when the costs to the Treasury soared during the high unemployment of 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-Mowat43-46_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mowat43-46-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing">Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Housing_in_the_United_Kingdom#History" title="Housing in the United Kingdom">Housing in the United Kingdom \xc2\xa7&#160;History</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_housing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Public housing in the United Kingdom">Public housing in the United Kingdom</a></div> \n<p>The rapid expansion of housing was a major success story of the interwar years, standing in sharp contrast to the United States, where new housing construction practically collapsed after 1929. The total housing stock In England and Wales was 7.6 million in 1911; 8.0 million in 1921; 9.4 million in 1931; and 11.3 million in 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The influential <a href="/wiki/Tudor_Walters_Report" title="Tudor Walters Report">Tudor Walters Report</a> of 1918 set the standards for council house design and location for the next 90 years.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It recommended housing in short terraces, spaced at 70 feet (21&#160;m) at a density of 12 to the acre.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> With the <a href="/wiki/Housing,_Town_Planning,_%26c._Act_1919" title="Housing, Town Planning, &amp;c. Act 1919">Housing, Town Planning, &amp;c. Act 1919</a> Lloyd George set up a system of government housing that followed his 1918 campaign promises of "homes fit for heroes."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Also known as the "Addison Act," it required local authorities to survey their housing needs, and start building houses to replace slums. The treasury subsidized the low rents.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Older women could now vote. Local politicians consulted with them and in response put more emphasis on such amenities as communal laundries, extra bedrooms, indoor lavatories, running hot water, separate parlours to demonstrate their respectability, and practical vegetable gardens rather than manicured lawns.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Progress was not automatic, as shown by the troubles of rural Norfolk. Many dreams were shattered as local authorities had to renege on promises they could not fulfill due to undue haste, impossible national deadlines, debilitating bureaucracy, lack of lumber, rising costs, and the non-affordability of rents by the rural poor.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In England and Wales 214,000 multi-unit council buildings were built by 1939; the Ministry of Health became largely a ministry of housing.<sup id="cite_ref-Mowat43-46_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mowat43-46-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Council housing accounted for 10 percent of the housing stock in Britain by 1938, peaking at 32 percent in 1980, and dropping to 18 percent by 1996, where it held steady for the next two decades. <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Increasingly the British ideal was home ownership, even among the working class. Rates of home ownership rose steadily from 15 percent before 1914, to 32 percent by 1938, and 67 percent by 1996. The construction industry sold the idea of home ownership to upscale renters. The mortgage lost its old stigma of a millstone round your neck to instead be seen as a smart long-term investment in suburbanized Britain. It appealed to aspirations of upward mobility and made possible the fastest rate of growth in working-class owner-occupation during the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The boom was largely financed by the savings ordinary Britons put into their <a href="/wiki/Building_societies" class="mw-redirect" title="Building societies">building societies</a>. Starting in the 1920s favourable tax policies encouraged substantial investment in the societies, creating huge reserves for lending. Beginning in 1927, the societies encouraged borrowing through gradual liberalization of mortgage terms.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservative_control">Conservative control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservative control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg/150px-Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg" width="150" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg/225px-Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg/300px-Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1022" data-file-height="1478" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanley_Baldwin_ggbain.35233.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a> was <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> Prime Minister between 1923\xe2\x80\x931924, 1924\xe2\x80\x931929 and 1935\xe2\x80\x931937.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_George_ministry" title="Lloyd George ministry">Lloyd George ministry</a> fell apart in 1922, and <a href="/wiki/Bonar_Law" title="Bonar Law">Bonar Law</a> became prime minister of a Conservative government. In May 1923 Bonar Law resigned because of ill health and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a>. Baldwin, as leader of the Conservative Party (1923\xe2\x80\x9337) and as Prime Minister (in 1923\xe2\x80\x9324, 1924\xe2\x80\x9329 and 1935\xe2\x80\x9337), dominated British politics.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> His mixture of strong social reforms and steady government proved a powerful election combination, with the result that the Conservatives governed Britain either by themselves or as the leading component of the <a href="/wiki/UK_National_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK National Government">National Government</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1935" title="United Kingdom general election, 1935">general election of 1935</a> Baldwin\'s was the last government to win over 50% of the vote. Baldwin\'s political strategy was to polarize the electorate so that voters would choose between the Conservatives on the right and the Labour Party on the left, squeezing out the Liberals in the middle.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The polarization did take place and while the Liberals remained active under Lloyd George, they won few seats. Baldwin\'s reputation soared in the 1920s and 1930s, but crashed after 1940 as he was blamed for the appeasement policies toward Germany, and as Churchill was made the Conservative icon by his admirers. Since the 1970s Baldwin\'s reputation has recovered somewhat.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg/220px-Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg" width="220" height="336" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg/330px-Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg/440px-Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg 2x" data-file-width="468" data-file-height="714" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Socialism_Would_Mean.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1929 Conservative poster attacking the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Having won an election just the year before, Baldwin\'s Conservative party had a comfortable majority in the Commons and could have waited another four years, but the government was concerned. Baldwin felt the need to receive a new mandate from the people. Oxford historian (and Conservative MP) <a href="/wiki/John_Marriott_(British_politician)" title="John Marriott (British politician)">J.A.R. Marriott</a> depicts the gloomy national mood: \n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>The times were still out of joint. Mr. Baldwin had indeed succeeded in negotiating (January 1923) a settlement of the British debt to the United States, but on terms which involved an annual payment of \xc2\xa334 million, at the existing rate of exchange. The French remained in the Ruhr. Peace had not yet been made with Turkey; unemployment was a standing menace to national recovery; there was continued unrest among the wage-earners, and a significant strike among farm labourers in Norfolk. Confronted by these difficulties, convinced that economic conditions in England called for a drastic change in fiscal policy, and urged thereto by the Imperial Conference of 1928, Mr. Baldwin decided to ask the country for a mandate for Preference and Protection.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The result however backfired on Baldwin, who lost a host of seats to Labour and the Liberals. For the first time in history, Labour formed a government. However, in 1924 Baldwin and the Conservatives returned with a large majority. <a href="/wiki/Ross_McKibbin" title="Ross McKibbin">Ross McKibbin</a> finds that the political culture of the interwar period was built around an anti-socialist middle class, supported by the Conservative leaders, especially Baldwin.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="King_George_V">King George V</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: King George V">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/King_George_V" class="mw-redirect" title="King George V">King George V</a></div>\n<p>King George V (reigned 1910\xe2\x80\x931936) was scandal free. He appeared hard working and became widely admired by the people of Britain and the Empire, as well as "<a href="/wiki/The_Establishment" title="The Establishment">The Establishment</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>. It was George V who established the modern norm of conduct for British royalty, reflecting middle-class values and virtues rather than upper-class lifestyles or vices<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>. Anti-intellectual and lacking the sophistication of his two royal predecessors, as well as their cosmopolitan experiences, he nevertheless understood the British Empire better than most of his ministers; indeed he explained, "it has always been my dream to identify myself with the great idea of Empire." <sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> He used his exceptional memory for details and faces to good effect in small talk with commoners and officials<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>. He invariably wielded his influence as a force of neutrality and moderation, seeing his role as mediator rather than final decision maker. <sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>. For example, in 1921 he had General <a href="/wiki/Jan_Smuts" title="Jan Smuts">Jan Smuts</a> draft a speech calling for a compromise truce to end the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence#Truce:_July\xe2\x80\x93December_1921" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish war of independence</a> and secured cabinet approval; the Irish also agreed and the war ended.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Historian <a href="/wiki/A._J._P._Taylor" title="A. J. P. Taylor">A. J. P. Taylor</a> praises the king\'s initiative as, "perhaps the greatest service performed by a British monarch in modern times."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> His transparent sense of duty, his loyalty, his impartiality, and his unfailing example of good taste inspired the people and discouraged politicians from manipulating him to their own advantage. King George V was by temperament a cautious and conservative man who never fully appreciated or approved the revolutionary changes underway in British society. Nevertheless, everyone understood that he was earnestly devoted to Britain and the British Commonwealth<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>.\n</p><p>The king\'s popularity was enhanced during the World War when he made more than a thousand visits to hospitals, factories, and military and naval installations. He thereby gave highly visible support to the morale of ordinary workers and servicemen.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> His Silver Jubilee in 1935 became a national festival of fervent rejoicing, tinged with a few complaints.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> His funeral and following commemorations were elaborately staged, very well attended ceremonies that redefined the role of royalty in a newly democratic nation. The people had new ways to affirm their loyalties, such as close attention to live radio broadcasts and followup newsreels. New ceremonies born of the commemoration of death in the Great War included the two-minute silence. Britain set up living memorials to honour and expand the king\'s lifelong belief in the physical, moral and social benefits of recreation and sports. The royal death thereby acted to enhance a shared Britishness. The funeral of King George VI in 1952 followed the same formula. Thereby the monarchy grew stronger and, more importantly, national cohesion was built up in the era of total war.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The king was the most active monarch in many ways since George III (reigned 1760\xe2\x80\x931820). Biographer <a href="/wiki/H._C._G._Matthew" class="mw-redirect" title="H. C. G. Matthew">H. C. G. Matthew</a> concludes:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>His was the busiest service of any nineteenth- or twentieth-century British monarch. He dealt with a remarkable and arduous series of crises: the reaction of the Unionists to the Parliament Bill and the home rule crisis, the complex coalition-forming of the First World War, the incorporation of the Labour Party into the working of constitutional government, the replacement of orthodox politics by a national government. George V\'s assiduous and non-partisan approach smoothed the process of political change which these crises represented.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBGeorgeV_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBGeorgeV-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Taxes rose sharply during the war and never returned to their old levels. A rich man paid 8% of his income in taxes before the war, and about a third afterwards. Much of the money went on unemployment benefits. About 5% of the national income every year was transferred from the rich to the poor. <a href="/wiki/A._J._P._Taylor" title="A. J. P. Taylor">A. J. P. Taylor</a> argues most people "were enjoying a richer life than any previously known in the history of the world: longer holidays, shorter hours, higher real wages."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The British economy was lacklustre in the 1920s, with sharp declines and high unemployment in heavy industry and coal, especially in Scotland and Wales. Exports of coal and steel halved by 1939 and the business community was slow to adopt the new labour and management principles coming from the US, such as <a href="/wiki/Fordism" title="Fordism">Fordism</a>, consumer credit, eliminating surplus capacity, designing a more structured management, and using greater economies of scale.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> For over a century the shipping industry had dominated world trade, but it remained in the doldrums despite various stimulus efforts by the government. With the very sharp decline in world trade after 1929, its condition became critical.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Chancellor of the Exchequer <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> put Britain back on the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a> in 1925, which many economists blame for the mediocre performance of the economy. Others point to a variety of factors, including the inflationary effects of the World War and supply-side shocks caused by reduced working hours after the war.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the late 1920s, economic performance had stabilised, but the overall situation was disappointing, for Britain had fallen behind the United States as the leading industrial power. There also remained a strong economic divide between the north and south of England during this period, with the south of England and the Midlands fairly prosperous by the Thirties, while parts of south Wales and the industrial north of England became known as "distressed areas" due to particularly high rates of unemployment and poverty. Despite this, the standard of living continued to improve as local councils built <a href="/wiki/Council_house" title="Council house">new houses</a> to let to families rehoused from outdated <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">slums</a>, with up to date facilities including indoor toilets, bathrooms and electric lighting now being included in the new properties. The private sector enjoyed a housebuilding boom during the 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labour">Labour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Labour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Interwar_unemployment_and_poverty_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Interwar unemployment and poverty in the United Kingdom">Interwar unemployment and poverty in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>During the war, <a href="/wiki/Trade_unions" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade unions">trade unions</a> were encouraged and their membership grew from 4.1 million in 1914 to 6.5 million in 1918. They peaked at 8.3 million in 1920 before relapsing to 5.4 million in 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Coal was a sick industry; the best seams were being exhausted, raising the cost. Demand fell as oil began replacing coal for fuel. The <a href="/wiki/1926_United_Kingdom_general_strike" title="1926 United Kingdom general strike">1926 general strike</a> was a nine-day nationwide walkout of 1.3 million railwaymen, transport workers, printers, dockers, iron workers and steelworkers supporting the 1.2 million coal miners who had been locked out by the owners. The miners had rejected the owners\' demands for longer hours and reduced pay in the face of falling prices.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> The Conservative government had provided a nine-month subsidy in 1925 but that was not enough to turn around a sick industry. To support the miners the <a href="/wiki/Trades_Union_Congress" title="Trades Union Congress">Trades Union Congress</a> (TUC), an umbrella organization of all trades unions, called out certain critical unions. The hope was the government would intervene to reorganize and rationalize the industry, and raise the subsidy. The Conservative government had stockpiled supplies and essential services continued in operation using students and middle class volunteers. All three major parties opposed the strike. The Labour Party leaders did not approve and feared it would tar the party with the image of radicalism, for the <a href="/wiki/Comintern" class="mw-redirect" title="Comintern">Comintern</a> in Moscow had sent instructions for Communists to aggressively promote the strike. The general strike itself was largely non-violent, but the miners\' lockout continued and there was violence in Scotland. It was the only general strike in British history, for TUC leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Bevin" title="Ernest Bevin">Ernest Bevin</a> considered it a mistake. Most historians treat it as a singular event with few long-term consequences, but <a href="/wiki/Martin_Pugh_(author)" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Pugh (author)">Martin Pugh</a> says it accelerated the movement of working-class voters to the Labour Party, which led to future gains.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Trade_Disputes_and_Trade_Unions_Act_1927" title="Trade Disputes and Trade Unions Act 1927">Trade Disputes and Trade Unions Act 1927</a> made general strikes illegal and ended the automatic payment of union dues to the Labour Party. That act was largely repealed in 1946. The coal industry used up the more accessible coal. As costs rose, output fell from 267 million tons in 1924 to 183 million in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The Labour government nationalised the mines in 1947.\n</p><p>Starting in 1909, Liberals, led especially by Lloyd George, promoted the idea of a minimum wage for farm workers. Resistance from landowners was strong, but success was achieved by 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> According to Robin Gowers and Timothy J. Hatton, the impact In England and Wales was significant. They estimate that it raised wages for farm labourers by 15 per cent by 1929, and by more than 20 per cent in the 1930s. It reduced the employment of such labourers by 54,000 (6.5 per cent) in 1929 and 97,000 (13.3 per cent) in 1937. They argue, "The minimum wage lifted out of poverty many families of farm labourers who remained employed, but it significantly lowered the incomes of farmers, particularly during the 1930s."<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the War many new food products became available to the typical household, with branded foods advertised for their convenience. The shortage of servants was felt in the kitchen, but now instead of an experienced cook spending hours on difficult custards and puddings the housewife could purchase instant foods in jars, or powders that could be quickly mixed. Breakfast porridge from branded, more finely milled, oats could now be cooked in two minutes, not 20. American-style dry cereals began to challenge the porridge and bacon and eggs of the middle classes, and the bread and margarine of the poor. Shops carried more bottled and canned goods and fresher meat, fish and vegetables. While wartime shipping shortages had sharply narrowed choices, the 1920s saw many new kinds of foods--especially fruits--imported from around the world, along with better quality packaging and hygiene. Middle class households now had ice boxes or electric refrigerators, which made for better storage and the convenience of buying in larger quantities.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Numerous studies in the Depression years documented that the average consumer ate better than before. <a href="/wiki/Seebohm_Rowntree" title="Seebohm Rowntree">Seebohm Rowntree</a> reported that the "standard to workers in 1936 was about 30 percent higher than it was in 1899."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> The dairy industry was producing too much, and profits were too low. So the government used the <a href="/wiki/Milk_Marketing_Board" title="Milk Marketing Board">Milk Marketing Board</a> to give a guaranteed price to dairy farmers \xe2\x80\x93 a policy ridiculed by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> as the "economics of Bedlam."<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Food prices were low, but the advantage went overwhelmingly to the middle and upper classes, with the poorest third of the population suffering from sustained poor nutrition. Starvation was not a factor, but widespread hunger was. The deleterious effects on poor children were obvious to teachers. In 1934, the government began a program of charging school children a <a href="/wiki/Halfpenny_(British_pre-decimal_coin)" title="Halfpenny (British pre-decimal coin)">halfpenny a day</a> for a third of a pint of milk. This dramatically improved their nutrition, and the new demand kept up the wholesale price of milk paid to farmers. About half the nation\'s school children participated by 1936. Milk was distributed for free in the Second World War, and participation rose to 90 percent. Indeed, the rationing system of the wartime years sharply improved the nutrition of poorest third, together with their capacity for manual labour. <sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Depression">Great Depression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Great Depression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Great Depression in the United Kingdom">Great Depression in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> originated on <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> in the United States in late 1929, and quickly spread to the rest of the world. The main impact of the economic slump was felt in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Unlike Germany, Canada and Australia, Britain had not experienced a boom in the 1920s, so the downturn was less severe and ended sooner.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Worldwide_crisis">Worldwide crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Worldwide crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By summer 1931 the world financial crisis began to overwhelm Britain; investors across the world started withdrawing their gold from London at the rate of \xc2\xa32\xc2\xbd million a day.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Credits of \xc2\xa325 million each from the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_France" title="Bank of France">Bank of France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York" title="Federal Reserve Bank of New York">Federal Reserve Bank of New York</a> and an issue of \xc2\xa315 million in fiduciary notes slowed, but did not reverse the British crisis. The financial crisis now caused a major political crisis in Britain in August 1931. With deficits mounting, the bankers demanded a balanced budget; the divided cabinet of Ramsay MacDonald\'s Labour government agreed; it proposed to raise taxes, cut spending and most controversially, to cut unemployment benefits by 20%. The attack on welfare was totally unacceptable to the Labour movement. MacDonald wanted to resign, but the King insisted he remain and form an all-party coalition "<a href="/wiki/National_Government_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Government (United Kingdom)">National government</a>." The Conservative and Liberal parties signed on, along with a small cadre of Labour, but the vast majority of Labour leaders denounced MacDonald as a traitor for leading the new government. Britain went off the gold standard, and suffered relatively less than other major countries in the Great Depression. In the 1931 British election the Labour Party was virtually destroyed, leaving MacDonald as Prime Minister of a largely Conservative coalition.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The flight of gold continued, however, and the Treasury finally was forced to abandon the gold standard in September 1931. Until now the government had religiously followed orthodox policies, which demanded balanced-budgets and the gold standard. Instead of the predicted disaster, cutting loose from gold proved a major advantage. Immediately the exchange rate of the pound fell by 25%, from $4.86 for one pound to $3.40. British exports were now much more competitive, which laid the ground for a gradual economic recovery. The worst was over.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Britain\'s world trade fell in half (1929\xe2\x80\x9333); the output of heavy industry fell by a third. Employment and profits plunged in nearly all sectors. At the depth in summer 1932, registered unemployed numbered 3.5 million, and many more had only part-time employment.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The government tried to work inside the Commonwealth, raising tariffs for products from the United States, France and Britain, while giving preference to Commonwealth members.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organized_protests">Organized protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Organized protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The north of England, Scotland, Northern Ireland, and Wales suffered particularly severe economic problems, especially if they depended on coal, steel or shipbuilding. Unemployment reached 70% in some mining localities at the start of the 1930s (with more than 3 million out of work nationally). The government was cautious and conservative, rejecting the Keynesian proposal for large-scale public works projects.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Doomsayers on the left such as <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Webb" class="mw-redirect" title="Sidney Webb">Sidney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Webb" title="Beatrice Webb">Beatrice Webb</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._A._Hobson" class="mw-redirect" title="J. A. Hobson">J. A. Hobson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/G._D._H._Cole" title="G. D. H. Cole">G. D. H. Cole</a> repeated the dire warnings they had been making for years about the imminent death of capitalism, only now far more people paid attention.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Starting in 1935 the <a href="/wiki/Left_Book_Club" title="Left Book Club">Left Book Club</a> provided a new warning every month, and built up the credibility of Soviet-style socialism as an alternative.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1936, by which time unemployment was lower, 200 unemployed men made a highly publicized march from <a href="/wiki/Jarrow" title="Jarrow">Jarrow</a> to London in a bid to show the plight of the industrial poor. Although much romanticized by the Left, the <a href="/wiki/Jarrow_Crusade" class="mw-redirect" title="Jarrow Crusade">Jarrow Crusade</a> marked a deep split in the Labour Party and resulted in no government action.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> Unemployment remained high until the war absorbed all the job seekers. <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">George Orwell</a>\'s book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Road_to_Wigan_Pier" title="The Road to Wigan Pier">The Road to Wigan Pier</a></i> gives a bleak overview of the hardships of the time.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The economic crisis of the early 1930s, and the response of the Labour and National governments to the depression, have generated much historical controversy. Apart from the major pockets of long-term high unemployment, Britain was generally prosperous. Historian <a href="/wiki/Piers_Brendon" title="Piers Brendon">Piers Brendon</a> writes: "Historians, however, have long since revised this grim picture, presenting the devil\'s decade as the cradle of the affluent society. Prices fell sharply between the wars and average incomes rose by about a third. The term "property-owning democracy" was coined in the 1920s, and three million houses were built during the 1930s. Land, labour and materials were cheap: a bungalow could be purchased for \xc2\xa3225 and a semi for \xc2\xa3450. The middle class also bought <a href="/wiki/Radiogram_(furniture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiogram (furniture)">radiograms</a>, telephones, <a href="/wiki/Three-piece_suite" class="mw-redirect" title="Three-piece suite">three-piece suites</a>, electric cookers, vacuum cleaners and golf clubs. They ate <a href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s_Corn_Flakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Kellogg&#39;s Corn Flakes">Kellogg\'s Corn Flakes</a> ("never miss a day"), drove to <a href="/wiki/Odeon_cinema" class="mw-redirect" title="Odeon cinema">Odeon cinemas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austin_7" title="Austin 7">Austin Sevens</a> (costing \xc2\xa3135 by 1930) and smoked <a href="/wiki/Craven_A" title="Craven A">Craven A</a> cigarettes, cork-tipped "to prevent sore throats". The depression spawned a consumer boom."<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the decades immediately following the Second World War, most historical opinion was critical of the governments of the period. Some historians, such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Skidelsky" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Skidelsky">Robert Skidelsky</a> in his <i>Politicians and the Slump</i>, compared the orthodox policies of the Labour and National governments unfavourably with the more radical proto-Keynesian measures advocated by <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oswald_Mosley" title="Oswald Mosley">Oswald Mosley</a>, and the more <a href="/wiki/Economic_interventionism" title="Economic interventionism">interventionist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keynesian" class="mw-redirect" title="Keynesian">Keynesian</a> responses in other economies: Franklin Roosevelt\'s <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> in the United States, the <a href="/wiki/First_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="First Labour Government of New Zealand">Labour government</a> in New Zealand, and the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party" title="Swedish Social Democratic Party">Social Democratic government</a> in Sweden. Since the 1970s opinion has become less uniformly hostile. In the preface to the 1994 edition, Skidelsky argues that recent experience of currency crises and <a href="/wiki/Capital_flight" title="Capital flight">capital flight</a> make it hard to be so critical of the politicians who wanted to achieve stability by cutting labour costs and defending the value of the currency.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commonwealth_and_Empire">Commonwealth and Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Commonwealth and Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historiography_of_the_British_Empire" title="Historiography of the British Empire">Historiography of the British Empire</a></div>\n<p>After taking over the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations_mandate" title="League of Nations mandate">League of Nations mandates</a> on certain German and Ottoman territories in 1919, the British Empire reached its territorial peak. The interwar years saw extensive efforts for economic and educational development of the colonies. The <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominions</a> were prosperous and largely took care of themselves. By far the most troublesome areas for London were India and Palestine.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Empire_1921.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/British_Empire_1921.png/400px-British_Empire_1921.png" width="400" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/British_Empire_1921.png/600px-British_Empire_1921.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/British_Empire_1921.png/800px-British_Empire_1921.png 2x" data-file-width="1425" data-file-height="625" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Empire_1921.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>British Empire at its territorial peak in 1921</div></div></div>\n<p>The Dominions (Canada, Australia, South Africa, and New Zealand) achieved virtual independence in foreign policy in the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster 1931</a>, though each depended heavily upon British naval protection.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> After 1931 trade policy favoured <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Preference" title="Imperial Preference">Imperial Preference</a> with higher tariffs against the U.S. and all others outside the Commonwealth.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In India, the forces of nationalism were now being organized by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Indian National Congress</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru" title="Jawaharlal Nehru">Jawaharlal Nehru</a>. India contributed significantly to victory in the World War, and was bitterly disappointed by the very limited benefits conferred in the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India_Act_1919" title="Government of India Act 1919">Government of India Act 1919</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> British fears of German wartime plots or postwar Communism following the <a href="/wiki/Ghadar_Mutiny" title="Ghadar Mutiny">Ghadar Mutiny</a> ensured that war-time strictures were renewed by the <a href="/wiki/Rowlatt_Act" title="Rowlatt Act">Rowlatt Act</a> of 1919 that suppressed dissent. Tensions escalated particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Punjab_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab region">Punjab region</a>, where repressive measures culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Jallianwala_Bagh_massacre" title="Jallianwala Bagh massacre">Amritsar Massacre</a>. In Britain public opinion was divided over the morality of the massacre, between those who saw it as having saved India from anarchy, and those who viewed it with revulsion.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Gandhi developed the technique of nonviolent resistance, claiming moral superiority over the British use of violence.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Multiple negotiations were held in the 1930s, but a strong reactionary movement in Britain, led by Winston Churchill, blocked the adoption of reforms that would satisfy Indian nationalists. Historian <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_James" title="Lawrence James">Lawrence James</a> states:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>From 1930 to 1935 [Churchill] was a Cassandra, warning the country that the government\'s policy of allowing self-determination for India would be a catastrophe for Britain and mark the beginning of the end for her Empire. His language was stark and his imagery apocalyptic: India was facing a prolonged crisis which successive governments had failed to resolve.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> The Conservatives in Parliament designed the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India_Act,_1935" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of India Act, 1935">Government of India Act, 1935</a> to create a federation that would facilitate continued British control and deflect the challenge of Congress.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> The Labour Party, although in a weak minority in the 1930s, gave support to Congress and worked with Indians in Britain; after 1945 it was in a position to grant India independence.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/History_of_Egypt" title="History of Egypt">Egypt</a> was nominally part of the Ottoman Empire, although under British rule, until 1914, when London declared it a protectorate. <a href="/wiki/Unilateral_Declaration_of_Egyptian_Independence" title="Unilateral Declaration of Egyptian Independence">Independence was formally granted in 1922</a>, though it continued to be a British <a href="/wiki/Client_state" title="Client state">client state</a> until 1954. British troops remained stationed to guard the Suez Canal. Egypt joined the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Iraq_(Mandate_administration)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Iraq (Mandate administration)">Iraq</a>, a British <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations_mandate" title="League of Nations mandate">mandate</a> since 1920, also gained membership of the League in its own right after achieving independence from Britain in 1932. Iraq remained under firm British guidance regarding foreign affairs, defence policy, and oil policy.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Palestine</a>, Britain was presented with the problem of mediating between the Arabs and increasing numbers of Jews. The <a href="/wiki/1917_Balfour_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="1917 Balfour Declaration">1917 Balfour Declaration</a>, which had been incorporated into the terms of the mandate, stated that a national home for the Jewish people would be established in Palestine. Tens of thousands of Jews immigrated from Europe. The Arab population <a href="/wiki/1936%E2%80%9339_Arab_revolt_in_Palestine" class="mw-redirect" title="1936\xe2\x80\x9339 Arab revolt in Palestine">revolted in 1936</a>. As the prospect of war with Germany loomed larger, Britain judged the support of Arabs as more important than the establishment of a Jewish homeland, and shifted to a pro-Arab stance, limiting Jewish immigration and in turn triggering a <a href="/wiki/Jewish_insurgency_in_Palestine" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish insurgency in Palestine">Jewish insurgency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominions_control_their_foreign_policies">Dominions control their foreign policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Dominions control their foreign policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ImperialConference.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/ImperialConference.jpg/220px-ImperialConference.jpg" width="220" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/ImperialConference.jpg/330px-ImperialConference.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/ImperialConference.jpg/440px-ImperialConference.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="507" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ImperialConference.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> with the British and Dominion prime ministers at the <a href="/wiki/1926_Imperial_Conference" title="1926 Imperial Conference">1926 Imperial Conference</a></div></div></div>\n<p>As Britain\'s Prime Minister, Lloyd George requested military assistance from the Dominions at the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Chanak_Crisis" title="Chanak Crisis">Chanak Crisis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> in 1922. He was rejected.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> The World War had greatly strengthened the sense of nationalism and self-confidence in the dominions. They were now independent members of the League of Nations, and refused to automatically follow requests from Britain\'s leaders. The right of the Dominions to set their own foreign policy, independent of Britain, was recognised at the <a href="/wiki/1923_Imperial_Conference" title="1923 Imperial Conference">1923 Imperial Conference</a>. The <a href="/wiki/1926_Imperial_Conference" title="1926 Imperial Conference">1926 Imperial Conference</a> issued the <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Declaration_of_1926" title="Balfour Declaration of 1926">Balfour Declaration of 1926</a>, declaring the Dominions to be "autonomous Communities within the British Empire, equal in status, in no way subordinate one to another" within a "<a href="/wiki/British_Commonwealth_of_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="British Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth of Nations</a>". This declaration was given legal substance under the 1931 <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster</a>. India, however, was denied dominion status and its foreign policy was set by London.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/History_of_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="History of Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> was overwhelmed by the economic disasters of the Great depression and voluntarily gave up its dominion status. It reverted to a crown colony under direct British control until it voted to join Canada in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> The Irish Free State broke its ties with London with a <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">new constitution</a> in 1937, making it a republic in all but name.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/International_relations_(1919%E2%80%931939)#Great_Britain" title="International relations (1919\xe2\x80\x931939)">International relations (1919\xe2\x80\x931939) \xc2\xa7&#160;Great Britain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a></div>\n<p>Britain had suffered little physical devastation during the war but the cost in death and disability and money were very high. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1918" title="United Kingdom general election, 1918">Khaki Election of 1918</a>, coming a month after the Allied victory over Germany, Lloyd George promised to impose a harsh treaty on Germany. At the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Conference,_1919" title="Paris Peace Conference, 1919">Paris Peace Conference</a> in early 1919, however, he took a much more moderate approach. France and Italy demanded and achieved harsh terms, including German admission of guilt for starting the war (which humiliated Germany), and a demand that Germany pay the entire Allied cost of the war, including veterans\' benefits and interest. Britain reluctantly supported the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a>, although many experts, most famously <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a>, thought it too harsh on Germany <sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Britain began to look on a restored Germany as an important trading partner and worried about the effect of reparations on the British economy. In the end the United States financed German debt payments to Britain, France and the other Allies through the <a href="/wiki/Dawes_Plan" title="Dawes Plan">Dawes Plan</a>, and Britain used this income to repay the loans it borrowed from the U.S. during the war.\n</p><p>Vivid memories of the horrors and deaths of the World War made Britain and its leaders strongly inclined to pacifism in the interwar era.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1920s">1920s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 1920s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Britain maintained close relationships with France and the United States, rejected isolationism, and sought world peace through naval arms limitation treaties,<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> and peace with Germany through the <a href="/wiki/Locarno_treaties" class="mw-redirect" title="Locarno treaties">Locarno treaties</a> of 1925. A main goal was to restore Germany to a peaceful, prosperous state.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>With disarmament high on the agenda, Britain played a major role following the United States in the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Naval_Conference" title="Washington Naval Conference">Washington Naval Conference</a> of 1921 in working toward naval disarmament of the major powers. By 1933 disarmament had collapsed and the issue became rearming for a war against Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the Washington Conference Britain abandoned the <a href="/wiki/Two_power_standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Two power standard">Two power standard</a> - her long-time policy of paramount naval strength equal to or greater than the next two naval powers combined. Instead it accepted equality with United States, and weakness in Asian waters relative to Japan. It promised to not strengthen the fortifications of Hong Kong, which were within range of Japan. The treaty with Japan was not renewed, But Japan at the time was not engaged in expansion activities of the sort that grew momentous from 1931 onward. London cut loose from Tokyo but moved much closer to Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Politically the coalition government of Prime Minister David Lloyd George depended primarily on Conservative Party support. He increasingly antagonized his supporters with foreign policy miscues. The <a href="/wiki/Chanak_Crisis" title="Chanak Crisis">Chanak Crisis</a> of 1922 brought Britain to the brink of war with Turkey, but the <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominions</a> were opposed and the British military was hesitant, so peace was preserved. This was one of the factors causing Conservative MPs to vote, at the <a href="/wiki/Carlton_Club_meeting" title="Carlton Club meeting">Carlton Club meeting</a>, to fight the next election as a separate party; Lloyd George then resigned as Prime Minister, ending the coalition government.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The success at Locarno in handling the German question impelled Foreign Secretary <a href="/wiki/Austen_Chamberlain" title="Austen Chamberlain">Austen Chamberlain</a>, working with France and Italy, to find a master solution to the diplomatic problems of Eastern Europe and the Balkans. It proved impossible to overcome mutual antagonisms, because Chamberlain\'s programme was flawed by his misperceptions and fallacious judgments.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s">1930s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 1930s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Appeasement" title="Appeasement">Appeasement</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_foreign_policy_of_the_Chamberlain_ministry" title="European foreign policy of the Chamberlain ministry">European foreign policy of the Chamberlain ministry</a></div>\n<p>The great challenge came from dictators, first <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> of Italy from 1923, then from 1933 <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> of a much more powerful <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>. Britain and France led the policy of non-interference in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> (1936\xe2\x80\x9339). The <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> proved disappointing to its supporters; it was unable to resolve any of the threats posed by the dictators. British policy was to "appease" them in the hopes they would be satiated. League-authorized sanctions against Italy for its invasion of Ethiopia had support in Britain but proved a failure and were dropped in 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Germany was the difficult case. By 1930 British leaders and intellectuals largely agreed that all major powers shared the blame for war in 1914, and not Germany alone as the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> specified. Therefore, they believed the punitive harshness of the Treaty of Versailles was unwarranted, and this view, adopted by politicians and the public, was largely responsible for supporting appeasement policies down to 1938. That is, German rejections of treaty provisions seemed justified.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coming_of_Second_World_War">Coming of Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Coming of Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Causes_of_World_War_II" title="Causes of World War II">Causes of World War II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_history_of_World_War_II" title="Diplomatic history of World War II">Diplomatic history of World War II</a></div>\n<p>By late 1938 it was clear that war was looming, and that Germany had the world\'s most powerful military. British military leaders warned that Germany would win a war, and Britain needed another year or two to catch up in terms of aviation and air defence. <a href="/wiki/Appeasement" title="Appeasement">Appeasement</a> of Germany\xe2\x80\x94giving in to its demands\xe2\x80\x94was the government\'s policy until early 1939. The final act of appeasement came when Britain and France sacrificed Czechoslovakia to Hitler\'s demands at the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Agreement" title="Munich Agreement">Munich Agreement</a> of 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> Instead of satiation Hitler then seized the rest of Czechoslovakia in March 1939 and menaced Poland. In response Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Neville Chamberlain</a> rejected further appeasement and stood firm in promising to defend Poland. Hitler unexpectedly <a href="/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact" title="Molotov\xe2\x80\x93Ribbentrop Pact">cut a deal</a> with <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> to divide Eastern Europe; when Germany did invade Poland in September 1939, Britain and France declared war; the British Commonwealth followed London\'s lead.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While the Church of England was historically identified with the upper classes, and with the rural gentry, <a href="/wiki/William_Temple_(bishop)" title="William Temple (bishop)">William Temple</a> (1881\xe2\x80\x931944) was both a prolific theologian and a social activist, preaching <a href="/wiki/Christian_socialism" title="Christian socialism">Christian socialism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> He served as bishop of Manchester and York, and in 1942 became Archbishop of Canturbury. He advocated a broad and inclusive membership in the Church of England as a means of continuing and expanding the church\'s position as the established church. Temple was troubled by the high degree of animosity inside, and between the leading religious groups in Britain. In the 1930s he promoted ecumenicism, working to establish better relationships with the Nonconformists, Jews and Catholics, managing in the process to overcome his anti-Catholic bias.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slow_decline_in_religiosity">Slow decline in religiosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Slow decline in religiosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although the overall population was growing steadily, and the Catholic membership was keeping pace, the Protestants were slipping behind. Out of 30\xe2\x80\x9350 million adults, they dropped slowly from 5.7 million members in 1920, and 5.4 million in 1940, to 4.3 million in 1970. <sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> The Church of England decline was parallel. Methodism, the largest of the Nonconformist churches reached a peak of 841,000 members in Great Britain in 1910, slipped to 802,000 in 1920, 792,000 in 1940 729,000 in 1960, and 488,000 in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup> The Nonconformists had built a strong base In industrial districts that specialized in mining textiles agriculture and fishing; those were declining industries, who share of the total male workforce Was in steady decline, from 21 percent in 1921 to 13 percent in 1951. As the families migrated to southern England, or to the suburbs, they often lost contact with their childhood religion.<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> The political reverberations were most serious for the Liberal Party, which was largely based in the nonconformist community, and which rapidly lost membership in the 1920s as its leadership quarreled, the Irish Catholics and many from the working-class moved to the Labour Party, and part of the middle class moved to the Conservative party.<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> Hoping to stem the membership decline, the three major Methodist groups merged in 1932. In Scotland the two major Presbyterian groups, the Church of Scotland and the United Free Church, merged in 1929 for the same reason. Nonetheless the steady declension continued.<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup> The nonconformist churches showed not just a decline in membership but a dramatic fall in enthusiasm. Sunday school attendance plummeted; there were far fewer new ministers. Antagonism toward the Anglican church sharply declined, and many prominent nonconformists became Anglicans, including some leading ministers. There was a falling away in the size and fervour of congregations, less interest in funding missionaries, a decline in intellectualism, and persistent complaints about the lack of money.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup> Commentator <a href="/wiki/Denis_William_Brogan" title="Denis William Brogan">D.W. Brogan</a> reported in 1943:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>in the generation that has passed since the great Liberal landslide of 1906, one of the greatest changes in the English religious and social landscape has been the decline of Nonconformity. Partly that decline has been due to the general weakening of the hold of Christianity on the English people, partly it is been due to the comparative irrelevance of the peculiarly Nonconformist (as a part from Christian) view of the contemporary world and its problems."<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>One aspect of the long-term decline in religiosity was that Protestant showed less and less interest in sending their children to religious schools. In localities across England, fierce battles were fought between the Nonconformists, Anglicans, and Catholics, each with their own school systems supported by taxes, and secular schools, and taxpayers. The Nonconformists had long taken the lead in fighting the Anglicans, who a century before had practically monopolized education. The Anglican share of the elementary school population fell from 57% in 1918 to 39% in 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup> With the sustained decline in Nonconformist enthusiasm their schools closed one after another. In 1902 the Methodist Church operated 738 schools; only 28 remained in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">&#91;130&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Britain continued to think of itself is a Christian country; there were a few atheists or nonbelievers, but unlike the continent, there was no anti-clericalism worthy of note. A third or more prayed every day. Large majorities used formal church services to mark birth, marriage and death.<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">&#91;131&#93;</a></sup> The great majority believed in God and heaven, although belief in hell fell off after all the deaths of the World War.<sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup> After 1918, Church of England services stopped practically all discussion of hell.<sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">&#91;133&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prayer_Book_crisis">Prayer Book crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Prayer Book crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a></div>\n<p>Parliament had governed the Church of England since 1688, but was increasingly eager to turn control over to the church itself. It passed the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England_Assembly_(Powers)_Act_1919" title="Church of England Assembly (Powers) Act 1919">Church of England Assembly (Powers) Act 1919</a> to establish the Church Assembly, with three houses for bishops, clergy, and laity, and permitted it to legislate regulations for the Church, subject to formal approval of Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">&#91;134&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A crisis suddenly emerged in 1927 over the Church\'s proposal to revise the classic <i>Book of Common Prayer,</i> which had been in daily use since 1662. The goal was to better incorporate moderate <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Catholicism" title="Anglo-Catholicism">Anglo-Catholicism</a> into the life of the Church. The bishops sought a more tolerant, comprehensive established Church. After internal debate the Church Assembly gave its approval. Evangelicals inside the Church, and Nonconformists outside, were outraged because they understood England\'s religious national identity to be emphatically Protestant and anti-Catholic. They denounced the revisions as a concession to ritualism and tolerance of Roman Catholicism. They mobilized support in parliament, which twice rejected the revisions after intensely heated debates. The Anglican hierarchy compromised in 1929, while strictly prohibiting extreme and Anglo-Catholic practices.<sup id="cite_ref-135" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-135">&#91;135&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-136" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-136">&#91;136&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divorce_and_the_abdication_of_the_King">Divorce and the abdication of the King</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Divorce and the abdication of the King">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Standards of morality in Britain changed dramatically after the world wars, in the direction of more personal freedom, especially in sexual matters. The Church tried to hold the line, and was especially concerned to stop the rapid trend toward divorce. In 1935 it reaffirmed that, "in no circumstances can Christian men or women re-marry during the lifetime of a wife or a husband."<sup id="cite_ref-137" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-137">&#91;137&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosmo_Lang" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmo Lang">Cosmo Lang</a>, held that the King, as the head of the Church of England, could not marry a divorc\xc3\xa9e.<sup id="cite_ref-138" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-138">&#91;138&#93;</a></sup> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a> objected vigorously, noting that "although it is true that standards are lower since the war it only leads people to expect a higher standard from their King."<sup id="cite_ref-139" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-139">&#91;139&#93;</a></sup> Baldwin was supported by his Conservative Party (except Churchill), as well as the Labour Party, and the prime ministers of the Commonwealth. King Edward VIII therefore was <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII_abdication_crisis" title="Edward VIII abdication crisis">forced to abdicate the throne in 1936</a> when he insisted on marrying an American divorc\xc3\xa9e. Although public opinion gave him considerable support, elite opinion was hostile, and he was practically forced into exile. Archbishop Lang in a radio broadcast lashed out, blaming the upper-class social circles that Edward frequented:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Even more strange and sad it is that he should have sought his happiness in a manner inconsistent with the Christian principles of marriage, and within a social circle whose standards and ways of life are alien to all the best instincts and traditions of the people....Let those who belong to this circle know that to-day they stand rebuked by the judgment of the nation which loved King Edward.<sup id="cite_ref-140" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-140">&#91;140&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>Edward\'s biographer <a href="/wiki/Philip_Ziegler" title="Philip Ziegler">Philip Ziegler</a> argues that Edward was poorly prepared to be King, because of deep personal weaknesses; he was inconsistent, superficial and incapable of resisting distractions, and handled the constitutional issues poorly.<sup id="cite_ref-141" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-141">&#91;141&#93;</a></sup> Frank Mort argues that cultural historians have read the abdication story not so much as a constitutional crisis, but as an indicator of:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>The ascendancy of a female ethos of domesticity and privacy....Intense interest in the King\'s affair ...[exemplified] this obsession with personal life, which was itself part of the media-fueled emotional character of the late 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-142" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-142">&#91;142&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/John_Charmley" title="John Charmley">John Charmley</a> argues in the history of the Conservative Party that Baldwin was pushing for more democracy, and less of an old aristocratic upper-class tone. Monarchy was to be a national foundation, whereby the head of the Church. the State, and the Empire, by drawing upon 1000 years of tradition, could unify the nation.  George V was an ideal fit: "an ordinary little man with the philistine tastes of most of his subjects, he could be presented as the archetypical English paterfamilias getting on with his duties without fuss." Charmley finds that George V and Baldwin, \xe2\x80\x9cmade a formidable conservative team, with their ordinary, honest, English decency proving the first (and most effective) bulwark against revolution.\xe2\x80\x9d Edward VIII, flaunting his upper-class playboy style, suffered from an unstable neurotic character. He needed a strong stabilizing partner\xe2\x80\x94a role Mrs. Simpson was unable to provide. Baldwin\xe2\x80\x99s final achievement was to smooth the way for Edward to abdicate in favor of his younger brother who became George VI. Father and son both demonstrated the value of a democratic king during the severe physical and psychological hardships of the world wars, and their tradition was carried on by Elizabeth II. <sup id="cite_ref-143" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-143">&#91;143&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers">Newspapers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Newspapers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the war, the major newspapers engaged in a large-scale circulation race. The political parties, which long had sponsored their own papers, could not keep up, and one after another their outlets were sold or closed down.<sup id="cite_ref-144" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-144">&#91;144&#93;</a></sup> Sales in the millions depended on popular stories, with a strong human interesting theme, as well as detailed sports reports with the latest scores. Serious news was a niche market and added very little to the circulation base. The niche was dominated by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and, to a lesser extent, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a>.</i> Consolidation was rampant, as local dailies were bought up and added to chains based in London. James Curran and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Seaton" title="Jean Seaton">Jean Seaton</a> report:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>after the death of Lord Northcliffe in 1922, four men\xe2\x80\x93Lords <a href="/wiki/Max_Aitken,_1st_Baron_Beaverbrook" title="Max Aitken, 1st Baron Beaverbrook">Beaverbrook</a> (1879\xe2\x80\x931964), <a href="/wiki/Harold_Harmsworth,_1st_Viscount_Rothermere" title="Harold Harmsworth, 1st Viscount Rothermere">Rothermere</a> (1868\xe2\x80\x931940), <a href="/wiki/William_Berry,_1st_Viscount_Camrose" title="William Berry, 1st Viscount Camrose">Camrose</a> (1879\xe2\x80\x931954) and <a href="/wiki/Gomer_Berry,_1st_Viscount_Kemsley" title="Gomer Berry, 1st Viscount Kemsley">Kemsley</a> (1883\xe2\x80\x931968)\xe2\x80\x93became the dominant figures in the inter-war press. In 1937, for instance, they owned nearly one in every two national and local daily papers sold in Britain, as well as one in every three Sunday papers that were sold. The combined circulation of all their newspapers amounted to over thirteen million.<sup id="cite_ref-145" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-145">&#91;145&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> of London was long the most influential prestige newspaper, although far from having the largest circulation. It gave far more attention to serious political and cultural news.<sup id="cite_ref-146" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-146">&#91;146&#93;</a></sup> In 1922, <a href="/wiki/John_Jacob_Astor,_1st_Baron_Astor_of_Hever" title="John Jacob Astor, 1st Baron Astor of Hever">John Jacob Astor</a> (1886\xe2\x80\x931971), son of the <a href="/wiki/William_Waldorf_Astor" title="William Waldorf Astor">1st Viscount Astor</a> (1849\xe2\x80\x931919), bought <i>The Times</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Harmsworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfred Harmsworth">Northcliffe estate</a>. The paper advocated <a href="/wiki/Appeasement" title="Appeasement">appeasement</a> of Hitler\'s demands. Its editor <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Dawson" title="Geoffrey Dawson">Geoffrey Dawson</a> was closely allied with Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Neville Chamberlain</a>, and pushed hard for the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Agreement" title="Munich Agreement">Munich Agreement</a> in 1938. Candid news reports by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Ebbut&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Norman Ebbut (page does not exist)">Norman Ebbut</a> from Berlin that warned of warmongering were rewritten in London to support the appeasement policy. In March 1939, however, it reversed course and called for urgent war preparations.<sup id="cite_ref-147" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-147">&#91;147&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-148" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-148">&#91;148&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expanded_leisure">Expanded leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Expanded leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As leisure, literacy, wealth, ease of travel, and a broadened sense of community grew in Britain from the late 19th century onward, there was more time and interest in leisure activities of all sorts, on the part of all classes.<sup id="cite_ref-_149-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note--149">&#91;149&#93;</a></sup> Drinking was differentiated by class. with upper-class clubs, and working-class and middle-class pubs. However, drinking as a way of spending leisure time and spare cash declined during the Depression and pub attendance never returned to 1930 levels; it fell far below prewar levels.<sup id="cite_ref-150" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-150">&#91;150&#93;</a></sup> Taxes were raised on beer, but there were more alternatives now at hand, such as cigarettes (which attracted 8/10 men, and 4/10 women), the talkies, the dance halls, and Greyhound racing. <a href="/wiki/Football_pools" title="Football pools">Football pools</a> offered the excitement of betting on a range of results. New estates with small, inexpensive houses offered gardening as an outdoor recreation. Church attendance declined to half the level of 1901.<sup id="cite_ref-151" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-151">&#91;151&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The annual holiday became common. Tourists flocked to seaside resorts; <a href="/wiki/Blackpool" title="Blackpool">Blackpool</a> hosted 7 million visitors a year in the 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-152" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-152">&#91;152&#93;</a></sup> Organized leisure was primarily a male activity, with middle-class women allowed in at the margins. Participation in sports and all sorts of leisure activities increased for the average Englishman, and his interest in spectator sports increased dramatically. By the 1920s the cinema and radio attracted all classes, ages and genders in very large numbers, with young women taking the lead.<sup id="cite_ref-153" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-153">&#91;153&#93;</a></sup> Working-class men were boisterous football spectators. They sang along at the music hall, <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_fancier" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon fancier">fancied their pigeons</a>, gambled on horse racing, and took the family to seaside resorts in summer. Political activists complained that working-class leisure diverted men away from revolutionary agitation.<sup id="cite_ref-154" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-154">&#91;154&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinema_and_radio">Cinema and radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Cinema and radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hitchcock,_Alfred_02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Hitchcock%2C_Alfred_02.jpg/220px-Hitchcock%2C_Alfred_02.jpg" width="220" height="244" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Hitchcock%2C_Alfred_02.jpg/330px-Hitchcock%2C_Alfred_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Hitchcock%2C_Alfred_02.jpg/440px-Hitchcock%2C_Alfred_02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="802" data-file-height="890" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hitchcock,_Alfred_02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Film director Alfred Hitchcock, 1955</div></div></div>\n<p>The British film industry emerged in the 1890s, and built heavily on the strong reputation of the London legitimate theatre for actors, directors, and producers.<sup id="cite_ref-155" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-155">&#91;155&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-156" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-156">&#91;156&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-157" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-157">&#91;157&#93;</a></sup> The problem was that the American market was so much larger and richer. It bought up the top talent, especially when Hollywood came to the fore in the 1920s and produced over 80 percent of the total world output. Efforts to fight back were futile \xe2\x80\x94 the government set a quota for British made films, but it failed. Hollywood furthermore dominated the lucrative Canadian and Australian markets. <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">Bollywood</a> (based in Bombay) dominated the huge Indian market.<sup id="cite_ref-158" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-158">&#91;158&#93;</a></sup> The most prominent directors remaining in London were <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a>, an expatriate Hungarian, and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>. There was a revival of creativity in the 1933\xe2\x80\x9345 era, especially with the arrival of Jewish filmmakers and actors fleeing the Nazis.<sup id="cite_ref-159" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-159">&#91;159&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-160" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-160">&#91;160&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, giant palaces were built for the huge audiences that wanted to see Hollywood films. In Liverpool 40 percent of the population attended one of the 69 cinemas once a week; 25 percent went twice. Traditionalists grumbled about the American cultural invasion, but the permanent impact was minor.<sup id="cite_ref-161" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-161">&#91;161&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-162" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-162">&#91;162&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In radio British audiences had no choice apart from the highbrow programming of the BBC, which had a monopoly on broadcasting. <a href="/wiki/John_Reith,_1st_Baron_Reith" title="John Reith, 1st Baron Reith">John Reith</a> (1889 \xe2\x80\x93 1971), an intensely moralistic engineer, was in full charge. His goal was to broadcast, "All that is best in every department of human knowledge, endeavour and achievement.... The preservation of a high moral tone is obviously of paramount importance."<sup id="cite_ref-163" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-163">&#91;163&#93;</a></sup> Reith succeeded in building a high wall against an American-style free-for-all in radio in which the goal was to attract the largest audiences and thereby secure the greatest advertising revenue. There was no paid advertising on the BBC; all the revenue came from a licence fee charged for the possession of receivers. Highbrow audiences, however, greatly enjoyed it.<sup id="cite_ref-164" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-164">&#91;164&#93;</a></sup> At a time when American, Australian and Canadian stations were drawing huge audiences cheering for their local teams with the broadcast of baseball, rugby and ice-hockey, the BBC emphasized service for a national, rather than a regional audience. Boat races were well covered along with tennis and horse racing, but the BBC was reluctant to spend its severely limited air time on long football or cricket games, regardless of their popularity.<sup id="cite_ref-165" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-165">&#91;165&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-166" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-166">&#91;166&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sport_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Sport in the United Kingdom">Sport in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The British showed a more profound interest in sports, and in greater variety, than any rival. <sup id="cite_ref-167" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-167">&#91;167&#93;</a></sup> They gave pride of place to such moral issues as sportsmanship and fair play.<sup id="cite_ref-_149-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note--149">&#91;149&#93;</a></sup> Cricket became symbolic of the Imperial spirit throughout the Empire. Football proved highly attractive to the urban working classes, which introduced the rowdy spectator to the sports world. In some sports there was significant controversy in the fight for amateur purity especially in rugby and rowing. New games became popular almost overnight, including golf, lawn tennis, cycling and hockey. Women were much more likely to enter these sports than the old established ones. The aristocracy and landed gentry, with their ironclad control over land rights, dominated hunting, shooting, fishing and horse racing.<sup id="cite_ref-168" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-168">&#91;168&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-169" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-169">&#91;169&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cricket had become well-established among the English upper class in the 18th century, and was a major factor in sports competition among the public schools. Army units around the Empire had time on their hands, and encouraged the locals to learn cricket so they could have some entertaining competition. Most of the Empire embraced cricket, with the exception of Canada. Cricket test matches (international) began by the 1870s; the most famous are those between Australia and Britain for <a href="/wiki/The_Ashes" title="The Ashes">The Ashes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-170" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-170">&#91;170&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For sports to become fully professionalized, coaching had to come first. It gradually professionalized in the Victorian era and the role was well established by 1914. In the First World War, military units sought out the coaches to supervise physical conditioning and develop morale-building teams.<sup id="cite_ref-171" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-171">&#91;171&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reading">Reading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interwar_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Reading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As literacy and leisure time expanded after 1900 reading became a popular pastime. New additions to adult fiction doubled during the 1920s, reaching 2800 new books a year by 1935. Libraries tripled their stock, and saw heavy demand for new fiction.<sup id="cite_ref-172" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-172">&#91;172&#93;</a></sup> A dramatic innovation was the inexpensive paperback, pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Allen_Lane" title="Allen Lane">Allen Lane</a> (1902\xe2\x80\x9370) at <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a> in 1935. The first titles included novels by Ernest Hemingway and Agatha Christie. They were sold cheaply (usually sixpence) in a wide variety of inexpensive stores such as Woolworth\'s. Penguin aimed at an educated middle class "middlebrow" audience. It avoided the downscale image of American paperbacks. The line signalled cultural self-improvement and political education. The more polemical Penguin Specials, typically with a leftist orientation for Labour readers, were widely distributed during the Second World War.<sup id="cite_ref-173" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-173">&#91;173&#93;</a></sup> However the war years caused a shortage of staff for publishers and book stores, and a severe shortage of rationed paper, worsened by the air raid on <a href="/wiki/Paternoster_Row" title="Paternoster Row">Paternoster Row</a> in 1940 that burned 5 million books in warehouses.<sup id="cite_ref-174" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-174">&#91;174&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Romantic fiction was especially popular, with <a href="/wiki/Mills_and_Boon" class="mw-redirect" title="Mills and Boon">Mills and Boon</a> the leading publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-175" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-175">&#91;175&#93;</a></sup> Romantic encounters were embodied in a principle of sexual purity that demonstrated not only social conservatism, but also how heroines could control their personal autonomy.<sup id="cite_ref-176" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-176">&#91;176&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-177" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-177">&#91;177&#93;</a></sup> Adventure magazines became quite popular, especially those published by <a href="/wiki/DC_Thomson" title="DC Thomson">DC Thomson</a>; the publisher sent observers around the country to talk to boys and learn what they wanted to read about. The story line in magazines, comic books and cinema that most appealed to boys was the glamorous heroism of British soldiers fighting wars that were exciting and just.<sup id="cite_ref-178" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-178">&#91;178&#93;</a></sup> D.C. Thomson issued the first <a href="/wiki/The_Dandy" title="The Dandy"><i>The Dandy Comic</i></a> in December 1937. It had a revolutionary design that broke away from the usual children\'s comics that were published broadsheet in size and not very colourful. Thomson capitalized on its success with a similar product <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beano" title="The Beano">The Beano</a></i> in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-179" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-179">&#91;179&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
