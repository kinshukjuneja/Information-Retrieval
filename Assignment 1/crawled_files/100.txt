18th century map of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The written <b>history of Philadelphia</b> begins on October 27, 1682, when the city was founded by <a href="/wiki/William_Penn" title="William Penn">William Penn</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">English Crown</a> <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pennsylvania" title="Province of Pennsylvania">Province of Pennsylvania</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill</a> rivers.\n</p><p>Before then, the area was inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Lenape (Delaware)</a> <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Indians</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">Swedish</a> settlers who arrived in the area in the early 1600s. <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> quickly grew into an important <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">colonial</a> city and during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolution</a> was the site of the <a href="/wiki/First_Continental_Congress" title="First Continental Congress">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congresses</a>. After the Revolution the city was chosen to be the temporary capital of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. At the beginning of the 19th century, the federal and state governments left Philadelphia, but the city remained the cultural and financial center of the country. Philadelphia became one of the first U.S. industrial centers and the city contained a variety of industries, the largest being <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> Philadelphia\'s government was controlled by a corrupt <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_machine" title="Political machine">political machine</a> and by the beginning of the 20th Century Philadelphia was described as "corrupt and contented." Various reform efforts slowly changed city government with the most significant in 1950 where a new city charter strengthened the position of <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> and weakened the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_City_Council" title="Philadelphia City Council">Philadelphia City Council</a>. At the same time Philadelphia moved its support from the Republican Party to the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> which has since created a strong Democratic organization. The city began a population decline in the 1950s as mostly white and middle-class families left for the suburbs. Many of Philadelphia\'s houses were in poor condition and lacked proper facilities, and <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">gang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mafia" title="Mafia">mafia</a> warfare plagued the city. Revitalization and <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> of certain neighborhoods started bringing people back to the city. Promotions and incentives in the 1990s and the early 21st century have improved the city\'s image and created a <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominium</a> boom in <a href="/wiki/Center_City,_Philadelphia" title="Center City, Philadelphia">Center City</a> and the surrounding areas that has slowed the population decline.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1681, King Charles II gave Penn a large piece of his newly acquired American land holdings to repay a debt the king owed to <a href="/wiki/Admiral_Sir_William_Penn" class="mw-redirect" title="Admiral Sir William Penn">Admiral Sir William Penn</a>, Penn\'s father. This land included present-day <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, though the claim as written would create a bloody conflict with <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> (dubbed <a href="/wiki/Cresap%27s_War" title="Cresap&#39;s War">Cresap\'s War</a>) over the land grant already owned by Lord Baltimore. Penn put together a colonial expedition and fleet, which set out for America in the middle of the following summer. Penn, sailing in the vanguard, first set foot on American soil at the colony at <a href="/wiki/New_Castle,_Delaware" title="New Castle, Delaware">New Castle, Delaware</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> An orderly change of government ensued, as was normal in an age used to the privileges and prerogatives of aristocracy and which antedated <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>: the colonists pledged allegiance to Penn as their new <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_colony" title="Proprietary colony">Proprietor</a>. The first <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_General_Assembly" title="Pennsylvania General Assembly">Pennsylvania General Assembly</a> was soon held in the colony.\n</p><p>Afterwards, Penn journeyed up the river and founded Philadelphia with a core group of accompanying Quakers and others seeking religious freedom on lands he purchased from the local chieftains of the Lenape or <a href="/wiki/Delaware_nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Delaware nation">Delaware nation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-smithsonian_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonian-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This began a long period of peaceful co-operation between the colony and the Delaware, in contrast to the frictions between the tribe and the Swedish and Dutch colonists.<sup id="cite_ref-AmHeritageBk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmHeritageBk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, the new colonists would not enjoy such easy relations with the rival and territorial <a href="/wiki/Susquehannock" title="Susquehannock">Conestoga peoples</a> to the west for a number of decades<sup id="cite_ref-smithsonian_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonian-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> as the English Quaker and German Anabaptist, Lutheran and Moravian settlers attracted to the religiously tolerant colony<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> worked their way northwest up the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill</a> and due west south of the hill country into the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Dutch" title="Pennsylvania Dutch">breadbasket lands</a> along the lower <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_River" title="Susquehanna River">Susquehanna River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AmHeritageBk_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmHeritageBk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cecilius_Calvert,_2nd_Baron_Baltimore" class="mw-redirect" title="Cecilius Calvert, 2nd Baron Baltimore">Lord Baltimore</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Maryland" title="Province of Maryland">Province of Maryland</a> had circa 1652\xe2\x80\x9353<sup id="cite_ref-AmHeritageBk_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmHeritageBk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> finished waging a decade long <i>declared war</i> against the Susquehannocks and the Dutch,<sup id="cite_ref-AmHeritageBk_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmHeritageBk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> who\'d been trading them <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">furs</a> for tools and firearms for some time.<sup id="cite_ref-AmHeritageBk_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmHeritageBk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Both groups had uneasy relations with the Delaware (Lenape) and the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AmHeritageBk_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmHeritageBk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, Penn\'s <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> government was not viewed favorably by the <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">Swedish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/British_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="British colonization of the Americas">English</a> settlers in what is now <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>. They had no "historical" allegiance to Pennsylvania, so they almost immediately began petitioning for their own Assembly. In 1704, they achieved their goal when the three southernmost counties of Pennsylvania were permitted to split off and become the new semi-autonomous <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Colony" title="Delaware Colony">colony of Lower Delaware</a>. New Castle, the most prominent, prosperous and influential settlement in the new colony, became the capital. During its brief period of ascendancy as an empire following the victory by <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden" title="Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden">Gustav the Great</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Breitenfeld_(1631)" title="Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)">Battle of Breitenfeld</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Sweden">Swedish</a> settlers arrived in the area in the early 17th century to found a nearby colony, <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a> in what is today southern <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. With the arrival of more numerous English colonists and development of the port on the Delaware, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> quickly grew into an important <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">colonial</a> city.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolution</a>, it was the site of the <a href="/wiki/First_Continental_Congress" title="First Continental Congress">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congresses</a>. After the Revolution, the city was chosen to be the temporary capital of the United States from 1790\xe2\x80\x931800.\n</p><p>At the beginning of the 19th century, the federal and state governments left Philadelphia, but the city continued for some years to be the country\'s cultural and financial center. Its large free black community aided fugitive slaves and founded the first independent black denomination in the nation, the <a href="/wiki/African_Methodist_Episcopal_Church" title="African Methodist Episcopal Church">African Methodist Episcopal Church</a>. Philadelphia became one of the first U.S. industrial centers with a variety of industries, the largest being <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>. It had many economic and family ties to the South, with southern planters maintaining second homes in the city and having business connections with banks, sending their daughters to French finishing schools run by refugees from <a href="/wiki/Saint-Domingue" title="Saint-Domingue">Saint-Domingue</a> (<a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>), selling their <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> to textile manufacturers, which in turn sold some products to the South, for instance, clothing for slaves. At the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, there were many southern sympathizers, although most city residents became firmly Union as the war went on.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, city government was controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a>; it established a <a href="/wiki/Political_machine" title="Political machine">political machine</a> that gained power through patronage.  By the beginning of the 20th century, Philadelphia was described as "corrupt and contented."  Various reform efforts slowly changed city government; in 1950, a new city charter strengthened the position of <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> and weakened the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_City_Council" title="Philadelphia City Council">Philadelphia City Council</a>. Beginning during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, voters changed from traditional support for the Republican Party to increasing support for the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> of President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, which has now been predominant in local politics for many decades.\n</p><p>The population grew dramatically at the end of the 19th and beginning of the 20th centuries, through immigration from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a> of blacks from the rural South and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican people">Puerto Ricans</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, all attracted to the city\'s expanding industrial jobs.  The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> was expanding and hired 10,000 workers from the South. Manufacturing plants and the US Navy Yard employed tens of thousands of industrial workers along the rivers, and the city was also a center of finance and publishing, with major universities. By the 1950s, much Philadelphia housing was aged and substandard. In the post-World War II era of suburbanization and construction of area highways, many middle-class families met their demand for newer housing by leaving the city for the suburbs.  Population decline accompanied the industrial restructuring and the loss of tens of thousands of jobs in the mid 20th century. With increasing poverty and social dislocation in the city, <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">gang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_crime_family" title="Philadelphia crime family">mafia</a> warfare plagued the city in from the mid-20th century to the early 21st century.\n</p><p>By the end of the 20th century and beginning of the 21st, revitalization and <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> of historic neighborhoods attracted an increase in middle-class population as people began to return to the city. New immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> have contributed their energy to the city. Promotions and incentives in the 1990s and the early 21st century have improved the city\'s image and created a <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominium</a> boom in <a href="/wiki/Center_City,_Philadelphia" title="Center City, Philadelphia">Center City</a> and the surrounding areas.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:167px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Penn_capitol_frieze.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b1/Penn_capitol_frieze.jpg" width="165" height="400" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="165" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Penn_capitol_frieze.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Penn\'s Treaty with the Lenape, from <a href="/wiki/US_Capitol_Rotunda" class="mw-redirect" title="US Capitol Rotunda">US Capitol Rotunda</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Before Philadelphia was founded, the area was inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Lenape (Delaware)</a> <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Indians</a>. The village of <i>Nitap\xc3\xa8kunk</i>, "Place that is easy to get to," was located in today\'s <a href="/wiki/Fairmount_Park" title="Fairmount Park">Fairmount Park</a> area.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The villages of <i>P\xc3\xa8mikpeka</i>, "Where the water flows,"<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Shackamaxon" title="Shackamaxon">Shackamaxon</a> were located on the Delaware River.\n</p><p>The European colonization of the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Valley" title="Delaware Valley">Delaware River Valley</a> (called the <i>Zuyd</i>, meaning "South" River, or <i>L\xc3\xabnapei Sipu</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> at the time) began in 1609 when a Dutch expedition led by Henry Hudson first entered the river in search of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a>.  The Valley, including the future location of Philadelphia, became part of the <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a> claim of the Dutch and Dutch explorer <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Jacobsen_Mey" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornelius Jacobsen Mey">Cornelius Jacobsen Mey</a> (after whom Cape May, New Jersey is named) charted the shoals Delaware Bay in the 1620s.  The Dutch built a fort on the west side of the bay at <a href="/wiki/Zwaanendael_Colony" title="Zwaanendael Colony">Swanendael</a>.\n</p><p>In 1637, Swedish, Dutch and German stockholders formed the <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a> Company to trade for furs and tobacco in North America. Under the command of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Minuit" title="Peter Minuit">Peter Minuit</a>, the company\'s first expedition sailed from Sweden late in 1637 in two ships, <i><a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Nyckel" title="Kalmar Nyckel">Kalmar Nyckel</a></i> and <i>Fogel Gri</i>. Minuit had been the governor of the <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a> from 1626 to 1631.  Resenting his dismissal by the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_West_India_Company" title="Dutch West India Company">Dutch West India Company</a> he had brought to the new project the knowledge that the Dutch colony had temporarily abandoned its efforts in the Delaware Valley to focus on the Hudson River valley to the north. (The Hudson was known to the Dutch as the <i>Noort</i>, or "North" river relative to "South" of the Delaware.)  Minuit and his partners further knew that the Dutch view of colonies held that actual occupation was necessary to secure legal claim.  The ships reached Delaware Bay in March 1638, and the settlers began to build a fort at the site of present-day <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_Delaware" title="Wilmington, Delaware">Wilmington, Delaware</a>. They named it <a href="/wiki/Fort_Christina" title="Fort Christina">Fort Christina</a>, in honor of the twelve-year-old <a href="/wiki/Christina_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Christina of Sweden">Queen Christina</a> of Sweden. It was the first permanent European settlement in the Delaware Valley.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Part of this colony eventually included land on the west side of the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> from just below the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill River</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Johan_Bj%C3%B6rnsson_Printz" title="Johan Bj\xc3\xb6rnsson Printz">Johan Bj\xc3\xb6rnsson Printz</a>, who it had been ennobled, was appointed to be the first royal governor of New Sweden, arriving in the colony on 15 February 1643. Under his ten-year rule, the administrative center of <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a> was moved north to <a href="/wiki/Tinicum_Township,_Delaware_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Tinicum Township, Delaware County, Pennsylvania">Tinicum Island</a> (to the immediate SW of today\'s Philadelphia), where he built Fort New Gothenburg and his own manor house which he called <a href="/wiki/The_Printzhof" title="The Printzhof">the Printzhof</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first English settlement occurred about 1642, when 50 <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> families from the <a href="/wiki/New_Haven_Colony" title="New Haven Colony">New Haven Colony</a> in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, led by George Lamberton, tried to establish a <a href="/wiki/Theocracy" title="Theocracy">theocracy</a> at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill River</a>.  The New Haven Colony had earlier struck a deal with the Lenape to buy much of New Jersey south of present-day Trenton.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Dutch and Swedes in the area burned the English colonists\'  buildings.  A Swedish court under Swedish Governor <a href="/wiki/Johan_Bj%C3%B6rnsson_Printz" title="Johan Bj\xc3\xb6rnsson Printz">Johan Bj\xc3\xb6rnsson Printz</a> convicted Lamberton of "trespassing, conspiring with the Indians."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The offshoot New Haven colony received no support.  The Puritan Governor <a href="/wiki/John_Winthrop" title="John Winthrop">John Winthrop</a> said it was dissolved owing to summer "sickness and mortality."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The disaster contributed to New Haven\'s losing control of its area to the larger <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Colony" title="Connecticut Colony">Connecticut Colony</a>.\n</p><p>In 1644, <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a> supported the <a href="/wiki/Susquehannock" title="Susquehannock">Susquehannock</a> in their victory in a war against the English Province of Maryland (led by General Harrison II). The Dutch never recognized the legitimacy of the Swedish claim and, in the late summer of 1655, Director-General <a href="/wiki/Peter_Stuyvesant" title="Peter Stuyvesant">Peter Stuyvesant</a> of New Amsterdam mustered a military expedition to the Delaware Valley to subdue the rogue colony. Although the colonists had to recognize the authority of <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a>, the Dutch terms were tolerant.  The <a href="/wiki/Swedish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish people">Swedish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finnish</a> settlers continued to enjoy a much local autonomy, having their own militia, religion, court, and lands. This official status lasted until the English conquest of <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a> in October 1664, and continued unofficially until the area was included in William Penn\'s charter for Pennsylvania in 1682.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> By 1682, the area of modern Philadelphia was inhabited by about fifty Europeans, mostly <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsistence farming">subsistence farmers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p3_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p3-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1681, as part of a repayment of a debt, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a> granted <a href="/wiki/William_Penn" title="William Penn">William Penn</a> a <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a> for what would become the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pennsylvania" title="Province of Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania colony</a>. Shortly after receiving the charter, Penn said he would lay out "a large Towne or Citty in the most Convenient place upon the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> for health &amp; Navigation."<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p4_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p4-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Penn wanted the city to live peacefully in the area, without a fortress or <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">walls</a>, so he bought the land from the Lenape. The <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legend</a> is that Penn made a treaty of friendship with Lenape chief <a href="/wiki/Tamanend" title="Tamanend">Tammany</a> under an elm tree at <a href="/wiki/Shackamaxon" title="Shackamaxon">Shackamaxon</a>, in what became the city\'s <a href="/wiki/Kensington_District,_Pennsylvania" title="Kensington District, Pennsylvania">Kensington District</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p4-5_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p4-5-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Penn envisioned a city where all people regardless of religion could worship freely and live together. Being a <a href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quaker</a>, Penn had experienced religious persecution. He also planned that the city\'s streets would be set up in a grid, with the idea that the city would be more like the rural towns of England than its crowded cities. The homes would be spread far apart and surrounded by gardens and orchards. The city granted the first purchasers land along the Delaware River for their homes. It had access to the Delaware Bay and Atlantic Ocean, and became an important port in the Thirteen Colonies. He named the city Philadelphia (<i>philos</i>, "love" or "friendship", and <i>adelphos</i>, "brother"); it was to have a commercial center for a market, state house, and other key buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Avery1999p19_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery1999p19-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seal_philadelphia_william_penn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ea/Seal_philadelphia_william_penn.jpg" width="150" height="173" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="150" data-file-height="173" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seal_philadelphia_william_penn.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Philadelphia\'s seal when founded</div></div></div>\n<p>Penn sent three commissioners to supervise the settlement and to set aside 10,000 acres (40&#160;km\xc2\xb2) for the city. The commissioners bought land from Swedes at the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Wicaco" class="mw-redirect" title="Wicaco">Wicaco</a>, and from there began to lay out the city toward the north. The area went about a mile along the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> between modern <a href="/wiki/South_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="South Street (Philadelphia)">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interstate_676" title="Interstate 676">Vine Streets</a>. Penn\'s ship anchored off the coast of <a href="/wiki/New_Castle,_Delaware" title="New Castle, Delaware">New Castle, Delaware</a>, on October 27, 1682, and he arrived in Philadelphia a few days after that.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He expanded the city west to the bank of the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill River</a>, for a total of 1,200 acres (4.8&#160;km\xc2\xb2). Streets were laid out in a <a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">gridiron</a> system. Except for the two widest streets, <a href="/wiki/Market_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Market Street (Philadelphia)">High (now Market)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broad_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Broad Street (Philadelphia)">Broad</a>, the streets were named after prominent landowners who owned adjacent lots. The streets were renamed in 1684; the ones running east-west were named after local trees (<a href="/wiki/Interstate_676" title="Interstate 676">Vine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Race_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Race Street (Philadelphia)">Sassafras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arch_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Arch Street (Philadelphia)">Mulberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Cherry Street (Philadelphia)">Cherry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Chestnut Street (Philadelphia)">Chestnut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walnut_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Walnut Street (Philadelphia)">Walnut</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Locust_Street_(Philadelphia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Locust Street (Philadelphia) (page does not exist)">Locust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spruce_Street_(Philadelphia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spruce Street (Philadelphia)">Spruce</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pine_Street_(Philadelphia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pine Street (Philadelphia) (page does not exist)">Pine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lombard_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="Lombard Street (Philadelphia)">Lombard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Street_(Philadelphia)" title="South Street (Philadelphia)">Cedar</a>) and the north-south streets were <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_numbered_streets_in_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="List of numbered streets in Philadelphia (page does not exist)">numbered</a>. Within the area, four squares (now named <a href="/wiki/Rittenhouse_Square" title="Rittenhouse Square">Rittenhouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logan_Circle_(Philadelphia)" title="Logan Circle (Philadelphia)">Logan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_Square_(Philadelphia)" title="Washington Square (Philadelphia)">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Square_(Philadelphia)" title="Franklin Square (Philadelphia)">Franklin</a>) were set aside as parks open for everyone. Penn designed a central square at the intersection of Broad and what is now Market Street to be surrounded by public buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p7_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p7-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some of the first settlers lived in caves dug out of the river bank, but the city grew with construction of homes, churches, and wharves. The new landowners did not share Penn\'s vision of a non-congested city. Most people bought land along the Delaware River instead of spreading westward towards the Schuylkill. The lots they bought were subdivided and resold with smaller streets constructed between them. Before 1704, few people lived west of Fourth Street.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p14-16_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p14-16-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_growth">Early growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Philadelphia grew from a few hundred inhabitants in 1683 to over 2,500 in 1701. The population was mostly English, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a>, Irish, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germans</a>, Swedes, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finns</a>,and Dutch. Before William Penn left Philadelphia for the last time on October 25, 1701 he issued the Charter of 1701.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The charter established Philadelphia as a city and gave the <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">aldermen</a>, and councilmen the authority to issue laws and ordinances and regulate markets and fairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p26_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p26-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The first known <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a> resident of Philadelphia was Jonas Aaron, a German who moved to the city in 1703. He is mentioned in an article entitled "A Philadelphia Business Directory of 1703," by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_H._Browning&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles H. Browning (page does not exist)">Charles H. Browning</a>. It was published in <i>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Historical_Register&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Historical Register (page does not exist)">American Historical Register</a>,</i>  in April, 1895.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Philadelphia became an important trading center and major port. Initially the city\'s main source of trade was with the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">West Indies</a>, which had established sugar cane plantations. It was part of the Triangle Trade, associated with Africa and the British Isles.  During <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_War" title="Queen Anne&#39;s War">Queen Anne\'s War</a> (1702 and 1713) with the French, trade was cut off to the West Indies, hurting Philadelphia financially. The end of the war brought brief prosperity to all of the British territories, but a <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">depression</a> in the 1720s stunted Philadelphia\'s growth. The 1720s and \'30s saw immigration from mostly Germany and northern <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> to Philadelphia and the surrounding countryside. The region was developed for agriculture and Philadelphia exported grains, lumber products and <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> seeds to Europe and elsewhere in the American colonies; this pulled the city out of the depression.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p35-37_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p35-37-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Philadelphia\'s pledge of religious tolerance attracted many other religions beside Quakers. <a href="/wiki/Mennonite" class="mw-redirect" title="Mennonite">Mennonites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jews</a> moved to the city and soon outnumbered the Quakers, but they continued to be powerful economically and politically. Political tensions existed between and within the religious groups, which also had national connections.  <a href="/wiki/Riot" title="Riot">Riots</a> in 1741 and 1742 took place over high bread prices and drunken sailors.  In October 1742 and the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Election_riot" title="Philadelphia Election riot">"Bloody Election"</a> riots, sailors attacked Quakers and pacifist Germans, whose peace politics were strained by the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Jenkins%27_Ear" title="War of Jenkins&#39; Ear">War of Jenkins\' Ear</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p60_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p60-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The city was plagued by pickpockets and other petty criminals. Working in the city government had such a poor reputation that fines were imposed on citizens who refused to serve an office after being chosen. One man fled Philadelphia to avoid serving as mayor.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p62_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p62-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Benjamin_Franklin_by_Jean-Baptiste_Greuze.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Benjamin_Franklin_by_Jean-Baptiste_Greuze.jpg/170px-Benjamin_Franklin_by_Jean-Baptiste_Greuze.jpg" width="170" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Benjamin_Franklin_by_Jean-Baptiste_Greuze.jpg/255px-Benjamin_Franklin_by_Jean-Baptiste_Greuze.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2f/Benjamin_Franklin_by_Jean-Baptiste_Greuze.jpg 2x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="355" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Benjamin_Franklin_by_Jean-Baptiste_Greuze.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Benjamin Franklin</div></div></div>\n<p>In the first half the 18th century, like other American cities, Philadelphia was dirty, with garbage and animals littering the streets. The roads were unpaved and in rainy seasons impassable. Early attempts to improve quality of life were ineffective as laws were poorly enforced.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p59_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p59-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> By the 1750s, Philadelphia was turning into a major city. <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church,_Philadelphia" title="Christ Church, Philadelphia">Christ Church</a> and the Pennsylvania State House, better known as <a href="/wiki/Independence_Hall_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independence Hall (United States)">Independence Hall</a>, were built.  Streets were paved and illuminated with oil lamps.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p68-69_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p68-69-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Philadelphia\'s first <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Bradford" title="Andrew Bradford">Andrew Bradford</a>\'s <i>American Weekly Mercury</i>, began publishing on December 22, 1719.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p65_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p65-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The city also developed culturally and scientifically. Schools, libraries and theaters were founded. <a href="/wiki/James_Logan_(statesman)" title="James Logan (statesman)">James Logan</a> arrived in Philadelphia in 1701 as a secretary for William Penn. He was the first to help establish Philadelphia as a place of culture and learning.<sup id="cite_ref-insight25_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-insight25-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Logan, who was the mayor of Philadelphia in the early 1720s, created one of the largest libraries in the colonies. He also helped guide other prominent Philadelphia residents, which included botanist <a href="/wiki/John_Bartram" title="John Bartram">John Bartram</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>. Benjamin Franklin arrived in Philadelphia in October 1723 and would play a large part in the city\'s development. To help protect the city from fire, Franklin founded the <a href="/wiki/Union_Fire_Company" title="Union Fire Company">Union Fire Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p61_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p61-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In the 1750s Franklin was named one of the city\'s post master generals and he established postal routes between Philadelphia, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a>, and elsewhere. He helped raise money to build the American colonies\' <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Hospital" title="Pennsylvania Hospital">first hospital</a>, which opened in 1752. That same year the <a href="/wiki/The_Academy_and_College_of_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="The Academy and College of Philadelphia">College of Philadelphia</a>, another project of Franklin\'s,  received its charter of incorporation.<sup id="cite_ref-insight25_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-insight25-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Threatened by French and Spanish <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a>, Franklin and others set up a volunteer group for defense and built two batteries.\n</p><p>When the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a> began in 1754 as part of the Seven Years\' War, Franklin recruited <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militias</a>. During the war, the city attracted many refugees from the western frontier. When <a href="/wiki/Pontiac%27s_Rebellion" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontiac&#39;s Rebellion">Pontiac\'s Rebellion</a> occurred in 1763, refugees again fled into the city, including a group of Lenape hiding from other Native Americans, angry at their pacifism, and white frontiersmen. The <a href="/wiki/Paxton_Boys" title="Paxton Boys">Paxton Boys</a> tried to follow them into Philadelphia for attacks, but was prevented by the city\'s militia and Franklin, who convinced them to leave.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p103-108_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p103-108-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolution">Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HillsMap1796.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/HillsMap1796.jpg/220px-HillsMap1796.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/HillsMap1796.jpg/330px-HillsMap1796.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/HillsMap1796.jpg/440px-HillsMap1796.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2800" data-file-height="2042" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HillsMap1796.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Philadelphia in 1796</div></div></div>\n<p>In the 1760s, the British Parliament\'s passage of the <a href="/wiki/Stamp_Act_1765" title="Stamp Act 1765">Stamp Act</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Townshend_Acts" title="Townshend Acts">Townshend Acts</a>, combined with other frustrations, increased political tension and anger against England in the colonies. Philadelphia residents joined <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycotts</a> of British goods. After the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Act" title="Tea Act">Tea Act</a> in 1773, there were threats against anyone who would store tea and any ships that brought tea up the Delaware. After the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party" title="Boston Tea Party">Boston Tea Party</a>, a shipment of tea had arrived in December, on the ship the <i>Polly</i>. A committee told the captain to depart without unloading his cargo.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p118_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p118-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A series of <a href="/wiki/Intolerable_Acts" title="Intolerable Acts">acts</a> in 1774 further angered the colonies; activists called for a general congress and they agreed to meet in Philadelphia. The <a href="/wiki/First_Continental_Congress" title="First Continental Congress">First Continental Congress</a> was held in September in <a href="/wiki/Carpenters%27_Hall" title="Carpenters&#39; Hall">Carpenters\' Hall</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> began in April 1775 following the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a> met in May at the Pennsylvania State House.  There they also met a year later to write and sign the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a> in July 1776. Philadelphia was important to the war effort; <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_(merchant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Morris (merchant)">Robert Morris</a> said,\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>You will consider Philadelphia, from its centrical situation, the extent of its commerce, the number of its artificers, manufactures and other circumstances, to be to the United States what the heart is to the human body in circulating the blood.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p134_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p134-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The port city was vulnerable to attack by the British by sea.  Officials recruited soldiers and studied defenses for invasion from <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a>, but built no forts or other installations.  In March 1776 two British <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> began a <a href="/wiki/Blockade" title="Blockade">blockade</a> of the mouth of Delaware Bay; British soldiers were moving south through <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> from New York. In December fear of invasion caused half the population to flee the city, including the Continental Congress, which moved to <a href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p128-129_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p128-129-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">General George Washington</a> pushed back the British advance at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Princeton" title="Battle of Princeton">battles of Princeton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trenton" title="Battle of Trenton">Trenton</a>, and the refugees and Congress returned. In September 1777, the British invaded Philadelphia from the south. Washington intercepted them at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brandywine" title="Battle of Brandywine">Battle of Brandywine</a> but was driven back. Thousands fled north into Pennsylvania and east into New Jersey; Congress moved to <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Pennsylvania" title="Lancaster, Pennsylvania">Lancaster</a> then to <a href="/wiki/York,_Pennsylvania" title="York, Pennsylvania">York</a>. British troops marched into the half-empty Philadelphia on September 23 to cheering <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Loyalist</a> crowds.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The occupation lasted ten months.  After the French entered the war on the side of the Continentals, the last British troops pulled out of Philadelphia on June 18, 1778, to help defend New York City.  Continentals arrived the same day and reoccupied the city supervised by <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Major General Benedict Arnold</a>, who had been appointed the city\'s military commander. The city government returned a week later, and the Continental Congress returned in early July.\n</p><p>Historian <a href="/wiki/Gary_B._Nash" title="Gary B. Nash">Gary B. Nash</a> emphasizes the role of the working class, and their distrust of their betters, in northern ports. He argues that working class artisans and skilled craftsmen made up a radical element in Philadelphia that took control of the city starting about 1770 and promoted a radical Democratic form of government during the revolution. They held power for a while, and used their control of the local militia disseminate their ideology to the working class and to stay in power until  the businessmen staged a conservative counterrevolution.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  Philadelphia suffered serious inflation, causing problems especially for the poor, who were unable to buy needed goods. This led to unrest in 1779, with people blaming the upper class and Loyalists. A riot in January by sailors striking for higher wages ended up with their attacking and dismantling ships. In the Fort Wilson Riot of October 4, men attacked <a href="/wiki/James_Wilson" title="James Wilson">James Wilson</a>, a signer of the Declaration of Independence who was accused of being a Loyalist sympathizer. Soldiers broke up the riot, but five people died and 17 were injured.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p146-147_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p146-147-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporary_capital">Temporary capital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Temporary capital">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Exterior_view_of_Independence_Hall_(circa_1770s).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Exterior_view_of_Independence_Hall_%28circa_1770s%29.jpg/220px-Exterior_view_of_Independence_Hall_%28circa_1770s%29.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Exterior_view_of_Independence_Hall_%28circa_1770s%29.jpg/330px-Exterior_view_of_Independence_Hall_%28circa_1770s%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/Exterior_view_of_Independence_Hall_%28circa_1770s%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="387" data-file-height="280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Exterior_view_of_Independence_Hall_(circa_1770s).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Independence Hall in the 1770s</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Mutiny_of_1783" title="Pennsylvania Mutiny of 1783">Pennsylvania Mutiny of 1783</a>, the United States Congress moved out of Philadelphia and settled in New York City, designated as the temporary capital. Besides the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Convention">Constitutional Convention</a> in May 1787, United States politics was no longer centered in Philadelphia. Due to political compromise, Congress chose a permanent capital to be built along the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a>.  Philadelphia was selected as the temporary United States capital for ten years starting in 1790. Congress occupied the Philadelphia County Courthouse, which became known as <a href="/wiki/Congress_Hall" title="Congress Hall">Congress Hall</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> worked at City Hall. Robert Morris donated his home at 6th and Market Street as a residence for President Washington, known as the <a href="/wiki/President%27s_House_(Philadelphia)" title="President&#39;s House (Philadelphia)">President\'s House</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yellow_fever_1793">Yellow fever 1793</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Yellow fever 1793">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After 1787, the city\'s economy grew rapidly in the postwar years.  Serious <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a> outbreaks in the 1790s interrupted development.  <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Rush" title="Benjamin Rush">Benjamin Rush</a> identified an outbreak in August 1793 as a <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Fever_Epidemic_of_1793" class="mw-redirect" title="Yellow Fever Epidemic of 1793">yellow fever epidemic</a>, the first in 30 years, which lasted four months. Two thousand refugees from <a href="/wiki/Saint-Domingue" title="Saint-Domingue">Saint-Domingue</a> had recently arrived in the city in flight from the <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">Haitian Revolution</a>.  They represented five percent of the city\'s total population.  They likely carried the disease from the island where it was endemic, and it was rapidly transmitted by mosquito bites to other residents. Fear of contracting the disease caused 20,000 residents to flee the city by mid-September, and some neighboring towns prohibited their entry.<sup id="cite_ref-Epidemic_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epidemic-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  Trade virtually stopped; Baltimore and New York <a href="/wiki/Quarantined" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarantined">quarantined</a> people and goods from Philadelphia.<sup id="cite_ref-Epidemic_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epidemic-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  People feared entering the city or interacting with its residents. The fever finally abated at the end of October with the onset of colder weather and was declared at an end by mid-November. The death toll was 4,000 to 5,000, in a population of 50,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p180-187_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p180-187-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Yellow fever outbreaks recurred in Philadelphia and other major ports through the nineteenth century, but none had as many fatalities as that of 1793. The 1798 epidemic in Philadelphia also prompted an exodus; an estimated 1,292 residents died.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p180-187_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p180-187-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery">Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Pennsylvania, which had abolished slavery in 1780, required any slaves brought to the city to be freed after six months\' residency. The state law was challenged by French colonial refugees from Saint-Domingue, who brought slaves with them, but defended by the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Abolition_Society" title="Pennsylvania Abolition Society">Pennsylvania Abolition Society</a>. Through 1796, 500 slaves from Saint-Domingue gained freedom in the city. Because of the violence accompanying the revolution on the island, Philadelphians, many of whom had southern ties, and residents of the Upper South worried that <a href="/wiki/Free_people_of_color" title="Free people of color">free people of color</a> would encourage slave insurrections in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the city\'s 10 years as federal capital, members of Congress were exempt from the abolition law, but the many slaveholders in the executive and judicial branches were not.  President Washington, vice-President Jefferson and others brought slaves as domestic servants, and evaded the law by regularly shifting their slaves out of the city before the 6-month deadline. Two of Washington\'s slaves escaped from the <a href="/wiki/President%27s_House_(Philadelphia)" title="President&#39;s House (Philadelphia)">President\'s House</a>, and he gradually replaced his slaves with German immigrants who were <a href="/wiki/Indentured_servants" class="mw-redirect" title="Indentured servants">indentured servants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UShist_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UShist-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The remains of the President\'s House were found during excavation for a new <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bell_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberty Bell Center">Liberty Bell Center</a>, leading to archeological work in 2007. In 2010, a memorial on the site opened to commemorate Washington\'s slaves and African Americans in Philadelphia and U.S. history, as well as to mark the house site.<sup id="cite_ref-UShist_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UShist-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_growth">Industrial growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Industrial growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg/170px-Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg" width="170" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg/255px-Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg/340px-Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg 2x" data-file-width="544" data-file-height="650" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia_8th_%26_Market_1840.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eighth and Market Streets, 1840</div></div></div>\n<p>The Pennsylvania state government left Philadelphia in 1799 and the United States government left in 1800.  By this time, the city had become one of the United States\' busiest ports and the country\'s largest city, with 67,787 people living in Philadelphia and its contiguous suburbs.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p218_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p218-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Philadelphia\'s maritime trade was interrupted by the <a href="/wiki/Embargo_Act_of_1807" title="Embargo Act of 1807">Embargo Act of 1807</a> and then the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>. After the war, Philadelphia\'s shipping industry never returned to its pre-embargo status, and New York City succeeded it as the busiest port and largest city.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p212-214_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p212-214-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The embargo and decrease in foreign trade led to the development of local factories to produce goods no longer available as imports. <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">Manufacturing plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundries</a> were built and Philadelphia became an important center of paper-related industries and the leather, shoe, and boot industries.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p235_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p235-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Coal and iron mines, and the construction of new roads, <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Railroad">railroads</a> helped Philadelphia\'s manufacturing power grow, and the city became the United States\' first major industrial city.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p208_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p208-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Major industrial projects included the <a href="/wiki/Fairmount_Water_Works" title="Fairmount Water Works">Waterworks</a>, iron water pipes, a gasworks, and the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Naval_Shipyard" title="Philadelphia Naval Shipyard">U.S. Naval Yard</a>. In response to exploitative working conditions, some 20,000 Philadelphia workers staged the <a href="/wiki/1835_Philadelphia_General_Strike" class="mw-redirect" title="1835 Philadelphia General Strike">first general strike in North America</a> in 1835, in which workers in the city won the ten-hour workday and an increase in wages.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  In addition to its industrial power, Philadelphia was the financial center of the country. Along with chartered and private banks, the city was the home of the <a href="/wiki/First_Bank_of_the_United_States" title="First Bank of the United States">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Bank_of_the_United_States" title="Second Bank of the United States">Second Banks of the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanics_National_Bank" title="Mechanics National Bank">Mechanics National Bank</a> and the first <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Mint" title="Philadelphia Mint">U.S. Mint</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Cultural institutions, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Academy_of_the_Fine_Arts" title="Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts">Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Natural_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy of Natural Sciences">Academy of Natural Sciences</a>, the Athenaeum and the <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Institute" title="Franklin Institute">Franklin Institute</a> also developed in the nineteenth century. The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_General_Assembly" title="Pennsylvania General Assembly">Pennsylvania General Assembly</a> passed the Free School Law of 1834 to create the public school system.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p340_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p340-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_rivalries">Ethnic rivalries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ethnic rivalries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia1844riot.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Philadelphia1844riot.jpg/220px-Philadelphia1844riot.jpg" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Philadelphia1844riot.jpg/330px-Philadelphia1844riot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2f/Philadelphia1844riot.jpg 2x" data-file-width="387" data-file-height="249" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia1844riot.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A nativist riot in <a href="/wiki/Southwark,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Southwark, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Southwark</a>, July 7, 1844</div></div></div>\n<p>In the mid and late 1840s, immigrants from Ireland and Germany streamed into the city, swelling the population of Philadelphia and its suburbs.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In Philadelphia, as the rich moved west of 7th Street, the poor moved into the upper class\' former homes, which were converted into tenements and boarding houses. Many small <a href="/wiki/Townhome" class="mw-redirect" title="Townhome">row houses</a> crowded alleyways and small streets, and these areas were filthy, filled with garbage and the smell of manure from animal pens. During the 1840s and 1850s, hundreds died each year in Philadelphia and the surrounding districts from diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, related to poor sanitation and diseases brought by immigrants; the poor suffered the most fatalities. Small rowhouses and tenement housing were constructed south of South Street.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p318_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p318-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Violence was a serious problem. <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">Gangs</a> like the Moyamensing Killers and the Blood Tubs controlled various neighborhoods. During the 1840s and early 1850s when volunteer fire companies, some of which were infiltrated by gangs, responded to a fire, fights with other fire companies often broke out. The lawlessness among fire companies virtually ended in 1853 and 1854 when the city took more control over their operations.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p346-348_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p346-348-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> during the 1840s and 50s violence was directed against immigrants by people who feared their competition for jobs and resented newcomers of different religions and ethnicity. <a href="/wiki/Nativism_(politics)" title="Nativism (politics)">Nativists</a> often held mostly <a href="/wiki/Anti-Catholicism" title="Anti-Catholicism">anti-Catholic</a>, anti-Irish meetings. Violence against immigrants also occurred, the worst being the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Nativist_Riots" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Nativist Riots">nativist riots in 1844</a>. Violence against African Americans was also common during the 1830s, 40s, and 50s. Immigrants competed with them for jobs, and deadly race riots resulted in the burning of African-American homes and churches. In 1841, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Sturge" title="Joseph Sturge">Joseph Sturge</a> commented "...there is probably no city in the known world where dislike, amounting to the hatred of the coloured population, prevails more than in the city of brotherly love!"<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p353_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p353-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Several anti-slavery societies had been formed and free blacks, Quakers and other abolitionists operated safe houses associated with the <a href="/wiki/Underground_Railroad" title="Underground Railroad">Underground Railroad</a>, but working class and ethnic whites opposed the <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionist</a> movement. \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg/170px-Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg/255px-Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg/340px-Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1497" data-file-height="2246" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Union_Club_Philly_Statue_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Washington_Grays_Monument" title="Washington Grays Monument">Washington Grays Monument</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_Wilson_(sculptor)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Wilson (sculptor)">John A Wilson</a> in front of <a href="/wiki/Union_League_of_Philadelphia" title="Union League of Philadelphia">Union League of Philadelphia</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The lawlessness and the difficulty in controlling it, along with residential development just north of Philadelphia, led to the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Consolidation,_1854" title="Act of Consolidation, 1854">Act of Consolidation in 1854</a>. The act passed on February 2, making Philadelphia\'s borders coterminous with <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Philadelphia County, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia County</a>, and incorporating various subdistrict within the county.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Once the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> began in 1861, Philadelphia\'s <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">southern</a> leanings were reduced.  Popular hostility shifted against southern sympathizers. Mobs threatened a secessionist newspaper and the homes of suspected sympathizers, and were only turned away by the police and Mayor Alexander Henry.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p394_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p394-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Philadelphia supported the war with soldiers, ammunition, and war ships and its manufacturers produced many army uniforms. Philadelphia was also a major receiving place of the wounded, with more than 157,000 soldiers and sailors treated within the city. Philadelphia began preparing for invasion in 1863, but the Confederate Army was repelled by Union forces at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Gettysburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p396-410_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p396-410-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_in_the_American_Civil_War" title="Philadelphia in the American Civil War">Philadelphia in the American Civil War</a></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_19th_century">Late 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Late 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia1855.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d9/Philadelphia1855.jpg/170px-Philadelphia1855.jpg" width="170" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d9/Philadelphia1855.jpg/255px-Philadelphia1855.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d9/Philadelphia1855.jpg/340px-Philadelphia1855.jpg 2x" data-file-width="603" data-file-height="761" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia1855.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From right to left: Congress Hall, Independence Hall, and the old City Hall in 1855</div></div></div>\n<p>In the years following the Civil War, Philadelphia\'s population continued to grow. The population grew from 565,529 in 1860 to 674,022 in 1870. By 1876, the city\'s population stood at 817,000. The dense population areas were not only growing north and south along the Delaware River, but also moving westward across the Schuylkill River.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p420_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p420-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> A large portion of the growth came from immigrants, still mostly Irish and German. In 1870, twenty-seven percent of Philadelphia\'s population was born outside the United States.\n</p><p>In February 1854, <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Consolidation,_1854" title="Act of Consolidation, 1854">the Act of Consolidation</a> made the city of Philadelphia inclusive of the entire county, doing away with all other municipalities.\n</p><p>By the 1880s, immigration from Russia, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, and Italy started rivaling immigration from Western Europe. Many of the immigrants from Russia and Eastern Europe were Jewish. In 1881, there were around 5,000 <a href="/wiki/Jewish_history_in_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish history in Philadelphia">Jews in the city</a>, and by 1905 the number had increased to 100,000. Philadelphia\'s Italian population grew from around 300 in 1870 to around 18,000 in 1900, with the majority settling in <a href="/wiki/South_Philadelphia" title="South Philadelphia">South Philadelphia</a>. Along with foreign immigration, domestic migration by African Americans from the South led to Philadelphia having the largest black population of a <a href="/wiki/Northern_United_States" title="Northern United States">Northern U.S.</a> city in this period. By 1876, nearly 25,000 African Americans living in Philadelphia, and by 1890 the population was near 40,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p488-491_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p488-491-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> While immigrants moved into the city, Philadelphia\'s rich left for newer housing in the suburbs, with commuting made easy by newly constructed railroads. During the 1880s much of Philadelphia\'s upper class moved into the growing suburbs along the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Main_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Pennsylvania Main Line">Pennsylvania Railroad\'s Main Line</a> west of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Centennial_Exhibition,_Opening_Day.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Centennial_Exhibition%2C_Opening_Day.jpg/220px-Centennial_Exhibition%2C_Opening_Day.jpg" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Centennial_Exhibition%2C_Opening_Day.jpg/330px-Centennial_Exhibition%2C_Opening_Day.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Centennial_Exhibition%2C_Opening_Day.jpg/440px-Centennial_Exhibition%2C_Opening_Day.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2548" data-file-height="1558" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Centennial_Exhibition,_Opening_Day.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Opening day ceremonies at the Centennial Exhibition, 1876, first World\'s Fair in the US</div></div></div>\n<p>Politically the city was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a>, which had developed a strong <a href="/wiki/Political_machine" title="Political machine">political machine</a>. The Republicans dominated the post-war elections, and corrupt officials made their way into the government and continued to control the city through voter fraud and intimidation. The Gas Trust was the hub of the city\'s political machine. The <a href="/wiki/Trust_(19th_century)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trust (19th century)">trust</a> controlled the gas company supplying lighting to the city. With the board under complete control by Republicans in 1865, they awarded contracts and perks for themselves and their cronies. Some government reform did occur during this time. The police department was reorganized; and volunteer fire companies were eliminated and were replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Fire_Department" title="Philadelphia Fire Department">paid fire department</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p437-439_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p437-439-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_education" title="Compulsory education">compulsory school</a> act passed in 1895, and the Public School Reorganization Act freed the city\'s education from the political machine. Higher education changed as well. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> moved to <a href="/wiki/West_Philadelphia" title="West Philadelphia">West Philadelphia</a> and reorganized to its modern form; and <a href="/wiki/Temple_University" title="Temple University">Temple University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drexel_University" title="Drexel University">Drexel University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Free_Library_of_Philadelphia" title="Free Library of Philadelphia">Free Library</a> were founded.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p498-501_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p498-501-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The city\'s major project was organizing and staging the <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Exposition" title="Centennial Exposition">Centennial Exposition</a>, the first  <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Fair" class="mw-redirect" title="World&#39;s Fair">World\'s Fair</a> in the United States, which celebrated the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Centennial" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Centennial">nation\'s Centennial</a>. Held in <a href="/wiki/Fairmount_Park" title="Fairmount Park">Fairmount Park</a>, exhibits included <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a>\'s telephone and the <a href="/wiki/Corliss_Steam_Engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Corliss Steam Engine">Corliss Steam Engine</a>. Beginning May 10, 1876, by the end of the Exposition on November 10, more than nine million people had visited the fair.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> The city undertook construction of a new city hall, designed to match its ambitions. The project was graft-ridden and it took twenty-three years to complete. Upon completion of its tower in 1894,<sup id="cite_ref-nps_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_City_Hall" title="Philadelphia City Hall">City Hall</a> was the tallest building in Philadelphia, a position it maintained until One <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Place" title="Liberty Place">Liberty Place</a> surpassed it in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p506_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p506-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Philadelphia\'s major industries of the era were the <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_Locomotive_Works" title="Baldwin Locomotive Works">Baldwin Locomotive Works</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Cramp_%26_Sons" title="William Cramp &amp; Sons">William Cramp &amp; Sons Ship and Engine Building Company</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>. Westward expansion of the Pennsylvania Railroad helped Philadelphia keep up with nearby New York City in domestic commerce, as both cities fought for dominance in transporting iron and coal resources from Pennsylvania. Philadelphia\'s other local railroad was the <a href="/wiki/Reading_Company" title="Reading Company">Reading Railroad</a>, but after a series of bankruptcies, it was taken over by New Yorkers. The <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1873" title="Panic of 1873">Panic of 1873</a>, which occurred when the New York City branch of the Philadelphia bank <a href="/wiki/Jay_Cooke" title="Jay Cooke">Jay Cooke</a> and Company failed, and another panic in the 1890s hampered Philadelphia\'s economic growth.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p429-433_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p429-433-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> While the depressions hurt the city, its diverse array of industries helped it weather difficult times. It had numerous iron and steel-related manufacturers, including Philadelphian-owned iron and steel works outside the city, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel" title="Bethlehem Steel">Bethlehem Iron Company</a> in the city by that name. The largest industry in Philadelphia was textiles. Philadelphia produced more textiles than any other U.S. city; in 1904 the textile industry employed more than 35 percent of the city\'s workers. The cigar, sugar, and oil industries also were strong in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p480-481_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p480-481-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> During this time the major department stores: <a href="/wiki/Wanamaker%27s" title="Wanamaker&#39;s">Wanamaker\'s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gimbels" title="Gimbels">Gimbels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strawbridge%27s" title="Strawbridge&#39;s">Strawbridge and Clothier</a>, and Lit Brothers, were developed along Market Street.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p485-486_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p485-486-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the end of the century, the city provided nine municipal swimming pools, making it a leader in the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_20th_century">Early 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Early 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Uss_G-4_1914.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Uss_G-4_1914.jpg/220px-Uss_G-4_1914.jpg" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Uss_G-4_1914.jpg/330px-Uss_G-4_1914.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Uss_G-4_1914.jpg/440px-Uss_G-4_1914.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="543" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Uss_G-4_1914.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>USS G-4 at the William Cramp &amp; Sons shipyard, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, on October 2, 1912</div></div></div><p>In the beginning of the 20th century Philadelphia had taken on a poor reputation. People both inside and outside of the city commented that Philadelphia and its citizens were dull and contented with its lack of change. <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper&#39;s Magazine">Harper\'s Magazine</a></i> commented that "The one thing unforgivable in Philadelphia is to be new, to be different from what has been."<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p535_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p535-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Along with an image of "dullness," Philadelphia was known for its political corruption. The Republican-controlled political machine, run by <a href="/wiki/Israel_Wilson_Durham" title="Israel Wilson Durham">Israel Durham</a>, permeated all parts of city government. One official estimated that US$5 million was wasted each year from graft in the city\'s infrastructure programs.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p537-547_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p537-547-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> The majority of residents were Republican, but voter fraud and bribery were still common. In 1905, the city enacted election reforms, such as personal voter registration and the establishing <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primaries</a> for all city offices. But, residents became complacent, and the city\'s political bosses continued in control. After 1907, Boss Durham retired and his successor, James McNichol, never controlled much outside <a href="/wiki/North_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="North Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">North Philadelphia</a>. The Vare brothers, George, Edwin, and <a href="/wiki/William_S._Vare" class="mw-redirect" title="William S. Vare">William</a>, had created their own organization in South Philadelphia.  With no central authority, Senator <a href="/wiki/Boies_Penrose" title="Boies Penrose">Boies Penrose</a> took charge. In 1910, infighting between McNichol and the Vares contributed to the reform candidate, <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Blankenburg" title="Rudolph Blankenburg">Rudolph Blankenburg</a>, to be elected mayor. During his administration, he made numerous cost-cutting measures and improvements to city services, but he served only one term.  The machine again gained control.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p537-547_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p537-547-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The policies of <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>\'s administration reunited reformers with the city\'s Republican Party and <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> temporarily halted the reform movement. In 1917, the murder of George Eppley, a police officer defending City Council primary candidate James Carey, ignited the reformers again.  They passed legislation to reduce the City Council from two houses to one, and provided council members an annual salary.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p563-564_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p563-564-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> With the deaths of McNichol in 1917 and Penrose in 1921, William Vare became the city\'s political boss. In the 1920s the public flouting of <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> laws, mob violence, and police involvement in illegal activities led Mayor W. Freeland Kendrick to appoint <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Brigadier General">Brigadier General</a> <a href="/wiki/Smedley_Butler" title="Smedley Butler">Smedley Butler</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> as director of public safety. Butler cracked down on bars and <a href="/wiki/Speakeasy" title="Speakeasy">speakeasies</a> and tried to stop corruption within the police force, but demand for liquor and political pressure made the job difficult, and he had little success. After two years, Butler left in January 1926 and most of his police reforms were repealed. On August 1, 1928, Boss Vare suffered a stroke, and two weeks later a grand jury investigation into the city\'s mob violence and other crimes began. Numerous police officers were dismissed or arrested as a result of the investigation, but no permanent change resulted.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p578-581_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p578-581-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Strong support among some residents for the Democratic presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Al_Smith" title="Al Smith">Al Smith</a>, who was Catholic, marked the city\'s turning away in the 20th century from the Republican Party.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p586_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p586-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Philadelphia continued to grow with immigrants coming from Eastern Europe and Italy, as well as African American migrants from the South.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p529-531_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p529-531-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Foreign immigration was briefly interrupted by World War I.  The demand for labor for the city\'s factories, including the new U.S. Naval Yard at <a href="/wiki/Hog_Island,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Hog Island, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Hog Island</a>, which constructed ships, trains, and other items needed in the war effort, helped attract blacks in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a>. In September 1918, cases of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_flu" title="Spanish flu">influenza pandemic</a> were reported at the Naval Yard and began to spread. Mortality on some days was several hundred people and, by the time the pandemic began to subside in October, more than 12,000 people had died.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The rising popularity of automobiles led to widening of roads and creation of <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Boulevard_(Philadelphia)" title="Roosevelt Boulevard (Philadelphia)">Northeast (Roosevelt) Boulevard</a> in 1914, the <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin_Parkway" title="Benjamin Franklin Parkway">Benjamin Franklin Parkway</a> in 1918, the changing of many existing streets to one-way streets in the early 1920s, and construction of the <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin_Bridge" title="Benjamin Franklin Bridge">Delaware River (Benjamin Franklin) Bridge</a> to New Jersey in 1926. Philadelphia began to modernize, steel and concrete <a href="/wiki/Skyscraper" title="Skyscraper">skyscrapers</a> were constructed, old buildings were wired for electricity, and the city\'s <a href="/wiki/WTEL_(AM)" title="WTEL (AM)">first commercial radio station</a> was founded.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> In 1907, the city constructed the <a href="/wiki/Market-Frankford_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Market-Frankford Line">first subway</a>.  It hosted the <a href="/wiki/Sesquicentennial_Exposition" title="Sesquicentennial Exposition">Sesqui-Centennial Exposition</a> in South Philadelphia, and in 1928 opened the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">Philadelphia Museum of Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p525-526_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p525-526-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p593-596_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p593-596-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg/600px-Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg" width="600" height="104" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg/900px-Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg/1200px-Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2276" data-file-height="393" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Downtown_Philadelphia_Pano_1913.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>Center City Philadelphia in 1913</center></div></div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Depression_and_World_War_II">Great Depression and World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Great Depression and World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the three years after the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_of_1929" title="Wall Street Crash of 1929">stock market crashed in 1929</a>, 50 Philadelphia banks closed. Of those only two were large, <a href="/wiki/Albert_M._Greenfield" title="Albert M. Greenfield">Albert M. Greenfield</a>\'s Bankers Trust Company and the Franklin Trust Company. <a href="/wiki/Savings_and_loan_association" title="Savings and loan association">Savings and loan associations</a> also faced trouble, with <a href="/wiki/Mortgages" class="mw-redirect" title="Mortgages">mortgages</a> of 19,000 properties being <a href="/wiki/Foreclosure" title="Foreclosure">foreclosed</a> in 1932 alone. By 1934, 1,600 of 3,400 savings and loan associations had shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p607-609_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p607-609-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>  From 1929 to 1933, regional manufacturing fell by 45 percent; factory payrolls fell by 60 percent; retail sales fell by 40 percent. Worst hit of all was construction, where payrolls dropped 84 percent. Unemployment peaked in 1933, when 11.5 percent of whites, 16.2 percent of African Americans, and 19.1 percent of foreign-born whites were out of work.<sup id="cite_ref-EoGP_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoGP-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Mayor <a href="/wiki/J._Hampton_Moore" title="J. Hampton Moore">J. Hampton Moore</a> blamed people\'s economic woes, not on the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, but on laziness and wastefulness, and claimed there was no starvation in the city. Soon after, he fired 3,500 city workers, instituted pay cuts, forced unpaid vacation, and reduced the number of contracts the city awarded. This saved Philadelphia millions of dollars, and the efforts kept the city from defaulting on its debts, but were unpopular among the unemployed. The city relied on state money to fund relief efforts.  Moore\'s successor <a href="/wiki/S._Davis_Wilson" class="mw-redirect" title="S. Davis Wilson">S. Davis Wilson</a> instituted numerous programs financed by Franklin D. Roosevelt\'s <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">Works Progress Administration</a>, despite condemning the program during his mayoral campaign. At the peak of WPA-financed jobs in 1936, 40,000 Philadelphians were employed under the program.<sup id="cite_ref-EoGP_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoGP-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p611-613_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p611-613-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With encouragement from the state government and labor\'s founding of the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Industrial_Organizations" title="Congress of Industrial Organizations">Congress of Industrial Organizations</a> (CIO), Philadelphia became a <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">union</a> city. Many trade unions discriminated against African Americans for years, and they were closed out of some labor advances. Workers\' dissatisfaction with conditions led to numerous strikes in the textile unions, and the CIO organized labor in other industries, with more strikes taking place. During the 1930s, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> began to grow in Philadelphia, influenced by the leadership of the Roosevelt administration during the Depression. A newly organized Independent Democratic Committee reached out to residents. In 1936, the <a href="/wiki/1936_Democratic_National_Convention" title="1936 Democratic National Convention">Democratic National Convention</a> was held in Philadelphia.  The majority of voters in the city reelected the Democrat <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> as president; they also voted for Democratic Congressmen and state representatives. City government continued to be dominated by Republicans, but the politicians were elected by small margins.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p618-622_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p618-622-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> in Europe and the threat of the U.S. becoming involved generated new jobs in defense-related industries. After the U.S. became involved in the war in 1941, the city mobilized. Philadelphia consistently met <a href="/wiki/War_bond" title="War bond">war bond</a> quotas and when the war ended in 1945, 183,850 residents were in the U.S. armed forces. With so many men serving in the military, there had been a labor shortage; businesses and industries hired women and workers from outside the city. In 1944, the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Transportation_Company" title="Philadelphia Transportation Company">Philadelphia Transportation Company</a> promoted African Americans to positions as motormen and conductors (from which they had previously been excluded) on public transportation vehicles. Resentful, other PTC workers protested and began a strike that nearly immobilized the city. President Roosevelt sent troops to replace the striking workers. After a federal ultimatum, the workers returned after six days.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p635-644_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p635-644-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reform_and_decline">Reform and decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reform and decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg/220px-Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg/330px-Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg/440px-Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg 2x" data-file-width="587" data-file-height="418" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Philadelphia_strike_1946.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mounted_police" title="Mounted police">Mounted police</a> clashing with strikers, one carrying an <a href="/wiki/American_flag" class="mw-redirect" title="American flag">American flag</a>, outside an electrical plant in Philadelphia, 1946</div></div></div><p>After World War II ended, Philadelphia had a serious housing shortage. Around half of the city\'s housing had been built in the 19th century, and many units lacked proper sanitary facilities, were overcrowded, and in poor condition. Competition for housing, as African Americans (many had come to the city in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a> from the South) and Puerto Ricans moved into new neighborhoods, resulted in racial tension.  The wealthier middle-class residents, often white, continued to move out to the suburbs in what became called <a href="/wiki/White_flight" title="White flight">white flight</a>.\n</p><p>The population peaked at more than two million residents in 1950; afterward the city\'s population declined while that of the neighboring suburban counties grew.  Some residents moved out of the region altogether due to restructuring of industry and loss of tens of thousands of jobs in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p669-670_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p669-670-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Philadelphia lost five percent of its population in the 1950s, three percent in the 1960s and more than thirteen percent in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p707_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p707-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Manufacturing and other major Philadelphia businesses, which had supported middle-class lives for the working class, were moving out of the area or shutting down in industrial restructuring, including major declines in railroads.\n</p><p>The city encouraged development projects in <a href="/wiki/University_City,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="University City, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">University City</a> in West Philadelphia and the area around <a href="/wiki/Temple_University" title="Temple University">Temple University</a> in North Philadelphia, it removed the <a href="/wiki/Penn_Center,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Penn Center, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">"Chinese Wall" elevated railway</a>, and developed <a href="/wiki/Market_East,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Market East, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Market Street East</a> around the transportation hub.  Some <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> occurred, with restoration of properties in historic neighborhoods such as <a href="/wiki/Society_Hill" title="Society Hill">Society Hill</a>, Rittenhouse Square, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Village,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Village, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Queen Village</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Fairmount,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Fairmount, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Fairmount area</a>. A non profit group <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.clevelandcaprice.com/actionphiladelphia.htm">Action Philadelphia</a> was formed to improve and promote Philadelphia\'s image.The <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_International_Airport" title="Philadelphia International Airport">airport</a> expanded, the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_Expressway" title="Schuylkill Expressway">Schuylkill Expressway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_95_in_Pennsylvania" title="Interstate 95 in Pennsylvania">Delaware Expressway (Interstate 95)</a> were built, <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Pennsylvania_Transportation_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Southeastern Pennsylvania Transportation Authority">SEPTA</a> was formed, and residential and industrial development took place in <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Philadelphia" title="Northeast Philadelphia">Northeast Philadelphia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p695-702,720_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p695-702,720-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Preparations for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bicentennial" title="United States Bicentennial">United States Bicentennial</a> in 1976 began in 1964. By the early 1970s, US$3 million had been spent but no plans were set. The planning group was reorganized and numerous citywide events were planned.  <a href="/wiki/Independence_National_Historical_Park" title="Independence National Historical Park">Independence National Historical Park</a> was restored and development of <a href="/wiki/Penn%27s_Landing" title="Penn&#39;s Landing">Penn\'s Landing</a> was completed. Less than half the expected visitors came to the city for the Bicentennial, but the event helped revive the identity of the city, inspiring annual neighborhood events and fairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p725-731_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p725-731-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JosephSClark.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/JosephSClark.jpg/170px-JosephSClark.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/JosephSClark.jpg/255px-JosephSClark.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/JosephSClark.jpg/340px-JosephSClark.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2102" data-file-height="2806" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JosephSClark.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mayor Joseph S. Clark</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1947, <a href="/wiki/Richardson_Dilworth" title="Richardson Dilworth">Richardson Dilworth</a> was selected as the Democratic candidate, but lost to incumbent mayor <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Samuel" title="Bernard Samuel">Bernard Samuel</a>. During the campaign Dilworth made numerous specific charges about corruption within city government.  The City Council set up a committee to investigate, with findings followed by a grand jury investigation. The five-year investigation and its findings garnered national attention. US$40 million in city spending was found to be unaccounted for, and the president judge of the Court of Common pleas had been tampering with court cases. The <a href="/wiki/Fire_marshal" title="Fire marshal">fire marshal</a> went to prison; and an official in the tax collection office, a water department employee, a plumbing inspector, and head of the police <a href="/wiki/Vice_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice unit">vice squad</a> each committed suicide after criminal exposures.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p652_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p652-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> The public and the press demanded reform and by the end of 1950, a new city charter was drafted. The new charter strengthened the position of the mayor and weakened the City Council. The Council would be made of ten councilmen elected by district and seven at large. City administration was streamlined and new boards and commissions were created.\n</p><p>In 1951, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_S._Clark" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph S. Clark">Joseph S. Clark</a> was elected as the first Democratic mayor in 80 years. Clark filled administration positions based on merit and worked to weed out corruption.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p654-657_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p654-657-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Despite reforms and the Clark administration, a powerful Democratic patronage organization eventually replaced the old Republican one.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Clark was succeeded by Richardson Dilworth, who continued the policies of his predecessor. Dilworth resigned to run for governor in 1962, and city council president <a href="/wiki/James_Hugh_Joseph_Tate" title="James Hugh Joseph Tate">James H. J. Tate</a> was elected as the city\'s first Irish Catholic mayor. Tate was elected mayor in 1963 and reelected in 1967 despite opposition from reformers who opposed him as an organization insider.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p621_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p621-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As elsewhere in major US cities, the 1960s was a turbulent decade for the city. Numerous <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_the_Vietnam_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Opposition to the Vietnam War">anti-war</a> protests took place, including large protests led by <a href="/wiki/Marie_Hicks" title="Marie Hicks">Marie Hicks</a> to desegregate <a href="/wiki/Girard_College" title="Girard College">Girard College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> Students took over the <a href="/wiki/Community_College_of_Philadelphia" title="Community College of Philadelphia">Community College of Philadelphia</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Sit-in" title="Sit-in">sit-in</a>, race riots broke out in Holmesburg Prison, and <a href="/wiki/1964_Philadelphia_race_riot" title="1964 Philadelphia race riot">a 1964 riot</a> along West <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Avenue" class="mw-redirect" title="Columbia Avenue">Columbia Avenue</a> killed two people, injured over 300 and caused around US$3 million in damages. Crime was also a serious problem. Primarily drug-related gang warfare plagued the city, and in 1970 crime was rated the city\'s number one problem in a City Planning Commission survey. The court system was overtaxed and the tactics of the police department under Police Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Frank_Rizzo" title="Frank Rizzo">Frank Rizzo</a> were controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p675-678_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p675-678-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Frank Rizzo was credited with preventing the level of violence seen in other cities at the time and was elected mayor in 1971.\n</p><p>The outspoken Rizzo, who was reelected in 1975, was a divisive figure who had loyal supporters and passionate opponents. Police and fire departments and cultural institutions were well supported under Rizzo, but other city departments like the Free Library, the Department of Welfare and Recreation, the City Planning Commission and the Streets Department experienced large cuts.<sup id="cite_ref-Weigley1982p722-724_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weigley1982p722-724-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> The radical group called <a href="/wiki/MOVE" title="MOVE">MOVE</a> formed in 1972, and tension soon developed with city officials.  The first major clash occurred in 1978 at the group\'s <a href="/wiki/Powelton_Village,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Powelton Village, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Powelton Village</a> headquarters, resulting in the death of a police officer.  Nine MOVE members were convicted at trial and sentenced to prison. In 1985, a stand-off occurred at the group\'s new headquarters in <a href="/wiki/West_Philadelphia" title="West Philadelphia">West Philadelphia</a>, whose residents were believed to be armed resisters. The police dropped a satchel bomb on the house from a helicopter; it set off a fire that killed eleven MOVE members, including five children, and destroyed sixty-two neighboring houses.<sup id="cite_ref-Concise7879_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Concise7879-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> Survivors sued the city in civil court and won damages.\n</p><p>Crime continued to be a problem in the 1980s. Deadly <a href="/wiki/Italian-American_Mafia" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian-American Mafia">Mafia</a> warfare plagued South Philadelphia, drug gangs and <a href="/wiki/Crack_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Crack house">crack houses</a> invaded the slums of the city, and the murder rate skyrocketed. <a href="/wiki/William_J._Green,_III" class="mw-redirect" title="William J. Green, III">William J. Green</a> became mayor in 1980, and in 1984 <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Goode" title="Wilson Goode">W. Wilson Goode</a> became Philadelphia\'s first African-American mayor. Development continued in areas in Old City and South Street, and large modern skyscrapers of glass and granite, designed by nationally known architects, were constructed in Center City. City employee labor contracts signed during the Rizzo administration helped set up a city financial crisis that Green and Goode were unable to prevent.  The city was near bankruptcy at the end of the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Concise7879_100-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Concise7879-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A group of <a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a> refugees had settled in Philadelphia after the end of the 1970s <a href="/wiki/Laotian_Civil_War" title="Laotian Civil War">Laotian Civil War</a> associated with the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.  They were attacked in discriminatory acts, and the city\'s Commission on Human Relations held hearings on the incidents. <a href="/wiki/Anne_Fadiman" title="Anne Fadiman">Anne Fadiman</a>, author of <i>The Spirit Catches You and You Fall Down</i>, said that lower-class residents resented the Hmong receiving a $100,000 federal grant for employment assistance when they were also out of work; they believed that American citizens should be getting assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-Fadiman_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fadiman-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>192</span></sup> Between 1982 and 1984, three quarters of the <a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a> people who had settled in Philadelphia left for other cities in the United States to join relatives who were already there.<sup id="cite_ref-Fadiman_102-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fadiman-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>195</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">Vietnamese</a> and other immigrants from Asia have settled in the city, many near the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Market" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Market">Italian Market</a> area.  In addition, numerous Hispanic immigrants from Central and South America have entered the city, settling in North Philadelphia.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Into_the_21st_century">Into the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Into the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg/220px-Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg" width="220" height="118" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg/330px-Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg/440px-Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="550" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Skyline_of_Philadelphia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Philadelphia skyline in 2004</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1992, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Rendell" title="Ed Rendell">Ed Rendell</a> was elected as the city\'s first Jewish mayor. At the time, the city had numerous unpaid bills, the lowest <a href="/wiki/Bond_credit_rating" title="Bond credit rating">bond rating</a> of the top fifty largest U.S. cities, and a budget deficit of US$250 million. Rendell attracted investment in the city, stabilized the city\'s finances, and produced small budget surpluses.<sup id="cite_ref-Concise7879_100-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Concise7879-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> Revitalization of parts of Philadelphia continued in the 1990s. In 1993, a new convention center was opened, creating a hotel boom with seventeen hotels opening between 1998 and 2000 when the city hosted the Republican National Convention.  The city began promoting heritage tourism, and producing festivals and entertainment to attract tourists.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Traveler" title="National Geographic Traveler">National Geographic Traveler</a></i> named Philadelphia America\'s Next Great City, citing its recent revitalization and general compact cityscape.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Former city council president <a href="/wiki/John_F._Street" title="John F. Street">John F. Street</a> was elected mayor in 1999 and city revitalization continued into the 21st century. The Street administration targeted some of the city\'s worst neighborhoods for revitalization and made considerable progress.<sup id="cite_ref-StreetTime_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StreetTime-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> Tax breaks created in 1997 and 2000 helped create a <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominium</a> boom in Center City, increasing the population of Center City and helping slow the city\'s 40-year population decline. The population of Center City rose to 88,000 in 2005 from 78,000 in 2000 and the number of households grew by 24 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The city has had struggles: a series of scandals in the 1990s plagued the police department, including underreporting of crime.<sup id="cite_ref-Concise7879_100-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Concise7879-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> The Street administration was plagued with scandal, with administration people being accused of awarding contracts based on campaign donations for Street\'s <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_mayoral_election,_2003" title="Philadelphia mayoral election, 2003">2003 reelection</a> campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-StreetTime_105-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StreetTime-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> The 2000s had a rise of violent crime after a decline in the 1990s. In 2006, Philadelphia\'s murder rate was 27.8 per 100,000 inhabitants versus a rate of 18.9 in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Nutter" title="Michael Nutter">Michael Nutter</a>, with a background in business, was elected as the city\'s third African-American mayor.  As of July 2009, he oversaw a decrease in the crime rate by 30% since July 2007.  Tourism has become one of the city\'s main industries; Philadelphia is now the 10th-most visited city in the US (behind Atlanta and ahead of Miami, Dallas, and Boston).  Nutter was instrumental in Philadelphia\'s Foreclosure Prevention Program, a program which has been copied by many cities throughout the country to stabilize their housing and retain residents in owned units.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>As part of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis%27_visit_to_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Pope Francis&#39; visit to the United States">Pope Francis\' visit to the United States</a>, directly after his visit to Cuba, Francis was to visit New York City, Washington, D.C., and Philadelphia. In Philadelphia, Francis attended The 2015 World Meeting of Families, coordinated by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Philadelphia" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Philadelphia">Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Philadelphia</a>. Pope Francis ended his first Papal visit to the U.S. by saying mass to a crowd of 1 million people on the <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin_Parkway" title="Benjamin Franklin Parkway">Benjamin Franklin Parkway</a>. While in Philadelphia, Francis attended many events, including a speech to a crowd of 50,000 people at <a href="/wiki/Independence_Hall" title="Independence Hall">Independence Hall</a> and a visit and blessing at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph%27s_University" title="Saint Joseph&#39;s University">Saint Joseph\'s University</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
