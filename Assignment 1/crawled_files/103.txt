For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Roundhead_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Roundhead (disambiguation)">Roundhead (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Pettie_Puritan_Roundhead.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/John_Pettie_Puritan_Roundhead.jpg/250px-John_Pettie_Puritan_Roundhead.jpg" width="250" height="394" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/John_Pettie_Puritan_Roundhead.jpg/375px-John_Pettie_Puritan_Roundhead.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/John_Pettie_Puritan_Roundhead.jpg 2x" data-file-width="381" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Pettie_Puritan_Roundhead.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>A Roundhead by <a href="/wiki/John_Pettie" title="John Pettie">John Pettie</a></center></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Roundheads</b> were supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament of England</a> during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> (1641-1652). Also known as <b>Parliamentarians</b>, they fought against King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a> and his supporters, known as the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Cavaliers</a> or Royalists, who claimed rule by <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a> and the principle of the "<a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right of kings</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The goal of the Roundhead party was to give the Parliament supreme control over <a href="/wiki/Executive_branch" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive branch">executive administration</a> of the  country/kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacaulay1856105_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacaulay1856105-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Roundhead&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most Roundheads sought <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> in place of the <a href="/wiki/Absolutist_monarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="Absolutist monarchy">absolutist monarchy</a> sought by Charles<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. However, at the end of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> in 1649, public antipathy towards the king was high enough to allow republican leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> to abolish the monarchy completely and establish the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a>.\n</p><p>The Roundhead commander-in-chief of the first Civil War, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fairfax" title="Thomas Fairfax">Thomas Fairfax</a>, remained a supporter of constitutional monarchy, as did many other Roundhead leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Edward_Montagu,_2nd_Earl_of_Manchester" title="Edward Montagu, 2nd Earl of Manchester">Edward Montagu, 2nd Earl of Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Devereux,_3rd_Earl_of_Essex" title="Robert Devereux, 3rd Earl of Essex">Robert Devereux, 3rd Earl of Essex</a>; however, this party was outmanoeuvred by the more politically-adept Cromwell and his radicals, who had the backing of the <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a> and took advantage of Charles\' perceived betrayal of England by allying with the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> against Parliament.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>England\'s many <a href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">Puritans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterians</a> were almost invariably Roundhead supporters, as were many smaller religious groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Independent_(religion)" title="Independent (religion)">Independents</a>. However many Roundheads were members of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, as were many Cavaliers.\n</p><p>Roundhead political factions included the proto-anarchist <a href="/wiki/Diggers" title="Diggers">Diggers</a>, the diverse group known as the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">Levellers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_eschatology" title="Christian eschatology">apocalyptic Christian</a> movement of the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Monarchists" title="Fifth Monarchists">Fifth Monarchists</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_background">Origins and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roundhead&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Frederick_Yeames_-_And_when_did_you_last_see_your_father%3F_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" 2x" data-file-width="3913" data-file-height="2015" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a Cavalier, "<a href="/wiki/William_Frederick_Yeames#And_When_Did_You_Last_See_Your_Father?" title="William Frederick Yeames">And when did you last see your father?</a>" \xe2\x80\x94 <a href="/wiki/William_Frederick_Yeames" title="William Frederick Yeames">William Frederick Yeames</a> (1878).</div></div></div>\n<p>Some Puritans, but by no means all, wore their hair closely cropped round the head or flat and there was thus an obvious contrast between them and the men of <a href="/wiki/1600%E2%80%9350_in_Western_European_fashion" class="mw-redirect" title="1600\xe2\x80\x9350 in Western European fashion">courtly fashion</a>, who wore <a href="/wiki/Ringlet_(haircut)" title="Ringlet (haircut)">long ringlets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the war and for a time afterwards, Roundhead was a term of derision<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\xe2\x80\x94in the <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a> it was a punishable offence to call a fellow soldier a Roundhead.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorden20092_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorden20092-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This contrasted with the term "Cavalier" to describe supporters of the Royalist cause. Cavalier also started out as a pejorative term\xe2\x80\x94the first proponents used it to compare members of the Royalist party with Spanish <i>Caballeros</i> who had abused Dutch Protestants during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a>\xe2\x80\x94but unlike Roundhead, Cavalier was embraced by those who were the target of the epithet and used by them to describe themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorden20092_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorden20092-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>"Roundheads" appears to have been first used as a term of derision toward the end of 1641, when the debates in Parliament in the <a href="/wiki/Clergy_Act_1640" title="Clergy Act 1640">Clergy Act 1640</a> were causing riots at <a href="/wiki/Westminster" title="Westminster">Westminster</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition" title="Encyclop\xc3\xa6dia Britannica Eleventh Edition"><i>Encyclop\xc3\xa6dia Britannica</i> Eleventh Edition</a> quotes a contemporary authority\'s description of the crowd gathered there: "They had the hair of their heads very few of them longer than their ears, whereupon it came to pass that those who usually with their cries attended at Westminster were by a nickname called <i>Roundheads</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The demonstrators included London apprentices and Roundhead was a term of derision for them because the regulations to which they had agreed included a provision for closely cropped hair.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorden20092_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorden20092-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/John_Rushworth" title="John Rushworth">John Rushworth</a> the word was first used on 27 December 1641 by a disbanded officer named David Hide.  During a riot, Hide is reported to have drawn his sword and said he would "cut the throat of those round-headed dogs that bawled against bishops".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Baxter" title="Richard Baxter">Richard Baxter</a> ascribes the origin of the term to a remark made by <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Maria_of_France" title="Henrietta Maria of France">Queen Henrietta Maria</a>, wife of Charles I, at the trial of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wentworth,_1st_Earl_of_Strafford" title="Thomas Wentworth, 1st Earl of Strafford">Thomas Wentworth, 1st Earl of Strafford</a>, earlier that year. Referring to <a href="/wiki/John_Pym" title="John Pym">John Pym</a>, she asked who the roundheaded man was.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The principal advisor to <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hyde,_1st_Earl_of_Clarendon" title="Edward Hyde, 1st Earl of Clarendon">Edward Hyde, 1st Earl of Clarendon</a>, remarked on the matter, "and from those contestations the two terms of <i>Roundhead</i> and <i>Cavalier</i> grew to be received in discourse,&#160;... they who were looked upon as servants to the king being then called <i>Cavaliers</i>, and the other of the rabble contemned and despised under the name of <i>Roundheads</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ironically, after Anglican Archbishop <a href="/wiki/William_Laud" title="William Laud">William Laud</a> made a statute in 1636 instructing all clergy to wear short hair, many Puritans rebelled to show their contempt for his authority and began to grow their hair even longer (as can be seen on <a href="/wiki/File:PuritanGallery.jpg" title="File:PuritanGallery.jpg">their portraits</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> though they continued to be known as Roundheads. The longer hair was more common among the "Independent" and "high ranking" Puritans (which included Cromwell), especially toward the end of the Protectorate, while the "Presbyterian" (<i>i.e.</i>, non-Independent) faction, and the military rank-and-file, continued to abhor long hair. By the end of this period some Independent Puritans were again derisively using the term Roundhead to refer to the Presbyterian Puritans.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanbury1844118,_635_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanbury1844118,_635-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Roundhead remained in use to describe those with republican tendencies up until the <a href="/wiki/Exclusion_Crisis" title="Exclusion Crisis">Exclusion Crisis</a> of 1678\xe2\x80\x931681; the term was then superseded by "<a href="/wiki/Whigs_(British_political_party)" title="Whigs (British political party)">Whig</a>", initially another term with pejorative connotations. Likewise during the Exclusion Bill crisis, the term <i>Cavalier</i> was replaced with "<a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory</a>", an Irish term introduced by their opponents, and also initially a pejorative term.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorden20094_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorden20094-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roundhead&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 40em; -webkit-column-width: 40em; column-width: 40em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFRoberts2006">Roberts 2006</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears.
