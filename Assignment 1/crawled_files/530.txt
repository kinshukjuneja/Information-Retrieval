1864 &#8211; March 28, 1931) was an American executive in <a href="/wiki/Professional_baseball" title="Professional baseball">professional baseball</a> who served as the founder and first president of the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> (AL).\n</p><p>Johnson developed the AL&#8212;a descendant of the minor league <a href="/wiki/Western_League_(U.S._baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Western League (U.S. baseball)">Western League</a>&#8212;into a "clean" alternative to the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>, which had become notorious for its rough-and-tumble atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> To encourage a more orderly environment, Johnson strongly supported the new league\'s umpires,<sup id="cite_ref-blal_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blal-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> which eventually included Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Billy_Evans" title="Billy Evans">Billy Evans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-01-24-56_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-01-24-56-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the help of league owners and managers such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Comiskey" title="Charles Comiskey">Charles Comiskey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Somers" title="Charles Somers">Charles Somers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_McAleer" title="Jimmy McAleer">Jimmy McAleer</a>, Johnson lured top talent to the AL, which soon rivaled the more established National League.<sup id="cite_ref-baseball_library_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baseball_library-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Johnson dominated the AL until the mid-1920s, when a public dispute with Baseball Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a> culminated in his forced resignation as league president.<sup id="cite_ref-blal_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blal-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Western_League">The Western League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Johnson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Western League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Norwalk,_Ohio" title="Norwalk, Ohio">Norwalk, Ohio</a>, Johnson went on to study law at <a href="/wiki/Marietta_College" title="Marietta College">Marietta College</a>, although he did not take his degree. He subsequently became the sports editor of a newspaper in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> During this time, Johnson befriended <a href="/wiki/Charles_Comiskey" title="Charles Comiskey">Charles Comiskey</a>, who was then manager of the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> At the urging of Comiskey and Reds owner <a href="/wiki/John_T._Brush" title="John T. Brush">John T. Brush</a>, Johnson was elected as president of the Western League, a faltering <a href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" class="mw-redirect" title="Minor league baseball">minor league</a>, at a reorganization meeting held in <a href="/wiki/1893_in_baseball" title="1893 in baseball">1893</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Johnson had criticized the National League for its rowdy atmosphere, which was driving away families and women.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He set about making baseball more friendly to both. Contrary to the practice of the time, Johnson gave his <a href="/wiki/Umpire_(baseball)" title="Umpire (baseball)">umpires</a> unqualified support and had little tolerance for players or managers who failed to show them due respect.<sup id="cite_ref-deadball_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deadball-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Johnson also fined and suspended players who used foul language on the field. Soon, the Western League was recognized as not only the strongest minor league, but also as the most effectively managed league in all of baseball.<sup id="cite_ref-deadball_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deadball-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_the_American_League">Formation of the American League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Johnson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation of the American League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Teams_of_the_American_League_(2350728586).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Teams_of_the_American_League_%282350728586%29.jpg/220px-Teams_of_the_American_League_%282350728586%29.jpg" width="220" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Teams_of_the_American_League_%282350728586%29.jpg/330px-Teams_of_the_American_League_%282350728586%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Teams_of_the_American_League_%282350728586%29.jpg/440px-Teams_of_the_American_League_%282350728586%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="587" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Teams_of_the_American_League_(2350728586).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ban Johnson (center) surrounded by the individual portraits of the eight American League teams</div></div></div>\n<p>Johnson, however, had a bigger plan&#8212;another major league. With the help of Comiskey, who had purchased the <a href="/wiki/Sioux_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Sioux City">Sioux City</a> franchise and moved it to <a href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul</a> in <a href="/wiki/1894_in_baseball" title="1894 in baseball">1894</a> after leaving the Reds, Johnson initiated an ambitious plan of expansion. He got his chance after the <a href="/wiki/1899_in_baseball" title="1899 in baseball">1899</a> season, when the National League dropped teams in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville</a> and Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Johnson moved the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Rapids,_Michigan" title="Grand Rapids, Michigan">Grand Rapids</a> franchise to Cleveland, where they would eventually become the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Indians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He also had Comiskey move his Saint Paul team to <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, where they eventually became the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">White Sox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The latter move was made with the blessing of the NL, which saw Comiskey\'s team as a way to head off any attempt to revive the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> For the 1900 season, the Western League was renamed as the American League, although it remained a minor league.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1900 season was an unqualified success, and Johnson received a 10-year contract extension.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In October, he withdrew the AL from the National Agreement (the formal understanding between the NL and the minor leagues). The final step came on January 28, 1901, when he declared the AL would operate as a major league. He then placed teams in Baltimore, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Buffalo Bisons were to be a member of the new American League and their manager Franklin was told right up to Jan. 29, 1901, that "Buffalo was in the league and not to worry", Ban Johnson unceremoniously dumped Buffalo and placed the franchise in Boston. It was later revealed that he not only had been negotiating surreptitiously with Boston people for several months, but also that he had money invested in the Boston franchise.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Johnson also had a large stake in the Washington franchise, which he kept until 1903.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_baseball_power">A baseball power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Johnson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: A baseball power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The NL then made a critical blunder by limiting salaries to $2,400&#8211;a low sum even by 1901 standards. Johnson, Comiskey and the other AL owners responded by raiding NL rosters, promising disgruntled players much higher salaries. Over 100 players "jumped" to the new league. After a two-year war in which the AL trounced the NL in attendance both seasons, the NL sued for peace.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Under a new National Agreement, the AL was formally recognized as the second major league. A three-man National Commission was set up, composed of both league presidents and Reds owner <a href="/wiki/Garry_Herrmann" class="mw-redirect" title="Garry Herrmann">Garry Herrmann</a>. Although Herrmann was nominal president of the commission, Johnson soon dominated the body.<sup id="cite_ref-bjohnson&amp;al_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjohnson&amp;al-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Johnson brooked no criticism, and made it very difficult for men he didn\'t like to buy into the league. For instance, when <a href="/wiki/Harry_Frazee" title="Harry Frazee">Harry Frazee</a> bought the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> in <a href="/wiki/1917_in_baseball" title="1917 in baseball">1917</a>, Johnson tried almost from the start to drive him out because Frazee had not been hand-picked by Johnson.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-01-01-22_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-01-01-22-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> At one point, Johnson even had ownership interests in the Cleveland and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins" title="Minnesota Twins">Washington</a> teams.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downfall">Downfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Johnson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Downfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Frazee dispute planted the seed for Johnson\'s downfall. Eventually, the league divided into two factions, with the Red Sox, White Sox and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> on one side (commonly known as "The Insurrectos") and the other five clubs (the Indians, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis_Browns" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Louis Browns">St. Louis Browns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Senators (1901\xe2\x80\x9360)">Washington Senators</a>, known as the "Loyal Five") on the other. By this time, Comiskey had become a bitter enemy of Johnson; the two men\'s once warm friendship had strained considerably. Johnson\'s authority eroded further that year when the Red Sox traded <a href="/wiki/Carl_Mays" title="Carl Mays">Carl Mays</a> to the Yankees in defiance of a Johnson order to suspend him after Mays had jumped the club.<sup id="cite_ref-blfrazee_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blfrazee-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Yankees went to court and received an injunction to allow Mays to play, as Johnson had demonstrated throughout the proceedings that his investment in the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> hindered his ability to be impartial.<sup id="cite_ref-blfrazee_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blfrazee-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The final nail in Johnson\'s coffin proved to be the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sox_Scandal" title="Black Sox Scandal">Black Sox Scandal</a>. Johnson paid no attention to Comiskey\'s claims that his White Sox may have been on the take from gamblers.<sup id="cite_ref-blal_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blal-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> However, when the scandal broke after the <a href="/wiki/1920_in_baseball" title="1920 in baseball">1920</a> season, the White Sox, Red Sox and Yankees threatened to pull out of the AL and join a new 12-team National League. The enlarged league would include a new team in Detroit unrelated to the Tigers, who were owned by Johnson loyalist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Navin" title="Frank Navin">Frank Navin</a>. However, Navin was in no mood for another war and persuaded the other five clubs to agree to appoint a new National Commission of non-baseball men. Federal District Court Judge <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a> was appointed as chairman. However, Landis would only accept an appointment as sole <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_of_Baseball" title="Commissioner of Baseball">Commissioner of Baseball</a>, with unlimited power over the game.<sup id="cite_ref-bllandis_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bllandis-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The owners were still reeling from the damage to baseball\'s reputation due to the Black Sox Scandal, and readily agreed to Landis\' demands.<sup id="cite_ref-bllandis_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bllandis-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the circumstances, a clash between the iron-willed Johnson and the iron-willed Landis was inevitable, and it happened prior to the <a href="/wiki/1924_World_Series" title="1924 World Series">1924 World Series</a>. Landis banned two <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> from the Series for attempting to bribe members of the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> late in the season. After <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Frisch" title="Frankie Frisch">Frankie Frisch</a> and two other Giants stars were implicated, only to be cleared by Landis, Johnson demanded that the Series be canceled. He publicly criticized Landis for his handling of the affair, and Landis threatened to resign if the AL owners didn\'t rein Johnson in. After the Series, the AL owners promised to remove Johnson from office if he stepped out of line again. Johnson remained on good behavior for two years, even getting an extension of his contract to <a href="/wiki/1935_in_baseball" title="1935 in baseball">1935</a> and a raise to $40,000 (he had previously made $25,000).\n</p><p>However, in <a href="/wiki/1926_in_baseball" title="1926 in baseball">1926</a>, Johnson criticized Landis for granting <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tris_Speaker" title="Tris Speaker">Tris Speaker</a> an amnesty after evidence surfaced that they had fixed a game in 1919. Landis demanded that the AL choose between him and Johnson. The AL owners were prepared to remove Johnson from office at their annual meeting in January <a href="/wiki/1927_in_baseball" title="1927 in baseball">1927</a>. Because Johnson was in ill health at the time, the owners decided to put him on an indefinite sabbatical instead. Johnson tried to return in the spring and acted as if nothing had changed. However, the situation had become untenable, and Johnson was forced to resign at the end of the season.<sup id="cite_ref-blal_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blal-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Frank Navin served as acting president of the American League until the owners selected Indians general manager <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Barnard" title="Ernest Barnard">Ernest Barnard</a> as president.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ban_Johnson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg/110px-Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg" width="110" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg/165px-Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg/220px-Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2233" data-file-height="2616" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ban_Johnson_HOF_plaque.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Johnson\'s plaque at the <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Baseball Hall of Fame</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Johnson died at age 67 in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nbhf_johnson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbhf_johnson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> just a few hours after his successor, Ernest Barnard. Johnson was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_1937" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 1937">1937</a> as one of its charter members. The athletic fieldhouse at <a href="/wiki/Marietta_College" title="Marietta College">Marietta College</a> is named in his honor. He was laid to rest at Riverside Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Spencer,_Indiana" title="Spencer, Indiana">Spencer, Indiana</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Will_Harridge" title="Will Harridge">Will Harridge</a>, who succeeded to the AL presidency in 1931, summed up Johnson\'s legacy: "He was the most brilliant man the game has ever known. He was more responsible for making baseball the national game than anyone in the history of the sport".<sup id="cite_ref-nbhf_johnson_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbhf_johnson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Jayhawk_Collegiate_League" title="Jayhawk Collegiate League">Jayhawk Collegiate League</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Liberal,_Kansas" title="Liberal, Kansas">Liberal</a> Bee Jays are named in Johnson\'s honor.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
