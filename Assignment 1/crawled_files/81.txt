title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[a kkap\xcb\x88p\xc9\x9blla]</a></span>; Italian for "in the manner of the chapel")<sup id="cite_ref-Holmes_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> music is specifically group or solo singing without <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">instrumental</a> <a href="/wiki/Accompaniment" title="Accompaniment">accompaniment</a>, or a piece intended to be performed in this way. It contrasts with <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantata</a>, which is usually accompanied singing. The term "a cappella" was originally intended to differentiate between <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">Renaissance</a> <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroque (music)">Baroque</a> <a href="/wiki/Concertato" title="Concertato">concertato</a> style. In the 19th century a renewed interest in Renaissance polyphony coupled with an ignorance of the fact that vocal parts were often doubled by instrumentalists led to the term coming to mean unaccompanied vocal music.<sup id="cite_ref-Holmes_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term is also used, albeit rarely, as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Alla_breve" title="Alla breve">alla breve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Religious origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A cappella music was originally used in religious music, especially <a href="/wiki/Church_music" title="Church music">church music</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Anasheed" class="mw-redirect" title="Anasheed">anasheed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zemirot" title="Zemirot">zemirot</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_chant" title="Gregorian chant">Gregorian chant</a> is an example of a cappella singing, as is the majority of secular vocal music from the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">Renaissance</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Madrigal_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrigal (music)">madrigal</a>, up until its development in the early <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> into an instrumentally-accompanied form, is also usually in a cappella form. Jewish and Christian music were originally a cappella,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs a reliable source; many Old Testament passages make reference to multiple instruments being used. &#91;By definition, Christianity began with the New Testament.&#93; (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and this practice has continued in both of these religions as well as in Islam.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian">Christian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Christian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphony</a> of Christian a cappella music began to develop in Europe around the late 15th century AD, with compositions by <a href="/wiki/Josquin_des_Prez" title="Josquin des Prez">Josquin des Prez</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The early a cappella polyphonies may have had an accompanying instrument, although this instrument would merely double the singers\' parts and was not independent. By the 16th century, a cappella <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphony</a> had further developed, but gradually, the <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantata</a> began to take the place of a cappella forms.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 16th century a cappella polyphony, nonetheless, continued to influence church composers throughout this period and to the present day. Recent evidence has shown that some of the early pieces by Palestrina, such as what was written for the Sistine Chapel was intended to be accompanied by an organ "doubling" some or all of the voices.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Such is seen in the life of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pierluigi_da_Palestrina" title="Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina">Palestrina</a> becoming a major influence on <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach</a>, most notably in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mass_in_B_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass in B Minor">Mass in B Minor</a></i>.\n</p><p>Other composers that utilized the a cappella style, if only for the occasional piece, were <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Claudio Monteverdi">Claudio Monteverdi</a> and his masterpiece, <i>Lagrime d\'amante al sepolcro dell\'amata</i> (A lover\'s tears at his beloved\'s grave), which was composed in 1610,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Gabrieli" title="Andrea Gabrieli">Andrea Gabrieli</a> when upon his death it was discovered many choral pieces, one of which was in the unaccompanied style.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Learning from the preceding two composeres, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Sch%C3%BCtz" title="Heinrich Sch\xc3\xbctz">Heinrich Sch\xc3\xbctz</a> utilized the a cappella style in numerous pieces, chief among these were the pieces in the <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorio</a> style, which were traditionally performed during the Easter week and dealt with the religious subject matter of that week, such as Christ\'s suffering and <a href="/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Passion (Christianity)">the Passion</a>. Five of Schutz\'s <i>Historien</i> were Easter pieces, and of these the latter three, which dealt with the passion from three different viewpoints, those of <a href="/wiki/Matthew_the_Apostle" title="Matthew the Apostle">Matthew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luke_the_Evangelist" title="Luke the Evangelist">Luke</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_the_Evangelist" title="John the Evangelist">John</a>, were all done a cappella style. This was a near requirement for this type of piece, and the parts of the crowd were sung while the solo parts which were the quoted parts from either Christ or the authors were performed in a <a href="/wiki/Plainchant" class="mw-redirect" title="Plainchant">plainchant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Byzantine_Rite">Byzantine Rite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Byzantine Rite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the Byzantine Rite of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>, the music performed in the liturgies is exclusively sung without instrumental accompaniment. Bishop Kallistos Ware says, "The service is sung, even though there may be no choir... In the Orthodox Church today, as in the early Church, singing is unaccompanied and instrumental music is not found."<sup id="cite_ref-Ware_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ware-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This <i>a cappella</i> behavior arises from strict interpretation of Psalms 150, which states, <i>Let every thing that hath breath praise the Lord. Praise ye the Lord.</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In keeping with this philosophy, early Russian <i>musika</i> which started appearing in the late 17th century, in what was known as <i>khorov\xc3\xafye kontsert\xc3\xaf</i> (choral concertos) made a cappella adaptations of Venetian-styled pieces, such as the treatise, <i>Grammatika musikiyskaya</i> (1675), by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Diletsky" class="mw-redirect" title="Nikolai Diletsky">Nikolai Diletsky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Divine Liturgies and Western Rite masses composed by famous composers such as <a href="/wiki/Liturgy_of_St._John_Chrysostom_(Tchaikovsky)" title="Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom (Tchaikovsky)">Peter Tchaikovsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liturgy_of_St._John_Chrysostom_(Rachmaninoff)" title="Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom (Rachmaninoff)">Sergei Rachmaninoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Arkhangelsky_(composer)" title="Alexander Arkhangelsky (composer)">Alexander Arkhangelsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liturgy_of_St._John_Chrysostom_(Leontovych)" title="Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom (Leontovych)">Mykola Leontovych</a> are fine examples of this.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_instruments_in_worship">Opposition to instruments in worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opposition to instruments in worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Present-day Christian religious bodies known for conducting their worship services without musical accompaniment include some <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> churches devoted to the <a href="/wiki/Regulative_principle_of_worship" title="Regulative principle of worship">regulative principle of worship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Regular_Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Regular Baptist">Old Regular Baptists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Primitive_Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Primitive Baptist">Primitive Baptists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Brethren" title="Plymouth Brethren">Plymouth Brethren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Christ" title="Churches of Christ">Churches of Christ</a>, Church of God (Guthrie, Oklahoma), the <a href="/wiki/Old_German_Baptist_Brethren" title="Old German Baptist Brethren">Old German Baptist Brethren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doukhobor" class="mw-redirect" title="Doukhobor">Doukhobors</a> the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Order_Mennonites" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Order Mennonites">Old Order Mennonites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Mennonites" title="Conservative Mennonites">Conservative Mennonites</a>. Certain <a href="/wiki/High_church" title="High church">high church</a> services and other musical events in liturgical churches (such as the Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> <a href="/wiki/Divine_Service_(Lutheran)" title="Divine Service (Lutheran)">Divine Service</a>) may be a cappella, a practice remaining from apostolic times. Many <a href="/wiki/Mennonite" class="mw-redirect" title="Mennonite">Mennonites</a> also conduct some or all of their services without instruments. <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Harp" title="Sacred Harp">Sacred Harp</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a>, is an a cappella style of religious singing with <a href="/wiki/Shape_notes" class="mw-redirect" title="Shape notes">shape notes</a>, usually sung at singing conventions.\n</p><p>Opponents of <a href="/wiki/Musical_instruments_in_the_Christian_worship" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical instruments in the Christian worship">musical instruments in the Christian worship</a> believe that such opposition is supported by the Christian scriptures and Church history. The scriptures typically referenced are <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew%2026:30;%20Acts%2016:25;%20Romans%2015:9;%201%20Corinthians%2014:15;%20Ephesians%205:19;%20Colossians%203:16;%20Hebrews%202:12,%2013:15;%20James%205:13;&amp;version=49;">Matthew 26:30; Acts 16:25; Romans 15:9; 1 Corinthians 14:15; Ephesians 5:19; Colossians 3:16; Hebrews 2:12, 13:15; James 5:13</a>, which show examples and exhortations for Christians to sing.<sup id="cite_ref-kurfees_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kurfees-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is no reference to instrumental music in early church worship in the New Testament, or in the worship of churches for the first six centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinnon_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinnon-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bales_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bales-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Several reasons have been posited throughout church history for the absence of instrumental music in church worship.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;nb 1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Christians who believe in a cappella music today believe that in the Israelite worship assembly during Temple worship only the Priests of Levi sang, played, and offered animal sacrifices, whereas in the church era, all Christians are commanded to sing praises to God. They believe that if God wanted instrumental music in New Testament worship, He would have commanded not just singing, but singing and playing like he did in the Hebrew scriptures.\n</p><p>The first recorded example of a musical instrument in Roman Catholic worship was a <a href="/wiki/Pipe_organ" title="Pipe organ">pipe organ</a> introduced by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Vitalian" title="Pope Vitalian">Pope Vitalian</a> into a cathedral in Rome around 670.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;nb 2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Instruments have divided Christendom since their introduction into worship. They were considered a Catholic innovation, not widely practiced until the 18th century, and were opposed vigorously in worship by a number of <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformers" title="Protestant Reformers">Protestant Reformers</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> (1483\xe2\x80\x931546),<sup id="cite_ref-M&amp;S_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M&amp;S-18">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_Zwingli" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulrich Zwingli">Ulrich Zwingli</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a> (1509\xe2\x80\x931564)<sup id="cite_ref-Calvin_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Calvin-19">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John Wesley</a> (1703\xe2\x80\x931791).<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-20">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Alexander Campbell referred to the use of an instrument in worship as "a cow bell in a concert".<sup id="cite_ref-ferg_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ferg-21">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Sir Walter Scott</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heart_of_Midlothian" title="The Heart of Midlothian">The Heart of Midlothian</a></i>, the heroine, <a href="/wiki/Jeanie_Deans" title="Jeanie Deans">Jeanie Deans</a>, a Scottish Presbyterian, writes to her father about the church situation she has found in England (bold added):\n</p>\n<dl><dd>The folk here are civil, and, like the barbarians unto the holy apostle, have shown me much kindness; and there are a sort of chosen people in the land, for they have some <b><a href="/wiki/Kirk" title="Kirk">kirks</a> without organs that are like ours</b>, and are called meeting-houses, where the minister preaches without a gown.<sup id="cite_ref-Scott_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acceptance_of_instruments_in_worship">Acceptance of instruments in worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acceptance of instruments in worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Those who do not adhere to the <a href="/wiki/Regulative_principle" class="mw-redirect" title="Regulative principle">regulative principle</a> of interpreting Christian scripture, believe that limiting praise to the unaccompanied chant of the early church is not commanded in scripture, and that churches in any age are free to offer their songs with or without musical instruments.\n</p><p>Those who subscribe to this interpretation believe that since the Christian scriptures never counter instrumental language with any negative judgment on instruments, opposition to instruments instead comes from an interpretation of history. There is no written opposition to musical instruments in any setting in the first century and a half of Christian churches (AD 33 to 180).<sup id="cite_ref-McKinnon2_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinnon2-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The use of instruments for Christian worship during this period is also undocumented. Toward the end of the 2nd century, Christians began condemning the instruments themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinnon3_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinnon3-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Those who oppose instruments today believe these Church Fathers had a better understanding of God\'s desire for the church,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but there are significant differences between the teachings of these Church Fathers and Christian opposition to instruments today.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Modern Christians typically believe it is acceptable to play instruments or to attend weddings, funerals, banquets, etc., where instruments are heard playing religious music. The Church Fathers made no exceptions.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinnon3_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinnon3-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Since the New Testament never condemns instruments themselves, much less in any of these settings, it is believed that "the church Fathers go beyond the New Testament in pronouncing a negative judgment on musical instruments."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Written opposition to instruments in worship began near the turn of the 5th century.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Modern opponents of instruments typically do not make the same assessment of instruments as these writers,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;nb 3&#93;</a></sup> who argued that God had allowed David the "evil" of using musical instruments in praise.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> While the Old Testament teaches that God specifically asked for musical instruments,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> modern concern is for worship based on the New Testament.</li></ul>\n<p>Since "a cappella" singing brought a new polyphony (more than one note at a time) with instrumental accompaniment, it is not surprising that Protestant reformers who opposed the instruments (such as Calvin and Zwingli) also opposed the polyphony.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> While Zwingli was destroying organs in Switzerland&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Luther called him a fanatic&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the Church of England was burning books of polyphony.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Holiness_Churches" class="mw-redirect" title="Holiness Churches">Holiness Churches</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Free_Methodist_Church" title="Free Methodist Church">Free Methodist Church</a> opposed the use of musical instruments in church worship until the mid-20th century. The Free Methodist Church allowed for local church decision on the use of either an organ or piano in the 1943 Conference before lifting the ban entirely in 1955.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish">Jewish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jewish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While worship in the <a href="/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem" title="Temple in Jerusalem">Temple in Jerusalem</a> included musical instruments (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=2+Chronicles+29:25\xe2\x80\x9329:27&amp;version=nrsv">2 Chronicles 29:25\xe2\x80\x9329:27</a>), traditional Jewish religious services in the Synagogue, both before and after the last destruction of the Temple, did not include musical instruments<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> given the practice of scriptural <a href="/wiki/Cantillation" title="Cantillation">cantillation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The use of musical instruments is traditionally forbidden on the Sabbath out of concern that players would be tempted to repair (or tune) their instruments, which is forbidden on those days. (This prohibition has been relaxed in many Reform and some Conservative congregations.) Similarly, when Jewish families and larger groups sing traditional Sabbath songs known as <a href="/wiki/Zemirot" title="Zemirot">zemirot</a> outside the context of formal religious services, they usually do so a cappella, and Bar and Bat Mitzvah celebrations on the Sabbath sometimes feature entertainment by a cappella ensembles. During <a href="/wiki/The_Three_Weeks" title="The Three Weeks">the Three Weeks</a> musical instruments are prohibited. Many Jews consider a portion of the 49-day period of the <a href="/wiki/Counting_of_the_Omer" title="Counting of the Omer">counting of the omer</a> between Passover and Shavuot to be a time of semi-mourning and instrumental music is not allowed during that time.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> This has led to a tradition of a cappella singing sometimes known as <i>sefirah</i> music.<sup id="cite_ref-Shircago_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shircago-37">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The popularization of the Jewish chant may be found in the writings of the Jewish philosopher <a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">Philo</a>, born 20 BC. Weaving together Jewish and Greek thought, Philo promoted praise without instruments, and taught that "silent singing" (without even vocal chords) was better still.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This view parted with the Jewish scriptures, where Israel offered praise with instruments by God\'s own command (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=2+Chronicles+29:25\xe2\x80\x9329:25&amp;version=nrsv">2 Chronicles 29:25</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Shofar" title="Shofar">shofar</a> is the only temple instrument still being used today in the synagogue,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and it is only used from <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Chodesh" title="Rosh Chodesh">Rosh Chodesh</a> <a href="/wiki/Elul" title="Elul">Elul</a> through the end of <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur" title="Yom Kippur">Yom Kippur</a>. The shofar is used by itself, without any vocal accompaniment, and is limited to a very strictly defined set of sounds and specific places in the synagogue service.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  However, silver <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a>, as described in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Numbers+10:1\xe2\x80\x9310:1&amp;version=nrsv">Numbers 10:1-10</a>, have been made in recent years and used in prayer services at the <a href="/wiki/Western_Wall" title="Western Wall">Western Wall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" The A Cappella Choir was "the first permanent organization of its kind in America."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An <i>a cappella</i> tradition was begun  in 1911 by <a href="/wiki/F._Melius_Christiansen" title="F. Melius Christiansen">F. Melius Christiansen</a>, a music faculty member at <a href="/wiki/St._Olaf_College" title="St. Olaf College">St. Olaf College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northfield,_Minnesota" title="Northfield, Minnesota">Northfield, Minnesota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Norwegian_Americans_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norwegian_Americans-44">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/St._Olaf_Choir" title="St. Olaf Choir">St. Olaf College Choir</a> was established as an outgrowth of the local <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Lutheran_Church_(Northfield,_Minnesota)" title="St. John&#39;s Lutheran Church (Northfield, Minnesota)">St. John\'s Lutheran Church</a>, where Christiansen was organist and the choir was composed, at least partially, of students from the nearby St. Olaf campus. The success of the ensemble was emulated by other regional conductors, and a tradition of a cappella choral music was born in the region at colleges like <a href="/wiki/Concordia_College,_Moorhead" class="mw-redirect" title="Concordia College, Moorhead">Concordia College</a> (Moorhead, Minnesota), <a href="/wiki/Augustana_College_(Illinois)" title="Augustana College (Illinois)">Augustana College</a> (Rock Island, Illinois), <a href="/wiki/Wartburg_College" title="Wartburg College">Wartburg College</a> (Waverly, Iowa), <a href="/wiki/Luther_College_(Iowa)" title="Luther College (Iowa)">Luther College</a> (Decorah, Iowa), <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_College" title="Gustavus Adolphus College">Gustavus Adolphus College</a> (St. Peter, Minnesota), <a href="/wiki/Augustana_College_(South_Dakota)" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustana College (South Dakota)">Augustana College</a> (Sioux Falls, South Dakota), and <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_University" title="Augsburg University">Augsburg University</a> (Minneapolis, Minnesota). The choirs typically range from 40 to 80 singers and are recognized for their efforts to perfect blend, intonation, phrasing and pitch in a large choral setting.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Movements in modern a cappella over the past century include <a href="/wiki/Barbershop_music" title="Barbershop music">barbershop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doo_wop" class="mw-redirect" title="Doo wop">doo wop</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Barbershop_Harmony_Society" title="Barbershop Harmony Society">Barbershop Harmony Society</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweet_Adelines_International" title="Sweet Adelines International">Sweet Adelines International</a>, and Harmony Inc. host educational events including Harmony University, Directors University, and the International Educational Symposium, and international contests and conventions, recognizing international champion <a href="/wiki/List_of_chorus_champions_by_year" class="mw-redirect" title="List of chorus champions by year">choruses</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_quartet_champions_by_year" class="mw-redirect" title="List of quartet champions by year">quartets</a>.\n</p><p>Many a cappella groups can be found in high schools and colleges. There are amateur Barbershop Harmony Society and professional groups that sing a cappella exclusively. Although a cappella is technically defined as singing without instrumental accompaniment, some groups use their voices to emulate instruments; others are more traditional and focus on harmonizing. A cappella styles range from gospel music to contemporary to barbershop quartets and choruses.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_A_Cappella_Society" title="Contemporary A Cappella Society">Contemporary A Cappella Society</a> (CASA) is a membership option for former students, whose funds support hosted competitions and events.\n</p><p>A cappella music was popularized between the late 2000s and the early to mid-2010s with media hits such as the 2009\xe2\x80\x932014 TV show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sing-Off" title="The Sing-Off">The Sing-Off</a></i> and the musical comedy film The <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Singers" class="mw-redirect" title="King&#39;s Singers">King\'s Singers</a> are credited with promoting interest in small-group a cappella performances in the 1960s. A cappella music attained renewed prominence from the late 1980s onward, spurred by the success of Top 40 recordings by artists such as <a href="/wiki/The_Manhattan_Transfer" title="The Manhattan Transfer">The Manhattan Transfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_McFerrin" title="Bobby McFerrin">Bobby McFerrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huey_Lewis_and_the_News" title="Huey Lewis and the News">Huey Lewis and the News</a>, <a href="/wiki/All-4-One" title="All-4-One">All-4-One</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Nylons" title="The Nylons">The Nylons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Backstreet_Boys" title="Backstreet Boys">Backstreet Boys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boyz_II_Men" title="Boyz II Men">Boyz II Men</a>, and <a href="/wiki/*NSYNC" class="mw-redirect" title="*NSYNC">*NSYNC</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Contemporary a cappella includes many vocal groups and bands who add <a href="/wiki/Vocal_percussion" title="Vocal percussion">vocal percussion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Beatboxing" title="Beatboxing">beatboxing</a> to create a pop/rock/gospel sound, in some cases very similar to bands with instruments. Examples of such professional groups include <a href="/wiki/Straight_No_Chaser_(group)" title="Straight No Chaser (group)">Straight No Chaser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentatonix" title="Pentatonix">Pentatonix</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_House_Jacks" title="The House Jacks">The House Jacks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rockapella" title="Rockapella">Rockapella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(vocal_band)" title="Mosaic (vocal band)">Mosaic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Home_Free_(group)" title="Home Free (group)">Home Free</a> and <a href="/wiki/M-pact" title="M-pact">M-pact</a>. There also remains a strong a cappella presence within Christian music, as some denominations purposefully do not use instruments during worship. Examples of such groups are <a href="/wiki/Take_6" title="Take 6">Take 6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glad_(band)" title="Glad (band)">Glad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acappella_(group)" title="Acappella (group)">Acappella</a>. Arrangements of popular music for small a cappella ensembles typically include one voice singing the lead melody, one singing a rhythmic bass line, and the remaining voices contributing chordal or <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphonic</a> accompaniment.\n</p><p>A cappella can also describe the isolated vocal track(s) from a <a href="/wiki/Multitrack_recording" title="Multitrack recording">multitrack recording</a> that originally included instrumentation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These vocal tracks may be <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remixed</a> or put onto vinyl records for DJs, or released to the public so that fans can remix them. One such example is the a cappella release of <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Album_(Jay-Z_album)" title="The Black Album (Jay-Z album)">Black Album</a></i>, which <a href="/wiki/Danger_Mouse_(artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Danger Mouse (artist)">Danger Mouse</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>\' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_(album)" title="The Beatles (album)">White Album</a></i> to create <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grey_Album" title="The Grey Album">The Grey Album</a></i>.\n</p><p>On their 1966 album titled <i>Album</i>, Peter, Paul and Mary included the song "Norman Normal." All the sounds on that song, both vocals and instruments, were created by Paul\'s voice, with no actual instruments used.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2013, an artist by the name <a href="/wiki/Smooth_McGroove" title="Smooth McGroove">Smooth McGroove</a> rose to prominence with his style of a cappella music.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> He is best known for his a cappella covers of <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> music tracks on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>in 2015, an a cappella version of Jerusalem by multi-instrumentalist <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Collier" title="Jacob Collier">Jacob Collier</a> was selected for <a href="/wiki/Beats_Electronics" title="Beats Electronics">Beats by Dre</a> "The Game Starts Here"  for the <a href="/wiki/England_at_the_Rugby_World_Cup" title="England at the Rugby World Cup">England Rugby World Cup</a> campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_theatre">Musical theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Musical theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A cappella has been used as the sole orchestration for original works of <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical theatre</a> that have had commercial runs <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a> (theatres in New York City with 99 to 500 seats) only four times. The first was Avenue X which opened on 28 January 1994 and ran for 77 performances. It was produced by <a href="/wiki/Playwrights_Horizons" title="Playwrights Horizons">Playwrights Horizons</a> with book by John Jiler, music and lyrics by Ray Leslee. The musical style of the show\'s score was primarily <a href="/wiki/Doo-Wop" class="mw-redirect" title="Doo-Wop">Doo-Wop</a> as the plot revolved around Doo-Wop group singers of the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/The_Kinsey_Sicks" title="The Kinsey Sicks">The Kinsey Sicks</a>, produced and starred in the critically acclaimed <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">off-Broadway</a> hit, "DRAGAPELLA! Starring the Kinsey Sicks" at New York\'s legendary <a href="/wiki/Studio_54" title="Studio 54">Studio 54</a>. That production received a nomination for a <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Lortel" title="Lucille Lortel">Lucille Lortel</a> award as Best Musical and a Drama Desk nomination for Best Lyrics. It was directed by Glenn Casale with original music and lyrics by Ben Schatz.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The a cappella musical <a href="/wiki/Perfect_Harmony_(musical)" title="Perfect Harmony (musical)">Perfect Harmony</a>, a comedy about two high school a cappella groups vying to win the National championship, made its Off Broadway debut at Theatre Row\'s Acorn Theatre on 42nd Street in New York City in October, 2010 after a successful out-of-town run at the Stoneham Theatre, in Stoneham, Massachusetts. Perfect Harmony features the hit music of The Jackson 5, Pat Benatar, Billy Idol, Marvin Gaye, Scandal, Tiffany, The Romantics, The Pretenders, The Temptations, The Contours, The Commodores, Tommy James &amp; the Shondells and The Partridge Family, and has been compared to a cross between <a href="/wiki/Altar_Boyz" title="Altar Boyz">Altar Boyz</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_25th_Annual_Putnam_County_Spelling_Bee" title="The 25th Annual Putnam County Spelling Bee">The 25th Annual Putnam County Spelling Bee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fourth a cappella musical to appear Off-Broadway, In Transit, premiered 5 October 2010 and was produced by <a href="/wiki/Primary_Stages" title="Primary Stages">Primary Stages</a> with book, music, and lyrics by Kristen Anderson-Lopez, James-Allen Ford, Russ Kaplan, and Sara Wordsworth. Set primarily in the New York City subway system its score features an eclectic mix of musical genres (including jazz, hip hop, Latin, rock, and country). In Transit incorporates vocal beat boxing into its contemporary a cappella arrangements through the use of a subway beat boxer character. Beat boxer and actor <a href="/wiki/Chesney_Snow" title="Chesney Snow">Chesney Snow</a> performed this role for the 2010 Primary Stages production.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> According to the show\'s website, it is scheduled to reopen for an open-ended commercial run in the Fall of 2011. In 2011, the production received four Lucille Lortel Award nominations including Outstanding Musical, Outer Critics Circle and Drama League nominations, as well as five Drama Desk nominations including Outstanding Musical and won for Outstanding Ensemble Performance.\n</p><p>In December 2016, In Transit became the first a cappella musical on Broadway.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barbershop_style">Barbershop style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span The earliest reports of this style of a cappella music involved African Americans. The earliest documented quartets all began in <a href="/wiki/Barber" title="Barber">barber</a> shops. In 1938, the first formal men\'s barbershop organization was formed, known as the Society for the Preservation and Encouragement of Barber Shop Quartet Singing in America (S.P.E.B.S.Q.S.A), and in 2004 rebranded itself and officially changed its public name to the <a href="/wiki/Barbershop_Harmony_Society" title="Barbershop Harmony Society">Barbershop Harmony Society</a> (BHS). Today the BHS has about 22,000 members in approximately 800 chapters across the United States and Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> and the barbershop style has spread around the world with organizations in many other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The Barbershop Harmony Society provides a highly organized competition structure for a cappella quartets and choruses singing in the barbershop style.\n</p><p>In 1945, the first formal women\'s barbershop organization, Sweet Adelines, was formed. In 1953, Sweet Adelines became an international organization, although it didn\'t change its name to <a href="/wiki/Sweet_Adelines_International" title="Sweet Adelines International">Sweet Adelines International</a> until 1991. The membership of nearly 25,000 women, all singing in English, includes choruses in most of the fifty United States as well as in Australia, Canada, Finland, Germany, Ireland, Japan, New Zealand, Spain, Sweden, the United Kingdom, and the Netherlands. Headquartered in Tulsa, Oklahoma, the organization encompasses more than 1,200 registered quartets and 600 choruses.\n</p><p>In 1959, a second women\'s barbershop organization started as a break off from Sweet Adelines due to ideological differences. Based on democratic principles which continue to this day, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.harmonyinc.org">Harmony, Inc.</a> is smaller than its counterpart, but has an atmosphere of friendship and competition. With about 2,500 members in the United States and Canada, Harmony, Inc. uses the same rules in contest that the Barbershop Harmony Society uses. Harmony, Inc. is registered in Providence, Rhode Island.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_and_high_school">Amateur and high school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Amateur and high school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The popularity of a cappella among high schools and amateurs was revived by television shows and movies such as <i>Glee</i> and <i>Pitch Perfect</i>. The Rensselyrics of <a href="/wiki/Rensselaer_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute">Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute</a> (formerly known as the RPI Glee Club), established in 1873 is perhaps the oldest known collegiate a cappella group.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="RPI glee club founding year not specified (December 2017)">additional citation(s) needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However the longest continuously-singing group is probably <a href="/wiki/The_Whiffenpoofs" title="The Whiffenpoofs">The Whiffenpoofs</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Yale_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yale-68">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> which was formed in 1909 and once included <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">Cole Porter</a> as a member.<sup id="cite_ref-Yale_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yale-68">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Collegiate a cappella groups grew throughout the 20th century. Some notable historical groups formed along the way include <a href="/wiki/Colgate_University" title="Colgate University">Colgate University</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/The_Colgate_Thirteen" title="The Colgate Thirteen">The Colgate 13</a> (1942), <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a>\'s Aires (1946), <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>\'s Cayuga\'s Waiters (1949) and The Hangovers (1968), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maine" title="University of Maine">University of Maine</a> Maine Steiners (1958), the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> Kingsmen (1949), the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.jabberwocks.com">Jabberwocks</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a> (1949), and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester" title="University of Rochester">University of Rochester</a> YellowJackets (1956).\n</p><p>All-women a cappella groups followed shortly, frequently as a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> of the men\'s groups: the <a href="/wiki/Smiffenpoofs" title="Smiffenpoofs">Smiffenpoofs</a> of <a href="/wiki/Smith_College" title="Smith College">Smith College</a> (1936), The Shwiffs of Connecticut College (The She-Whiffenpoofs, 1944), and <a href="/wiki/The_Chattertocks_of_Brown_University" class="mw-redirect" title="The Chattertocks of Brown University">The Chattertocks of Brown University</a> (1951). A cappella groups exploded in popularity beginning in the 1990s, fueled in part by a change in style popularized by the <a href="/wiki/Beelzebubs" title="Beelzebubs">Tufts University Beelzebubs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dear_Abbeys" class="mw-redirect" title="Dear Abbeys">Boston University Dear Abbeys</a>. The new style used voices to emulate modern rock instruments, including <a href="/wiki/Vocal_percussion" title="Vocal percussion">vocal percussion</a>/"<a href="/wiki/Beatboxing" title="Beatboxing">beatboxing</a>". Some larger universities now have multiple groups. Groups often join one another in on-campus concerts, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Georgetown_Chimes" title="The Georgetown Chimes">the Georgetown Chimes</a>\' Cherry Tree Massacre, a 3-weekend a cappella festival held each February since 1975, where over a hundred collegiate groups have appeared, as well as <a href="/wiki/List_of_quartet_champions_by_year" class="mw-redirect" title="List of quartet champions by year">International Quartet Champions</a> <a href="/wiki/Boston_Common_(quartet)" title="Boston Common (quartet)">The Boston Common</a> and the contemporary commercial a cappella group <a href="/wiki/Rockapella" title="Rockapella">Rockapella</a>. Co-ed groups have produced many up-and-coming and major artists, including <a href="/wiki/John_Legend" title="John Legend">John Legend</a>, an alumnus of the Counterparts at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sara_Bareilles" title="Sara Bareilles">Sara Bareilles</a>, an alumna of Awaken A Cappella at <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">University of California, Los Angeles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mira_Sorvino" title="Mira Sorvino">Mira Sorvino</a> is an alumna of <a href="/wiki/The_Harvard-Radcliffe_Veritones" title="The Harvard-Radcliffe Veritones">the Harvard-Radcliffe Veritones</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a>, where she had the solo on <a href="/wiki/Only_You_(Yazoo_song)" title="Only You (Yazoo song)">Only You</a> by <a href="/wiki/Yazoo_(band)" title="Yazoo (band)">Yaz</a>.\n</p><p>A cappella is gaining popularity among South Asians with the emergence of primarily Hindi-English College groups. The first South Asian a cappella group was <a href="/wiki/Penn_Masala" title="Penn Masala">Penn Masala</a>, founded in 1996 at the University of Pennsylvania. Co-ed South Asian a cappella groups are also gaining in popularity. The first co-ed south Asian a cappella was Anokha, from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Maryland">University of Maryland</a>, formed in 2001. Also, Dil se, another co-ed a cappella from UC Berkeley, hosts the "Anahat" competition at the University of California, Berkeley annually. Maize Mirchi, the co-ed a cappella group from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> hosts "Sa Re Ga Ma Pella", an annual South Asian a cappella invitational with various groups from the Midwest. Another South Asian group from the Midwest is Chai Town who is based in the University of Illinois at Urbana- Champaign.\n</p><p>Jewish-interest groups such as <a href="/wiki/Queens_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Queens College">Queens College</a>\'s Tizmoret, <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University" title="Tufts University">Tufts University</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Shir_Appeal" title="Shir Appeal">Shir Appeal</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>\'s Rhythm and Jews, <a href="/wiki/Binghamton_University" title="Binghamton href="#cite_note-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Increased interest in modern a cappella (particularly collegiate a cappella) can be seen in the growth of awards such as the Contemporary A Cappella Recording Awards (overseen by the <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_A_Cappella_Society" title="Contemporary A Cappella Society">Contemporary A Cappella Society</a>) and competitions such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Championship_of_Collegiate_A_Cappella" title="International Championship of Collegiate A Cappella">International Championship of Collegiate A Cappella</a> for college groups and the <a href="/wiki/Harmony_Sweepstakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Harmony Sweepstakes">Harmony Sweepstakes</a> for all groups. In December 2009, a new television competition series called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sing-Off" title="The Sing-Off">The Sing-Off</a></i> aired on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>. The show featured eight a cappella groups from the United States and Puerto Rico vying for the prize of $100,000 and a recording contract with <a href="/wiki/Epic_Records" title="Epic Records">Epic Records</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sony_Music" title="Sony Music">Sony Music</a>. The show was judged by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Folds" title="Ben Folds">Ben Folds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Stockman" title="Shawn Stockman">Shawn Stockman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Scherzinger" title="Nicole Scherzinger">Nicole Scherzinger</a> and was won by an all-male group from Puerto Rico called <a href="/wiki/Nota_(group)" title="Nota (group)">Nota</a>. The show returned for a second, third and fourth season, won by <a href="/wiki/Committed_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Committed (group)">Committed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentatonix" title="Pentatonix">Pentatonix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Home_Free_(group)" title="Home Free (group)">Home Free</a> respectively.\n</p><p>Each year, hundreds of Collegiate a cappella groups submit their strongest songs in a competition to be on The Best of College A Cappella (BOCA), an album compilation of tracks from the best college a cappella groups around the world. The album is produced by Varsity Vocals&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 which also produces the International Championship of Collegiate A Cappella&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 and <a href="/wiki/Deke_Sharon" title="Deke Sharon">Deke Sharon</a>. ). According to ethnomusicologist Joshua S. Dunchan, "BOCA carries considerable cache and respect within the field despite the appearance of other compilations in part, perhaps, because of its longevity and the prestige of the individuals behind it."<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Collegiate a cappella groups may also submit their tracks to Voices Only, a two-disc series released at the beginning of each school year. A Voices Only album has been released every year since 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition, all women\'s a cappella groups can send their strongest song tracks to the Women\'s A Cappella Association (WACA) for its annual best of women\'s a cappella album. WACA offers another medium for women\'s voices to receive recognition and has released an album every year since 2014, featuring women\'s groups from across the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emulating_instruments">Emulating instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Emulating instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=A_cappella&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. One of the earliest 20th century practitioners of this method were <a href="/wiki/The_Mills_Brothers" title="The Mills Brothers">The Mills Brothers</a> whose early recordings of the 1930s clearly stated on the label that all instrumentation was done vocally. More recently, "<a href="/wiki/Twilight_Zone_(dance_song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Twilight Zone (dance song)">Twilight Zone</a>" by <a href="/wiki/2_Unlimited" title="2 Unlimited">2 Unlimited</a> was sung a cappella to the instrumentation on the comedy television series <i>Tompkins Square</i>. Another famous example of emulating instrumentation instead of singing the words is the theme song for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Addams_Family" title="The New Addams Family">The New Addams Family</a></i> series on <a href="/wiki/Fox_Family_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Fox Family Channel">Fox Family Channel</a> (now <a href="/wiki/ABC_Family" class="mw-redirect" title="ABC Family">ABC Family</a>). Groups such as <a href="/wiki/Vocal_Sampling" title="Vocal Sampling">Vocal Sampling</a> and Undivided emulate Latin rhythms a cappella. In the 1960s, the Swingle Singers used their voices to emulate musical instruments to Baroque and Classical music. Vocal artist <a href="/wiki/Bobby_McFerrin" title="Bobby McFerrin">Bobby McFerrin</a> is famous for his instrumental emulation. A cappella group <a href="/wiki/Naturally_Seven" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturally Seven">Naturally Seven</a> recreates entire songs using vocal tones for every instrument.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Swingle_Singers" class="mw-redirect" title="Swingle Singers">Swingle Singers</a> used nonsense words to sound like instruments, but have been known to produce non-verbal versions of musical instruments. <a href="/wiki/Beatboxing" title="Beatboxing">Beatboxing</a>, more accurately known as vocal percussion, is a technique used in a cappella music popularized by the <a href="/wiki/Hip-hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip-hop">hip-hop</a> community, where <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rap</a> is often performed a cappella also. The advent of vocal percussion added new dimensions to the a cappella genre and has become very prevalent in modern arrangements.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Jazz vocalist <a href="/wiki/Petra_Haden" title="Petra Haden">Petra Haden</a> used a four-track recorder to produce an a cappella version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Who_Sell_Out" title="The Who Sell Out">The Who Sell Out</a></i> including the instruments and fake advertisements on her album <i><a href="/wiki/Petra_Haden_Sings:_The_Who_Sell_Out" title="Petra Haden Sings: The Who Sell Out">Petra Haden Sings: The Who Sell Out</a></i> in 2005. Haden has also released a cappella versions of <a href="/wiki/Journey_(band)" title="Journey (band)">Journey</a>\'s "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Stop_Believin%27" title="Don&#39;t Stop Believin&#39;">Don\'t Stop Believin\'</a>", <a href="/wiki/The_Beach_Boys" title="The Beach Boys">The Beach Boys</a>\' "<a href="/wiki/God_Only_Knows" title="God Only Knows">God Only Knows</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>\'s "<a href="/wiki/Thriller_(song)" title="Thriller (song)">Thriller</a>".\n</p><p>Christian rock group <a href="/wiki/Relient_K" title="Relient K">Relient K</a> recorded the song "Plead the Fifth" a cappella on its album <i><a href="/wiki/Five_Score_and_Seven_Years_Ago" title="Five Score and Seven Years Ago">Five Score and Seven Years Ago</a></i>. href="#cite_ref-14">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The absence of instrumental music is rooted in various hermeneutic principles (ways of interpreting the Bible) which determine what is appropriate for worship. Among such principles are the <a href="/wiki/Regulative_principle_of_worship" title="Regulative principle of worship">regulative principle of worship</a> (Ulrich Zwingli), <a href="/wiki/Sola_scriptura" title="Sola scriptura">Sola scriptura</a> (Martin Luther and Ulrich Zwingli), and the history of <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymn</a> in "Christianity". Dispensationalism emphasizes the differences between the old (Law of Moses) and the new (Jesus and the Apostles) covenants, emphasizing that the majority of the practices from the Law of Moses were replaced by the teachings of Jesus and the Apostles. The absence of instrumental music in early church worship is significant given the abundance of Hebrew Bible references and commands to worship God with harp, lyre and cymbal. After several hundred years of Tabernacle worship without references to instrumental music, King David (ca 1500 BC) introduced musical instruments into Temple worship reportedly because of a commandment from God, complete with who was to sing, who was to play, and what instruments were to be used.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">McKinnon maintained that the organ was the first instrument to be introduced into worship and the next was the trumpet.
