href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_England" title="Privy Council of England">PC</a> (29 August 1628 \xe2\x80\x93 22 August 1701), of <a href="/wiki/Stowe,_Kilkhampton" title="Stowe, Kilkhampton">Stowe</a> in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Kilkhampton" title="Kilkhampton">Kilkhampton</a> in Cornwall, was an English <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalist</a> soldier and statesman during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a> who played a major role in the 1660 <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(1660)" title="Restoration (1660)">Restoration of the Monarchy</a> and was later appointed <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dnb_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thepeerage_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thepeerage-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cokayne_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cokayne-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He was the first in his family to adopt the modernised spelling as <i>Granville</i> of their ancient surname <i>Grenville</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> which emphasised their supposed ancient 11th-century origin from the <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> manor of <a href="/wiki/Granville,_Manche" title="Granville, Manche">Granville, Manche</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Granville,_1st_Earl_of_Bath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>He was the eldest son and heir of Sir <a href="/wiki/Bevil_Grenville" title="Bevil Grenville">Bevil Grenville</a> (1596\xe2\x80\x931643) <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_manor" title="Lord of the manor">lord of the manors</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bideford" title="Bideford">Bideford</a> in Devon and <a href="/wiki/Stowe,_Kilkhampton" title="Stowe, Kilkhampton">Stowe, Kilkhampton</a> in Cornwall, a <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalist</a> soldier  killed in action in heroic circumstances at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lansdowne" title="Battle of Lansdowne">Battle of Lansdowne</a> in 1643 during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a>. Sir Bevil served as <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Cornwall (UK Parliament constituency)">Cornwall</a> 1621\xe2\x80\x931625 and 1640\xe2\x80\x9342, and for <a href="/wiki/Launceston_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Launceston (UK Parliament constituency)">Launceston</a> in 1625\xe2\x80\x931629 and 1640. John\'s mother was Grace Smythe,<sup id="cite_ref-dnb_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> a daughter by his second marriage of Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Smith_(MP_for_Exeter)" title="George Smith (MP for Exeter)">George Smith</a> (d. 1619) of Madworthy, near Exeter,<sup id="cite_ref-Vivian_1531,_p._569_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vivian_1531,_p._569-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Devon, a merchant who served as <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> for <a href="/wiki/Exeter_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Exeter (UK Parliament constituency)">Exeter</a> in 1604, was three times Mayor of Exeter and was Exeter\'s richest citizen, possessing 25 <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manors</a> or part manors.<sup id="cite_ref-Vivian_1531,_p._569_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vivian_1531,_p._569-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> John had thirteen siblings, all by <a href="/wiki/Royal_Warrant_of_Precedence" title="Royal Warrant of Precedence">Royal Warrant of Precedence</a> granted the rank and title of Earl\'s children by King Charles II on 20 August 1675, in recognition of their father\'s services.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Grace\'s half-sister Elizabeth Smythe was the wife of Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Monk" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Monk">Thomas Monk</a> (1570\xe2\x80\x931627) of <a href="/wiki/Potheridge" title="Potheridge">Potheridge</a>, Devon, <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> for <a href="/wiki/Camelford_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Camelford (UK Parliament constituency)">Camelford</a> in 1626, and mother of the great General <a href="/wiki/George_Monck,_1st_Duke_of_Albemarle" title="George Monck, 1st Duke of Albemarle">George Monck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Albemarle" title="Duke of Albemarle">Duke of Albemarle</a>, KG (1608\xe2\x80\x931670), the main figure behind the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(1660)" title="Restoration (1660)">Restoration of the Monarchy</a> in 1660. It was largely due to his close kinship to his first cousin the Duke that Sir John Grenville was raised to the peerage in 1660 as <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Bath" title="Earl of Bath">Earl of Bath</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and was also granted the reversion of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Albemarle" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Albemarle">Duchy of Albemarle</a> in the event of the failure of George Monck\'s male issue.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Granville,_1st_Earl_of_Bath&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Granville fought in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> in his father\'s <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiment</a> on the side of the defeated King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> (1625\xe2\x80\x931649).<sup id="cite_ref-dnb_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He was created a <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a> due to his bravery, and became a <a href="/wiki/Gentleman_of_the_Bedchamber" title="Gentleman of the Bedchamber">Gentleman of the Bedchamber</a> to Prince <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Siarl II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Wales" title="Principality of Wales">Wales</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-dnb_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> later King <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> (1660\xe2\x80\x931685).\n</p><p>He accompanied the future King <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> into exile, and mediated with the <a href="/wiki/Long_Parliament" title="Long Parliament">Long Parliament</a>. After the Scilly Isles rebelled against the Parliamentary government in 1648, he was sent by Charles II to be the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_the_Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of the Scilly Isles">Governor of the Scilly Isles</a> in place of the Parliamentarian Governor <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Buller_(1613%E2%80%931679)" title="Anthony Buller (1613\xe2\x80\x931679)">Anthony Buller</a>. Under his governorship, the Scilly Isles became a base for Royalist privateers attacking both English and Dutch vessels in the Western Approaches and in May 1651 he was ousted by Parliamentary forces under <a href="/wiki/Robert_Blake_(admiral)" title="Robert Blake (admiral)">Robert Blake</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1660, Granville was instrumental in the negotiations between his first cousin <a href="/wiki/George_Monck,_1st_Duke_of_Albemarle" title="George Monck, 1st Duke of Albemarle">George Monck</a>, and Charles II that led to the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">Restoration of the Monarchy</a> to that King. Shortly after the Restoration, he contested the succession of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Albemarle" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Albemarle">Duchy of Albemarle</a>, but lost. On 20 April 1661<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> he was created by King Charles II <a href="/wiki/Baron_Granville" title="Baron Granville">Baron Granville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viscount_Granville" class="mw-redirect" title="Viscount Granville">Viscount Granville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Bath" title="Earl of Bath">Earl of Bath</a> and in 1663 was invested as a <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_England" title="Privy Council of England">Privy Councillor</a>. In 1665, he served, in title at least, as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</a>, although he never went to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and consequently is not considered a true holder of that office. In about 1680, he rebuilt his ancestral seat of <a href="/wiki/Stowe_House,_Kilkhampton" class="mw-redirect" title="Stowe House, Kilkhampton">Stowe House</a> in Cornwall in a grand style befitting his new noble status. His cousin the Duke of Albemarle between 1660 and his death in 1670, had commenced rebuilding on a similarly grand scale of his own ancestral seat of <a href="/wiki/Potheridge" title="Potheridge">Potheridge</a> in Devon, about 18 miles to the east of Stowe. Both new mansions were demolished within a few decades.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. The charges appear in the form of musical pipes of a wind-instrument, similar to <a href="/wiki/Pan-pipes" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan-pipes">pan-pipes</a>. Authoritative sources on heraldry suggest the charges to be variously "clarions" (used by <a href="/wiki/John_Guillim" title="John Guillim">Guillim</a> (d.1621)), the most usual blazon, which are however generally defined as a form of trumpet; "rests" is another common blazon, denoting lance-rests supposedly used by a mounted knight; "organ-rests" is also met with, a seemingly meaningless term (Gibbon (1682)). Other terms are "clavicymbal", "clarichord" and "sufflue" (used by Leigh in his Armory of 1562 and by Boswell, 1572),<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> the latter being a device for blowing (French: souffler) air into an organ.,<sup id="cite_ref-heraldica.org_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica.org-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Guillim suggested the charge may be a rudder,<sup id="cite_ref-heraldica.org_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica.org-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> but in which case it is shown upside down, when compared to that charge used for example on the tomb at <a href="/wiki/Callington,_Cornwall" title="Callington, Cornwall">Callington</a> of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Willoughby,_1st_Baron_Willoughby_de_Broke" title="Robert Willoughby, 1st Baron Willoughby de Broke">Robert Willoughby, 1st Baron Willoughby de Broke</a>.
