Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Priscillianism</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> belief system developed in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a>) in the 4th century by <a href="/wiki/Priscillian" title="Priscillian">Priscillian</a>, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnostic</a>-<a href="/wiki/Manichaeism" title="Manichaeism">Manichaean</a> doctrines taught by <i>Marcus</i>, an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a> from <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Egypt" title="Memphis, Egypt">Memphis</a>, and later considered a <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a> by both the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Teaching"><span class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#Notes"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">Notes</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Marcus, a native of Memphis in Egypt, came to Spain and taught Gnostic and Manichean theories. Two of his followers, a Spanish lady named Agape and the <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetorician</a> Helpidius, converted Priscillian,<sup id="cite_ref-Wace_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wace-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> who was a layman "of noble birth, of great riches, bold, restless, eloquent, learned through much reading, very ready at debate and discussion".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Through his oratorical gifts and reputation for extreme <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">asceticism</a> he attracted a large following, including a <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetorician</a> named Helpidus and two bishops, Instantius and Salvianus. The new sect became an oath-bound society, attracting the attention of the bishop <a href="/wiki/Hyginus_of_Cordoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyginus of Cordoba">Hyginus of Cordoba</a>. Hyginus made his fears known to Hydatius, Bishop of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Spain" title="M\xc3\xa9rida, Spain">Emerita</a>, and Ithacius of <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Ossonoba</a>.  The bishops of <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquitaine" title="Aquitaine">Aquitaine</a> then held a synod at <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a> in 380. Though summoned, the Priscillianists refused to appear, and the synod pronounced sentence of excommunication against the four leaders, Instantius, Salvianus, Helpidius and Priscillian.<sup id="cite_ref-Healy_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Healy-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ithacius was chosen to enforce the synod\'s decrees, but he failed to bring the heretics to terms.  In defiance Priscillian was ordained to the priesthood and appointed <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Avila" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Avila">Bishop of Avila</a>. Ithacius then appealed to the imperial authorities. The Emperor <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> issued a decree which deprived the Priscillianists of their churches and sentenced them to exile. Instantius, Salvianus and Priscillian proceeded to Rome to gain the aid of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Damasus_I" title="Pope Damasus I">Pope Damasus I</a> (also from Iberia) in having this sentence revoked. On their journey they were joined by many supporters from Gaul. Euchrocia and her daughter Procula, amongst these, ministered of their substance to Priscillian and his colleagues. A promiscuous crowd of others, especially women, are mentioned. In consequence, injurious reports, probably calumnies, were vigorously circulated against Priscillian and his retinue.<sup id="cite_ref-Wace_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wace-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Denied an audience by Pope Damasus, they went to Milan to make a similar request of <a href="/wiki/St_Ambrose" class="mw-redirect" title="St Ambrose">St Ambrose</a>, but with the same result. They then resorted to intrigue and bribery at the Court with such success that they were not only freed from the sentence of exile, but permitted to regain possession of their churches in Hispania, where, under the patronage of the imperial officials, they enjoyed such power as to compel Ithacius to leave the country. He, in turn, appealed to <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a>, but before anything had been accomplished the emperor was murdered in Lyon, and <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Maximus" title="Magnus Maximus">Magnus Maximus</a> had taken his place.<sup id="cite_ref-Healy_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Healy-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Maximus treated the matter not as one of ecclesiastical rivalry, but as one of morality and society.<sup id="cite_ref-Wace_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wace-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> As he wished to curry favour with the orthodox bishops and to replenish his treasury through confiscations, the Emperor gave orders for a synod, which was held in <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> in 384. Instantius was first tried and condemned to deposition. Thereupon Priscillian appealed to the emperor at <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a>. Ithacius acted as his accuser and was so vehement in his denunciations that <a href="/wiki/St_Martin_of_Tours" class="mw-redirect" title="St Martin of Tours">St Martin of Tours</a>, who was then in Trier, intervened, and, after expressing his disapproval of bringing an ecclesiastical case before a civil tribunal, obtained from the emperor a promise not to carry his condemnation to the extent of shedding blood.\n</p><p>After St Martin had left the city, the emperor appointed the Prefect Evodius as judge. He found Priscillian and some others guilty of the crime of magic. This decision was reported to the emperor, who killed Priscillian and several of his followers; the property of others was confiscated and they were banished. The conduct of Ithacius immediately met with the severest reprobation. St Martin, hearing what had taken place, returned to Trier and compelled the emperor to rescind an order to the <a href="/wiki/Military_tribune" title="Military tribune">military tribunes</a>, already on their way to Iberia to extirpate the heresy. There is no ground in the condemnation and death of Priscillian for the charge made against the Church of having invoked the civil authority to punish heretics. The pope censured not only the actions of Ithacius but also that of the emperor. St Ambrose was equally stern in his denunciation of the case and some of the Gallican bishops, who were in Trier under the leadership of Theognistus, broke off communion with Ithacius, who was subsequently deposed from his see by a synod of Hispanic bishops, and his friend and abettor Hydatius, was compelled to resign.\n</p><p>After the death of Priscillian and his followers, however, the numbers and zeal of the heretics only increased. In 400 another synod was held in <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a> to deal with this problem; many, including bishops Symphonius and Dictinnius were there reconciled to the Church.  Dictinnius was the author of a book "Libra" (Scales), a moral treatise from the Priscillianist viewpoint. The upheaval in the Iberian Peninsula due to the invasion of the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Suevi" class="mw-redirect" title="Suevi">Suevi</a> aided the spread of Priscillianism. <a href="/wiki/Paulus_Orosius" class="mw-redirect" title="Paulus Orosius">Paulus Orosius</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecian</a> priest from northwest Hispania, wrote to <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">St Augustine</a> (415) to enlist his aid in combating the heresy. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_I" title="Pope Leo I">Pope Leo I</a> at a later date took active steps for its repression and at his urgent insistence councils were held in 446 and 447 at <a href="/wiki/Astorga,_Spain" title="Astorga, Spain">Astorga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a>. In spite of these efforts the sect continued to spread during the fifth century. In the following century it began to decline, and after the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Braga" title="First Council of Braga">First Council of Braga</a> (563), which specifically targeted its doctrines, it soon died out.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching">Teaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Teaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The foundation of the doctrines of the Priscillianists was Gnostic-Manichaean <a href="/wiki/Dualistic_cosmology" title="Dualistic cosmology">dualism</a>, a belief in the existence of two kingdoms, one of Light and one of Darkness. Angels and the souls of men were said to be severed from the substance of the Deity. Human souls were intended to conquer the Kingdom of Darkness, but fell and were imprisoned in material bodies. Thus both kingdoms were represented in man, and hence a conflict symbolized on the side of Light by the Twelve Patriarchs, heavenly spirits, who corresponded to certain of man\'s powers, and on the side of Darkness by the Signs of the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">Zodiac</a>, the symbols of matter and the lower kingdom. The salvation of man consists in liberation from the domination of matter. The twelve heavenly spirits having failed to accomplish their release, the Saviour came in a heavenly body which appeared to be like that of other men, and through His doctrine and His apparent death released the souls of the men from the influence of earthly matter.\n</p><p>These doctrines could be harmonized with the teaching of Scripture only by a complex system of <a href="/wiki/Exegesis" title="Exegesis">exegesis</a>, rejecting conventional interpretations and relying on personal inspiration. The Priscillians respected most of the Old Testament but rejected the creation story.  Several of the apocryphal Scriptures were acknowledged to be genuine and inspired. Because the Priscillians believe that matter and nature were evil, they became ascetics and fasted on Sundays and Christmas Day.  Because their doctrines were esoteric and exoteric, and because it was believed that men in general could not understand the higher paths, the Priscillianists, or at least those of them who were enlightened, were permitted to tell lies for the sake of a holy end.  <a href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Augustine">Augustine</a> wrote a famous work, "Contra Mendacium" ("Against Lying") in reaction to this doctrine.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings_and_rediscovery">Writings and rediscovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writings and rediscovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some writings by Priscillian were accounted Orthodox and were not burned. For instance he divided the Pauline epistles (including the Epistle to the Hebrews) into a series of texts on their theological points and wrote an introduction to each section. These "canons" survived in a form edited by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrinus_(Spanish_bishop)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Peregrinus (Spanish bishop) (page does not exist)">Peregrinus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They contain a strong call to a life of personal piety and asceticism, including <a href="/wiki/Celibacy" title="Celibacy">celibacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_vegetarianism" title="Christian vegetarianism">abstinence from meat</a> and wine. The charismatic gifts of all believers are equally affirmed. Study of scripture is urged. Priscillian placed considerable weight on the <a href="/wiki/Deuterocanonical" class="mw-redirect" title="Deuterocanonical">deuterocanonical</a> books of the Bible, not as being inspired but as helpful in discerning truth and error; however several of the books were considered to be genuine and inspired.<sup id="cite_ref-Healy_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Healy-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was long thought that all the writings of Priscillian himself had perished, but in 1885, Georg Schepss discovered at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wurzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Wurzburg">University of Wurzburg</a> eleven genuine tracts, published in the Vienna Corpus 1886. Though they bear Priscillian\'s name, four describing Priscillian\'s trial appear to have been written by a close follower.\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Raymond_E._Brown" title="Raymond E. Brown">Raymond Brown\'s</a> <i>Epistle of John</i>, the source of the <i><a href="/wiki/Comma_Johanneum" class="mw-redirect" title="Comma Johanneum">Comma Johanneum</a></i> appears to be the Latin book <i>Liber Apologeticus</i> by Priscillian.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
