Free throws are generally awarded after a <a href="/wiki/Personal_foul_(basketball)" title="Personal foul (basketball)">foul</a> on the shooter by the opposing team. Each successful free throw is worth one point.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div percentage by good players. In the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a>, most players make 70\xe2\x80\x9380% of their attempts. The league\'s best shooters (such as <a href="/wiki/Steve_Nash" title="Steve Nash">Steve Nash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Barry" title="Rick Barry">Rick Barry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Allen" title="Ray Allen">Ray Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Calder%C3%B3n_(basketball)" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Calder\xc3\xb3n (basketball)">Jos\xc3\xa9 Calder\xc3\xb3n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Curry" title="Stephen Curry">Stephen Curry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Miller" title="Reggie Miller">Reggie Miller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Durant" title="Kevin Durant">Kevin Durant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Nowitzki" title="Dirk Nowitzki">Dirk Nowitzki</a>) can make roughly 90% of their attempts over a season, while notoriously poor shooters (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Howard" title="Dwight Howard">Dwight Howard</a>, <a href="/wiki/DeAndre_Jordan" title="DeAndre Jordan">DeAndre Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilt_Chamberlain" title="Wilt Chamberlain">Wilt Chamberlain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andre_Drummond" title="Andre Drummond">Andre Drummond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andris_Biedrins" class="mw-redirect" title="Andris Biedrins">Andris Biedrins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Dudley" title="Chris Dudley">Chris Dudley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Wallace" title="Ben Wallace">Ben Wallace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal" title="Shaquille O&#39;Neal">Shaquille O\'Neal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Rodman" title="Dennis Rodman">Dennis Rodman</a>) may struggle to make 50% of them. During a foul shot, a player\'s foot must be completely behind the foul line. If a player lines up with part of his or her foot on the line, a violation is called and the shot does not count. Foul shots are worth one point.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="When_free_throws_are_awarded">When free throws are awarded</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_throw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: When free throws are awarded">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:239px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg/237px-GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg" width="237" height="254" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg/356px-GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg/474px-GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2156" data-file-height="2312" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GT_vs_Centenary_Free_Throw_2006.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Players waiting on the side during a free throw at a <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Tech_Yellow_Jackets_basketball" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgia Tech Yellow Jackets basketball">Georgia Tech Yellow Jackets</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Centenary_Gentlemen_basketball" title="Centenary Gentlemen basketball">Centenary Gentlemen</a> <a href="/wiki/College_basketball" title="College basketball">college basketball</a> game.</div></div></div>\n<p>There are many situations when free throws can be awarded.\n</p><p>The first and most common is when a player is fouled while in the act of shooting. If the player misses the shot during the foul, the player receives either two or three free throws depending on whether the shot was taken in front of or behind the three-point line. If, despite the foul, the player still makes the attempted shot, the number of free throws is reduced to one, and the basket counts. This is known as a <a href="/wiki/Three-point_play" title="Three-point play">three-point</a> or <a href="/wiki/Four-point_play" title="Four-point play">four-point play</a>, depending on the value of the made basket.\n</p><p>The second is when the fouling team is in the team <a href="/wiki/Bonus_(basketball)" title="Bonus (basketball)">bonus (or foul penalty) situation</a>. This happens when, in a single period, a team commits a set number of fouls whether or not in the act of shooting. In FIBA, (W)NBA and NCAA women\'s play, the limit is four fouls per quarter; in the NBA, starting with the fifth foul (fourth in overtime), or the second in the final 2 minutes if the team has less than 5 fouls (4 in OT), the opposing team gets two free throws. In the WNBA, the fouled player shoots two free throws starting with the opponent\'s fifth foul (4th in overtime), or second team foul in the final minute if that team has committed under 5 fouls in a period (4 in overtime). In FIBA and NCAA women\'s basketball, the fouled player also shoots two free throws starting with the opponent\'s fifth foul in a period, considering that team fouls accrue from the fourth period on, as all overtimes are extensions of it for purposes of accrued team fouls. In NCAA men\'s basketball, beginning with the seventh foul of the half, one free throw is awarded; if the player makes the free throw, another is given. This is called shooting a "one-and-one". Starting with the tenth foul of the half, two free throws are awarded. In addition, overtime is considered an extension of the second half for purposes of accumulated team fouls. Free throws are not awarded for offensive fouls (most often <a href="/wiki/Personal_foul_(basketball)" title="Personal foul (basketball)">charging fouls</a>), even if the team fouled is in the bonus. The number of fouls that triggers a penalty is higher in college men\'s basketball because the game is divided into two 20-minute halves, as opposed to quarters of 12 minutes in the NBA or 10 minutes in the WNBA, college women\'s basketball, or FIBA play (note that the college women\'s game was played in 20-minute halves before 2015\xe2\x80\x9316). As in professional play, a foul in the act of shooting is a two- or three-shot foul, depending on the value of the shot attempt, with one free throw being awarded if the shot is good.\n</p><p>If a player is <a href="/wiki/Injured" class="mw-redirect" title="Injured">injured</a> upon being fouled and cannot shoot free throws, the offensive team may designate any player from the bench to shoot in the place of the injured player in college; in the NBA, the opposing team designates the player to shoot, and the injured player can\'t return, unless the foul committed was a flagrant-2, in which case the player\'s own team also gets to pick the replacement shooter. If a player fouled takes exception to the foul, and starts or participates in a <a href="/wiki/Fight" class="mw-redirect" title="Fight">fight</a>, and gets ejected, he or she is not allowed to take his or her free throws, and the opposing team will choose a replacement shooter. In all other circumstances, the fouled player must shoot his or her own foul shots.\n</p><p>If a player, coach, or team staff (e.g., doctor, statistician) shows poor sportsmanship, which may include arguing with a <a href="/wiki/Official_(basketball)" title="Official (basketball)">referee</a>, that person may get charged with a more serious foul called a <a href="/wiki/Technical_foul" title="Technical foul">technical foul</a>. In the NBA, a technical foul results in one free throw attempt for the other team. In FIBA play, technical fouls result in two free throws in all situations. Under NCAA rules, technical fouls are divided into "Class&#160;A" (violent or serious unsportsmanlike conduct) and "Class&#160;B" (less egregious violations such as hanging on the rim or delay of game). Class&#160;A technicals result in two free throws, and Class&#160;B technicals result in one. At all levels, the opposing team may choose any player who is currently on the court to shoot the free throws, and is then awarded possession of the ball after the free throws. Since there is no opportunity for a <a href="/wiki/Rebound_(basketball)" title="Rebound (basketball)">rebound</a>, these free throws are shot with no players on the lane.\n</p><p>Finally, if a referee deems a foul extremely aggressive, or that it did not show an attempt to play the ball, the referee can call an even more severe foul, known as an "unsportsmanlike foul" in international play or a "<a href="/wiki/Flagrant_foul" title="Flagrant foul">flagrant foul</a>" in the NBA and NCAA basketball. This foul is charged against the player (and depending on the severity of the offense, can even be ejected), and the opponent gets two free throws and possession of the ball afterwards. Unlike technical fouls, the player fouled must shoot the awarded free throws.\n</p><p>Fouls "away from the ball" (fouls that do not occur on the shooter or near the ball) are handled like the second case above in most situations. Many times defenders hold their opponent to prevent them from catching an in-bound pass or fight through screens and thus are called for fouls. These fouls are almost always treated as normal personal fouls. In the NBA, when there are only two minutes left on the clock of either half, off-ball fouls when the fouling team is over the limit are rewarded with one free throw and possession of the ball. It is therefore common for a losing team to deliberately single out its opponent\'s poor free throw shooters, regardless of their dominance in other aspects of the game (as in the cases of <a href="/wiki/Ben_Wallace" title="Ben Wallace">Ben Wallace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal" title="Shaquille O&#39;Neal">Shaquille O\'Neal</a>), as the targets of deliberate fouls until the two-minute mark, after which the losing team plays intense defense for the rest of the game; this strategy is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Hack-a-Shaq" title="Hack-a-Shaq">Hack-a-Shaq</a>". It is believed that this rule was instituted because of <a href="/wiki/Wilt_Chamberlain" title="Wilt Chamberlain">Wilt Chamberlain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Previously teams had been allowed to foul any player on the court regardless of whether that player had possession of the ball, with only two free throws awarded to the fouled player. This motivated teams to chase poor free throw shooters, such as Chamberlain, around the court in attempt to foul him in an effort to extend the game. To discourage this practice, the NBA changed the rule to award one free throw and possession of the ball to a player who is fouled away from the ball in the last two minutes of the fourth quarter. This rule does not apply in international or NCAA play and in fact plays a very vital strategic role in the NCAA Tournament.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_throw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SackoLF2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/SackoLF2.jpg/300px-SackoLF2.jpg" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/SackoLF2.jpg/450px-SackoLF2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/SackoLF2.jpg/600px-SackoLF2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SackoLF2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatimatou_Sacko" class="extiw" title="fr:Fatimatou Sacko">Fatimatou Sacko</a> for her first free throw.</div></div></div>\n<p>Free throws are organized in procession. The shooter takes his place behind the free throw line (5.8&#160;m (19&#160;ft) from the base line, 4.6&#160;m (15&#160;ft) from the basket). All other players must stand in their correct places until the ball leaves the shooter\'s hands: up to four people in the NCAA rules<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and three people in the FIBA rules<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> from the defensive team and two people from the shooting team line up along the sides of the restricted area (keyhole, paint, lane). These players are usually the ones that rebound the ball. Three line up on each side. A defensive player always takes the place closest to the basket.\n</p><p>The remaining players must remain behind the three-point line and the "free throw line extended" (an imaginary line extended from the free throw line in both directions to the sidelines).\n</p><p>Leaving their designated places before the ball leaves the shooter\'s hands, or interfering with the ball, are violations. In addition, the shooter must release the ball within five seconds (ten seconds in the United States) and must not step on or over the free throw line until the ball touches the hoop. Players are, however, permitted to jump while attempting the free throw, provided they do not leave the designated area at any point.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a title="FIBA">FIBA</a> rules, if the shooter does not commit a violation, and the ball goes in the basket, the attempt is successful, regardless of violations committed by any class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_throw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Prior to the 1954\xe2\x80\x9355 season, the NBA established the rule that a backcourt foul would result in a "three to make two" situation (up to three attempts to make two free throws) if the violating team was over the team foul limit.<sup id="cite_ref-nbaRules_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbaRules-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> By 1979, the rule had been extended to the penalty situation for flagrant fouls, fouls made in the act of shooting (resulting in a miss), and fouls due to the swinging of the elbows.<sup id="cite_ref-winick_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winick-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Prior to the 1981\xe2\x80\x9382 season, this rule and the related "two to make one" rule were abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-nbaRules_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbaRules-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1956, in response to reports that Wilt Chamberlain was able to dunk free throws, the NCAA established a rule requiring that free throw shooters keep both feet behind the free throw line during an attempt.<sup id="cite_ref-toledo_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toledo-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The NBA later adopted this rule.<sup id="cite_ref-silverman_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-silverman-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategy">Strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a players are notoriously bad free throw shooters. Historical examples of star players who were poor shooters include Wilt Chamberlain, Ben Wallace, and Shaquille O\'Neal.<sup id="cite_ref-fixler20121213_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fixler20121213-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This allows a strategy where a team intentionally fouls this player, hoping that they will miss one or both shots and the defending team will re-gain possession of the ball. This strategy was dubbed <a href="/wiki/Hack-a-Shaq" title="Hack-a-Shaq">Hack-a-Shaq</a> when famously used on Shaquille O\'Neal. Dwight Howard, DeAndre Jordan and Andre Drummond<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> has been subjected to this strategy as well,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> as have <a href="/wiki/Hack-a-Shaq#Application_against_other_players" title="Hack-a-Shaq">other players</a>.\n</p><p>It can be advantageous for a trailing team to intentionally foul late in a close game. While this allows the leading team to shoot free throws and increase their lead, it also stops the clock, similar to a time out in <a href="/wiki/Gridiron_football" title="Gridiron football">football</a> (in basketball, a time out may only be called by a team with possession of the ball). As well, it allows the trailing team the chance to take possession if the second free throw is missed, and if it is made awards possession outright to the trailing team. Therefore, many teams substitute players with high free throw percentages when they are leading late in games to combat this strategy. It is seen as a last resort strategy with little chance of success, but if it is not employed the leading team may run out the clock and end the game.\n</p><p>As well, a defensive team leading by three may foul an offensive team intentionally late in the game, if the game clock is no greater than a couple of seconds. In such circumstances, the opposing team would most likely not have enough time for multiple possessions. Down three points, the opposing team\'s obvious strategy would be to attempt a <a href="/wiki/Three-point_field_goal" title="Three-point field goal">three-pointer</a> to try to send the game into overtime; however, the leading team may attempt to foul the trailing team before a shot is attempted, as this would lead to only two free throws which is not enough to tie the game. If the trailing team is placed into the situation of shooting two free throws, they must attempt to intentionally miss the second free throw in the hopes of securing the offensive rebound (which is harder than normal with free throws, as the defending team is automatically given position) and scoring before the game clock expires \xe2\x80\x93 if the first free throw is made, a two-pointer would tie the game, and a three-pointer would win the game; if the first free throw is missed, only a three-pointer would tie the game. Theoretically, adopting this strategy makes a leading team four times more likely to win the game than allowing the trailing team to attempt a three-pointer.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_throw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The majority of adult professional players shoot free throws in the overhand style, despite both theoretical and practical demonstrations that the underhand style (aka "granny-style") usually produces better average results.<sup id="cite_ref-discover_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discover-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CT_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CT-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Famously, in the NBA, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Barry" title="Rick Barry">Rick Barry</a> retired in 1980 while ranked first in NBA history at the time with his 0.900 free throw percentage.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> There were only a few professional players who have utilized this technique, while the vast majority of them resisted adopting the technique for the fear of ridicule or for other similar reasons unrelated to performance.<sup id="cite_ref-discover_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discover-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CT_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CT-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
