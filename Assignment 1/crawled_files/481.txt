title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span island to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> archipelago between <a href="/wiki/Tresco,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Tresco, Isles of Scilly">Tresco</a>, 1.5 kilometres (0.9&#160;mi) to the west and <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Martin&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Martin\'s</a> 300 metres (330&#160;yd) to the east.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  \nApproximately 16 hectares (40 acres) in area the island consists of a series of <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> <a href="/wiki/Tor_(rock_formation)" title="Tor (rock formation)">tors</a> with the highest point, Great Hill, rising to 40 metres (130&#160;ft) at its eastern end.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The low-lying land is overlain with <a href="/wiki/Glacial_till" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial till">glacial till</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Outwash_gravels&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Outwash gravels (page does not exist)">outwash gravels</a> with <a href="/wiki/Glacial_erratics" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial erratics">glacial erratics</a> abundant on the north coast beaches which indicates the southern limit of outwash from an ice sheet<sup id="cite_ref-Anon_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anon-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> for which it is designated a <a href="/wiki/Geological_Conservation_Review" title="Geological Conservation Review">Geological Conservation Review</a> site.<sup id="cite_ref-Natural_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Natural-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is evidence of occupation from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age</a> to the early 19th century and the island was still being grazed in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> An early Christian chapel exists on the island, it was  possibly dedicated to a saint called Theon.\n</p><p>The island lies within both the Isles of Scilly <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Heritage Coast">Heritage Coast</a> and the Isles of Scilly <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>. It is managed by the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly_Wildlife_Trust" title="Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust">Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust</a> which has an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_stewardship_(England)#Higher_Level_Stewardship" title="Environmental stewardship (England)">Higher Level Stewardship</a> (HLS) agreement.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%A4n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The coastline of Te\xc3\xa4n consists of a number of bays and sandy beaches which link to offshore rocks and carns at low tide. The western part of the island has low-lying ground linking granite carns, and field boundaries from the <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> period can be seen at extreme low tides.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> One of the carns, Old Man, has an early structure, a <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Scillonian_entrance_grave" title="Scillonian entrance grave">entrance grave</a>, as does Great Hill in the east of the island. Roman type brooches have been found in a grave on Old Man.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Sixteen early Christian graves have been found under the east wall of St Theona\'s chapel which was built later on top of the graves. There was probably an earlier wooden chapel.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A Parliamentary survey of 1652 reported one man living in a ruined house on the island <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and, in 1684, there was a thatched cottage between East Porth and West Porth which belonged to a Mr Nance who is reputed to have introduced <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> burning to Scilly. Kelp burning provides <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> for glass making and the practice continued in the islands until 1835. Kelp burning only produces 2-3 percent sodium carbonate and during the 19th century more efficient commercial and industrial methods ended the practice locally. Rights to areas of kelp were allocated to families and in 1787 Thomas Woodcock, his son and James Ashford (all of St Martin\'s) were accused of "having trespassed on his (Nance\'s) preserves". After the hearing the court decided that the cutting of ore-weed and the making of kelp on Te\xc3\xa4n was the prescriptive right of Nance, and the trespassers were fined 2s 6d each.<sup id="cite_ref-Vivian_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vivian-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> His family continued to live on Te\xc3\xa4n for several more generations and by 1717 there were ten people living on the island, but in 1752 <a href="/wiki/William_Borlase" title="William Borlase">William Borlase</a> only saw fields of corn and ruined buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In the 19th century Woodley reported occasional occupation, a few acres of cultivation and sheep grazing<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and a 1919 guide book reported just a rabbit warren.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Cattle were still being grazed in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_history">Natural history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%A4n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island was first notified as a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> in 1971 and re-notified in 1986 under the 1981 Act. The SSSI was last assessed on 8 September 2009 and was found to be favourable. The assessment found that the vascular plant assemblage was all recorded apart from four-leaved allseed <i><a href="/wiki/Polycarpon_tetraphyllum" title="Polycarpon tetraphyllum">Polycarpon tetraphyllum</a></i>. A key issue was lack of management for orange bird\xe2\x80\x99s-foot (<i><a href="/wiki/Ornithopus" title="Ornithopus">Ornithopus pinnatus</a></i>) which needs short turf, and can be addressed with the HLS agreement held by the Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust.<sup id="cite_ref-Anon_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anon-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%A4n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Human activity continued until relatively recent and is evident with 8 ha of the island surrounded by hedges and once cultivated. This area, with deeper soils, is dominated by bracken (<i><a href="/wiki/Pteridium_aquilinum" title="Pteridium aquilinum">Pteridium aquilinum</a></i>) and still has relic pasture plants such as rye grass (<i><a href="/wiki/Lolium_perenne" title="Lolium perenne">Lolium perenne</a></i>), red clover (<i><a href="/wiki/Trifolium_pratense" title="Trifolium pratense">Trifolium pratense</a></i>), hop trefoil (<i><a href="/wiki/Trifolium_campestre" title="Trifolium campestre">Trifolium campestre</a></i>) and black knapweed (<i><a href="/wiki/Centaurea_nigra" title="Centaurea nigra">Centaurea nigra</a></i>). The maritime grassland around St Helen\'s Porth, and on the south coast, has abundant thrift (<i><a href="/wiki/Armeria_maritima" title="Armeria maritima">Armeria maritima</a></i>) and sea campion (<i><a href="/wiki/Silene" title="Silene">Silene maritime</a></i>), and near Clodgie Point orange bird\xe2\x80\x99s-foot occurs. The <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dune</a> grassland area behind East and West Porth is important for the very rare dwarf pansy (<i><a href="/wiki/Viola_(plant)" title="Viola (plant)">Viola kitaibeliana</a></i>). The summit of Great Hill has a small area of <a href="/wiki/Lowland_heath" title="Lowland heath">lowland heath</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Anon_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anon-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rare_plants">Rare plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%A4n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rare plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li>Shore dock (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rumex_rupestris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rumex rupestris (page does not exist)">Rumex rupestris</a></i>) first recorded here in 1984 and still there in 2005; a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="United Kingdom Biodiversity Action Plan">BAP</a> species and is one of the primary reasons why the Isles of Scilly is a <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> (SAC).<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SAC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAC-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Four-leaved allseed (<i><a href="/wiki/Polycarpon_tetraphyllum" title="Polycarpon tetraphyllum">Polycarpon tetraphyllum</a></i>) last recorded in 1990</li>\n<li>Saltwort (<i><a href="/wiki/Salsola_kali" title="Salsola kali">Salsola kali</a></i>) recorded in 2009, once found on most sandy beaches in Scilly and now a rare plant; previous record on Scilly was 2004 on <a href="/wiki/Samson,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Samson, Isles of Scilly">Samson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%A4n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The only mammals found on Te\xc3\xa4n are the Brown Rat (<i><a href="/wiki/Rattus_norvegicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Rattus norvegicus">Rattus norvegicus</a></i>) and the House Mouse (<i><a href="/wiki/Mus_musculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mus musculus">Mus musculus</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Rabbit (<i><a href="/wiki/Oryctolagus_cuniculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Oryctolagus cuniculus">Oryctolagus cuniculus</a></i>) may be extinct, and with no grazing animals on the island, plants that prefer a short sward such as orange bird\xe2\x80\x99s foot may become extinct. In 1850 J. W. North reported that Te\xc3\xa4n "is a preserve of white rabbits"!<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Scilly Shrew (<i><a href="/wiki/Crocidura_suaveolens" class="mw-redirect" title="Crocidura suaveolens">Crocidura suaveolens</a></i>) bones have been found in Roman or early medieval <a href="/wiki/Middens" class="mw-redirect" title="Middens">middens</a> and it was last recorded in 1964. There are no recent records.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_birds">Breeding birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%A4n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The SSSI citation lists five species of breeding birds on the island including the Puffin (<i><a href="/wiki/Fratercula_arctica" class="mw-redirect" title="Fratercula arctica">Fratercula arctica</a></i>). Other breeding seabirds are the Kittiwake (<i><a href="/wiki/Rissa_tridactyla" class="mw-redirect" title="Rissa tridactyla">Rissa tridactyla</a></i>), Herring Gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_argentatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus argentatus">Larus argentatus</a></i>), Lesser Black-backed Gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_fuscus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus fuscus">Larus fuscus</a></i>) and a small number of Greater Black-backed Gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_marinus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus marinus">Larus marinus</a></i>). The seabird breeding colonies are in decline on the Isles of Scilly and in the years 2006\xe2\x80\x9309 the Kittiwake has failed to breed on all the islands bar one chick raised on St Agnes in 2009. Of the species listed above, none have been recorded as breeding on Te\xc3\xa4n in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-Hudson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A pair of Marsh Harrier (<i><a href="/wiki/Circus_aeruginosus" class="mw-redirect" title="Circus aeruginosus">Circus aeruginosus</a></i>) bred on Te\xc3\xa4n in 2008 with two juveniles seen.<sup id="cite_ref-Hudson_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Invertebrates">Invertebrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Te%C3%A4n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Invertebrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Te\xc3\xa4n was the site of ground breaking mark and re\xe2\x80\x93capture population studies of the Common Blue (<i><a href="/wiki/Polyommatus_icarus" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyommatus icarus">Polyommatus icarus</a></i>) butterfly by <a href="/wiki/Entomologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Entomologists">entomologists</a> <a href="/wiki/E._B._Ford" title="E. B. Ford">E. B. Ford</a> and Prof <a href="/w/index.php?title=W._H._Dowdeswell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="W. H. Dowdeswell (page does not exist)">W. H. Dowdeswell</a> who camped on the island from 26 August to 8 September 1938. They marked each insect with a dot of cellulose paint so that it was possible to tell the date of first capture and any subsequent recaptures. The Common Blue is not a migratory butterfly and no marked butterflies were captured on the west side of St Martin\xe2\x80\x99s, so additions to the Te\xc3\xa4n population were likely to be mainly emergences and losses due to death. It was noted that the normal form of the butterfly was found on St Mary\xe2\x80\x99s, Tresco and St Martin\xe2\x80\x99s whilst on Te\xc3\xa4n there is a separate <a href="/wiki/Race_(biology)" title="Race (biology)">race</a>, due to isolation. \n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Ford described them as "<i>The females obtained in the summer (I have no knowledge of the spring form) have an extensive scattering of pale silvery-blue scales, so that they are most unlike those found elsewhere, which are neither blackish or else marked with a violet shade. Moreover, the form from Te\xc3\xa4n is associated with a characteristic  variation on the under-side of the hind-wings, which affects both sexes; for in a large proportion of the specimens the two spots placed along the coastal margin are united, forming a short curved line, and other varieties in spotting are frequent. We seem to here a stage in the evolution of an independent sub-species</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> </p></blockquote>\n<p>Recent visits have not found the Common Blue to be significantly different on Te\xc3\xa4n so Ford\xe2\x80\x99s remarkable form no longer seems to exist; unusual female colour forms and aberrations may just occur more frequently on Scilly than elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>Red Barbed Ant (<i>Formica rufibarbis</i>)</b>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Formica_rufibarbis" title="Formica rufibarbis">Red Barbed Ant</a> has been described as \xe2\x80\xb3<i>...perhaps the rarest resident animal in mainland Britain</i>\xe2\x80\xb3 with only four nests in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> and extinct in Cornwall (last recorded in 1907). Found on St Martin\'s, the Eastern Isles and also Te\xc3\xa4n where it was recorded in 2008. It\'s favoured habitat is open heathland with plenty of bare ground. <a href="/wiki/Queen_ant" title="Queen ant">Queens</a> from St Martin\'s are captured and taken to Surrey to maintain those colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
