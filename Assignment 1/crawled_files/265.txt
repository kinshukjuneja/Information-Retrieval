title="Kingdom of Gwent">Gwent</a>, later combined into <a href="/wiki/Morgannwg" class="mw-redirect" title="Morgannwg">Morgannwg</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Wales in the High  Middle Ages</b> covers the 11th to 13th centuries in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Wales" title="History of Wales">Welsh history</a>. Beginning shortly before the <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Wales" title="Norman invasion of Wales">Norman invasion</a> of the 1060s and ending with the <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Wales by Edward I">Conquest of Wales by Edward I</a> between 1278 and 1283, it was a period of significant political, cultural and social change for the country. \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_first_millennium">End of the first millennium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_High_Middle_Ages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: End of the first millennium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>By the later centuries of the first millennium, according to Wendy Davies, a clearer pattern of development is seen, and the expansion and subsequent domination of the Welsh <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Kingdom of Gwynedd</a>, a province of north-west Wales, is fairly well-established. The aforementioned kingdoms of the south-east seem to have remained relatively isolated until the eleventh century (102).\n</p><p>Throughout this period, the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> exerted some influence over Wales, if only by settlement at times. In the sixth century, the Saxons appear to have attacked Wales; however, "relations between the English and the British of Wales were not entirely hostile" after these attacks (W. Davies, 113). For some 200 years starting in the seventh century, from the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>, there were sporadic raids and skirmishes in both Wales and England, perpetrated by both powers.\n</p><p>With the later establishment of a legitimate <a href="/wiki/English_monarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="English monarchy">English monarchy</a>, the southern Welsh kingdoms sought out <a href="/wiki/King_Alfred" class="mw-redirect" title="King Alfred">King Alfred</a>\xe2\x80\x99s protection against the kings of Gwynedd, and "they thus accepted Alfred\xe2\x80\x99s lordship \xe2\x80\xa6 in so doing" (114). Throughout the tenth century, Davies says, "ravagings went on," in Gwynedd, the south-east, in <a href="/wiki/Dyfed" title="Dyfed">Dyfed</a>, and often perpetrated by Mercian kings, all this despite courtly appearances of friendship.
