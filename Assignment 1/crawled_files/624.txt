<a href="/wiki/Non-departmental_public_body" title="Non-departmental public body">non-departmental public body</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" title="Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs</a>. It is responsible for ensuring that <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>\'s natural environment, including its land, <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>, freshwater and <a href="/wiki/Marine_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine environment">marine environments</a>, geology and soils, are protected and improved. It also has a responsibility to help people enjoy, understand and access the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a>.\n</p><p>Natural England focuses its activities and resources on four strategic outcomes:\n</p>\n<ul><li>a healthy natural environment</li>\n<li>enjoyment of the natural environment</li>\n<li>sustainable use of the natural environment</li>\n<li>a secure environmental future</li></ul>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles_and_responsibilities">Roles and responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Roles and responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Non-departmental_public_body" title="Non-departmental public body">non-departmental public body</a> (NDPB), Natural England is independent of government. However, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Environment,_Food_%26_Rural_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Environment, Food &amp; Rural Affairs">Secretary of State for Environment, Food &amp; Rural Affairs</a> has the legal power to issue guidance to Natural England on various matters,<sup id="cite_ref-NERC_Act_2006_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NERC_Act_2006-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> a constraint that was not placed on its predecessor NDPBs.\n</p><p>Its powers include defining <a href="/wiki/Ancient_woodland" title="Ancient woodland">ancient woodlands</a>, awarding grants, designating <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Sites of Special Scientific Interest</a>, managing certain <a href="/wiki/National_nature_reserve_(United_Kingdom)" title="National nature reserve (United Kingdom)">national nature reserves</a>, overseeing access to open country and other recreation rights, and enforcing the associated regulations. It is also responsible for the administration of numerous grant schemes and frameworks that finance the development and conservation of the natural environment, for example <a href="/wiki/Environmental_stewardship" title="Environmental stewardship">environmental stewardship</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_Stewardship_Scheme" title="Countryside Stewardship Scheme">Countryside Stewardship Scheme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_sensitive_area" title="Environmentally sensitive area">environmentally sensitive areas</a>, and the Access to Nature Scheme. It has been severely criticised recently for ignoring scientific data and granting extended badger cull licences to DEFRA.\n</p><p>Natural England\'s latest corporate plan sets out its goals and detailed objectives.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is responsible for the delivery of some of Defra\'s public service agreements (e.g. reversing the long-term decline in the number of farmland birds by 2020 and improving public access to the countryside).\n</p><p>Natural England takes its finance, human resources and estates services from the Defra Shared Services organisation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Information technology services are outsourced to IBM.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Natural England was established on 1 October 2006 by the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Environment_and_Rural_Communities_Act_2006" title="Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006">Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NERC_Act_2006_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NERC_Act_2006-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> which implemented the recommendations of a rural review by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Haskins,_Baron_Haskins_of_Skidby" class="mw-redirect" title="Christopher Haskins, Baron Haskins of Skidby">The Baron Haskins of Skidby</a>. It was formed by the amalgamation of three founder bodies:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Countryside_Agency" title="Countryside Agency">Countryside Agency</a>, the landscape, access and recreation elements</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/English_Nature" title="English Nature">English Nature</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rural_Development_Service" title="Rural Development Service">Rural Development Service</a>, the environmental land management functions of <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" title="Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs</a> (Defra)</li></ul>\n<p>It received the powers of the founder bodies.\n</p><p>Natural England joined the <a href="/wiki/10:10" title="10:10">10:10</a> project in 2009 in a bid to reduce its own carbon footprint. One year later they announced that they had reduced their carbon emissions (according to 10:10\'s criteria) by <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://maps.google.co.uk/maps/ms?ie=UTF8&amp;hl=en&amp;msa=0&amp;msid=200812294318285167943.00049c6673e031918c091&amp;ll=53.865486,-1.999512&amp;spn=8.792252,26.806641&amp;t=h&amp;z=6">13%</a>.\n</p><p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Doughty" title="Martin Doughty">Sir Martin Doughty</a>, the Chairman of Natural England, warned the Prime Minister of the potential danger of <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_crops" title="Genetically modified crops">genetically modified crops</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  However, in 2012, Poul Christensen, CBE, the next Chairman of Natural England, said that middle England should embrace new technologies like <a href="/wiki/GM_crops" class="mw-redirect" title="GM crops">GM crops</a> as long as there were adequate testing and safeguards.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_of_the_Natural_Environment">State of the Natural Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: State of the Natural Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In May 2008, Natural England published a report, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://publications.naturalengland.org.uk/publication/31043?category=118044"><i>State of the Natural Environment</i></a>, which brought together statistics and facts about England\'s environment. The report was intended to be used by environmental organisations as a benchmark and source for policy development. It complements reports on different topics produced by other organisations:\n</p>\n<ul><li>on environmental facts and figures, by the <a href="/wiki/Environment_Agency" title="Environment Agency">Environment Agency</a></li>\n<li>on heritage counts, by the <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a></li>\n<li>on the state of the UK\'s birds, by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a></li>\n<li>on the state of Britain\'s butterflies, by the <a href="/wiki/Butterfly_Conservation" title="Butterfly Conservation">Butterfly Conservation</a></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_exercise">Green exercise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Green exercise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Natural England funded eight pilot <a href="/wiki/Green_exercise" title="Green exercise">green exercise</a> projects through local regional partnerships. These projects increased levels of physical activity and people\'s connections to their local green spaces. However, it was not clear whether these projects really changed people\'s long-term attitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_infrastructure">Green infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Green infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Natural England is promoting the concept of <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">green infrastructure</a> as a way to deliver a wide range of benefits for people and the natural environment together. It believes that green infrastructure should be delivered via the spatial planning system, as an integral part of new development everywhere, and also form a key part of proposals to regenerate existing urban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Natural England is working with partners in the growth areas, growth points and proposed eco-towns to prepare and implement green infrastructure strategies and demonstrate good practice on the ground.\n</p><p>Natural England is one of the steering group partners of <a href="/wiki/Neighbourhoods_Green" title="Neighbourhoods Green">Neighbourhoods Green</a>, a green Infrastructure partnership initiative which works with social landlords and housing associations to highlight the importance of, and raise the overall quality of, design and management for open and green space in social housing.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_challenge">Legal challenge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legal challenge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Natural England was challenged in High Court in 2006 by Peter Boggis, a pensioner who protected his house from erosion. Natural England claimed that as the site of Boggis\'s house, at <a href="/wiki/Easton_Bavents" title="Easton Bavents">Easton Bavents</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Southwold" title="Southwold">Southwold</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> coast was a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI), the protection went against the scientific community\'s interests. Natural England lost the case in 2009, when Mr. Justice Blair, the brother of the former Prime Minister, ruled that Mr. Boggis\' "human predicament" was more important than the site\'s SSSI status.
