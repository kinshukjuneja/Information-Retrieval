It is the last period of the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> era; the following <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> period belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> era. The concept of the Permian was introduced in 1841 by geologist Sir <a href="/wiki/Roderick_Murchison" title="Roderick Murchison">Roderick Murchison</a>, who named it after the city of <a href="/wiki/Perm" title="Perm">Perm</a>.\n</p><p>The Permian witnessed the diversification of the early <a href="/wiki/Amniote" title="Amniote">amniotes</a> into the ancestral groups of the <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lepidosauria" title="Lepidosauria">lepidosaurs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Archosaur" title="Archosaur">archosaurs</a>. The world at the time was dominated by two continents known as <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia_(continent)" title="Siberia (continent)">Siberia</a>, surrounded by a global ocean called <a href="/wiki/Panthalassa" title="Panthalassa">Panthalassa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous_rainforest_collapse" title="Carboniferous rainforest collapse">Carboniferous rainforest collapse</a> left behind vast regions of <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> within the continental interior.<sup id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010RainforestCollapse_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010RainforestCollapse-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amniote" title="Amniote">Amniotes</a>, who could better cope with these drier conditions, rose to dominance in place of their amphibian ancestors.\n</p><p>The Permian (along with the Paleozoic) ended with the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event">Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event</a>, the largest mass extinction in Earth\'s history, in which nearly 96% of marine species and 70% of terrestrial species died out.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It would take well into the Triassic for life to recover from this catastrophe.<sup id="cite_ref-kgs.ku.edu_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kgs.ku.edu-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Recovery from the Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event was protracted; on land, ecosystems took 30 million years to recover.<sup id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" This could have in part caused the widespread extinctions of marine species at the end of the period by severely reducing shallow coastal areas preferred by many marine organisms.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleogeography">Paleogeography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleogeography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png/230px-280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png" width="230" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png/345px-280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png/460px-280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png 2x" data-file-width="563" data-file-height="596" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:280_Ma_plate_tectonic_reconstruction.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Geography of the Permian world</div></div></div>\n<p>During the Permian, all the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s major landmasses were collected into a single supercontinent known as <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>. Pangaea straddled the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> and extended toward the poles, with a corresponding effect on ocean currents in the single great ocean ("<a href="/wiki/Panthalassa" title="Panthalassa">Panthalassa</a>", the "universal sea"), and the <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Tethys_Ocean" title="Paleo-Tethys Ocean">Paleo-Tethys Ocean</a>, a large ocean that was between Asia and Gondwana. The <a href="/wiki/Cimmeria_(continent)" title="Cimmeria (continent)">Cimmeria</a> continent <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rifted</a> away from <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> and drifted north to <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a>, causing the Paleo-Tethys Ocean to shrink. A new ocean was growing on its southern end, the <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethys Ocean</a>, an ocean that would dominate much of the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> era. Large continental landmass interiors experience climates with extreme variations of heat and cold ("<a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climate</a>") and <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> conditions with highly seasonal rainfall patterns. <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">Deserts</a> seem to have been widespread on Pangaea. Such dry conditions favored <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperms</a>, plants with seeds enclosed in a protective cover, over plants such as <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> that disperse <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a> in a wetter environment. The first modern trees (<a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">ginkgos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycads</a>) appeared in the Permian.\n</p><p>Three general areas are especially noted for their extensive Permian deposits&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> (where Perm itself is located), China, and the southwest of North America, including the <a href="/wiki/Red_Beds_of_Texas_and_Oklahoma" title="Red Beds of Texas and Oklahoma">Texas red beds.</a> The <a href="/wiki/Permian_Basin_(North_America)" title="Permian Basin (North America)">Permian Basin</a> in the U.S. states of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> is so named because it has one of the thickest deposits of Permian rocks in the world.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_biota">Marine biota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marine biota">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Permian marine deposits are rich in <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> <a href="/wiki/Mollusk" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brachiopod" title="Brachiopod">brachiopods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ucmp.berkeley.edu_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucmp.berkeley.edu-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Fossilized shells of two kinds of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> are widely used to identify Permian strata and correlate them between sites: <a href="/wiki/Fusulinid" class="mw-redirect" title="Fusulinid">fusulinids</a>, a kind of shelled amoeba-like <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protist</a> that is one of the <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminiferans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonite" class="mw-redirect" title="Ammonite">ammonoids</a>, shelled <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a> that are distant relatives of the modern <a href="/wiki/Nautilus" title="Nautilus">nautilus</a>. By the close of the Permian, <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">trilobites</a> and a host of other marine groups became extinct.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial_biota">Terrestrial biota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Terrestrial biota">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Terrestrial life in the Permian included diverse plants, <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, and various types of <a href="/wiki/Permian_tetrapods" class="mw-redirect" title="Permian tetrapods">tetrapods</a>. The period saw a massive desert covering the interior of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>. The warm zone spread in the northern hemisphere, where extensive dry desert appeared.<sup id="cite_ref-ucmp.berkeley.edu_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucmp.berkeley.edu-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The rocks formed at that time were stained red by iron oxides, the result of intense heating by the sun of a surface devoid of vegetation cover.  A number of older types of plants and animals died out or became marginal elements.\n</p><p>The Permian began with the Carboniferous flora still flourishing. About the middle of the Permian a major transition in vegetation began. The swamp-loving <a href="/wiki/Lycopod" class="mw-redirect" title="Lycopod">lycopod</a> trees of the Carboniferous, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lepidodendron" title="Lepidodendron">Lepidodendron</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sigillaria" title="Sigillaria">Sigillaria</a></i>, were progressively replaced in the continental interior by the more advanced <a href="/wiki/Seed_ferns" class="mw-redirect" title="Seed ferns">seed ferns</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Conifers" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifers">conifers</a>. At the close of the Permian, lycopod and <a href="/wiki/Equisetopsida" title="Equisetopsida">equisete</a> <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> reminiscent of Carboniferous flora survived only on a series of equatorial islands in the <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Tethys_Ocean" title="Paleo-Tethys Ocean">Paleo-Tethys Ocean</a> that later would become <a href="/wiki/South_China_(continent)" title="South China (continent)">South China</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Permian saw the radiation of many important conifer groups, including the ancestors of many present-day families. Rich forests were present in many areas, with a diverse mix of plant groups. The southern continent saw extensive seed fern forests of the <i><a href="/wiki/Glossopteris" title="Glossopteris">Glossopteris</a> </i> flora. Oxygen levels were probably high there. The <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">ginkgos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycads</a> also appeared during this period.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Insects">Insects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Insects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>From the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvanian_(geology)" title="Pennsylvanian (geology)">Pennsylvanian</a> subperiod of the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> period until well into the Permian, the most successful insects were primitive <a href="/wiki/Blattoptera" title="Blattoptera">relatives of cockroaches</a>. Six fast legs, four well-developed folding wings, fairly good eyes, long, well-developed antennae (olfactory), an omnivorous digestive system, a receptacle for storing sperm, a <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> that could support and protect, as well as a form of gizzard and efficient mouth parts, gave it formidable advantages over other herbivorous animals. About 90% of insects at the start of the Permian were cockroach-like insects (<a href="/wiki/Blattoptera" title="Blattoptera">"Blattopterans"</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Primitive forms of <a href="/wiki/Dragonflies" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragonflies">dragonflies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Odonata" title="Odonata">Odonata</a>) were the dominant aerial predators and probably dominated terrestrial insect predation as well. True Odonata appeared in the Permian,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and all are effectively <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_insects" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquatic insects">semi-aquatic insects</a> (aquatic immature stages, and terrestrial adults), as are all modern odonates. Their prototypes are the oldest winged fossils,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a>, and are different in several respects from the wings of other insects.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Fossils suggest they may have possessed many modern attributes even by the late <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>, and it is possible that they captured small vertebrates, for at least <a href="/wiki/Meganeuropsis" title="Meganeuropsis">one species</a> had a wing span of 71&#160;cm (28&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Several other insect groups appeared or flourished during the Permian, including the <a href="/wiki/Coleoptera" class="mw-redirect" title="Coleoptera">Coleoptera</a> (beetles) and <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">Hemiptera</a> (true bugs).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Synapsid_and_amphibian_fauna">Synapsid and amphibian fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Synapsid and amphibian fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Early Permian terrestrial faunas were dominated by <a href="/wiki/Pelycosaur" title="Pelycosaur">pelycosaurs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diadectidae" title="Diadectidae">diadectids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Huttenlocker,_A._K._2012._Pp._90_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huttenlocker,_A._K._2012._Pp._90-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> the middle Permian by primitive <a href="/wiki/Therapsids" class="mw-redirect" title="Therapsids">therapsids</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Dinocephalia" title="Dinocephalia">dinocephalia</a>, and the late Permian by more advanced therapsids such as <a href="/wiki/Gorgonopsia" title="Gorgonopsia">gorgonopsians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dicynodont" title="Dicynodont">dicynodonts</a>. Towards the very end of the Permian the first <a href="/wiki/Archosauriformes" title="Archosauriformes">archosaurs</a> appeared, a group that would give rise to the <a href="/wiki/Crurotarsans" class="mw-redirect" title="Crurotarsans">crurotarsans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">following period</a>. Also appearing at the end of the Permian were the first <a href="/wiki/Cynodont" title="Cynodont">cynodonts</a>, which would go on to evolve into <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> during the Triassic. Another group of therapsids, the <a href="/wiki/Therocephalia" title="Therocephalia">therocephalians</a> (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lycosuchus" title="Lycosuchus">Lycosuchus</a></i>), arose in the Middle Permian.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> There were no aerial vertebrates (with the exception of gliding reptiles, the <a href="/wiki/Avicephala" title="Avicephala">avicephalans</a>).\n</p><p>The Permian period saw the development of a fully terrestrial fauna and the appearance of the first <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">large</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a>. It was the high tide of the <a href="/wiki/Anapsid" title="Anapsid">anapsids</a> in the form of the massive <a href="/wiki/Pareiasaur" title="Pareiasaur">Pareiasaurs</a> and host of smaller, generally lizard-like groups. A group of small reptiles, the <a href="/wiki/Diapsids" class="mw-redirect" title="Diapsids">diapsids</a>, started to abound. These were the ancestors to most modern reptiles and the ruling dinosaurs as well as pterosaurs and crocodiles.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Synapsid" title="Synapsid">synapsid</a>, early ancestors to mammals, also thrived at this time. Synapsids included some large members such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dimetrodon" title="Dimetrodon">Dimetrodon</a></i>. The special adaptations of reptiles enabled them to flourish in the drier climate of the Permian and they grew to dominate the vertebrates.<sup id="cite_ref-Huttenlocker,_A._K._2012._Pp._90_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huttenlocker,_A._K._2012._Pp._90-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Permian amphibians consisted of <a href="/wiki/Temnospondyli" title="Temnospondyli">temnospondyli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lepospondyli" title="Lepospondyli">lepospondyli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Batrachosauria" title="Batrachosauria">batrachosaurs</a>.\n</p>\n<center>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px"><div style="width: 155px">\n\t\t\t<div Late Permian, North European Russia (Northern Dvina)\n</p>\n\t\t\t</div>\n\t\t</div></li>\n</ul>\n</center>\n<h2><span id="Permian.E2.80.93Triassic_extinction_event"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic_extinction_event">Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Extinction_Intensity.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Extinction_Intensity.svg/300px-Extinction_Intensity.svg.png" width="300" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Extinction_Intensity.svg/450px-Extinction_Intensity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Extinction_Intensity.svg/600px-Extinction_Intensity.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="577" data-file-height="428" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Extinction_Intensity.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event, labeled "End P" here, is the most significant extinction event in this plot for marine <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> which produce large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Fossils" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossils">fossils</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event">Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event</a></div>\n<p>The Permian ended with the most extensive <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction event</a> recorded in <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event">Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event</a>. 90% to 95% of marine species became <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a>, as well as 70% of all land organisms. It is also the only known mass extinction of insects.<sup id="cite_ref-kgs.ku.edu_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kgs.ku.edu-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  Recovery from the Permian-Triassic extinction event was protracted; on land, ecosystems took 30 million years to recover.<sup id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">Trilobites</a>, which had thrived since <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> times, finally became extinct before the end of the Permian. <a href="/wiki/Nautilus" title="Nautilus">Nautiluses</a>, a species of cephalopods, surprisingly survived this occurrence.\n</p><p>There is evidence that magma, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalt</a>, poured onto the surface in what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Traps" title="Siberian Traps">Siberian Traps</a>, for thousands of years, contributing to the environmental stress that led to mass extinction. The reduced coastal habitat and highly increased aridity probably also contributed. Based on the amount of lava estimated to have been produced during this period, the worst-case scenario is the release of enough carbon dioxide from the eruptions to raise world temperatures five degrees Celsius.<sup id="cite_ref-palaeos.com_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-palaeos.com-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another hypothesis involves ocean venting of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> gas. Portions of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep ocean</a> will periodically lose all of its dissolved oxygen allowing bacteria that live without oxygen to flourish and produce hydrogen sulfide gas. If enough hydrogen sulfide accumulates in an <a href="/wiki/Anoxic_event" title="Anoxic event">anoxic zone</a>, the gas can rise into the atmosphere. Oxidizing gases in the atmosphere would destroy the toxic gas, but the hydrogen sulfide would soon consume all of the atmospheric gas available. Hydrogen sulfide levels might have increased dramatically over a few hundred years. Models of such an event indicate that the gas would destroy <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> in the upper atmosphere allowing <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation to kill off species that had survived the toxic gas.<sup id="cite_ref-Kump_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kump-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#Life_adapted_to_hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">There are species</a> that can metabolize hydrogen sulfide.\n</p><p>Another hypothesis builds on the flood basalt eruption theory. An increase in temperature of five degrees Celsius would not be enough to explain the death of 95% of life. But such warming could slowly raise ocean temperatures until <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">frozen methane reservoirs</a> below the ocean floor near coastlines melted, expelling enough methane (among the most potent <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>) into the atmosphere to raise world temperatures an additional five degrees Celsius. The frozen methane hypothesis helps explain the increase in carbon-12 levels found midway in the Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic boundary layer. It also helps explain why the first phase of the layer\'s extinctions was land-based, the second was marine-based (and starting right after the increase in C-12 levels), and the third land-based again.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An even more speculative hypothesis is that intense radiation from a nearby <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> was responsible for the extinctions.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It has been hypothesised that huge <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorite</a> <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact crater</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wilkes_Land_crater" title="Wilkes Land crater">Wilkes Land crater</a>) with a diameter of around 500 kilometers in Antarctica represents an impact event that may be related to the extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-big_bang_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-big_bang-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  The crater is located at a depth of 1.6 kilometers beneath the ice of Wilkes Land in eastern Antarctica. The scientists speculate that this impact may have caused the Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event, although its age is bracketed only between 100 million and 500 million years ago. They also speculate that it may have contributed in some way to the separation of Australia from the Antarctic landmass, which were both part of a supercontinent called <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>. Levels of iridium and quartz fracturing in the Permian-Triassic layer do not approach those of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_boundary" title="Cretaceous\xe2\x80\x93Paleogene boundary">Cretaceous\xe2\x80\x93Paleogene boundary</a> layer. Given that a far greater proportion of species and individual organisms became extinct during the former, doubt is cast on the significance of a meteorite impact in creating the latter. Further doubt has been cast on this theory based on fossils in Greenland that show the extinction to have been gradual, lasting about eighty thousand years, with three distinct phases.<sup id="cite_ref-Shen2011_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shen2011-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many scientists argue that the Permian\xe2\x80\x93Triassic extinction event was caused by a combination of some or all of the hypotheses above and other factors; the formation of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> decreased the number of coastal habitats and may have contributed to the extinction of many <a href="/wiki/Clades" class="mw-redirect" title="Clades">clades</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
