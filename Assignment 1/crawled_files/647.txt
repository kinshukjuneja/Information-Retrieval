href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> in <a href="/wiki/Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)" title="Monument Park (Yankee Stadium)">Monument Park</a> at <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium" title="Yankee Stadium">Yankee Stadium</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> and its participating clubs have <a href="/wiki/Retired_number" title="Retired number">retired</a> various <a href="/wiki/Uniform_number_(Major_League_Baseball)" title="Uniform number (Major League Baseball)">uniform numbers</a> over the course of time, ensuring that those numbers are never worn again and thus will always be associated with particular players or managers of note. The use of numbers on uniforms to better identify one player from another, and hence to boost sales of <a href="/wiki/Baseball_scorekeeping#Traditional_scorekeeping" title="Baseball scorekeeping">scorecards</a>, was tried briefly by the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> of 1916, but this failed.  The first team to permanently adopt the practice was the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> of 1929. By 1932, all 16 major league clubs were issuing numbers, and by 1937, the leagues passed rules requiring it.\n</p><p>The Yankees\' original approach was to simply assign the numbers 1 through 8 to the regular starting lineup in their normal batting order. Hence, <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> wore number 3 and <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Lou Gehrig</a> number 4. The first major leaguer whose number was retired was Gehrig, in July 1939, following his retirement due to <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a>, which became known popularly as Lou Gehrig\'s Disease.\n</p><p>Since then, over 150 other people have had their numbers retired, some with more than one team. This includes managers and coaches, as Major League Baseball is the only one of the <a href="/wiki/Major_professional_sports_leagues_in_the_United_States_and_Canada" title="Major professional sports leagues in the United States and Canada">major North American professional leagues</a> in which the coaching staff wear the same uniforms as players. Three numbers have been retired in honor of people not directly involved on the playing field \xe2\x80\x93 all three for team executives. Some of the game\'s early stars, such as <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christy_Mathewson" title="Christy Mathewson">Christy Mathewson</a>, retired before numbers came into usage. Teams often celebrate their retired numbers and other honored people by hanging banners with the numbers and names. Early stars, as well as honored non-players, will often have numberless banners hanging along with the retired numbers. Because fewer and fewer players stay with one team long enough to warrant their number being retired, some players believe that getting their number retired is a greater honor than going into the <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Baseball Hall of Fame</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ron_Santo" title="Ron Santo">Ron Santo</a>, upon his number 10 being retired by the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> on the last day of the 2003 regular season, enthusiastically told the <a href="/wiki/Wrigley_Field" title="Wrigley Field">Wrigley Field</a> crowd as his #10 flag was hoisted, "This is <i>my</i> Hall of Fame!"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, Santo would be inducted into the Hall of Fame in July 2012, nearly two years after his death, after <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2012#Veterans_Committee" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2012">being voted in</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Committee" title="Veterans Committee">Veterans Committee</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It is very rare for a team to reissue a retired number, and usually requires a special circumstance, such as the person for whom the number was retired returning to the team in a player, coach or manager role.  <a href="/wiki/Harold_Baines" title="Harold Baines">Harold Baines</a> provides one example of this when he returned to the White Sox href="#cite_note-10">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In cases of franchise relocation, the handling of existing retired numbers is at the discretion of team management.  The team may decide to continue honoring the retired numbers (as did the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a>), or it may choose to make a "fresh start" and reissue the numbers (as the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a> have done).\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> returned <a href="/wiki/Willard_Hershberger" title="Willard Hershberger">Willard Hershberger</a>\'s number 5 to circulation two years after his death. href="/wiki/Hank_Aaron" title="Hank Aaron">Hank Aaron</a>\'s #44 was retired by both the Braves and Brewers.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Reggie_Jackson" title="Reggie Jackson">Reggie Jackson</a> had his Stengel\'s #37 was retired by both the Yankees and Mets. Anderson\'s #10 was retired by the Reds, and his #11 was retired by the Tigers.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_methods_of_recognition">Alternative methods of recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_retired_numbers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alternative methods of recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A number of teams have formal or informal policies of only retiring numbers of players inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame, although there is no league-wide uniformity and teams sometimes break their own guidelines.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-baggarly2015_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baggarly2015-22">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  As an alternative to retiring numbers, many teams have established other means of honoring former players, such as team-specific Halls of Fame (<a href="/wiki/Angels_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Angels Hall of Fame">Angels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Allen_Jr._Braves_Museum_and_Hall_of_Fame" title="Ivan Allen Jr. Braves Museum and Hall of Fame">Braves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Milwaukee Brewers Hall of Fame">Brewers</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals_Hall_of_Fame_Museum" title="St. Louis Cardinals Hall of Fame Museum">Cardinals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland Indians Hall of Fame">Indians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners_Hall_of_Fame" title="Seattle Mariners Hall of Fame">Mariners</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets_Hall_of_Fame" title="New York Mets Hall of Fame">Mets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_Hall_of_Fame" title="Baltimore Orioles Hall of Fame">Orioles</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres_Hall_of_Fame" title="San Diego Padres Hall of Fame">Padres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas Rangers Hall of Fame">Rangers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="Cincinnati Reds Hall of Fame and Museum">Reds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox_Hall_of_Fame" title="Boston Red Sox Hall of Fame">Red Sox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Minnesota Twins Hall of Fame">Twins</a>) or Walls of Fame (<a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants_Wall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="San Francisco Giants Wall of Fame">Giants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Baseball_Wall_of_Fame" title="Philadelphia Baseball Wall of Fame">Phillies</a>), a Ring of Honor (<a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals#Ring_of_Honor" title="Washington Nationals">Nationals</a>) or Level of Excellence (<a href="/wiki/Blue_Jays_Level_of_Excellence" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue Jays Level of Excellence">Blue Jays</a>).  In addition, several teams have kept certain numbers out of circulation since a player left, but have not formally retired them.<sup id="cite_ref-goold2015_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goold2015-23">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-schulman2016_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schulman2016-24">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Only one team does not currently have any retired jersey numbers (other than Robinson\'s #42 retired in all of MLB).  The <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a> franchise had retired jerseys in honor of four players when known as the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a>, but un-retired them upon moving to Washington. The Nationals have established a "Ring of Honor" instead, which includes two of those Expos players, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Carter" title="Gary Carter">Gary Carter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andre_Dawson" title="Andre Dawson">Andre Dawson</a>; the Nationals\' first manager, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Robinson" title="Frank Robinson">Frank Robinson</a>;  players from the original <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="History of the Washington Senators (1901\xe2\x80\x9360)">Washington Senators</a> <a href="/wiki/Joe_Cronin" title="Joe Cronin">Joe Cronin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Ferrell" title="Rick Ferrell">Rick Ferrell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goose_Goslin" title="Goose Goslin">Goose Goslin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bucky_Harris" title="Bucky Harris">Bucky Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Johnson" title="Walter Johnson">Walter Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heinie_Manush" title="Heinie Manush">Heinie Manush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Rice" title="Sam Rice">Sam Rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harmon_Killebrew" title="Harmon Killebrew">Harmon Killebrew</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Early_Wynn" title="Early Wynn">Early Wynn</a>, as well as owner <a href="/wiki/Clark_Griffith" title="Clark Griffith">Clark Griffith</a>; players from the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Senators_(1961%E2%80%9371)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Senators (1961\xe2\x80\x9371)">second Senators franchise</a> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Howard_(baseball)" title="Frank Howard (baseball)">Frank Howard</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Homestead_Grays" title="Homestead Grays">Homestead Grays</a> players <a href="/wiki/Cool_Papa_Bell" title="Cool Papa Bell">Cool Papa Bell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Brown_(Negro_Leagues_pitcher)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ray Brown (Negro Leagues pitcher)">Ray Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Josh_Gibson" title="Josh Gibson">Josh Gibson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buck_Leonard" title="Buck Leonard">Buck Leonard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Posey" title="Cumberland Posey">Cumberland Posey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jud_Wilson" title="Jud Wilson">Jud Wilson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins#Retired_numbers" title="Miami Marlins">Miami Marlins</a> had previously retired #5 in honor of their first team president, the late <a href="/wiki/Carl_Barger" title="Carl Barger">Carl Barger</a>, but un-retired it entering the 2012 season. The Marlins did not have another retired number until September 26, 2016, when #16 was retired following the death of pitcher <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Fern%C3%A1ndez_(pitcher)" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Fern\xc3\xa1ndez (pitcher)">Jos\xc3\xa9 Fern\xc3\xa1ndez</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbers_kept_out_of_circulation">Numbers kept out of circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_retired_numbers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Numbers kept out of circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some teams have not formally retired certain numbers, but nonetheless kept them out of circulation. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>\' current policy is only to retire the numbers of longtime club members if they are inducted into the Hall of Fame; the lone exception was longtime Dodger player and coach <a href="/wiki/Jim_Gilliam" title="Jim Gilliam">Jim Gilliam</a>, whose #19 was retired when he died of a <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemorrhage" class="mw-redirect" title="Cerebral hemorrhage">cerebral hemorrhage</a> during the Dodgers\' 1978 postseason run.  Nevertheless, the Dodgers have informally kept <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Valenzuela" title="Fernando Valenzuela">Fernando Valenzuela</a>\'s #34 out of circulation since he last played for the team in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> have a similar policy, and have kept <a href="/wiki/Tim_Lincecum" title="Tim Lincecum">Tim Lincecum</a>\'s #55 out of circulation since he departed after the 2015 season, though it is not formally retired.<sup id="cite_ref-schulman2016_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schulman2016-24">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Milwaukee Brewers have not issued <a href="/wiki/Jim_Gantner" title="Jim Gantner">Jim Gantner</a>\xe2\x80\x99s #17 since his retirement. \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> have kept the following numbers out of circulation since the departure of a popular member of the team who wore it: #14 (<a href="/wiki/Lou_Piniella" title="Lou Piniella">Lou Piniella</a>), #19 (<a href="/wiki/Jay_Buhner" title="Jay Buhner">Jay Buhner</a>), and #51 (initially for <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a>, and later for <a href="/wiki/Ichiro_Suzuki" title="Ichiro Suzuki">Ichiro Suzuki</a> until his return in 2018). Similarly, no one had worn #33 for the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Rockies" title="Colorado Rockies">Colorado Rockies</a> since the departure of <a href="/wiki/Larry_Walker" title="Larry Walker">Larry Walker</a> in 2004 until Walker gave his approval for <a href="/wiki/Justin_Morneau" title="Justin Morneau">Justin Morneau</a>, a fellow <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> native who had idolized Walker as a boy, to wear it when Morneau was with the team from 2014 to 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On Opening Day of the 2012 season, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> unveiled a memorial "Kid 8" logo to honor the late <a href="/wiki/Gary_Carter" title="Gary Carter">Gary Carter</a>. Although no Met has worn the number 8 since Carter\'s election to the Hall of Fame, it is not retired. Following <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a>\' retirement in 1973, Mets owner <a href="/wiki/Joan_Whitney_Payson" title="Joan Whitney Payson">Joan Payson</a> promised him that the team would not reissue his #24; since then, the only Met to wear it has been Rickey Henderson from 1999 to 2000 (with the exception of a minor league call-up named <a href="/wiki/Kelvin_Torve" title="Kelvin Torve">Kelvin Torve</a>, who was inexplicably issued #24 in August 1990 before fan complaints prompted the team to change his number to #39 ten days later<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>).  \n</p><p>The New York Yankees have not re-issued <a href="/wiki/Paul_O%27Neill_(baseball)" title="Paul O&#39;Neill (baseball)">Paul O\'Neill</a>\'s #21 since he ended his career, except for a brief period in 2008 when <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Ensberg" title="Morgan Ensberg">Morgan Ensberg</a> and then <a href="/wiki/LaTroy_Hawkins" title="LaTroy Hawkins">LaTroy Hawkins</a> wore #21, before fan complaints led Hawkins to change his number to #22 in April.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The Baltimore Orioles have not re-issued numbers 7, 44, and 46 since the passing of <a href="/wiki/Cal_Ripken,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Cal Ripken, Sr.">Cal Ripken, Sr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elrod_Hendricks" title="Elrod Hendricks">Elrod Hendricks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Flanagan_(baseball)" title="Mike Flanagan (baseball)">Mike Flanagan</a> respectively. retirement.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_retired_by_Major_League_Baseball">Number retired by Major League Baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_retired_numbers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Number retired by Major League Baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Normally the individual clubs are responsible for retiring numbers. On April 15, 1997, Major League Baseball took the unusual move of retiring a number for all teams. On the 50th anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Robinson" title="Jackie Robinson">Jackie Robinson</a>\'s breaking the major league color barrier, his number 42 was retired throughout the majors, at the order of Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Bud_Selig" title="Bud Selig">Bud Selig</a>. This meant that no future player on any major league team could wear number 42, although players wearing #42 at the time were allowed to continue wearing it (<a href="/wiki/Mariano_Rivera" title="Mariano Rivera">Mariano Rivera</a> was the last active player to be grandfathered in, retiring after the 2013 season).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Starting in the 2007 season, the 60th anniversary of Robinson\'s Major League debut, players and coaches have all worn the number 42 as a tribute to Robinson on <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Robinson_Day" title="Jackie Robinson Day">Jackie Robinson Day</a>, April 15.\n</p><p>There is a lobby to have uniform #21 retired in all of baseball to honor <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Clemente" title="Roberto Clemente">Roberto Clemente</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
