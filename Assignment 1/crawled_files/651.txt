id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Besant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The son of a merchant, he was born at <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> and attended school at St Paul\'s, Southsea, Stockwell Grammar, London and <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King\'s College London</a>.\nIn 1855, he was admitted as a <a href="/wiki/Pensioner" title="Pensioner">pensioner</a> to <a href="/wiki/Christ%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Christ&#39;s College, Cambridge">Christ\'s College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, where he graduated in 1859 as 18th <a href="/wiki/Wrangler_(University_of_Cambridge)" title="Wrangler (University of Cambridge)">wrangler</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> After a year as Mathematical Master at <a href="/wiki/Rossall_School" title="Rossall School">Rossall School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fleetwood" title="Fleetwood">Fleetwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, and a year at <a href="/wiki/Leamington_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Leamington College">Leamington College</a>, he spent six years as professor of mathematics at the Royal College, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>.  A decline in health compelled him to resign, and he returned to England and settled in London in 1867.  From 1868 to 1885 he held the position of Secretary to the <a href="/wiki/Palestine_Exploration_Fund" title="Palestine Exploration Fund">Palestine Exploration Fund</a>.  In 1871, he was admitted to <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Inn" title="Lincoln&#39;s Inn">Lincoln\'s Inn</a>.\n</p><p>In 1868 he published <i>Studies in French Poetry</i>. Three years later he began his collaboration with writer <a href="/wiki/James_Rice_(writer)" title="James Rice (writer)">James Rice</a>. Among their joint productions are <i>Ready-money Mortiboy</i> (1872), and <i>The Golden Butterfly</i> (1876), both, especially the latter, very successful. This association was ended by the death of Rice in 1882. Thereafter Besant continued to write voluminously by himself, his main novels being <i>All in a Garden Fair</i> (which <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a> credited in <i>Something of Myself</i> with inspiring him to leave India and make a career as a writer), <i>Dorothy Forster</i> (his own favorite), <i>Children of Gibeon</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/All_Sorts_and_Conditions_of_Men" title="All Sorts and Conditions of Men">All Sorts and Conditions of Men</a></i>. The two last belonged to a series in which he endeavored to arouse the public conscience to the hardship among the poorest classes of cities. In this crusade Besant had considerable success, the establishment of The People\'s Palace in the East of London being one result. In addition to his fiction, Besant wrote largely on the history and <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> of London. His plans for this topic were left unfinished: among his books on this subject is <i>London in the 18th Century</i>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Sir Walter Besant memorial near the Waterloo bridge.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg/220px-Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg/330px-Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg/440px-Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3424" data-file-height="3424" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Walter_Besant_memorial_near_the_Waterloo_bridge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>Besant was a <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">freemason</a>, joining the Lodge of Harmony in Mauritius in 1862. He became Master of Marquis of Dalhousie Lodge, London in 1873, having joined in 1869. He was one of the founders of the first Masonic research lodge, <a href="/wiki/Quatuor_Coronati_Lodge" title="Quatuor Coronati Lodge">Quatuor Coronati Lodge</a> No 2076, of which he was the first treasurer from 1886.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was also one of the founders<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and first chair<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Authors" title="Society of Authors">Society of Authors</a> in 1884. He was knighted in the <a href="/wiki/1895_Birthday_Honours" title="1895 Birthday Honours">1895 Birthday Honours</a>.\n</p><p>He was treasurer of the \'Atlantic Union\', an association which sought to improve social relations between Britons and Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  He died in <a href="/wiki/Frognal" title="Frognal">Frognal</a> in London on 9 June 1901, aged 64.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
