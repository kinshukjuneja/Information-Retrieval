the Roman Empire</b> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">protohistorical</a> period during which the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> interacted with the area that is now <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, which was known to them as "<a href="/wiki/Caledonia" title="Caledonia">Caledonia</a>". <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman legions</a> arrived around <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;71, <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">having conquered</a> the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_tribes_in_Britain_and_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic tribes in Britain and Ireland">Celtic tribes</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a>" (<a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>) over the preceding three decades. Aiming to annex all of the island of "<a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a>", Romans under <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Petillius_Cerialis" title="Quintus Petillius Cerialis">Q.&#160;Petilius Cerialis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gn.&#160;Julius Agricola</a> invaded the <a href="/wiki/Caledonians" title="Caledonians">Caledonians</a> in the 70s and 80s.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Agricola_(book)" title="Agricola (book)">An account</a> by Agricola\'s son-in-law <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> mentions a Roman victory at "<a href="/wiki/Mons_Graupius" class="mw-redirect" title="Mons Graupius">Mons Graupius</a>" which became the namesake of the <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Mountains" title="Grampian Mountains">Grampians</a> but has been questioned by modern scholarship. The Romans then seem to have repeated <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">an earlier Greek</a> circumnavigation of the island and received submission from local tribes, establishing <a href="/wiki/Limes" title="Limes">their border</a> of actual control first along the <a href="/wiki/Gask_Ridge" title="Gask Ridge">Gask Ridge</a> before withdrawing to a line south of the <a href="/wiki/Solway_Firth" title="Solway Firth">Solway Firth</a>. This line was fortified as <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a>. Several Roman commanders attempted to fully conquer lands north of this line, including a brief expansion that was fortified as the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Wall" title="Antonine Wall">Antonine Wall</a>. Despite grandiose claims made by <a href="/wiki/Description_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Description of Britain">an 18th-century forged manuscript</a>, however, it is now believed that the Romans at no point controlled even half of present-day Scotland and that Roman legions ceased to affect the area after around 211.\n</p><p>The history of the period is complex and not well-documented. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Valentia_(Roman_Britain)" title="Valentia (Roman Britain)">Valentia</a>, for instance, may have been the lands between the two Roman walls, or the territory around and south of Hadrian\'s Wall, or <a href="/wiki/Roman_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Wales">Roman Wales</a>. Romans held most of their Caledonian territory only a little over 40 years; they probably only held Scottish land for about 80 years, if at all. Some Scottish historians such as <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Moffat" title="Alistair Moffat">Alistair Moffat</a> maintain Roman influence was inconsequential.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scots</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>" proper would not emerge as unified ideas <a href="/wiki/Origins_of_the_Kingdom_of_Alba" title="Origins of the Kingdom of Alba">until centuries later</a>. In fact, the Roman Empire influenced every part of Scotland during the period: by the time of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_withdrawal_from_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman withdrawal from Britain">Roman withdrawal from Britain</a> around 410, the various <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Iron Age">Iron Age tribes native to the area</a> had united as, or fallen under the control of, the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, while the southern half of the country was overrun by tribes of <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romanized Britons</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Scoti" title="Scoti">Scoti</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Irish</a> raiders) who would give Scotland its English name, had begun to settle along the west coast. All three groups may have been involved in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Conspiracy" title="Great Conspiracy">Great Conspiracy</a> that overran Roman Britain in 367. The era saw the emergence of the earliest historical accounts of the natives. The most enduring legacies of Rome, however, were <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Christianity in Scotland">Christianity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_literature" title="Scottish literature">literacy</a>, both of which arrived indirectly via <a href="/wiki/Irish_missionaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish missionaries">Irish missionaries</a>.\n</p>\n<div of Scottish history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The dawn of Scottish history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Add_19391_19-20.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Add_19391_19-20.png/250px-Add_19391_19-20.png" width="250" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Add_19391_19-20.png/375px-Add_19391_19-20.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Add_19391_19-20.png/500px-Add_19391_19-20.png 2x" data-file-width="2512" data-file-height="1828" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Add_19391_19-20.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An early Greek map from Ptolemy\'s description of the British Isles (c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1300</span>), showing his rotation of Scotland to the right.</div></div></div>\n<p>Scotland had been inhabited for thousands of years before the Romans arrived. However, it is only during the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman period</a> that Scotland is recorded in writing.\n</p><p>The work <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Cosmos" class="mw-redirect" title="On the Cosmos">On the Cosmos</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Aristotle" title="Pseudo-Aristotle">Pseudo-Aristotle</a> mentions two "very large" islands called <a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a> (<a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Ierne_(placename)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ierne (placename)">Ierne</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LSJBrettanike_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSJBrettanike-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Greek explorer and geographer <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> visited Britain sometime between 322 and 285&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span> and may have circumnavigated the mainland, which he describes as being triangular in shape. In his work <i>On the Ocean</i>, he refers to the most northerly point as <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Orcas</i></i> (<a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The earliest written record of a formal connection between Rome and Scotland is the attendance of the "King of Orkney" who was one of 11 British kings who submitted to the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> at <a href="/wiki/Camulodunum" title="Camulodunum">Colchester</a> in <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;43 following the <a href="/wiki/Roman_invasion_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman invasion of Britain">invasion of southern Britain</a> three months earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The long distances and short period of time involved strongly suggest a prior connection between Rome and Orkney, although no evidence of this has been found and the contrast with later <a href="/wiki/Caledonia" title="Caledonia">Caledonian</a> resistance is striking.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Originals of <i>On the Ocean</i> do not survive, but copies are known to have existed in the 1st century so at the least a rudimentary knowledge of the geography of north Britain would have been available to Roman military intelligence.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a>, the Roman geographer, recorded in his <i>De Chorographia</i>, written around <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;43, that there were 30 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Orkney_islands" title="List of Orkney islands">Orkney islands</a> and seven <i>Haemodae</i> (possibly <a href="/wiki/List_of_Shetland_islands" title="List of Shetland islands">Shetland</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-B12_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B12-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There is certainly evidence of an Orcadian connection with Rome prior to <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;60 from pottery found at the  <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Gurness" title="Broch of Gurness">Broch of Gurness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>By the time of <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> (<abbr title="Died">d.</abbr>&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;79), Roman knowledge of the geography of Scotland had extended to the <i>Hebudes</i> (<a href="/wiki/The_Hebrides" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hebrides">The Hebrides</a>), <i>Dumna</i> (probably the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Forest" title="Caledonian Forest">Caledonian Forest</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Caledonians" title="Caledonians">Caledonians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-B12_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B12-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, possibly drawing on earlier sources of information as well as more contemporary accounts from the <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Agricolan</a> invasion, identified 18 tribes in Scotland in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_Geography" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemy&#39;s Geography">his <i>Geography</i></a>, but many of the names are obscure. His information becomes much less reliable in the north and west, suggesting early Roman knowledge of these area was confined to observations from the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-B12_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B12-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Famously, his co\xc3\xb6rdinates place most of Scotland north of Hadrian\'s Wall bent at a right angle, stretching due eastward from the rest of Britain.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a title="Glenelg, Highland">Glenelg</a></div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Iron Age">Scottish Iron Age</a></div>\n<p>Ptolemy\'s tribes located north of the Forth-Clyde isthmus include the <a href="/wiki/Cornovii_(Caithness)" title="Cornovii (Caithness)">Cornovii</a> in <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>, the Caereni, Smertae, Carnonacae, Decantae, Lugi and Creones also north of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Glen" title="Great Glen">Great Glen</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Taexali" title="Taexali">Taexali</a> in the north-east, the <a href="/wiki/Epidii" title="Epidii">Epidii</a> in <a href="/wiki/Argyll" title="Argyll">Argyll</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Venicones" title="Venicones">Venicones</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a>, the Caledonians in the central <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> and the Vacomagi centred near <a href="/wiki/Strathmore,_Angus_and_Perth_%26_Kinross" class="mw-redirect" title="Strathmore, Angus and Perth &amp; Kinross">Strathmore</a>. It is likely that all of these cultures spoke a form of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic language">Celtic language</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Pritennic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pritennic">Pritennic</a>. The occupants of southern Scotland were the <a href="/wiki/Damnonii" title="Damnonii">Damnonii</a> in the Clyde valley, the <a href="/wiki/Novantae" title="Novantae">Novantae</a> in Galloway, the <a href="/wiki/Selgovae" title="Selgovae">Selgovae</a> on the south coast and the <a href="/wiki/Votadini" title="Votadini">Votadini</a> to the east. These peoples may have spoken a form of <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic</a> language.\n</p><p>Despite the discovery of many hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> sites in Scotland there is still a great deal that remains to be explained about the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> life in the early Christian era. <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a> for this period is problematic and chronological sequences are poorly understood.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> For a variety of reasons much of the archaeological work to date in Scotland has concentrated on the islands of the west and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">north</a> and both excavations and analysis of societal structures on the mainland are more limited in scope.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The peoples of early Iron Age Scotland, particularly in the north and west, lived in substantial stone buildings called <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_roundhouse" title="Atlantic roundhouse">Atlantic roundhouses</a>. The remains of hundreds of these houses exist throughout the country, some merely piles of rubble, others with impressive towers and outbuildings. They date from about 800&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span> to <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;300 with the most imposing structures having been created around the 2nd&#160;century&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span>. The most massive constructions that date from this time are the circular broch towers. On average, the ruins only survive up to a few metres above ground level, although there are five extant examples of towers whose walls still exceed 6.5&#160;m (21&#160;ft) in height.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> There are at least 100 broch sites in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Despite extensive research, their purpose and the nature of the societies that created them are still a matter of debate.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In some parts of Iron Age Scotland, quite unlike almost all of recorded history right up to the present day, there does not seem to have been an <a href="/wiki/Social_hierarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="Social hierarchy">hierarchical elite</a>. Studies have shown that these stone roundhouses, with massively thick walls must have contained virtually the entire population of islands such as <a href="/wiki/Barra" title="Barra">Barra</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Uist" title="North Uist">North Uist</a>. Iron Age settlement patterns in Scotland are not homogenous, but in these places there is no sign of a privileged class living in large castles or forts, or of an elite priestly caste or of peasants with no access to the kind of accommodation enjoyed by the middle classes.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over 400 <a href="/wiki/Souterrain" title="Souterrain">souterrains</a> have been discovered in Scotland, many of them in the south-east, and although few have been dated those that have suggest a construction date in the 2nd or 3rd centuries. The purpose of these small underground structures is also obscure. They are usually found close to settlements (whose timber frames are much less well-preserved) and may have been for storing perishable agricultural products.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg/220px-TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg/330px-TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg/440px-TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3967" data-file-height="2540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TySkyeOldManOfStorr20030828r23f07.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"A gloomy journey amongst uninhabited islands"</div></div></div>\n<p>Scotland also has numerous <a href="/wiki/Vitrified_fort" title="Vitrified fort">vitrified forts</a> but again an accurate chronology has proven to be evasive. Extensive studies of such a fort at Finavon Hill near <a href="/wiki/Forfar" title="Forfar">Forfar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Angus,_Scotland" title="Angus, Scotland">Angus</a>, using a variety of techniques, suggest dates for the destruction of the site in either the last two centuries&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span> or the mid-1st millennium. The lack of Roman artifacts (common in local souterrain sites) suggests that many sites were abandoned before the arrival of the legions.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unlike the earlier <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Ages</a>, which have provided massive monuments to the dead, Iron Age burial sites in Scotland are rare, and a recent find at <a href="/wiki/Dunbar" title="Dunbar">Dunbar</a> may provide further insight into the culture of this period. A similar site of a warrior\'s grave at <a href="/wiki/Alloa" title="Alloa">Alloa</a> has been provisionally dated to <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;90\xe2\x80\x93130.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> A traveller called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_of_Tarsus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Demetrius of Tarsus (page does not exist)">Demetrius</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tarsus,_Mersin" title="Tarsus, Mersin">Tarsus</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> the tale of an expedition to the west coast in or shortly before <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;83. He stated that it was "a gloomy journey amongst uninhabited islands" but that he had visited one which was the retreat of holy men. He mentioned neither the <a href="/wiki/Druids" class="mw-redirect" title="Druids">druids</a> nor the name of <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">the island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_invasion_of_Caledonia">The invasion of Caledonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The invasion of Caledonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Caledonia" title="Caledonia">Caledonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caledonians" title="Caledonians">Caledonians</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg/200px-Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg" width="200" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg/300px-Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg/400px-Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1788" data-file-height="2102" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Agricola.Campaigns.80.84.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>The apparently cordial beginnings recorded in Colchester did not last. We know nothing of the foreign policies of the senior leaders in mainland Scotland in the 1st century, but by <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;71 the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_governors_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Roman governors of Britain">Roman governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Petillius_Cerialis" title="Quintus Petillius Cerialis">Quintus Petillius Cerialis</a> had launched an invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The Votadini, who occupied the south-east of Scotland, came under Roman sway at an early stage and Cerialis sent one <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">division</a> north through their territory to the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Firth of Forth</a>. The XXth Legion took a western route through <a href="/wiki/Annandale,_Dumfries_and_Galloway" class="mw-redirect" title="Annandale, Dumfries and Galloway">Annandale</a> in an attempt to encircle and isolate the Selgovae who occupied the central <a href="/wiki/Southern_Uplands" title="Southern Uplands">Southern Uplands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Early success tempted Cerialis further north and he began constructing a line of <a href="/wiki/Glenblocker_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Glenblocker fort">Glenblocker forts</a> to the north and west of the <a href="/wiki/Gask_Ridge" title="Gask Ridge">Gask Ridge</a> which marked a frontier between the <a href="/wiki/Venicones" title="Venicones">Venicones</a> to the south and the Caledonians to the north.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the summer of <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;78 <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gnaeus Julius Agricola</a> arrived in Britain to take up his appointment as the new governor. Two years later his legions constructed a substantial <a href="/wiki/Roman_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman fort">fort</a> at <a href="/wiki/Trimontium_(Newstead)" title="Trimontium (Newstead)">Trimontium</a> near <a href="/wiki/Melrose,_Scottish_Borders" title="Melrose, Scottish Borders">Melrose</a>. Excavations in the 20th century produced significant finds including the foundations of several successive structures, <a href="/wiki/Roman_coins" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman coins">Roman coins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>. Remains from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a> were also found, including a collection of Roman <a href="/wiki/Armour" title="Armour">armour</a> (with ornate cavalry parade helmets), and horse fittings (with bronze saddleplates and studded leather <a href="/wiki/Barding#Champron" title="Barding">chamfrons</a>). Agricola is said to have pushed his armies to the estuary of the "River Taus" (usually assumed to be the <a href="/wiki/River_Tay" title="River Tay">River Tay</a>) and established forts there, including a legionary fortress at <a href="/wiki/Inchtuthil" title="Inchtuthil">Inchtuthil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mons_Graupius">Mons Graupius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mons Graupius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg/100px-Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg" width="100" height="289" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg/150px-Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg/200px-Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg 2x" data-file-width="615" data-file-height="1777" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Statue_of_Agricola_at_Bath.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gnaeus Julius Agricola</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In the summer of <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;84 the Romans faced the massed armies of the Caledonians at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mons_Graupius" title="Battle of Mons Graupius">Battle of Mons Graupius</a>. Agricola, whose forces included a fleet, arrived at the site with <a href="/wiki/Light_infantry" title="Light infantry">light infantry</a> bolstered with British <a href="/wiki/Auxiliaries_(Roman_military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliaries (Roman military)">auxiliaries</a>. It is estimated that a total of 20,000 Romans faced 30,000 Caledonian <a href="/wiki/Warrior" title="Warrior">warriors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Agricola put his auxiliaries in the front line, keeping the legions in reserve, and relied on close-quarters fighting to make the Caledonians\' unpointed slashing swords useless. Even though the Caledonians were put to rout and therefore lost this battle, two thirds of their army managed to escape and hide in the Scottish Highlands or the "trackless wilds" as <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> called them. Battle casualties were estimated by Tacitus to be about 10,000 on the Caledonian side and roughly 360 on the Roman side. A number of authors have reckoned the battle to have occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Mountains" title="Grampian Mountains">Grampian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mounth" title="Mounth">Mounth</a> within sight of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>. In particular, Roy,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Surenne, Watt, Hogan and others have advanced notions that the site of the battle may have been <a href="/wiki/Kempstone_Hill" title="Kempstone Hill">Kempstone Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Megray_Hill" title="Megray Hill">Megray Hill</a> or other knolls near the <a href="/wiki/Raedykes" title="Raedykes">Raedykes</a> <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">Roman camp</a>. These points of high ground are proximate to the <a href="/wiki/Elsick_Mounth" title="Elsick Mounth">Elsick Mounth</a>, an ancient <a href="/wiki/Trackway" title="Trackway">trackway</a> used by Romans and Caledonians for military manoeuvres.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogan_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Other suggestions include the hill of <a href="/wiki/Bennachie" title="Bennachie">Bennachie</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire_(traditional)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aberdeenshire (traditional)">Aberdeenshire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gask_Ridge" title="Gask Ridge">Gask Ridge</a> not far from <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sutherland" title="Sutherland">Sutherland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolf_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolf-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> It has also been suggested that in the absence of any archaeological evidence and Tacitus\' low estimates of Roman casualties, that the battle was simply fabricated.<sup id="cite_ref-Henig_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henig-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calgacus">Calgacus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Calgacus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first resident of Scotland to appear in history by name was <a href="/wiki/Calgacus" title="Calgacus">Calgacus</a> ("the Swordsman"), a leader of the Caledonians at Mons Graupius, who is referred to by Tacitus in the <i>Agricola</i> as "the most distinguished for birth and valour among the chieftains".<sup id="cite_ref-t30_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t30-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Tacitus even invented a speech for him in advance of the battle in which he describes the Romans as:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Robbers of the world, having by their universal plunder exhausted the land, they rifle the deep. If the enemy be rich, they are rapacious; if he be poor, they lust for dominion; neither the east nor the west has been able to satisfy them. Alone among men they covet with equal eagerness poverty and riches. To robbery, slaughter, plunder, they give the lying name of empire; they make a solitude and call it peace.<sup id="cite_ref-t30_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t30-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Calgacus\' fate is unknown but, according to Tacitus, after the battle Agricola ordered the <a href="/wiki/Prefect" title="Prefect">prefect</a> of the fleet to sail around the north of Scotland to confirm that Britain was an island and to receive the surrender of the <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orcadians</a>. It was proclaimed that Agricola had finally subdued all the tribes of Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> However, the Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Cassius_Dio" title="Cassius Dio">Cassius Dio</a> reports that this circumnavigation resulted in <a href="/wiki/Titus" title="Titus">Titus</a> receiving his 15th acclamation as emperor in <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;79. This is five years before Mons Graupius is believed by most historians to have taken place.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG/160px-Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG" width="160" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG/240px-Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG/320px-Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1904" data-file-height="2433" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bridgeness_slab_detail.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> cavalryman trampling conquered <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, on <a href="/wiki/The_Bridgeness_Slab" class="mw-redirect" title="The Bridgeness Slab">The Bridgeness Slab</a>, a tablet found at <a href="/wiki/Bo%27ness" title="Bo&#39;ness">Bo\'ness</a> dated to around <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;142 and now in the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Scotland" title="National Museum of Scotland">National Museum of Scotland</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Marching camps may have been constructed along the southern shores of the <a href="/wiki/Moray_Firth" title="Moray Firth">Moray Firth</a>, although their existence is questioned.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolf_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolf-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The total size of the Roman garrison in Scotland during the <a href="/wiki/Flavian_dynasty" title="Flavian dynasty">Flavian</a> period of occupation is thought to have been some 25,000 troops, requiring 16\xe2\x80\x9319,000 tons of grain per annum.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the material to construct the forts was substantial, estimated at 1 million <a href="/wiki/Cubic_feet" class="mw-redirect" title="Cubic feet">cubic feet</a> (28,315&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of timber during the 1st century. Ten tons of buried nails were discovered at the Inchtuthil site, which may have had a garrison of up to 6,000 men and which itself consumed 30&#160;linear kilometres of wood for the walls alone, which would have used up 100&#160;hectares (247&#160;acres) of forest.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Soon after his announcement of victory, Agricola was recalled to Rome by <a href="/wiki/Domitian" title="Domitian">Domitian</a> and his post passed to an unknown successor, possibly  <a href="/wiki/Sallustius_Lucullus" title="Sallustius Lucullus">Sallustius Lucullus</a>. Agricola\'s successors were seemingly unable or unwilling to further subdue the far north. This inability to continue to hold the far north may be in part due to the limited military resources available to the Roman Proconsul after the recall of the Legio II Adiutrix from Britain, to support Domitian\'s war in Dacia. Despite his apparent successes, Agricola himself fell out of favour and it is possible that Domitian may have been informed of the fraudulence of his claims to have won a significant victory.<sup id="cite_ref-Henig_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henig-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The fortress at Inchtuthil was dismantled before its completion and the other fortifications of the Gask Ridge (erected to consolidate the Roman presence in Scotland in the aftermath of Mons Graupius) were abandoned within the space of a few years. It is possible that the costs of a drawn-out war outweighed any economic or political benefit and it was deemed more profitable to leave the Caledonians to themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> By <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;87 the occupation was limited to the Southern Uplands and by the end of the 1st century the northern limit of Roman expansion was a line drawn between the <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solway_Firth" title="Solway Firth">Solway Firth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elginhaugh" title="Elginhaugh">Elginhaugh</a> fort, in <a href="/wiki/Midlothian" title="Midlothian">Midlothian</a>, dates to about this period as may <a href="/wiki/Castle_Greg" title="Castle Greg">Castle Greg</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Lothian" title="West Lothian">West Lothian</a>, which was most likely used as a monitoring base for an east-west road running along the foot of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Pentland_Hills" title="Pentland Hills">Pentlands</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/River_Forth" title="River Forth">Forth</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Clyde Valley">Clyde Valley</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Presumably as a consequence of the Roman advance, various hill forts such as Dun Mor in Perthshire, which had been abandoned by the natives long ago, were re-occupied. Some new ones may even have been constructed in the northeast, such as Hill O\'Christ\'s Kirk in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlements_and_southern_brochs">Settlements and southern brochs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Settlements and southern brochs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EdinsHallBroch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/EdinsHallBroch.jpg/300px-EdinsHallBroch.jpg" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/EdinsHallBroch.jpg/450px-EdinsHallBroch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/EdinsHallBroch.jpg/600px-EdinsHallBroch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EdinsHallBroch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Edin%27s_Hall_Broch" title="Edin&#39;s Hall Broch">Edin\'s Hall Broch</a>, showing intramural chambers</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_Geography" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemy&#39;s Geography">Geography</a></i> identifies 19 "towns" from intelligence gathered during the Agricolan campaigns. No archaeological evidence of any truly urban places has been found from this time and the names may have indicated hill forts or temporary market and meeting places. Most of the names are obscure: <i>Devana</i> may be the modern <a href="/wiki/Banchory" title="Banchory">Banchory</a>; <i>Alauna</i> ("the rock") in the west is probably <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton_Rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Dumbarton Rock">Dumbarton Rock</a> and the place of the same name in the east Lowlands may be the site of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Castle" title="Edinburgh Castle">Edinburgh Castle</a>. <i>Lindon</i> may be <a href="/wiki/Balloch,_West_Dunbartonshire" title="Balloch, West Dunbartonshire">Balloch</a> on <a href="/wiki/Loch_Lomond" title="Loch Lomond">Loch Lomond</a> side.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are the remains of various broch towers in southern Scotland that appear to date from the period immediately prior to or following Agricola\'s invasion. They are about fifteen in number and are found in four locations: the Forth valley, close to the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Tay" title="Firth of Tay">Firth of Tay</a>, the far south-west and the eastern <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Borders</a>. Their existence so far from the main centres of broch-building is something of a mystery. The destruction of the Leckie broch may have come at the hands of the Roman invaders, yet like the nearby site of Fairy Knowe at <a href="/wiki/Buchlyvie" title="Buchlyvie">Buchlyvie</a> a substantial amount of both Roman and native artefacts have been recovered there. Both structures were built in the late 1st century and were evidently high-status buildings. The inhabitants raised sheep, cattle and pigs, and benefited from a range of wild game including <a href="/wiki/Red_Deer" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Deer">Red Deer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boar" class="mw-redirect" title="Boar">Wild Boar</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Edin%27s_Hall_Broch" title="Edin&#39;s Hall Broch">Edin\'s Hall Broch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berwickshire" title="Berwickshire">Berwickshire</a> is the best preserved southern broch and although the ruins are superficially similar to some of the larger Orcadian broch villages it is unlikely that the tower was ever more than a single story high. There is an absence of Roman artefacts at this site. Various theories for the existence of these structures have been proposed, including their construction by northern invaders following the withdrawal of Roman troops after the Agricolan advance, or by allies of Rome encouraged to emulate the impressive northern style in order to suppress native resistance, perhaps even the Orcadian chiefs whose positive relationship with Rome may have continued from the beginnings of Romano-British relations.<sup id="cite_ref-Arm_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arm-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Hadrian.27s_Wall"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hadrian\'s_Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hadrian&#039;s Wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-one_source" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="Question book-new.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>relies largely or entirely on a single <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">source</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire##" title="Talk:Scotland during the Roman Empire">talk page</a>. In his last year of office he hosted a visit to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">province</a> by the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a> that resulted in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Rigore Valli Aeli</i>, "the line along Hadrian\'s frontier").\n</p><p>This line of occupation of Britain was consolidated as one of the <i><a href="/wiki/Limes" title="Limes">limites</a></i> (defensible frontiers) of the empire by its construction. It is a stone <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortification</a> built across the width of what is now northern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The wall was 80 <a href="/wiki/Roman_mile" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman mile">Roman miles</a> (73.5 statute miles or 117 kilometres) long,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> its width and height dependent on the construction materials which were available nearby. East of the <a href="/wiki/River_Irthing" title="River Irthing">River Irthing</a> the wall was made from squared stone and measured 3&#160;metres (9.7&#160;ft) wide and 5\xe2\x80\x936 metres (16\xe2\x80\x9320&#160;ft) high, while west of the river the wall was originally made from turf and measured 6&#160;metres (20&#160;ft) wide and 3.5&#160;metres (11.5&#160;ft) high; it was subsequently rebuilt in stone. The wall was augmented by various ditches, <a href="/wiki/Berm" title="Berm">berms</a>, and forts.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg/200px-Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg" width="200" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg/300px-Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg/400px-Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1915" data-file-height="1809" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scotland.south.Ptolemy.map.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antonine_Wall">Antonine Wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Antonine Wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Wall" title="Antonine Wall">Antonine Wall</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Quintus_Lollius_Urbicus" title="Quintus Lollius Urbicus">Quintus Lollius Urbicus</a> was made governor of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> in 138, by the new Emperor <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">Antoninus Pius</a>. Urbicus was the son of a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libyan</a> landowner<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> and a native of <a href="/wiki/Numidia" title="Numidia">Numidia</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>). Prior to coming to Britain he served during the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Rebellion" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Rebellion">Jewish Rebellion</a> (132\xe2\x80\x9335), and then governing <a href="/wiki/Germania_Inferior" title="Germania Inferior">Germania Inferior</a>.\n</p><p>Antoninus Pius soon reversed the containment policy of his predecessor Hadrian, and Urbicus was ordered to begin the reconquest of <a href="/wiki/Lowland_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowland Scotland">Lowland Scotland</a> by moving north. Between 139 and 140 he rebuilt a fort at <a href="/wiki/Coria_(Corbridge)" title="Coria (Corbridge)">Corbridge</a> and by 142 or 143, commemorative coins were issued celebrating a victory in Britain. It is therefore likely that Urbicus led the reoccupation of southern Scotland c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;141</span>, probably using the <a href="/wiki/Legio_II_Augusta" title="Legio II Augusta">2nd Augustan Legion</a>. He evidently campaigned against several <a href="/wiki/British_tribes" class="mw-redirect" title="British tribes">British tribes</a> (possibly including factions of the northern Brigantes), certainly against the lowland tribes of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the Votadini and Selgovae of the Scottish Borders region, and the Damnonii of Strathclyde. His total force may have been about 16,500 men.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It seems likely that Urbicus planned his campaign of attack from Corbridge, advancing north and leaving garrison forts at High Rochester in Northumberland and possibly also at Trimontium as he struck towards the Firth of Forth. Having secured an overland supply route for military personnel and equipment along <a href="/wiki/Dere_Street" title="Dere Street">Dere Street</a>, Urbicus very likely set up a supply port at Carriden for the supply of grain and other foodstuffs before proceeding against the Damnonii.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bar_Hill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bar_Hill.jpg/220px-Bar_Hill.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bar_Hill.jpg/330px-Bar_Hill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bar_Hill.jpg/440px-Bar_Hill.jpg 2x" data-file-width="633" data-file-height="475" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bar_Hill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The course of the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Wall" title="Antonine Wall">Antonine Wall</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Bar_Hill_Fort" title="Bar Hill Fort">Bar Hill</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Success was swift and the construction of a new <i>limes</i> between the Firth of Forth and the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Firth of Clyde</a> commenced. Contingents from at least one British legion are known to have assisted in the erection of the new turf barrier, as evidenced by an inscription from the fort at <a href="/wiki/Old_Kilpatrick" title="Old Kilpatrick">Old Kilpatrick</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Wall" title="Antonine Wall">Antonine Wall</a>\'s western terminus. Today, the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sward" class="extiw" title="wikt:sward">sward</a>-covered wall is the remains of a defensive line made of <a href="/wiki/Sod" title="Sod">turf</a> circa 7&#160;metres (20&#160;ft) high, with nineteen forts. It was constructed after <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;139 and extended for 60&#160;km (37&#160;mi).\n</p><p>It was possibly after the defences were finished that Urbicus turned his attention upon the fourth lowland Scottish tribe, the Novantae who inhabited the Dumfries and Galloway peninsula. The main lowland tribes, sandwiched as they were between Hadrian\'s Wall of stone to the south and the new turf wall to the north, later formed a confederation against Roman rule, collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Maeatae" title="Maeatae">Maeatae</a>. The Antonine Wall had a variety of purposes. It provided a defensive line against the Caledonians. It cut off the Maeatae from their Caledonian allies and created a buffer zone north of Hadrian\'s Wall. It also facilitated troop movements between east and west, but its main purpose may not have been primarily military. It enabled Rome to control and tax trade and may have prevented potentially disloyal new subjects of Roman rule from communicating with their independent brethren to the north and coordinating revolts.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Urbicus achieved an impressive series of military successes, but like Agricola\'s they were short-lived. Having taken twelve years to build, the wall was overrun and abandoned soon after <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;160.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The destruction of some of the southern brochs may date to the Antonine advance, the hypothesis being that whether or not they had previously been symbols of Roman patronage they had now outlived their usefulness from a Roman point of view.<sup id="cite_ref-Arm_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arm-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="3rd_century">3rd century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 3rd century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Roman_invasion_of_Caledonia_208%E2%80%93210" title="Roman invasion of Caledonia 208\xe2\x80\x93210">Roman invasion of Caledonia 208\xe2\x80\x93210</a></div>\n<p>The Roman frontier became Hadrian\'s Wall again, although Roman incursions into Scotland continued. Initially outpost forts were occupied in the south-west and <a href="/wiki/Trimontium_(Newstead)" title="Trimontium (Newstead)">Trimontium</a> remained in use but they too were abandoned after the mid-180s.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mither_tap_summit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Mither_tap_summit.jpg/220px-Mither_tap_summit.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Mither_tap_summit.jpg/330px-Mither_tap_summit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Mither_tap_summit.jpg/440px-Mither_tap_summit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mither_tap_summit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rural <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>, looking from the heights of <a href="/wiki/Bennachie" title="Bennachie">Bennachie</a> towards the lower-lying land in which Roman camps were situated.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cramondinscription.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Cramondinscription.jpg/200px-Cramondinscription.jpg" width="200" height="343" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Cramondinscription.jpg/300px-Cramondinscription.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/Cramondinscription.jpg 2x" data-file-width="322" data-file-height="553" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cramondinscription.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The inscription on the Roman altar at <a href="/wiki/Cramond_Roman_Fort" title="Cramond Roman Fort">Cramond Roman Fort</a> dedicated to the mothers of Alaterva and of the fields.</div></div></div>\n<p>Roman troops, however, penetrated far into the north of modern Scotland several more times. Indeed, there is a greater density of Roman marching camps in Scotland than anywhere else in Europe, as a result of at least four major attempts to subdue the area. The Antonine Wall was occupied again for a brief period after <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;197.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The most <a href="/wiki/Roman_invasion_of_Caledonia_208%E2%80%93210" title="Roman invasion of Caledonia 208\xe2\x80\x93210">notable invasion</a> was in 209 when the emperor <a href="/wiki/Septimius_Severus" title="Septimius Severus">Septimius Severus</a>, claiming to be provoked by the belligerence of the Maeatae, campaigned against the Caledonian Confederacy. Severus invaded Caledonia with an army perhaps over 40,000 strong.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Dio_Cassius" class="mw-redirect" title="Dio Cassius">Dio Cassius</a>, he inflicted genocidal depredations on the natives and incurred the loss of 50,000 of his own men to the attrition of <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla</a> tactics, although it is likely that these figures are a significant exaggeration.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A string of forts was constructed in the north-east (some of which may date from the earlier Antonine campaign). These include camps associated with the Elsick Mounth, such as <a href="/wiki/Normandykes" title="Normandykes">Normandykes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ythan_Wells" title="Ythan Wells">Ythan Wells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deers_Den" title="Deers Den">Deers Den</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glenmailen" class="mw-redirect" title="Glenmailen">Glenmailen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogan_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> However, only two forts in Scotland, at <a href="/wiki/Cramond" title="Cramond">Cramond</a> and Carpow (in the Tay valley) are definitely known to have been permanently occupied during this incursion before the troops were withdrawn again to Hadrian\'s Wall circa 213.<sup id="cite_ref-H198_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H198-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> There is some evidence that these campaigns are coincident with the wholesale destruction and abandonment of souterrains in southern Scotland. This may have been due either to Roman military aggression or the collapse of local grain markets in the wake of Roman withdrawal.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 210, Severus\' campaigning had made significant gains, but his campaign was cut short when he fell fatally ill, dying at <a href="/wiki/Eboracum" title="Eboracum">Eboracum</a> in 211. Although his son <a href="/wiki/Caracalla" title="Caracalla">Caracalla</a> continued campaigning the following year, he soon settled for peace. The Romans never campaigned deep into Caledonia again: they soon withdrew south permanently to Hadrian\'s Wall.<sup id="cite_ref-H198_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H198-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was during the negotiations to purchase the truce necessary to secure the Roman retreat to the wall that the first recorded utterance, attributable with any reasonable degree of confidence, to a native of Scotland was made. When Julia Domna, wife of Septimius Severus, criticised the sexual morals of the Caledonian women, the wife (whose name is unknown) of the Caledonian chief Argentocoxos allegedly replied: "We fulfill the demands of nature in a much better way than do you Roman women; for we consort openly with the best men, whereas you let yourselves be debauched in secret by the vilest."<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Little is known about this alliance of Iron Age tribes, which may have been augmented by fugitives from Roman rule further south. The exact location of "Caledonia" is unknown, and the boundaries are unlikely to have been fixed.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The name itself is a Roman one, as used by Tacitus, Ptolemy, Pliny the Elder and <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Annaeus_Lucanus" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcus Annaeus Lucanus">Lucan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> but the name by which the Caledonians referred to themselves is unknown. It is likely that the prior to the Roman invasions, political control in the region was highly decentralised and no evidence has emerged of any specific Caledonian military or political leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-Woolis_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woolis-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Later excursions by the Romans were generally limited to the scouting expeditions in the buffer zone that developed between the walls, trading contacts, bribes to purchase truces from the natives, and eventually the spread of Christianity. The <a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Cosmography" title="Ravenna Cosmography">Ravenna Cosmography</a> utilises a 3rd- or 4th-century Roman map and identifies four <i>loci</i> (meeting places, possibly markets) in southern Scotland. <i>Locus Maponi</i> is possibly the modern Lochmabenstane near <a href="/wiki/Gretna,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Gretna, Scotland">Gretna</a> which continued to be used as a muster point well into the historic period. Two of the others indicate meeting places of the Damnonii and Selgovae, and the fourth, <i>Manavi</i> may be <a href="/wiki/Clackmannan" title="Clackmannan">Clackmannan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> From the time of Caracalla onwards, no further attempts were made to permanently occupy territory in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-H198_64-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H198-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Picts">Picts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Picts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pictish_stone_strathpeffer_eagle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Pictish_stone_strathpeffer_eagle.jpg/220px-Pictish_stone_strathpeffer_eagle.jpg" width="220" height="236" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/24/Pictish_stone_strathpeffer_eagle.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="302" data-file-height="324" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pictish_stone_strathpeffer_eagle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Clach_an_Tiompain" title="Clach an Tiompain">Clach an Tiompain</a>, a Pictish symbol stone in <a href="/wiki/Strathpeffer" title="Strathpeffer">Strathpeffer</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The intermittent Roman presence in Scotland coincided with the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Confederation" title="Confederation">confederation</a> of tribes who lived to the north of the Forth and Clyde from Roman times until the 10th century. They are often assumed to have been the descendants of the Caledonians though the evidence for this connection is circumstantial and the name by which the Picts called themselves is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> They are often said to have tattooed themselves, but evidence for this is limited. Naturalistic depictions of Pictish nobles, hunters and warriors, male and female, without obvious tattoos, are found on their <a href="/wiki/Pictish_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Pictish stones">monumental stones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaels</a> of Dalriada called the Picts <i><a href="/wiki/Cruithne_(people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cruithne (people)">Cruithne</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> and Irish poets portrayed their Pictish counterparts as very much like themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg/220px-Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg" width="220" height="302" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg/330px-Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg/440px-Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loch_Tay_Crannog.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Crannog" title="Crannog">crannog</a> on <a href="/wiki/Loch_Tay" title="Loch Tay">Loch Tay</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The Pictish relationship with Rome appears to have been less overtly hostile than their Caledonian predecessors, at least in the beginning. There were no more pitched battles and conflict was generally limited to raiding parties from both sides of the frontier until immediately prior to and after the Roman retreat from Britannia.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Their apparent success in holding back Roman forces cannot be explained solely with reference to the remoteness of Caledonia or the difficulties of the terrain. In part it may have been due to the difficulties encountered in subjugating a population that did not conform to the strictures of local governance that Roman power usually depended on to operate through.<sup id="cite_ref-Woolis_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woolis-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The technology of everyday life is not well recorded, but archaeological evidence shows it to have been similar to that in Ireland and Anglo-Saxon England. Recently evidence has been found of <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">watermills</a> in Pictland and <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kilns</a> were used for drying kernels of wheat or barley, not otherwise easy in the changeable, temperate climate.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Although constructed in earlier times, brochs, roundhouses and <a href="/wiki/Crannogs" class="mw-redirect" title="Crannogs">crannogs</a> remained in use into and beyond the Pictish period.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Elsewhere in Scotland <a href="/wiki/Wheelhouse_(archaeology)" title="Wheelhouse (archaeology)">wheelhouses</a> were constructed, probably for ritualistic purposes, in the west and north. Their geographical locations are highly restricted, which suggests that they may have been contained within a political or cultural frontier of some kind and the co-incidence of their arrival and departure being associated with the period of Roman influence in Scotland is a matter of ongoing debate. It is not known whether the culture that constructed them was "Pictish" as such although they would certainly have been known to the Picts.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As Rome\'s power waned, the Picts were emboldened. War bands raided south of Hadrian\'s Wall in earnest in 342, 360, and 365 and they participated with the <a href="/wiki/Attacotti" title="Attacotti">Attacotti</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Conspiracy" title="Great Conspiracy">Great Conspiracy</a> of 367. Rome fought back, mounting a campaign under <a href="/wiki/Comes_Britanniarum" title="Comes Britanniarum">Count</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Count_Theodosius" title="Count Theodosius">Theodosius</a> in 369 which re\xc3\xabstablished a province which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Valentia_(Roman_Britain)" title="Valentia (Roman Britain)">Valentia</a> in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">emperor</a>. Its location is unclear, but it is sometimes placed on or beyond Hadrian\'s Wall. Another campaign was mounted in 384, but both were short-lived successes. <a href="/wiki/Roman_withdrawal_from_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman withdrawal from Britain">Rome had fully withdrawn from Britain</a> by 410, never to return.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_study">Modern study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modern study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png/220px-Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png/330px-Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png/440px-Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="298" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_fortificationsinnorthernScotland2.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Cawdor_(Roman_Fort)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cawdor (Roman Fort)">fort at Cawdor</a> is located near <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>From the mid-18th century to the mid-19th century, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bertram" title="Charles Bertram">Charles Bertram</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Literary_forgery" title="Literary forgery">forged</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Description_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Description of Britain">Description of Britain</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">De Situ Britanni\xc3\xa6</i>) placed <a href="/wiki/Valentia_(Roman_Britain)" title="Valentia (Roman Britain)">Valentia</a> squarely between Hadrian\'s Wall and the Antonine Wall and even gave Rome a "short-lived" <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">province</a> named <a href="/wiki/Vespasiana" title="Vespasiana">Vespasiana</a> <i>beyond</i> the Antonine Wall in lowland Scotland. The work is now known to have been one of the most successful historical forgeries in history and it is no longer believed to contain any truthful independent content.\n</p><p>In 1984, a candidate for a <a href="/wiki/Roman_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman fort">Roman fort</a> was identified by <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">aerial photography</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cawdor_(Roman_Fort)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cawdor (Roman Fort)">Easter Galcantray</a>, south west of <a href="/wiki/Cawdor" title="Cawdor">Cawdor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The site was excavated between 1984 and 1988 and several features were identified which are supportive of this classification. If confirmed, it would be one of the most northerly known Roman forts in the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The possibility that the legions reached further north in Scotland is suggested by discoveries in Easter Ross. The sites of temporary camps have been proposed at <a href="/wiki/Portmahomack" title="Portmahomack">Portmahomack</a> in 1949, although this has not been confirmed,<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> In 1991 an investigation of <a href="/wiki/Muir_of_Tarradale" title="Muir of Tarradale">Tarradale</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Isle" title="Black Isle">Black Isle</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Beauly_Firth" title="Beauly Firth">Beauly Firth</a> concluded that "the site appears to conform to the morphology of a Roman camp or fort."<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical">Historical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Historical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG/220px-Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG/330px-Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG/440px-Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2945" data-file-height="1980" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_period_figures_by_Wm_Hole.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Notable figures from the Roman period in Scottish history as depicted by the Victorian artist <a href="/wiki/William_Hole_(artist)" title="William Hole (artist)">William Hole</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The military presence of Rome lasted for little more than 40 years for most of Scotland and only as much as 80 years in total anywhere. It\'s now generally considered that at no time was even half of Scotland\'s land mass under Roman control.<sup id="cite_ref-H198_64-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H198-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scotland has inherited two main features from the Roman period, although mostly indirectly: the use of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> for its languages and the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> as the predominant religion. Through Christianity, the <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin language</a> would become used by the natives of Scotland for the purposes of church and government for centuries more.\n</p><p>Roman influence assisted the spread of Christianity throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, but there is little evidence of a direct link between the Roman Empire and Christian missions north of Hadrian\'s Wall. Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ninian" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Ninian">Ninian</a> is credited as the first <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> active in Scotland. He is briefly mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> who states that around 397 he set up his base at <a href="/wiki/Whithorn" title="Whithorn">Whithorn</a> in the south-west of Scotland, building a stone church there, known as <a href="/wiki/Candida_Casa" title="Candida Casa">Candida Casa</a>. More recently it has been suggested that Ninian was the 6th-century missionary <a href="/wiki/Finnian_of_Moville" class="mw-redirect" title="Finnian of Moville">Finnian of Moville</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> but either way Roman influence on early Christianity in Scotland does not seem to have been significant.\n</p><p>Although little more than a series of relatively brief interludes of military occupation,<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Imperial Rome was ruthless and brutal in pursuit of its ends.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Genocide was a familiar part of its foreign policy and it is clear that the invasions and occupations cost thousands of lives. Alistair Moffat writes:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The reality is that the Romans came to what is now Scotland, they saw, they burned, killed, stole and occasionally conquered, and then they left a tremendous mess behind them, clearing away native settlements and covering good farmland with the remains of ditches, banks, roads, and other sorts of ancient military debris. Like most imperialists they arrived to make money, to gain political advantage and to exploit the resources of their colonies at virtually any price to the conquered. And remarkably, in Britain, in Scotland, we continue to admire them for it.<sup id="cite_ref-Moffat_2005_p._226_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moffat_2005_p._226-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>All the more surprising given that the <a href="/wiki/Vindolanda_tablets" title="Vindolanda tablets">Vindolanda tablets</a><sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> show that the Roman nickname for the north British locals was <i>Brittunculi</i> meaning "nasty little Britons".<sup id="cite_ref-Moffat_2005_p._226_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moffat_2005_p._226-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Similarly, William Hanson concludes that:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>For many years it has been almost axiomatic in studies of the period that the Roman conquest must have had some major medium or long-term impact on Scotland. On present evidence that cannot be substantiated either in terms of environment, economy, or, indeed, society. The impact appears to have been very limited. The general picture remains one of broad continuity, not of disruption.... The Roman presence in Scotland was little more than a series of brief interludes within a longer continuum of indigenous development."<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>The Romans\' part in the clearances of the once extensive Caledonian forest remains a matter of debate.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> That these forests were once considerably more extensive than they are now is not in dispute, but the timing and causes of the reduction are. The 16th-century writer <a href="/wiki/Hector_Boece" title="Hector Boece">Hector Boece</a> believed that the woods in Roman times stretched north from Stirling into <a href="/wiki/Atholl" title="Atholl">Atholl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lochaber" title="Lochaber">Lochaber</a> and was inhabited by white bulls with "crisp and curland mane, like feirs lionis".<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> Later historians such as <a href="/wiki/P._F._Tytler" class="mw-redirect" title="P. F. Tytler">P. F. Tytler</a> and <a href="/wiki/W._F._Skene" class="mw-redirect" title="W. F. Skene">W. F. Skene</a> followed suit as did the 20th-century naturalist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Fraser_Darling" title="Frank Fraser Darling">Frank Fraser Darling</a>. Modern techniques, including <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">palynology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a> suggest a more complex picture. Changing post-glacial climates may have allowed for a maximum forest cover between 4000 and 3000&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span> and deforestation of the Southern uplands, caused both climatically and anthropogenically, was well underway by the time the legions arrived.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> Extensive analyses of Black Loch in <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a> suggest that arable land spread at the expense of forest from about 2000&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span> until the 1st-century Roman advance. Thereafter, there was re-growth of birch, oak and hazel for a period of five centuries, suggesting the invasions had a very negative impact on the native population.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> The situation outside the Roman-held areas is harder to assess, but the long-term influence of Rome may not have been substantial.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg/200px-Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg" width="200" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg/300px-Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg/400px-Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1794" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yr.Hen.Ogledd.550.650.Koch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>The archaeological legacy of Rome in Scotland is of interest, but sparse, especially in the north. Almost all the sites are essentially military in nature and include about 650&#160;km (400&#160;mi) of roads.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> Overall, it is hard to detect any direct connections between native architecture and settlement patterns and Roman influence.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> Elsewhere in Europe, new kingdoms and languages emerged from the remnants of the once-mighty Roman world. In Scotland, the Celtic Iron Age way of life, often troubled, but never extinguished by Rome, simply re-asserted itself. In the north the Picts continued to be the main power prior to the arrival and subsequent domination of the Scots of <a href="/wiki/Dalriada" class="mw-redirect" title="Dalriada">Dalriada</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Damnonii" title="Damnonii">Damnonii</a> eventually formed the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Strathclyde" title="Kingdom of Strathclyde">Kingdom of Strathclyde</a> based at <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton_Castle" title="Dumbarton Castle">Dumbarton Rock</a>. South of the Forth, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> speaking <a href="/wiki/Brython" class="mw-redirect" title="Brython">Brythonic</a> kingdoms of <i><a href="/wiki/Hen_Ogledd" title="Hen Ogledd">Yr Hen Ogledd</a></i> (English: "The Old North") flourished during the 5th\xe2\x80\x937th centuries.\n</p><p>The most enduring Roman legacy may be that created by Hadrian\'s Wall. Its line approximates the border between modern Scotland and England and it created a distinction between the northern third and southern two-thirds of the island of Great Britain that plays a part in modern political debate. This is probably coincidental however, as there is little to suggest its influence played an important role in the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Early_Middle_Ages" title="Scotland in the Early Middle Ages">early Medieval period</a> after the fall of Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Legio_IX_Hispana" title="Legio IX Hispana">9th Spanish Legion</a> participated in the Roman invasion of Britain, suffering losses under Quintus Petillius Cerialis in the rebellion of <a href="/wiki/Boudica" title="Boudica">Boudica</a> of 61, and setting up a fortress in 71 that later became part of <a href="/wiki/Eburacum" class="mw-redirect" title="Eburacum">Eburacum</a>. Although some authors have claimed that the 9th Legion disappeared in 117,<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> there are extant records for it later than that year, and it was probably annihilated in the east of the Roman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> For a time it was believed, at least by some British historians, that the legion vanished during its conflicts in present-day Scotland.
