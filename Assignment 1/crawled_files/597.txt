corporation">statutory corporation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. \nThe Commission\xe2\x80\x99s primary objective was to appoint auditors to a range of local public bodies in England, set the standards for auditors and oversee their work. The Commission closed on 31 March 2015, with its functions being transferred to the voluntary, not-for-profit or private sector.\n</p><p>On 13 August 2010, it was leaked to the media, ahead of an official announcement, that the Commission was to be scrapped.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcac_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcac-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 2009-10 the Commission cost the central government \xc2\xa328m to run, with the remainder of its income coming from audit fees charged to local public bodies.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Audit Commission was established under the Local Government Finance Act 1982, to appoint auditors to all local authorities in England and Wales and it became operational on 1 April 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990 extended the remit of the Commission to cover health service bodies. Legislation covering the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s activities was consolidated into the Audit Commission Act 1998. In 1985-86 the commission led the investigation of the <a href="/wiki/Rate-capping_rebellion" title="Rate-capping rebellion">rate-capping rebellion</a> which resulted in 32 Lambeth councillors and 47 Liverpool councillors being surcharged and banned from office.\n</p><p>The Commission gained responsibility for auditing the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> in 1990, and fire and rescue services in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcac_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcac-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1996 the Commission began joint reviews of social services (with the Social Services Inspectorate of the Department of Health), and in 1997, reviews of local education authorities (LEAs) jointly with <a href="/wiki/OFSTED" class="mw-redirect" title="OFSTED">OFSTED</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelly466_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly466-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 1 April 2005 the Commission\'s remit in Wales transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Auditor_General_for_Wales" title="Auditor General for Wales">Auditor General for Wales</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Westminster_Council_.27homes-for-votes.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Westminster_Council_\'homes-for-votes\'">Westminster Council \'homes-for-votes\'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Westminster Council &#039;homes-for-votes&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gerrymandering" title="Gerrymandering">gerrymandering</a> scandal at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_City_Council" title="Westminster City Council">Westminster Council</a> was uncovered by the Audit Commission\'s District Auditor, John Magill, who found that between 1987 and 1989, council houses were sold at below market value to families likely to vote Conservative.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mr Magill found the former leader of the council, Dame <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Porter" title="Shirley Porter">Shirley Porter</a> and five other council officials \'jointly and severally\' liable for repaying \xc2\xa336.1 million to the council.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Mr Magill\'s verdict was upheld in the House of Lords in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Dame Shirley Porter eventually settled in 2004, paying \xc2\xa312.3 million to Westminster Council.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Fraud_Initiative">National Fraud Initiative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National Fraud Initiative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since 1996 the Audit Commission has run the National Fraud Initiative,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> a UK-wide anti-fraud programme. Between 1996 and 2013 it traced \xc2\xa31.17 billion in fraud, including \xc2\xa3215m in 2008-9,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> as more councils provided data and most recently \xc2\xa3203m in 2012-13.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Closure">Closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 13 August 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government">Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Pickles" title="Eric Pickles">Eric Pickles</a>, announced that the Commission was to be scrapped, with its functions being transferred to the voluntary, not-for-profit or private sector.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcac_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcac-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The government aimed to save \xc2\xa350m annually, with the Commission\'s function transferred to local council <a href="/wiki/Ombudsmen" class="mw-redirect" title="Ombudsmen">ombudsmen</a> and private accounting firms.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcac_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcac-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Accounting body <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Chartered_Certified_Accountants" title="Association of Chartered Certified Accountants">ACCA</a> expressed doubt that the private sector would match the commission\xe2\x80\x99s experience and consistency.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Financial_Reporting_Council" title="Financial Reporting Council">Financial Reporting Council</a> suggested to a House of Lords committee that Government should not sell the Audit Commission\'s practice to any of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Four_auditors" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Four auditors">Big Four auditors</a>, otherwise their dominance of the audit market would be further enhanced.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2012 a proposed employee-owned firm won only one of ten regional contracts, and was launched as a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Mazars" title="Mazars">Mazars</a>. <a href="/wiki/Grant_Thornton" class="mw-redirect" title="Grant Thornton">Grant Thornton</a> won the largest share, 4 contracts, and took on about 350 staff from the Commission. <a href="/wiki/KPMG" title="KPMG">KPMG</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_%26_Young" title="Ernst &amp; Young">Ernst &amp; Young</a> won 3 and 2 contracts respectively. The Commission estimated that audit fee savings at \'up to 40%\' will arise as a result of these arrangements.\n</p><p>A small number of staff were retained at the Audit Commission to monitor these contracts until the Commission was abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Legislation to abolish the Commission was included in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Audit_and_Accountability_Act_2014" title="Local Audit and Accountability Act 2014">Local Audit and Accountability Act 2014</a>, and the Commission was formally closed on 31 March 2015.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Functions of the Audit Commission included:\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Audit</b>: Auditors appointed by the Audit Commission were responsible for auditing <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">local government in England</a>, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="NHS Trust">National Health Service Trusts</a> and other local agencies in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, covering local government, health, housing, fire and rescue and community safety.</li>\n<li><b>Research</b>: The Audit Commission published studies that analysed and commented upon wide ranging social and financial issues in the UK.</li>\n<li><b>Data-matching</b>: The National Fraud Initiative compared data from a wide range of sources (including data from UKBA, Local Government, Central Government Departments and the NHS) to help participating organisations discover cases of fraud, overpayment and error.</li></ul>\n<p>The Audit Commission worked in partnership with, but operated independently of, a number of Government Departments including the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Communities and Local Government">Department for Communities and Local Government</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Health_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Health (UK)">Department of Health</a>.\n</p><p>Previously the Commission also produced performance assessments for councils, fire and rescue services, and housing organisations. In July 2009, it launched the Comprehensive Area Assessment, which assessed the effectiveness of local public services.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Between 1983 and 1998 the Commission was self-funding, operating purely on income from audit fees. In 1998 the central government began providing grants to the Commission due to its new responsibilities under "Best Value" legislation, and for the cost of setting up the Best Value and Housing Inspectorates.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelly466_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly466-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2009-10 the Commission had operating income of \xc2\xa3213.1m. 86% of this came from fees charged to bodies audited; just 13% (\xc2\xa328.0m) came from central government grants.<sup id="cite_ref-AC2010_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AC2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Around 70% of the Commission\'s income in 2009-10 came from the local government sector, with the remainder coming from the health sector.<sup id="cite_ref-AC2010_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AC2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Before the Coalition government announced further cuts, the Commission had planned to cut spending by \xc2\xa332.1m by March 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-AC2010_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AC2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\nThirty percent of the Commission\'s audits were carried out by five private audit firms.<sup id="cite_ref-AC2010_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AC2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chairman_and_Commissioners">Chairman and Commissioners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chairman and Commissioners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The governing board of the Audit Commission was made up of Commissioners appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Communities_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Communities and Local Government">Department of Communities and Local Government</a>. Previous chairman included Jeremy Newman, former Chief Executive of BDO International (2008-2011) and Michael O\'Higgins (2006-2012), who had for 10 years previously been managing partner of PA Consulting.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Controller_of_Audit_.2F_Chief_Executive"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Controller_of_Audit_/_Chief_Executive">Controller of Audit / Chief Executive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Controller of Audit / Chief Executive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Marcine Waterman took up the post of Controller of Audit in September 2012.\n</p><p>Previous incumbents include Eugene Sullivan, Steve Bundred (later of <a href="/wiki/Monitor_(NHS)" title="Monitor (NHS)">Monitor</a>), Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Banham" title="John Banham">John Banham</a> (later of the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_British_Industry" title="Confederation of British Industry">CBI</a>), <a href="/wiki/Howard_Davies_(economist)" title="Howard Davies (economist)">Sir Howard Davies</a> (later of the CBI), <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a>, FSA (<a href="/wiki/Financial_Services_Authority" title="Financial Services Authority">Financial Services Authority</a>) and LSE (<a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Sir_Andrew_Foster" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Andrew Foster">Sir Andrew Foster</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_controversy">Criticism and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Audit_Commission_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticism and controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>The Commission had been criticised for its methods, particularly how it rated councils and health organisations. Liberal Democrat MP <a href="/wiki/Vince_Cable" title="Vince Cable">Vince Cable</a> described the process organisations go through to earn stars as "disrespectful and utterly perverse".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Occupational psychologist <a href="/wiki/John_Seddon" title="John Seddon">John Seddon</a> called for the Commission to be scrapped,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> which led to what <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> described as a "caustic personal attack" on Seddon from the Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In 2009, the Commission caused controversy when it published a report<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> into the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%932011_Icelandic_financial_crisis" title="2008\xe2\x80\x932011 Icelandic financial crisis">2008\xe2\x80\x932011 Icelandic financial crisis</a> which accused seven local authorities of acting negligently by depositing \xc2\xa333 million into Icelandic banks a few days before they collapsed in October 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Audit Commission had already stated that it too had deposited \xc2\xa310 million in Icelandic banks in the months leading up to the collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In 2010, Eric Pickles and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Neill" title="Bob Neill">Bob Neill</a> accused the Commission of "shocking excess" in its expenditure, such as hiring external venues for staff training. Michael O\'Higgins, the Commission\'s chairman, defended the expenditure on BBC Radio 4\'s <a href="/wiki/Today_(BBC_Radio_4)" title="Today (BBC Radio 4)"><i>Today</i> programme</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>One of the features of the criticism of the Audit Commission was an apparent campaign carried out in the press. On 3 November, newspapers carried articles about alleged extravagant spending by the Audit Commission after its abolition was announced.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
