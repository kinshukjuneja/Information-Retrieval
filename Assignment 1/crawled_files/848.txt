(October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> sliver of the ancient continent of <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> (which later formed the bulk of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>). During the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> period the crustal region which became Scotland formed part of the continental shelf of Laurentia, then still south of the equator. Laurentia was separated from the continent of <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a> (which later became <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_region" title="Baltic region">Baltic region</a>) by the diminishing <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Ocean" title="Iapetus Ocean">Iapetus Ocean</a>. The two ancient continents moved toward one another through the Cambrian and <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> periods, with <a href="/wiki/Tectonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> folding during the <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> pushing the first Scottish land above water. The final collision occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> period, with the Scottish segment of the Laurentian plate smashing into <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a> (which contained what is now most of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>), a motile subcontinent which had previously joined with Baltica. This impact threw up a massive chain of mountains (at least as tall as the present-day <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>) and saw the formation of the granitic <a href="/wiki/West_Highland" class="mw-redirect" title="West Highland">West Highland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Mountains" title="Grampian Mountains">Grampian</a> mountain chains and (through the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>) a period of volcanic activity in central and eastern Scotland. During the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> periods, with the <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Ocean" title="Iapetus Ocean">Iapetus Ocean</a> entirely closed, Scotland lay near the centre of the <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaean</a> supercontinent. At the start of the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a>, a constructive plate boundary (at which tectonic plates move apart) became active between Laurentia and Eurasia, pushing the two apart (and parting Scotland from Laurentia). This recession opened the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> for the first time, and the consequent <a href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Subduction zone">subduction zone</a> at the western plate margin led to a renewed period of <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>, this time on Scotland\'s west coast, producing fresh mountains on <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Skye" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Skye">Skye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jura,_Scotland" title="Jura, Scotland">Jura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Mull" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Mull">Mull</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B9m" title="R\xc3\xb9m">R\xc3\xb9m</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a>.\n</p><p>This tectonic activity produced the basis of Scotland\'s topography: ancient mountains in the North and South of the country, partially eroded by 400 million years of water and ice with a wide fertile valley between them, and a newer, wilder western terrain. With Scotland now in the northern temperate zone, it was subjected to numerous glaciations in the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> periods, the ice sheets and their attendant glaciers carving the landscape into a typical postglacial one, overdeeping<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> river valleys into the characteristic U-shape and leaving the upland areas covered with glacial <a href="/wiki/Corrie_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corrie (geology)">corries</a> and dramatic pyramidal peaks. In lowland areas the ice deposited rich fields of fertile glacial till and eroded the softer material surrounding the extinct volcanoes (particularly the older Carboniferous ones), leaving many <a href="/wiki/Crag_and_tail" title="Crag and tail">crags</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_modern_humans">Before modern humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Before modern humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the last <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a>, around 130,000\xe2\x80\x9370,000 BC, there were times when climate in Europe was warmer than it is today, and after the <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a> came to prominence<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> there was another mild spell around 40,000 BC. Neanderthal sites have been found in the south of England from this era, though no traces or early modern humans have been found.\n</p><p>Glaciers then scoured their way across most of Britain, and it was only after the ice retreated about 15,000 years ago that Scotland again became habitable.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hunter-gatherers">Hunter-gatherers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hunter-gatherers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As the climate improved, <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">mesolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherers" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter-gatherers">hunter-gatherers</a> extended their range into Scotland. The earliest evidence to date is the <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> artefacts found at Howburn Farm, near <a href="/wiki/Elsrickle" title="Elsrickle">Elsrickle</a> in 2005. This is the first and so far the only evidence of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a> human habitation in Scotland, around 12,000 BC, which appears to fall between the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lomond_Stadial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lomond Stadial (page does not exist)">Lomond Stadial</a> periods when cold conditions returned relatively briefly.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An early settlement at <a href="/wiki/Cramond" title="Cramond">Cramond</a>, near what is today <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, has been dated to around <a href="/wiki/9th_millennium_BC" title="9th millennium BC">8500 BC</a>. Pits and stakeholes suggest a hunter-gatherer encampment, and <a href="/wiki/Microlith" title="Microlith">microlith</a> stone tools made at the site predate finds of similar style in England. Although no bones or shells had survived the acid soil, numerous carbonised hazelnut shells indicate cooking in a similar way to finds at other Mesolithic period sites, including the slightly earlier <a href="/wiki/Star_Carr" title="Star Carr">Star Carr</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Howick_house" title="Howick house">Howick house</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, dated to <a href="/wiki/8th_millennium_BC" title="8th millennium BC">7600 BC</a> ("Britain\'s oldest house"), where post holes indicate a very substantial construction, interpreted as a permanent residence for hunting people. This suggests that hunter-gatherers could also have settled down in Scotland.\n</p><p>Other sites on the east coast and at lochs and rivers, and large numbers of rock shelters and shell <a href="/wiki/Midden" title="Midden">middens</a> around the west coast and islands, build up a picture of highly mobile people, often using sites seasonally and having boats for fishing and for transporting stone tools from sites where suitable materials were found. Finds of flint tools on Ben Lawers and at Glen Dee (a mountain pass through the Cairngorms) show that these people were capable of travelling well inland across the hills.\n</p><p>At a rock shelter and shell midden at <a href="/wiki/Sand,_Applecross" title="Sand, Applecross">Sand, Applecross</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ross_and_Cromarty" title="Ross and Cromarty">Wester Ross</a> facing <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Skye" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Skye">Skye</a>, excavations have shown that around 7500 BC people had tools of bone, stone and antlers, were living off shellfish, fish, and deer using "pot-boiler" stones as a cooking method, were making beads from seashells, and had ochre pigment and used shellfish which can produce purple dye.\n</p><p><span id="Neolithic"></span>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Farmers_and_monument_builders">Farmers and monument builders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Farmers and monument builders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg/280px-Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg" width="280" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg/420px-Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg/560px-Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> dwellings at <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">Skara Brae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a></div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_British_Isles" title="Neolithic British Isles">Neolithic British Isles</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> farming brought permanent settlements. At <a href="/wiki/Balbridie" title="Balbridie">Balbridie</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a> crop markings were investigated, and ditches and post holes found, revealing a massive timber-framed building dating to about 3600 BC. An almost identical building, with evidence of pottery, was excavated at Claish near Stirling.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> On the islet of <a href="/wiki/Eilean_Domhnuill" class="mw-redirect" title="Eilean Domhnuill">Eilean Domhnuill</a>, in Loch Olabhat on <a href="/wiki/North_Uist" title="North Uist">North Uist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unstan_ware" title="Unstan ware">Unstan ware</a> pottery suggests a date of 3200\xe2\x80\x932800 BC for what may be the earliest <a href="/wiki/Crannog" title="Crannog">crannog</a>.\n</p><p>The remainder of this section focuses mainly on the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a>, where there is a Neolithic landscape rich in sites amazingly preserved by prevalent use of the local stone which appears on the shore ready-split into convenient building slabs. There are many other examples across the country, many under the care of <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a>.\n</p><p>At the wonderfully well preserved stone house at <a href="/wiki/Knap_of_Howar" title="Knap of Howar">Knap of Howar</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Papa_Westray" title="Papa Westray">Papa Westray</a> (occupied from 3500 BC to 3100 BC) the walls stand to a low eaves height, and the stone furniture is intact. Evidence from <a href="/wiki/Middens" class="mw-redirect" title="Middens">middens</a> shows that the inhabitants kept cattle, sheep and pigs, farmed barley and wheat and gathered shellfish, as well as fishing for species which must be caught from boats using lines. Finely made and decorated <a href="/wiki/Unstan_ware" title="Unstan ware">Unstan ware</a> pottery links the inhabitants to <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a> tombs nearby and to sites far afield, including <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balbrindi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Balbrindi (page does not exist)">Balbrindi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eilean_Domhnuill" class="mw-redirect" title="Eilean Domhnuill">Eilean Domhnuill</a>.\n</p><p>The houses at <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">Skara Brae</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mainland,_Orkney" title="Mainland, Orkney">Mainland</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a> are very similar, but are grouped into a village linked by low passageways. This settlement was occupied from about 3000 BC to 2500 BC. Pottery found here is of the <a href="/wiki/Grooved_ware_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Grooved ware people">grooved ware</a> style which is found across Britain as far away as <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>.\n</p><p>About 6 miles (10&#160;km) from Skara Brae, <a href="/wiki/Grooved_ware" title="Grooved ware">grooved ware</a> pottery was found at the <a href="/wiki/Standing_Stones_of_Stenness" title="Standing Stones of Stenness">Standing Stones of Stenness</a> (originally a circle) which lie centrally in a close group of three major monuments. <a href="/wiki/Maeshowe" title="Maeshowe">Maeshowe</a>, the finest example of the <a href="/wiki/Passage_grave" title="Passage grave">passage grave</a> type of <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a> (radiocarbon dated to before 2700 BC) lies just to the east. The <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Brodgar" title="Ring of Brodgar">Ring of Brodgar</a> circle of standing stones is across a bridge immediately to the north. This circle was one of the first to be analysed by Professor <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Thom" title="Alexander Thom">Alexander Thom</a> to establish the likely use of standing stones as astronomical observatories. Another <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> village has been found nearby at <a href="/wiki/Barnhouse_Settlement" title="Barnhouse Settlement">Barnhouse Settlement</a>, and the inference is that these farming people were the builders and users of these mysterious structures.\n</p><p>Like the standing stones at <a href="/wiki/Callanish" title="Callanish">Callanish</a> on <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Lewis" title="Isle of Lewis">Lewis</a> and other standing stones across Scotland, these monuments form part of the Europe-wide <a href="/wiki/Megalithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Megalithic">Megalithic</a> culture which also produced <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> and the stone rows at <a href="/wiki/Carnac" title="Carnac">Carnac</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>.\n</p><p>Further evidence can be found in <a href="/wiki/Kilmartin_Glen" title="Kilmartin Glen">Kilmartin Glen</a> with its Stone Circles, Standing Stones and Rock Art\n</p><p>The widespread connections of these people are shown by offerings imported from <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and left on the sacred hilltop at <a href="/wiki/Cairnpapple_Hill" title="Cairnpapple Hill">Cairnpapple Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Lothian" title="West Lothian">West Lothian</a>, as early as 3500 BC.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age">Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bronze_Age_burial_cist,_Cairnpapple,_West_Lothian.jpg" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Bronze_Age_burial_cist%2C_Cairnpapple%2C_West_Lothian.jpg/240px-Bronze_Age_burial_cist%2C_Cairnpapple%2C_West_Lothian.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bronze-age burial cist, Cairnpapple West Lothian</div></div></div>\n<p>The cairns and <a href="/wiki/Megalithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Megalithic">megalithic</a> monuments continued into the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, which saw metals as an additional material<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> rather than a replacement for flint. However, there was a decline in both the building of large new structures and the total area under cultivation from about 2500 B.C.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Clava_cairn" title="Clava cairn">Clava cairns</a> and standing stones near <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> show complex geometries and astronomical alignments, with smaller, perhaps individual, tombs instead of the communal Neolithic tombs.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">Mummies</a> dating from 1600\xe2\x80\x931300 BC have been discovered at <a href="/wiki/Cladh_Hallan" title="Cladh Hallan">Cladh Hallan</a> on <a href="/wiki/South_Uist" title="South Uist">South Uist</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hill_forts" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill forts">Hill forts</a> were introduced, such as <a href="/wiki/Eildon_Hill" title="Eildon Hill">Eildon Hill</a> near Melrose in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Scottish Borders</a>, which goes back to around 1000 BC and which accommodated several hundred houses on a fortified hilltop. Excavation at <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Castle" title="Edinburgh Castle">Edinburgh Castle</a> found late <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> material from about 850 BC.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age">Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">British Iron Age</a></div>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Scotland&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Evidence that at times occupants neglected the defences might suggest that symbolic power was as significant as warfare.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Traprain_Law,_East_Lothian.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Traprain_Law%2C_East_Lothian.jpg/220px-Traprain_Law%2C_East_Lothian.jpg" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Traprain_Law%2C_East_Lothian.jpg/330px-Traprain_Law%2C_East_Lothian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Traprain_Law%2C_East_Lothian.jpg/440px-Traprain_Law%2C_East_Lothian.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3948" data-file-height="2241" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Traprain_Law,_East_Lothian.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Traprain Law, East Lothian</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Brythonic</a> (or "<a href="/wiki/Pritennic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pritennic">Pritennic</a>") <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> culture and language spread into southern Scotland at some time after the 8th century BC, possibly through cultural contact rather than mass invasion, and systems of kingdoms developed.\n</p><p>Larger fortified settlements expanded, such as the <a href="/wiki/Votadini" title="Votadini">Votadini</a> stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Traprain_Law" title="Traprain Law">Traprain Law</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Lothian" title="East Lothian">East Lothian</a>, which was the size of a town. Huge numbers of small <a href="/wiki/Dun" title="Dun">duns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ringfort" title="Ringfort">ring forts</a> were built on any suitable <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">crag</a> or hillock. The spectacular <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">brochs</a> were built, most impressively the nearly complete broch at <a href="/wiki/Mousa" title="Mousa">Mousa</a> on <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Shetland Islands">Shetland</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Souterrain" title="Souterrain">Souterrain</a> underground passageways were constructed, though their purpose is obscure. Island settlements linked with land by a causeway, the <a href="/wiki/Crannog" title="Crannog">crannogs</a>, became common; it is thought that their function was defensive.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_-_guide_books">Access - guide books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Access - guide books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The government website, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.historic-scotland.gov.uk/">Historic Scotland</a>, lists many accessible sites and monuments including most of those mentioned above, and others are also freely accessible, making exploration of the distant past open to anyone with a guide book and map.
