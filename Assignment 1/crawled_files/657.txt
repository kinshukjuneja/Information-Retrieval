style="text-align:right;font-size:115%;padding-top: 0.6em;"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:History_of_the_British_Isles" template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Hiberno-Roman relations</b> refers to the relationships (mainly commercial and cultural) which existed between <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a>) and the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, which lasted from the 1st to the 5th century AD in Western Europe. Ireland was one of the few areas of western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> not conquered by <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Characteristics"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> class="toclevel-1 tocsection-10"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiberno-Roman_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> never annexed <a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name for Ireland) into the Roman Empire, but did exert influence on the island, although only a small amount of evidence of this has survived.\n</p><p>This influence was expressed in three characteristic ways: commercial; cultural and religious; and military.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial">Commercial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiberno-Roman_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commercial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The relationship between Rome and Hibernia was mostly commercial. In 1995, scholar Richard Warner wrote that after emperor <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a>\' invasion of southern <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>, the trade routes between the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean sea">Mediterranean sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britannia" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Britannia">Roman Britannia</a> encompassed even Hibernia.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The geographer <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, in his map of the 1st century AD, pinpointed the coastal settlements and tribes of Ireland, showing a knowledge that (it is suggested) only merchants could have achieved in that century. Additionally, there are many Roman archaeological objects (mainly <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_coins" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman coins">Roman coins</a>) found in areas of central and southern Ireland (such as <a href="/wiki/Hill_of_Tara" title="Hill of Tara">Tara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cashel,_County_Tipperary" title="Cashel, County Tipperary">Cashel</a>), that reveal a relationship. Roman coins have also been found at <a href="/wiki/Newgrange" title="Newgrange">Newgrange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles-Edwards" title="Thomas Charles-Edwards">Thomas Charles-Edwards</a>, who wrote about the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Dark_Age" title="Irish Dark Age">Irish Dark Age</a>, between the 1st and 3rd century there was a depopulating <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a> from Hibernia toward rich <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>, that had an economy based on <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">villa</a> farming and needed <a href="/wiki/Slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave">slaves</a> to perform the heaviest labour in agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_and_religious">Cultural and religious</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiberno-Roman_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural and religious">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The religious influence of the late Roman Empire involved the conversion to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> of many Irish people before the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a> in the century when the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> disappeared.<sup id="cite_ref-Saint_Palladius_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saint_Palladius-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The first reliable historical event in Irish history, recorded in the <i>Chronicle</i> of <a href="/wiki/Prosper_of_Aquitaine" title="Prosper of Aquitaine">Prosper of Aquitaine</a>, is the ordination by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Celestine_I" title="Pope Celestine I">Pope Celestine I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Palladius_(bishop_of_Ireland)" title="Palladius (bishop of Ireland)">Palladius</a> as the first bishop to Irish Christians in 431 - which demonstrates that there were already Christians living in Ireland, before Palladius or Patrick. Prosper says in his <i>Contra Collatorem</i> that by this act Celestine "made the barbarian island Christian", although it is clear the <a href="/wiki/Christianisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Christianisation">Christianisation</a> of Ireland was a longer and more gradual process.\n</p><p>Apart from the introduction of a new religion, the cultural influence from Rome can be seen even in the clothes (and glades) of high-ranking people inside Celtic tribes of the 3rd and 4th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ogham" title="Ogham">Ogham</a> <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">writing system</a> (which was probably first invented in the 4th century at Irish settlements in west <a href="/wiki/History_of_Wales" title="History of Wales">Wales</a>), was derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> after contact and intermarriage with <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romanized Britons</a> with a knowledge of written Latin.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In fact, several <a href="/wiki/Ogham_inscription" title="Ogham inscription">Ogham stones</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> are <a href="/wiki/Bilingual" class="mw-redirect" title="Bilingual">bilingual</a>, containing both <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> and Latin-influenced <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brythonic</a> (the ancestor of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>) inscriptions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiberno-Roman_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There is some evidence of possible exploratory expeditions during the time of <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gnaeus Julius Agricola</a>, although the interpretation of this is a matter of debate amongst historians. In places like <a href="/wiki/Drumanagh" title="Drumanagh">Drumanagh</a> (interpreted by some historians to be the site of a possible <a href="/wiki/Roman_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman fort">Roman fort</a> or temporary camp) and <a href="/wiki/Lambay_island" class="mw-redirect" title="Lambay island">Lambay island</a>, some Roman military-related finds may be evidence for some form of Roman presence.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  The most commonly advanced interpretation is that any military presence was to provide security for traders, possibly in the form of an annual market where Romano-British and Irish met to trade.  Other interpretations, however, suggest these may be merely Roman trading outposts, or native Irish settlements which traded with Roman Britain. Later, during the collapse of Roman authority in the 4th and 5th centuries, Irish tribes raided Britain and may have brought back Roman knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Classical_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical civilization">classical civilization</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_presence_in_Hibernia">Roman presence in Hibernia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiberno-Roman_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman presence in Hibernia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The question of whether the Romans ever landed in Ireland has long been the subject of speculation, but in recent years firmer theories have emerged. Historian Vittorio di Martino writes in his book <i>Roman Ireland</i> that Agricola promoted an exploratory expedition to Hibernia, similar to the one <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a> sent to explore southern <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> in 61 AD, in order to organize a following <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nile_exploration_by_Roman_emperor_Nero&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nile exploration by Roman emperor Nero (page does not exist)">military expedition to conquer</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> (though this never came about because of his death).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg/170px-Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg" width="170" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg/255px-Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg/340px-Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="594" data-file-height="760" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaius_Cornelius_Tacitus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> wrote that the Roman general Agricola in 82 crossed the sea (of Ireland?) from western Britain and conquered "tribes unknown" to Romans</div></div></div>\n<p>Indeed, the Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> mentions that <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Agricola</a>, while governor of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> (AD 78 - 84), considered conquering Ireland, believing it could be held with one <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">legion</a> plus <a href="/wiki/Auxiliaries_(Roman_military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliaries (Roman military)">auxiliaries</a> and entertained an exiled Irish prince, thinking to use him as a pretext for a possible invasion of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This Roman author tells us that around those years Agricola had with him an Irish chieftain (may be <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BAathal_Techtmar" title="T\xc3\xbaathal Techtmar">T\xc3\xbaathal Techtmar</a>) who later returned to conquer Ireland with an army. Excavations at sites linked to the tale of T\xc3\xbaathal have produced Roman material of the late 1st or early 2nd centuries. It would be consistent for T\xc3\xbaathal to have been that Irish chieftain.\n</p><p>Clearly, neither Agricola nor his successors ever conquered Ireland, but in recent years, <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a> has challenged the belief that the Romans never set foot on the island. Roman and Romano-British artefacts have been found primarily in Leinster, notably a fortified site on the promontory of <a href="/wiki/Drumanagh" title="Drumanagh">Drumanagh</a>, fifteen miles north of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, and burials on the nearby island of <a href="/wiki/Lambay" class="mw-redirect" title="Lambay">Lambay</a>, both close to where T\xc3\xbaathal Techtmar is supposed to have landed, and other sites associated with T\xc3\xbaathal such as Tara and <a href="/wiki/Clogher" title="Clogher">Clogher</a>.\n</p><p>However, whether this is evidence of trade, diplomacy or military activity is a matter of controversy. It is possible that the Romans may have given support to T\xc3\xbaathal, or someone like him, to regain his throne in the interests of having a friendly neighbour who could restrain Irish raiding.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Furthermore, the 2nd-century Roman poet <a href="/wiki/Juvenal" title="Juvenal">Juvenal</a>, who may have served in Britain under Agricola, wrote that "arms had been taken beyond the shores of <i>Iuverna</i> (Hibernia)",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the coincidence of dates is striking. Although Juvenal is not writing history, it is possible that he is referring to a genuine Roman military expedition to Ireland, according to Philip Freeman.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is also speculated that such an invasion may have been the origin of the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Brigantes" title="Brigantes">Brigantes</a> in Ireland as noted in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s 2nd century Geography. The Brigantes were a rebellious <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">British</a> tribe only recently conquered in Agricola\'s time. The dispossessed nobility may have been ready recruits for T\xc3\xbaathal\'s invasion force, or the Romans may have found it a convenient way of getting rid of troublesome subjects, just as <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_VI_%26_I" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI &amp; I">James VI &amp; I</a> planted rebellious Scots in Ireland in the 16th and 17th century. Other tribal names associated with the south-east, including the <a href="/wiki/Domnainn" class="mw-redirect" title="Domnainn">Domnainn</a>, related to the British <a href="/wiki/Dumnonii" title="Dumnonii">Dumnonii</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Menapii" title="Menapii">Menapii</a>, also known from <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> (Roman France), may also date from such an invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Church_influence">Roman Church influence</span><span One of the first churches in Hibernia was founded by Saint Palladius in 420 AD, with the name <i>House of the Romans</i> (Teach-na-Roman, actual Tigroney).<sup id="cite_ref-Saint_Palladius_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saint_Palladius-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  However, actual contacts with Rome and Italy seem to have been erratic for much of this period, and there were also contacts with Egyptian Christianity. \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Romano-Britons" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-Britons">Romano-British</a> Saint Patrick promoted the creation of <a href="/wiki/Monasteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Monasteries">monasteries</a> in Hibernia and the older <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">druid</a> tradition collapsed, in the face of the new religion he brought.<sup id="cite_ref-cah_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cah-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the monastic culture that followed the Christianisation of Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> learning was preserved in Ireland during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> in contrast to some other parts of Europe, where the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a> followed the <a href="/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Decline of the Roman Empire">loss of Roman imperial authority over Western Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cah_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cah-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Eer_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eer-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In those monasteries, <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Latin" title="Hiberno-Latin">Hiberno-Latin</a> was a learned sort of <a href="/wiki/Romance_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Romance language">Latin</a> literature created and spread by Irish monks during the period from the 6th to the 10th centuries.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
