title="Northwest Indian War">Northwest Indian War</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Charles Scott</b> (April 1739&#160;&#8211;&#32;October<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>22, 1813) was an 18th-century American soldier who was elected the fourth <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Kentucky" title="Governor of Kentucky">Governor of Kentucky</a> in 1808. Orphaned in his teens, Scott enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Regiment" title="Virginia Regiment">Virginia Regiment</a> in October 1755 and served as a scout and escort during the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>. He quickly rose through the ranks to become a <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States)" title="Captain (United States)">captain</a>. After the war, he married and engaged in agricultural pursuits on land left to him by his father, but he returned to active military service in 1775 as the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> began to grow in intensity. In August 1776, he was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">colonel</a> and given command of the <a href="/wiki/5th_Virginia_Regiment" title="5th Virginia Regiment">5th Virginia Regiment</a>. The 5th Virginia joined <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> later that year, serving with him for the duration of the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_campaign" title="Philadelphia campaign">Philadelphia campaign</a>. Scott commanded Washington\'s light infantry, and by late 1778 was also serving as his chief of intelligence. <a href="/wiki/Furlough" title="Furlough">Furloughed</a> at the end of the Philadelphia campaign, Scott returned to active service in March 1779 and was ordered to <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> to assist General <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Lincoln" title="Benjamin Lincoln">Benjamin Lincoln</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War">southern theater</a>. He arrived in <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, just as <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Henry Clinton</a> had begun his <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Charleston" title="Siege of Charleston">siege of the city</a>. Scott was taken as a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a> when Charleston surrendered. Paroled in March 1781 and <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_exchange" title="Prisoner exchange">exchanged</a> for <a href="/wiki/Francis_Rawdon-Hastings,_1st_Marquess_of_Hastings" title="Francis Rawdon-Hastings, 1st Marquess of Hastings">Lord Rawdon</a> in July 1782, Scott managed to complete a few recruiting assignments before the war ended.\n</p><p>After the war, Scott visited the western frontier in 1785 and began to make preparations for a permanent relocation. He resettled near present-day <a href="/wiki/Versailles,_Kentucky" title="Versailles, Kentucky">Versailles, Kentucky</a>, in 1787. Confronted by the dangers of Indian raids, Scott raised a company of volunteers in 1790 and joined <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Harmar" title="Josiah Harmar">Josiah Harmar</a> for an expedition against the Indians. After <a href="/wiki/Harmar%27s_Defeat" class="mw-redirect" title="Harmar&#39;s Defeat">Harmar\'s Defeat</a>, President Washington ordered <a href="/wiki/Arthur_St._Clair" title="Arthur St. Clair">Arthur St. Clair</a> to prepare for an invasion of Indian lands in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territory" title="Northwest Territory">Northwest Territory</a>. In the meantime, Scott, by now holding the rank of <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general" title="Brigadier general">brigadier general</a> in the Virginia militia, was ordered to conduct a series of preliminary raids. In July 1791, he led the most notable and successful of these raids against the village of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Ouiatenon" title="Fort Ouiatenon">Ouiatenon</a>. St. Clair\'s main invasion, conducted later that year, was a failure. Shortly after the separation of Kentucky from Virginia in 1792, the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_General_Assembly" title="Kentucky General Assembly">Kentucky General Assembly</a> commissioned Scott as a <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">major general</a> and gave him command of the 2nd Division of the Kentucky militia. Scott\'s division cooperated with <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Wayne" title="Anthony Wayne">"Mad" Anthony Wayne</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_the_United_States" title="Legion of the United States">Legion of the United States</a> for the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Indian_War" title="Northwest Indian War">Northwest Indian War</a>, including their decisive victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fallen_Timbers" title="Battle of Fallen Timbers">Battle of Fallen Timbers</a>.\n</p><p>Having previously served in the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="Virginia House of Delegates">Virginia House of Delegates</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/United_States_electoral_college" class="mw-redirect" title="United States electoral college">presidential elector</a>, the aging Scott now ran for governor. His 1808 campaign was skillfully managed by his step-son-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Bledsoe" title="Jesse Bledsoe">Jesse Bledsoe</a>, and he won a convincing victory over <a href="/wiki/John_Allen_(soldier)" title="John Allen (soldier)">John Allen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Clay" title="Green Clay">Green Clay</a>. A fall on the icy steps of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Governor%27s_Mansion_(Frankfort,_Kentucky)" title="Old Governor&#39;s Mansion (Frankfort, Kentucky)">governor\'s mansion</a> early in his term confined Scott to crutches for the rest of his life, and left him heavily reliant on Bledsoe, whom he appointed <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_of_Kentucky" title="Secretary of State of Kentucky">Secretary of State</a>. Although he frequently clashed with the state legislature over domestic matters, the primary concern of his administration was the increasing tension between the United States and Great Britain that eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>. Scott\'s decision to appoint <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Harrison" title="William Henry Harrison">William Henry Harrison</a> as <a href="/wiki/Brevet_(military)" title="Brevet (military)">brevet</a> major general in the Kentucky militia, although probably in violation of the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Constitution" title="Kentucky Constitution">state constitution</a> as Harrison was not a resident of the state, was nonetheless praised by the state\'s citizens. After his term expired, Scott returned to his Canewood estate. His health declined rapidly, and he died on October<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>22, 1813. <a href="/wiki/Scott_County,_Kentucky" title="Scott County, Kentucky">Scott County, Kentucky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scott_County,_Indiana" title="Scott County, Indiana">Scott County, Indiana</a>, are named in his honor, as are the cities of <a href="/wiki/Scottsville,_Kentucky" title="Scottsville, Kentucky">Scottsville, Kentucky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scottsville,_Virginia" title="Scottsville, Virginia">Scottsville, Virginia</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_family">Early life and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Charles Scott was born in 1739, probably in April, in the part of <a href="/wiki/Goochland_County,_Virginia" title="Goochland County, Virginia">Goochland County, Virginia</a>, that is now <a href="/wiki/Powhatan_County,_Virginia" title="Powhatan County, Virginia">Powhatan County</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles"><span title="This text contradicts material elsewhere on this page.">contradictory</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-kye803_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kye803-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His father, Samuel Scott, was a farmer and member of the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_House_of_Burgesses" class="mw-redirect" title="Virginia House of Burgesses">Virginia House of Burgesses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs16_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs16-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His mother, whose name is not known, died most likely around 1745.<sup id="cite_ref-ward2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Scott had an older brother, John, and three younger siblings, Edward, Joseph, and Martha.<sup id="cite_ref-ward2_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He received only a basic education from his parents and in the rural Virginia schools near his home.<sup id="cite_ref-powell20_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powell20-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Shortly after his father died in 1755, Scott was apprenticed to a carpenter.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs16_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs16-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward3_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward3-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In late July 1755, a local court was preparing to place him  with a guardian, but in October, before the court acted, Scott enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Regiment" title="Virginia Regiment">Virginia Regiment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward3_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward3-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He was assigned to David Bell\'s <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward3_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward3-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> During the early part of the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>, he won praise from his superiors as a frontier scout and woodsman.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs16_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs16-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Most of his fellow soldiers were undisciplined and poorly trained, allowing Scott to stand out and quickly rise to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Corporal" title="Corporal">corporal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward4_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward4-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By June 1756, he had been promoted to <a href="/wiki/Sergeant" title="Sergeant">sergeant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward4_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward4-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scott served under <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Braddock_Expedition" title="Braddock Expedition">Braddock Expedition</a>, a failed attempt to capture <a href="/wiki/Fort_Duquesne" title="Fort Duquesne">Fort Duquesne</a> from the French.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> For most of 1756 and the early part of 1757, he divided his time between <a href="/wiki/Fort_Cumberland_(Maryland)" title="Fort Cumberland (Maryland)">Fort Cumberland</a> and Fort Washington, conducting scouting and escort missions.<sup id="cite_ref-ward5_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward5-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In April 1757, David Bell was relieved of his command as part of a general downsizing of Washington\'s regiment, and Scott was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States)" title="Captain (United States)">Captain</a> Robert McKenzie at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Pearsall" title="Fort Pearsall">Fort Pearsall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward5_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward5-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In August and September, Washington sent Scott and a small scouting party on two reconnaissance missions to Fort Duquesne in preparation for an assault on that fort, but the party learned little on either mission.<sup id="cite_ref-ward6_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward6-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In November, Scott was part of the <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Expedition" title="Forbes Expedition">Forbes Expedition</a> that captured the fort.<sup id="cite_ref-ward6_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward6-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He spent the latter part of the year at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Loudoun_(Virginia)" title="Fort Loudoun (Virginia)">Fort Loudoun</a>, where Washington promoted him to <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">ensign</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward6_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward6-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scott spent most of 1759 conducting escort missions and constructing roads and forts.<sup id="cite_ref-ward7_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward7-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During this time, Virginia\'s forces were taken from George Washington and put under the control of <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Colonel</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Byrd_III" title="William Byrd III">William Byrd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward7_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward7-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In July 1760, Scott was named the fifth captain of a group of Virginia troops that Byrd led on an expedition against the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a> in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-ward7_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward7-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Scott\'s exact role in the campaign is not known.<sup id="cite_ref-ward7_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward7-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The expedition was a success, and Virginia Governor <a href="/wiki/Francis_Fauquier" title="Francis Fauquier">Francis Fauquier</a> ordered the force disbanded in February 1762; Scott had left the army at some unknown date prior to that.<sup id="cite_ref-ward8_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward8-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sometime prior to 1762, Scott\'s older brother, John, died, leaving Scott to inherit his father\'s land near the <a href="/wiki/James_River" title="James River">James River</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muddy_Creek_(Virginia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Muddy Creek (Virginia) (page does not exist)">Muddy Creek</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward2_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Having left the army, he had settled on his inherited farm by late 1761.<sup id="cite_ref-ward8_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward8-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> On February<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>25, 1762, he married Frances Sweeney from <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_County,_Virginia" title="Cumberland County, Virginia">Cumberland County, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> With the help of approximately 10 <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slaves</a>, Scott engaged in growing <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> and milling flour on his farm.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In July 1766, he was named one of two captains in the local militia.<sup id="cite_ref-ward9_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward9-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Over the next several years, Scott and his wife had four boys and four or five girls.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs16_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs16-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolutionary_War">Revolutionary Virginia</div></div></div>\n<p>As the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> intensified in 1775, Scott raised a company of volunteers in Cumberland County.<sup id="cite_ref-ward9_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward9-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It was the first company formed south of the James River to participate in the Revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The company stood ready to aid <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Henry" title="Patrick Henry">Patrick Henry</a> in an anticipated clash with <a href="/wiki/John_Murray,_4th_Earl_of_Dunmore" title="John Murray, 4th Earl of Dunmore">Lord Dunmore</a> at <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Virginia" title="Williamsburg, Virginia">Williamsburg, Virginia</a>, in May 1775, but Dunmore abandoned the city in June, and they joined units from the surrounding counties in Williamsburg later that month.<sup id="cite_ref-ward10_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward10-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In July, the <a href="/wiki/Third_Virginia_Convention" title="Third Virginia Convention">Virginia Convention</a> created two regiments of Virginia troops, one under Patrick Henry and the other under <a href="/wiki/William_Woodford" title="William Woodford">William Woodford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward12_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward12-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> As those leaders departed for Williamsburg, the Conventions acknowledged Scott as temporary commander-in-chief of the volunteers already assembled there.<sup id="cite_ref-ward12_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward12-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> On August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>17, 1775, he was elected <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant colonel (United States)">lieutenant colonel</a> of Woodford\'s regiment, the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Virginia_Regiment" title="2nd Virginia Regiment">2nd Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward12_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward12-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> His younger brother, Joseph, served as a <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">lieutenant</a> in the regiment.<sup id="cite_ref-ward12_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward12-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In December, Woodford dispatched Scott and 150 men to <a href="/wiki/Great_Bridge,_Virginia" title="Great Bridge, Virginia">Great Bridge, Virginia</a>, to defend a crossing point on the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_River_(Virginia)" title="Elizabeth River (Virginia)">Elizabeth River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward14_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward14-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Days later, this force played a significant role in the December<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>9, 1775, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Great_Bridge" title="Battle of Great Bridge">Battle of Great Bridge</a> by killing British Captain <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Fordyce&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles Fordyce (page does not exist)">Charles Fordyce</a>, thereby halting the British advance on the crossing.<sup id="cite_ref-ward15_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward15-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Following the battle, colonial forces were able to occupy the city of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>, and Lord Dunmore eventually departed from Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs16_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs16-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward17_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward17-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On February<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>13, 1776, the 2nd Virginia became a part of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>; Scott retained his rank of lieutenant colonel during the transition.<sup id="cite_ref-ward19_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward19-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> After spending the winter with part of the 2nd Virginia in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk,_Virginia" title="Suffolk, Virginia">Suffolk</a>, Scott was chosen by the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a> as colonel of the <a href="/wiki/5th_Virginia_Regiment" title="5th Virginia Regiment">5th Virginia Regiment</a> on August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>12, 1776; he replaced Colonel <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Peachy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William Peachy (page does not exist)">William Peachy</a>, who had resigned.<sup id="cite_ref-powell20_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powell20-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward20_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward20-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The 5th Virginia was stationed in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_Virginia" title="Hampton, Virginia">Hampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Virginia" title="Portsmouth, Virginia">Portsmouth</a> through the end of September.<sup id="cite_ref-ward20_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward20-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> They were then ordered to join George Washington in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, eventually repairing to the city of <a href="/wiki/Trenton,_New_Jersey" title="Trenton, New Jersey">Trenton</a> in November.<sup id="cite_ref-ward20_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward20-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philadelphia_campaign">Philadelphia campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philadelphia campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_campaign" title="Philadelphia campaign">Philadelphia campaign</a></div>\n<p>Serving as part of <a href="/wiki/Adam_Stephen" title="Adam Stephen">Adam Stephen</a>\'s brigade, Scott\'s 5th Virginia Regiment fought in the colonial victory at the December<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>26 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trenton" title="Battle of Trenton">Battle of Trenton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> During the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Assunpink_Creek" title="Battle of the Assunpink Creek">Battle of the Assunpink Creek</a> on January<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2, 1777, the 5th Virginia helped slow the advance of a combined force of British light infantry and <a href="/wiki/Hessian_(soldiers)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hessian (soldiers)">Hessian</a> mercenaries toward Trenton.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Major George Johnston, a member of the 5th Virginia, opined that Scott had "acquired immortal honor" from his performance at Assunpink Creek.<sup id="cite_ref-ward26_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward26-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Following these battles, Washington\'s main force prepared to spend the winter at <a href="/wiki/Morristown,_New_Jersey" title="Morristown, New Jersey">Morristown, New Jersey</a>, while Scott\'s regiment was based at nearby <a href="/wiki/Chatham,_New_Jersey" title="Chatham, New Jersey">Chatham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward28_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward28-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> From this base, he led light infantry raids against British foraging parties.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In his most notable engagement&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the February<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Drake%27s_Farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Drake&#39;s Farm">Battle of Drake\'s Farm</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 he defeated a superior combination of British and Hessian soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen623_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen623-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He led another notable raid against a large British force of about 2,000 at the February<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>8 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Quibbletown" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Quibbletown">Battle of Quibbletown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" height="251" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2031" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:General_George_Washington_at_Trenton_by_John_Trumbull.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>George Washington, commander of the colonial forces during the Philadelphia Campaign</div></div></div>\n<p>In March 1777, Scott returned to his Virginia farm, taking his first <a href="/wiki/Furlough" title="Furlough">furlough</a> in more than a year.<sup id="cite_ref-ward31_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward31-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In recognition of his service with Washington, Congress commissioned him a <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general" title="Brigadier general">brigadier general</a> on April<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2, 1777.<sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen623_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen623-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-trowbridge_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trowbridge-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> At Washington\'s request, he returned to Trenton on May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>10, 1777.<sup id="cite_ref-ward31_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward31-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> His 4th Virginia Brigade and another brigade under William Woodford constituted the Virginia <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">division</a>, commanded by Adam Stephen, who had been promoted to <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">major general</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen623_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen623-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> With Stephen and Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/William_Maxwell_(Continental_Army_general)" title="William Maxwell (Continental Army general)">William Maxwell</a> ill, Scott assumed temporary command of the division between May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>19 and 24.<sup id="cite_ref-ward32_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward32-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Washington spent much of mid-1777 trying to anticipate and counter the moves of British General <a href="/wiki/William_Howe,_5th_Viscount_Howe" title="William Howe, 5th Viscount Howe">William Howe</a>, and the lull in the fighting allowed Scott time to file a protest with Congress regarding how his seniority and rank had been calculated.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> After eight months of deliberation, Congress concurred with Scott\'s protest, placing him ahead of fellow brigadier general <a href="/wiki/George_Weedon" title="George Weedon">George Weedon</a> in seniority.<sup id="cite_ref-ward34_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward34-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the September<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>11 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brandywine" title="Battle of Brandywine">Battle of Brandywine</a>, the 4th Virginia Brigade stubbornly resisted the advance of General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a>, but was ultimately forced to retreat.<sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen623_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen623-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Following the British victory, Howe marched toward <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia</a>, stopping briefly at <a href="/wiki/Germantown,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Germantown, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Germantown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward37_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward37-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Scott persistently advocated for an attack on Howe\'s position at Germantown, and although he was initially in the minority among Washington\'s generals, he ultimately prevailed upon Washington to conduct the attack.<sup id="cite_ref-ward39_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward39-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On October<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>4, 1777, the 4th Virginia attacked the British in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Germantown" title="Battle of Germantown">Battle of Germantown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen624_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen624-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Because of their circuitous route to the battle, the field was already covered by heavy smoke from <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">muskets</a> and a fire set by the British in a dry <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a> field when they arrived; they and the other colonial forces were lost in the smoke and retreated.<sup id="cite_ref-ward39_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward39-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the defeat at Germantown, Washington\'s troops took a position in the hills surrounding <a href="/wiki/Whitemarsh_Township,_Montgomery_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Whitemarsh Township, Montgomery County, Pennsylvania">Whitemarsh, Pennsylvania</a>, about 14 miles (23&#160;km) from Philadelphia.<sup id="cite_ref-ward40_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward40-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Scott and four other generals initially favored an attack on Philadelphia in December, but after hearing Washington\'s assessment of the enemy\'s defenses there, they abandoned the idea.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Marsh" title="Battle of White Marsh">a series of skirmishes with Howe\'s men near Whitemarsh</a>, Washington\'s army camped for the winter at <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Scott was afforded the luxury of boarding at the farm of Samuel Jones, about three miles from the camp, but rode out to inspect his brigade daily.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Washington granted him a furlough in mid-March 1778, and he returned to Valley Forge on May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>20, 1778.<sup id="cite_ref-ward46_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward46-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When Washington and his men abandoned Valley Forge in mid-June 1778, Scott was ordered to take 1,500 light infantrymen and harass the British forces as they marched across New Jersey.<sup id="cite_ref-ward48_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward48-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> On June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>26, the <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_du_Motier,_marquis_de_Lafayette" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilbert du Motier, marquis de Lafayette">Marquis de Lafayette</a> joined Scott with an additional 1,000 men, in anticipation of a major offensive the next day.<sup id="cite_ref-ward48_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward48-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lee_(general)" title="Charles Lee (general)">Charles Lee</a> was chosen to command the operation, which was delayed by one day due to inadequate communications and delays in forwarding provisions.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Lee shared no battle plan with his generals, later claiming he had insufficient intelligence to form one.<sup id="cite_ref-ward49_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward49-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> On the morning of June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>28, Lee launched the attack, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monmouth" title="Battle of Monmouth">Battle of Monmouth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward49_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward49-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> During the battle, Scott observed American artillerymen retreating.<sup id="cite_ref-ward49_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward49-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Not realizing that the men had only run out of ammunition, Scott believed the retreat was a sign of the collapse of the American offensive and ordered his men to retreat as well.<sup id="cite_ref-ward49_41-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward49-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Lacking a battle plan for guidance, William Maxwell and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Wayne" title="Anthony Wayne">Anthony Wayne</a>, whose units were fighting adjacent to Scott\'s men, also ordered a retreat.<sup id="cite_ref-ward49_41-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward49-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> With such a great number of his men retreating, Lee fell back and eventually aborted the offensive.<sup id="cite_ref-ward49_41-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward49-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Although Washington\'s main force arrived and stopped the British advance, Scott\'s retreat was partially blamed for giving them control of the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward51_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward51-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Tradition holds that, in the aftermath of the battle, Scott witnessed Washington excoriating Lee in a profanity-laden tirade, but biographer Harry M. Ward considered it unlikely that Scott was present at the meeting.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Lee was later <a href="/wiki/Court-martial" title="Court-martial">court-martialed</a> for the retreat and suspended from command.<sup id="cite_ref-ward52_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward52-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the Battle of Monmouth, the British retreated to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward51_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward51-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> On August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>14, Scott was given command of a new light infantry corps organized by Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-ward53_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward53-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> He also served as Washington\'s chief of intelligence, conducting constant scouting missions from the Americans\' new base at <a href="/wiki/White_Plains,_New_York" title="White Plains, New York">White Plains, New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward53_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward53-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> While Scott\'s men engaged in a few skirmishes with British scouting parties, neither Washington\'s army nor the British force at New York City conducted any major operations before Scott was furloughed in November 1778.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_in_the_southern_theater_and_capture">Service in the southern theater and capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Service in the southern theater and capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War">Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War</a></div>\n<p>A March 1779 letter from Washington to Scott, still on furlough in Virginia, ordered him to recruit volunteers in Virginia and join Washington at <a href="/wiki/Middlebrook_encampment" title="Middlebrook encampment">Middlebrook</a> on May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1.<sup id="cite_ref-ward68_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward68-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Men and supplies proved difficult to obtain, delaying Scott\'s return; during the delay, Washington ordered the recruits to <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> to join <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Lincoln" title="Benjamin Lincoln">Benjamin Lincoln</a>, who was in command of the militia forces there.<sup id="cite_ref-ward69_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward69-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Reports of significant British troop movements toward <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> had convinced Washington that the enemy was preparing an invasion from the south.<sup id="cite_ref-ward69_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward69-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A portly, white-haired man wearing a black jacket with gold epaulets, a gold vest, and a high collared, white shirt" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg/170px-General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg" width="170" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg/255px-General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg/340px-General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg 2x" data-file-width="432" data-file-height="530" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Benjamin_Lincoln-restored.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Benjamin Lincoln, commander of the forces at Charleston, South Carolina</div></div></div>\n<p>Soon after Washington\'s orders were delivered, a British raiding party under <a href="/wiki/George_Collier" title="George Collier">George Collier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Mathew" title="Edward Mathew">Edward Mathew</a> arrived in Virginia to capture or destroy supplies that might otherwise be sent southward to aid the reinforcements going to South Carolina.<sup id="cite_ref-ward70_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward70-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Scott\'s orders changed again; the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="Virginia House of Delegates">Virginia House of Delegates</a> ordered him to immediately prepare defenses against Collier and Mathew\'s raids.<sup id="cite_ref-ward70_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward70-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> When it became clear to both the legislature and Washington that Collier and Mathew intended only to raid supplies, not to invade, they concluded that the local militia would be able to sufficiently protect Virginia\'s interests and that Scott should continue to recruit men to reinforce the south.<sup id="cite_ref-ward71_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward71-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The legislators presented him with a horse, a firearm, and 500 <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pounds sterling</a> for his quick response to the threat.<sup id="cite_ref-ward71_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward71-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scott\'s recruiting difficulties in Virginia continued, despite the implementation of a <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_the_United_States" title="Conscription in the United States">draft</a> by the state legislature.<sup id="cite_ref-ward72_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward72-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Finally, in October 1779, he forwarded troops sent to him from Washington\'s Northern Army on to Lincoln in South Carolina, fulfilling his quota.<sup id="cite_ref-ward73_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward73-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> He retained only <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Buford" title="Abraham Buford">Abraham Buford</a>\'s regiment with him in Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-ward73_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward73-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> In February 1780, about 750 men sent by Washington under William Woodford arrived at Scott\'s camp in <a href="/wiki/Petersburg,_Virginia" title="Petersburg, Virginia">Petersburg, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward74_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward74-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Virginia authorities, fearing that the British force to the south under General <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Henry Clinton</a> would turn north to Virginia, detained Scott and Woodford until it was clear that Clinton\'s object was Lincoln\'s position at <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward74_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward74-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On March<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>30, 1780, Scott arrived in Charleston just as Clinton was <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Charleston" title="Siege of Charleston">laying siege to the city</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen624_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen624-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> He was captured when the city surrendered on May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>12, 1780, and was held as a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a> at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pleasant,_South_Carolina" title="Mount Pleasant, South Carolina">Haddrell\'s Point</a> near Charleston.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen624_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen624-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Although he was a prisoner, he was given freedom to move within a six-mile radius and was allowed to correspond and trade with acquaintances in Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> With the death of William Woodford on November<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>13, 1780, he became primarily responsible for the welfare of the Virginia troops at Haddrell\'s Point.<sup id="cite_ref-ward78_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward78-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> He requested his parole on account of ill health on January<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>30, 1781, and in late March, Charles Cornwallis granted the request.<sup id="cite_ref-ward81_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward81-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 1782, Scott was <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_exchange" title="Prisoner exchange">exchanged</a> for <a href="/wiki/Francis_Rawdon-Hastings,_1st_Marquess_of_Hastings" title="Francis Rawdon-Hastings, 1st Marquess of Hastings">Lord Rawdon</a>, ending his parole.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Washington informed him that he was back on active duty and ordered him to assist General <a href="/wiki/Peter_Muhlenberg" title="Peter Muhlenberg">Peter Muhlenberg</a>\'s recruiting efforts in Virginia, then to report to General <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward83_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward83-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Greene wrote that he did not have a command for Scott, and requested that he remain with Muhlenberg in Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-ward83_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward83-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The few troops he was able to recruit were sent to a depot at <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Virginia" title="Winchester, Virginia">Winchester, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward86_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward86-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> When the preliminary articles of peace between the United States and Great Britain were signed in March 1783, recruiting stopped altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-ward86_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward86-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Scott was <a href="/wiki/Brevet_(military)" title="Brevet (military)">brevetted</a> to major general on September<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>30, 1783, just prior to his discharge from the Continental Army.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fredriksen624_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fredriksen624-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Following the war, he became one of the founding members of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Cincinnati" title="Society of the Cincinnati">Society of the Cincinnati</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement_in_Kentucky_and_early_political_career">Settlement in Kentucky and early political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Settlement in Kentucky and early political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peyton_Short.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A red-haired man wearing a black jacket and white high-collared shirt" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Peyton_Short.jpg/170px-Peyton_Short.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Peyton_Short.jpg/255px-Peyton_Short.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b1/Peyton_Short.jpg 2x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peyton_Short.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Peyton Short accompanied Scott to Kentucky in 1785.</div></div></div>\n<p>In October 1783, the Virginia Legislature authorized Scott to commission superintendents and surveyors to survey the lands given to soldiers for their service in the Revolutionary War.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Enticed by glowing reports of Kentucky by his friend, <a href="/wiki/James_Wilkinson" title="James Wilkinson">James Wilkinson</a>, he arranged for a cabin to be built for him near the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_River" title="Kentucky River">Kentucky River</a>, although the builder apparently laid only the cornerstone.<sup id="cite_ref-ward90_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward90-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Scott first visited Kentucky in mid-1785.<sup id="cite_ref-ward91_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward91-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Traveling with <a href="/wiki/Peyton_Short" title="Peyton Short">Peyton Short</a>, one of Wilkinson\'s business partners, he came to Limestone (present-day <a href="/wiki/Maysville,_Kentucky" title="Maysville, Kentucky">Maysville, Kentucky</a>) via the <a href="/wiki/Monongahela_River" title="Monongahela River">Monongahela</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio</a> Rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-ward91_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward91-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Scott and Short then traveled overland to the Kentucky River to examine the land they would later claim.<sup id="cite_ref-ward91_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward91-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Scott\'s stay in Kentucky was a short one; he had returned to his farm in Virginia by September 1785.<sup id="cite_ref-ward91_60-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward91-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On his return to Virginia, Scott employed <a href="/wiki/Edward_Carrington" title="Edward Carrington">Edward Carrington</a>, former quartermaster general of the Southern Army, to set his financial affairs in order in preparation for a move to Kentucky.<sup id="cite_ref-ward91_60-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward91-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Carrington purchased Scott\'s Virginia farm in 1785, but allowed the family to live there until they moved to the frontier.<sup id="cite_ref-ward92_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward92-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> In 1787, Scott settled near the city of <a href="/wiki/Versailles,_Kentucky" title="Versailles, Kentucky">Versailles, Kentucky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Between his military claims and those of his children, the Scott family was entitled to 21,035 acres (8,513&#160;ha) in <a href="/wiki/Fayette_County,_Kentucky" title="Fayette County, Kentucky">Fayette</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_County,_Kentucky" title="Bourbon County, Kentucky">Bourbon</a> counties.<sup id="cite_ref-clark13_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark13-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Scott constructed a two-story <a href="/wiki/Log_cabin" title="Log cabin">log cabin</a>, a stockade, and a tobacco inspection warehouse.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In June 1787, <a href="/wiki/Shawnee" title="Shawnee">Shawnee</a> warriors killed and <a href="/wiki/Scalping" title="Scalping">scalped</a> his son, Samuel, while he was crossing the Ohio River in a canoe; the elder Scott watched helplessly from the riverbank.<sup id="cite_ref-ward96_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward96-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Although a small party of settlers pursued the Shawnees back across the river, they were not able to overtake them.<sup id="cite_ref-ward97_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward97-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> In volume three of <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>\'s <i>The Winning of the West</i>, he stated that Scott "delighted in war" against the Indians after the death of his son.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson220_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson220-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scott focused on the development of his homestead as a way to deal with the grief of losing his son.<sup id="cite_ref-ward97_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward97-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The settlement became known as Scott\'s Landing, and Scott briefly served as a tobacco inspector for the area.<sup id="cite_ref-ward97_64-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward97-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Determined to make Scott\'s Landing the centerpiece of a larger settlement called Petersburg, he began selling lots near the settlement in November 1788.<sup id="cite_ref-ward98_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward98-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Among those who purchased lots were James Wilkinson, Abraham Buford, Judge <a href="/wiki/George_Muter" title="George Muter">George Muter</a>, and future <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">Congressman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Kentucky" title="Governor of Kentucky">Kentucky Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Greenup" title="Christopher Greenup">Christopher Greenup</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward98_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward98-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scott was one of 37 men who founded the Kentucky Society for the Promotion of Useful Knowledge in 1787.<sup id="cite_ref-ward99_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward99-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Although he did not participate in any of the ten statehood conventions that sought to separate Kentucky from Virginia, he supported the idea in principle.<sup id="cite_ref-ward100_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward100-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Woodford_County,_Kentucky" title="Woodford County, Kentucky">Woodford County</a> was formed from the part of Fayette County that included Scott\'s fledgling settlement, Scott declined appointment as the new county\'s lieutenant.<sup id="cite_ref-ward101_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward101-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> He consented to be a candidate to represent the county in the Virginia House of Delegates.<sup id="cite_ref-ward101_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward101-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> During his single term, he served on the committee on privileges and election and on several special committees, including one that recommended that President George Washington supply a military guard at <a href="/wiki/Big_Bone_Lick_State_Park" title="Big Bone Lick State Park">Big Bone Lick</a> to facilitate the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Saltern" title="Saltern">saltworks</a> there.<sup id="cite_ref-ward101_69-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward101-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Northwest_Indian_War">Northwest Indian War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Northwest Indian War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="A man with stringy, gray hair wearing a navy jacket with gold epaulets and collar and a high-collared white shirt gathered at the neck" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg/170px-Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg" width="170" height="202" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg/255px-Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg/340px-Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="364" data-file-height="432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Josiah_Harmar_by_Raphaelle_Peale.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Josiah Harmar\'s failed campaign convinced Kentuckians that only local militia could effectively fight the Indians of the Northwest Territory.</div></div></div>\n<p>As tensions mounted between the Indians in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territory" title="Northwest Territory">Northwest Territory</a> and settlers on the Kentucky frontier, President Washington began sanctioning joint operations between federal army troops and local frontier militia against the Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson223_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson223-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> In April 1790, Scott raised a contingent of volunteers from Bourbon and Fayette counties to join <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Harmar" title="Josiah Harmar">Josiah Harmar</a> in a raid against the <a href="/wiki/Western_Confederacy" title="Western Confederacy">Western Confederacy</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Scioto_River" title="Scioto River">Scioto River</a> in what would become the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson224_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson224-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> The combined force of regulars and militia departed from Limestone on April<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>18, 1790, crossing the Ohio River and marching to the upper Scioto.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson224_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson224-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> From there, they headed south, toward the present-day city of <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Ohio" title="Portsmouth, Ohio">Portsmouth, Ohio</a>, and discovered an abandoned Indian camp.<sup id="cite_ref-ward102_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward102-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Fresh footprints, including those of a well-known Shawnee warrior&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 nicknamed Reel Foot because of his two <a href="/wiki/Club_foot" class="mw-redirect" title="Club foot">club feet</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 led away from the camp site.<sup id="cite_ref-ward102_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward102-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Scott sent a small detachment to follow the tracks; ultimately, they discovered and killed four Shawnee, including Reel Foot.<sup id="cite_ref-ward102_72-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward102-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Other than this, the expedition accomplished nothing, and it disbanded on August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>27, 1790.<sup id="cite_ref-ward102_72-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward102-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 1790, Harmar and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_St._Clair" title="Arthur St. Clair">Arthur St. Clair</a> were ordered to lead <a href="/wiki/Harmar_Campaign" title="Harmar Campaign">another expedition</a> against the Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-ward103_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward103-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Harmar had hoped that Scott, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Shelby" title="Isaac Shelby">Isaac Shelby</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Logan" title="Benjamin Logan">Benjamin Logan</a> would join the campaign and lead the Kentucky militia, but all three declined.<sup id="cite_ref-ward103_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward103-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Scott had been elected to represent Woodford County in the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_General_Assembly" title="Virginia General Assembly">Virginia General Assembly</a>, and his legislative duty prevented his service.<sup id="cite_ref-ward103_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward103-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> He believed that the Kentucky militiamen would only serve under Colonel Robert Trotter, a veteran of Logan\'s earlier Indian fighting campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-ward103_73-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward103-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, command of the Kentucky militiamen was given to Major John Hardin, and many militiamen refused to join the campaign, just as Scott had predicted.<sup id="cite_ref-ward103_73-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward103-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> During the expedition, Scott\'s son, Merritt, who was serving as a captain in the Woodford County militia, was killed and scalped.<sup id="cite_ref-ward103_73-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward103-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The entire expedition was a failure, and it solidified the Kentucky militiamen\'s strong distrust of Harmar; most vowed never to fight alongside him again.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson224_71-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson224-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During Harmar\'s Campaign, Scott was serving in the state legislature in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward104_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward104-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> He was once again appointed to the committee on privileges and election.<sup id="cite_ref-ward104_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward104-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> He also served on the committee on propositions and grievances and several special committees.<sup id="cite_ref-ward104_74-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward104-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> On December<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>30, 1790, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Virginia" title="Governor of Virginia">Virginia Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Beverley_Randolph" title="Beverley Randolph">Beverley Randolph</a>, possibly acting on a recommendation from Washington, appointed Scott brigadier general in the Virginia militia and gave him command of the entire District of Kentucky.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson227_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson227-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> His primary responsibility was overseeing a line of 18 outposts along the Ohio River.<sup id="cite_ref-ward108_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward108-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> In January 1791, President Washington accepted <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senator">U.S. Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Brown_(Kentucky)" title="John Brown (Kentucky)">John Brown</a>\'s suggestion to appoint a Kentucky Board of War, composed of Brown, Scott, Isaac Shelby, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Innes" title="Harry Innes">Harry Innes</a>, and Benjamin Logan.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson227_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson227-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nhok70_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok70-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The committee was empowered to call out local militia to act in conjunction with federal troops against the Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-ward107_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward107-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> They recommended assembling an army of volunteers to locate and destroy Indian settlements north of the Ohio River.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok70_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok70-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Later that month, Washington approved a plan to invade the Indians\' homelands via a raid from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Washington,_Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Washington, Cincinnati, Ohio">Fort Washington</a> (near present-day <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-nelson228_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson228-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Most Kentuckians were displeased with Washington\'s choice of Arthur St. Clair, by then suffering from <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a> and unable to mount his own horse unassisted, as overall commander of the invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok70_77-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok70-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Scott was chosen to serve under St. Clair as commander of the 1,000 militiamen who took part in the invasion, about one-third of the total force.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson228_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson228-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Blackberry_Campaign">The Blackberry Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Blackberry Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Washington ordered Scott to conduct a series of preliminary raids in mid-1791 that would keep the enemy occupied while St. Clair assembled the primary invasion force.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson229_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson229-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Both Isaac Shelby and Benjamin Logan had hoped to lead the campaign, and neither would accept a lesser position.<sup id="cite_ref-ward109_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward109-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Shelby nevertheless supported the campaign, while Logan actively opposed it.<sup id="cite_ref-ward109_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward109-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Scott issued a call for volunteers to assemble at <a href="/wiki/Frankfort,_Kentucky" title="Frankfort, Kentucky">Frankfort, Kentucky</a>, on May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>15, 1791, to carry out these raids.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson229_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson229-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Kentuckians responded favorably to the idea of an all-militia campaign, and 852 men volunteered for service, although Scott was only authorized to take 750; Senator John Brown was among the volunteers.<sup id="cite_ref-ward109_81-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward109-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> After a brief delay to learn the fate of a failed diplomatic mission to the <a href="/wiki/Miami_people" title="Miami people">Miami</a> tribes in the Northwest Territory, Scott\'s men departed from Fort Washington on May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>24.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> The militiamen crossed the Ohio toward a clutch of Miami, <a href="/wiki/Kickapoo_people" title="Kickapoo people">Kickapoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wea" title="Wea">Wea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Potawatomi" title="Potawatomi">Potawatomi</a> settlements near the location of present-day <a href="/wiki/Lafayette,_Indiana" title="Lafayette, Indiana">Lafayette, Indiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward109_81-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward109-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nelson230_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson230-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> For eight days, they crossed rugged terrain and were bedraggled by frequent rainstorms.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson230_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson230-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> The harsh conditions spoiled the militia\'s supplies, and they resorted to gathering the blackberries that were growing in the area; for this reason, the expedition earned the nickname the "Blackberry Campaign".<sup id="cite_ref-nelson230_83-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson230-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As Scott\'s men reached an open prairie near the Wea settlement of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Ouiatenon" title="Fort Ouiatenon">Ouiatenon</a> on June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1, they were discovered by an enemy scout and hurried to attack the villages before the residents could react.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson230_83-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson230-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> When the main force reached the villages, they found the residents hurriedly fleeing across the <a href="/wiki/Wabash_River" title="Wabash River">Wabash River</a> in canoes.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson231_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson231-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Aided by cover fire from a Kickapoo village on the other side of the river, they were able to escape before Scott\'s men could attack.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson231_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson231-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The river was too wide to ford at Scott\'s location, so he sent a detachment under James Wilkinson in one direction and a detachment under Thomas Barbee in the other to find a place to ford the river.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson231_84-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson231-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Wilkinson did not find a suitable location, but located and killed a small band of Indians before returning.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson231_84-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson231-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Barbee located a crossing and conducted a brief raid against the Indians on the other side before returning to Scott.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson231_84-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson231-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The next morning, Scott\'s main force burned the nearby villages and crops, while a detachment under Wilkinson set out for the settlement of Kethtippecannunk.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok70_77-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok70-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nelson231_84-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson231-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The inhabitants of this village had fled across <a href="/wiki/Eel_River_(Wabash_River)" class="mw-redirect" title="Eel River (Wabash River)">Eel Creek</a>, and after a brief and ineffective firefight, Wilkinson\'s men burned the city and returned to Scott.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson231_84-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson231-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> In his official report, Scott noted that many of Kethtippecannunk\'s residents were French and speculated that it was connected to, perhaps dependent upon, the French settlement of <a href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward112_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward112-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Low on supplies, Scott and his men ended their campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson232_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson232-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> On the return trip, two men drowned in the <a href="/wiki/White_River_(Indiana)" title="White River (Indiana)">White River</a>; these were the only deaths among Scott\'s men.<sup id="cite_ref-ward114_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward114-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Five others were wounded but survived.<sup id="cite_ref-ward114_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward114-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> In total, they had killed 38 Indians and taken 57 more prisoner.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson232_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson232-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Scott sent 12 men ahead with the official report for Arthur St. Clair\'s review; the rest of the men arrived at Fort Steuben (present-day <a href="/wiki/Clarksville,_Indiana" title="Clarksville, Indiana">Clarksville, Indiana</a>) on June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>15.<sup id="cite_ref-ward114_87-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward114-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> The next day, they recrossed the Ohio River and received their discharge papers at <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward115_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward115-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="St._Clair_expedition">St. Clair expedition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: St. Clair expedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Scott\'s Wabash Campaign was well-received both in Kentucky and by the Washington administration.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson232_86-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson232-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> On June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>24, 1791, Arthur St. Clair encouraged the Board of War to organize a second expedition into the Wabash region and to remove their outposts along the Ohio River to free up manpower and finances as a prelude to his larger invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson233_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson233-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Scott questioned the wisdom of removing the outposts and convinced his fellow members of the Board of War to retain one at Big Bone Lick and one guarding an <a href="/wiki/Ironworks" title="Ironworks">ironworks</a> at the mouth of the Kentucky River.<sup id="cite_ref-ward115_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward115-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nelson233_89-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson233-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> His instincts later proved to be right; a month later, Indian raiders tried to deny the frontier settlers access to salt by capturing Big Bone Lick, but they were repelled by the militia stationed at the outpost there.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson233_89-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson233-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Scott also did not believe that 500 men, St. Clair\'s requested number for the second Wabash expedition, was sufficient for an effective operation.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson233_89-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson233-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A white-haired man wearing a navy jacket with gold lapels and epaulets and a high-collared white shirt" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg/220px-ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg" width="220" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg/330px-ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg/440px-ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1247" data-file-height="1425" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ArthurStClairOfficialPortrait-restored.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arthur St. Clair led a failed expedition against the Northwest Indians in late 1791.</div></div></div>\n<p>In July, Scott gave permission to Bourbon County resident <a href="/wiki/John_Edwards_(Kentucky)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Edwards (Kentucky)">John Edwards</a> to lead 300 men against a band of Indians suspected of stealing horses on the Kentucky side of the Ohio River.<sup id="cite_ref-ward115_88-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward115-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> Although Edwards\' expedition almost reached the <a href="/wiki/Sandusky_River" title="Sandusky River">Sandusky River</a>, they found only deserted villages.<sup id="cite_ref-ward116_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward116-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Unknown to the volunteers, they narrowly missed being ambushed by the Indians in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-ward116_90-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward116-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Many of the men who accompanied Edwards accused him of cowardice.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson233_89-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson233-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Due to illness, Scott was unable to lead the expedition St. Clair requested; instead, he chose his friend, James Wilkinson, to lead it.<sup id="cite_ref-ward115_88-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward115-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> Wilkinson\'s men departed on August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1.<sup id="cite_ref-ward116_90-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward116-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> During their expedition, they destroyed the evacuated village of Kikiah (also called <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kenapacomaqua" title="Battle of Kenapacomaqua">Kenapocomoco</a>), the rebuilt settlement of <a href="/wiki/Ouiatenon" title="Ouiatenon">Ouiatenon</a>, a small Kickapoo village, and several other small settlements in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-ward116_90-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward116-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Returning by the same route that Scott\'s previous expedition had, Wilkinson\'s men were back in Kentucky by August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>21.<sup id="cite_ref-ward116_90-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward116-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Scott\'s and Wilkinson\'s campaigns took a heavy toll on the Northwest Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-ward116_90-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward116-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> In particular, the Weas and Kickapoos signed a peace treaty with the United States the following year, and the Kickapoos migrated farther into <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward116_90-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward116-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>St. Clair continued his preparations for invading the northwest despite the fact that, by now, he admitted he was unfit for combat due to his ill health.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson234_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson234-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Like Harmar, he was also unpopular in Kentucky, and Scott had to conduct a draft to raise the militiamen needed for St. Clair\'s expedition.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok71_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok71-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> He and most other officers in Kentucky claimed they were too ill to lead the men; most actually feared losing the respect of Kentuckians through their association with St. Clair.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson234_91-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson234-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Colonel William Oldham was the highest-ranking soldier willing to lead the Kentuckians.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson234_91-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson234-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>St. Clair\'s party left Fort Washington on October<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok71_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok71-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> On November<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>3, he ordered his men to make camp on a small tributary of the Wabash River, mistakenly believing they were camping on the <a href="/wiki/St._Marys_River_(Indiana_and_Ohio)" title="St. Marys River (Indiana and Ohio)">St. Marys River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok71_92-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok71-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> His intent was for the men to construct some protective works the next day, but before sunrise, a combined group of Miami and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadians</a> <a href="/wiki/St._Clair%27s_Defeat" title="St. Clair&#39;s Defeat">attacked</a> the party, routing them and capturing part of their artillery and most of their supplies.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok71_92-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok71-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> Of St. Clair\'s force of 1,400 men, 600 were killed and 300 captured during the attack.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson234_91-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson234-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> The Kentucky militiamen scattered during the attack, and their leader, Colonel Oldham, was killed.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson234_91-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson234-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, they and most citizens in Kentucky blamed St. Clair for the entire debacle.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson234_91-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson234-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> St. Clair retreated to Fort Washington, and on November<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>24, Scott joined him there with 200 mounted volunteers in case the Indians decided to pursue him and invade Kentucky.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson235_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson235-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> When it became apparent that no Indian invasion was imminent, Scott\'s men returned home.<sup id="cite_ref-ward118_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward118-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> As a result of St. Clair\'s campaign, tribes that had previously been neutral in the conflict&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 including the <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Delawares</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Wyandots</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 allied with the Miami and Shawnee against the frontiersmen.<sup id="cite_ref-ward118_94-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward118-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_with_the_Legion_of_the_United_States">Service with the Legion of the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Service with the Legion of the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After <a href="/wiki/St._Clair%27s_Defeat" title="St. Clair&#39;s Defeat">St. Clair\'s Defeat</a>, President Washington asked Congress to authorize the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_the_United_States" title="Legion of the United States">Legion of the United States</a>, a 5,000-man force to fight the Indians in the Northwest.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson236_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson236-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Congress approved the proposal in March 1792, and Scott learned from a friend in Philadelphia that he was being considered as commander of the Legion.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson236_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson236-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately Washington concluded that he was "of inadequate abilities"; his known vice of drinking too much alcohol also concerned Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson236_95-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson236-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Instead, Washington chose "Mad" Anthony Wayne to command the Legion.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok71_92-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok71-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> On June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>4, 1792&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 just days after Kentucky officially gained statehood&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_General_Assembly" title="Kentucky General Assembly">Kentucky General Assembly</a> commissioned Scott and Benjamin Logan as major generals in the state militia.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson237_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson237-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> On June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>25, Scott was given command of the militia\'s 2nd Division, which was charged with operating north of the Kentucky River; Logan\'s 1st Division operated south of the river.<sup id="cite_ref-ward120_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward120-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The new state legislature had also appointed a five-man committee to select a city to be the new <a href="/wiki/State_capital" class="mw-redirect" title="State capital">state capital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward123_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward123-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Scott applied to have Petersburg, still a fledgling settlement, designated as the capital.<sup id="cite_ref-clark13_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark13-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Other localities&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 including Frankfort, <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington</a>, Louisville, and <a href="/wiki/Boonesborough,_Kentucky" title="Boonesborough, Kentucky">Boonesborough</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 also applied. Frankfort was eventually chosen,<sup id="cite_ref-ward123_98-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward123-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> as Scott\'s failure to secure Petersburg\'s designation as the state capital contributed more than anything else to the settlement\'s failure to even become a viable city.<sup id="cite_ref-ward123_98-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward123-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Scott\'s son, Charles, Jr., wrote to his brother Daniel that their father was planning to run for Congress in 1792; although Charles, Jr. expressed confidence that his father would be elected, his campaign apparently never materialized or faltered shortly after it began.<sup id="cite_ref-ward125_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward125-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> He was chosen as a <a href="/wiki/United_States_electoral_college" class="mw-redirect" title="United States electoral college">presidential elector</a> in 1793.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthony_Wayne,_uniform.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A man with white hair, wearing a blue jacket with gold lapels, buttons, and epaulets, a white shirt, and a black tie" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Anthony_Wayne%2C_uniform.jpg/220px-Anthony_Wayne%2C_uniform.jpg" width="220" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/Anthony_Wayne%2C_uniform.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="378" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthony_Wayne,_uniform.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Mad" Anthony Wayne, commander of the Legion of the United States</div></div></div>\n<p>Wayne originally intended to use Kentucky militiamen in preemptive strikes against the Indians and to conduct the main invasion using only federal troops, but by the time he moved to Fort Washington in mid-1793, he had assembled fewer than 3,000 of the 5,000 troops he had anticipated.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson239_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson239-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> He now requested that Scott\'s and Logan\'s men join his main force.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson240_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson240-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> Logan flatly refused to cooperate with a federal officer, but Scott eventually agreed, and Wayne commissioned him an officer in the federal army on July<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1, 1793.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson240_101-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson240-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> He and Governor Isaac Shelby instituted a draft to raise the 1,500 troops he was to command in Wayne\'s operation.<sup id="cite_ref-ward130_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward130-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> When he joined Wayne at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Jefferson_(Ohio)" title="Fort Jefferson (Ohio)">Fort Jefferson</a> on October<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>21, 1793, he had only been able to raise 1,000 men.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson241_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson241-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward131_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward131-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On November<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>4, Wayne ordered Scott\'s militiamen to destroy a nearby Delaware village.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson242_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson242-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> Still resentful and distrustful of federal officers and aware that Wayne would not launch a major offensive so close to winter, the men were not enthusiastic about the mission, which many of them considered trivial.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson242_105-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson242-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward134_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward134-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> That night, 501 of them deserted their camp, though Wayne noted in his report that he believed Scott and his officers had done all they could do to prevent the desertions.<sup id="cite_ref-ward134_106-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward134-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> Scott attempted to continue the mission with his remaining men, but inclement weather prevented him from conducting a major offensive.<sup id="cite_ref-ward134_106-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward134-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, the men were only able to disperse a small hunting camp before continuing on to Fort Washington and mustering out on November<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>10.<sup id="cite_ref-ward134_106-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward134-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> Wayne ordered Scott to return with a full quota of troops after the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson242_105-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson242-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tensions cooled between Wayne and the Kentuckians over the winter of 1793\xe2\x80\x9394.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson243_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson243-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> Wayne noticed that, despite their obstinance, the Kentucky volunteers appeared to be good soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson243_107-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson243-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> The militiamen, after observing Wayne, concluded that he&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 unlike Harmar and St. Clair&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 knew how to combat the Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson243_107-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson243-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> Wayne augmented his popularity in Kentucky by building <a href="/wiki/Fort_Recovery" title="Fort Recovery">Fort Recovery</a> over the winter on the site of St. Clair\'s defeat.<sup id="cite_ref-ward136_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward136-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> The Indians\' victory over St. Clair had become a part of their lore and inspired them to continue the fight against the western settlers; Wayne\'s construction of a fort on this site was a blow to the Indian psyche, and his re-burial of some 600 skulls that the Indians had dug up and scattered across the area was popular with Kentuckians, since many of their own were among the dead.<sup id="cite_ref-ward134_106-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward134-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> While Scott came to respect Wayne personally, his friend, James Wilkinson, began an anonymous campaign to tarnish Wayne\'s image, coveting command of the Northwest expedition for himself.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson244_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson244-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> Scott, on leave in Philadelphia at the time, wrote to <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">Secretary of War</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a> to defend Wayne\'s reputation, breaching his friendship with Wilkinson.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson244_109-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson244-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scott returned to Kentucky from Philadelphia in June 1794, mustered 1,500 militiamen, and joined Wayne at <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_Ohio" title="Greenville, Ohio">Fort Greeneville</a> on July<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>27.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson245_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson245-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> He and Thomas Barbee led this force in support of Wayne\'s 1,000 regular troops.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok71_92-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok71-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> The combined force marched quickly and captured the recently evacuated Indian town of Grand Glaize on August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>8.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson246_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson246-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> Here, Wayne ordered the construction of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Defiance_(Ohio)" title="Fort Defiance (Ohio)">Fort Defiance</a>, which took approximately a week.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson246_111-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson246-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> Scott was responsible for the naming of the fort; while observing its construction, he declared, "I defy the English, Indians, and all the devils in hell to take it."<sup id="cite_ref-nelson246_111-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson246-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> Based upon intelligence provided by Scott\'s mounted volunteers, Wayne ordered his force to march toward <a href="/wiki/Fort_Miami_(Ohio)" title="Fort Miami (Ohio)">Fort Miami</a> on August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>14, anticipating a battle with a combined British and Indian force of 2,400 there.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson247_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson247-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> About 8:45<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>a.m. on August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>20, Major William Price\'s brigade of volunteers engaged the Indian force near Fort Miami, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fallen_Timbers" title="Battle of Fallen Timbers">Battle of Fallen Timbers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson247_112-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson247-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> The well-positioned Indian force turned back Price\'s men, but Wayne ordered his regulars to conduct a vigorous <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonet</a> charge, which routed the Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson248_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson248-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> Major William Campbell, the British commander of Fort Miami, refused to open the fort to his Indian allies, and Wayne\'s force won a decisive victory.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson248_113-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson248-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the battle, Wayne ordered Scott\'s volunteers to conduct numerous raids within a fifty-mile radius of their position.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson249_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson249-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> Due to a lack of pack horses in Wayne\'s force, the mounted volunteers were also employed transporting supplies between forts throughout September 1794.<sup id="cite_ref-ward145_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward145-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup> They eventually grew weary of garrison duty and complained that the use of their personal horses to transport goods had injured the animals.<sup id="cite_ref-ward145_115-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward145-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup> Many threatened to <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">mutiny</a> if not discharged.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson249_114-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson249-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> On October<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>13, 1794, Wayne finally ordered them home.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson251_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson251-116">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup> In a commendation of Wayne issued on December<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>4, 1794, the U.S. House of Representatives specifically thanked Scott and his men for their service at the Battle of Fallen Timbers.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson250_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson250-117">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Greenville" title="Treaty of Greenville">Treaty of Greenville</a> formally ended the war in mid-1795.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson249_114-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson249-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_political_career">Later political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Later political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1795, Scott traveled to Philadelphia to help clarify service records that would determine the final pay of the men who served under him before returning to his farm in Kentucky.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup> He continued to serve, nominally, as major general of the 2nd militia division of the state militia until 1799.<sup id="cite_ref-ward152_119-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward152-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> Celebrations of Scott\'s military heroism were held all over Kentucky, sparking his interest in a political career.<sup id="cite_ref-ward152_119-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward152-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> With the advent of the <a href="/wiki/First_Party_System" title="First Party System">First Party System</a>, he declared himself a <a href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican_Party" title="Democratic-Republican Party">Democratic-Republican</a>, as did most Kentuckians.<sup id="cite_ref-ward152_119-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward152-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> In 1800, he was chosen as a presidential elector for his district by a vote of 75 to 44 over <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caleb_Wallace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Caleb Wallace (page does not exist)">Caleb Wallace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward152_119-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward152-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> Scott and his fellow electors all cast their votes for the ticket of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Burr" title="Aaron Burr">Aaron Burr</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward152_119-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward152-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1803, Secretary of War <a href="/wiki/Henry_Dearborn" title="Henry Dearborn">Henry Dearborn</a> appointed Scott and Governor <a href="/wiki/James_Garrard" title="James Garrard">James Garrard</a> to evaluate sites in Kentucky on which to construct a replacement for Fort Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-ward153_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward153-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> Garrard, a central Kentucky native, insisted that the fort should be built at Frankfort.<sup id="cite_ref-ward153_120-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward153-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> Scott disagreed, contending that the fort should not be in the state\'s interior and that the hilly terrain around Frankfort was unsuitable for constructing a fort.<sup id="cite_ref-ward153_120-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward153-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> He waited several days for an appointment with Garrard to try and reach an agreement, but when he was unable to secure one, he asked Dearborn for permission to act alone.<sup id="cite_ref-ward153_120-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward153-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> Dearborn granted the request and accepted Scott\'s recommendation of a site in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Kentucky" title="Newport, Kentucky">Newport, Kentucky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward153_120-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward153-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> In 1804, Scott was again chosen a presidential elector with minimal opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-ward155_121-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward155-121">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1797, Scott\'s son Daniel, who had settled in Virginia, died.<sup id="cite_ref-ward151_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward151-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> In late 1799 or early 1800, his last son, Charles, Jr., also died.<sup id="cite_ref-ward151_122-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward151-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> His daughter Martha married future U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/George_M._Bibb" title="George M. Bibb">George M. Bibb</a> in 1799 and moved to <a href="/wiki/Daviess_County,_Kentucky" title="Daviess County, Kentucky">Daviess County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward155_121-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward155-121">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> Daughter Mary had married and left the farm prior to Scott\'s return from military service, and youngest daughter Nancy left the farm near the turn of the 19th century, although she never married.<sup id="cite_ref-ward156_123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward156-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup> After the death of his wife on October<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>6, 1804, he moved in with his daughter and son-in-law, John and Mary Postlethwait, in Lexington.<sup id="cite_ref-ward155_121-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward155-121">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ward156_123-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward156-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup> He sold his farm in Woodford County in October 1805.<sup id="cite_ref-ward156_123-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward156-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As tensions between the U.S. and Great Britain escalated in the wake of the June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>22, 1807, <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake%E2%80%93Leopard_Affair" class="mw-redirect" title="Chesapeake\xe2\x80\x93Leopard Affair"><i>Chesapeake</i>\xe2\x80\x93<i>Leopard</i> Affair</a>, Scott applied to Governor Christopher Greenup to raise a mounted militia unit in anticipation of an outbreak of hostilities.<sup id="cite_ref-ward159_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward159-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> Although Greenup granted the requested authorization, Scott remarried on July<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>25, 1807, and never assembled the militia unit.<sup id="cite_ref-ward159_124-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward159-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> His second wife, Judith Cary (Bell) Gist, was the 57-year-old widow of Colonel <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Gist" title="Nathaniel Gist">Nathaniel Gist</a>, who had been a prisoner of war with Scott during the Revolutionary War.<sup id="cite_ref-ward159_124-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward159-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> After their marriage, they moved to Canewood, Gist\'s family\'s plantation in Bourbon and <a href="/wiki/Clark_County,_Kentucky" title="Clark County, Kentucky">Clark</a> counties.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gubernatorial_election_of_1808">Gubernatorial election of 1808</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gubernatorial election of 1808">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Green_Clay.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A man with gray hair and a ruddy complexion wearing a black jacket and frilly white vest" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/General_Green_Clay.jpg/170px-General_Green_Clay.jpg" width="170" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/General_Green_Clay.jpg/255px-General_Green_Clay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/41/General_Green_Clay.jpg 2x" data-file-width="317" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Green_Clay.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green Clay, one of Scott\'s opponents in the 1808 gubernatorial election</div></div></div>\n<p>As the celebrations in honor of Scott\'s military career continued across Kentucky, he began to consider the possibility of running for governor in 1808.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> By mid-1806, state senator <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Posey" title="Thomas Posey">Thomas Posey</a> and Lexington lawyer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Todd" title="Thomas Todd">Thomas Todd</a> had already declared their candidacies.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> Posey had been chosen speaker pro tem of the state Senate and, with the death of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Kentucky" title="Lieutenant Governor of Kentucky">Lieutenant Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Caldwell_(Kentucky_politician)" title="John Caldwell (Kentucky politician)">John Caldwell</a> in 1804, had assumed the role of acting lieutenant governor and presiding officer in the Senate.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> He subsequently lost his senatorial re-election bid, but continued to act as lieutenant governor and preside over the Senate.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> His opponents claimed that since he was no longer a member of the Senate, he was not qualified to act as lieutenant governor; additionally, they charged that he was sympathetic to the hated <a href="/wiki/Federalist_Party" title="Federalist Party">Federalist Party</a>, even though he self-identified as a Democratic-Republican.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> Although he was not successfully unseated as the Senate\'s presiding officer, the controversy diminished his chances in the 1808 election.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> In 1807, Todd removed himself from contention as well, accepting Governor Greenup\'s appointment to the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Court_of_Appeals" title="Kentucky Court of Appeals">Kentucky Court of Appeals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Posey\'s diminished candidacy and Todd\'s exit from the race left only one major impediment to Scott\'s potential candidacy.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> A movement began in Kentucky to draft former Governor Isaac Shelby as a candidate for another term.<sup id="cite_ref-ward158_125-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward158-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> Known as "Old King\'s Mountain" for his heroic role in the Revolutionary War <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_King%27s_Mountain" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of King&#39;s Mountain">Battle of King\'s Mountain</a>, Shelby could match Scott\'s military appeal, and as a former delegate to Kentucky\'s statehood and constitutional conventions and a former governor, his political experience far exceeded Scott\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-ward159_124-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward159-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately Shelby declined to run, and Scott officially declared his candidacy on February<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>11, 1808.<sup id="cite_ref-ward161_126-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward161-126">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Allen_(soldier)" title="John Allen (soldier)">John Allen</a> had by then declared his candidacy and <a href="/wiki/Green_Clay" title="Green Clay">Green Clay</a>\'s announcement followed Scott\'s by about a month.<sup id="cite_ref-ward161_126-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward161-126">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup> Scott\'s campaign was managed by his stepson-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Bledsoe" title="Jesse Bledsoe">Jesse Bledsoe</a>, a law professor at <a href="/wiki/Transylvania_University" title="Transylvania University">Transylvania University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs17_21-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs17-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-clark13_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark13-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Bledsoe was among the most able politicians in the state, though he preferred the role of "kingmaker" to that of candidate.<sup id="cite_ref-ward162_127-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward162-127">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Allen and Clay, both lawyers by profession, were hurt by a general distrust of lawyers by the Kentucky electorate.<sup id="cite_ref-ward163_128-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward163-128">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> Further, Allen had served as general counsel for <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Burr" title="Aaron Burr">Aaron Burr</a>, and several anonymous letters to the state\'s newspapers accused him of being privy to <a href="/wiki/Burr_conspiracy" title="Burr conspiracy">Burr\'s alleged scheme to create an independent state in the southwest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward163_128-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward163-128">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> was among those who vigorously defended Allen from the charges.<sup id="cite_ref-ward163_128-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward163-128">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> Scott also frequently spoke in highly complimentary terms of Allen.<sup id="cite_ref-clark13_62-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark13-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> As a legislator, Green Clay pushed for measures favorable to debtors; he consequently enjoyed strong support from settlers south of the <a href="/wiki/Green_River_(Kentucky)" title="Green River (Kentucky)">Green River</a>, many of whom were <a href="/wiki/Squatting" title="Squatting">squatters</a> and land speculators who owed substantial debts to the state.<sup id="cite_ref-ward164_129-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward164-129">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup> To counter Scott\'s hero image, Clay supporters pointed to his service with <a href="/wiki/George_Rogers_Clark" title="George Rogers Clark">George Rogers Clark</a> in a 1782 expedition against the Shawnee, but the impact of this line of campaigning was minimal.<sup id="cite_ref-ward165_130-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward165-130">&#91;130&#93;</a></sup> As the most senior Revolutionary War officer in Kentucky, Scott became the recognized leader of the state\'s veterans\' lobby.<sup id="cite_ref-ward162_127-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward162-127">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">Independence Day</a> celebrations held around the state just before the August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1 election provided a boost for his campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-ward166_131-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward166-131">&#91;131&#93;</a></sup> On election day, he garnered 22,050 votes, compared to 8,430 votes for Allen and 5,516 votes for Clay.<sup id="cite_ref-kye804_132-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kye804-132">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governorship">Governorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Governorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg/220px-Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg/330px-Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg/440px-Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="531" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Kentucky_Governor%27s_Mansion.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A fall on the icy steps of the governor\'s mansion crippled Scott for life.</div></div></div>\n<p>Among Scott\'s first acts as governor was appointing Jesse Bledsoe as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_of_Kentucky" title="Secretary of State of Kentucky">Secretary of State</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward170_133-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward170-133">&#91;133&#93;</a></sup> Bledsoe delivered Scott\'s first address to the legislature on December<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>13, 1808.<sup id="cite_ref-ward171_134-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward171-134">&#91;134&#93;</a></sup> Later that winter, Scott was injured when he slipped on the icy steps of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Governor%27s_Mansion_(Frankfort,_Kentucky)" title="Old Governor&#39;s Mansion (Frankfort, Kentucky)">governor\'s mansion</a>; the injury left him confined to crutches for the rest of his life and rendered him even more dependent on Bledsoe to perform many of his official functions.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs18_135-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs18-135">&#91;135&#93;</a></sup> His physical condition continued to worsen throughout his term as governor.<sup id="cite_ref-ward182_136-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward182-136">&#91;136&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In domestic matters, Scott advocated increased salaries for public officials, economic development measures, and heavy punishments for persistent criminals.<sup id="cite_ref-kye804_132-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kye804-132">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup> While he desired a tax code that would preclude the need for the state to borrow money, he encouraged legislators to keep taxes as low as possible.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs18_135-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs18-135">&#91;135&#93;</a></sup> He also urged them to convert the militia into a youth army.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs18_135-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs18-135">&#91;135&#93;</a></sup> The General Assembly routinely ignored his calls for reform but did pass a measure he advocated that allowed debtors a one-year stay on collection of their debts if they provided both <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Security_interest" title="Security interest">security</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs19_137-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs19-137">&#91;137&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scott frequently clashed with the legislature, including once when the Senate refused to confirm the appointment of Dr. Walter Brashear as lieutenant colonel commandant of the state militia\'s second regiment.<sup id="cite_ref-ward174_138-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward174-138">&#91;138&#93;</a></sup> The governor refused to nominate anyone else for the position, saying that Brashear was the best person for it, and he assumed the senators would not want to be sent a worse nomination.<sup id="cite_ref-ward174_138-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward174-138">&#91;138&#93;</a></sup> He employed his gubernatorial veto three times over the course of his tenure, but all three were overridden by the legislature.<sup id="cite_ref-ward175_139-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward175-139">&#91;139&#93;</a></sup> Measures creating <a href="/wiki/Harrison_County,_Kentucky" title="Harrison County, Kentucky">Harrison County</a> and allowing squatters to purchase occupied land on more favorable terms were both vetoed because Scott felt that they had been passed too hastily to allow proper debate.<sup id="cite_ref-ward175_139-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward175-139">&#91;139&#93;</a></sup> Scott also vetoed the revocation of a pension granted to recently retired Kentucky Court of Appeals justice <a href="/wiki/George_Muter" title="George Muter">George Muter</a>, because he felt it undermined citizens\' confidence in the promises of their government.<sup id="cite_ref-kygovs18_135-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kygovs18-135">&#91;135&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Throughout his term, Scott was dogged by rumors of heavy drinking and frequent use of profanity.<sup id="cite_ref-kye804_132-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kye804-132">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup> On one occasion, an unnamed individual believed his reputation had been injured by something Scott had said and challenged him to a <a href="/wiki/Duel" title="Duel">duel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-clark14_140-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark14-140">&#91;140&#93;</a></sup> He ignored the challenge, after which the challenger threatened to expose him as a coward.<sup id="cite_ref-clark14_140-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark14-140">&#91;140&#93;</a></sup> Scott was supposed to have replied, "Post and be damned; but if you do, you will only post yourself a damned liar, and everybody else will say so."<sup id="cite_ref-clark14_140-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark14-140">&#91;140&#93;</a></sup> On another occasion, after reviewing a speech written for him by Bledsoe, the governor was said to have remarked, "Well, Mr. Bledsoe, I know you think you are a damned sight smarter than I am, and so you are in many respects; but this message as it is now, won\'t do at all; I\'ll be damned if it will."<sup id="cite_ref-ward170_133-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward170-133">&#91;133&#93;</a></sup> When Bledsoe asked what was wrong with the speech, he reportedly replied, "Why, damn it to hell, why don\'t you put a good solemn prayer at the end of it, and talk about Providence, and the protection of Heaven, and all that?"<sup id="cite_ref-ward170_133-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward170-133">&#91;133&#93;</a></sup> After the governor campaigned for <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Marshall_(politician)" title="Humphrey Marshall (politician)">Humphrey Marshall</a>\'s opponent in the 1809 legislative elections, Marshall published an article in the <i>Western World</i> newspaper that accused him of appearing in front of the court house drunk on election day.<sup id="cite_ref-ward178_141-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward178-141">&#91;141&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For most of Scott\'s tenure as governor, tensions between the U.S. and Great Britain escalated.<sup id="cite_ref-ward180_142-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward180-142">&#91;142&#93;</a></sup> Sentiment in favor of a U.S. declaration of war against the British was particularly strong in Kentucky.<sup id="cite_ref-ward181_143-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward181-143">&#91;143&#93;</a></sup> Most Kentuckians resented the replacement of the <a href="/wiki/Embargo_Act_of_1807" title="Embargo Act of 1807">Embargo Act of 1807</a> with the weaker <a href="/wiki/Non-Intercourse_Act_(1809)" title="Non-Intercourse Act (1809)">Non-Intercourse Act of 1809</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macon%27s_Bill_Number_2" title="Macon&#39;s Bill Number 2">Macon\'s Bill Number 2</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward181_143-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward181-143">&#91;143&#93;</a></sup> Kentucky Senator Henry Clay became the acknowledged leader of the <a href="/wiki/War_hawk" title="War hawk">war hawks</a> in Congress.<sup id="cite_ref-ward181_143-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward181-143">&#91;143&#93;</a></sup> During an address to the General Assembly on December<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>4, 1810, Scott expressed little hope of peacefully resolving U.S. grievances against Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-ward183_144-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward183-144">&#91;144&#93;</a></sup> He reminded the General Assembly that France had also violated the United States\' maritime rights and urged equal treatment of the two countries for their offenses.<sup id="cite_ref-kye804_132-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kye804-132">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A slightly built man with brown hair wearing a black jacket with gold epaulets and leaf details, a white shirt, and a black tie, holding a sabre in his left hand" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg/170px-William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg" width="170" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg/255px-William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg/340px-William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg 2x" data-file-width="936" data-file-height="1156" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Henry_Harrison_by_Rembrandt_Peale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>William Henry Harrison, supreme commander of the Army of the Northwest</div></div></div>\n<p>In September 1811, <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Harrison" title="William Henry Harrison">William Henry Harrison</a>, then governor of <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Territory" title="Indiana Territory">Indiana Territory</a>, visited Kentucky and directed Colonel Samuel Wells to recruit Kentuckians for a new federal regiment then being formed by the authority of Secretary of War <a href="/wiki/William_Eustis" title="William Eustis">William Eustis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward184_145-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward184-145">&#91;145&#93;</a></sup> Harrison had not applied to Scott for permission to recruit in the state, and many Kentuckians&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 from Scott\'s political enemy, Humphrey Marshall, to his trusted advisor, Jesse Bledsoe&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 perceived this as a slight to the governor.<sup id="cite_ref-ward185_146-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward185-146">&#91;146&#93;</a></sup> Ignoring Bledsoe\'s indignation, Scott refused to make an issue of the <i>faux pas</i> and instead became one of the staunchest supporters of Harrison\'s rising career.<sup id="cite_ref-ward185_146-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward185-146">&#91;146&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 1811, a messenger brought news to Kentucky of former <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Kentucky" title="Attorney General of Kentucky">Kentucky Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Hamilton_Daveiss" title="Joseph Hamilton Daveiss">Joseph Hamilton Daveiss</a>\'s death at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tippecanoe" title="Battle of Tippecanoe">Battle of Tippecanoe</a>, amplifying Kentuckians\' outcry for war with the British and Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-clark15_147-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark15-147">&#91;147&#93;</a></sup> In anticipation of a federal call for volunteers, Scott published messages in the state\'s newspapers in February and April 1812 whipping up support for the impending war effort.<sup id="cite_ref-ward188_148-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward188-148">&#91;148&#93;</a></sup> By the end of July, the state\'s quota of 5,500 volunteers had been met.<sup id="cite_ref-ward189_149-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward189-149">&#91;149&#93;</a></sup> On August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>14, 1812, Scott greeted two regiments of soldiers at the governor\'s mansion just prior to their muster at <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Kentucky" title="Georgetown, Kentucky">Georgetown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward190_150-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward190-150">&#91;150&#93;</a></sup> He hobbled among the soldiers with his crutch, then turned and hammered it against the mansion\'s steps and was heard to mutter "If it hadn\'t been for you, I could have gone with the boys myself."<sup id="cite_ref-ward191_151-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward191-151">&#91;151&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On August<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>25, 1812, Scott\'s last day in office, he appointed Harrison brevet major general over the Kentucky militia.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok91_152-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok91-152">&#91;152&#93;</a></sup> The appointment was made on advice from incoming Governor Isaac Shelby and Henry Clay.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok91_152-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok91-152">&#91;152&#93;</a></sup> The brevet ensured that Harrison, and not <a href="/wiki/James_Winchester" title="James Winchester">James Winchester</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 who was unpopular in Kentucky and with his own troops&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 would lead the state\'s military forces in the war.<sup id="cite_ref-nhok91_152-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok91-152">&#91;152&#93;</a></sup> Biographer Harry M. Ward noted that Harrison\'s commission was unconstitutional both because he was not a citizen of the state and because the state militia\'s allotment of major generals had already been filled.<sup id="cite_ref-ward192_153-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward192-153">&#91;153&#93;</a></sup> Kentucky historian <a href="/wiki/Lowell_H._Harrison" title="Lowell H. Harrison">Lowell H. Harrison</a> concurred that the commission was "probably illegal", but further noted that it was "acclaimed across the state".<sup id="cite_ref-nhok91_152-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhok91-152">&#91;152&#93;</a></sup> The show of confidence from Scott and his aides influenced President <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> to appoint Harrison as supreme commander of the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Northwest_(United_States)" title="Army of the Northwest (United States)">Army of the Northwest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward192_153-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward192-153">&#91;153&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scott_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following his term as governor, Scott retired to his Canewood estate with his wife and youngest step-daughter, Mary Cecil Gist.<sup id="cite_ref-ward194_154-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward194-154">&#91;154&#93;</a></sup> Two of his stepdaughters had married during his term as governor.<sup id="cite_ref-ward194_154-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward194-154">&#91;154&#93;</a></sup>  In 1809, Anna Maria Gist married Captain <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_G._S._Hart" title="Nathaniel G. S. Hart">Nathaniel G. S. Hart</a>, who was killed in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Frenchtown" title="Battle of Frenchtown">River Raisin Massacre</a> in January 1813.<sup id="cite_ref-155" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-155">&#91;155&#93;</a></sup> Eliza Violet Gist married <a href="/wiki/Francis_Preston_Blair" title="Francis Preston Blair">Francis Preston Blair</a> on July<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>21, 1812, just prior to the expiration of Scott\'s term as governor.<sup id="cite_ref-ward194_154-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward194-154">&#91;154&#93;</a></sup> The governor opined that Blair, who was slightly built, stoop-shouldered, and suffering from <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, would leave Eliza a widow within six months.<sup id="cite_ref-ward194_154-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward194-154">&#91;154&#93;</a></sup> Blair survived the tuberculosis and went on to become a trusted advisor to President <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ward194_154-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward194-154">&#91;154&#93;</a></sup> He outlived Scott\'s prediction for him by more than sixty years.<sup id="cite_ref-ward194_154-5" class="reference"><a
