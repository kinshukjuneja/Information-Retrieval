/></a></div>\n<p>The history of <b>Wales in the Roman era</b> began in 48 AD with a military invasion by the imperial <a href="/wiki/Roman_governor" title="Roman governor">governor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>. The conquest would be completed by 78, and Roman rule would endure until the region was abandoned in AD 383. Once the conquest was complete, the region and the people living there would be a virtually anonymous part of Roman Britain until the Roman departure.\n</p><p>Roman rule in Wales was a military occupation, except for the southern coastal region of <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Gower_Peninsula" title="Gower Peninsula">Gower Peninsula</a>, where there is a legacy of <a href="/wiki/Romanization_(cultural)" title="Romanization (cultural)">Romanisation</a>, and some southern sites such as <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a>. The only town in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> founded by the Romans, <a href="/wiki/Caerwent" title="Caerwent">Caerwent</a>, is located in South Wales. Wales was a rich source of mineral wealth, and the Romans used their <a href="/wiki/Roman_engineering" title="Roman engineering">engineering</a> <a href="/wiki/Roman_technology" title="Roman technology">technology</a> to extract large amounts of gold, copper, and lead, as well as modest amounts of some other metals such as <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and silver.\n</p><p>It is the Roman campaigns of conquest that are most widely known, due to the spirited but unsuccessful defence of their homelands by two native tribes, the <a href="/wiki/Silures" title="Silures">Silures</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ordovices" title="Ordovices">Ordovices</a>. Aside from the many Roman-related finds along the southern coast, Roman archaeological remains in Wales consist almost entirely of military roads and fortifications.<sup id="cite_ref-1" the eve of the Roman invasion of Wales, the <a href="/wiki/Military_of_ancient_Rome" title="Military of ancient Rome">Roman military</a> under <a href="/wiki/Roman_governor" title="Roman governor">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Aulus_Plautius" title="Aulus Plautius">Aulus Plautius</a> was in control of all of southeastern Britain as well as <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>, perhaps including the lowland <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">English Midlands</a> as far as the <a href="/wiki/Dee_Estuary" title="Dee Estuary">Dee Estuary</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Mersey" title="River Mersey">River Mersey</a>, and having an understanding with the <a href="/wiki/Brigantes" title="Brigantes">Brigantes</a> to the north.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They controlled most of the islands centers of wealth, as well as much of its trade and resources.\n</p><p>In Wales the known tribes (the list may be incomplete) included the <a href="/wiki/Ordovices" title="Ordovices">Ordovices</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deceangli" title="Deceangli">Deceangli</a> in the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Silures" title="Silures">Silures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demetae" title="Demetae">Demetae</a> in the south. Archaeology combined with ancient Greek and Roman accounts have shown that there was exploitation of natural resources, such as copper, gold, tin, lead and silver at multiple locations in Britain, including in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Apart from this we have little knowledge of the Welsh tribes of this era.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasion_and_conquest">Invasion and conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Invasion and conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">Roman conquest of Britain</a></div>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg/240px-Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg" width="240" height="240" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg/360px-Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg/480px-Wales.Roman.Conquest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3076" data-file-height="3080" /></a></div>\n<p>In AD 47 or 48 the new governor, <a href="/wiki/Publius_Ostorius_Scapula" title="Publius Ostorius Scapula">Publius Ostorius Scapula</a>, moved against the Deceangli along the northeastern coast of Wales, devastating their lands.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He campaigned successfully but indecisively against the Silures and then the Ordovices, the most notable feature of which is the leadership of both tribes against him by <a href="/wiki/Caratacus" title="Caratacus">Caratacus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Scapula died in 52, the same year that the resurgent Silures inflicted a defeat on one of the Roman legions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Scapula was succeeded by a number of governors who made steady but inconclusive gains against the two tribes. <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Suetonius_Paulinus" title="Gaius Suetonius Paulinus">Gaius Suetonius Paulinus</a> was in the process of conquering <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> in AD 60 when the revolt led by <a href="/wiki/Boudica" title="Boudica">Boudica</a> in the east forced a delay in the final conquest of Wales.\n</p><p>There followed a decade of relative peace while Roman imperial attention was focused elsewhere. When expansion into Wales resumed in 73, Roman progress was steady and successful under <a href="/wiki/Frontinus" title="Frontinus">Sextus Julius Frontinus</a>, who decisively defeated the Silures,<sup id="cite_ref-Davies2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> followed by the success of <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gnaeus Julius Agricola</a> in defeating the Ordovices, and in completing the conquest of Anglesey in AD 77\xe2\x80\x9378.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is no indication of any Roman campaigns against the Demetae, and their territory was not planted with a series of forts, nor overlaid with roads, suggesting that they quickly made their peace with Rome. The main fort in their territory was at <a href="/wiki/Moridunum_(Carmarthen)" title="Moridunum (Carmarthen)">Moridunum</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a>), built around AD 75, and it eventually became the centre of a Roman <i><a href="/wiki/Civitas" title="Civitas">civitas</a></i>. The Demetae are the only pre-Roman Welsh tribe that would emerge from Roman rule with their tribal name intact.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales_in_Roman_Society">Wales in Roman Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wales in Roman Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg/220px-Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg" width="220" height="229" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg/330px-Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg/440px-Roman.Britain.Mining.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3136" data-file-height="3262" /></a></div>\n<p>The mineral wealth of Britain was well-known prior to the Roman invasion and was one of the expected benefits of conquest. All mineral extractions were state-sponsored and under military control, as mineral rights belonged to the emperor.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> His agents soon found substantial deposits of gold, copper, and lead in Wales, along with some <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and silver. Gold was mined at <a href="/wiki/Dolaucothi_Gold_Mines" title="Dolaucothi Gold Mines">Dolaucothi</a> prior to the invasion, but <a href="/wiki/Roman_engineering" title="Roman engineering">Roman engineering</a> would be applied to greatly increase the amount extracted, and to extract huge amounts of the other metals. This would continue until the process was no longer practical or profitable, at which time the mine would be abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones3_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones3-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Modern scholars have made efforts to quantify the value of these extracted metals to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_economy" title="Roman economy">Roman economy</a>, and to determine the point at which the Roman occupation of Britain was "profitable" to the Empire. While these efforts have not produced deterministic results, the benefits to Rome were substantial. The gold production at Dolaucothi alone may have been of economic significance.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_production">Industrial production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industrial production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman.Britain.Production.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Roman.Britain.Production.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Roman.Britain.Production.jpg/220px-Roman.Britain.Production.jpg" width="220" height="233" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Roman.Britain.Production.jpg/330px-Roman.Britain.Production.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Roman.Britain.Production.jpg/440px-Roman.Britain.Production.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2700" data-file-height="2862" /></a></div>\n<p>The production of goods for trade and export in Roman Britain was concentrated in the south and east, with virtually none situated in Wales.\n</p><p>This was largely due to circumstance, with iron forges located near iron supplies, <a href="/wiki/Pewter" title="Pewter">pewter</a> (tin with some lead or copper) moulds located near the tin supplies and suitable soil (for the moulds), clusters of <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kilns</a> located near suitable clayey soil, grain-drying ovens located in agricultural areas where sheep raising (for wool) was also located, and salt production concentrated in its historical pre-Roman locations. Glass-making sites were located in or near urban centres.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones3_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones3-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Wales none of the needed materials were available in suitable combination, and the forested, mountainous countryside was not amenable to this kind of industrialisation.\n</p><p>Clusters of <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tileries</a>, both large and small, were at first operated by the Roman military to meet their own needs, and so there were temporary sites wherever the army went and could find suitable soil. This included a few places in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, as Roman influence grew, the army was able to obtain tiles from civilian sources who located their kilns in the lowland areas containing good soil, and then shipped the tiles to wherever they were needed.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanisation">Romanisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Romanisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg/220px-Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg" width="220" height="233" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg/330px-Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg/440px-Roman.Britain.Romanisation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="3176" /></a></div>\n<p>The Romans occupied the whole of the area now known as Wales, where they built <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads" title="Roman roads">Roman roads</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">castra</a></i>, mined gold at <a href="/wiki/Luentinum" title="Luentinum">Luentinum</a> and conducted commerce, but their interest in the area was limited because of the difficult geography and shortage of flat agricultural land. Most of the Roman remains in Wales are military in nature. <a href="/wiki/Sarn_Helen" title="Sarn Helen">Sarn Helen</a>, a major highway, linked the North with South Wales.\n</p><p>The area was controlled by <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman legionary</a> bases at <a href="/wiki/Deva_Victrix" title="Deva Victrix">Deva Victrix</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Isca_Augusta" title="Isca Augusta">Isca Augusta</a> (<a href="/wiki/Caerleon" title="Caerleon">Caerleon</a>), two of the three such bases in Roman Britain, with roads linking these bases to <a href="/wiki/Auxiliaries_(Roman_military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliaries (Roman military)">auxiliaries\'</a> forts such as <a href="/wiki/Segontium" title="Segontium">Segontium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Caernarfon" title="Caernarfon">Caernarfon</a>) and Moridunum (<a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a>).\n</p><p>The best indicators of Romanising acculturation is the presence of urban sites (areas with towns, <i><a href="/wiki/Colonia_(Roman)" title="Colonia (Roman)">coloniae</a></i>, and tribal <i><a href="/wiki/Civitas" title="Civitas">civitates</a></i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Villa" title="Villa">villas</a></i> in the countryside. In Wales, this can be said only of the southeasternmost coastal region of <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>. The only <i>civitates</i> in Wales were at <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caerwent" title="Caerwent">Caerwent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> There were three small urban sites near Caerwent, and these and <a href="/wiki/Blestium" title="Blestium">Roman Monmouth</a> were the only other "urbanised" sites in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the southwestern homeland of the <a href="/wiki/Demetae" title="Demetae">Demetae</a>, several sites have been classified as <i>villas</i> in the past,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> but excavation of these and examination of sites as yet unexcavated suggest that they are pre-Roman family homesteads, sometimes updated through Roman technology (such as stone masonry), but having a native character quite different than the true Roman-derived <i>villas</i> that are found to the east, such as in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Perhaps surprisingly, the presence of Roman-era Latin inscriptions is not suggestive of Romanisation. They are most numerous at military sites, and their occurrence elsewhere depended on access to suitable stone and the presence of stonemasons, as well as patronage. The Roman fort complex at <a href="/wiki/Tomen_y_Mur" title="Tomen y Mur">Tomen y Mur</a> near the coast of northwestern Wales has produced more inscriptions than either <a href="/wiki/Segontium" title="Segontium">Segontium</a> (near modern Caernarfon) or <a href="/wiki/Noviomagus_Reginorum" title="Noviomagus Reginorum">Noviomagus Reginorum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chichester" title="Chichester">Chichester</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hill_forts">Hill forts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hill forts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In areas of civil control, such as the territories of a <i><a href="/wiki/Civitas" title="Civitas">civitas</a></i>, the fortification and occupation of <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> was banned as a matter of Roman policy. However, further inland and northward, a number of pre-Roman hill forts continued to be used in the Roman Era, while others were abandoned during the Roman Era, and still others were newly occupied. The inference is that local leaders who were willing to accommodate Roman interests were encouraged and allowed to continue, providing local leadership under local law and custom.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is virtually no evidence to shed light on the practice of religion in Wales during the Roman era, save the anecdotal account of the strange appearance and bloodthirsty customs of the <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">druids</a> of Anglesey by <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> during the conquest of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It is fortunate for Rome\'s reputation that Tacitus described the druids as horrible, else it would be a story of the Roman massacre of defenceless, unarmed men and women. The likelihood of partisan propaganda and an appeal to salacious interests combine to suggest that the account merits suspicion.\n</p><p>The Welsh region of Britain was not significant to the Romanisation of the island and contains almost no buildings related to religious practice, save where the Roman military was located, and these reflect the practices of non-native soldiers. Any native religious sites would have been constructed of wood that has not survived and so are difficult to locate anywhere in Britain, let alone in mountainous, forest-covered Wales.\n</p><p>The time of the arrival of Christianity to Wales is unknown. Archaeology suggests that it came to Roman Britain slowly, gaining adherents among coastal merchants and in the upper classes first, and never becoming widespread outside of the southeast in the Roman Era.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> There is also evidence of a preference for non-Christian devotion in parts of Britain, such as in the upper regions of the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Estuary" title="Severn Estuary">Severn Estuary</a> in the 4th century, from the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">River Wye</a> continuously around the coast of the estuary, up to and including <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></i>, written c. 540, <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> provides a story of the martyrdom of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Alban" title="Saint Alban">Saint Alban</a> at <a href="/wiki/Verulamium" title="Verulamium">Verulamium</a>, and of <a href="/wiki/Julius_and_Aaron" title="Julius and Aaron">Julius and Aaron</a> at <i>Legionum Urbis</i>, the \'City of the Legion\', saying that this occurred during a persecution of Christians at a time when \'decrees\' against them were issued.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a> repeats the story in his <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Ecclesiastical History</a></i>, written c. 731.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The otherwise unspecified \'City of the Legion\' is arguably <a href="/wiki/Caerleon" title="Caerleon">Caerleon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> <i>Caerllion</i>, the \'Fortress of the Legion\', and the only candidate with a long and continuous military presence that lay within a Romanised region of Britain, with nearby towns and a Roman <i><a href="/wiki/Civitas" title="Civitas">civitas</a></i>. Other candidates are <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carlisle" title="Carlisle">Carlisle</a>, though both were located far from the Romanised area of Britain and had a transitory, more military-oriented history.\n</p><p>A parenthetical note concerns <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a>, a patron saint of Ireland. He was a <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Briton</a> born c. 387 in <i>Banna Venta Berniae</i>, a location that is unknown due to the transcription errors in surviving manuscripts. His home is a matter of conjecture, with sites near <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a> farvoured by some,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> while coastal <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a> is favoured by others.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_settlement">Irish settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Irish settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg/220px-Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg" width="220" height="279" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg/330px-Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg/440px-Britain.Deisi.Laigin.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1880" data-file-height="2386" /></a></div>\n<p>By the middle of the 4th century the Roman presence in Britain was no longer vigorous. Once-unfortified towns were now being surrounded by defensive walls, including both <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caerwent" title="Caerwent">Caerwent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Political control finally collapsed and a number of alien tribes then took advantage of the situation, raiding widely throughout the island, joined by Roman soldiers who had deserted and by elements of the native Britons themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Order was restored in 369, but Roman Britain would not recover.\n</p><p>It was at this time<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> that Wales received an infusion of settlers from southern Ireland, the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_Liath%C3%A1in" title="U\xc3\xad Liath\xc3\xa1in">U\xc3\xad Liath\xc3\xa1in</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laigin" title="Laigin">Laigin</a>, and possibly <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9isi" title="D\xc3\xa9isi">D\xc3\xa9isi</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> the last no longer seen as certain, with only the first two verified by reliable sources and place-name evidence. The Irish were concentrated along the southern and western coasts, in Anglesey and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a> (excepting the <a href="/wiki/Cantref" title="Cantref">cantrefi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cantref_Arfon" title="Cantref Arfon">Arfon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arllechwedd" title="Arllechwedd">Arllechwedd</a>), and in the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Demetae" title="Demetae">Demetae</a>.\n</p><p>The circumstances of their arrival are unknown, and theories include categorising them as "raiders", as "invaders" who established a hegemony, and as "<a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">foederati</a>" invited by the Romans. It might as easily have been the consequence of a depopulation in Wales caused by plague or famine, both of which were usually ignored by ancient chroniclers.\n</p><p>What is known is that their characteristically Irish circular huts are found where they settled; that the inscription stones found in Wales, whether in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ogham_inscription" title="Ogham inscription">ogham</a> or both, are characteristically Irish; that when both Latin and ogham are present on a stone, the name in the Latin text is given in <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">Brittonic</a> form while the same name is given in Irish form in ogham;<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and that medieval Welsh royal genealogies include Irish-named ancestors<sup id="cite_ref-Phil1_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phil1-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Phil2_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phil2-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> who also appear in the native Irish narrative <i><a href="/wiki/The_Expulsion_of_the_D%C3%A9isi" title="The Expulsion of the D\xc3\xa9isi">The Expulsion of the D\xc3\xa9isi</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This phenomenon may however be the result of later influences and again only the presence of the U\xc3\xad Liath\xc3\xa1in and Laigin in Wales has been verified.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_Roman_era">End of the Roman era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: End of the Roman era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Southern_wall_of_the_Roman_city_of_Venta_Silurum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1162370.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Southern_wall_of_the_Roman_city_of_Venta_Silurum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1162370.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roman Walls at Caerwent (<a href="/wiki/Venta_Silurum" title="Venta Silurum">Venta Silurum</a>), erected c. 350.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/End_of_Roman_rule_in_Britain" title="End of Roman rule in Britain">End of Roman rule in Britain</a></div>\n<p>Historical accounts tell of the upheavals in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> during the 3rd and 4th centuries, with notice of the withdrawal of troops from <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> in support of the imperial ambitions of Roman generals stationed there. In much of Wales, where Roman troops were the only indication of Roman rule, that rule ended when troops left and did not return. The end came to different regions at different times.\n</p><p>Tradition holds that Roman customs held on for several years in southern Wales, lasting into the end of the 5th century, and that is true in part. Caerwent continued to be occupied after the Roman departure, while Carmarthen was probably abandoned in the late 4th century.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In addition, southwestern Wales was the tribal territory of the Demetae, who had never become thoroughly Romanised. The entire region of southernmost and southwestern Wales had been settled by Irish newcomers in the late 4th century, and it seems far-fetched to suggest that they were ever fully Romanised.\n</p><p><b>Magnus Maximus</b>\n</p>\n<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/38px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png" width="38" height="40" class="noviewer" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/57px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/76px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="410" data-file-height="430" /></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikisource" title="Wikisource">Wikisource</a> has original text related to this article:\n<div style="margin-left: 10px;"><b><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Mabinogion/The_Dream_of_Maxen_Wledig" class="extiw" title="wikisource:The Mabinogion/The Dream of Maxen Wledig">The Dream of Maxen Wledig</a></b></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>In Welsh literary tradition, <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Maximus" title="Magnus Maximus">Magnus Maximus</a> is the central figure in the emergence of a free Britain in the post-Roman era. Royal and religious genealogies compiled in the Middle Ages have him as the ancestor of kings and saints.<sup id="cite_ref-Phil1_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phil1-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Phil2_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phil2-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In the Welsh story of <i>Breuddwyd Macsen Wledig</i> (<i>The Dream of Emperor Maximus</i>), he is Emperor of Rome and marries a wondrous British woman, telling her that she may name her desires, to be received as a wedding portion. She asks that her father be given sovereignty over Britain, thus formalising the transfer of authority from Rome back to the Britons themselves.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg/150px-Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg" width="150" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg/225px-Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg/300px-Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="402" data-file-height="567" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pillar_of_Eliseg.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Remains of the <a href="/wiki/Pillar_of_Eliseg" title="Pillar of Eliseg">Pillar of Eliseg</a> near the town of <a href="/wiki/Llangollen" title="Llangollen">Llangollen</a>, Wales, erected c. 855. It lists Magnus Maximus as an ancestor of a medieval Welsh king.</div></div></div>\n<p>Historically Magnus Maximus was a Roman general who served in Britain in the late 4th century, launching his successful bid for imperial power from Britain in 383. This is the last date for any evidence of a Roman military presence in Wales, the western <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a>, and Deva (i.e., the entire non-Romanised region of Britain south of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a>). Coins dated later than 383 have been excavated along the Wall, suggesting that troops were not stripped from it, as was once thought.<sup id="cite_ref-Frere1_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frere1-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></i> written c. 540, <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> says that Maximus left Britain not only with all of its Roman troops, but also with all of its armed bands, governors, and the flower of its youth, never to return.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Having left with the troops and senior administrators, and planning to continue as the ruler of Britain, his practical course was to transfer local authority to local rulers. Welsh legend provides a mythic story that says he did exactly that.\n</p><p>After he became emperor of the Western Roman Empire, Maximus would return to Britain to campaign against the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> and Scots (i.e., Irish), probably in support of Rome\'s long-standing allies the <a href="/wiki/Damnonii" title="Damnonii">Damnonii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Votadini" title="Votadini">Votadini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Novantae" title="Novantae">Novantae</a> (all located in modern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>). While there he likely made similar arrangements for a formal transfer of authority to local chiefs: the later rulers of <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a>, home to the Novantae, would claim Maximus as the founder of their line, the same as did the Welsh kings.<sup id="cite_ref-Frere1_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frere1-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Maximus would rule the Roman West until he was killed in 388. A succession of governors would rule southeastern Britain until 407, but there is nothing to suggest that any Roman effort was made to regain control of the west or north after 383, and that year would be the definitive end of the Roman era in Wales.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Roman_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Wendy_Davies" title="Wendy Davies">Wendy Davies</a> has argued that the later medieval Welsh approach to property and estates was a Roman legacy, but this issue and others related to legacy are not yet resolved. For example, <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Alcock" title="Leslie Alcock">Leslie Alcock</a> has argued that that approach to property and estates cannot pre-date the 6th century and is thus post-Roman.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was little <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> linguistic heritage left to the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh language</a>, only a number of borrowings from the Latin <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a>. With the absence of early written Welsh sources there is no way of knowing when these borrowings were incorporated into Welsh, and may date from a later post-Roman era when the language of literacy was still Latin. Borrowings include a few common words and word forms. For example, Welsh <i>ffenestr</i> is from Latin <i>fenestra</i>, \'window\'; <i>llyfr</i> is from <i>liber</i>, \'book\'; <i>ysgrif</i> is from <i>scribo</i>, \'scribe\'; and the suffix <i>-wys</i> found in Welsh folk names is derived from the Latin suffix <i>-\xc4\x93ns\xc4\x93s</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> There are a few military terms, such as <i>caer</i> from Latin <i>castra</i>, \'fortress\'. <i>Eglwys</i>, meaning \'church\', is ultimately derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>kl\xc4\x93ros</i>.\n</p><p>Welsh kings would later use the authority of <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Maximus" title="Magnus Maximus">Magnus Maximus</a> as the basis of their inherited political legitimacy. While imperial Roman entries in Welsh royal genealogies lack any historical foundation, they serve to illustrate the belief that legitimate royal authority began with Magnus Maximus. As told in <i>The Dream of Emperor Maximus</i>, Maximus married a Briton, and their supposed children are given in genealogies as the ancestors of kings. Tracing ancestries back further, Roman emperors are listed as the sons of earlier Roman emperors, thus incorporating many famous Romans (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I">Constantine the Great</a>) into the royal genealogies.\n</p><p>The kings of medieval <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a> trace their origins to the northern British kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Manaw_Gododdin" title="Manaw Gododdin">Manaw Gododdin</a> (located in modern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>), and they also claim a connection to Roman authority in their genealogies ("Eternus son of Paternus son of Tacitus"). This claim may be either an independent one, or was perhaps an invention intended to rival the legitimacy of kings claiming descent from the historical Maximus.\n</p><p>Gwyn A. Williams argues that even at the time of the erection of <a href="/wiki/Offa%27s_Dyke" title="Offa&#39;s Dyke">Offa\'s Dyke</a> (that divided Wales from medieval England) the people to its west saw themselves as "Roman", citing the number of Latin inscriptions still being made into the 8th century.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
