class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CRBP-_CHINOOK_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Columbia River Bar <a href="/wiki/Pilot_boat" title="Pilot boat">pilot boat</a> <i>CHINOOK</i></div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>maritime pilot</b>, also known as a <b>marine pilot</b>, <b>harbor pilot</b> or <b>bar pilot</b> and sometimes simply called a <b>pilot</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">sailor</a> who maneuvers <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> through dangerous or congested waters, such as <a href="/wiki/Harbor" title="Harbor">harbors</a> or <a href="/wiki/River_mouth" title="River mouth">river mouths</a>. They are normally a former ship captain and a highly experienced shiphandler possessing detailed knowledge of the particular waterway, e.g. actual depth, direction and strength of the wind, current and tide at any time of the day. The pilot is a navigational expert for the port of call. \n</p><p>Maneuvering a ship through the shallow water to berth / unberth in a port requires teamwork which involves, apart from the port pilot, the ship\'s captain (jointly responsible), ship\'s crew, port tugs, and shore linesmen. Since the pilot is on board the ship, they control the tugs and linesmen through a radio, and the ship directly. The ship\'s captain ensures their crew carry out the pilot\'s orders.\n</p><p>High skill is required to be a pilot as the channels through which the ships move towards the port are  normally too narrow and shallow for the size of the ships, with the stopping distance of the ships being only a few nautical miles and the fact that ships do not steer at slow speed. Even if a ship captain is a regular visitor to a certain port, they can not match the expertise and experience of the Pilot.\n</p><p>In an unfortunate case of an accident, high pollution is a risk as a ship may carry thousands of tonnes of fuel for her own consumption.(Clean up cost and other damages of the <a href="/wiki/Exxon_Valdez" title="Exxon Valdez">Exxon Valdez</a> disaster was around $5 billion).\nAlso if a ship is wrecked in the channel, the channel and the port could be closed for months until the shipwreck is removed.\n</p><p>Most ports have compulsory pilotage.\n</p><p>Legally, the master has full responsibility for safe navigation of their vessel, even if a pilot is on board. If they have clear grounds that the pilot may jeopardize the safety of navigation, they can relieve the pilot from their duties and ask for another pilot or, if not compulsory to have a pilot on board, navigate the vessel without one. Only in transit of the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a> and in Canada does the pilot have the full responsibility for the navigation of the vessel.  \n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a>, Section 742 of the Merchant Shipping Act 1894 defines a pilot as "any person not belonging to a ship who has the conduct thereof." In other words, someone other than a member of the crew who has control over the speed, direction, and movement of the ship. The current <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> legislation governing pilotage is the <a href="/wiki/Pilotage_Act_1987" title="Pilotage Act 1987">Pilotage Act 1987</a>.\n</p><p>Pilotage is one of the oldest professions, as old as sea travel, and it is one of the most important in maritime safety. Lagos using nautical charts</div></div></div>\n<p>The work functions of the pilot go back to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times, when locally experienced <a href="/wiki/Harbour" class="mw-redirect" title="Harbour">harbour</a> captains, mainly local <a href="/wiki/Fishermen" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishermen">fishermen</a>, were employed by incoming ships\' captains to bring their trading vessels into port safely. Eventually, because the act of pilotage needed to be regulated and to ensure that pilots had adequate insurance, the harbours licensed pilots.  The <a href="/wiki/California_Board_of_Pilot_Commissioners" title="California Board of Pilot Commissioners">California Board of Pilot Commissioners</a> was the first government agency created by California\'s legislature, in 1850.\n</p><p>Before harbour boards were established, pilots known as <b>hobblers</b> would compete with one another.  The first to reach an incoming ship would navigate it to the docks and receive payment.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAn_Laoghaire" title="D\xc3\xban Laoghaire">D\xc3\xban Laoghaire</a>, Ireland, there is a monument to the hobblers who lost their lives.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> they were known as "hovellers" and worked alongside and in competition with the licensed pilots, but were sometimes blamed as <a href="/wiki/Wrecking_(shipwreck)" title="Wrecking (shipwreck)">wreckers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> George Byng Gattie defends the hovellers or "hobilers" as lifesavers in his 1890 book about the <a href="/wiki/Goodwin_Sands" title="Goodwin Sands">Goodwin Sands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although licensed by the harbour to operate within their jurisdiction, pilots were generally self-employed, and so had to have quick transport to get from the port to the incoming ships. As pilots were often still dual-employed, they used their own <a href="/wiki/Fishing_boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing boat">fishing boats</a> to reach the incoming vessels. But fishing boats were heavy working boats, and filled with fishing equipment, hence a new type of boat was required.\n</p><p>Early boats were developed from single-masted <a href="/wiki/Cutter_(boat)" title="Cutter (boat)">cutters</a> and twin-masted <a href="/wiki/Yawl" title="Yawl">yawls</a>, and later into the specialist <a href="/wiki/Pilot_cutter" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilot cutter">pilot cutter</a>. These were effectively light-weight and over-powered single masted boats with large steeply angled keels, making them deep <a href="/wiki/Draft_(hull)" title="Draft (hull)">draft</a> under power and shallow draft in lighter sail. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Henderson_(pilot)" title="Joseph Henderson (pilot)">Joseph Henderson</a> was an early American harbor pilot. He is well known for being a <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook" title="Sandy Hook">Sandy Hook</a> Pilot for the New York harbor and along the Atlantic Coast during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>. \n</p><p>Some historic pilot vessels are still sailing:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Eighteen <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel_Pilot_Cutter" class="mw-redirect" title="Bristol Channel Pilot Cutter">Bristol Channel Pilot Cutters</a> are believed to survive worldwide. the stopping distance of a <a href="/wiki/Supertanker" class="mw-redirect" title="Supertanker">supertanker</a> is typically measured in miles/kilometers and even a slight error in judgment can cause <a href="/wiki/Million" class="mw-redirect" title="Million">millions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dollar" title="Dollar">dollars</a> in damage. For this reason, many years of experience in an operating area are required to qualify as a pilot.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  For example, the <a href="/wiki/California_Board_of_Pilot_Commissioners" title="California Board of Pilot Commissioners">California Board of Pilot Commissioners</a> requires that pilot trainees must have a master\'s license, two years command experience on tugs or deep draft vessels, and pass a written exam and simulator exercise, followed by a period of up to three years training gaining experience with all types of vessel and docking facilities.  Following licensing, pilots are required to engage in extensive continuing educational programs.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By far the most challenging part of any ship\'s voyage is the passage through the narrow waterways that lead to <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a> and the final <a href="/wiki/Dock_(maritime)" title="Dock (maritime)">docking</a> of the ship. The pilot brings to the ship expertise in handling large vessels in confined waterways and expert local knowledge of the port. In addition to bringing local maritime expertise on board, unlike the vessel\'s captain the pilot is insulated from the economic pressures (e.g., getting the ship from point A to point B on time, regardless of weather conditions, traffic, or other navigation issues) that can compromise safety. Instead of being part of the ship\'s crew, pilots are employed locally and therefore act on behalf of the public rather than of the shipowners.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. helicopter or <a href="/wiki/Pilot_boat" title="Pilot boat">pilot boat</a> and climbs a <a href="/wiki/Pilot_ladder" title="Pilot ladder">pilot ladder</a> sometimes up to 40 feet (~12 metres) to the <a href="/wiki/Deck_(ship)" title="Deck (ship)">deck</a> of the largest <a href="/wiki/Container_ship" title="Container ship">container</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tank_ship" class="mw-redirect" title="Tank ship">tanker</a> ships. Climbing the pilot ladder can be dangerous, even more so in rough seas considering that both the ship to be piloted and the pilot\'s own vessel are usually both moving. With outgoing vessels, a pilot boat returns the pilot to land after the ship has successfully negotiated coastal waters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pilots specifically use <a href="/wiki/Pilotage" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilotage">pilotage</a> techniques relying on nearby visual reference points and local knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>, swells, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents</a>, depths and <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">shoals</a> that might not be readily identifiable on <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">nautical charts</a> without first hand experience in the waters in question.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Beyond the experience and training of regular ships\' captains, pilots also receive special, ongoing training to stay on top of their profession. Pilots are required by law in most major sea ports of the world for large ships.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.\nIn every case, during the time passed aboard for operation, the pilot will remain under the Master\'s authority, and always out of "ship\'s command chain". The pilot remains aboard as an important and indispensable -Consultant of the Master-.\n</p><p>However, in some countries, Masters (and sometimes other ranks too) of ships that call at only a few ports and so have strong local knowledge and experience of navigating in those ports, such as a <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a>, may be issued with a <b>pilotage exemption certificate</b>, which relieves them of the need to take a pilot on board.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
