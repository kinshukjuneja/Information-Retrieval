&#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/\xc9\x99n/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">\xc9\x99n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">LAHN</span>-s\xc9\x99n</i></a>, (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Lannstevan</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (rarely spelled <b>Lanson</b>  as a local abbreviation) is a town, ancient borough, and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It is one mile (1.6&#160;km) west of the middle stage of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">River Tamar</a>, which constitutes almost the entire border between <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>. The landscape of the town is generally steep particularly at a sharp south-western knoll topped by <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Castle" title="Launceston Castle">Launceston Castle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These gradients fall down to the <a href="/wiki/River_Kensey" title="River Kensey">River Kensey</a> and smaller tributaries.\n</p><p>The town centre itself is bypassed and is no longer physically a main thoroughfare. The <a href="/wiki/A388_road" title="A388 road">A388</a> still runs through the town close to the centre. The town remains figuratively the "gateway to Cornwall", due to having the <a href="/wiki/A30_road_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="A30 road (England)">A30</a>, one of the two dual carriageways into the county pass directly next to the town. The other dual carriageway and alternative main point of entry is at <a href="/wiki/Saltash" title="Saltash">Saltash</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Tamar_Bridge" title="Tamar Bridge">Tamar Bridge</a> and was completed in 1962. There are smaller points of entry to Cornwall on minor roads.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Launceston_Steam_Railway" title="Launceston Steam Railway">Launceston Steam Railway</a> <a href="/wiki/Narrow_gauge_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrow gauge railway">narrow-gauge</a> <a href="/wiki/Heritage_railway" title="Heritage railway">heritage railway</a> runs as a tourist attraction during the summer months. It was restored for aesthetic and industrial heritage purposes and runs along a short rural route, it is popular with visitors but does not run for much of the year.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Launceston_Castle" title="Launceston Castle">Launceston Castle</a> was built by <a href="/wiki/Robert,_Count_of_Mortain" title="Robert, Count of Mortain">Robert, Count of Mortain</a> (half-brother of William the Conqueror) <abbr title="circa (about)">c.</abbr> 1070 to control the surrounding area. Launceston was the <i><a href="/wiki/Caput" title="Caput">caput</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Feudal_barony_of_Launceston" title="Feudal barony of Launceston">feudal barony of Launceston</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Cornwall" title="Earl of Cornwall">Earldom of Cornwall</a> until replaced by <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel" title="Lostwithiel">Lostwithiel</a> in the 13th century. Launceston was later the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> of Cornwall until 1835 when <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a> replaced it.\n</p><p>Two civil parishes serve the town and its outskirts, of which the central more built-up administrative unit housed 8,952 residents at the 2011 census.<sup id="cite_ref-oldname_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldname-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Three <a href="/wiki/Wards_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Wards of the United Kingdom">electoral wards</a> include reference to the town, their total population, from 2011 census data, being 11,837 and two ecclesiastical parishes serve the former single parish, with three churches and a large swathe of land to the north and west part of the area.\n</p><p>Launceston\'s motto <i lang="fr" title="French language text">"Royale et Loyale"</i> (English translation: Royal and Loyal) is a reference to its adherence to the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Cavalier</a> cause during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> of the mid-17th century. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Cornish name of "Launceston", <i>Lannstevan</i>, means the "church enclosure of St Stephen" and is derived from the former <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> at St Stephen\'s a few miles north-west (the castle and town were originally named Dunheved) and the <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a> placename element <a href="/wiki/Llan_(placename_element)" class="mw-redirect" title="Llan (placename element)">lan-</a>. Dunheved was the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_Brittonic_languages" title="Southwestern Brittonic languages">Southwestern Brittonic</a> name for the town in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Cornwall#Relationship_with_Wessex" title="History of Cornwall">West Saxon</a> period.\n</p><p>The earliest known Cornish mint was at Launceston, which operated on a minimal scale at the time of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_the_Unready" title="\xc3\x86thelred the Unready">\xc3\x86thelred the Unready</a> before Cornwall received full diocesan jurisdiction in the year 994 AD. Only one specimen is known to exist. In the reign of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>, the mint was moved to Dunheved and remained in existence until the reign of Henry II, 1160.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III of England</a>, another <a href="/wiki/Mint_(coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mint (coin)">mint</a> was established in Launceston.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Launceston_Castle" title="Launceston Castle">Launceston Castle</a>, in good repair, is a <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey_castle" title="Motte-and-bailey castle">motte-and-bailey castle</a>, and was built by <a href="/wiki/Robert,_Count_of_Mortain" title="Robert, Count of Mortain">Robert, Count of Mortain</a> (half-brother of William the Conqueror) <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1070</span> to dominate the surrounding area. Launceston was the <i><a href="/wiki/Caput" title="Caput">caput</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Feudal_barony_of_Launceston" title="Feudal barony of Launceston">feudal barony of Launceston</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Cornwall" title="Earl of Cornwall">Earldom of Cornwall</a> until replaced by <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel" title="Lostwithiel">Lostwithiel</a> in the 13th century. Launceston was later the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> of Cornwall until 1835 when <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a> replaced it. The lands of Robert, Count of Mortain, became the core holdings of the feudal barony of Launceston, and the Fleming family continued to hold most of their manors from that barony, as can be seen from entries in the Book of Fees.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> (1086) it is recorded that Launceston was held by the Count of Mortain, and that he had his castle there. There was land for 10 ploughs, 1 villein and 13 smallholders with 4 ploughs, 2 mills which paid 40/- (\xc2\xa32 sterling) and 40 acres of pasture. The value of the manor was only \xc2\xa34 though it had formerly been worth \xc2\xa320.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Roman Catholic martyr <a href="/wiki/Cuthbert_Mayne" title="Cuthbert Mayne">Cuthbert Mayne</a> was executed at Launceston &#8212; a legacy of memorials and a church exists.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_War">Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> Launceston was known to be <i lang="fr" title="French language text">Royale et Loyale</i> to <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a>, hence its <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">coat of arms</a>. His son, who was later crowned <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a>, stayed in the town for a couple of days en route to the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Cavalier</a> army based further west.\n</p><p>In 1643, the Parliamentarian forces under the command of Major General James Chudleigh advanced in an attempt to capture Launceston from the Royalists. The Royalist commander, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Hopton,_1st_Baron_Hopton" title="Ralph Hopton, 1st Baron Hopton">Ralph Hopton, 1st Baron Hopton</a>, stationed his forces on the summit of Beacon Hill, a steep hill which overlooks the town. The Parliamentarians captured the foot of the hill, but were unable to dislodge the Royalist forces from the top. Hopton led a counterattack down the hill and, despite fierce fighting and the arrival of Parliamentary reinforcements, forced Chudleigh\'s troops to retreat.<sup id="cite_ref-ECW_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ECW-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Grenville,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Richard Grenville, 1st Baronet">Sir Richard Grenville, 1st Baronet</a> was committed by Prince Charles to Launceston Prison for refusing to obey Lord Hopton; Grenville had already quarrelled with General <a href="/wiki/George_Goring,_Lord_Goring" title="George Goring, Lord Goring">George Goring, Lord Goring</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Launceston has the only document in the UK signed by <a href="/wiki/Mary_II_of_England" title="Mary II of England">Mary II of England</a> and her husband, <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III of England</a>. Launceston is said to have gained its historical importance from being the furthest into Cornwall that Justices and other Officers of the Crown felt safe to venture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (A more realistic reason was the very poor means of transport within Cornwall at the time which did not begin to be improved until the late 18th century.) When the situation had been improved Bodmin became the county town where the assizes were held (in 1835). Launceston\'s role as the <i>de facto</i> county town of Cornwall became established in the 13th century but it was never officially designated as the county town.<sup id="cite_ref-Balchin,_W._G._V._1967_p._25_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Balchin,_W._G._V._1967_p._25-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Viscount_Launceston" title="Viscount Launceston">Viscount Launceston</a> was a title of nobility created in 1726 (as Viscount of Launceston) but is now extinct. In the early 19th century, Launceston gave its name to the settlement, <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Tasmania" title="Launceston, Tasmania">Launceston, Tasmania</a>, which is now the second largest city in Tasmania.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg/220px-Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg/330px-Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg/440px-Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Prior%27s_Bridge_and_the_River_Kensey_-_geograph.org.uk_-_323493.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Prior\'s Bridge over the <a href="/wiki/River_Kensey" title="River Kensey">River Kensey</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Launceston is a market town, castle, recreational and heritage railway visitor town and the main shopping centre for the adjoining rural areas of west Devon and east Cornwall. The town is mainly built on the south side of a large hill almost immune to <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>, unlike its northern neighbourhood, <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Cornwall" title="Newport, Cornwall">Newport</a> in part on a slightly wider plain at the bottom of the hill, which is susceptible to flooding by the <a href="/wiki/River_Kensey" title="River Kensey">River Kensey</a>.\n</p><p>The suburb of Newport is recorded for the first time during the 13th century. The natural advantages of the Launceston district had been recognised by the Anglo-Saxon monks of St Stephen and by the Norman lord of Cornwall in the reign of King William I. At this point in the course of the River Tamar it is joined by four tributaries within a short distance and its flood plain is relatively large, while further south the Tamar valley is narrow and meandering.<sup id="cite_ref-Balchin,_W._G._V._1967_p._25_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Balchin,_W._G._V._1967_p._25-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Launceston is connected to the <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30</a> trunk route, a <a href="/wiki/Dual_carriageway" title="Dual carriageway">dual carriageway</a> <a href="/wiki/Bypass_(road)" title="Bypass (road)">bypass</a> carrying its road traffic south of the town. The bypass crosses the River Tamar on the <i>Dunheved Bridge</i> built in 1975-6 and substantially rebuilt 2006\xe2\x80\x937.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Through this the town is approximately 42 miles (67&#160;km) west of <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>, 26 miles (42&#160;km) north-east of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> and 21 miles (34&#160;km) east of the smaller regional centre of <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a>. It is roughly midway between the north coast of Cornwall (at <a href="/wiki/Bude" title="Bude">Bude</a>) and the south coast (at <a href="/wiki/Saltash" title="Saltash">Saltash</a>).\n</p><p>Launceston <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> comprises the town whereas St Stephens by Launceston Rural civil parish covers all outskirts, save for those south of the town. These each convene to discuss events funding, recreational funding and general planning matters.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Stourscombe SSSI, a geological <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a>, one mile to the east of Launceston, is designated \xe2\x80\xb3... for the best inland exposure of the Upper <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> in South West England and the type locality of the Stourscombe Beds (Upper <a href="/wiki/Famennian" title="Famennian">Famennian</a>).\xe2\x80\xb3<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_buildings">Notable buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notable buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Launceston_gate.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Launceston_gate.JPG/220px-Launceston_gate.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Launceston_gate.JPG/330px-Launceston_gate.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Launceston_gate.JPG/440px-Launceston_gate.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Launceston_gate.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>South Gate</div></div></div>\n<p>Part of the town wall is still in existence including the South Gate of two arches. The White Hart Hotel incorporates a Norman doorway possibly removed from the Friary at St.Thomas\'. New Bridge (early 16th century) crosses the River Tamar: it is of granite. Two old bridges cross the River Kensey: one mediaeval and one built in 1580. The Baptist chapel is late 18th century and a number of Georgian houses may also be seen.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Three nonconformist churches/chapels served the 19th century town: Wesleyan Methodist, Bible Christian, and Calvinist.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> North Cornwall and West Devon\'s <a href="/wiki/Elim_Pentecostal_Church" title="Elim Pentecostal Church">Elim Pentecostal Church</a> is in the town.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_House,_Cornwall" title="Lawrence House, Cornwall">Lawrence House</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Grade_II*_listed" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade II* listed">Grade II* listed</a> building on Castle St, houses the town class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Launceston Chamber of Trade President\'s Chain showing the coat of arms and motto</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Launceston_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Launceston (UK Parliament constituency)">Launceston</a> was a Parliamentary Borough from medieval times, with the right to return two members of parliament. However, before the 19th century Reform Acts the right to vote was not held by all the residents but only by the freemen of the borough, and by 1832 there were fewer than 50 and it had come to be regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Rotten_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotten borough">rotten borough</a>, one of many in Cornwall. Neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Newport_(Cornwall)_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Newport (Cornwall) (UK Parliament constituency)">Newport</a> was also a borough with two MPs of its own. Launceston lost one of its two MPs and Newport both by the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Great Reform Act</a> of 1832; the area included in the borough of Launceston was considerably extended to enable the franchise to be opened up. It finally lost its right to separate representation in 1885, and became part of a new county constituency of Cornwall named after the town. Since 1918 it has been part of the North Cornwall parliamentary constituency. The current MP, since 2015, is the Conservative Scott Mann.\n</p><p>Launceston was once regarded as the capital of Cornwall (although it was never officially the county town - see above), and in 1973 the <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a> visited to receive his <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> dues from the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a>.\n</p><p>The arms of the town are Gu. a triple circular tower in a pyramidical form Or the first battlements mounted with cannon of the last, all within a bordure Az. charged with eight towers domed on the second. A badge was granted on 26 Mar 1906, being the first ever granted to a civic body: A keep or castle Gold.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Launceston is <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twinned</a> with Plestin-les-Gr\xc3\xa8ves in Brittany, France.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The outskirts of Launceston host some large retail businesses with convenience, niche and fine weather tourist-catering commerce in the town centre. The three main industrial estates are Pennygillam, Scarne and Newport. The town has benefited from immigrants from primarily Eastern European countries. This has allowed the town to sustain its primary industries, which it might not otherwise have been able to support due to the low population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (July 2017)">original research?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Launceston is located on the <a href="/wiki/A30_road_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="A30 road (England)">A30</a> trunk road into Cornwall and has a number of transport based business mainly located in Pennygillam Industrial estate. A major local business is the <a href="/wiki/DS_Smith" title="DS Smith">DS Smith</a> packaging products manufacturing plant. Launceston is located in predominately rural area and has a number of business dedicated to supporting the surrounding rural economy.\n</p><p>The town has several restaurants, caf\xc3\xa9s, take aways and a number of pubs. There are fewer pubs than in the Victorian era, a national trend with the larger ones tending to survive which specialise in their food, others of which provide live music events on a weekly basis. Launceston has one nightclub located at Pennygillam.\n</p><p>A tucking mill was established in the 15th century by the Flemings in the north of the town (Newport), water-powered, continuing in use for corn until 1968. A manuscript left by Richard Robbins (died 1910) records eight tanneries in the town in the 19th century. A mechanics\' institute was founded in 1847 at the Central Subscription Room. The gasworks was established as early as 1834 by Waygood &amp; Porter of <a href="/wiki/Beaminster" title="Beaminster">Beaminster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Duchy_Originals_from_Waitrose" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy Originals from Waitrose">Duchy Originals</a> company first manufactured its products in 2006 by opening a factory in Launceston making sweet and savoury pastry products but made a loss of \xc2\xa3447,158 in the financial year 2006/7.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> During 2009 the bakery in Launceston was sold at a loss, contributing to the Duchy Originals company making a loss for 2009 - 10.<sup id="cite_ref-Duchy_Originals_reports_Loss_as_sales_slump_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duchy_Originals_reports_Loss_as_sales_slump-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Natural_Fibre_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural Fibre Company">Natural Fibre Company</a> (TNFC) is a British <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> mill based in Launceston and is the only small-scale full range textile mill in the UK. The main focus of the business is to add value to naturally coloured raw fleece which is bought from farmers, smallholders and rare sheep breeders.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg/220px-Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg" width="220" height="307" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg/330px-Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg/440px-Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg 2x" data-file-width="458" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunheved_Cross_-_geograph.org.uk_-_429070.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dunheved Cross</div></div></div>\n<p>The poet <a href="/wiki/Charles_Causley" title="Charles Causley">Charles Causley</a> was a native and long-standing resident of the town where he was both born and died. He was at one time contender for <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Poet Laureate">Poet Laureate</a> and died in 2003, aged 86. His grave is in the St Thomas Churchyard near the house he lived in, which carries a blue plaque.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He contributed the account of Launceston to a feature in the <i>Sunday Times magazine</i> called "Village England". He describes it as belonging to England rather than to Cornwall "<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Launceston is one of the most important towns in Daphne du Maurier\'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Inn_(novel)" title="Jamaica Inn (novel)">Jamaica Inn</a></i>.\n</p><p>Launceston annually hosted the "Castle Rock" music festival in July, which took place on the lower grounds of the castle which overlooks the town (within the outer walls). As well as a vibrant mix of local bands, the 2006 festival was headlined by <a href="/wiki/Capdown" title="Capdown">Capdown</a> which massively improved the event\'s profile. The first concert was performed in 2000 and featured a young artist who was unknown at the time, <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Cullum" title="Jamie Cullum">Jamie Cullum</a>. The festival was headlined on two occasions by local Rock band Syrup, who were signed to Great West Records, which was set up by <a href="/wiki/Big_Country" title="Big Country">Big Country</a> bass player, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Butler_(musician)" title="Tony Butler (musician)">Tony Butler</a>, and his long term friend, Luke Maguire.\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Cornish_%26_Devon_Post" title="Cornish &amp; Devon Post">Cornish &amp; Devon Post</a></i> is one of the newspapers for the district and its office is in the town. Local-specific editions of the paper and other publications are produced. It was founded in 1856 and incorporates the <i>Launceston Weekly News</i>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Cornish_Guardian" title="Cornish Guardian">Cornish Guardian</a></i> North Cornwall edition covers Launceston. Four FM radio stations can be received: <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Cornwall" title="BBC Radio Cornwall">BBC Radio Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pirate_FM" title="Pirate FM">Pirate FM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_South_West" title="Heart South West">Heart South West</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radyo_an_Gernewegva" title="Radyo an Gernewegva">Radyo an Gernewegva</a> (online or via large aerials). In December 2013 a periodic community audio magazine was incepted <i>The Launceston Podcast</i> with catch-up services online.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sporting clubs include <a href="/wiki/Non-League_football" title="Non-League football">amateur football</a> club <a href="/wiki/Launceston_F.C." title="Launceston F.C.">Launceston F.C.</a> who play at Pennygillam, rugby union side <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Rugby_Club" title="Launceston Rugby Club">Launceston Rugby Club</a> who play at Polson Bridge, Launceston Cricket Club, Launceston Golf Club and Dunheved Bowling Club.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are three community-based drama groups within Launceston itself. Launceston Amateur Dramatic Society (LADS) is the longest established and can trace its roots back over a century (although its name has varied).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Oksigen Theatre is mainly concerned with training children in acting and produces one high quality show each year under the direction of Oksana Wroath, an ex-professional Russian stage actress.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> McQueen\'s Dance and Theatre CIC teaches children and adults a range of dance skills in its own premises. Oksigen Theatre and LADS perform mostly in the Town Hall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="mw-redirect" title="Tregadillett">Tregadillett</a> three miles west, the same clergy alternating services to provide for a large attendance and operating a combined website.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Grade_I_listed_buildings_in_Cornwall" title="Grade I listed buildings in Cornwall">Grade I listed</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">church</a> of <a href="/wiki/St_Mary_Magdalene%27s_Church,_Launceston" title="St Mary Magdalene&#39;s Church, Launceston">St Mary Magdalene</a> was built in 1511\xe2\x80\x931524 by Sir Henry Trecarrel of Trecarrel near the town as a memorial to his infant son who died whilst being bathed. The ornate carvings in granite originally carved for the mansion he began to build at Trecarrel, <a href="/wiki/Lezant" title="Lezant">Lezant</a> have withstood the test of time. The tower dates from the 14th century, an earlier church and graveyard having occupied the site. Its grand organ was presented by a member of the Morice family of <a href="/wiki/Werrington,_Cornwall" title="Werrington, Cornwall">Werrington Park</a>. The donor was either <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Morice,_3rd_Baronet" title="Sir William Morice, 3rd Baronet">Sir William Morice, 3rd Baronet</a> (1707\xe2\x80\x9350)<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> or his successor <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Morice_(MP_for_Launceston)" title="Humphry Morice (MP for Launceston)">Humphry Morice</a> MP (1723\xe2\x80\x9385). The casework is elaborate, "a superb example of 18th century woodwork; the 18th century pipework is also of very high quality."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On St Stephen\'s Hill the main road through the north of the town is the Roman Catholic Church to Cuthbert Mayne (see <a href="#History">#History</a>) designed by Arthur Langdon in a blend of <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_architecture" title="Byzantine architecture">Byzantine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a>. It was built in 1911 by local mason F H Nicholls of Lewannick, with carpentry by J H Harry, the oak doors by Mr Clifton of Ashwater, and the <a href="/wiki/Cupola" title="Cupola">copper dome</a> by T Chapman (Junior) of Launceston. The Lady Chapel was added in 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Five schools are in the town:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Three primary schools (St Catherine\'s Church of England Primary, St Stephen\'s Community Primary School and Launceston Community Primary School) cater for pupils aged 4 to 11.</li>\n<li>\'St. Joseph\'s School\', St Stephens Hill is an independent day school for boys and girls from age 3, going from nursery through the early years, junior department and into the senior school. The senior school boys and girls have been accepted into the sixth form from September 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Launceston_College_(Cornwall)" class="mw-redirect" title="Launceston College (Cornwall)">Launceston College</a> was first established in 1409 and became a boys grammar school with boarding house before becoming fully inclusive and educating students aged 11 to 18 or 19.</li></ul>\n<p>A former student of the last-mentioned college is actor Sir <a href="/wiki/Roger_Moore" title="Roger Moore">Roger Moore</a>. In 1962, Horwell Grammar School for Girls, Dunheved Road, was merged with the school and in 1965 the former Pennygillam School was added to form the present day comprehensive school which is still known as Launceston College. Since the 19th century (exact date unknown) the College has been at the southern end of Dunheved Road, approximately one kilometre from the town centre. In 1966 H. Spencer Toy its principal published <i>A History of Education at Launceston</i> detailing the development of education in the town and surrounding area.\n</p><p>Launceston Community Primary School has the colloquial pseudonym Windmill Primary School, being situated adjacent to the site of the town\'s former windmill for grinding grain in Coronation Park.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Launceston_railway_station" title="Launceston railway station">Launceston railway station</a></div>\n<p>Launceston is no longer connected to the national railway network, but was for almost a century served by two railway lines. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> (GWR) branch from <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> terminated in the town and the <a href="/wiki/London_and_South_Western_Railway" title="London and South Western Railway">London and South Western Railway</a> (LSWR) Exeter to Padstow <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall_Railway" title="North Cornwall Railway">North Cornwall Railway</a> passed through. The stations were adjacent. The GWR station closed to passengers in 1952 after which all trains used the LSWR station until the North Cornwall line closed in 1966. The site of the station has been redeveloped for light industrial use. Currently, the town is 10 miles away from the nearest station at <a href="/wiki/Gunnislake" title="Gunnislake">Gunnislake</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Steam_Railway" title="Launceston Steam Railway">Launceston Steam Railway</a> <a href="/wiki/Narrow_gauge_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrow gauge railway">narrow-gauge</a> <a href="/wiki/Heritage_railway" title="Heritage railway">heritage railway</a> uses an adjacent site and runs for 2.5 miles (4.0&#160;km) westward to Newmills along the valley of the Kensey.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_residents">Notable residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Notable residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The writer and historian, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Rendell" title="Joan Rendell">Joan Rendell</a> lived at <a href="/wiki/Yeolmbridge" title="Yeolmbridge">Yeolmbridge</a> near Launceston.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Francis_Vyvyan_Jago_Arundell" class="mw-redirect" title="Francis Vyvyan Jago Arundell">Francis Vyvyan Jago Arundell</a> was born at Launceston in July 1780, being the only son of Thomas Jago, a solicitor in that town, who had married Catherine, a daughter of Mr. Bolt, a surgeon at Launceston. He was in later life the Rector of <a href="/wiki/Landulph" title="Landulph">Landulph</a>, an antiquary and an oriental traveller.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Causley" title="Charles Causley">Charles Causley</a>, poet</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mary_Ann_Davenport" title="Mary Ann Davenport">Mary Ann Davenport</a>, actress, born at Launceston in 1759</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/James_Ruse" title="James Ruse">James Ruse</a>, a Cornishman from Launceston, arrived in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> aboard the transport <i>Scarborough</i>, part of the <a href="/wiki/First_Fleet" title="First Fleet">First Fleet</a> of Australian convict ships, in 1788.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/John_McGeoch" title="John McGeoch">John McGeoch</a>, seminal new wave guitarist</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Agnes_Prest" title="Agnes Prest">Agnes Prest</a>, One of the "Marian Martyrs", was burned at the stake at Southernhay, Exeter on 15 August 1557</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Launceston,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150205171836/http://www.cornwall.gov.uk/council-and-democracy/data-and-research/data-by-topic/population/town-populations">"Launceston Town populations 2011 census"</a>.
