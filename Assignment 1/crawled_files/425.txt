The period of prehistory prior to occupation by the genus <i>Homo</i> is part of the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Scotland" title="Geology of Scotland">geology of Scotland</a>. Although some <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">written records</a> refer to Shetland during the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire" title="Scotland during the Roman Empire">Roman invasions of Scotland</a>, prehistory in Shetland does not end until the later part of the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Early_Middle_Ages" title="Scotland in the Early Middle Ages">Early Historic Period</a> in Scotland, around AD 900.\n</p><p>Due to the practice, dating to at least the early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of building in stone on virtually treeless islands, Shetland is extremely rich in physical remains of the prehistoric eras and there are over 5,000 archaeological sites all told.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mesolithic and Neolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Midden" title="Midden">midden</a> site at West Voe on the south coast of Mainland, dated to 4320-4030 BC, has provided the first evidence of Mesolithic human activity on Shetland.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The same site provides dates for early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> activity and finds at <a href="/wiki/Scord_of_Brouster" title="Scord of Brouster">Scord of Brouster</a> in Walls have been dated to 3400 BC. This site includes a cluster of six or seven walled fields and three stone circular houses that contains the earliest <a href="/wiki/Hoe_(tool)" title="Hoe (tool)">hoe</a>-blades found so far in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> "Shetland knives" are stone tools that date from this period made from <a href="/wiki/Felsite" title="Felsite">felsite</a> from <a href="/wiki/Northmavine" title="Northmavine">Northmavine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sch10_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sch10-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg/220px-Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg/330px-Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg/440px-Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="467" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanydale_Temple_20080820_02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Staneydale_Temple" class="mw-redirect" title="Staneydale Temple">Staneydale Temple</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Heel-shaped_cairn" title="Heel-shaped cairn">Heel-shaped cairns</a>,  are a style of <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a> unique to Shetland, with a particularly large example on the island of <a href="/wiki/Vementry" title="Vementry">Vementry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sch10_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sch10-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Staneydale_Temple" class="mw-redirect" title="Staneydale Temple">Staneydale Temple</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bixter" title="Bixter">Bixter</a> is a large Neolithic ruin containing an oval chamber. Around it are the remains of houses, walls and cairns of the same period. There are numerous Neolithic era <a href="/wiki/Standing_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing stones">standing stones</a> including those at Yoxie on <a href="/wiki/Whalsay" title="Whalsay">Whalsay</a> and at Boardastubble, <a href="/wiki/Unst" title="Unst">Unst</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hjaltadans" title="Hjaltadans">Hjaltadans</a> on <a href="/wiki/Fetlar" title="Fetlar">Fetlar</a> is a ring of stones,  although there are no true <a href="/wiki/Stone_circle" title="Stone circle">stone circles</a> as such in Shetland.<sup id="cite_ref-RCAHMS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCAHMS-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Funzie_Girt_dyke" class="mw-redirect" title="Funzie Girt dyke">Funzie Girt</a> is a remarkable dividing wall that ran for 4 kilometres (2.5&#160;mi) across the island of Fetlar. The level of organisation involved suggest a relatively high population for Shetland in the Neolithic, perhaps as much as 10,000.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_51353.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Mousa" title="Broch of Mousa">Broch of Mousa</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Pottery shards found at the important site of <a href="/wiki/Jarlshof" title="Jarlshof">Jarlshof</a> indicate that there was Neolithic activity there although the main settlement dates from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This includes a <a href="/wiki/Forge" title="Forge">smithy</a>, a cluster of <a href="/wiki/Wheelhouse_(archaeology)" title="Wheelhouse (archaeology)">wheelhouses</a> and a later <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">broch</a>. The site has provided evidence of habitation during various phases right up until <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> times.<sup id="cite_ref-jarl_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jarl-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Numerous brochs were erected during the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Mousa" title="Broch of Mousa">Broch of Mousa</a> is the finest preserved example in Scotland of these round towers.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In addition to Mousa there are significant broch ruins at <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Clickimin" title="Broch of Clickimin">Clickimin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Culswick" title="Broch of Culswick">Culswick</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Old_Scatness" title="Old Scatness">Old Scatness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_West_Burrafirth" title="Broch of West Burrafirth">West Burrafirth</a>, although their origin and purpose is a matter of some controversy.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  In 2011 the collective site, "<a href="/wiki/The_Crucible_of_Iron_Age_Shetland" class="mw-redirect" title="The Crucible of Iron Age Shetland">The Crucible of Iron Age Shetland</a>" including Broch of Mousa, Old Scatness and Jarlshof  joined the UKs "Tentative List" of <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites_in_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Sites in Scotland">World Heritage Sites</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC311_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC311-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Travelers_in_antiquity">Travelers in antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Travelers in antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In AD 43 and 77 the Roman authors <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> referred to the seven islands they call <i>Haemodae</i> and <i>Acmodae</i> respectively, both of which are assumed to be Shetland. <a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">Thule</a> is first mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marseille#Prehistory_and_classical_antiquity" title="Marseille">Massilia</a> when he visited Britain sometime between 322 and 285 BC, but it is unlikely he meant Shetland as he believed it was six days sail north of Britain and one day from the frozen sea. However, another early written reference to the Shetland islands may have been when <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> reported that the Roman fleet had seen "Thule" on a voyage that included <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> in AD 98.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Watson (1926) states that Tacitus was referring to Shetland, although Breeze (2002) and D. Ellis Evans (1975) are more skeptical.<sup id="cite_ref-Breeze_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Breeze-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Roman presence in Scotland was little more than a series of relatively brief interludes of partial military occupation "within a longer continuum of indigenous development"<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and there is no evidence of any direct contact between Shetland and Roman forces.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Iron_Age">Later Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Ninians_5947.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" Isle</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The later Iron Age inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">Northern Isles</a> were probably <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a>, although the historical record is sparse. Hunter (2000) states in relation to King <a href="/wiki/Bridei_I_of_the_Picts" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridei I of the Picts">Bridei I of the Picts</a> in the sixth century AD: "As for Shetland, Orkney, Skye and the Western Isles, their inhabitants, most of whom appear to have been Pictish in culture and speech at this time, are likely to have regarded Bridei as a fairly distant presence."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/St_Ninian%27s_Isle" title="St Ninian&#39;s Isle">St Ninian\'s Isle</a> Treasure was discovered in 1958 by a local schoolboy, Douglas Coutts. Coutts was helping visiting archaeologists led by A. C. O\'Dell of Aberdeen University at a dig on the island. The silver bowls, jewellery and other pieces are believed to date from approximately AD 800. O\'Dell stated that "The treasure is the best survival of Scottish silver metalwork from the period" and that "the brooches show a variety of typical Pictish forms, with both animal-head and lobed geometrical forms of terminal".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viking_era">Viking era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Viking era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Shetland was colonised by <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a> in the late 8th and 9th centuries;<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> the fate of the previous indigenous population is uncertain. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Orkneyinga_Saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkneyinga Saga">Orkneyinga Saga</a></i>, Vikings then used the islands as a base for <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirate</a> expeditions against Norway and the coasts of mainland Scotland. In response, Norwegian king <a href="/wiki/Harald_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Harald I of Norway">Harald H\xc3\xa5rfagre</a> ("Harald Fair Hair") annexed the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">Northern Isles</a> (comprising Orkney and Shetland) in 875 and <a href="/wiki/Rognvald_Eysteinsson" title="Rognvald Eysteinsson">Rognvald Eysteinsson</a> received Orkney and Shetland from Harald as an <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Orkney" title="Earl of Orkney">earldom</a> in reparation for the death of his son in battle in Scotland. Some scholars believe that this story is apocryphal and based on the later voyages of <a href="/wiki/Magnus_III_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus III of Norway">Magnus Barelegs</a>. Nonetheless, as the Viking era developed Shetland emerged from the prehistoric period and into the era of <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">written history</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r853264625">.mw-parser-output .refbegin{font-size:90%;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .refbegin-hanging-indents>ul{list-style-type:none;margin-left:0}.mw-parser-output .refbegin-hanging-indents>ul>li,.mw-parser-output .refbegin-hanging-indents>dl>dd{margin-left:0;padding-left:3.2em;text-indent:-3.2em;list-style:none}.mw-parser-output .refbegin-100{font-size:100%}</style><div class="refbegin" style="">\n<dl><dt>Notes</dt></dl>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n</div>\n<dl><dt>Footnotes</dt></dl>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-jarl-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-jarl_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jarl_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.shetland-heritage.co.uk/brochures/area_pages/south_mainland/jarlshof_&amp;_scatness.htm">" Jarlshof &amp; Scatness"</a> shetland-heritage.co.uk.
