He is today considered to be the chief representative of the mature <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphonic</a> style of the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Flemish_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Franco-Flemish school">Franco-Flemish school</a>, and one of the three most famous and influential musicians in Europe at the end of the 16th century (the other two being <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pierluigi_da_Palestrina" title="Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina">Palestrina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Luis_de_Victoria" title="Tom\xc3\xa1s Luis de Victoria">Victoria</a>).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orlando_di_Lasso.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Orlando_di_Lasso.png/220px-Orlando_di_Lasso.png" width="220" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Orlando_di_Lasso.png/330px-Orlando_di_Lasso.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Orlando_di_Lasso.png/440px-Orlando_di_Lasso.png 2x" data-file-width="860" data-file-height="885" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orlando_di_Lasso.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Orlande de Lassus</div></div></div>\n<p>Orlando de Lassus was born in <a href="/wiki/Mons" title="Mons">Mons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Hainaut" title="County of Hainaut">County of Hainaut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Netherlands" title="Habsburg Netherlands">Habsburg Netherlands</a> (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>). Information about his early years is scanty, although some uncorroborated stories have survived, the most famous of which is that he was kidnapped three times because of the singular beauty of his singing voice. At the age of twelve, he left the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ferrante_Gonzaga" title="Ferrante Gonzaga">Ferrante Gonzaga</a> and went to <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> (from 1547 to 1549). While in Milan, he made the acquaintance of the madrigalist <a href="/wiki/Spirito_l%27Hoste_da_Reggio" class="mw-redirect" title="Spirito l&#39;Hoste da Reggio">Spirito l\'Hoste da Reggio</a>, a formative influence on his early musical style.\n</p><p>He then worked as a singer and a composer for Costantino Castrioto in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> in the early 1550s, and his first works are presumed to date from this time. Next he moved to Rome, where he worked for <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Cosimo I de&#39; Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Cosimo I de\' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany</a>, who maintained a household there, and in 1553, he became <i><a href="/wiki/Maestro_di_cappella" class="mw-redirect" title="Maestro di cappella">maestro di cappella</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Saint_John_Lateran" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of Saint John Lateran">Basilica of Saint John Lateran</a>, the ecumenical mother church of Rome and a spectacularly prestigious post indeed for a man only twenty-one years old. However, he stayed there for only a year. (Palestrina would assume this post a year later, in 1555.)\n</p><p>No solid evidence survives for his whereabouts in 1554, but there are contemporary claims that he traveled in France and England. In 1555 he returned to the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> and had his early works published in <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> (1555\xe2\x80\x931556). In 1556 he joined the court of <a href="/wiki/Albert_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Albert V, Duke of Bavaria">Albrecht V, Duke of Bavaria</a>, who was consciously attempting to create a musical establishment on a par with the major courts in Italy. Lassus was one of several Netherlanders to work there, and by far the most famous. He evidently was happy in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and decided to settle there. In 1558 he married Regina W\xc3\xa4ckinger, the daughter of a maid of honor of the Duchess. They had two sons, both of whom became composers, and his daughter married the painter <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_Aachen" title="Hans von Aachen">Hans von Aachen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> By 1563 Lassus had been appointed <i>maestro di cappella</i>, succeeding <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Daser" title="Ludwig Daser">Ludwig Daser</a> in the post. Lassus remained in the service of Albrecht V and his heir, <a href="/wiki/William_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="William V, Duke of Bavaria">Wilhelm V</a>, for the rest of his life.\n</p><p>By the 1560s Lassus had become quite famous, and composers began to go to Munich to study with him. <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Gabrieli" title="Andrea Gabrieli">Andrea Gabrieli</a> went there in 1562, and possibly remained in the chapel for a year. <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Gabrieli" title="Giovanni Gabrieli">Giovanni Gabrieli</a> also possibly studied with him in the 1570s. His renown had spread outside of strictly musical circles, for in 1570 <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Maximilian_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Maximilian II">Emperor Maximilian II</a> conferred nobility upon him, a rare circumstance for a composer. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII" title="Pope Gregory XIII">Pope Gregory XIII</a> knighted him and in 1571, and again in 1573, the king of France, <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_France" title="Charles IX of France">Charles IX</a>, invited him to visit. Some of these kings and aristocrats attempted to woo him away from Munich with more attractive offers, but Lassus was evidently more interested in the stability of his position, and the splendid performance opportunities of Albrecht\'s court, than in financial gain. "I do not want to leave my house, my garden, and the other good things in Munich", he wrote to the Duke of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony" title="Electorate of Saxony">Electorate of Saxony</a> in 1580, upon receiving an offer for a position in <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>.\n</p><p>In the late 1570s and 1580s Lassus made several visits to Italy, where he encountered the most modern styles and trends. In <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Ferrara</a>, the center of avant-garde activity, he doubtless heard the <a href="/wiki/Madrigal_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrigal (music)">madrigals</a> being composed for the d\'<a href="/wiki/House_of_Este" title="House of Este">Este</a> court. However, his own style remained conservative and became simpler and more refined as he aged. In the 1590s his health began to decline, and he went to a doctor named Thomas Mermann for treatment of what was called "melancholia hypocondriaca", but he was still able to compose as well as travel occasionally. His final work was often considered one of his best pieces: an exquisite set of twenty-one <i><a href="/wiki/Madrigali_spirituali" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrigali spirituali">madrigali spirituali</a></i> known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Lagrime_di_San_Pietro" title="Lagrime di San Pietro">Lagrime di San Pietro</a></i> ("Tears of St. Peter"), which he dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VIII" title="Pope Clement VIII">Pope Clement VIII</a>, and which was published posthumously in 1595. Lassus died in Munich on 14 June 1594, the same day that his employer decided to dismiss him for economic reasons. He never saw the letter. He was buried in Munich in the Alter Franziskaner Friedhof, a cemetery that was cleared of gravestones in 1789 and is now the site of <a href="/wiki/Max-Joseph-Platz" title="Max-Joseph-Platz">Max-Joseph-Platz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_influence">Music and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One of the most prolific, versatile, and universal composers of the late Renaissance, Lassus wrote over 2,000 works in all Latin, French, Italian and German vocal genres known in his time. These include 530 <a href="/wiki/Motet" title="Motet">motets</a>, 175 Italian <a href="/wiki/Madrigal_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrigal (music)">madrigals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villanella" title="Villanella">villanellas</a>, 150 French <a href="/wiki/Chanson" title="Chanson">chansons</a>, and 90 German <a href="/wiki/Lied" title="Lied">lieder</a>. No strictly instrumental music by Lassus is known to survive, or ever to have existed: an interesting omission for a composer otherwise so wide-ranging and prolific, during an age when instrumental music was becoming an ever-more prominent means of expression, all over Europe. The German music publisher <a href="/wiki/Adam_Berg_(music_publisher)" class="mw-redirect" title="Adam Berg (music publisher)">Adam Berg</a> dedicated 5 volumes of his <i>Patrocinium musicum</i> (published from 1573\xe2\x80\x931580) to Lassus\' music.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacred_music">Sacred music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sacred music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lassus remained <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Catholic</a> during this age of religious discord, though this neither hindered him in writing worldly secular songs nor in employing music originally to racy texts in his Magnificats and <a href="/wiki/Parody_masses" class="mw-redirect" title="Parody masses">masses employing parody technique.</a> Nevertheless, the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>, which under <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> influence was reaching a peak in Bavaria in the late sixteenth century, had a demonstrable impact on Lassus\' late work, including the liturgical music for the Roman Rite, the burgeoning number of Magnificats, the settings of the Catholic Ulenberg Psalter (1588), and especially the great penitential cycle of spiritual madrigals, the <a href="/wiki/Lagrime_di_San_Pietro" title="Lagrime di San Pietro">Lagrime di San Pietro</a> (1594).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Masses">Masses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Masses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Almost 60 masses have survived complete; most of them are imitation masses (called <a href="/wiki/Parody_masses" class="mw-redirect" title="Parody masses">parody masses</a>) using as melodic source material secular works written by himself or other composers. Technically impressive, they are nevertheless the most conservative part of his output. He usually conformed the style of the mass to the style of the source material, which ranged from <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_chant" title="Gregorian chant">Gregorian chant</a> to contemporary madrigals, but always maintained an expressive and reverent character in the final product.\n</p><p>Several of his masses are based on extremely secular French chansons; some of the source materials were outright obscene. <i>Entre vous filles de quinze ans</i>, "Oh you fifteen-year old girls", by <a href="/wiki/Clemens_non_Papa" class="mw-redirect" title="Clemens non Papa">Clemens non Papa</a>, gave him source material for his 1581 <i>Missa entre vous filles</i>, probably the most scandalous of the lot. This practice was not only accepted but encouraged by his employer, which can be confirmed by evidence from their correspondence, much of which has survived.\n</p><p>In addition to his traditional imitation masses, he wrote a considerable quantity of <i>missae breves</i>, "brief masses", syllablic short masses meant for brief services (for example, on days when Duke Albrecht went hunting:  evidently he did not want to be detained by long-winded <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphonic</a> music). The most extreme of these is a work actually known as the <i>J\xc3\xa4ger Mass</i> (<i>Missa venatorum</i>)\xe2\x80\x94the "Hunter\'s Mass".\n</p><p>Some of his masses show influence from the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_School_(music)" title="Venetian School (music)">Venetian School</a>, particularly in their use of <a href="/wiki/Venetian_polychoral_style" title="Venetian polychoral style">polychoral</a> techniques (for example, in the eight-voice <i>Missa osculetur me</i>, based on his own motet). Three of his masses are for double choir, and they may have been influential on the Venetians themselves; after all, <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Gabrieli" title="Andrea Gabrieli">Andrea Gabrieli</a> visited Lassus in Munich in 1562, and many of Lassus\'s works were published in Venice. Even though Lassus used the contemporary, sonorous Venetian style, his harmonic language remained conservative in these works:  he adapted the texture of the Venetians to his own artistic ends.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Motets_and_other_sacred_music">Motets and other sacred music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Motets and other sacred music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Lassus is one of the composers of a style known as <i><a href="/wiki/Musica_reservata" title="Musica reservata">musica reservata</a></i>\xe2\x80\x94a term which has survived in many contemporary references, many of them seemingly contradictory. The exact meaning of the term is a matter of fierce debate, though a rough consensus among musicologists is that it involves intensely expressive setting of text and <a href="/wiki/Chromaticism" title="Chromaticism">chromaticism</a>, and that it may have referred to music specifically written for connoisseurs. A famous composition by Lassus representative of this style is his series of 12 motets entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Prophetiae_Sibyllarum" title="Prophetiae Sibyllarum">Prophetiae Sibyllarum</a></i>, in a wildly chromatic idiom which anticipates the work of <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Gesualdo" title="Carlo Gesualdo">Gesualdo</a>; some of the chord progressions in this piece were not to be heard again until the 20th century.\n</p><p>Lassus wrote four settings of the <a href="/wiki/Passion_(music)" title="Passion (music)">Passion</a>, one for each of the Evangelists, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Matthew" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Matthew">St. Matthew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Mark" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Mark">Mark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Luke" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Luke">Luke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_John" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of John">John</a>. All are for <i><a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a></i> voices. He sets the words of <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a> and the narration of the Evangelist as chant, while setting the passages for groups polyphonically.\n</p><p>As a composer of motets, Lassus was one of the most diverse and prodigious of the entire Renaissance. His output varies from the sublime to the ridiculous, and he showed a sense of humor not often associated with sacred music:  for example, one of his motets satirizes poor singers (his setting of <i>Super flumina Babylonis</i>, for five voices) which includes stuttering, stopping and starting, and general confusion; it is related in concept if not in style to Mozart\'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Musical_Joke" title="A Musical Joke">A Musical Joke</a></i>. Many of his motets were composed for ceremonial occasions, as could be expected of a court composer who was required to provide music for visits of dignitaries, weddings, treaties and other events of state. But it was as a composer of religious motets that Lassus achieved his widest and most lasting fame.\n</p><p>Lassus\'s 1584 setting of the seven <a href="/wiki/Penitential_Psalms" title="Penitential Psalms">Penitential Psalms</a> of David (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penitential_Psalms_(Lassus)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Penitential Psalms (Lassus) (page does not exist)">Psalmi Davidis poenitentiales</a></i>) is one of the most famous collections of psalm settings of the entire Renaissance. The counterpoint is free, avoiding the pervasive <a href="/wiki/Imitation_(music)" title="Imitation (music)">imitation</a> of the Netherlanders such as <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Gombert" title="Nicolas Gombert">Gombert</a>, and occasionally using expressive devices foreign to Palestrina. As elsewhere, Lassus strives for emotional impact, and uses a variety of texture and care in text-setting towards that end. The penultimate piece in the collection, his setting of the <i><a href="/wiki/Psalm_130" title="Psalm 130">De profundis</a></i> (Psalm 129/130), is considered by many scholars to be one of the high-water marks of Renaissance polyphony, ranking alongside the two settings of the same text by <a href="/wiki/Josquin_des_Prez" title="Josquin des Prez">Josquin des Prez</a>.\n</p><p>Among his other liturgical compositions are <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canticle" title="Canticle">canticles</a> (including over 100 <a href="/wiki/Magnificat" title="Magnificat">Magnificats</a>), <a href="/wiki/Responsories_for_Holy_Week" title="Responsories for Holy Week">responsories for Holy Week</a>, Passions, <a href="/wiki/Lamentations_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lamentations (music)">Lamentations</a>, and some independent pieces for major feasts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secular_music">Secular music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Secular music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lassus wrote in all the prominent secular forms of the time. In the preface to his collection of German songs, Lassus lists his secular works: <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> madrigals and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> chansons, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> songs. He is probably the only Renaissance composer to write prolifically in five languages&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> in addition to those mentioned above&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 and he wrote with equal fluency in each. Many of his songs became hugely popular, circulating widely in Europe. In these various secular songs, he conforms to the manner of the country of origin while still showing his characteristic originality, wit, and terseness of statement.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Madrigals">Madrigals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Madrigals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orlando_de_Lassus2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Orlando_de_Lassus2.jpg/220px-Orlando_de_Lassus2.jpg" width="220" height="310" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/Orlando_de_Lassus2.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="314" data-file-height="442" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orlando_de_Lassus2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lassus leading a chamber ensemble, painted by Hans Mielich</div></div></div>\n<p>In his <a href="/wiki/Madrigal" title="Madrigal">madrigals</a>, many of which he wrote during his stay in Rome, his style is clear and concise, and he wrote tunes which were easily memorable; he also "signed" his work by frequently using the word \'lasso\' (and often setting with the sol-fege syllables la-sol, i.e. A-G in the key of C). His choice of poetry varied widely, from <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> for his more serious work to the lightest verse for some of his amusing <a href="/wiki/Canzonetta" title="Canzonetta">canzonettas</a>.\n</p><p>Lassus often preferred cyclic madrigals, i.e. settings of multiple poems in a group as a set of related pieces of music. For example, his fourth book of madrigals for five voices begins with a complete <a href="/wiki/Sestina" title="Sestina">sestina</a> by Petrarch, continues with two-part <a href="/wiki/Sonnet" title="Sonnet">sonnets</a>, and concludes with another sestina:  therefore the entire book can be heard as a unified composition with each madrigal a subsidiary part.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chansons">Chansons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chansons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Another form which Lassus cultivated was the French chanson, of which he wrote about 150. Most of them date from the 1550s, but he continued to write them even after he was in Germany:  his last productions in this genre come from the 1580s. They were enormously popular in Europe, and of all his works, they were the most widely arranged for instruments such as lute and keyboard. Most were collected in the 1570s and 1580s in three publications:  one by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Phal%C3%A8se_the_Elder" title="Pierre Phal\xc3\xa8se the Elder">Pierre Phal\xc3\xa8se the Elder</a> in 1571, and two by Le Roy and Ballard in 1576 and 1584. Stylistically, they ranged from the dignified and serious, to playful, bawdy, and amorous compositions, as well as drinking songs suited to taverns. Lassus followed the polished, lyrical style of <a href="/wiki/Claudin_de_Sermisy" title="Claudin de Sermisy">Sermisy</a> rather than the programmatic style of <a href="/wiki/Cl%C3%A9ment_Janequin" title="Cl\xc3\xa9ment Janequin">Cl\xc3\xa9ment Janequin</a> for his writing.\n</p><p>One of the most famous of Lassus\'s drinking songs was used by <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> in <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Part_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry IV, Part II">Henry IV, Part II</a>. English words are fitted to <i>Un jour vis un foulon qui fouloit</i> (as <i>Monsieur Mingo</i>) and sung by the drunken Justice Silence, in Act V, Scene iii.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="German_lieder">German lieder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: German lieder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A third type of secular composition by Lassus was the German <a href="/wiki/Lied" title="Lied">lied</a>. Most of these he evidently intended for a different audience, since they are considerably different in tone and style from either the chansons or madrigals; in addition, he wrote them later in life, with none appearing until 1567, when he was already well-established at Munich. Many are on religious subjects, although light and comic verse is represented as well. He also wrote drinking songs in German, and contrasting with his parallel work in the genre of the chanson, he also wrote songs on the unfortunate aspects of overindulgence.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_songs">Dutch songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dutch songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the preface to his collection of German songs, Lassus states that he had composed Dutch songs. However, no Dutch song has been preserved.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orlande_de_Lassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Gnome-mime-audio-openclipart.svg/50px-Gnome-mime-audio-openclipart.svg.png" width="50" height="50" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Gnome-mime-audio-openclipart.svg/75px-Gnome-mime-audio-openclipart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Gnome-mime-audio-openclipart.svg/100px-Gnome-mime-audio-openclipart.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="160" data-file-height="160" /></div></div></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist" style="line-height:1.1em"><div class="haudio">\n<div style="padding:4px 0"><a href="/wiki/File:ResonetInLaudibus.ogg" title="File:ResonetInLaudibus.ogg">Resonet in laudibus</a></div>\n<div><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="204.98285714286" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="ResonetInLaudibus.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cd/ResonetInLaudibus.ogg" type="audio/ogg;
