class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Death of Llywelyn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Wales by Edward I">Conquest of Wales by Edward I</a></div>\n<p>After the death of <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd" title="Llywelyn ap Gruffudd">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd</a>, his brother <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Gruffydd" title="Dafydd ap Gruffydd">Dafydd ap Gruffydd</a> carried on resistance for a few months, but was never able to control any large area. He was captured and executed by hanging, drawing and quartering at Shrewsbury in 1283. King <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a> now had complete control of Wales. The <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Rhuddlan" title="Statute of Rhuddlan">Statute of Rhuddlan</a> was issued from <a href="/wiki/Rhuddlan_Castle" title="Rhuddlan Castle">Rhuddlan Castle</a> in north Wales in 1284. The Statute divided parts of Wales into the counties of <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merionethshire" title="Merionethshire">Merioneth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caernarfonshire" title="Caernarfonshire">Caernarvon</a>, created out of the remnants of Llewelyn\'s Gwynedd. It introduced the English common law system, and abolished <a href="/wiki/Welsh_law" title="Welsh law">Welsh law</a> for criminal cases, though it remained in use for civil cases. It allowed the King to appoint royal officials such as sheriffs, coroners, and bailiffs to collect taxes and administer justice. In addition, the offices of justice and chamberlain were created to assist the sheriff. The <a href="/wiki/Marcher_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcher Lords">Marcher Lords</a> retained most of their independence, as they had prior to the conquest.Most of the Marcher Lords were by now Cambro Norman i.e. Norman Welsh through intermarriage.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="King_Edward_I_and_rebellions">King Edward I and rebellions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: King Edward I and rebellions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg/250px-SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg/375px-SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg/500px-SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SDJ_Harlech_Castle_Gatehouse.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Harlech_Castle" title="Harlech Castle">Harlech Castle</a> was one of a series built by <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a> to consolidate his conquest.</div></div></div>\n<p>King Edward I built a ring of impressive stone castles to consolidate his domination of Wales, and crowned his conquest by giving the title <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a> to his son and heir in 1301.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Wales became, effectively, part of England, even though its people spoke a different language and had a different culture. English kings paid lip service to their responsibilities by appointing a Council of Wales, sometimes presided over by the heir to the throne.  This Council normally sat in <a href="/wiki/Ludlow" title="Ludlow">Ludlow</a>, now in England but at that time still part of the disputed border area of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">Welsh Marches</a>. Welsh literature, particularly poetry, continued to flourish however, with the lesser nobility now taking over from the princes as the patrons of the <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">poets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bards" class="mw-redirect" title="Bards">bards</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Gwilym" title="Dafydd ap Gwilym">Dafydd ap Gwilym</a> who flourished in the middle of the fourteenth century is considered by many to be the greatest of the Welsh poets.\n</p><p>There were a number of rebellions including ones led by <a href="/wiki/Madog_ap_Llywelyn" title="Madog ap Llywelyn">Madog ap Llywelyn</a> in 1294-5 <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_Bren" title="Llywelyn Bren">Llywelyn Bren</a>, Lord of Senghenydd, in 1316-18. In the 1370s the last representative in the male line of the ruling house of Gwynedd, <a href="/wiki/Owain_Lawgoch" title="Owain Lawgoch">Owain Lawgoch</a>, twice planned an invasion of Wales with French support. The English government responded to the threat by sending an agent to assassinate Owain in <a href="/wiki/Poitou" title="Poitou">Poitou</a> in 1378.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Black_Death">The Black Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Black Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubonic_plague_map.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Bubonic_plague_map.PNG/220px-Bubonic_plague_map.PNG" width="220" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Bubonic_plague_map.PNG/330px-Bubonic_plague_map.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Bubonic_plague_map.PNG/440px-Bubonic_plague_map.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="1108" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubonic_plague_map.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Black Death rapidly spread along the major European sea and land trade routes.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> arrived in Wales in late 1348. What records survive indicate that about 30% of the population died, in line with the average mortality through most of Europe. \n</p>\n<h2><span id="Glynd.C5.B5r.27s_rebellion"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Glynd\xc5\xb5r\'s_rebellion">Glynd\xc5\xb5r\'s rebellion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Glynd\xc5\xb5r&#039;s rebellion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1400, a Welsh nobleman, <a href="/wiki/Owain_Glynd%C5%B5r" title="Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r">Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r</a>, revolted against King <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">Henry IV of England</a>. Owain inflicted a number of defeats on the English forces and for a few years controlled most of Wales.  Some of his achievements included holding the first Welsh Parliament at <a href="/wiki/Machynlleth" title="Machynlleth">Machynlleth</a> and plans for two universities. Eventually the king\'s forces were able to regain control of Wales and the rebellion died out, but Owain himself was never captured, betrayed nor tempted by Royal pardons. His rebellion caused a great upsurge in Welsh identity and he was widely supported by Welsh people throughout the country.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a response to Glynd\xc5\xb5r\'s rebellion, the English parliament passed the <a href="/wiki/Penal_Laws_against_Wales_1402" title="Penal Laws against Wales 1402">Penal Laws in 1402</a>. These prohibited the Welsh from carrying arms, from holding office and from dwelling in fortified towns. These prohibitions also applied to Englishmen who married Welsh women. These laws remained in force after the rebellion, although in practice they were gradually relaxed.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wars_of_the_Roses">Wars of the Roses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wars of the Roses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry7England.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Henry7England.jpg/160px-Henry7England.jpg" width="160" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Henry7England.jpg/240px-Henry7England.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Henry7England.jpg/320px-Henry7England.jpg 2x" data-file-width="337" data-file-height="438" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry7England.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Henry Tudor, later King Henry VII</div></div></div>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Roses" title="Wars of the Roses">Wars of the Roses</a> over the English throne, which began in 1455, both sides made considerable use of Welsh troops. The main figures in Wales were the two <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Pembroke" title="Earl of Pembroke">Earls of Pembroke</a>, the Yorkist Earl <a href="/wiki/William_Herbert,_1st_Earl_of_Pembroke_(1423-1469)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Herbert, 1st Earl of Pembroke (1423-1469)">William Herbert</a> and the Lancastrian <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Tudor,_1st_Duke_of_Bedford" class="mw-redirect" title="Jasper Tudor, 1st Duke of Bedford">Jasper Tudor</a>. In 1485 Jasper\'s nephew, Henry Tudor, landed in Wales with a small force to launch his bid for the throne of England. Henry was of Welsh descent, counting princes such as <a href="/wiki/Rhys_ap_Gruffydd" title="Rhys ap Gruffydd">Rhys ap Gruffydd</a> (The Lord Rhys) among his ancestors, and his cause gained much support in Wales. Henry defeated King <a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">Richard III of England</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bosworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Bosworth">Battle of Bosworth</a> with an army containing many Welsh soldiers and gained the throne as King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Annexation_to_England">Annexation to England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Annexation to England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Under Henry VII\'s son, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535-1542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535-1542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535-1542</a> were passed, annexing Wales to England in legal terms, abolishing the Welsh legal system, and banning the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh language</a> from any official role or status, but it did for the first time define the <a href="/wiki/Wales-England_border" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales-England border">Wales-England border</a> and allowed members representing constituencies in Wales to be elected to the English Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They also abolished any legal distinction between the Welsh and the English, thereby effectively ending the Penal Code although this was not formally repealed.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
