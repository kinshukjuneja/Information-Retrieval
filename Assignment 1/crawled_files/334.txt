&#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/\xc9\x9b/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">\xc9\x9b</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress It is the most westerly major town in Cornwall<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and is about 64 miles (103&#160;km) west-southwest of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> and 255 miles (410&#160;km) west-southwest of London. Situated in the shelter of <a href="/wiki/Mount%27s_Bay" title="Mount&#39;s Bay">Mount\'s Bay</a>, the town faces south-east onto the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, is bordered to the west by the <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> port of <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a>, to the north by the civil parish of <a href="/wiki/Madron" title="Madron">Madron</a> and to the east by the civil parish of <a href="/wiki/Ludgvan" title="Ludgvan">Ludgvan</a>.\n</p><p>The civil parish includes the town of Newlyn and the villages of <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul,_Cornwall" title="Paul, Cornwall">Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gulval" title="Gulval">Gulval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heamoor" title="Heamoor">Heamoor</a>.\n</p><p>Granted various <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charters</a> from 1512 onwards and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Penzance\xe2\x80\x94<i>Pennsans</i>; "holy headland" in the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\xe2\x80\x94refers to the location of a chapel nowadays called <a href="/wiki/Anthony_the_Great" title="Anthony the Great">St Anthony\'s</a> that is said to have stood over a thousand years ago on the headland to the west of what became Penzance Harbour. There are no early documents mentioning an actual dedication to St Anthony which seems to depend entirely on tradition and may be groundless.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The only remaining object from this chapel is a carved figure, now largely eroded, known as "St Raffidy" which can be found in the churchyard of the parish church of St Mary\'s near the original site of the chapel.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Until the 1930s this history was also reflected in the choice of <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> for the town, the severed "holy head" of <a href="/wiki/St_John_the_Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="St John the Baptist">St John the Baptist</a>. It can still be seen on the civic <a href="/wiki/Regalia" title="Regalia">regalia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">Mayor</a> of Penzance and on several important <a href="/wiki/Landmark" title="Landmark">landmarks</a> in the town.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory_to_Early_Medieval_period">Prehistory to Early Medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prehistory to Early Medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>About 400 prehistoric stone axes, known as Group 1 axes and made from <a href="/wiki/Elvan" title="Elvan">greenstone</a>, have been found all over Britain, which from <a href="/wiki/Petrological" class="mw-redirect" title="Petrological">petrological</a> analysis appear to come from west Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Although the quarry has not been identified, it has been suggested that the <i>Gear</i>, a rock now submerged half a mile from the shore at Penzance, may be the site. A significant amount of trade is indicated as many have been found elsewhere in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The earliest evidence of settlement in Penzance is from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. A number of bronze implements such as a <a href="/wiki/Palstave" title="Palstave">palstave</a>, a spear-head, a knife, and pins, along with much pottery and large quantities of charcoal were discovered when building a new housing estate, at Tredarvah, to the west of Alverton.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The defensive earthwork known as <a href="/wiki/Lescudjack_Hill_Fort" title="Lescudjack Hill Fort">Lescudjack Castle</a> is not excavated, but almost certainly belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A single rampart encloses three acres of hilltop, and would have dominated the approach to the area from the east. There are no signs of the additional ramparts reported by <a href="/wiki/William_Hals" title="William Hals">William Hals</a> in about 1730,<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the site is now surrounded by housing with allotments. Excavations in 2008, 1 kilometre (0.62&#160;mi) to the west at <a href="/wiki/Penwith_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Penwith College">Penwith College</a> found an enclosure ditch and pottery indicating a settlement, and an evolving field system with ditches and interconnecting pits suggesting water management.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There are traces of a rampart and ditch to the west of Penzance at Mount Misery, and an <a href="/wiki/Lesingey_Round" title="Lesingey Round">oval rampart and ditch at Lesingey</a> above the <a href="/wiki/St_Just_in_Penwith" title="St Just in Penwith">St Just</a> road, which together with Lescudjack, overlook the coast of Penzance and Newlyn.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Until recently, there was little evidence for anything but an early and short Roman occupation of Cornwall, and there have so far been only three finds in Penzance. In August 1899 two coins of <a href="/wiki/Vespasian" title="Vespasian">Vespasian</a> (69\xe2\x80\x9379 AD) were found in an ancient trench in Penzance Cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The coins were eight feet below ground together with some cow bones, and are now in the <a href="/wiki/Penlee_House" title="Penlee House">Penlee House Museum</a>. Another coin, found in 1934 in the Alverton area, depicts <a href="/wiki/Sol_(mythology)" title="Sol (mythology)">Sol</a>, the Roman sun god.<sup id="cite_ref-PenleeH_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PenleeH-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It is described as a \xe2\x80\xb3<i>coin of the reign of Constantine the Great</i>\xe2\x80\xb3, and was also donated to the museum.<sup id="cite_ref-PenleeH_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PenleeH-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A 30&#160;mm (1 3/16&#160;in)<i> <a href="/wiki/Sestertius" title="Sestertius">sestertius</a></i> was found on a building site in or around Penzance about ten years previously, and was presented to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_of_Cornwall" title="Royal Institution of Cornwall">Royal Institution of Cornwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Larger quantities of Roman coins have been found nearby, at <a href="/wiki/Marazion_Marsh" title="Marazion Marsh">Marazion Marsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerris" title="Kerris">Kerris</a> in Paul parish, but there is no evidence of any Roman settlement in the area, although nearby villages such as <a href="/wiki/Chysauster" class="mw-redirect" title="Chysauster">Chysauster</a> were occupied at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hundreds_of_Cornwall" title="Hundreds of Cornwall">Hundred</a> of <a href="/wiki/Penwith_Hundred" title="Penwith Hundred">Penwith</a> had its ancient centre at <a href="/wiki/Gwithian" title="Gwithian">Connerton</a>, now buried beneath the sands of Gwithian Towans at <a href="/wiki/Gwithian" title="Gwithian">Gwithian</a>. A Hundred was a <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Saxon</a> administrative unit which was sub-divided into <a href="/wiki/Tithings" class="mw-redirect" title="Tithings">tithings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Manor_of_Alverton" title="Manor of Alverton">Manor of Alverton</a>, with an area of 64 Cornish acres, gave its name to the second largest tithing in Penwith. The manor included Penzance as well as parts of Madron, Paul, St Buryan and Sancreed.\n</p><p>Although Penzance is not mentioned in the survey document the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a>, it is likely that the area would have been included. Domesday records that in 1066 the Manor of Alwarton was owned by Alward who was dispossessed by <a href="/wiki/Robert,_Count_of_Mortain" title="Robert, Count of Mortain">Robert, Count of Mortain</a>, a half-brother of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>. The name Alward and <i>tun</i>, a personal name combined with a town or settlement suffix, indicate Saxon land ownership. In Cornwall the <i>tun</i> indicates a manorial centre such as <a href="/wiki/Helston" title="Helston">Helston</a> or Connerton. The change of ownership in 1066 was a change from one alien landlord to another, and the name Alverton lives on as the western part of Penzance from St John\xe2\x80\x99s Hall, to the housing estate on the west side of the River Laregan.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_and_Late_Middle_Ages">High and Late Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High and Late Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first mention of the name Pensans is in the <a href="/wiki/Assize" class="mw-redirect" title="Assize">Assize</a> Roll of 1284,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and the first mention of the actual church that gave Penzance its name is in a manuscript written by William Borlase in 1750:\n\xe2\x80\xb3The ancient chapel belonging to the town of Penzance may be seen in a fish cellar, near the key; it is small and as I remember had the image of the Virgin Mary in it.\xe2\x80\xb3<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The chapel was built of <a href="/wiki/Elvan" title="Elvan">greenstone</a> and about 30&#160;ft in length and 15&#160;ft in breadth of which only a fragment remained <i>in situ</i>. In around 1800 the chapel was converted to a fish cellar. A carving in "Ludgvan granite" thought to be of <a href="/wiki/Anthony_the_Great" title="Anthony the Great">St Anthony</a> was removed in about 1830 and was used in the wall of a pig sty, which was further vandalised in 1850 when "a stranger ... taking fancy to the stony countenance and rough hands, they were broken off and carried away as relics ...". The remains of the vandalised relic were taken to St Mary\'s Churchyard by a mason who told Mr Millett that he "popped St Raffidy into a wheelbarrow and trundle him off to the chapel yard."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The carving remains in St Mary\xe2\x80\x99s Churchyard and has been dated by Prof <a href="/wiki/Charles_Thomas_(historian)" title="Charles Thomas (historian)">Charles Thomas</a> as early 12th century. There are no early documents mentioning the dedication to St Anthony; this seems to depend entirely on tradition and may be groundless. A licence for Divine Service in the Chapel of <a href="/wiki/Gabriel" title="Gabriel">St Gabriel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raphael_(archangel)" title="Raphael (archangel)">St Raphael</a> was granted in 1429, but nothing more is known of this chapel except, possibly, for the mason who mentioned \xe2\x80\xb3St Raffidy\xe2\x80\xb3 in 1850. Adjoining the chapel is St Anthony\'s Gardens, named in 1933 and containing an archway said to have been taken from the chapel site.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Dominating the skyline above the harbour is the present church of St Mary\'s. A St Mary\'s Chapel is mentioned in a 1548 document which states that it was founded by Sir Henry Tyes, Knight, Lord of the Manor of Alverton, who gave a \xc2\xa34 <a href="/wiki/Stipend" title="Stipend">stipend</a> for a priest.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> There is an earlier document from 1379, when Bishop Brantyngham licensed for services "the chapel of Blessed Mary of Pensande".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> At this date it was probably used as a <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapel of ease</a>, and not used for Sunday services, which would have affected the attendance at the Parish Church in Madron.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Further evidence of historical settlement from this period is in the St Clare area of the town, where a chapel existed to St Clare or Cleer. The earliest reference is a lease of 1584: "...a certain chapel situate below the high road between Pensaunce and Madderne."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In the early part of the 19th century the foundations of a building, said to be the chapel, was discovered, and enough was exposed to show the shape of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> An episcopal licence cannot be traced for this chapel.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The name, St Clare, lives on in the town as "St Clare Street", which is part of the road from Penzance to Madron, and the St Clare cricket ground at the top of the hill.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Medieval economy</dt></dl>\n<p>Markets were held on a fixed day each week, and fairs on fixed date(s) each year. To obtain either, a manorial lord had to apply for a <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a>. The right to hold a market each Wednesday was granted by <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">King Edward III</a> to Alice de Lisle, sister of Lord Tyes and widow of Warin de Lisle, on 25 April 1332; a fair, lasting seven days at the Feast of St Peter <i>ad Vincula</i> on 1 August; and another fair of seven days on 24 August at Mousehole for the feast of St Bartholomew \xe2\x80\x93 later to be held in Penzance. The settlement was growing in importance as the weekly Wednesday market was confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">King Henry IV</a> and three further fairs, each of two days, were granted on 8 April 1404. These were at the Feast of the Conception of Virgin Mary (8 December), St Peter <i>in Cathedra</i> (22 February) and the Nativity of the Virgin Mary (8 September).<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is not known when a quay was built at Penzance as there is no grant or licence, but an Inquest as to the Manor of Alverton in 1322 records eight fishing boats each paying 2 shillings each, and an unspecified number at Mousehole each paying 12 shillings. There was also a payment of 8 shillings for the rent of <i>logii</i> (huts or sheds) of foreign fishermen, i.e. those outside the manor. At a second Inquest in 1327 the number had risen to 13 at Penzance; with 16 recorded at Mousehole and both now paying only 1 shilling each: the total rent for <i>logii</i> was 8s 6d (42\xc2\xbdp) with 17 tenants paying 6d (2\xc2\xbdp) each. Both Inquests record 29 <a href="/wiki/Burgess_(title)" title="Burgess (title)">burgesses</a> at Penzance and 40 at Mousehole. A burgess paid his rent with money rather than with personal services, and this indicates that Penzance and Mousehole were considered to be towns.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A comparison of the settlements in West Cornwall can be made with the annual payments, based on the number of fishing boats, made to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> in 1337: Porthia (St Ives) \xc2\xa36; Mosehole (Mousehole) \xc2\xa35; Marcasion (Marazion) \xc2\xa33; Pensanns (Penzance) 12s (60p); Londeseynde (Land\'s End), (Sennen Cove) 10s (50p); Nywelyn (Newlyn) 10s; and Portmynster (Porthminster, St Ives) 2s (10p).<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1425, 1432 and 1440 ships in Penzance were licensed to carry pilgrims to the shrine of <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">St James of Compostella</a>, in north-west <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In medieval times and later, Penzance was subject to frequent raiding by "Turkish pirates", in fact <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Corsairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary Corsairs">Barbary Corsairs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the period before Penzance gained borough status in 1614 the village and surrounding areas continued to be within the control of the Manor of Alverton and was subject to the taxation regime of that manor.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tudor_period">Tudor period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tudor period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the summer of 1578 Penzance was visited by the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">plague</a>. The burial registers of Madron (where all Penzance births, deaths and marriages were recorded) shows a massive increase in deaths for 1578, from 12 the previous year to 155. This is estimated to be about 10% of the population of the village at the time. The plague also returned in 1647 and the registers again show an increase of from 22 burials to 217 in one year.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Being at the far west of Cornwall, Penzance and the surrounding villages have been sacked many times by foreign fleets. On 23 July 1595,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  several years after the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> of 1588, a Spanish force of four galleys transporting 400 arquebusiers under <a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)" title="Don (honorific)">Don</a> <a href="/wiki/Carlos_de_Amesquita" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlos de Amesquita">Carlos de Amesquita</a>, which had been patrolling the Channel, landed troops in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. The local militias, which formed the cornerstone of their anti-invasion measures and numbered several hundred men, threw down their arms and fled in panic. Only Francis Godolphin, Deputy Lord Lieutenant of Cornwall and commander of the militias along with 12 of his soldiers stood to offer some kind of resistance. Amesquita\'s force seized supplies, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Cornwall">raided and burned</a> Penzance and surrounding villages, held a mass, and sailed away to successfully engage and put to flee a Dutch squadron of 46 ships .<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Penzance_as_a_town_since_1614">Penzance as a town since 1614</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Penzance as a town since 1614">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The reason for Penzance\'s relative success probably stems from the 15th, 16th and 17th centuries when King Henry IV granted the town a royal market in 1404.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> in 1512 granted the right to charge harbour dues,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">King James I</a> granted the town the status of a Borough in 1614. The Charter defined the bounds of the town by an artificial line formed by a half-mile circle, measured from the market cross in the Greenmarket. The granting of Borough status made the town independent of the County Courts, a right held until County Councils came into being in 1888. Other privileges included owning land and property; imposing fines for breaking <a href="/wiki/Bylaws" class="mw-redirect" title="Bylaws">bylaws</a>; holding a civil court with jurisdiction over cases not exceeding \xc2\xa350; and providing a prison. The Charter also confirmed the harbour rights given earlier in 1512 and granted two weekly markets to be held on Tuesdays and Thursday; which replaced a single market previously held on Wednesdays. Seven fairs were granted (or confirmed):\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a>, the Sunday after <a href="/wiki/Whitsun" title="Whitsun">Whitsun</a> - still held</li>\n<li>The Thursday before St Andrew\'s Day (30 November)</li>\n<li>St Peter\'s Day (1 August); first granted in 1332</li>\n<li>St Bartholomew\'s Day (24 August); originally granted to <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a> but now obsolete probably due to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Cornwall">Spanish Raid of 1595</a></li>\n<li>St Mary the Virgin\'s Day (8 September); granted in 1404</li>\n<li>The Conception of St Mary the Virgin\'s Day (8 September); granted in 1404</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Chair_of_Peter" class="mw-redirect" title="Feast of the Chair of Peter">St Peter\'s Day in Cathedra</a> (22 February); granted in 1404</li></ul>\n<p><a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">The Crown</a> was paid a perpetual rent of five marks (\xc2\xa33 6s 8d / \xc2\xa33.33) in acknowledgment of the rights granted by the Charter, which was paid until 1832, but there was no grant of Parliamentary representation.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The old arms of Penzance were the head of St John the Baptist on a charger, with the legend "Pensans anno Domini 1614". The arms of the borough are Arg. a Paschal lamb proper in base a Maltese cross Az. on a chief embattled of the last between two keys in saltire wards upwards Or and a saltire couped Arg. a plate charged with a dagger point downwards Gu.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Within a year the new Borough bought a <i>substantial degree of freedom</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Manor_of_Alverton" title="Manor of Alverton">Manor of Alverton</a> then known as Alverton and Penzance for \xc2\xa334 plus a perpetual annuity of \xc2\xa31 which was last paid in 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> A market-house and Guildhall was built, and together with the rights bought in 1615, provided almost all the borough income for more than two centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The southern arm of the pier was built in 1766 and extended in 1785,<sup id="cite_ref-Kittridge_1989_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kittridge_1989-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> to add to the first pier of which was built in 1512.<sup id="cite_ref-Guthrie_1994_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guthrie_1994-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> Penzance was sacked by the Parliamentarian forces of <a href="/wiki/Sir_Thomas_Fairfax" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Thomas Fairfax">Sir Thomas Fairfax</a> apparently for the kindness shown to <a href="/wiki/George_Goring,_1st_Earl_of_Norwich" title="George Goring, 1st Earl of Norwich">Lord Goring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Hopton,_1st_Baron_Hopton" title="Ralph Hopton, 1st Baron Hopton">Lord Hopton</a>\'s troops during the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Further Civic improvements included the construction in 1759 of a reservoir which supplied water to public pumps in the streets.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\nIn 1768 a friendly society of Tradesmen was formed at Penzance with 101 members living within three miles of the town.  The members met on the first Monday in each month at the King\'s Head, kept by Richard Runnals. The benefits of the Society were: a member being sick, lame or infirm would receive seven shillings a week. [gout and rupture were common, and excepted from payment unless \'needful\'. Aged and infirm members were allowed 3/6p per week. Three pounds was given toward the funeral of a member and 10 pounds to the widow or children. All members were to attend the funeral or be fined a shilling. How long this association lasted is not known.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Penzance has a long-standing association with the local parish of <a href="/wiki/Madron" title="Madron">Madron</a>. Madron Church was in fact the centre of most religious activity in the town until 1871,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> when St Mary\'s Church (until this period a <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapel of ease</a>) was granted parish status by church authorities though it had been registered since the new church was built in 1832.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1755_seiche">1755 seiche</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1755 seiche">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On 1 November 1755 the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_earthquake" class="mw-redirect" title="Lisbon earthquake">Lisbon earthquake</a> caused <a href="/wiki/Seiche" title="Seiche">seiching</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">standing wave</a> in an enclosed or partially enclosed body of water, along the Cornish coast, and particularly in Mount\'s Bay, which is prone to seiching. At around 2:00&#160;pm, the sea rose eight feet in Penzance, came in at great speed, and fell at the same rate. Little damage was recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span id="1801.E2.80.931848"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1801\xe2\x80\x931848">1801\xe2\x80\x931848</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1801\xe2\x80\x931848">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Penzance,_Egyptian_house.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Penzance%2C_Egyptian_house.jpg/220px-Penzance%2C_Egyptian_house.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Penzance%2C_Egyptian_house.jpg/330px-Penzance%2C_Egyptian_house.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Penzance%2C_Egyptian_house.jpg/440px-Penzance%2C_Egyptian_house.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Penzance,_Egyptian_house.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egyptian House</div></div></div>\n<p>At the start of the 19th century (1801), the town had a population of 2,248. The census, which is taken every ten years, recorded a peak population in 1861 of 3,843, but it then declined, as in most of Cornwall, through the remainder of the century, being just 3,088 in 1901.<sup id="cite_ref-UK_&amp;_Ireland_Genealogogy_-_Penzance_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UK_&amp;_Ireland_Genealogogy_-_Penzance-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the time <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> came to the throne in 1837, Penzance had established itself as an important regional centre. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Geological_Society_of_Cornwall" title="Royal Geological Society of Cornwall">Royal Geological Society of Cornwall</a> was founded in the town in 1814<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and about 1817 was responsible for introducing a miner\'s safety tamping bar, which attracted the <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">Prince Regent</a> to become its patron.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboat</a> in Cornwall was bought by the people of Penzance in 1803 but it was sold in 1812 due to lack of funds to keep it in operation.<sup id="cite_ref-Lifeboat_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lifeboat-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The pier had been extended again in 1812 and John Matthews opened a small dry dock in 1814, the first in the <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West</a>. In 1840 Nicholas Holman of <a href="/wiki/St_Just_in_Penwith" title="St Just in Penwith">St Just</a> opened a branch of his foundry business on the quayside.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> These facilities proved valuable in supporting the steamships that were soon calling at the harbour in increasing numbers.\n</p><p>Gas lighting was introduced in 1830 and the old Market House was demolished in 1836. Its <a href="/wiki/Market_Building,_Penzance" title="Market Building, Penzance">replacement</a>, designed by W. Harris of <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, was completed at the top of Market Jew Street in 1838. (The name Market Jew comes from the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> <i>Marghas Yow</i>, meaning <i>Thursday Market</i>, the name of a nearby village now absorbed into <a href="/wiki/Marazion" title="Marazion">Marazion</a>, to which Market Jew Street leads.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>) St Mary\'s Church, another prominent feature of the Penzance skyline, was completed in 1836, while a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> church was built in 1843. Another familiar building from this period is the eccentric <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_House,_Penzance" title="Egyptian House, Penzance">Egyptian House</a> in Chapel Street, built in 1830. The first part of the Promenade along the sea front dates from 1844.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="1849.E2.80.931900"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1849\xe2\x80\x931900">1849\xe2\x80\x931900</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1849\xe2\x80\x931900">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>After the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Local_board_of_health#Public_Health_Act_1848" title="Local board of health">Public Health Act (1848)</a>, Penzance was one of the first towns to petition to form a <a href="/wiki/Local_board_of_health" title="Local board of health">local board of health</a>, doing so in September that year.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Following a report by a government inspector in February, the Board was established in 1849 which led to many facilities to enhance public health. The report<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> shows that most streets were <a href="/wiki/Macadam" title="Macadam">macadamised</a> or sometimes paved, and the town was lit by 121 gas lamps from October to March each year, although they were not lit when there was a full moon. Water was supplied from 6 public pumps, and there were a further 53 private wells. There were no sewage pipes at the time, waste being collected from the main streets by a refuse cart.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Railway station</dt></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Penzance_railway_station" title="Penzance railway station">Penzance railway station</a>, the terminus of the <a href="/wiki/West_Cornwall_Railway" title="West Cornwall Railway">West Cornwall Railway</a>, opened on 11 March 1852<sup id="cite_ref-Langley_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Langley-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> on the eastern side of the harbour, although trains only ran to <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">Redruth</a> at first. From 25 August 1852 the line was extended to <a href="/wiki/Truro_railway_station" title="Truro railway station">Truro</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway" title="Cornwall Railway">Cornwall Railway</a> linking that place with <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_railway_station" title="Plymouth railway station">Plymouth</a> was not opened until 4 May 1859. Passengers and goods had to change trains at Truro as the West Cornwall had been built using the <span class="nowrap">4&#160;ft&#160;<span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in</span> (<span class="nowrap">1,435&#160;mm</span>) <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a>, but the Cornwall Railway was built to the <span class="nowrap">7&#160;ft</span> (<span class="nowrap">2,134&#160;mm</span>) <a href="/wiki/Broad_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Broad gauge">broad gauge</a>. The West Cornwall Railway Act included a clause that it would be converted to broad gauge once it had been connected to another broad gauge line, but the company could not raise the funds to do so.\n</p><p>The line was sold to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> and its "Associated Companies" (the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_and_Exeter_Railway" title="Bristol and Exeter Railway">Bristol and Exeter Railway</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Devon_Railway_Company" title="South Devon Railway Company">South Devon Railway</a>) on 1 January 1866. The new owners quickly converted the line to <a href="/wiki/Dual_gauge" title="Dual gauge">mixed gauge</a> using three rails so that both broad and "narrow" trains could operate. Broad gauge <a href="/wiki/Goods_train" class="mw-redirect" title="Goods train">goods trains</a> started running in November that year, with through passenger trains running to <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a> from 1 March 1867.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The last broad gauge train arrived at 8.49pm on 20 May 1892, having left London Paddington at 10.15am that morning. The two locomotives, numbers <a href="/wiki/GWR_1076_Class" title="GWR 1076 Class">1256</a> and <a href="/wiki/GWR_3521_Class" title="GWR 3521 Class">3557</a>, took the carriages away to <a href="/wiki/Swindon_Works" title="Swindon Works">Swindon Works</a> at 9.57pm, and all trains since have been standard gauge.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The ability of the railway to carry fresh produce to distant markets such as <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, London and <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> enabled local farmers and fishermen to sell more produce and at better prices. The special "perishable" train soon became a feature of the railway, these being fast extra goods trains carrying <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broccoli" title="Broccoli">broccoli</a> or fish depending on the season. In August 1861 1,787 tons of potatoes, 867 tons of broccoli, and 1,063 tons of fish were dispatched from the station.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Fruit and flowers were also carried; the mild climate around Penzance and on the <a href="/wiki/Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Scilly Isles">Scilly Isles</a> meant that they were ready for market earlier and could command high prices.\n</p><p>The completion of the railway through Cornwall made it easier for tourists and invalids to enjoy the mild climate of Penzance. Bathing machines had been advertised for hire on the beach as early as 1823,<sup id="cite_ref-Guthrie_1994_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guthrie_1994-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and the town was already "noted for the pleasantness of its situation, the salubrity of its air, and the beauty of its natives".<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The town\'s first official guide book was published in 1860 and the Queen\'s Hotel opened on the seafront the following year. It was so successful that it was extended in 1871 and 1908.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Harbour</dt></dl>\n<p>At the same time as the railway was being built more improvements were being made to the harbour, with a second pier on the eastern side of the harbour, the Albert Pier, completed in 1853 to provide even better shelter for shipping,<sup id="cite_ref-Kittridge_1989_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kittridge_1989-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a> built on the Old Pier in 1855. The Scilly Isles Steam Navigation Company was founded in 1858 and placed in service the first steam ship on the route, <i>SS Little Western</i>. In 1870 the new <a href="/wiki/West_Cornwall_Steam_Ship_Company" title="West Cornwall Steam Ship Company">West Cornwall Steam Ship Company</a> joined the route, taking over the Scilly Isles Company the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1853 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution" title="Royal National Lifeboat Institution">Royal National Lifeboat Institution</a> stationed one of their boats in the town, the first since 1812, and maintained a station here until 1908 when the Watson Class <i>Elizabeth Blanche</i> was transferred to Newlyn as the first step towards setting up <a href="/wiki/Penlee_Lifeboat_Station" title="Penlee Lifeboat Station">Penlee Lifeboat Station</a>. The RNLI still use a boat house built on Jennings Street near the Promenade in 1884 to promote their activities.<sup id="cite_ref-Lifeboat_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lifeboat-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Penzance, with its dry dock and engineering facilities, was chosen as the western depot for <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a> that serviced all the lighthouses and <a href="/wiki/Lightvessel" title="Lightvessel">lightships</a> from <a href="/wiki/Start_Point,_Devon" title="Start Point, Devon">Start Point</a> to <a href="/wiki/Trevose_Head" title="Trevose Head">Trevose Head</a>. It was opened in October 1866<sup id="cite_ref-Carter_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carter-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> adjacent to the harbour and the <a href="/wiki/Buoy" title="Buoy">Buoy</a> Store became the Trinity House National Lighthouse Museum until 2005 when <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a> closed the museum.\n</p>\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Port_de_Penzance.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Penzance Harbour.</div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>Improvements</dt></dl>\n<p>In 1875 a local newspaper described the railway station as <i>a large dog\'s house of the nastiest and draughtiest kind</i><sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> but a series of works improved this part of the town during the 1880s. The original railway station was rebuilt with the present buildings and <a href="/wiki/Train_shed" title="Train shed">train shed</a> over the platforms (1880). The lower end of Market Jew Street was widened and a new road was built to link the station with the harbour over the Ross <a href="/wiki/Swing_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Swing Bridge">Swing Bridge</a> (1881), allowing the construction of proper sewers beneath. A larger dry dock replaced Matthews\' original facility (1880), and a floating harbour was made (1884) with <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">lock</a> gates to keep in the water at low tide.\n</p><p>Around the headland, public baths were opened on the Promenade in 1887 and the Morrab Gardens with its sub-tropical plants was opened two years later. A bandstand was added to the gardens in 1897.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1901 the town had a population of 3,088. The decennial census recorded a continuing decline in population until 1921, when just 2,616 people were recorded. The population then climbed to 4,888 (1931) then 5,545 (1951) \xe2\x80\x93 thus more than doubling in 30 years. It was now larger than at any time in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-UK_&amp;_Ireland_Genealogogy_-_Penzance_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UK_&amp;_Ireland_Genealogogy_-_Penzance-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> (The census boundaries changed in 1981 so these figures do not directly compare with those stated for the current population.)\n</p><p>A proposed electric <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tramway</a> along the Promenade to <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a>, which would have continued as a <a href="/wiki/Light_railway" title="Light railway">light railway</a> to <a href="/wiki/St_Just_in_Penwith" title="St Just in Penwith">St Just</a>, failed to gain authorisation in 1898. Instead <a href="/wiki/GWR_road_motor_services" title="GWR road motor services">motor buses</a> were put into service on 31 October 1903.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> These linked Penzance with <a href="/wiki/Marazion" title="Marazion">Marazion</a> and were operated by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a>, being introduced only 11 weeks after the railway\'s pioneering service between <a href="/wiki/Helston" title="Helston">Helston</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Lizard" title="The Lizard">the Lizard</a>. They were considered a success, carrying 16,091 passengers by the end of the year, so were followed the next spring by further routes to <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land&#39;s End">Land\'s End</a> and St Just. These services developed into the <a href="/wiki/First_Kernow" title="First Kernow">First Kernow</a> bus network that currently serves the area and is still centred on a terminus alongside <a href="/wiki/Penzance_railway_station" title="Penzance railway station">Penzance railway station</a>.\n</p><p>In 1912, Penzance erected its first electric street lamps and the town\'s first cinema opened.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The dry dock was sold on 25 August 1904 to N. Holman and Sons Limited, the engineering business that had been trading in Penzance since 1840. New workshops would be built during the 1930s, and the facility continued to be used by the Scilly ferries and other merchant ships, as well as Trinity House, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Maritime_Auxiliary_Service" title="Royal Maritime Auxiliary Service">Royal Maritime Auxiliary Service</a>. In 1951 a new vessel for the <a href="/wiki/King_Harry_Ferry" title="King Harry Ferry">King Harry Ferry</a> on the <a href="/wiki/River_Fal" title="River Fal">River Fal</a> was launched, built on the keel of an old <a href="/wiki/Landing_craft" title="Landing craft">landing craft</a>. A steam tug, the <i>Primrose</i>, was built in 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Land was reclaimed beside the Albert Pier in the 1930s to allow the railway station to be enlarged at a cost of \xc2\xa3134,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The 1880 building was retained, but extra platforms and sidings were provided to handle more perishable goods, as well as the increasing numbers of tourists.\n</p><p>In 1905 a new bandstand was built on the Promenade opposite the Queen\'s Hotel, and the Pavilion Theatre opened nearby in 1911, complete with a roof garden and caf\xc3\xa9.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_56-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Travel to Penzance was easier than ever, with the Great Western Railway introducing the <i><a href="/wiki/Cornish_Riviera_Express" title="Cornish Riviera Express">Cornish Riviera Express</a></i> on 1 July 1904, which left <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a> at 10:10<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am and arrived in Penzance just 7 hours later, two hours faster than the previous quickest service.<sup id="cite_ref-Langley_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Langley-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> (In 2018 it left Paddington at 10:03<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>am and took 5 hours and 8 minutes.) The railway promoted local tourism with postcards that were sold at its railway stations, and an annual guide book, <i><a href="/wiki/Cornish_Riviera_Express#Publicity" title="Cornish Riviera Express">The Cornish Riviera</a></i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Petre_Mais" title="Petre Mais">SPB Mais</a> described the town as "a suburb of Covent Garden, and a great fishing centre ... there is always something going on in its harbour".<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>1923 saw a new road link the harbour area and the Promenade, and in 1933 the St. Anthony Gardens were built, followed two years later by the Jubilee Bathing Pool opposite. Tourists could now make full use of the whole seafront between Penzance and Newlyn harbours.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30</a> from London to Land\xe2\x80\x99s End is a <a href="/wiki/Trunk_road" title="Trunk road">trunk road</a> as far as the Chy-an-Mor roundabout, a mile (1.6&#160;km) to the east of Penzance.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> After bypassing Penzance to the north the road continues to Land\xe2\x80\x99s End mainly as a rural A route. The distance from Penzance to London is 275 miles (443&#160;km) or about 5 hours by car.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Penzance_railway_station" title="Penzance railway station">Penzance railway station</a> is at the eastern end of Market Jew Street and close to the harbour. It is the southernmost station on the UK mainland rail network. It is the western terminus of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Main_Line" title="Cornish Main Line">Cornish Main Line</a> which runs above the beach to Marazion, affording passengers good views of St. Michael\'s Mount and Mount\'s Bay. Most services<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> are operated by <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway_(train_operating_company)" title="Great Western Railway (train operating company)">Great Western Railway</a>, both local services to <a href="/wiki/St_Erth_railway_station" title="St Erth railway station">St Erth</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_railway_station" title="St Ives railway station">St Ives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hayle_railway_station" title="Hayle railway station">Hayle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camborne_railway_station" title="Camborne railway station">Camborne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redruth_railway_station" title="Redruth railway station">Redruth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Truro_railway_station" title="Truro railway station">Truro</a>, and direct trains linking Penzance with <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_railway_station" title="Plymouth railway station">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exeter_St_Davids_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Exeter St Davids railway station">Exeter St Davids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Temple_Meads_railway_station" title="Bristol Temple Meads railway station">Bristol Temple Meads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reading_railway_station" title="Reading railway station">Reading</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Riviera" title="Night Riviera">Night Riviera</a></i> train offers an overnight <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_car" title="Sleeping car">sleeping car</a> service to and from Reading and London. Journey time to Plymouth is typically under 2 hours; to Bristol around 4 hours, and London less than 5\xc2\xbd hours.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/CrossCountry" title="CrossCountry">CrossCountry</a> run a small number of services (departing in the morning, arriving in the evening) providing a service to destinations such as <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_New_Street_railway_station" title="Birmingham New Street railway station">Birmingham New Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton_railway_station" title="Wolverhampton railway station">Wolverhampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derby_railway_station" title="Derby railway station">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheffield railway station">Sheffield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Piccadilly_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Piccadilly railway station">Manchester Piccadilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds_railway_station" title="Leeds railway station">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/York_railway_station" title="York railway station">York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_railway_station" title="Newcastle railway station">Newcastle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Waverley_railway_station" title="Edinburgh Waverley railway station">Edinburgh</a>.\n</p><p>The bus and coach station is next to the railway station from where <a href="/wiki/National_Express_Coaches" title="National Express Coaches">National Express</a> operates coach services to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Coach_Station" title="Victoria Coach Station">Victoria Coach Station</a> (taking around 9 hours) via <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a>. Local bus services run by <a href="/wiki/First_Kernow" title="First Kernow">First Kernow</a> connect Penzance with most major settlements in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cornwall" title="St Ives, Cornwall">St Ives</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Just_in_Penwith" title="St Just in Penwith">St Just</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Buryan" title="St Buryan">St Buryan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land&#39;s End">Land\'s End</a> and also <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>. \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg/220px-Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg/330px-Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg/440px-Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2800" data-file-height="1863" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scillonian_III_Penzance_285.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scillonian III docked in Penzance Harbour</div></div></div>\n<p>A ferry service operates between Penzance Harbour and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>. <i>The <a href="/wiki/Scillonian_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Scillonian III">Scillonian III</a></i>, carries both foot-passengers and cargo. Sailing time is about 2 hours and 40 minutes. For 49 years, <a href="/wiki/Penzance_Heliport" title="Penzance Heliport">Penzance Heliport</a> had a helicopter route to the Isles of Scilly run by <a href="/wiki/British_International_Helicopters" title="British International Helicopters">British International</a>, but this ended on 1 November 2012 due to rising costs and falling passenger numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> The company had sought to sell its current base, and relocate elsewhere in West Cornwall to raise funds, although no suitable site was found in time to save the service. A bus service run by the Skybus Airline Service connects with <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End_Airport" title="Land&#39;s End Airport">Land\'s End Airport</a> for fixed wing flights (15 minutes) to the Isles of Scilly. The buses leave from the railway station, near the <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxi</a> rank, rather than the bus station.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Newquay_Airport" title="Newquay Airport">Newquay Airport</a> is 41 miles (66&#160;km) away and offers flights to <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">Gatwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stansted_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Stansted Airport">Stansted</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> and many other places, including an increasing number of foreign destinations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Pzarms.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Old_Pzarms.gif/220px-Old_Pzarms.gif" width="220" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/Old_Pzarms.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="236" data-file-height="254" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Pzarms.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Common seal of the Borough of Penzance, used in lieu of a <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> 1614\xe2\x80\x931934 (now the Mayoral <a href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" class="mw-redirect" title="Seal (device)">Seal</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>Penzance was an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_borough" title="Ancient borough">ancient borough</a>, which became a <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a> in 1835.  Until 1934 the Municipal Borough of Penzance referred only to the town, but in 1934 the borough absorbed the nearby settlements of <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a>, Paul and <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Paul,_Cornwall" title="Paul, Cornwall">Paul</a> Urban District), <a href="/wiki/Gulval" title="Gulval">Gulval</a>  (from <a href="/wiki/West_Penwith_Rural_District" title="West Penwith Rural District">West Penwith Rural District</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Heamoor" title="Heamoor">Heamoor</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Madron" title="Madron">Madron</a> Urban District).\n</p><p>In 1974 the Penzance Borough was abolished and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a> District Council.  Initially the area of the former borough was <a href="/wiki/Unparished_area" title="Unparished area">unparished</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charter_Trustees" class="mw-redirect" title="Charter Trustees">Charter Trustees</a> were appointed, but in 1980 the civil parish of Penzance was reformed in the unparished area, and the new <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish council</a> elected to be called Penzance <a href="/wiki/Town_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Town Council">Town Council</a>. Penwith District Council was abolished in 2009, and the principal local authority in the area is now <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a>. For the purposes of election to Cornwall Council the civil parish of Penzance returns 6 members representing the Penzance East Division, Penzance Central Division, Penzance Promenade Division, Heamoor and Gulval and Newlyn and Mousehole Division.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Civil_Parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Parish">Civil Parish</a> of Penzance was further extended in 2004 under District of Penwith (Electoral Changes) Order 2002<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> to include Eastern Green, formerly part of the <a href="/wiki/Ludgvan" title="Ludgvan">Ludgvan</a> civil parish area.\n</p><p>Penzance Town Council does not have in place a system of political registration, so councillors do not form groups of any kind and technically act independently; however, the current political composition of the council (as of 22 August 2009) is as follows: Independent 10, <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> 8, <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a> 1 with one vacancy.\n</p><p>Penzance also elects a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mayors_of_Penzance" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Mayors of Penzance">mayor</a> every year in May from the members of Penzance town council.  Although mayors have a political affiliation, this position is largely ceremonial. The current mayor is Cllr. Dick Cliffe.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Penzance_Aerial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Penzance_Aerial.jpg/220px-Penzance_Aerial.jpg" width="220" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Penzance_Aerial.jpg/330px-Penzance_Aerial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Penzance_Aerial.jpg/440px-Penzance_Aerial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="555" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Penzance_Aerial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Penzance Harbour and surrounding area as seen from the air</div></div></div>\n<p>The economy of Penzance has, like those of many Cornish communities, suffered from the decline of the traditional industries of fishing, mining and agriculture. Penzance now has a mixed economy consisting of light industrial, tourism and retail businesses. However, like the rest of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, housing remains comparatively expensive, wages low and unemployment high. In 2007, house prices rose 274% from 10 years prior, the fastest rise in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The fishing port of <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a>, which falls within the parish boundaries, provides some employment in the area, but has also been greatly affected by the decline in the fishing industry over the last 30 years. In the 2004 index of deprivation Penzance is listed as having 3 wards within the top 10% for employment deprivation, Penzance East (125th most deprived in England) Penzance West (200th most deprived in England), and Penzance Central (712th most deprived in England).<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> 18-31% of households in the parish are described as "poor households".<sup id="cite_ref-poverty_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poverty-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The Penzance East Ward also has one of the highest unemployment rates in Cornwall, stated as 15.4%.<sup id="cite_ref-poverty_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poverty-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Sir_Humphry_Davy" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Humphry Davy">Sir Humphry Davy</a>\xe2\x80\x99s contribution to the mining industry, the <a href="/wiki/The_Miners_Association" title="The Miners Association">Miners\' Association</a> began <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> classes in Penzance. As mining in the area became more complex, the Penzance Mining and Science School was founded in 1890. The school continued to teach mining until 1910, when it was amalgamated with Camborne and Redruth Mining School forming the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Metalliferous_Mining" title="School of Metalliferous Mining">School of Metalliferous Mining</a> in Camborne, which is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Camborne_School_of_Mines" title="Camborne School of Mines">Camborne School of Mines</a>. This institution has now moved to the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Universities_in_Cornwall" title="Combined Universities in Cornwall">Combined Universities in Cornwall</a> campus at <a href="/wiki/Tremough" class="mw-redirect" title="Tremough">Tremough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>. From 1663, Penzance<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Stannary_town" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary town">coinage town</a>, responsible for the collection of tin taxation on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a>; it held this status for 176 years.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/William_Pryce" title="William Pryce">William Pryce</a> in his 1778 book <i>Mineralogia Cornubiensis</i>, Penzance coined more tin than the towns of <a href="/wiki/Liskeard" title="Liskeard">Liskeard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel" title="Lostwithiel">Lostwithiel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helston" title="Helston">Helston</a> put together.\n</p><p>Penzance also had its own submarine mine situated off the coast of the town next to the area known as <a href="/wiki/Wherrytown" title="Wherrytown">Wherrytown</a>. The mine, known as Wheal Wherry Mine, was worked from 1778 to 1798 and again from 1836 to 1840.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Founded by "a poor 57 year old miner" named Thomas Curtis, the mine was said to be "very rich at depth" and was connected to the shore by a wooden bridge; the ore was transported by <a href="/wiki/Wherry" title="Wherry">wherry</a> boat. The mine suffered considerable damage in 1798 when an American ship broke anchor off nearby <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a> and smashed into the bridge and head gear. Later attempts at mining were not as profitable.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 19th century and until 1912, Penzance had the largest tin <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> house in Cornwall, operated by the Bolitho family. The smelting works were situated at <a href="/wiki/Chyandour" title="Chyandour">Chyandour</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> As a consequence of this concentration of mining wealth, Penzance became a centre for commercial banking. The Bolitho Bank (now part of <a href="/wiki/Barclays_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Barclays Bank">Barclays Bank</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> and the Penzance Bank were two of the largest, although the latter collapsed in 1896.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main are classified as \xe2\x80\xb3conservation areas\xe2\x80\xb3 under the Penwith local plan<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> and are subject to special planning laws. The current <a href="/wiki/Conservation_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation area">conservation area</a> forms most of the core of the town of Penzance and the historic harbour areas of Newlyn and Mousehole.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> A number of Georgian and Regency buildings are present in the town. However, the majority of developments in the town centre itself are of mixed date, including several 20th-century buildings \xe2\x80\x93 one of which, the former Pearl Assurance building (now the Tremenheere <a href="/wiki/J_D_Wetherspoon" class="mw-redirect" title="J D Wetherspoon">Wetherspoon\'s</a> pub), was subject to comment by <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Betjeman" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir John Betjeman">Sir John Betjeman</a><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> who wrote, in 1963:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"Penzance has done much to destroy its attractive character. The older houses in the narrow centre round the market hall have been pulled down and third-rate commercial \'contemporary\', of which the Pearl Assurance building is a nasty example, are turning it into <a href="/wiki/Slough" title="Slough">Slough</a>".</p></blockquote><p> The <a href="/wiki/Market_Building,_Penzance" title="Market Building, Penzance">Market House and Old Town Hall</a> (1836-38) in Market Jew Street was designed by William Harris, District Surveyor for Bristol, in the Greek Revival style. It has a grand Ionic portico and is surmounted by a dome. Alterations for Lloyds Bank were carried out in 1922-25.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Penzance\'s former main street Chapel Street has a number of interesting features, including the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_House,_Penzance" title="Egyptian House, Penzance">Egyptian House</a>, the Union Hotel (including a Georgian <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a> which is no longer in use) and Branwell House, where the mother and aunt of the famous <a href="/wiki/Bront%C3%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Bront\xc3\xab">Bront\xc3\xab sisters</a> once lived. The Georgian theatre was built in c. 1787, closed in 1831, and is said to be where the first public announcement of <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Nelson\'s</a> victory at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Trafalgar</a> took place.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Regency_style" class="mw-redirect" title="Regency style">Regency</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian</a> terraces and houses (such as Regents Square and Clarence Street) are common in some parts of the town. The nearby sub-tropical <a href="/wiki/Morrab_Gardens" title="Morrab Gardens">Morrab Gardens</a>, has a large collection of tender trees and shrubs, many of which cannot be grown outdoors anywhere else in the UK. Also of interest is the seafront with its <a href="/wiki/Promenade" class="mw-redirect" title="Promenade">promenade</a> and the open-air seawater Jubilee <a href="/wiki/Lido_(swimming_pool)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lido (swimming pool)">Bathing Pool</a> (one of the oldest surviving <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> swimming baths in the country), built during Penzance\'s heyday as a fashionable <a href="/wiki/Seaside_resort" title="Seaside resort">seaside resort</a>. The pool was designed by Captain F. Latham, the Penzance Borough Engineer, and opened in 1935, the year of <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King George V</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Silver_Jubilee" class="mw-redirect" title="Silver Jubilee">Silver Jubilee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Grade_II_listed" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade II listed">grade II listed</a> pool is triangular with graceful curves and is considered the best surviving example of its type, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Saltdean_Lido" title="Saltdean Lido">Saltdean Lido</a> in Brighton.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> In early 2018, work began to add a <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">Geothermal</a> section to the pool, becoming the first of its kind in the United Kingdom to use this type of technology when it eventually opens<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Penzance promenade has been destroyed in parts several times by storms. The most recent example was on 7 March 1962 (Ash Wednesday), when large parts of the western end of the promenade, the nearby Bedford Bolitho Gardens (now a play park) and the village of <a href="/wiki/Wherrytown" title="Wherrytown">Wherrytown</a> suffered severe damage.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> On the outskirts of town is <a href="/wiki/Trereife_House" title="Trereife House">Trereife House</a>, a grade II listed <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne_style_architecture" title="Queen Anne style architecture">Queen Anne style</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manor_house" title="Manor house">manor house</a> which now offers accommodation and hosts events.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Penlee Quarry which is within the boundaries of the Penzance parish is a geological <a href="/wiki/SSSI" class="mw-redirect" title="SSSI">SSSI</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Penzance is home to two state run <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">comprehensive schools</a> (Mount\'s Bay Academy and <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy_School" title="Humphry Davy School">Humphry Davy School</a>) Its only independent school, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bolitho_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bolitho School (page does not exist)">Bolitho School</a> closed in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Bolitho School was an independent <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> school and was founded in the early 1990s following the financial collapse of the former School of St. Clare.\n</p><p>Post 16 education is catered for by Penwith College, founded in 1981 from the sixth form departments of the former Penzance Girls\' <a href="/wiki/Grammar_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammar School">Grammar School</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy_Grammar_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Humphry Davy Grammar School">Humphry Davy Grammar School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the Penzance parish there are 8 <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">primary schools</a>, including the newly created Pensans Primary School which was formed in 2006 from the former Penzance <a href="/wiki/Junior_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior School">Junior School</a> and the Lescudjack Infants School. There is also a special educational needs school within the parish boundary named Nancealverne.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Every June since 1991 the <a href="/wiki/Golowan_Festival" title="Golowan Festival">Golowan Festival</a> (which includes <a href="/wiki/Mazey_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Mazey Day">Mazey Day</a>) has been held in the town.\nBefore the 1930s Penzance was the scene of large <a href="/wiki/West_Cornwall_May_Day_celebrations" title="West Cornwall May Day celebrations">May Day celebrations</a>, which saw local children making and using tin \'May horns\' and \'May whistles\'; a small revival of these traditions took place on 4 May 2008. The <a href="/wiki/Feast_day" class="mw-redirect" title="Feast day">feast day</a> of <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a> was also celebrated in Penzance. The Corpus Christi fair has been a long-standing event in the town, and is currently undergoing attempts to revive it in a more traditional format.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Golowan4.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Golowan4.JPG/220px-Golowan4.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Golowan4.JPG/330px-Golowan4.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Golowan4.JPG/440px-Golowan4.JPG 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Golowan4.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mayor and <a href="/wiki/Mock_Mayor" title="Mock Mayor">Mock Mayor</a> speeches at the Golowan Festival 2005.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Allantide" title="Allantide">Allantide</a>, a Cornish version of Halloween, was also a popular activity in the town. Many of these customs were recorded by local antiquarian <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Ann_Courtney" title="Margaret Ann Courtney">M. A. Courtney</a> and have influenced historical views of traditional Cornish cultural activities.\n</p><p>In October 2010 the first full festival of music and the arts - the Penzance Proms -  was held (23\xe2\x80\x9331 October)\n</p><p>Every December Penzance holds the <a href="/wiki/Montol_Festival" title="Montol Festival">Montol Festival</a> a community arts event reviving many of the Cornish customs of Christmas, including traditional costumes, masks and <a href="/wiki/Guise_dancing" title="Guise dancing">guise dancing</a>. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_theatre">Music and theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Music and theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Penzance is the home of the <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Comic_opera" title="Comic opera">comic opera</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pirates_of_Penzance" title="The Pirates of Penzance">The Pirates of Penzance</a></i>. At the time the libretto was written, 1879, Penzance had become popular as a peaceful resort town, so the idea of it being overrun by pirates was amusing to contemporaries.\n</p><p>Penzance is home to the Acorn Arts Centre, sited within a former Methodist chapel. This provides a mixture of theatre, film, dance music and cabaret and is partially public funded. The Savoy is an independent cinema located in the town which opened in 1912 and was originally named the Victoria Hall Music Hall, the Savoy is one of the locations of performances sponsored by the Penwith Film Society (an arts cinema society based in the Penwith area). It is reputedly the oldest continuously used cinema in Britain.  Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Penzance was also home to a further 3 cinemas and at least 2 theatres, one of which, the Pavilion Theatre, is now home to an amusement arcade.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_galleries">Art galleries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Art galleries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Penzance is home to the new <a href="/wiki/Newlyn_Art_Gallery" title="Newlyn Art Gallery">Newlyn Art Gallery</a> establishment "The Exchange" which opened in 2007. Penzance is also the home of <a href="/wiki/Penlee_House" title="Penlee House">Penlee House</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Art_gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Art gallery">art gallery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a> notable for its collection of <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">paintings</a> by members of the <a href="/wiki/Newlyn_School" title="Newlyn School">Newlyn School</a>. Within Penzance town centre there are a growing number of commercial art galleries.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As in other Cornish towns and villages <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a> was historically the predominant Christian denomination. Prior to the 1980s Penzance had six Methodist churches, but this number has now been reduced to two, Chapel Street and High Street. There are Methodist churches in most of the surrounding villages including Newlyn\'s Trinity Methodist which operates <i>The Centre</i>, a busy multi-use church and community facility. Penzance is also home to a <a href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> citadel, a Roman Catholic church, two <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> parish churches (formerly three), a <a href="/wiki/Christadelphian" class="mw-redirect" title="Christadelphian">Christadelphian</a> meeting hall,<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> two <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelical</a> independent churches, the Penwith <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> moot, an independent <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> church and a <a href="/wiki/Buddhist" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> meditation group.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Church,_Penzance" title="St Mary&#39;s Church, Penzance">St Mary\'s Church</a> was built in 1832\xe2\x80\x9335, St Paul\'s (now closed) in 1843 and St John\'s in 1881. Penzance was formerly in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Madron" title="Madron">Madron</a> but St Mary\'s parish was established in 1871 and St Paul\'s in 1869. Two medieval chapels (<i>see above</i>, History) are known to have existed before the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Reformation</a>. St Mary\'s chapel was consecrated in 1680 but was in existence three hundred years earlier and the licensing of it in 1379 is recorded. The chaplain\'s stipend was \xc2\xa34 p.a. from the manor of Alverton. In 1549 its endowments were seized by the Crown though the burgesses made representations that they should not be. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lamplugh" title="Thomas Lamplugh">Thomas Lamplugh</a> Bishop of Exeter by much cajolery induced the mayor and burgesses to consent to consecration of the chapel in 1680. The curate then had a stipend of \xc2\xa35 p.a.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Arthur Langdon (1896) described a Cornish cross in front of the Market House. This cross stood in the Green Market until 1829 when it was removed to a house in North Street close to its original position. About 1868 it was moved again to the western end of the Market House. It is ornamented on the front and the sides. On the back there is said to be an inscription: PCMBUNT /QUICUMQ / VENIENT // COP / PIO / UM // O / + / X (described in 1850).  <sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Penzance is the home of Cornwall\'s most successful rugby team, the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Pirates" title="Cornish Pirates">Cornish Pirates</a> (Penzance and Newlyn RFC). The <a href="/wiki/RFU_Championship" title="RFU Championship">Championship</a> side moved to <a href="/wiki/Kenwyn_Rugby_Ground" title="Kenwyn Rugby Ground">Truro</a> in 2005 in a bid to reach the Premiership and was renamed the Cornish Pirates. In 2006 the side moved again, this time to the home ground of <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a> Rugby Club, before returning to Penzance in 2010 to play, once more, at the <a href="/wiki/Mennaye_Field" title="Mennaye Field">Mennaye Field</a>. The club has twice reached the play-off final for promotion to the <a href="/wiki/English_Premiership_(rugby_union)" class="mw-redirect" title="English Premiership (rugby union)">top tier</a> of English rugby in seasons <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_RFU_Championship" title="2010\xe2\x80\x9311 RFU Championship">2010\xe2\x80\x9311</a> and <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_RFU_Championship" title="2011\xe2\x80\x9312 RFU Championship">2011\xe2\x80\x9312</a>. Penzance was the home of <a href="/wiki/Mount%27s_Bay_RFC" class="mw-redirect" title="Mount&#39;s Bay RFC">Mount\'s Bay RFC</a>, founded in 1999, originally as a team for local players who could not play for the professional Cornish Pirates. They won promotion seven times in eight seasons to reach the <a href="/wiki/National_League_1" title="National League 1">third tier</a> of English rugby before folding in 2009 due to financial problems. The Pirates Amateurs are part of the Cornish Pirates and play in the lower levels of the league system. They ended their first season in second place in the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Cornwall_League_1" title="2010\xe2\x80\x9311 Cornwall League 1">Tribute Cornwall One</a> and won promotion to <a href="/wiki/Tribute_Cornwall/Devon" title="Tribute Cornwall/Devon">Tribute Cornwall/Devon</a> for 2011\xe2\x80\x9312 season, where they continue to play. They also won the Cornwall Clubs Cup in 2010\xe2\x80\x9311.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Penzance_A.F.C." title="Penzance A.F.C.">Penzance A.F.C.</a> were relegated from the Carlsberg <a href="/wiki/South_West_Peninsula_League" title="South West Peninsula League">South West Peninsula League</a> Premier Division in season <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_South_West_Peninsula_League" title="2012\xe2\x80\x9313 South West Peninsula League">2012\xe2\x80\x9313</a> and now play in Division One West. The club was one of the founding members of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_County_Football_Association" title="Cornwall County Football Association">Cornwall County Football Association</a> and the first winners of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Senior_Cup" title="Cornwall Senior Cup">Cornwall Senior Cup</a> in season 1892\xe2\x80\x9393. Penzance have won the Senior Cup on ten occasions, the last in 1980\xe2\x80\x9381.\n</p><p>Former England and <a href="/wiki/Surrey_County_Cricket_Club" title="Surrey County Cricket Club">Surrey</a> cricketer <a href="/wiki/Jack_Richards" title="Jack Richards">Jack Richards</a> (born Clifton James Richards) was born in Penzance. He played eight test matches and was the <a href="/wiki/Wicket_keeper" class="mw-redirect" title="Wicket keeper">wicket keeper</a> during England\'s <a href="/wiki/English_cricket_team_in_Australia_in_1986%E2%80%9387" title="English cricket team in Australia in 1986\xe2\x80\x9387">1986 Ashes</a> win in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. He learnt his cricket with the <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy_School" title="Humphry Davy School">Humphry Davy Grammar School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penzance_Cricket_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Penzance Cricket Club">Penzance Cricket Club</a>. The cricket club was founded in 1829 and are Cornwall\xe2\x80\x99s most successful club having been champions on 23 occasions and have had more players play for Cornwall than any other club. The club currently plays in the top tier of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Cricket_League" title="Cornwall Cricket League">Cornwall Cricket League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Penzance Wheelers, Britain\'s most south-westerly cycling club was founded in the 1930s. Their most famous member is <a href="/wiki/Tom_Southam" title="Tom Southam">Tom Southam</a> who represented Great Britain in five <a href="/wiki/UCI_Road_World_Championships" title="UCI Road World Championships">World Championships</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> Penzance Wheelers predecessors was the Penzance Bicycle Club who were in existence in 1880 and, for example, on 30 April had a run to <a href="/wiki/St_Just_in_Penwith" title="St Just in Penwith">St Just</a>, returning via <a href="/wiki/Trewellard" title="Trewellard">Trewellard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pendeen" title="Pendeen">Pendeen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> Mount\'s Bay Harriers, a <a href="/wiki/Triathlon" title="Triathlon">triathlon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Road_running" title="Road running">running</a> club founded in 2005 are based at <a href="/wiki/Mounts_Bay_Academy" title="Mounts Bay Academy">Mount\'s Bay School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heamoor" title="Heamoor">Heamoor</a>. Athletes from the club participate at most road races and triathlons in Cornwall as well as many further afield.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Other sporting clubs and organisations include Penzance Hockey Club, with male and female teams, based at the Penzance Astro Park, Penzance Tennis Club at Penlee Park and lawn bowling at Penzance Bowling Club and Penlee (Newlyn) Bowling Club.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mini_Transat_6.50" title="Mini Transat 6.50">Mini Transat 6.50</a> (now the Transit 6.50) transatlantic yacht race started from Penzance (hosted by <a href="/wiki/Penzance_Sailing_Club" title="Penzance Sailing Club">Penzance Sailing Club</a>) from its conception in 1977 to the fourth edition of the race in 1983.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span FM.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the area between Marazion and Penzance was mainly marsh, people tended to avoid the Eastern Green because of the <i>"White Lady"</i>. She would jump onto a horse (already with rider) and ride pillion as far as the Red River, Chyandour (not the <a href="/wiki/Red_River,_Marazion" class="mw-redirect" title="Red River, Marazion">Red River</a> at Marazion). Her identity and reasons for haunting are unknown. Mr William Richards of Chapel Street is reputed to be the last person to have seen her.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span Penzance was the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Maria_Branwell" title="Maria Branwell">Maria Branwell</a>, mother of three famous novelists \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Bront\xc3\xab">Charlotte Bront\xc3\xab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Bront\xc3\xab">Emily Bront\xc3\xab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Bront%C3%AB" title="Anne Bront\xc3\xab">Anne Bront\xc3\xab</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sir_Humphry_Davy">Sir Humphry Davy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sir Humphry Davy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sir_Humphry_Davy" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Humphry Davy">Sir Humphry Davy</a></div>\n<p>Penzance was the birthplace of the chemist Sir <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a>. Davy was President of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> and invented the process of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>; was the first person to isolate <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>; was the first person to discover <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a>; as well as proving (with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>) that diamonds are made of pure carbon. Today he is possibly best known as the inventor of the Miner\'s Safety Lamp, or <a href="/wiki/Davy_lamp" title="Davy lamp">Davy lamp</a>. There is a statue of Davy at the top of Market Jew Street, near the house in which he was born. One of Penzance\'s secondary schools is also named after the scientist, and runs as a music and maths community college. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dunkin_(Penzance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Dunkin (Penzance)">Robert Dunkin</a>, a Penzance sadler and maker of scientific instruments taught Davy the basis of practical science.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twinning">Twinning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penzance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Twinning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Penzance is <a href="/wiki/Town_twinning" class="mw-redirect" title="Town twinning">twinned</a> with the following towns:<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Concarneau" title="Concarneau">Concarneau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, France</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bendigo,_Victoria" class="mw-redirect" title="Bendigo, Victoria">Bendigo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, Australia</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Nevada_City,_California" title="Nevada City, California">Nevada City</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, USA</li></ul>\n<p>and\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cuxhaven" title="Cuxhaven">Cuxhaven</a>, Germany. From 1967 to 1974 Penzance was twinned with Cuxhaven, though between 1974 and 2009 this twinning arrangement was passed to the now defunct <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a> District Council.
