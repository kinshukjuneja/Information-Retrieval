class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dorset_Geology.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Geological map of Dorset</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Dorset</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/\xc9\x94\xcb\x90r/: &#39;ar&#39; in &#39;war&#39;">\xc9\x94\xcb\x90r</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/\xc9\xaa/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">\xc9\xaa</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> (or <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">archaically</a>, <b>Dorsetshire</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">county</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a> on the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> coast. Covering an area of 2,653 square kilometres (1,024&#160;sq&#160;mi); it borders <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a> to the north-west, <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> to the north-east, and <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> to the east. The great variation in its landscape owes much to the underlying geology which includes an almost unbroken sequence of rocks from 200 Ma to 40 Ma and superficial deposits from 2 Ma to the present.<sup id="cite_ref-CHA5-6_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHA5-6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In general the oldest rocks (<a href="/wiki/Early_Jurassic" title="Early Jurassic">Early Jurassic</a>) appear in the far west of the county, with the most recent (<a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>) in the far east. Jurassic rocks also underlie the Blackmore Vale and comprise much of the coastal cliff in the west and south of the county; and although younger Cretaceous rocks crown some of the highpoints in the west, they are mainly to be found in the centre and east of the county.<sup id="cite_ref-CHA6_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHA6-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Dorset\'s coastline is one of the most visited and studied coastlines in the world because it shows, along the course of 95 miles (153&#160;km) (including some of east <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>), rocks from the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> and up to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>, documenting the entire <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> era with well-preserved <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Throughout Dorset there are a number of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> ridges.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The largest and most notable is the band of <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> that runs from the south-west to the north-east of the county and forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Chalk_Group" title="Chalk Group">Chalk Group</a> that underlies much of the south of England, including <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Downs" title="South Downs">South Downs</a>. Between the bands of limestone and chalk are wide <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> <a href="/wiki/River_valley" class="mw-redirect" title="River valley">vales</a> with <a href="/wiki/Flood_plain" class="mw-redirect" title="Flood plain">flood plains</a>.\n</p><p>South-east Dorset, around <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a>, lies on younger and less resistant beds: Eocene clays (mainly <a href="/wiki/London_Clay" title="London Clay">London Clay</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravels</a>. These rocks produce thin soils that historically have supported a <a href="/wiki/Heathland" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathland">heathland</a> habitat. The chalk and limestone hills of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Purbeck" title="Isle of Purbeck">Purbecks</a> lie atop Britain\'s largest onshore <a href="/wiki/Oil_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil field">oil field</a>. The field, operated from <a href="/wiki/Wytch_Farm" title="Wytch Farm">Wytch Farm</a>, produces a high-quality oil and has the world\'s oldest continuously pumping well at <a href="/wiki/Kimmeridge" title="Kimmeridge">Kimmeridge</a>, which has been in use since the early 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The source of this oil is the organic-rich shales found in the lower lias. Landslides along the coast have been known to ignite these shales causing cliff fires, the most recent of which occurred in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NatEng_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatEng-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Permian_and_Triassic"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Permian and Triassic</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-2"><a href="#380\xe2\x80\x93201_Ma_(late_Devonian_\xe2\x80\x93_late_Triassic)"><span class="tocnumber">1.1</span> <span class="toctext">380&#8211;201 Ma (late Devonian &#8211; late Triassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 380\xe2\x80\x93201 Ma (late Devonian \xe2\x80\x93 late Triassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>380 million years ago (<a href="/wiki/Year#Symbols_y_and_yr" title="Year">Ma</a>) the landmass that was later to form Southern Britain was some 19 degrees south of the equator and lay on the northern shore of an ocean basin that separated the continents of <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gondwanaland" class="mw-redirect" title="Gondwanaland">Gondwanaland</a>. When these continents collided to form the single super-continent of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>, the sediments on the ocean floor were pushed up and over while the molten rock below the surface was forced out.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Today, these <a href="/wiki/Igneous_intrusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Igneous intrusion">igneous intrusions</a> and the red <a href="/wiki/Sandstones" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandstones">sandstones</a>, visible in the neighbouring county of Devon, slope away from west to east and are deeply buried beneath younger deposits in Dorset.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>[Needs section on the Triassic adding]\n</p><p>Around 204 Ma Dorset, now 30 degrees north, was under water and the first <a href="/wiki/Ammonite" class="mw-redirect" title="Ammonite">ammonites</a> (<i>Psiloceras planorbis</i>) appear among the shales and limestones that make up the lower Lias.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="204.E2.80.93185_Ma_.28late_Triassic_.E2.80.93_early_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="204\xe2\x80\x93185_Ma_(late_Triassic_\xe2\x80\x93_early_Jurassic)">204&#8211;185 Ma (late Triassic &#8211; early Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 204\xe2\x80\x93185 Ma (late Triassic \xe2\x80\x93 early Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg/220px-Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg/330px-Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg/440px-Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3195" data-file-height="1972" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The blue lias cliffs at Lyme Regis</div></div></div>\n<p>Formed somewhere between 185 Ma and 204 Ma, in what was then a shallow marine environment, the <b>Lower Lias</b> <a href="/wiki/Lias_Group" title="Lias Group">Lias</a> is composed of <a href="/wiki/Blue_Lias" title="Blue Lias">Blue Lias</a>, Black Ven Marls and Green Ammonite Beds. Mostly covered by lush vegetation, it forms the floor of <a href="/wiki/Marshwood_Vale" title="Marshwood Vale">Marshwood Vale</a> in the west of Dorset and can be seen in stream beds, where the land has been excavated, and along the coast to the west of <a href="/wiki/Seatown" title="Seatown">Seatown</a>. The sides of the vale are mainly made from the clays and sands of the upper and lower Lias while younger strata from the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous_Period" class="mw-redirect" title="Cretaceous Period">Cretaceous Period</a> (145-66 Ma), crown the higher points.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Blue Lias is the lowest of the Liassic stratum and where it is visible on the coast near <a href="/wiki/Lyme_Regis" title="Lyme Regis">Lyme Regis</a> is layered with hard limestone and oil-rich shale.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The iron <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrites</a> in the clay can heat up when exposed to the air and occasionally ignite the shales.<sup id="cite_ref-E23_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E23-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Around Lyme Regis, during the 18th and 19th centuries, the collecting and sale of fossils became a popular occupation. Landslides and the excavation of the clays, used in cement production, exposed not only an abundance of ammonites of varying size, but much larger specimens such as <a href="/wiki/Ichthyosaur" title="Ichthyosaur">ichthyosaurs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plesiosaur" class="mw-redirect" title="Plesiosaur">plesiosaurs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E23_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E23-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Although these rocks were formed underwater, the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_wood" title="Fossil wood">fossil wood</a>, land-dwelling animals and a <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaur</a> suggest that dry land was close by.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic">Jurassic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jurassic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="185.E2.80.93182_Ma_.28early_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="185\xe2\x80\x93182_Ma_(early_Jurassic)">185&#8211;182 Ma (early Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 185\xe2\x80\x93182 Ma (early Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Laid down between 185 Ma and 182 Ma years ago in a shallower marine environment that was subjected to much more turbulent weather conditions, the sediments of the <b>Middle Lias</b> are not as muddy as those in the lower Lias, and are much nearer to silt and sand.<sup id="cite_ref-E25_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E25-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> These sands are thought to have come from the islands that now form part of Cornwall and South Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-E25_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E25-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Again, inland exposure is poor, although the middle Lias is visible along the spring line of the hills that surround the Marshwood Vale. The cliffs either side of <a href="/wiki/Eype" title="Eype">Eype</a> and the coastal stretch between <a href="/wiki/Thorncombe" title="Thorncombe">Thorncombe</a> and Watton Cliff provide the best view.<sup id="cite_ref-E25_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E25-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The base of the middle Lias is composed of three thick layers of calcareous sandstone beds separated by marls. These resistant bands form massive buttresses along the sea-cliffs and, where eroded, boulder aprons on the foreshore.\n</p><p>Above these three layers is the Eype clay, which was probably deposited in deeper, calmer waters.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Ammonites found in this layer have apparently been attacked by larger crustaceans. The fossilised remains of large numbers of brittle stars, found towards the end of this deposition, indicate that they were rapidly covered. Theories put forward suggest a great storm or tidal wave was the cause and indeed many of the remains appear to have been swept along the sediment, some losing limbs on the way. This particular section of the lias is appropriately known as the "starfish bed".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The succeeding layers of Downcliffe and Thorncombe sands also appear to have been deposited in a periodically stormy environment where silts and sands dominate. Pebbles and cobbles are found here, encrusted with fossilised animals, which suggests that some of the sediments were already <a href="/wiki/Lithification#Lithification" title="Lithification">lithified</a>; there is also some evidence of localised fault activity.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The last layer in the middle Lias is the Marlstone Rock Bed.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="182.E2.80.93174_Ma_.28early_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="182\xe2\x80\x93174_Ma_(early_Jurassic)">182&#8211;174 Ma (early Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 182\xe2\x80\x93174 Ma (early Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:East_Cliffs,_West_Bay,_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/East_Cliffs%2C_West_Bay%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg/220px-East_Cliffs%2C_West_Bay%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/East_Cliffs%2C_West_Bay%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg/330px-East_Cliffs%2C_West_Bay%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/East_Cliffs%2C_West_Bay%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg/440px-East_Cliffs%2C_West_Bay%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:East_Cliffs,_West_Bay,_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_758051.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The distinctive yellow cliffs near West Bay, formed of Bridport sand</div></div></div>\n<p>The lowest part of the <b>Upper Lias</b>, formed around 182 Ma, is known as the junction bed and despite representing 3,000,000 years, is only 0.6 to 1.5 metres thick, where it can be seen at <a href="/wiki/Eype_Mouth" title="Eype Mouth">Eype Mouth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E29_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E29-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In Yorkshire the equivalent strata is around 100 metres thick, suggesting that Dorset remained underwater much longer and was robbed of supplies of sand and clay. This bed is composed of fine-grained limestones from white to pale pink in colour.<sup id="cite_ref-E29_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E29-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The clearest evidence of fault activity exists in a section of cliff between West Bay and Eype Mouth, where the junction bed thickens in a section of steps, and fractures in the rock contain sediment from the ocean bed which, most likely, was sucked in as the cracks opened.<sup id="cite_ref-E29_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E29-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The remaining part of the upper Lias is the bright yellow <a href="/wiki/Bridport_Sand_Formation" title="Bridport Sand Formation">Bridport sandstone</a> that gives the cliffs between West Bay and Burton Bradstock their distinctive colour.<sup id="cite_ref-E31_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E31-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The sands form a band that runs inland to <a href="/wiki/Yeovil" title="Yeovil">Yeovil</a>. In the east of the county, these sands are some 900 metres below the surface and form the Wytch Farm oilfield\xe2\x80\x99s middle reservoir.<sup id="cite_ref-E31_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E31-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="174.E2.80.93169_Ma_.28early_.E2.80.93_middle_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="174\xe2\x80\x93169_Ma_(early_\xe2\x80\x93_middle_Jurassic)">174&#8211;169 Ma (early &#8211; middle Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 174\xe2\x80\x93169 Ma (early \xe2\x80\x93 middle Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The succeeding <a href="/wiki/Inferior_Oolite" title="Inferior Oolite">Inferior Oolite</a> was deposited in a shallow, tropical marine environment, 35 degrees north of the equator. It is only 2 metres thick in the south but increases to 20 metres in the north, near Sherborne.<sup id="cite_ref-E32_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E32-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It contains many fossils, including bryozoans, brachiopods, ammonites, belemnites, gastropods, bivalves and echinoids, and like the previous middle and upper Lias shows signs of fault-controlled deposition.<sup id="cite_ref-E32_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E32-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It appears in some places that the sea floor was breached and sediments and fossils were trapped like those in the junction bed between West Bay and Eype. Most of Dorset was starved of sediment and condensed limestones collected.<sup id="cite_ref-E32_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E32-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> These limestone beds are often iron rich, lending them a rusty colour. One particular bed, exposed at Burton Cliff, contains large numbers of orange, discus-shaped concretions, which on closer inspection reveal themselves to be pieces of shell from a species of mussel, coated with a thin layer of iron-rich sediment.<sup id="cite_ref-E33_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E33-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the upper section of the Inferior Oolite there are two beds packed with numerous types of sponge, which can be seen around Shipton Gorge and Burton Bradstock.<sup id="cite_ref-E34_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E34-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Despite being formed in a marine environment, the oolite, like the lower Lias, contains some fossilised remains of land-dwelling creatures, including two species of <i><a href="/wiki/Megalosaurus" title="Megalosaurus">Megalosaurus</a></i> that were found near Sherborne.<sup id="cite_ref-E34_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E34-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="169.E2.80.93167_Ma_.28middle_.E2.80.93_late_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="169\xe2\x80\x93167_Ma_(middle_\xe2\x80\x93_late_Jurassic)">169&#8211;167 Ma (middle &#8211; late Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 169\xe2\x80\x93167 Ma (middle \xe2\x80\x93 late Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Fuller%27s_Earth_Formation" title="Fuller&#39;s Earth Formation">Fuller\'s Earth Formation</a> clays were deposited across much of southern Dorset when the continental shelf subsided, forcing the region deeper underwater. The lower clays are visible at <a href="/wiki/Burton_Bradstock" title="Burton Bradstock">Burton Bradstock</a> and contain large numbers of <i>Bositra buchi</i>. It has been suggested that these bivalves were able to swim freely and thus able to avoid the soupy mud on the sea floor.<sup id="cite_ref-E35/36_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E35/36-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> A limestone known as Fuller\'s Earth Rock is found in the north-west of the county, between the clays south and east of Sherborne. It is rich in ammonites and bivalves.<sup id="cite_ref-E36_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E36-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Previously referred to as the Upper Fuller\'s Earth Clay, the succeeding Frome Clay can be seen to the west of West Bay where it forms the major part of the West Cliff. It contains many <a href="/wiki/Brachiopods" class="mw-redirect" title="Brachiopods">Brachiopods</a>, particularly in the Weymouth Anticline where the oyster beds are 5 metres thick.<sup id="cite_ref-E36_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E36-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> At the junction between the Frome Clay and the overlying Forest Marble is the Boueti Bed, so called because of the large numbers of the brachiopod, <i>Goniorhynchia boueti</i> found there. It is best viewed on the <a href="/wiki/Herbury" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbury">Herbury</a> peninsula south of <a href="/wiki/Langton_Herring" title="Langton Herring">Langton Herring</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E36_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E36-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The limestone known as Forest Marble is not a true metamorphic marble but it takes a high polish, and has been used as a building material and marble substitute for many years.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Outcrops of Forest Marble are concentrated around the Weymouth Anticline, the coastal escarpment between Burton Bradstock and <a href="/wiki/Abbotsbury" title="Abbotsbury">Abbotsbury</a>, and inland as far as <a href="/wiki/Bothenhampton" title="Bothenhampton">Bothenhampton</a>, where they disappear below younger Cretaceous deposits. Surfacing once more at <a href="/wiki/Rampisham" title="Rampisham">Rampisham</a>, they turn east and then north into <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E38_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E38-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The limestone does not form one continuous bed as previously thought, but rather forms pockets in a formation which is predominately clay. It is likely that severe tropical storms swept up large quantities of shells and other invertebrates and deposited them in this way.<sup id="cite_ref-E38_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E38-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The formation of the Forest Marble suggests a shallowing of the sea and the remains of pieces of tree, and bits of land-dwelling animals, are found among the turtles, frogs and salamanders within.<sup id="cite_ref-E38_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E38-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="167.E2.80.93156_Ma_.28late_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="167\xe2\x80\x93156_Ma_(late_Jurassic)">167&#8211;156 Ma (late Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 167\xe2\x80\x93156 Ma (late Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Between 167 Ma and 156 Ma, the future Dorset, at 36 degrees north, still had a tropical climate and the limestones, clays, silts and sands from this period were laid down in a marine environment that ranged from deep water to tidal shallows.<sup id="cite_ref-E39_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E39-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Above the Forest Marble lies the <a href="/wiki/Cornbrash" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornbrash">Cornbrash</a> sequence which is dominated by rubbly limestone. Unsuitable for building stone, these limestones have been used lime production and road construction. In the north of the county an already lithified Cornbrash floor was being eroded, and the resulting pebbles gathered serpulid worms, bryozoans and bivalves as they rolled around beneath the shallow sea.<sup id="cite_ref-E39_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E39-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The shallow water in which these limestones were deposited was followed by marginally deeper water in which the <a href="/wiki/Kellaways_Formation" title="Kellaways Formation">Kellaway Beds</a> were laid down. The Kellaway Beds are only visible in the banks of streams and rivers, and the brick pits at <a href="/wiki/Chickerell" title="Chickerell">Chickerell</a> and Rampisham where the succeeding <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Clay_Formation" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford Clay Formation">Oxford Clays</a> were quarried.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The Oxford Clays were deposited in much deeper water and cover the valley floors between Weymouth and the Blackmore Vale.They can also be seen in the cliffs at Tidmoor Point, <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Hill,_Dorset" title="Jordan Hill, Dorset">Jordan Hill</a> and the previously mentioned brick pits at Chickerell.<sup id="cite_ref-E40_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E40-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Poor exposure, coupled with a decline in the brick industry has made detailed mapping of the clays difficult which now relies heavily on trenches dug for services. These excavations have unearthed some impressive examples of <a href="/wiki/Septarian_Nodule" class="mw-redirect" title="Septarian Nodule">Septarian Nodules</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E40_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E40-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The exposure of Nothe Grit, to the south of Weymouth, suggests a return to shallower water conditions and a more turbulent environment. In the following 4.5 million years a cycle of lagoonal, tidal and deep water environments caused by a world-wide fluctuations in sea levels, deposited beds of sand, clay and limestone across the entire county.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The Corallian strata can be seen on the Weymouth anticline, between Abbotsbury and <a href="/wiki/Osmington" title="Osmington">Osmington</a>. It then disappears to emerge as a low escarpment between <a href="/wiki/Mappowder" title="Mappowder">Mappowder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cucklington" title="Cucklington">Cucklington</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a> border. Near the top of this formation is the Ringstead Waxy Clay in which large delta shaped oysters are found and at the top of the Corallian is the Ringstead Coral Bed.<sup id="cite_ref-E42_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E42-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Benecliff grit, a soft sandstone containing large doggers, is well exposed at the headland, Bran Point. Once a reservoir, oil sometimes seeps from the cliff here, and an oily residue is present in the sands nearby.<sup id="cite_ref-E42_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E42-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="156.E2.80.93150_Ma_.28Late_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="156\xe2\x80\x93150_Ma_(Late_Jurassic)">156&#8211;150 Ma (Late Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 156\xe2\x80\x93150 Ma (Late Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beach_and_cliffs,_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Beach_and_cliffs%2C_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg/220px-Beach_and_cliffs%2C_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Beach_and_cliffs%2C_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg/330px-Beach_and_cliffs%2C_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Beach_and_cliffs%2C_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg/440px-Beach_and_cliffs%2C_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beach_and_cliffs,_Egmont_Bight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_900296.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kimmeridge Clay in the cliffs at Egmont Bight</div></div></div>\n<p>The varied rock types of the Corallian are superseded by the Kimmeridge clay, a bed of dark clays and shales up to 500 metres thick. These deposits occurred in a deep marine environment, 38 degrees north, 140 Ma to 145 Ma.<sup id="cite_ref-E43_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E43-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Deposited in an oxygen deficient environment, the Kimmeridge beds make up the major part of the Purbeck Monocline and the periphery of the Weymouth Anticline. They form a low lying band running north to south through the <a href="/wiki/Blackmore_Vale" title="Blackmore Vale">Blackmore Vale</a>, and underpin the strip of land between the isle of Portland and the mainland.<sup id="cite_ref-E43_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E43-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The unusual iron rich sediment near Abbotsbury was, in a departure from the norm, deposited in shallow iron rich water and the pale bands throughout the formation were caused by <a href="/wiki/Coccolith" title="Coccolith">Coccoliths</a> following periodic blooms of algae.\n</p><p>Many of the fossilised remains are squashed flat, indicating that the Kimmeridge beds were heavily compacted, perhaps to an eighth of their original thickness. This was not sufficient to produce oil reservoirs like those beneath the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, yet the oil rich shales have, in the past, been economically important as a local fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="150-145_Ma_.28Late_Jurassic.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="150-145_Ma_(Late_Jurassic)">150-145 Ma (Late Jurassic)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 150-145 Ma (Late Jurassic)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>It is unclear whether the formations created between the Upper Jurassic and Lower Cretaceous, were deposited across the whole of Dorset like the preceding beds, and subsequently eroded, or only formed in the areas in which they are now found.<sup id="cite_ref-E47_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E47-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Portland sands were created in a deep marine environment whereas the <a href="/wiki/Portland_stone" title="Portland stone">Portland stone</a> was a near-shore deposit.<sup id="cite_ref-E47_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E47-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The lower Portland Sand formation, usually greyish blue in colour, is a <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> best viewed on the West Weares, Gad Cliff and Hounstout. The overlying Portland stone is a distinctive white <a href="/wiki/Oolite" title="Oolite">oolite</a> containing thick shelled fossils and marine algae.<sup id="cite_ref-E48_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E48-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The marine algae which formed large reefs, is responsible for the occasional stacked and arched bands in the formation and is best viewed in the Roach, an extremely fossiliferous limestone confined to the north of <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Portland" title="Isle of Portland">Isle of Portland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To the north of the oolite is a fine grained limestone, which was deposited in calm, shallow water. It contains flint-like rocks known as <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> and if it wasn\'t for the presence of very large ammonites, might be mistaken for an early chalk.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Previously thought to be around the middle of the Purbeck Strata, modern research suggests that the boundary between the Jurassic and Cretaceous periods lies within the basal few metres of the Lower Purbeck.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="145.E2.80.93140_Ma_.28Late_Jurassic_.E2.80.93_early_Cretaceous.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="145\xe2\x80\x93140_Ma_(Late_Jurassic_\xe2\x80\x93_early_Cretaceous)">145&#8211;140 Ma (Late Jurassic &#8211; early Cretaceous)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 145\xe2\x80\x93140 Ma (Late Jurassic \xe2\x80\x93 early Cretaceous)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fossil_Forest,_West_Lulworth,_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Fossil_Forest%2C_West_Lulworth%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg/220px-Fossil_Forest%2C_West_Lulworth%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Fossil_Forest%2C_West_Lulworth%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg/330px-Fossil_Forest%2C_West_Lulworth%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Fossil_Forest%2C_West_Lulworth%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg/440px-Fossil_Forest%2C_West_Lulworth%2C_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fossil_Forest,_West_Lulworth,_Dorset_-_geograph.org.uk_-_603310.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Part of the fossil forest, West Lulworth</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Purbeck_Group" title="Purbeck Group">Purbeck Limestone Group</a> is a succession of limestones, shales and clays named after the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Purbeck" title="Isle of Purbeck">Isle of Purbeck</a>, laid down mainly in very shallow lagoons and freshwater lakes, with only occasional marine influence at the lower levels where <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporites</a> are found.<sup id="cite_ref-E51_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E51-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The muddled sediments around Lulworth are thought to be caused by a thick layer of these evaporites dissolving. There is also evidence of uplift and erosion of earlier formations. The climate around this time had changed from a tropical to a sub-tropical one, with pronounced wet and dry seasons as indicated by the growth rings in the fosslised trees also found in the lower levels of the Purbeck strata.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> These trees grew around the low coastal areas and were, from time to time, subjected to drowning from the sea. The fallen trees and their stumps became covered by <a href="/wiki/Stromatolites" class="mw-redirect" title="Stromatolites">stromatolites</a> which trapped sediments. The limestones at this level, which vary greatly in colour, are composed of densely packed shells of the freshwater snail <i><a href="/wiki/Viviparus" title="Viviparus">Viviparus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Their ability to take a very high polish has led them to become known as <a href="/wiki/Purbeck_Marble" title="Purbeck Marble">Purbeck Marble</a>. Like the older Forest Marble it is a sedimentary marble rather than a true metamorphic one.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Purbeck Limestone Group has yielded a diverse selection of fossilised remains, including rare reptiles, amphibians and mammals, for which the formation has become internationally important.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cretaceous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="140.E2.80.93125_Ma_.28early_Cretaceous.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="140\xe2\x80\x93125_Ma_(early_Cretaceous)">140&#8211;125 Ma (early Cretaceous)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 140\xe2\x80\x93125 Ma (early Cretaceous)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wealden_Supergroup" class="mw-redirect" title="Wealden Supergroup">Wealden Beds</a> are confined to the south and increase in thickness from 107 metres to 716 metres, between <a href="/wiki/Upwey,_Dorset" title="Upwey, Dorset">Upwey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swanage" title="Swanage">Swanage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E57_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E57-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> By 140 million years ago, the waters covering Dorset\'s landmass had subsided leaving the area almost completely terrestrial save for the large rivers that dominated it.<sup id="cite_ref-E57_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E57-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> These rivers drained the upland areas to the north and west, and were responsible for depositing much of the succeeding sediment across southern Dorset.<sup id="cite_ref-E57_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E57-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Comprising clays, silts, sands and grits, they cover the floors of the valleys between Lulworth and Swanage. The sands and grits are also exposed in the cliffs there, and form ridges along the valleys.<sup id="cite_ref-E57_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E57-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Traces of granite from Cornubia, the landmass that later became Cornwall, were also washed down.<sup id="cite_ref-E59_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E59-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The Wealden Beds contain a great deal of plant matter and <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> indicating periodic fires in the region. At <a href="/wiki/Mupe_Bay" title="Mupe Bay">Mupe Bay</a>, some of the sands have been bound together by oil and formed into sandstone boulders, suggesting that oil production had already begun when these sands were deposited.<sup id="cite_ref-E59_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E59-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="125.E2.80.93100_Ma_.28early_.E2.80.93_late_Cretaceous.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="125\xe2\x80\x93100_Ma_(early_\xe2\x80\x93_late_Cretaceous)">125&#8211;100 Ma (early &#8211; late Cretaceous)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 125\xe2\x80\x93100 Ma (early \xe2\x80\x93 late Cretaceous)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The marginal marine incursions that occurred in the previous period increased in severity until Dorset was completely underwater. The geology from this period indicates a gradual flooding of the county from the south-east to the north-west.<sup id="cite_ref-E60_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E60-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The earliest deposit, the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Greensand_Group" title="Lower Greensand Group">Lower Greensand</a>, covers the preceding Wealden Beds in the south, and the Oxford and Kimmeridge clays in the north.<sup id="cite_ref-E60_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E60-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> It is so called because of its green colour which is caused by the presence of the mineral <a href="/wiki/Glauconite" title="Glauconite">glauconite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E62_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E62-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the west, overlying the eroded Lias, is a thin layer of <a href="/wiki/Gault_Clay" class="mw-redirect" title="Gault Clay">Gault Clay</a> which decreases in depth from 27 metres to 7 metres the further west it stretches, and can be located by the spring lines around the chalk ridges and escarpments.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The water that sits between these two beds is responsible for the many landslips which have occurred throughout the county\'s history.<sup id="cite_ref-E62_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E62-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Around <a href="/wiki/Compton_Valence" title="Compton Valence">Compton Valence</a>, it lies on the <a href="/wiki/Fuller%27s_earth" title="Fuller&#39;s earth">Fuller\'s earth</a> whereas between <a href="/wiki/Ringstead,_Dorset" class="mw-redirect" title="Ringstead, Dorset">Ringstead</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_Nothe" title="White Nothe">White Nothe</a>, it covers the Kimmeridge Clay and the Portland and Purbeck stratas.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Because geologists are certain all the formations from the Lias to the Kimmeridge Clay were deposited over the whole of the county, the Gault clay reveals the scale of uplift and erosion which must have taken place before Dorset was flooded.<sup id="cite_ref-E61_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E61-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The overlying Gault is revealed in the spring lines around the chalk ridges and escarpments.<sup id="cite_ref-E61_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E61-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> This water is also responsible for the many landslips throughout Dorset\'s history from the prehistoric to modern times.<sup id="cite_ref-E62_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E62-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Above the Gault is the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Greensand" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Greensand">Upper Greensand</a>, which varies from the golden sands that crown the West Dorset heights, such as <a href="/wiki/Pilsdon_Pen" title="Pilsdon Pen">Pilsdon Pen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_Cap" title="Golden Cap">Golden Cap</a>, to the rubbly sandstones found in the centre of the county.<sup id="cite_ref-E62_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E62-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The fossiliferous, green sandstone found around <a href="/wiki/Shaftesbury" title="Shaftesbury">Shaftesbury</a> contains many ammonites which, when compared to other deposits, indicate condensed deposition during different periods at different locations and detailed analysis reveals that basement structures were still active at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-E62_50-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E62-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="100.E2.80.9365_Ma_.28late_Cretaceous_.E2.80.93_Paleocene.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="100\xe2\x80\x9365_Ma_(late_Cretaceous_\xe2\x80\x93_Paleocene)">100&#8211;65 Ma (late Cretaceous &#8211; Paleocene)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 100\xe2\x80\x9365 Ma (late Cretaceous \xe2\x80\x93 Paleocene)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The sea levels remained more or less unchanged until the end of the Cretaceous period and despite Dorset having moved even further north to 40 degrees, the climate had reverted to a tropical one. During this period, the sea deposited an enormous amount of chalk across the county, perhaps as much as 300 metres more than there is currently.<sup id="cite_ref-E63_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E63-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The first chalk deposits contained sands and grits thought to have originated in Cornubia. This gritty chalk was deposited across central Dorset and can clearly be seen in the sides of <a href="/wiki/Eggardon_Hill" title="Eggardon Hill">Eggardon Hill</a>. As the water deepened, <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coccolith" title="Coccolith">coccoliths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a> became the main source for the calcereous soup which would later form the Dorset chalk.<sup id="cite_ref-E63_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E63-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The chalk forms a narrow strip inland between <a href="/wiki/Studland" title="Studland">Studland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worbarrow_Bay" title="Worbarrow Bay">Worbarrow Bay</a> when it reaches the coast once more. A much wider strip continues westward along the coast until it meets the wide swathe of chalk that cuts north-west across the county and forms the south-east point of the <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_Basin" title="Hampshire Basin">Hampshire Basin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E64_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E64-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Generally the Dorset chalk is too soft to be used as a building stone except along the <a href="/wiki/Wessex_Ridgeway" title="Wessex Ridgeway">Ridgeway</a> where faulting and folding produced a particularly hard variety popularly used by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaics</a>. The chalk often contains larger fossils including <a href="/wiki/Ammonite" class="mw-redirect" title="Ammonite">ammonites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belemnitida" title="Belemnitida">belemnites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brachiopod" title="Brachiopod">brachiopods</a>. Flint occurs throughout in both nodule and tabular layers.<sup id="cite_ref-E64_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E64-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a> rich layer of sediment, found around the world at the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous\xe2\x80\x93Paleogene extinction event">K-Pg</a> boundary, is missing in Dorset. This may be due to the uplift and erosion that removed the upper layers of the chalk.<sup id="cite_ref-E66_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E66-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The sediments resting on the Dorset chalk are, for the same reason, distinctly different from those in other parts of the world, and the remains of the plants and animals within, much more recent.<sup id="cite_ref-E68_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E68-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleogene_and_Neogene">Paleogene and Neogene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Paleogene and Neogene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="65.E2.80.9340_Ma_.28Paleocene_.E2.80.93_Eocene.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="65\xe2\x80\x9340_Ma_(Paleocene_\xe2\x80\x93_Eocene)">65&#8211;40 Ma (Paleocene &#8211; Eocene)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 65\xe2\x80\x9340 Ma (Paleocene \xe2\x80\x93 Eocene)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish,_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle,_Dorset.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish%2C_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle%2C_Dorset.jpg/220px-The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish%2C_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle%2C_Dorset.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish%2C_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle%2C_Dorset.jpg/330px-The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish%2C_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle%2C_Dorset.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish%2C_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle%2C_Dorset.jpg/440px-The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish%2C_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle%2C_Dorset.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_rim_of_Culpeppers_Dish,_Affpuddle_and_Turnerspuddle,_Dorset.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The edge of Culpepper\'s Dish, a large <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">doline</a> on Briantspuddle Heath</div></div></div>\n<p>The expansion of the North Atlantic, which first occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> period, now reached a point where it was affecting the Dorset landscape. Areas that were previously underwater were exposed to erosion as the chalk was folded and forced upwards.<sup id="cite_ref-E69_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E69-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The early deposits from this period, found across South-East England, do not occur in Dorset. The small amounts found in Devon however, coupled with the many <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">solution holes</a> found across the Dorset chalk, suggests that erosion may have removed them.<sup id="cite_ref-E69-70_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E69-70-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Some of the larger solution holes, known as dolines, are found on the heaths between <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Dorset" title="Dorchester, Dorset">Dorchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bere_Regis" title="Bere Regis">Bere Regis</a>. Near <a href="/wiki/Briantspuddle" title="Briantspuddle">Briantspuddle</a> is Culpepper\'s Dish, 86 metres wide and over 21 metres deep.<sup id="cite_ref-E70_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E70-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first deposits found from this period in Dorset are the Reading Formation clays. A sub-division of the <a href="/wiki/Lambeth_Group" title="Lambeth Group">Lambeth Group</a>, they are visible above the chalk at Studland. Mottled, brightly coloured and generally lacking fossils, the clays are thought to be deposited beneath <a href="/wiki/Lagoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Lagoons">lagoons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E70_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E70-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> In the east of the county, however, fossilised remains of gastropods and sharks\' teeth have been found alongside flint pebbles, clearly identifying these clays as marine deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-E71_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E71-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bracklesham_Group" title="Bracklesham Group">Bracklesham Group</a>, found across much of the Dorset portion of the Hampshire Basin, were mainly deposited by rivers and form the foundations of the Dorset heathland. The frequent and heavy rains which occurred during this period transported large amounts of material from northern uplands across the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Purbeck" title="Isle of Purbeck">Purbeck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weymouth,_Dorset" title="Weymouth, Dorset">Weymouth</a> areas.<sup id="cite_ref-E71_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E71-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> A thick layer of Chert gravel from the Purbeck Strata can be found on Blackdown Hill near <a href="/wiki/Portesham" title="Portesham">Portesham</a>, but it is not known whether it was carried there or whether it is the remains of an earlier deposit.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Blocks of Quartzite can be found across the downlands of Southern Dorset and these sarsons have been used in some of the local prehistoric monuments.<sup id="cite_ref-E71_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E71-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To the north and east of the county, much finer sands and gravels are found, including ball clay which was widely mined on the heaths around Wareham.<sup id="cite_ref-E72_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E72-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> The fine particles which make up the clay are believed to have originated in the west where rocks were being broken down by the alternating dry, humid conditions and torrential rain, which in turn formed rivers and carried the material to its current position.<sup id="cite_ref-E72_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E72-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Overlying the clay is the Agglestone grit, an iron cemented sandstone which forms the <a href="/wiki/Agglestone_Rock" title="Agglestone Rock">Agglestone</a> near Studland, and has been used in the buildings in and around the heathland where it is found.<sup id="cite_ref-E73_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E73-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg/220px-Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg/330px-Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg/440px-Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stair_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_24710.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The crumpled rock strata at Stair Hole caused by a collision between the African and Eurasian plates</div></div></div>\n<p>Fossilised palms taken from the cliffs at Bournemouth confirm that Dorset had a tropical climate at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-E73_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E73-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Creech_Barrow_Hill" title="Creech Barrow Hill">Creech Barrow Hill</a> is crowned with a unique early Eocene limestone that has yielded some important fossils. Outcrops of rock from the Barton Group occur along the coast between Bournemouth and <a href="/wiki/Hengistbury_Head" title="Hengistbury Head">Hengistbury Head</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E74_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E74-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="45.E2.80.931.5_Ma_.28Eocene_.E2.80.93_Pleistocene.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="45\xe2\x80\x931.5_Ma_(Eocene_\xe2\x80\x93_Pleistocene)">45&#8211;1.5 Ma (Eocene &#8211; Pleistocene)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 45\xe2\x80\x931.5 Ma (Eocene \xe2\x80\x93 Pleistocene)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During this period, Dorset drifted a further 9 degrees north, the climate cooled rapidly and by 37 Ma was <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperateness">temperate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E75_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E75-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Mainly terrestrial with fresh and brackish water environments, the county would, despite its low-lying nature, remain largely without marine influences except for some minor incursions at the end of the ice ages. The late <a href="/wiki/Palaeogene" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeogene">Palaeogene</a> deposits that filled the Hampshire Basin as the sea levels fell are not found in Dorset, nor are the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a>, which are absent from virtually all of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E75_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E75-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The collision of the <a href="/wiki/African_plate" class="mw-redirect" title="African plate">African</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_plate" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurasian plate">Eurasian plates</a> that occurred around this time caused a violent uplift in the south of the county, the results of which can be seen clearly at <a href="/wiki/Stair_Hole" title="Stair Hole">Stair Hole</a>, where the strata run almost vertically.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Further, but less dramatic folding and uplift, along the county\'s subsurface faults, coupled with continuing erosion of Jurassic and Cretaceous sediments, created Dorset\'s modern landscape and drainage patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-E76_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E76-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quaternary">Quaternary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Quaternary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="1.5_Ma.E2.80.93Present_.28Pleistocene_.E2.80.93_Holocene.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1.5_Ma\xe2\x80\x93Present_(Pleistocene_\xe2\x80\x93_Holocene)">1.5 Ma&#8211;Present (Pleistocene &#8211; Holocene)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 1.5 Ma\xe2\x80\x93Present (Pleistocene \xe2\x80\x93 Holocene)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg/220px-Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg/330px-Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg/440px-Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cliffs_at_the_north_end_of_White_Hole_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1099825.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Raised beach deposits at <a href="/wiki/Portland_Raised_Beach" title="Portland Raised Beach">White Hole</a>, Portland</div></div></div>\n<p>During the last 1,500,000 years, Britain was subjected to a number of extreme fluctuations in temperature which included three ice ages. Dorset was not affected by glaciation but did experience very low temperatures with permafrost on the high ground.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Sudden changes in sea-level, and successive warming and cooling, took their toll on the landscape.<sup id="cite_ref-E77_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E77-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> The two <a href="/wiki/Portland_Raised_Beach" title="Portland Raised Beach">raised beaches</a> at Portland created 210,000 and 125,000 years ago are a dramatic testament to the fluctuations in sea levels. The cause of these two fluctuations was twofold: the growth of the ice, which absorbed large quantities of water, and the weight of the ice in the north that caused the landmass to tilt.<sup id="cite_ref-E78_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E78-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the summer months, partial melting of the ice created large rivers that washed enormous amounts of sand and gravel across the county, forming huge terraces in both the upland areas and valleys.<sup id="cite_ref-E78_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E78-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> These rivers cut down into the landscape as they made their way to the lower sea levels. This action caused many large-scale landslips, most notably around  Shaftesbury and Abbotsbury.<sup id="cite_ref-E78_72-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E78-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Landslips caused by marine erosion still occur today, with some of the largest taking place along the West Dorset Coast. Fossilised remains, including the teeth and tusks of <a href="/wiki/Elephantidae" title="Elephantidae">elephants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoths</a> dating back 500,000 years, are often exposed.<sup id="cite_ref-E79_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E79-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Flint and Chert tools from early human visitors have been retrieved from the river terraces around the Dorset and Devon border, although these may have been transported here with the other river deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-E79_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E79-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chesil_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Chesil Bank">Chesil Bank</a> a 29 kilometres (18&#160;mi) long <a href="/wiki/Barrier_beach" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrier beach">barrier beach</a> stretching from <a href="/wiki/West_Bay,_Dorset" title="West Bay, Dorset">West Bay</a> to Portland, was created 10,000 years ago, when sea levels rose at the end of the last ice age.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_Strata,_Durdle_Door_-_geograph.org.uk_-_274762.jpg" class="internal" Monocline</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_development">Structural development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Structural development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Variscan_orogeny" title="Variscan orogeny">Variscan orogeny</a> is the oldest event for which there is evidence in Dorset, forming a major series of <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrust faults</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> and older rocks. These thrusts trend west&#8211;east, dip moderately towards the south and have controlled later basin development and subsequent phases of <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(geology)" title="Inversion (geology)">inversion</a>. During the Permo-Triassic the area was affected by a phase of rifting that reactivated the older thrusts in extension.<sup id="cite_ref-Chadwick_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chadwick-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> These extensional faults were themselves reactivated during further rifting in Jurassic to early Cretaceous times. The area experienced the effects of the early stages of Alpine collision during the Late Cretaceous to <a href="/wiki/Palaeogene" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeogene">Palaeogene</a>, with reverse reactivation of many of the extensional faults, creating the most visible of the structures in the Dorset area, such as the Purbeck Monocline.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Litton_Cheney_Fault">Litton Cheney Fault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Litton Cheney Fault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Litton Cheney Fault is a west&#8211;east-trending, south-dipping normal fault. The fault was active during deposition of the Lias Group and again during the Late Jurassic to Early Cretaceous. There is evidence of minor reversal on the fault during the Late Cretaceous to Palaeogene inversion.<sup id="cite_ref-Butler_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Abbotsbury.E2.80.93Ridgeway_Fault"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Abbotsbury\xe2\x80\x93Ridgeway_Fault">Abbotsbury&#8211;Ridgeway Fault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Abbotsbury\xe2\x80\x93Ridgeway Fault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This major west&#8211;east-trending, south-dipping normal fault was mainly active during the Late Jurassic to Early Cretaceous, as shown by the local preservation of the Wealden in its hanging wall. It was reactivated as a reverse fault during the Late Cretaceous to Palaeogene, and now juxtaposes Portland and Purbeck beds in the hanging wall against Chalk in the footwall. The fault is not directly linked to faults beneath the Triassic salt layer.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvey_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Weymouth_Anticline.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Weymouth_Anticline.png/260px-Weymouth_Anticline.png" width="260" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Weymouth_Anticline.png/390px-Weymouth_Anticline.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Weymouth_Anticline.png/520px-Weymouth_Anticline.png 2x" data-file-width="1142" data-file-height="585" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Weymouth_Anticline.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cross-section over the Weymouth Anticline</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weymouth_Anticline">Weymouth Anticline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Weymouth Anticline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Weymouth Anticline has a west&#8211;east trending axis and plunges to the east. Strata beneath the Upper Triassic halite are unfolded indicating that the salt is acting as a <a href="/wiki/Decollement" class="mw-redirect" title="Decollement">decollement</a>. The anticline is a result of inversion along the Abbotsbury&#8211;Ridgeway Fault, although it has been suggested that it developed by the tightening of an earlier rollover anticline, formed during an earlier extensional phase, due to the <a href="/wiki/Salt_tectonics#Salt_detached_fault_systems" title="Salt tectonics">soft-linked nature</a> of the faulting.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvey_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Purbeck_Monocline">Purbeck Monocline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Purbeck Monocline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Purbeck_Monocline" title="Purbeck Monocline">Purbeck Monocline</a></div>\n<p>This monoclinal structure forms part of a semi-continuous structure extending eastwards through the Isle of Wight as the Portland-Wight Monocline (or Disturbance). This was formed by reverse reactivation of the Purbeck&#8211;Isle of Wight fault zone.<sup id="cite_ref-Beeley_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beeley-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Dorset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-CHA5-6-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-CHA5-6_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Chaffey (pp.5-6)</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-CHA6-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-CHA6_2-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Chaffey (p.6)</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">House (1993) pp.1-4</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Chaffey pp.5-7</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Cullingford (p.122)</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.webcitation.org/65ctcW4NE?url=http://www.bp.com/liveassets/bp_internet/globalbp/STAGING/global_assets/downloads/U/uk_asset_wytch_farm.pdf">"Wytch Farm"</a> <span class="cs1-format">(PDF)</span>. <i>Asset Portfolio</i>.
