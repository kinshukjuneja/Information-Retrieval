href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/%C3%89riu" title="\xc3\x89riu">\xc3\x89riu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erin" title="Erin">Erin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iverni" title="Iverni">Iverni</a></div>\n<p>The modern Irish <i>\xc3\x89ire</i> evolved from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> word <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89riu" title="\xc3\x89riu">\xc3\x89riu</a></i>, which was the name of a <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> goddess. <i>\xc3\x89riu</i> is generally believed to have been the matron goddess of Ireland, a goddess of sovereignty, or simply a goddess of the land. The origin of <i>\xc3\x89riu</i> has been traced to the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Celtic">Proto-Celtic</a> reconstruction *<i>\xce\xa6\xc4\xabwerjon-</i> (nominative singular <i>\xce\xa6\xc4\xabwerj\xc5\xab</i> &lt; Pre-Proto-Celtic <i>-j\xc5\x8d</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This suggests a descent from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> reconstruction *<i>piHwerjon-</i>, likely related to the adjectival stem *<i>piHwer-</i> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i>p\xc4\xabvan</i>, <i>p\xc4\xabvar\xc4\xab</i> and <i>p\xc4\xabvara</i> meaning "fat, full, abounding"). This would suggest a meaning of "abundant land".\n</p><p>This Proto-Celtic form became <i>\xc4\xaaweri\xc5\xab</i> or <i>\xc4\xaaveri\xc5\xab</i> in <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Proto-Goidelic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is highly likely that explorers borrowed and modified this term. During his exploration of northwest Europe (circa 320 BC), <a href="/wiki/Pytheas_of_Massilia" class="mw-redirect" title="Pytheas of Massilia">Pytheas of Massilia</a> called the island  <i>Ierne</i> (written <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\xb8\xce\xad\xcf\x81\xce\xbd\xce\xb7</span>). In his book <i>Geographia</i> (circa 150 AD), <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemaeus</a> called the island  <i>Iouernia</i> (written <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\xb8\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\xb5\xcf\x81\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1</span>). Based on these historical accounts, the Roman Empire called the island <i><a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a></i>.\n</p><p>The evolution of the word would follow as such:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Celtic">Proto-Celtic</a>  *<i>\xce\xa6\xc4\xabwerjon-</i> (nominative singular *<i>\xce\xa6\xc4\xabwerj\xc5\xab</i>)\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Proto-Goidelic</a>  *<i>\xc4\xaaweri\xc5\xab</i> or *<i>\xc4\xaaveri\xc5\xab</i>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a>  <i>\xc3\x89riu</i>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Modern Irish</a>  <i>\xc3\x89ire</i></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>A 19th century proposal, which does not follow modern standards of etymology, derives the name from <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>\xc3\xac</i> (island) + <i>thairr</i> (west) + <i>f\xc3\xb3nn</i> (land), which together give <i>\xc3\xac-iar-fh\xc3\xb3nn</i>, or "westland isle"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>This is similar in meaning to the <a href="/wiki/Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse language">Norse</a> name for Irish people, "west men", which subsequently gave its name to the Icelandic island of <a href="/wiki/Vestmannaeyjar" title="Vestmannaeyjar">Vestmannaeyjar</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Difference_between_.C3.89ire_and_Erin"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_between_\xc3\x89ire_and_Erin">Difference between <i>\xc3\x89ire</i> and <i>Erin</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Difference between \xc3\x89ire and Erin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While <i>\xc3\x89ire</i> is simply the name for the island of Ireland in the Irish language, and sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <i>Erin</i> is a common poetic name for Ireland, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Erin_go_bragh" title="Erin go bragh">Erin go bragh</a></i>. The distinction between the two is one of the difference between <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">cases</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nouns" class="mw-redirect" title="Nouns">nouns</a> in Irish. <i>\xc3\x89ire</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">nominative case</a>, the case that (in the modern Gaelic languages) is used for nouns that are the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a> of a sentence, i.e., the noun that is <i>doing</i> something as well as the direct <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a> of a sentence. <i>Erin</i> derives from <i>\xc3\x89irinn</i>, the Irish <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative case</a> of <i>\xc3\x89ire</i>, which has replaced the nominative case in <a href="/wiki/Gaeltacht_na_nD%C3%A9ise" title="Gaeltacht na nD\xc3\xa9ise">D\xc3\xa9ise</a> Irish and some non-standard sub-dialects elsewhere, in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> (where the usual word for Ireland is <i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">\xc3\x88irinn</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a> (a form of Gaelic), where the word is spelled "Nerin," with the initial <i>n-</i> probably representing a fossilisation of the preposition <i>in</i>/<i>an</i> "in" (cf. Irish <i>in \xc3\x89irinn</i>, Scottish <i>an \xc3\x88irinn</i>/<i>ann an \xc3\x88irinn</i> "in Ireland"). The <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a>, <i>\xc3\x89ireann</i>, is used in the Gaelic forms of the titles of companies and institutions in Ireland e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Iarnr%C3%B3d_%C3%89ireann" title="Iarnr\xc3\xb3d \xc3\x89ireann">Iarnr\xc3\xb3d \xc3\x89ireann</a></i> (<i>Irish Rail</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann">D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann</a></i> (<i>Irish Parliament</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Poblacht na h\xc3\x89ireann</a></i> (<i>The Republic of Ireland</i>) or <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Tuaisceart \xc3\x89ireann</a></i> (<i>Northern Ireland</i>)\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_state_name">As a navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_Irish_state" title="Names of the Irish state">Names of the Irish state</a></div>\n<p>Article 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Irish constitution</a> adopted in 1937 by the government under <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a> states that <i>\xc3\x89ire</i> is the name of the state, or in the English language, <i>Ireland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Constitution\'s English-language <a href="/wiki/Irish_Constitution#Preamble_(full_text)" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Constitution">preamble</a> also described the population as "We, the people of \xc3\x89ire". Despite the fact that Article 8 designated Irish as the "national" and "first official" language, <i>\xc3\x89ire</i> has to some extent passed out of everyday conversation and literature, and the state is referred to as <i>Ireland</i> or its equivalent in all other languages.\nThe name "\xc3\x89ire" has been used on Irish <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamps_of_Ireland" title="Postage stamps of Ireland">postage stamps</a> since 1922;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> on all <a href="/wiki/Irish_coinage" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish coinage">Irish coinage</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Irish_euro_coins" title="Irish euro coins">Irish euro coins</a>); and together with "Ireland" on passports and other official state documents issued since 1937. "\xc3\x89ire" is used on the <a href="/wiki/Seal_of_the_President_of_Ireland" title="Seal of the President of Ireland">Seal of the President of Ireland</a>.\n</p><p>Initially after independence the United Kingdom insisted on using only the name "Eire" and refused to accept the name "Ireland". It adopted the <a href="/wiki/Eire_(Confirmation_of_Agreements)_Act_1938" title="Eire (Confirmation of Agreements) Act 1938">Eire (Confirmation of Agreements) Act 1938</a> putting in law that position. At the <a href="/wiki/1948_Summer_Olympics" title="1948 Summer Olympics">1948 Summer Olympics</a> in London the organisers insisted that the Irish team march under the banner "Eire" notwithstanding that every other team was marching according to what their name was in English.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The UK Government used what some Irish politicians stated were "sneering titles such as Eirish".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The UK Government would refer to "Eire Ministers" and the "Eireann Army" and generally avoid all reference to "Ireland" in connection with the state. The <a href="/wiki/Ireland_Act_1949" title="Ireland Act 1949">Ireland Act 1949</a> changed this to "Republic of Ireland". It was not until after the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> that the UK government accepted the preferred name of simply "Ireland", at the same time as Ireland dropped its territorial claim over Northern Ireland.\n</p><p>Before the 1937 Constitution, "Saorst\xc3\xa1t \xc3\x89ireann" (the Irish name of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>) was generally used.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(Ireland)" title="The Emergency (Ireland)">the Emergency</a> (as <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> was known), Irish ships had "EIRE" (and the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Ireland" title="Flag of Ireland">Irish tricolour</a>) painted large on their sides and deck, to identify them as neutrals.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SS_Irish_Oak.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/SS_Irish_Oak.jpg/220px-SS_Irish_Oak.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/SS_Irish_Oak.jpg/330px-SS_Irish_Oak.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/SS_Irish_Oak.jpg/440px-SS_Irish_Oak.jpg 2x" data-file-width="566" data-file-height="389" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SS_Irish_Oak.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/SS_Irish_Oak_(1919)" title="SS Irish Oak (1919)">Irish Oak</a></i> torpedoed mid-Atlantic, oil by <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_King_(artist)" title="Kenneth King (artist)">Kenneth King</a>, showing "EIRE" prominently. (<a href="/wiki/National_Maritime_Museum_of_Ireland" title="National Maritime Museum of Ireland">National Maritime Museum of Ireland</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1922\xe2\x80\x931938 the <a href="/wiki/List_of_international_license_plate_codes#I" class="mw-redirect" title="List of international license plate codes">international plate on Irish cars</a> was "SE". From 1938 to 1962 it was marked "EIR", short for \xc3\x89ire. In 1961 statutory instrument no. 269 allowed "IRL",<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and by 1962 "IRL" had been adopted. Irish politician Bernard Commons <a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta D\xc3\xa1la">TD</a> suggested to the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il" class="mw-redirect" title="D\xc3\xa1il">D\xc3\xa1il</a> in 1950 that the government examine "the tourist identification plate bearing the letters EIR ... with a view to the adoption of identification letters more readily associated with this country by foreigners".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> "EIR" is also shown in other legislation such as the car insurance statutory instrument no. 383 of 1952 and no. 82 of 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_International_Civil_Aviation" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on International Civil Aviation">1947 Convention</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_registration#List_of_countries/regions_and_their_current_registration_prefixes_and_patterns" title="Aircraft registration">Irish-registered aircraft</a> have carried a registration mark starting "EI" for \xc3\x89ire.\n</p><p>\nFrom January 2007, the Irish government nameplates at meetings of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> have borne both <i>\xc3\x89ire</i> and <i>Ireland</i>, following the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_European_Union" title="Languages of the European Union">working language of the European Union</a>.</p><div style="clear:left;"></div>\n<h2><span id="Spelling_Eire_rather_than_.C3.89ire"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling_Eire_rather_than_\xc3\x89ire">Spelling <i>Eire</i> rather than <i>\xc3\x89ire</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spelling Eire rather than \xc3\x89ire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When Irish-language texts were printed in <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_type" title="Gaelic type">Gaelic type</a>, diacritics were retained on upper-case letters as for lower-case letters. From the later 1940s, in conjunction with other reforms, printing switched to the same "<a href="/wiki/Roman_type" title="Roman type">Roman type</a>" used for most other <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> languages. There was some uncertainty about whether the <i><a href="/wiki/S%C3%ADneadh_fada" class="mw-redirect" title="S\xc3\xadneadh fada">s\xc3\xadneadh fada</a></i> (acute accent) should be written on upper-case letters. While it was preserved in all-Irish texts, it was often omitted when short fragments of Irish appeared alone or in English texts. <a href="/wiki/Noel_Davern" title="Noel Davern">Noel Davern</a> asked in the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann">D\xc3\xa1il</a> in 1974 why Irish stamps had <i>EIRE</i> rather than <i>\xc3\x89IRE</i>. The reply from the <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Posts_and_Telegraphs_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister for Posts and Telegraphs (Ireland)">Minister for Posts and Telegraphs</a> was:<sup id="cite_ref-dailq1974_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dailq1974-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The accent has been omitted on most Irish stamps issued over the past ten years in the interests of artistic balance and in accordance with a common practice in the printing of Irish in Roman script for display purposes. This is a prevailing typographical convention and is common to several European languages, including French.\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Davern considered the spelling <i>Eire</i> to be worse than a misspelling, because <i>eire</i> is a separate word, meaning "a burden, load or encumbrance".<sup id="cite_ref-dailq1974_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dailq1974-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  The minister stated, "The word on the stamp ... does not mean \'eire\' and it is not understood to mean \'eire\' by anybody except Davern."<sup id="cite_ref-dailq1974_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dailq1974-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Stamps later reverted to a Gaelic type with the accent preserved.\n</p><p>In 1938 the British government provided in the <a href="/wiki/Eire_(Confirmation_of_Agreements)_Act_1938" title="Eire (Confirmation of Agreements) Act 1938">Eire (Confirmation of Agreements) Act 1938</a> that British legislation could henceforth refer to the Irish Free State as "Eire" (but not as "Ireland"). The 1938 Act was repealed in 1981, and in 1996 a British journalist described <i>Eire</i> as "now an oddity rarely used, an out-of-date reference".<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson1996_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Founded in 1937, the <a href="/wiki/Eire_Society_of_Boston" title="Eire Society of Boston">Eire Society of Boston</a> is an influential <a href="/wiki/Irish-American" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish-American">Irish-American</a> group.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>\xc3\x89ire has also been incorporated into the names of Irish commercial and social entities, such as <a href="/wiki/Eir_(telecommunications)" title="Eir (telecommunications)">Eir</a> (formerly Eircom and Telecom \xc3\x89ireann) and its former mobile phone network, <a href="/wiki/Eircell" title="Eircell">Eircell</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 2006 the Irish electricity network was devolved to <a href="/wiki/EirGrid" title="EirGrid">EirGrid</a>. The company "BetEire Flow" (<a href="/wiki/EFlow" title="EFlow">eFlow</a>), named as a <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">pun</a> on "better", is a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> consortium running the electronic tolling system at the <a href="/wiki/West-Link" title="West-Link">West-Link</a> bridge west of Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> According to the Dublin <a href="/wiki/Companies_Registration_Office_(Ireland)" title="Companies Registration Office (Ireland)">Companies Registration Office</a> in 2008, over 500 company names incorporate the word \xc3\x89ire in some form.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sometimes the incorporation is used for humorous or ironic effect, such as the sub reddit for Irish software developers named "DevelEire",<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> (a reference to <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Cormac_%C3%93_Gr%C3%A1da" title="Cormac \xc3\x93 Gr\xc3\xa1da">Cormac \xc3\x93 Gr\xc3\xa1da</a>\'s "\xc3\x89irvana" paper in 2007 on the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Tiger" title="Celtic Tiger">Celtic Tiger</a> economy<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> (a reference to <a href="/wiki/Nirvana" title="Nirvana">Nirvana</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
