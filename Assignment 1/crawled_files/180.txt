id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(400%E2%80%93800)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At the start of the period, Ireland had emerged from a mysterious decline that archaeological evidence suggests had hit population levels and standards of living from c. 100\xe2\x80\x93300 CE, called the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Dark_Age" title="Irish Dark Age">Irish Dark Age</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles-Edwards" title="Thomas Charles-Edwards">Thomas Charles-Edwards</a>. The population was entirely rural and dispersed, with small <a href="/wiki/Ringfort" title="Ringfort">ringforts</a> the largest centres of human occupation. Some 40,000 of these are known, while there may have been as many as 50,000<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, and "archaeologists are agreed that the vast bulk of them are the farm enclosures of the well-to-do of early medieval Ireland". <a href="/wiki/Souterrains" class="mw-redirect" title="Souterrains">Souterrains</a>, underground passages and chambers for hiding in or escaping through, are common.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn237\xe2\x80\x93249,_550_(quoted)_to_553_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn237\xe2\x80\x93249,_550_(quoted)_to_553-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is likely that raiding <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> for slaves and other loot gave an important boost to an otherwise almost entirely agricultural economy. The lakeside enclosures called <a href="/wiki/Crannog" title="Crannog">crann\xc3\xb3gs</a> continued to be used and seem especially associated with crafts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn257-259_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn257-259-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The older view that early medieval Irish farming concentrated on livestock has been overturned by <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> studies and other evidence, and it is now clear that cereal farming was increasingly important from about AD 200 onwards, with <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oats" class="mw-redirect" title="Oats">oats</a> more important crops than <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, wheat and others.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn264-265,_559-536_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn264-265,_559-536-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Cattle were greatly prized, and cattle-raiding constituted a large part of warfare, so that cattle needed the constant presence of a herdsman in daylight hours and were put in an enclosure at night.  By the end of the period the largest herds were probably those of monasteries. Generally mild Irish winters seem to have meant they were never put in roofed shelters in winter, although young calves might spend a period in the house.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn549_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn549-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There was very considerable clearance of forests in the early part of the period, such that by the 9th century, large tracts of forest appear to have been rare, and the native <a href="/wiki/Scots_pine" title="Scots pine">Scots pine</a> cleared almost to extinction; the large areas of <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogland</a> were harder for the medieval Irish to affect.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn568-573_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn568-573-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 800, small towns had started to form around some of the larger monasteries, such as <a href="/wiki/Trim,_County_Meath" title="Trim, County Meath">Trim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lismore,_County_Waterford" title="Lismore, County Waterford">Lismore</a>, and some kings were based in them, but the foundation of larger towns by the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> had yet to occur.  Otherwise kings lived in ringforts larger than the norm, but generally similar; however the possession of luxury objects such as elaborate <a href="/wiki/Celtic_brooch" title="Celtic brooch">Celtic brooches</a> was much greater among royalty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn550-553_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn550-553-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The latter part of the period was the peak of the Irish contribution to <a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular art</a>, whose surviving products include <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscripts</a>, most famously the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Kells" title="Book of Kells">Book of Kells</a>, brooches, which were worn by clergy as well nobles, carved stone <a href="/wiki/High_cross" title="High cross">high crosses</a>, and other isolated survivals of metalwork, such as the <a href="/wiki/Derrynaflan_Chalice" title="Derrynaflan Chalice">Derrynaflan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ardagh_Hoard" title="Ardagh Hoard">Ardagh Hoards</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Christian_history">Early Christian history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(400%E2%80%93800)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Christian history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Recorded Irish history begins with the introduction of Christianity and Latin literacy, beginning in the 5th century or possibly slightly before. When compared to neighbouring <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">Insular</a> societies, early Christian Ireland is well documented, at least for later periods, but these sources are not easy to interpret. Many questions remain unanswered and the study of early Christian Ireland continues to produce new theories and new discoveries. Since the later 19th century, when scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Kuno_Meyer" title="Kuno Meyer">Kuno Meyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whitley_Stokes" title="Whitley Stokes">Whitley Stokes</a> applied an increasingly rigorous approach to the study of written sources, a great deal of new information has been extracted from the written material. New fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Paleobotany" title="Paleobotany">paleobotany</a>, have contributed to the debate, while the volume of <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> evidence has increased.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_history">Ecclesiastical history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(400%E2%80%93800)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Christianity</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg/220px-Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg/330px-Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg/440px-Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2970" data-file-height="1950" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallarus_Oratory_Rainbow_20070916.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gallarus_Oratory" title="Gallarus Oratory">Gallarus Oratory</a>, one of the earliest churches built in Ireland</div></div></div>\n<p>The first reliable historical event in Irish history, recorded in the <i>Chronicle</i> of <a href="/wiki/Prosper_of_Aquitaine" title="Prosper of Aquitaine">Prosper of Aquitaine</a>, is the ordination by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Celestine_I" title="Pope Celestine I">Pope Celestine I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Palladius_(bishop_of_Ireland)" title="Palladius (bishop of Ireland)">Palladius</a> as the first bishop to Irish Christians in 431 \xe2\x80\x93 which demonstrates that there were already Christians living in Ireland. Prosper says in his <i>Contra Collatorem</i> that by this act Celestine "made the barbarian island Christian", although it is clear the <a href="/wiki/Christianisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Christianisation">Christianisation</a> of the island was a longer and more gradual process. The mission of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a> is traditionally dated around the same time&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the earliest date for his arrival in Ireland in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_annals" title="Irish annals">Irish annals</a> is 432&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 although Patrick\'s own writings contain nothing securely dateable.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is likely that Palladius\' activities were in the south of Ireland, perhaps associated with <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Cashel" title="Rock of Cashel">Cashel</a>, while Patrick\'s were later, in the north, and associated with <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a>.\n</p><p>By the early 6th century the church had developed separate dioceses, with bishops as the most senior ecclesiastical figures, but the country was still predominantly pagan. The <a href="/wiki/High_Kings_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="High Kings of Ireland">High Kings of Ireland</a> continued pagan practices until the reign of <a href="/wiki/Diarmait_mac_Cerbaill" title="Diarmait mac Cerbaill">Diarmait mac Cerbaill</a> c. 558, traditionally the first Christian High King. The monastic movement, headed by abbots, took hold in the mid 6th century, and by 700 Ireland was at least nominally a Christian country, with the church fully part of Irish society. The status of ecclesiastics was regulated by secular law, and many leading ecclesiastics came from aristocratic Irish families. Monasteries in the 8th century even went to war with each other.<sup id="cite_ref-hughes_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hughes-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the 7th century on, Irish churchmen such as <a href="/wiki/Columbanus" title="Columbanus">Columbanus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">Columba</a> were active in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a>. The mixing of Irish, <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> styles created the <a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular</a> style of art, represented by the <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne_Gospels" title="Lindisfarne Gospels">Lindisfarne Gospels</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Kells" title="Book of Kells">Book of Kells</a>. Ireland\'s reputation for scholarship was such that many scholars travelled from Britain and the European mainland to study in Irish schools.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_history">Political history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(400%E2%80%93800)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Eoin_MacNeill" title="Eoin MacNeill">Eoin MacNeill</a> identified the "oldest certain fact in the political history of Ireland" as the existence in late prehistory of a pentarchy, probably consisting of the <i>c\xc3\xb3iceda</i> or "fifths" of the <a href="/wiki/Ulaid" title="Ulaid">Ulaid</a> (Ulster), the <a href="/wiki/Connachta" title="Connachta">Connachta</a> (Connacht), the <a href="/wiki/Laigin" title="Laigin">Laigin</a> (Leinster), Mumu (<a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Meath" title="Kingdom of Meath">Mide</a> (Meath), although some accounts discount Mide and split Mumu in two.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, by the dawn of history this pentarchy no longer existed. The rise of new dynasties, notably the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill">U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill</a> in the north and midlands and the <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B3ganachta" title="E\xc3\xb3ganachta">E\xc3\xb3ganachta</a> in the south-west, changed the political landscape. The U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill, or their parent group the Connachta, reduced the former fifth of the Ulaid to counties <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">Down</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">Antrim</a> in the 4th of 5th century, establishing the tributary kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Airg%C3%ADalla" title="Airg\xc3\xadalla">Airg\xc3\xadalla</a> in the centre and the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Ailech" class="mw-redirect" title="Ailech">Ailech</a> in the west of the old province. Early <a href="/wiki/Irish_annals" title="Irish annals">Irish annals</a> also show regular warfare between the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill and the Laigin in the midlands, with the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill conquering as far south as the <a href="/wiki/County_Kildare" title="County Kildare">Kildare</a>/<a href="/wiki/County_Offaly" title="County Offaly">Offaly</a> border, and claiming the kingship of <a href="/wiki/Hill_of_Tara" title="Hill of Tara">Tara</a>, beginning to be conceptualised as the <a href="/wiki/High_King_of_Ireland" title="High King of Ireland">High Kingship of Ireland</a>. This led to a new division of the country into two-halves, <i>Leth Cuinn</i>, "Conn\'s half" after <a href="/wiki/Conn_of_the_Hundred_Battles" title="Conn of the Hundred Battles">Conn of the Hundred Battles</a>, supposed ancestor of the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill and Connachta, in the north, and <i>Leth Moga</i>, "Mug\'s half", after <a href="/wiki/Mug_Nuadat" title="Mug Nuadat">Mug Nuadat</a>, supposed ancestor of the Eoganachta, in the south. Dynastic propaganda claimed this was a traditional division dating back to the 2nd century, but it probably originated in the 8th, at the height of U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill power.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn182-234_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE\xc3\x93_Cr\xc3\xb3in\xc3\xadn182-234-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
