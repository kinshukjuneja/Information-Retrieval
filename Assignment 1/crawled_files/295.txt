Americans">Irish-American</a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Ulster Scots</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_dialects" title="Ulster Scots dialects">Ulster-Scots</a>: <i>Ulst\xc3\xa8r-Scotch</i>), also called <b>Ulster Scots people</b> (<i>Ulst\xc3\xa8r-Scotch fowk</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or, outside the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, <b>Scots-Irish</b> (<i>Scotch-Airisch</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> are an <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, found mostly in the province of <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> and to a lesser extent in the rest of Ireland. Their ancestors were mostly Protestant <a href="/wiki/Presbyterians" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterians">Presbyterians</a> <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowland Scottish</a> migrants,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> the largest numbers coming from <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanarkshire" title="Lanarkshire">Lanarkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renfrewshire" title="Renfrewshire">Renfrewshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Scottish Borders</a>, with others coming from further north in the Scottish Lowlands and, to a much lesser extent,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a>.\n</p><p>These Scots migrated to Ireland in large numbers both as a result of the government-sanctioned <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Plantation_(settlement_or_colony)" title="Plantation (settlement or colony)">planned process of colonisation</a> which took place under the auspices of <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI of Scotland and I of England</a> on land confiscated from members of the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_nobility_of_Ireland" title="Gaelic nobility of Ireland">Gaelic nobility of Ireland</a> who <a href="/wiki/Flight_of_the_Earls" title="Flight of the Earls">fled Ulster</a> and as part of a larger migration or unplanned wave of settlement.\n</p><p>Ulster Scots emigrated onwards from Ireland in significant numbers to what is now the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and to all corners of the then-worldwide <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>\xe2\x80\x94what are now <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, to <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a> and to a lesser extent to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_Americans" title="Scotch-Irish Americans">Scotch-Irish</a> (or Scots-Irish) is a traditional term for Ulster Scots who emigrated to North America.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="St George&#39;s Cross">St George\'s Cross</a>).</div></div></div>\n<p>The first major influx of border English and Lowland Scots into Ulster came in the first two decades of the 17th century.\n</p><p>First, before the <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a> and even before the <a href="/wiki/Flight_of_the_Earls" title="Flight of the Earls">Flight of the Earls</a>, there was the 1606 independent Scottish settlement in east <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">Down</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">Antrim</a>. It was led by adventurers <a href="/wiki/James_Hamilton,_1st_Viscount_Claneboye" title="James Hamilton, 1st Viscount Claneboye">James Hamilton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sir_Hugh_Montgomery" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Hugh Montgomery">Sir Hugh Montgomery</a>, two Ayrshire <a href="/wiki/Laird" title="Laird">lairds</a>. Montgomery was granted half of Conn O\'Neill\'s land as a reward for helping him escape from prison. Hamilton forced himself in on this deal when he discovered it and, after three years of bickering, the final settlement gave Hamilton and Montgomery each one-third of the land.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Starting in 1609, Scots began arriving into state-sponsored settlements as part of the <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a>. This scheme was intended to confiscate all the lands of the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_nobility_of_Ireland" title="Gaelic nobility of Ireland">Gaelic Irish nobility</a> in Ulster and to settle the province with Protestant Scottish and English colonists. Under this scheme, a substantial number of Scots were settled, mostly in the south and west of Ulster, on confiscated land.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>While many of the Scottish planters in Ulster came from southwest Scotland, a large number came from the southeast, including the unstable regions right along the border with England (the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Scottish Borders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>). These groups were from the Borderers or <a href="/wiki/Border_Reivers" title="Border Reivers">Border Reivers</a> culture, which had familial links on both sides of the Anglo-Scottish border. \nContrary to popular misinformation and due mainly to the simplification of Irish history for political agenda purposes, these Reivers were not all Protestants, many if not a majority would have been at least nominally Roman Catholics.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\nThe plan was that moving these Borderers to Ireland would both solve the Borders problem and tie down Ulster. This was of particular concern to <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a> when he became King of England, since he knew Scottish instability could jeopardise his chances of ruling both kingdoms effectively.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">Irish Rebellion of 1641</a>, the native Irish <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a> attempted to extirpate the English and Scottish settlers in revenge for being driven off their ancestral land, resulting in severe violence, massacres and ultimately leading to the deaths of between four and six thousand settlers over the winter of 1641\xe2\x80\x9342.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Native Irish civilians were massacred in return.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By 1642, native Irish were in <i>de facto</i> control of much of the island under a <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Ireland" title="Confederate Ireland">Confederate Ireland</a>, with about a third under the control of the opposition. However, many Ulster-Scots Presbyterians joined with the Irish in rebellion and aided them in driving the English out.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The Ulster-Scottish population in Ireland was quite possibly<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (March 2016)">weasel&#160;words</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> preserved from complete annihilation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Puffery" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="Unverified descriptions (March 2016)">peacock&#160;term</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> during the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederate_Wars" title="Irish Confederate Wars">Irish Confederate Wars</a>, when a Scottish <a href="/wiki/Covenanter" title="Covenanter">Covenanter</a> army was landed in the province to protect the Ulster-Scottish settlers from native Irish landowners.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The war itself, part of the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Wars of the Three Kingdoms</a>, ended in the 1650s, with the <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">Cromwellian conquest of Ireland</a>. At the head of the army, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> conquered all of Ireland. Defeating the Irish Confederates and <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">English Royalists</a> on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">English Parliamentarians</a>, he and his forces employed methods and inflicted casualties among the civilian Irish population that have long been commonly considered by contemporary sources, historians and the popular culture to be outside of the accepted military ethics of the day (see more on the <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland#Historical_debate" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">debate here</a>). After the Cromwellian war in Ireland was over, many of their soldiers settled permanently in eastern Ulster.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1652" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Settlement 1652">Act of Settlement 1652</a>, all Catholic-owned land was confiscated and the <a href="/wiki/British_Plantations_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="British Plantations in Ireland">British Plantations in Ireland</a>, which had been destroyed by the rebellion of 1641, were restored. However, due to the Scots\' enmity to the English Parliament in the final stages of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, English settlers rather than Scots were the main beneficiary of this scheme.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>There was a generation of calm in Ireland until another war broke out in 1689, again due to political conflict closely aligned with ethnic and religious differences. The <a href="/wiki/Williamite_war_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Williamite war in Ireland">Williamite war in Ireland</a> (1689\xe2\x80\x9391) was fought between <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobites</a> who supported the restoration of the Catholic <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II</a> to the throne of England and <a href="/wiki/Williamite" title="Williamite">Williamites</a> who supported the Protestant <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William of Orange</a>. The majority of the <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Protestant colonists</a> throughout Ireland but particularly in Ulster, fought on the Williamite side in the war against the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobites</a>. The fear of a repeat of the massacres of 1641, fear of retribution for religious persecution, as well as their wish to hold on to lands which had been confiscated from Catholic landowners, were all principal motivating factors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The Williamite forces, composed of British, Dutch, <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" class="mw-redirect" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a> and Danish armies, as well as troops raised in Ulster,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> ended Jacobite resistance by 1691, confirming the Protestant minority\'s monopoly on power in Ireland. Their victories at <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Derry" title="Siege of Derry">Derry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Boyne" title="Battle of the Boyne">the Boyne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Aughrim" title="Battle of Aughrim">Aughrim</a> are <a href="/wiki/Orange_walk" title="Orange walk">still commemorated</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Order" title="Orange Order">Orange Order</a> into the 21st century.\n</p><p>Finally, another major influx of Scots into northern Ireland occurred in the late 1690s, when tens of thousands of people fled a famine in Scotland to come to Ulster.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was only after the 1690s that Scottish settlers and their descendants, the majority of whom were <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a>, gained numeric superiority in Ulster, though still a minority in Ireland as a whole. Along with <a href="/wiki/Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholics">Catholics</a>, they were legally disadvantaged by the <a href="/wiki/Penal_Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Penal Laws">Penal Laws</a>, which gave full rights only to members of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> <a href="/wiki/State_church" class="mw-redirect" title="State church">state church</a>), who were mainly <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Irish">Anglo-Irish</a> (themselves often <a href="/wiki/Absentee_landlord" title="Absentee landlord">absentee landlords</a>), native Irish <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">converts or the descendants of English settlers</a>. For this reason, up until the 19th century, there was considerable disharmony between <a href="/wiki/English_Dissenters" title="English Dissenters">Dissenters</a> and the ruling <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a> in Ireland. With the enforcement of <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne\'s</a> 1703 <a href="/wiki/Test_Act" title="Test Act">Test Act</a>, which caused further discrimination against all who did not participate in the <a href="/wiki/Established_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Established church">established church</a>, considerable numbers of Ulster-Scots migrated to the colonies in <a href="/wiki/British_America" title="British America">British America</a> throughout the 18th and 19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In fact, these \'Scots-Irish\' from Ulster and Lowland Scotland comprised the most numerous group of immigrants from Great Britain and Ireland to the American colonies in the years prior the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, with an estimated 150,000 leaving northern Ireland at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Towards the end of the 18th century, many Ulster-Scots Presbyterians ignored religious differences and, along with many Catholic Gaelic Irish, joined the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_United_Irishmen" title="Society of United Irishmen">United Irishmen</a> to participate in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1798" title="Irish Rebellion of 1798">Irish Rebellion of 1798</a> in support of <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">republican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egalitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Egalitarian">egalitarian</a> ideals.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span generations after arriving in Ulster, considerable numbers of Ulster-Scots emigrated to <a href="/wiki/British_America" title="British America">the North American colonies of Great Britain</a>. Between 1717 and 1775, an estimated 200,000 migrated to what became the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Around the same time, the British took control of the territory of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>, allowing many Ulster-Scots to migrate to these areas as well. These people are known as the <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_Canadians" title="Scotch-Irish Canadians">Scotch-Irish Canadians</a>.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census" title="United States Census">United States Census</a> of 2000, 4.3 million Americans (1.5% of the population of the United States) claimed <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotch-Irish American">Scotch-Irish</a> ancestry. The <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster Scots Agency">Ulster Scots Agency</a> points to industry, language, music, sport, religion and myriad traditions brought to Ulster from the Scottish lowlands. In particular, the origin of <a href="/wiki/Country_and_Western_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Country and Western music">country and Western music</a> was extensively from Ulster Scots folk music. \n</p><p>The cultural traditions and aspects of this culture including its links to country music are articulated in <a href="/wiki/David_Hackett_Fischer" title="David Hackett Fischer">David Hackett Fischer</a>\'s book, <i><a href="/wiki/Albion%27s_Seed:_Four_British_Folkways_in_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Albion&#39;s Seed: Four British Folkways in America">Albion\'s Seed: Four British Folkways in America</a></i>. In 2010\'s documentary <i>The Hamely Tongue</i>, filmmaker Deagl\xc3\xa1n O Moch\xc3\xa1in traces back the origins of this culture and language, and relates its manifestations in today\'s Ireland. The film\'s title refers to James Fenton\'s book, <i>The Hamely Tongue: A personal record of Ulster-Scots in County Antrim.</i> \n</p><p>Most Ulster Scots speak <a href="/wiki/Ulster_English" title="Ulster English">Ulster English</a> as a <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first language</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_dialects" title="Ulster Scots dialects">Ulster Scots</a> is the local dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Lowland Scots</a> language which has, since the 1980s, also been called "Ullans", a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> popularised by the physician, amateur historian and politician <a
