href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/\xc9\x9b/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">\xc9\x9b</span><span title="&#39;f&#39; in &#39;find&#39;">f</span><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/\xc9\x9b/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">\xc9\x9b</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/\xc9\x99/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">\xc9\x99</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is the British monarch\'s personal representative in each county of the United Kingdom. Historically, each lieutenant was responsible for organising the county\'s <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a>. In 1871, the lieutenant\'s responsibility over the local militia was removed. However, it was not until 1921 that they formally lost the right to call upon able-bodied men to fight when needed.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lord-lieutenant is now an honorary titular position usually awarded to a retired notable person in the county.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lieutenants were first appointed to a number of <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">English counties</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">King Henry VIII</a> in the 1540s, when the military functions of the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriff</a> were handed over to him. He raised and was responsible for the efficiency of the local <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> units of the county, and afterwards of the <a href="/wiki/Yeomanry" title="Yeomanry">yeomanry</a>, and volunteers. He was commander of these forces, whose officers he appointed.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These commissions were originally of temporary duration, and only when the situation required the local militia to be specially supervised and well prepared; often where invasion by <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> or France might be expected.\n</p><p>Lieutenancies soon became more organised, probably in the reign of his successor <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">King Edward VI</a>, their establishment being approved by the English parliament in 1550. However, it was not until the threat of invasion by the forces of Spain in 1585 that lieutenants were appointed to all counties and <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">counties corporate</a> and became in effect permanent. Although some counties were left without lieutenants during the 1590s, following the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a>, the office continued to exist, and was retained by <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">King James I</a> even after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585%E2%80%931604)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585\xe2\x80\x931604)">Anglo-Spanish War</a>.\n</p><p>The office was abolished under the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth</a>, but was re-established following the <a href="/wiki/The_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="The Restoration">Restoration</a> under the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Militia_Act_1662" title="City of London Militia Act 1662">City of London Militia Act 1662</a>, which declared that:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[T]he King\'s most Excellent Majesty, his Heirs and Successors, shall and may from Time to Time, as Occasion shall require, issue forth several Commissions of Lieutenancy to such Persons as his Majesty, his Heirs and Successors, shall think fit to be his Majesty\'s Lieutenants for the several and respective Counties, Cities and Places of England and Dominion of Wales, and Town of Berwick upon Tweed.\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Although not explicitly stated, from that date lieutenants were appointed to "counties at large", with their jurisdiction including the counties corporate within the parent county.<sup id="cite_ref-webb_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For example, lieutenants of Devon in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries appointed <a href="/wiki/Deputy_lieutenant" title="Deputy lieutenant">deputy lieutenants</a> to the City of Exeter, and were sometimes described as the "Lieutenant of Devon and Exeter"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The one exception was <a href="/wiki/Haverfordwest" title="Haverfordwest">Haverfordwest</a>, to which a lieutenant continued to be appointed until 1974. The origin of this anomaly may have lain in the former <a href="/wiki/County_palatine" title="County palatine">palatine</a> status of Pembrokeshire.<sup id="cite_ref-webb_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> was uniquely given a commission of lieutenancy, and was exempt from the authority of the lieutenant of Middlesex. The <a href="/wiki/Constable_of_the_Tower_of_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Constable of the Tower of London">Constable of the Tower of London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Warden_of_the_Cinque_Ports" class="mw-redirect" title="Warden of the Cinque Ports">Warden of the Cinque Ports</a> were <i><a href="/wiki/Ex-officio" class="mw-redirect" title="Ex-officio">ex-officio</a></i> lieutenants for the <a href="/wiki/Tower_division" title="Tower division">Tower Hamlets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cinque_Ports" title="Cinque Ports">Cinque Ports</a> respectively, which were treated as counties in legislation regarding lieutenancy and militia affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-webb_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The official title of the office at this time was His or Her Majesty\'s "Lieutenant for the county of x", but as almost all office-holders were <a href="/wiki/Peers_of_the_realm" class="mw-redirect" title="Peers of the realm">peers of the realm</a>, they were referred to as "Lord-Lieutenant".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>An Act to make the Militia of this Kingdom more useful</i> (Geo 2, c.9) was passed by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Parliament of Ireland</a> in 1715. This provided for the issuing of commissions to appoint persons as "his Majesty\'s lieutenant or lieutenants, governor or governors, and commissioners of array for the several and respective counties, cities, and places of Ireland". The lieutenants were empowered to embody militia regiments.  When the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> became independent of the UK in 1922, British rule ceased to exist.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1921, with the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, lord lieutenants continued to be appointed through the Governor of Northern Ireland to the six counties and two county boroughs. In 1973 the counties and county boroughs were abolished as local government units in Northern Ireland, and lord-lieutenants are now appointed directly by the Queen to "counties and county boroughs... as defined for local government purposes immediately before 1 October 1973".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Colin_Lindsay,_3rd_Earl_of_Balcarres" title="Colin Lindsay, 3rd Earl of Balcarres">Colin, Earl of Balcarres</a> was appointed Lord Lieutenant of Fife in 1688,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and lieutenants were appointed to a few counties from about 1715, it was not until 1794 that permanent lieutenancies were established by <a href="/wiki/Warrant_(law)" title="Warrant (law)">Royal Warrant</a>. By the <a href="/wiki/Militia_Act_1797" class="mw-redirect" title="Militia Act 1797">Militia Act 1797</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> the lieutenants appointed "for the Counties, Stewartries, Cities, and Places" were given powers to raise and command county militia units.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Provost" title="Lord Provost">Lord Provosts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, Glasgow, <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> are, by virtue of office, also the lord-lieutenant of their respective city.\n</p><p>While in their lieutenancies, lord-lieutenants are among the few individuals in Scotland officially permitted to fly the banner of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Arms_of_Scotland" title="Royal Arms of Scotland">Royal Arms of Scotland</a>, or "<a href="/wiki/Royal_Banner_of_Scotland" title="Royal Banner of Scotland">The Lion Rampant</a>" as it is more commonly known.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Militia_Act_1802" title="Militia Act 1802">Militia Act 1802</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> provided for the appointment of lieutenants to "Lieutenants for the Counties, Ridings, and Places" in England and Wales, and gave them command of the county militia. In the case of towns or cities which were <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">counties of themselves</a>, the "chief magistrate" (meaning the mayor, chief bailiff or other head of the corporation) had the authority to appoint deputy lieutenants in the absence of an appointment of a lieutenant by the crown. \n</p><p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regulation_of_the_Forces_Act_1871&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Regulation of the Forces Act 1871 (page does not exist)">Regulation of the Forces Act 1871</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> removed the lieutenant as head of the county militia,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> as the jurisdiction, duties and command exercised by the lieutenant were revested in the crown, but the power of recommending for first appointments was reserved to the lieutenant.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Militia_Act_1882&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Militia Act 1882 (page does not exist)">Militia Act 1882</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> revested the jurisdiction of the lieutenants in the crown.\n</p><p>The lieutenancies were reestablished on a new basis by Section 29 of the 1882 Act which stated that "Her Majesty shall from time to time appoint lieutenants for the several counties in the United Kingdom". Counties for lieutenancy purposes were also redefined as "a county at large, with the exception that each riding of the county of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">York</a> shall be a separate county". The text of the <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a> appointing lieutenants under the act stated they were to be:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...Our Lieutenant of and in the County of X and of all cities boroughs liberties places incorporated and privileged and other places whatsoever within the said county and the limits and precincts of the same.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>This was a formal recognition of the situation that had existed since 1662 that the lieutenancies for the majority of counties corporate in England were held jointly with their associated county\xe2\x80\x94for example a lieutenant was now appointed for "the County of Gloucester, and the City and County of Gloucester, and the City and County of City of Bristol".\n</p><p>Haverfordwest was permitted to retain a lieutenant while the Tower Hamlets and Cinque Ports were to continue to be regarded as counties for lieutenancy purposes.\n</p><p>From 1889 lieutenancy counties in England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> were to correspond to groupings of <a href="/wiki/Administrative_county" title="Administrative county">administrative counties</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_boroughs" class="mw-redirect" title="County boroughs">county boroughs</a> established by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>. The creation of a new <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a> also led to the ending of the <a href="/wiki/Tower_division" title="Tower division">Tower Hamlets</a> lieutenancy. The Act also extinguished the lieutenancy of the Cinque Ports.\n</p><p>Section 69 of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Ireland)_Act_1898" title="Local Government (Ireland) Act 1898">Local Government (Ireland) Act 1898</a> realigned the lieutenancy counties with the new administrative counties created by the Act. The one exception was <a href="/wiki/County_Tipperary" title="County Tipperary">County Tipperary</a>, which although administered by two <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a>, was to remain united for lieutenancy. In contrast to the legislation in England and Wales, each <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> was to have its own lieutenant, and those <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">counties corporate</a> not made county boroughs were abolished. The effect of this was to create a lieutenant for the county boroughs of <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Belfast" title="Lord Lieutenant of Belfast">Belfast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_the_City_of_Londonderry" title="Lord Lieutenant of the City of Londonderry">Londonderry</a>, and to abolish those for the city of <a href="/wiki/Kilkenny" title="Kilkenny">Kilkenny</a>, borough of <a href="/wiki/Drogheda" title="Drogheda">Drogheda</a> and town of <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a>.\n</p><p>The office of lieutenant was honorary and held during the royal pleasure, but virtually for life. Appointment to the office is by letters-patent under the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_the_Realm" title="Great Seal of the Realm">great seal</a>. Usually, though not necessarily, the person appointed lieutenant was also appointed <a href="/wiki/Custos_rotulorum" title="Custos rotulorum">custos rotulorum</a> or keeper of the rolls. Appointments to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=County_bench&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="County bench (page does not exist)">county bench</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates</a> were usually made on the recommendation of the lieutenant.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Territorial_and_Reserve_Forces_Act_1907" title="Territorial and Reserve Forces Act 1907">Territorial and Reserve Forces Act 1907</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> established County Territorial Force Associations, of which the lieutenant was to be head, styled president of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=County_association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="County association (page does not exist)">county association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It restated the combination of counties and counties corporate as lieutenancy counties.\n</p><p>In 1921, with the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, lieutenants continued to be appointed through the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Governor of Northern Ireland">Governor of Northern Ireland</a> to the six counties and two county boroughs. The creation of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> in the following year saw the remaining county lieutenancies in Ireland abolished. In 1973 the counties and county boroughs were abolished as local government units in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and lord-lieutenants are now appointed directly by the sovereign to "counties and county boroughs... as defined for local government purposes immediately before 1 October 1973". In 1975 the term lord-lieutenant officially replaced that of lieutenant.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Local government reform in England in 1965 led to the appointment of lieutenants to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.greaterlondonlieutenancy.com/">Greater London</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a>, and the abolition of those of the counties of <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdon</a>.\n</p><p>A more fundamental reform of local government throughout England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> (outside <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>) created a new structure of metropolitan, non-metropolitan and Welsh counties in 1974. Section 218 of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> that established the new system stated: "Her Majesty shall appoint a lord-lieutenant for each county in England and Wales and for Greater London..." The Act appears to be the first statutory use of the term "lord-lieutenant" for lieutenants to counties.\n</p><p>Existing lord lieutenants were assigned to one of the corresponding new counties wherever possible. Where this could not be done, the existing office-holder became a lieutenant of a county, junior to the lord-lieutenant.  For example, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Montgomeryshire" title="Lord Lieutenant of Montgomeryshire">Lord Lieutenant of Montgomeryshire</a> was appointed <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Powys" title="Lord Lieutenant of Powys">Lord Lieutenant of Powys</a>, with those of Breconshire and Radnorshire each being designated as simply "Lieutenant of Powys". This measure was temporary, and no lieutenants have been appointed in this way since 1974, although the power still exists.\n</p><p>In 1975 counties ceased to be used for local government purposes in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Scotland)_Act_1973" title="Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973">Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973</a> replaced the counties with regions, and each region was to have one or more lord-lieutenants appointed.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The areas to which they were appointed approximated to the counties and were based and were defined in terms of the new local government districts.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day">Present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span government reorganisation, and since that date lord-lieutenants have been appointed to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenancy_areas_of_Scotland" title="Lieutenancy areas of Scotland">"lieutenancy areas"</a>, roughly equivalent to the old Scottish counties.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Partial reform of local government in England since 1995 has led to the creation of so-called <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial counties</a> to which lord-lieutenants are now appointed. The <a href="/wiki/Lieutenancies_Act_1997" title="Lieutenancies Act 1997">Lieutenancies Act 1997</a> is the most recent piece of primary legislation dealing with Lieutenancies in England and includes the definitive list of the current areas used. Ceremonial counties may comprise combinations of county council areas and unitary authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the local government re-organisation of 1996 in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, lord-lieutenants are now appointed to <a href="/wiki/Preserved_counties_of_Wales" title="Preserved counties of Wales">"preserved counties"</a>, i.e. the counties used for administrative purposes from 1974 to 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> was unaffected by changes introduced since 1882. It has a Commission of Lieutenancy rather than a single lord-lieutenant. The head of the commission is the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_the_City_of_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Mayor of the City of London">Lord Mayor of the City of London</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duties">Duties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Duties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lord-lieutenants are the monarch\'s representatives in their respective lieutenancies. It is their foremost duty to uphold the dignity of <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a>, and in so doing they seek to promote a spirit of co-operation and good atmosphere through the time they give to voluntary and benevolent organisations and through the interest they take in the business and social life of their counties.\n</p><p>The modern responsibilities of lords-lieutenant include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Arranging visits of members of the <a href="/wiki/British_Royal_Family" class="mw-redirect" title="British Royal Family">royal family</a> and escorting royal visitors;</li>\n<li>Presenting medals and awards on behalf of the sovereign, and advising on <a href="/wiki/British_honours_system" class="mw-redirect" title="British honours system">honours</a> nominations;</li>\n<li>Participating in civic, voluntary and social activities within the lieutenancy;</li>\n<li>Acting as liaison with local units of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">Army</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> and their associated cadet forces;</li>\n<li>Leading the local <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistracy</a> as chairman of the Advisory Committee on <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Peace">Justices of the Peace</a>; and</li>\n<li>Chairing the local Advisory Committee for the Appointment of the General Commissioners of <a href="/wiki/Income_Tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Income Tax">Income Tax</a>, a tribunal which hears appeals against decisions made by the <a href="/wiki/HM_Revenue_and_Customs" title="HM Revenue and Customs">HM Revenue and Customs</a> on a variety of different tax related matters.</li></ul>\n<p>As the sovereign\'s representative in his or her county, a lord-lieutenant remains non-political and may not hold office in any political party. They are appointed for life, although the customary age of retirement is 75 and the sovereign may remove them.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deputies">Deputies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Deputies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Each lord-lieutenant is supported by a vice lord-lieutenant and <a href="/wiki/Deputy_lieutenant" title="Deputy lieutenant">deputy lieutenants</a> that he or she appoints. The vice lord-lieutenant takes over when the lord-lieutenant is abroad, ill, or otherwise incapacitated. The lord-lieutenant appoints between thirty and forty deputy lieutenants depending on the county\'s population.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remuneration">Remuneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Remuneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>They are unpaid, but receive minimal allowances for secretarial help, mileage allowance and a driver. Male lord-lieutenants receive an allowance for the ceremonial uniform, worn when receiving members of the Royal Family and on other formal occasions. There is no uniform for a female lord-lieutenant, but there is a badge which can be worn on ceremonial occasions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniform">Uniform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Uniform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthony_Ashley-Cooper,_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Anthony_Ashley-Cooper%2C_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg/170px-Anthony_Ashley-Cooper%2C_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg" width="170" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Anthony_Ashley-Cooper%2C_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg/255px-Anthony_Ashley-Cooper%2C_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Anthony_Ashley-Cooper%2C_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg/340px-Anthony_Ashley-Cooper%2C_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg 2x" data-file-width="732" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthony_Ashley-Cooper,_9th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lord Lieutenant, full dress uniform (early 20th century)</div></div></div>\n<p>Since at least the eighteenth century a military-style uniform has been worn by male lieutenants (appropriate to the military origins of the post). Since 1831 this has been analogous to the uniform worn by a general staff officer, but with silver lace in place of the gold worn by regular officers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Over time, the design of the uniform changed in line with changes made to army uniform.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> At present, it is a dark blue uniform in the style of a General Officer\'s <a href="/wiki/British_Army_uniform#No._1:_Temperate_ceremonial_uniform" class="mw-redirect" title="British Army uniform">Army No. 1 dress</a> (but with buttons, shoulder-boards, sash etc. in silver rather than gold). A cap is worn, and a sword with a steel scabbard. The badge on the cap etc. varies depending on where the Lieutenant\'s County is situated: a rose is worn in England, shamrocks in Northern Ireland, a thistle in Scotland and Prince-of-Wales feathers in Wales. The uniform for a male vice lord-lieutenant and for deputy lieutenants is of a similar style, but with features to distinguish it from that of a lord-lieutenant.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</a> was the head of the British administration in Ireland until the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> in 1922.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_address_for_lord-lieutenants">Forms of address for lord-lieutenants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Forms of address for lord-lieutenants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Written: \'(Title and name), Her Majesty\'s Lord-Lieutenant\'</li>\n<li>Salutation: \'Dear Lord-Lieutenant\'</li>\n<li>In a speech: \'My Lord-Lieutenant\'</li>\n<li>In conversation: \'(Title and name)\' or \'Lord-Lieutenant\'.</li>\n<li>Plural: \'Lord-lieutenants\',<sup id="cite_ref-royalweb_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royalweb-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> although the historic form of \'Lords-Lieutenant\' frequently appears.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_equivalents">Colonial equivalents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Colonial equivalents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the colonies of the English Empire, and subsequently the British Empire, the duties of lords lieutenant were generally performed by the <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">commander-in-chief</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Governor" title="Governor">governor</a>. Both offices may have been occupied by the same person.\n</p><p>By way of an example, this is still the case in Britain\'s second, and oldest remaining, colony, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Dockyard,_Bermuda" title="Royal Naval Dockyard, Bermuda">Royal Navy\'s headquarters, main base, and dockyard for the North America and West Indies Station</a> was established following independence of the United States of America. The colony had raised militia and volunteer forces since official settlement in 1612 (with a troop-of-horse added later), and a small force of regular infantry from 1701 to 1783. A large regular army garrison was built up after 1794, and the reserve forces faded away following the conclusion of the American War of 1812 as the local government lost interest in paying for their upkeep. From this point until the 1960s, governors were almost exclusively senior officers of the Royal Artillery or Royal Engineers who were also military commanders-in-chief (and initially also vice admirals). Attempts to rekindle the militia without a Militia Act or funds from the colonial government were made throughout the century under the authorisation of the Governor and Commander-in-Chief, but none proved lasting. The colonial government was finally compelled to raise militia and volunteer forces (the <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Militia_Artillery" title="Bermuda Militia Artillery">Bermuda Militia Artillery</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Volunteer_Rifle_Corps" title="Bermuda Volunteer Rifle Corps">Bermuda Volunteer Rifle Corps</a>) by act in the 1890s (the <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Cadet_Corps" title="Bermuda Cadet Corps">Bermuda Cadet Corps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Volunteer_Engineers" title="Bermuda Volunteer Engineers">Bermuda Volunteer Engineers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Militia_Infantry" title="Bermuda Militia Infantry">Bermuda Militia Infantry</a> were added at later dates), and these fell under the Governor and Commander-in-Chief, as well as under operational control of his junior, the brigadier in charge of the Bermuda Command (or <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Garrison" title="Bermuda Garrison">Bermuda Garrison</a>), which included the regular as well as the part-time military (as opposed to naval) forces in the colony. Although the Royal Naval and the regular army establishments have been withdrawn from Bermuda, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Bermuda" title="Governor of Bermuda">Governor of Bermuda</a> remains the Commander-in-Chief (though most recent office holders have not been career army officers) of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bermuda_Regiment" title="Royal Bermuda Regiment">Royal Bermuda Regiment</a> (a 1965 amalgam of the BMA and BVRC, which had both been reorganised in line with the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_Army_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Territorial Army (United Kingdom)">Territorial Army</a> after the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Approximate_equivalents_in_other_countries">Approximate equivalents in other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Approximate equivalents in other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the role of Prefect (<i><a href="/wiki/Prefect_(France)" title="Prefect (France)">pr\xc3\xa9fet</a></i> in French, <i>prefetto</i> in Italian) is different to that of lord-lieutenant, as the regional and departmental prefects of France are responsible for delivering as well as control functions of public services. In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> <i>Landsh\xc3\xb6vding</i> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> "Fylkesmann", the regional governor is responsible for administrative control functions of services delivered and decisions made by local and county municipalities, as well as representing the king in the region. In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Commissioner" title="King&#39;s Commissioner">King\'s Commissioner</a> (<i>Commissaris van de Koning</i> in Dutch) is appointed by the monarch, but unlike a lord-lieutenant, belongs to a political party.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Deputy_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Lieutenant">Deputy Lieutenant</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">Ceremonial counties of England</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="High Sheriff">High Sheriff</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lieutenancy_areas_of_Scotland" title="Lieutenancy areas of Scotland">Lieutenancy areas of Scotland</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Preserved_counties_of_Wales" title="Preserved counties of Wales">Preserved counties of Wales</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_the_Lord_Lieutenants_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="List of the Lord Lieutenants of the United Kingdom">List of the Lord Lieutenants of the United Kingdom</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord-lieutenant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
