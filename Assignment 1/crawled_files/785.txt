colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Paolo Caliari</b>, known as <b>Paolo Veronese</b> (1528 \xe2\x80\x93 19 April 1588), was an <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painter" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance painter">Italian Renaissance painter</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, known for large-format <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history paintings</a> of religion and mythology, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wedding_at_Cana" title="The Wedding at Cana">The Wedding at Cana</a></i> (1563)  and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Feast_in_the_House_of_Levi" title="The Feast in the House of Levi">The Feast in the House of Levi</a></i> (1573). Included with <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, a generation older, and <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a>, a decade senior, Veronese is one of the "great trio that dominated Venetian painting of the <i><a href="/wiki/Cinquecento" title="Cinquecento">cinquecento</a></i>" and the <a href="/wiki/Late_Renaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Renaissance">Late Renaissance</a> in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosand,_107_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosand,_107-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Known as a supreme colorist, and after an early period with <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a>, Paolo Veronese developed a naturalist style of painting, influenced by Titian.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His most famous works are elaborate narrative cycles, executed in a dramatic and colorful style, full of majestic architectural settings and glittering pageantry. His large paintings of biblical feasts, crowded with figures, painted for the refectories of monasteries in Venice and Verona are especially famous, and he was also the leading Venetian painter of ceilings. Most of these works remain <i>in situ</i>, or at least in Venice, and his representation in most museums is mainly composed of smaller works such as portraits that do not always show him at his best or most typical.\n</p><p>He has always been appreciated for "the chromatic brilliance of his palette, the splendor and sensibility of his brushwork, the aristocratic elegance of his figures, and the magnificence of his spectacle", but his work has been felt "not to permit expression of the profound, the human, or the sublime", and of the "great trio" he has often been the least appreciated by modern criticism.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosand,_107_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosand,_107-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, "many of the greatest artists ... may be counted among his admirers, including <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Rubens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Antoine_Watteau" title="Jean-Antoine Watteau">Watteau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Tiepolo" title="Giovanni Battista Tiepolo">Tiepolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eug\xc3\xa8ne Delacroix">Delacroix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Renoir</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Penny,_333_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penny,_333-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div 1547</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_and_names">Birth and names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paolo_Veronese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Birth and names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Veronese took his usual name from his birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a>, then the largest possession of Venice on the mainland. The census in Verona attests that Veronese was born sometime in 1528 to a stonecutter, or spezapreda, named Gabriele, and his wife Catherina. He was their fifth child.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was common for surnames to be taken from a father\'s profession, and thus Veronese was known as Paolo Spezapreda. He later changed his name to Paolo Caliari, because his mother was the illegitimate daughter of a nobleman called Antonio Caliari.<sup id="cite_ref-Penny,_331_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penny,_331-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  His earliest known painting is signed "P. Caliari F., "the first known instance in which he used this surname", and after using "Paolo Veronese" for several years in Venice, after about 1575 he resumed signing his paintings as "Paolo Caliari".<sup id="cite_ref-Penny,_331_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penny,_331-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  He was often called "Paolo Veronese" before the last century to distinguish him from another painter from Verona, "Alessandro Veronese", now known as <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Turchi" title="Alessandro Turchi">Alessandro Turchi</a> (1578\xe2\x80\x931649).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth">Youth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Veronese was apprenticed with <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Badile" title="Antonio Badile">Antonio Badile</a>, who was later to become his father-in-law, and in 1544 was an apprentice of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Francesco_Caroto" title="Giovanni Francesco Caroto">Giovanni Francesco Caroto</a>; both were leading painters in Verona.<sup id="cite_ref-Penny,_331_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penny,_331-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  An altarpiece painted by Badile in 1543 includes striking passages that were most likely the work of his fifteen-year-old apprentice; Veronese\'s precocious gifts soon surpassed the level of the workshop, and by 1544 he was no longer residing with Badile.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Although trained in the culture of <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerism</a> then popular in <a href="/wiki/Parma" title="Parma">Parma</a>, he soon developed his own preference for a more radiant palette.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In his late teens he painted works for important churches in Verona, and in 1551 he was commissioned by the Venetian branch of the important <a href="/wiki/Giustiniani" title="Giustiniani">Giustiniani</a> family to paint the altarpiece for their chapel in the church of <a href="/wiki/San_Francesco_della_Vigna" title="San Francesco della Vigna">San Francesco della Vigna</a>, which was then being entirely rebuilt to the design of <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Sansovino" title="Jacopo Sansovino">Jacopo Sansovino</a>.  In the same year he worked on the decoration of the Villa Soranzo near <a href="/wiki/Treviso" title="Treviso">Treviso</a>, with his fellow Veronese <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Zelotti" title="Giovanni Battista Zelotti">Giovanni Battista Zelotti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anselmo_Canneri" title="Anselmo Canneri">Anselmo Canneri</a>; only fragments of the frescos remain, but they seem to have been important in establishing his reputation.  The description by <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Ridolfi" title="Carlo Ridolfi">Carlo Ridolfi</a> nearly a century later mentions that one of the mythological subjects was <i>The Family of Darius before Alexander</i>, the rare subject in Veronese\'s grandest treatment of secular history, now in the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="National Gallery, London">National Gallery, London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1552 Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Gonzaga" title="Ercole Gonzaga">Ercole Gonzaga</a>, great-uncle of the ruling <a href="/wiki/Guglielmo_Gonzaga,_Duke_of_Mantua" title="Guglielmo Gonzaga, Duke of Mantua">Guglielmo Gonzaga, Duke of Mantua</a>, commissioned an altarpiece for <a href="/wiki/Mantua_Cathedral" title="Mantua Cathedral">Mantua Cathedral</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Caen" title="Caen">Caen</a>, France), which Veronese painted <i>in situ</i>. He doubtless used his time in <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a> to study the ceilings by <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Romano" title="Giulio Romano">Giulio Romano</a>; it was a painter of ceiling frescos that he would initially make his mark in Venice, where he based himself permanently from the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venice">Venice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paolo_Veronese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Venice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg/300px-Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg" width="300" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg/450px-Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg/600px-Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1696" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paolo_Veronese_-_St_Mark_-_WGA24798.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>St Mark</i>, <a href="/wiki/San_Sebastiano,_Venice" title="San Sebastiano, Venice">San Sebastiano</a> (1556\xe2\x80\x9357)</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Palazzetto_del_Veronese_(Venice).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Palazzetto_del_Veronese_%28Venice%29.jpg/220px-Palazzetto_del_Veronese_%28Venice%29.jpg" width="220" height="349" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Palazzetto_del_Veronese_%28Venice%29.jpg/330px-Palazzetto_del_Veronese_%28Venice%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Palazzetto_del_Veronese_%28Venice%29.jpg/440px-Palazzetto_del_Veronese_%28Venice%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4575" data-file-height="7267" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Palazzetto_del_Veronese_(Venice).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>House of Veronese in Venice</div></div></div>\n<p>Veronese moved to Venice in 1553 after obtaining his first state commission, ceilings in fresco decorating the <i>Sala dei Cosiglio dei Dieci</i> (the Hall of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ten" title="Council of Ten">Council of Ten</a>) and the adjoining <i>Sala dei Tre Capi del Consiglio</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Doge%27s_Palace,_Venice" class="mw-redirect" title="Doge&#39;s Palace, Venice">Doge\'s Palace</a>, in the new rooms replacing those lost in the fire of 1547.  His panel of <i>Jupiter Expelling the Vices</i> for the former is now in the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>.  He then painted a <i>History of Esther</i> in the ceiling for the church of <a href="/wiki/San_Sebastiano,_Venice" title="San Sebastiano, Venice">San Sebastiano</a> (1556\xe2\x80\x9357). It was these ceiling paintings and those of 1557 in the <a href="/wiki/Biblioteca_Marciana" title="Biblioteca Marciana">Marciana Library</a> (for which he was awarded a prize judged by Titian and Sansovino) that established him as a master among his Venetian contemporaries.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Already these works indicate Veronese\'s mastery in reflecting both the subtle foreshortening of the figures of <a href="/wiki/Correggio" class="mw-redirect" title="Correggio">Correggio</a> and the heroism of those by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Villa_Barbaro_and_refectory_paintings">Villa Barbaro and refectory paintings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paolo_Veronese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Villa Barbaro and refectory paintings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By 1556 Veronese was commissioned to paint the first of his monumental banquet scenes, the <i>Feast in the House of Simon</i>, which would not be concluded until 1570. Owing to its scattered composition and lack of focus, however, it was not his most successful refectory mural.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the late 1550s, during a break in his work for San Sebastiano, Veronese decorated the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Barbaro" title="Villa Barbaro">Villa Barbaro</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maser,_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="Maser, Italy">Maser</a>, a newly finished building by the architect <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a>. The frescoes were designed to unite humanistic culture with Christian spirituality; wall paintings included portraits of the Barbaro family,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and the ceilings opened to blue skies and mythological figures. Veronese\'s decorations employed complex perspective and <a href="/wiki/Trompe_l%27oeil" class="mw-redirect" title="Trompe l&#39;oeil">trompe l\'oeil</a>, and resulted in a luminescent and inspired visual poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-Rearick,_page_10,_1998_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rearick,_page_10,_1998-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The encounter between architect and artist was a triumph.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg/300px-Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg" width="300" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg/450px-Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg/600px-Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4214" data-file-height="2863" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paolo_Veronese_008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Wedding_at_Cana" title="The Wedding at Cana">The Wedding at Cana</a></i>, 1562\xe2\x80\x931563. <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>Wedding at Cana</i>, painted in 1562\xe2\x80\x931563, was also collaboration with Palladio. It was commissioned by the Benedictine monks for the San Giorgio Maggiore Monastery, on a small island across from Saint Mark\'s, in Venice. The contract insisted on the huge size (to cover 66 square meters), and that the quality of pigment and colors should be of premium quality. For example, the contract specified that the blues should contain the precious mineral lapis-lazuli.(<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>) The contract also specified that the painting should include as many figures as possible. There are a number of portraits (including those of Titian and Tintoretto, as well as a self-portrait of Veronese) staged upon a canvas surface nearly ten meters wide. The scene, taken from the New Testament Book of John, II, 1\xe2\x80\x9311, represents the first miracle performed by Jesus, the making of wine from water, at a marriage in Cana, Galilee. The foreground celebration, a frieze of figures painted in the most shimmering finery, is flanked by two sets of stairs leading back to a terrace, Roman colonnades, and a brilliant sky.<sup id="cite_ref-Rearick,_page_10,_1998_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rearick,_page_10,_1998-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the refectory paintings, as in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Family_of_Darius_before_Alexander" title="The Family of Darius before Alexander">The Family of Darius before Alexander</a></i> (1565\xe2\x80\x931570),<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  Veronese arranged the architecture to run mostly parallel to the picture plane, accentuating the processional character of the composition. The artist\'s decorative genius was to recognize that dramatic perspectival effects would have been tiresome in a living room or chapel, and that the narrative of the picture could best be absorbed as a colorful diversion.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> These paintings offer little in the representation of emotion; rather, they illustrate the carefully composed movement of their subjects along a primarily horizontal axis. Most of all they are about the incandescence of light and color.<sup id="cite_ref-Rearick,_page_13,_1988_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rearick,_page_13,_1988-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The exaltation of such visual effects may have been a reflection of the artist\'s personal well-being, for in 1565 Veronese married Elena Badile, the daughter of his first master, and by whom he would eventually have a daughter and four sons.<sup id="cite_ref-Rearick,_page_13,_1988_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rearick,_page_13,_1988-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Also painted between 1565\xe2\x80\x9370 is his <i>Madonna and Child with St. Elizabeth, the Infant St. John the Baptist, and St. Justina </i>in the <a href="/wiki/Timken_Museum_of_Art" title="Timken Museum of Art">Timken Museum of Art</a>, San Diego. St. Justina, a patroness of Padua and Venice, is at the right with the Blessed Virgin Mother and the Christ child in the center. In contrast to Italian works of a century earlier the infant is rendered convincingly as an infant. What makes one stop and take notice in this painting is the infant\'s reaching out to St. Justina, since a baby of this age would normally limit his gaze to his mother. Completing the work is St. Elizabeth, the cousin of Mary and mother of St. John the Baptist, located on the left. The artist delicately balances the forms of the extended Holy Family and renders them using a superb balance of warm and cool colors.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Feast_in_the_House_of_Levi"><i>The Feast in the House of Levi</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paolo_Veronese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Feast in the House of Levi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1573 Veronese completed the commission for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Feast_in_the_House_of_Levi" title="The Feast in the House of Levi">The Feast in the House of Levi</a></i>, a last-supper painting for the rear wall of the refectory at the <a href="/wiki/Santi_Giovanni_e_Paolo,_Venice" title="Santi Giovanni e Paolo, Venice">Basilica di Santi Giovanni e Paolo</a>, Castello, Venice. Originally titled <i>The Last Supper</i>, the painting was to replace a Titian painting burnt in a fire; Veronese\'s oversized (5.55m x 12.80m) replacement depicted a Last Supper banquet scene that included German soldiers, dwarves, and animals \xe2\x80\x94 the human and animal exotica usual to Veronese\'s representational narratives.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Artistically, <i>The Feast in the House of Levi</i> indicates Veronese\'s technical development in using intense and luminous colours for texture, attention to narrative coherence, the acute representation of human emotion, and the psychologically subtle interplay occurring among the characters who crowd the scene.<sup id="cite_ref-Rearick,_p._14,_1988_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rearick,_p._14,_1988-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_%282%29.jpg/600px-Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_%282%29.jpg" width="600" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_%282%29.jpg/900px-Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_%282%29.jpg/1200px-Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2567" data-file-height="1148" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Banquet_in_the_House_of_Levi_by_Paolo_Veronese_-_Accademia_-_Venice_2016_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Feast_in_the_House_of_Levi" title="The Feast in the House of Levi">The Feast in the House of Levi</a></i> (1573) featured people and animals that the Inquisition perceived as heretical. The Inquisitors\' <a href="/wiki/The_Reformation_and_Art#Art_and_the_Counter-Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="The Reformation and Art">investigation</a> found no heresy, yet ordered Paolo Veronese to re-title the painting something other than <i>The Last Supper</i>, the original title.</div></div></div>\n<p>Given the subject of the painting, the biblical Last Supper, the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanistic</a> depictions of the characters lacked the piousness usual to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic art">Roman Catholic art</a> depicting the Christ character and the events of his life; and the <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">Inquisition</a> readily noticed Veronese\'s irreligiosity. By the 1570s, the theology of the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a> had given legal authority to Roman Catholic doctrine in Venice, which was a new, political development for an artist such as Veronese. Professionally, in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Republic_of_Venice" title="History of the Republic of Venice">Venetian republic</a> of the Late\xe2\x80\x93Renaissance, for an artist, painting crowd scenes had acquired political ramifications regarding who and what appeared in a religious painting commissioned from him, regardless of the patron or patroness.\n</p><p>A decade earlier, the Benedictine monks who commissioned <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wedding_at_Cana" title="The Wedding at Cana">The Wedding at Cana</a></i> (1563) had directed Veronese, as an artist, to freely include as many human figures as would fit in the banquet scene. In contrast, a decade later, Veronese encountered legal, religious constraints that determined the suitability (theological, political, sociological) of who and what he depicted in a painting\xe2\x80\x94thus, on 18 July 1573, the Inquisition legally summoned Veronese before a tribunal, to explain the presence of what Church doctrine considered characters, animals, and indecorum extraneous to an image of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">Last Supper of the Christ</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The tribunal\'s interrogation of the painter Veronese was cautionary, rather than punitive; political, rather than judicial; nonetheless, Veronese explained to the Inquisitiors that \xe2\x80\x9cwe painters take the same liberties as poets and madmen\xe2\x80\x9d in telling a story. Although the Inquisition\'s tribunal ordered Veronese to repaint the last-supper scene, he opposed their remedy to his theological offences, yet was compelled to re-title the painting from the sacramental <i>The Last Supper</i> to <i>The Feast in the House of Levi</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  That an artist, such as Paolo Veronese, had successfully perdured against the Inquisition\'s implied accusation of <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>, indicated he had the discreet political support of a <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(post-Roman_Europe)" title="Patrician (post-Roman Europe)">patrician</a> patron of the arts.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessment">Assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paolo_Veronese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg/170px-Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg" width="170" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg/255px-Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg/340px-Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1379" data-file-height="1720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Veronese-pers%C3%A9e-rennes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perseus_and_Andromeda_(Veronese)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Perseus and Andromeda (Veronese) (page does not exist)">Perseus and Andromeda</a></i> (1576\xe2\x80\x9378)</div></div></div>\n<p>An artist\'s biography of Paolo Veronese was included to the second edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Most_Excellent_Painters,_Sculptors,_and_Architects" title="Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects">Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects</a></i> (1568), by <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a>, with an improved coverage of the painters of the <a href="/wiki/Florentine_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Florentine School">Florentine School</a>.\n</p><p>A fuller biography of Veronese had to await <i>Le maraviglie dell\xe2\x80\x99 Arte ovvero, Le vite degli Illustri Pittori Veneti and dello Stato</i> (1648), by <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Ridolfi" title="Carlo Ridolfi">Carlo Ridolfi</a>, a compilation of the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_school_(art)" title="Venetian school (art)">Venetian School</a> painters. Ridolfi said that Veronese\'s painting of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Feast_in_the_House_of_Levi" title="The Feast in the House of Levi">The Feast in the House of Levi</a></i> (1573) is \xe2\x80\x9cby far, the most important source for our knowledge of his art,\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-Penny,_333_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penny,_333-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> because \xe2\x80\x9cit gave rein to joy, made beauty majestic, made laughter, itself, more festive.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-Rearick,_page_14,_1988_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rearick,_page_14,_1988-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Moreover, in <i>Paintings in the Louvre</i> (1987), <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Gowing" title="Lawrence Gowing">Lawrence Gowing</a>\xe2\x80\x99s modern assessment of Paolo Veronese\xe2\x80\x99s artistic achievement is that:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The French had no doubts, as the critic <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Gautier" title="Th\xc3\xa9ophile Gautier">Th\xc3\xa9ophile Gautier</a> wrote in 1860, that Veronese was the greatest colorist who ever lived\xe2\x80\x94greater than Titian, Rubens, or Rembrandt because he established the harmony of natural tones in place of the modelling in dark and light that remained the method of academic chiaroscuro. Delacroix wrote that Veronese made light without violent contrasts, "which we are always told is impossible, and maintained the strength of hue in shadow".</p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>This innovation could not be better described. Veronese\xe2\x80\x99s bright outdoor harmonies enlightened and inspired the whole nineteenth century. He was the foundation of modern painting. But whether his style is in fact naturalistic, as the <a href="/wiki/Impressionists" class="mw-redirect" title="Impressionists">Impressionists</a> thought, or a most subtle and beautiful imaginative invention must remain a question for each age to answer for itself.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_practices">Working practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paolo_Veronese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Working practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In addition to the ceiling creations and wall paintings, Veronese also produced altarpieces (<i>The Consecration of Saint Nicholas</i>, 1561\xe2\x80\x932, London\'s <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="National Gallery, London">National Gallery</a> <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>), paintings on mythological subjects (<i>Venus and Mars</i>, 1578, New York <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>), and portraits (<i>Portrait of a Lady</i>, 1555, <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>). A significant number of compositional sketches in pen, ink and wash, figure studies in chalk, and <a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">chiaroscuro</a> <a href="/wiki/Modelli" class="mw-redirect" title="Modelli">modelli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ricordo" class="mw-redirect" title="Ricordo">ricordi</a> survive.\n</p><p>He headed a family workshop, including his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Caliari" title="Benedetto Caliari">Benedetto</a> (1538\xe2\x80\x9398) as well as his sons <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Caliari" title="Carlo Caliari">Carlo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gabriele_Caliari" title="Gabriele Caliari">Gabriele</a>, and his nephew <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Benfatto" title="Luigi Benfatto">Luigi Benfatto</a> (also called dal Friso; 1559\xe2\x80\x931611), that remained active for a decade or so after his death in Venice in 1588, signing their work "Haeredes Pauli" ("Heirs of Paolo"), and continuing to use his drawings.  According to <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Penny" title="Nicholas Penny">Nicholas Penny</a>, "The role of the workshop seems to have increased steadily, and after 1580 it is rare that we can feel confident that Veronese\'s was the sole hand involved".<sup id="cite_ref-Penny,_333_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penny,_333-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Among his pupils were his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Zelotti" title="Giovanni Battista Zelotti">Giovanni Battista Zelotti</a> and later, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Antonio_Fasolo" title="Giovanni Antonio Fasolo">Giovanni Antonio Fasolo</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigismondo_de_Stefani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sigismondo de Stefani (page does not exist)">Sigismondo de Stefani</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anselmo_Canneri" title="Anselmo Canneri">Anselmo Canneri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  The Caliari family continued and another Paolo Caliari published the first <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">monograph</a> on his ancestor in 1888.<sup id="cite_ref-Penny,_333_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penny,_333-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Veronese was one of the first painters whose drawings were sought by collectors during his lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-31"
