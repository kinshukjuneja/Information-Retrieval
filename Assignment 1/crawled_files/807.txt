links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/River_Solent" title="River Solent">River Solent</a> was the largest tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_River" title="Channel River">Channel River</a> that drained the <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_Basin" title="Hampshire Basin">Hampshire Basin</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Early_Pleistocene" title="Early Pleistocene">Early Pleistocene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Pliocene">late Pliocene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Antoine_et_al._2003_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Antoine_et_al._2003-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  During the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesolithic period">Mesolithic period</a>, sea levels in northern Europe were some 30 to 40 metres lower than today, and the land that became Britain was a peninsula of northern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Momber_et_al._2011_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Momber_et_al._2011-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-King_1954_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King_1954-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The island was formed at the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">Ice Age</a>, about 8\xe2\x80\x939000 years ago, as sea levels rose, and the land of southern Britain sunk due to the <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a> of the north that had been under the ice.<sup id="cite_ref-Lagarde_et_al._2003._4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lagarde_et_al._2003.-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This flooded the former river valley of the <a href="/wiki/Solent" title="Solent">Solent</a> to the north, and the future <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> to the south, and cut Wight off from the island of Britain. Once open to the sea, tidal scouring widened the Solent.<sup id="cite_ref-Larsonneur_et_al._1982_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larsonneur_et_al._1982-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first inhabitants are assumed to have been hunter-gatherers migrating by land during the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> \n(Old Stone Age) period, as the ice age began to recede and the climate improved.  The island has no visible Paleolithic or <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> (Middle Stone Age) sites, but flints from these periods have been found and are on display at Carisbrooke Castle.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic">Neolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are theories that, during the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Era_(geology)" title="Era (geology)">era</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bouldnor_Cliff" title="Bouldnor Cliff">Bouldnor</a> was a seaport that traded with the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, or that nomadic gatherers roamed over wider areas than previously thought, because wheat was present there 8,000 years ago, hundreds if not thousands of years before it is known to have been grown in northern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_et_al._2015_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_et_al._2015-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cunliffe_2008_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunliffe_2008-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Balter_2015_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Balter_2015-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Longstone near Mottistone (the standing stone on the hill later becoming an Anglo-Saxon meeting place, this \'moot-stone\' giving name to the nearby settlement) is one of the only three surviving <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> (New Stone Age) sites, along with a long barrow on Afton Down and a \'mortuary enclosure\' on Tennyson Down.<sup id="cite_ref-invectis.co.uk_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-invectis.co.uk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age">Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Between the Neolithic and Roman eras, Southeastern Britain experienced significant in migration from the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie,_et_al._2015_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie,_et_al._2015-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Finds of late <a href="/wiki/Late_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Iron Age">Late Iron Age</a> coins, such as the South Wight<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and Shalfleet Hoards, suggest trading links both with nearby tribes and further afield.  The coins and <a href="/wiki/Ingot" title="Ingot">ingots</a> from these hoards had been defaced before burial, for reasons that are unknown; this feature has only been found in coin finds from Hampshire and Wight.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The variety of origin of the coins found locally is more marked than elsewhere in Britain and suggests that the island may have had a degree of political separation.<sup id="cite_ref-Wellington_2001_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wellington_2001-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The island has over 240 bronze age burial mounds or round barrows, nearly all on the chalk downs.  One plough-damaged barrow on Gallibury Down was excavated during 1979-80 and dated to between 1600-1400 BCE. There is also evidence from aerial photographs of ring ditches (the remains of barrows) on the limestone near Bembridge.  The only significant Iron Age find is at Chillerton Down, where there was a hill fort. However three of the Roman villa sites have also produced late Iron Age pottery, which suggests a continuity of occupation.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age Britain</a> had large reserves of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> from <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon" title="Mining in Cornwall and Devon">mining in Cornwall and Devon</a>. Tin is necessary to <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelt</a> <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>. At that time the sea level was much lower, and so tin for export could be brought across the <a href="/wiki/Solent" title="Solent">Solent</a> at low tide on carts,<sup id="cite_ref-Adams_1877_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adams_1877-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hawkes_1984_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawkes_1984-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> or possibly on boats such as those found at <a href="/wiki/Ferriby_Boats" title="Ferriby Boats">Ferriby</a>. A shortage of tin during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Late Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a> and trade disruptions in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> around 1300&#160;BCE may have forced metalworkers to seek an alternative to bronze.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snodgrass_1971_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snodgrass_1971-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>It has been suggested that a 1st century BCE reference to <i>\xce\x8a\xce\xba\xcf\x84\xce\xb9\xce\xbd</i> (<i>Ictin</i>) by the Graeco-Sicilian <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> might refer to the Isle of Wight, although <a href="/wiki/St._Michael%27s_Mount" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Michael&#39;s Mount">St. Michael\'s Mount</a> in Cornwall is now considered more likely.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> A century later, <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Pliny the Elder</a> uses its <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name <i>Vectis</i> and in the mid 2nd century <a href="/wiki/Geographia_(Ptolemy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographia (Ptolemy)">Ptolemy</a> confirms the position of Vectis as <i>"...below Magnus Portus"</i> (probably the port near <a href="/wiki/Bosham" title="Bosham">Bosham</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Roman historian Suetonius calls the island "insula Vecta" in his account of its capture in year 43,<sup id="cite_ref-Julia_Skinner_2012_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Julia_Skinner_2012-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> referring to the future emperor <a href="/wiki/Vespasian" title="Vespasian">Vespasian</a>, who <i>"proceeded to Britain where he fought thirty battles, subjugated two warlike tribes, and captured more than twenty towns, besides the entire Isle of Vectis"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The form <i>Vectis</i> seems reasonably robust but Rivet and Smith<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> were uncertain of its etymology. A gloss on an 1164 manuscript of Nennius equates <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>wiht</i> with Latin <i>divorcium</i>, which has encouraged writers to think that the island sits like a lever (Latin <i>vectis</i>) between the two arms of the Solent. The word could be <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brittonic</a>, from a Celtic root akin to Irish <i>fecht</i> "journey" and Welsh <i>gwaith</i> "work".  A detailed study in 2010<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> draws attention to the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Proto-Germanic</a> word <i>*wextiz</i>, which would have been <i>Vectis</i> in Latin, and survives in various modern-language forms, including Modern English <i>whit</i> "something small" (English <i><a href="/wiki/Wight" title="Wight">wight</a></i> is considered a revival from Middle English), German <i>wicht</i> "dwarf, imp", Dutch <i>wicht</i> "little girl" and Norwegian <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A6ttir" title="V\xc3\xa6ttir"><i>vette</i></a> "being, creature (especially supernatural)". This might suggest that the meaning is something like "daughter island" or "little companion"; however if Germanic languages were not widely spoken in Britain during the Roman era, as has been the consensus, then they would be an unlikely source for the Latin <i>Vectis</i>. However more recently it has been argued that the inhabitants of southern Britain at this time may indeed have been Germanic,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> rather than Celtic, which could re-open the possibility of the island\'s name having Germanic roots.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brading_mosaic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Brading_mosaic.jpg/220px-Brading_mosaic.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Brading_mosaic.jpg/330px-Brading_mosaic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Brading_mosaic.jpg/440px-Brading_mosaic.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1365" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brading_mosaic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roman mosaic at Brading Roman Villa</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_era">Roman era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Roman era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Romans</a> occupied southern Britain, including the Isle of Wight, for nearly four hundred years.  The Romans built no towns or roads on the island, but it became an agricultural centre, and at least seven <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villas</a> are known.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The Roman villas at <a href="/wiki/Newport_Roman_Villa" title="Newport Roman Villa">Newport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brading_Roman_Villa" title="Brading Roman Villa">Brading</a> have been excavated and are open to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> When fully developed around 300 AD, Brading was probably the largest on the Island, being a courtyard villa with impressive mosaics, suggesting a good income was being made from the agricultural produce of the island.<sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At <a href="/wiki/Brading_Roman_Villa" title="Brading Roman Villa">Brading Villa</a> a field system can be seen near <a href="/wiki/Brading_Down" title="Brading Down">Brading Down</a>; the remains associated with the villa can be seen as low banks. The chalk downland was cultivated in prehistoric and Roman times because the light chalk soil was not too difficult to plough.<sup id="cite_ref-invectis.co.uk_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-invectis.co.uk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  It is very likely indeed that the Romans grew vines, as the climate was then warmer than it is now, and on this basis nearby <a href="/wiki/Adgestone" title="Adgestone">Adgestone</a> Vineyard claims to be the oldest in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the late fourth or early fifth centuries AD, Roman troops and officials had withdrawn from Britain.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jutish_kingdom">Jutish kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jutish kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>\'s ecclesiastical history, <i>Vecta</i> [sic], along with parts of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> and most of <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, was settled by <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutes</a>.\n</p><p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Cerdic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cerdic">Cerdic</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Cynric" title="Cynric">Cynric</a> conquered the island in 530. The <i>Chronicle</i> states that after Cerdic died in 534, the island was given to his nephews <a href="/wiki/Stuf" class="mw-redirect" title="Stuf">Stuf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wihtgar" class="mw-redirect" title="Wihtgar">Wihtgar</a>. However, it is uncertain whether "Wihtgar", who is believed to have died in 544, was a real person or came from a misunderstood place name. The "Men of Wight" were known as "Wihtwara". Carisbrooke was known as the "Fort of the Men of Wight" ("Wihtwarasburgh"), or alternatively the fort may have been named after Wihtgar.<sup id="cite_ref-Julia_Skinner_2012_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Julia_Skinner_2012-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is believed that the island became a <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_the_Isle_of_Wight" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of the Isle of Wight">Jutish kingdom</a> ruled by King <a href="/wiki/Stuf" class="mw-redirect" title="Stuf">Stuf</a> and his successors, until 661 when it was invaded by <a href="/wiki/Wulfhere_of_Mercia" title="Wulfhere of Mercia">Wulfhere of Mercia</a> and forcibly converted to Christianity. When he returned to <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a> the island reverted to paganism.  <a href="/wiki/Arwald" title="Arwald">Arwald</a> was its last Jutish King and the last pagan king in Anglo-Saxon England until the Vikings.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bede describes the invasion of the island in 686 (noting that Bede was writing fifty years later, and some of his dates are considered approximate) by <a href="/wiki/Caedwalla" class="mw-redirect" title="Caedwalla">Caedwalla</a>, a Wessex King.  He writes: "After Caedwalla had obtained possession of the kingdom of the Gewissae, he took also the Isle of Wight, which till then was entirely given over to idolatry, and by merciless slaughter, endeavoured to destroy all the inhabitants thereof, and to place in their stead people from his own province; binding himself by a vow, though it is said that he was not yet regenerated in Christ, to give the fourth part of the land and of the spoil to the Lord, if he took the Island. He fulfilled this vow by giving the same for the service of the Lord to Bishop Wilfrid...". Arwald died in action, and his nephews were betrayed to Caedwalla and executed. Bede adds that 300 "hides" (each being the land that could support a family) were given to the Church.\n</p><p>Little archaeological evidence of this period survives. The main sites are cemeteries at Chessell Down and Bowcombe Down, both excavated in the nineteenth century to produce skeletons, iron swords, knives, and jewellery such as brooches and buckles. The biggest brooches are in the British Museum but some swords, parts of shields, small brooches and buckles are retained in the county archaeological centre.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Saxon_period_and_the_Vikings">The Saxon period and the Vikings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Saxon period and the Vikings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From about 685-686 the island can be considered part of Wessex, and after the West Saxon kings ruled all England, then part of England.\n</p><p>The Anglo Saxon Chronicle tells how <i>Wiht-land</i> suffered from <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> raids: "<i>And then another time they lay in the Isle of Wight, and meanwhile ate out of Hampshire and of Sussex</i>". <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>\'s navy defeated the Danes in 871 after they had "ravaged Devon and the Isle of Wight".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Around 900 the kingdom was divided into shires, and the island became part of the <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shire</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>.\n</p><p>During the second wave of Viking attacks in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ethelred_the_Unready" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethelred the Unready">Ethelred the Unready</a> (975-1014) the island was often used by them as a temporary winter base, when they were unable to shelter with their \'cousins\' in Normandy.\n</p><p>In 1002 Ethelred ordered the killing of all Danes in England, in the <a href="/wiki/St._Brice%27s_Day_Massacre" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Brice&#39;s Day Massacre">St. Brice\'s Day Massacre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  From 1003 onwards, <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Forkbeard" title="Sweyn Forkbeard">Sweyn Forkbeard</a> raided England, which may partly have been to avenge the massacre.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Also in 1002 Ethelred had married the Norman princess Emma, and then persuaded the Normans not to give the Vikings shelter.  Thereafter they were more regular visitors to the island.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  In 1006 the Chronicle records: "<i>when it drew near to winter, the army travelled home, and after Martinmas the raiding-army came to its secure base on the Isle of Wight and there provided themselves everywhere whatever they needed</i>", and then records raids that they made from there into southern England.\n</p><p>In 1013 Sweyn began a more concerted invasion, and Ethelred was forced to flee to the island: "<i>And then at midwinter the King turned from the fleet to the Isle of Wight, and was there for the season; and after the season turned across the sea to Richard [of Normandy], and was there until the happy event of Swein\'s death occurred</i>.".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After Ethelred himself died, England came under Danish rule, and the island continued to be a base for their fleets as recorded by the Chronicle in 1022: "Here King Cnut went out with his ships to Wight."\n</p><p>After England returned to an English king, occasional Viking raids continued; in 1048 the Chronicle records: "a great earth-tremor widely in England. And in the same year Sandwich and Wight were raided, and the best men that were there were killed; and King Edward [the Confessor] and the earls went out after them in their ships".  The Viking practice of sub-dividing the kingdom amongst powerful Earls had survived their departure, and Wessex came under the control of <a href="/wiki/Earl_Godwin" class="mw-redirect" title="Earl Godwin">Earl Godwin</a>, and then his son <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold Godwinson</a>, the future King Harold.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the war of succession in 1066, the island was used as a base by various contenders.  Firstly "came Earl Tostig from beyond the sea into Wight, and with as great a fleet as he could get, and there was given both money and provisions; and then went from there, and did harm everywhere along the sea-cost he could get to" and later his brother Harold Godwinson "...went into Wight, and lay there all the summer and the autumn; and a land army was kept everywhere by the sea, although in the end it was to no avail".  Both men had manors on the island - Harold at Kern and <a href="/wiki/Tostig_Godwinson" title="Tostig Godwinson">Tostig</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nunwell" title="Nunwell">Nunwell</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-british-history.ac.uk1912_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-british-history.ac.uk1912-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Norman_Conquest">The Norman Conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Norman Conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a> in 1066, the Isle of Wight and the rest of England entered the Norman era.  The island has never since been successfully invaded by a foreign army.\n</p><p>In the Domesday book of 1086 the Island\'s name is "Wit". <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> gave its manorial rights to his kinsman <a href="/wiki/William_FitzOsbern" class="mw-redirect" title="William FitzOsbern">William FitzOsbern</a>, who became <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Isle_of_Wight" title="Lord of the Isle of Wight">Lord of the Isle of Wight</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carisbrooke_Priory" title="Carisbrooke Priory">Carisbrooke Priory</a> and the fort of <a href="/wiki/Carisbrooke_Castle" title="Carisbrooke Castle">Carisbrooke Castle</a> were founded (probably the site had previously been fortified, originally by Romans and subsequently by Jutes or Saxons; there are remains of a late Saxon wall or <i>burgh</i> as defence against Viking raiders). Thereafter allegiance on the island was sworn to FitzOsbern as \'Lord\' rather than the king, a role subsequently granted to the de Redvers family by Henry I after his succession in 1100.\n</p><p>For nearly 200 years the island was a semi-independent feudal fiefdom, with the <a href="/wiki/Richard_de_Redvers" title="Richard de Redvers">de Redvers</a> family ruling from Carisbrooke. The final private owner was the Norman Countess <a href="/wiki/Isabel_de_Forz,_suo_jure_8th_Countess_of_Devon" title="Isabel de Forz, suo jure 8th Countess of Devon">Isabella de Fortibus</a>, who inherited it aged 25 after her brother died suddenly. She ruled for thirty years, during which time all of her children died. On her deathbed in 1293 she was persuaded to sell the island to Edward I, and so it came under control of the English crown.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Newtown,_Isle_of_Wight" title="Newtown, Isle of Wight">Newtown</a> was laid out by a Bishop of Winchester and his ecclesiastical estate at Swainston, and granted Borough status in 1256. It did not prosper, partly because it was in competition with the existing ports of Yarmouth, Newport and Southampton, and partly because of the extensive damage it later suffered during French raids (especially in 1377).<sup id="cite_ref-invectis.co.uk_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-invectis.co.uk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Newport was laid out in the late 12th Century as the \'new port\' for Carisbrooke Castle, the town designed on a grid system, unusual for its time.\n</p><p>Notable surviving monuments from this period include the 11th century church of St. Boniface at Bonchurch, the 12th century church at Yaverland, and the Norman entrance arch (now to the churchyard) at Binstead.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_period">Medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg/220px-Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg/330px-Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg/440px-Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carisbrooke_Castle_gate_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>15th century gatehouse of Carisbrooke Castle</div></div></div>\n<p>On 20 April 1313 the ship St. Marie was wrecked in Chale Bay, its cargo of wine plundered by local landowners.  This led to the construction of <a href="/wiki/St._Catherine%27s_Oratory" title="St. Catherine&#39;s Oratory">St. Catherine\'s Oratory</a> as penance for the crime.\n</p><p>In 1374, the fleet of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_S%C3%A1nchez_de_Tovar" title="Fernando S\xc3\xa1nchez de Tovar">Fernando S\xc3\xa1nchez de Tovar</a>, sacked and burned the island.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1377 French raiders attacked and burned several towns, including Newtown, and laid siege to Carisbrooke Castle.  <a href="/wiki/Peter_de_Heyno" title="Peter de Heyno">Peter de Heynoe</a>, Lord of Stenbury, is said to have killed the French commander with a crossbow shot aimed through an arrow slit still visible in the castle\'s west wall.\n</p><p>The island continued to be governed by a Lordship, but this was now a royal appointment (the position evolving over the years into a Captaincy and eventually a Governorship). It is sometimes said there was a brief interruption in 1444 when <a href="/wiki/Henry_Beauchamp,_1st_Duke_of_Warwick" title="Henry Beauchamp, 1st Duke of Warwick">Henry Beauchamp</a> was crowned King of the Isle of Wight,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">King Henry VI</a> placing the crown on his head;  the regal title supposedly expired when he died heirless in 1446. But there little evidence to support this story.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> and its permanent base at <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, and ordered fortifications to be built at Yarmouth, Cowes, East Cowes and Sandown, sometimes using stone from the dissolved monasteries.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="16th_century">16th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 16th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/French_invasion_of_the_Isle_of_Wight" title="French invasion of the Isle of Wight">French invaded the island</a> on 21 July 1545. English ships engaged the French navy; two days earlier, the <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Rose" title="Mary Rose">Mary Rose</a></i> had sunk, apparently without damage.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  Sir Richard Worsley, Captain of the Island, commanded the local <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> who resisted the attack, after French troops landed on the southern coast intending to use the island as a base against the mainland.  However, the local militia defeated the French troops at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bonchurch" title="Battle of Bonchurch">Battle of Bonchurch</a> (although one account claims the French were successful at Bonchurch but defeated at Bembridge).<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1587 two Catholic missionaries Anderton and Marsden, originally from Lancashire but trained in France, returned to England in disguise, heading for Dover. However, due to a gale their ship landed in Cowes.  Fellow passengers had overheard them praying and they were reported on arrival to Governor Carey, taken to London for trial, then executed by <a href="/wiki/Hanging,_drawing_and_quartering" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanging, drawing and quartering">hanging, drawing and quartering</a> in Cowes. They were declared "venerable" by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">Pope Pius XI</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On 23 July 1588 some of the decisive action in the naval battle against the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> took place within sight of the island, off <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Portland" title="Isle of Portland">Portland</a>.  Afterwards the threat of Spanish attacks remained, and the outer fortifications of Carisbrooke Castle were built between 1597 and 1602.\n</p><p>In the late 16th century the bubonic plague arrived in Newport.  <a href="/wiki/God%27s_Providence_House,_Newport" title="God&#39;s Providence House, Newport">God\'s Providence House</a> is traditionally the only house in the town where no-one died.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Three of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a>\'s most influential courtiers came from Newport:  Dr Edes, her chaplain, Dr James, her doctor, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fleming_(judge)" title="Thomas Fleming (judge)">Thomas Fleming</a>, who became Lord Chief Justice and presided over the trial of <a href="/wiki/Guy_Fawkes" title="Guy Fawkes">Guy Fawkes</a> in 1606.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_and_18th_centuries">17th and 18th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 17th and 18th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Towards the end of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> in November 1647, <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> rode out from London to avoid capture by the army, hoping to escape to <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>. He became lost in the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a>, missed the intended ship, and so fled to the Isle of Wight.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> But its Governor Colonel <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hammond_(English_army_officer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Hammond (English army officer)">Robert Hammond</a> had already declared for Parliament, and so he imprisoned Charles in Carisbrooke Castle.\n</p><p>The king made several failed attempts to escape, one time famously getting stuck in a castle window.  He also had letters smuggled out, most of which were intercepted, which he signed \'J\' to disguise his identity (donkeys operating the castle\'s water-well have subsequently been given names beginning with \'J\').  In September 1648 he discussed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Newport" title="Treaty of Newport">Treaty of Newport</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> faction of Parliament, which would have granted him a constitutional monarchy. However, he had no intention of accepting its restrictions, and so also conspired for the Scots to invade England on his behalf.  This led to his eventual execution on 30 January 1649.\n</p><p>In 1669, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Robert_Holmes" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Robert Holmes">Sir Robert Holmes</a> became governor, and re-organised the island\'s defences.  Yarmouth\'s castle was reduced in size, and he built a house for himself alongside, now the George Hotel.  His position as the local vice-admiral entitled him to two-thirds of the value of any prizes captured in home waters, and he preyed enthusiastically on foreign ships that happened to sail nearby.  On one of his raids he captured a part-completed statue of the French king, and had a likeness of his own head attached instead.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain_in_the_Seven_Years%27_War" title="Great Britain in the Seven Years&#39; War">Seven Years\' War</a>, the island was used as a staging post for British troops departing on expeditions against the French coast, such as the <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Rochefort" title="Raid on Rochefort">Raid on Rochefort</a>. During 1759, with a <a href="/wiki/Planned_French_Invasion_of_Britain_(1759)" class="mw-redirect" title="Planned French Invasion of Britain (1759)">planned French invasion imminent</a>, a large force of soldiers was stationed there. The French called off their invasion following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Quiberon_Bay" title="Battle of Quiberon Bay">Battle of Quiberon Bay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_19th_century">Early 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Early 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The famous boat-building firm of <a href="/wiki/J._Samuel_White" title="J. Samuel White">J. Samuel White</a> was established on the Island in 1802.  Other noteworthy marine manufacturers followed over the 19th and 20th centuries including <a href="/wiki/Saunders-Roe" title="Saunders-Roe">Saunders-Roe</a> a key manufacturer of the <a href="/wiki/Flying-boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Flying-boat">Flying-boats</a> and the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Hovercraft" title="Hovercraft">hovercraft</a>. The tradition of maritime industry continues on the Island today.\n</p><p>The famous <a href="/wiki/Regency" class="mw-redirect" title="Regency">Regency</a> architect <a href="/wiki/John_Nash_(architect)" title="John Nash (architect)">John Nash</a> lived on the island, and designed Newport\'s Guidhall, completed in 1816.  It now houses the island\'s history museum.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Victorian_era">Victorian era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Victorian era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the 1860s, what remains in real terms the most expensive ever government spending project saw fortifications built on the island and in the Solent, as well as elsewhere along the south coast, including the <a href="/wiki/Palmerston_Forts" title="Palmerston Forts">Palmerston Forts</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Needles_Battery" title="The Needles Battery">The Needles Battery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Victoria_(Isle_of_Wight)" title="Fort Victoria (Isle of Wight)">Fort Victoria</a>, because of fears about the strength of the French and their possible invasion.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a> spent childhood holidays on the island and became fond of it.  When Queen she made <a href="/wiki/Osborne_House" title="Osborne House">Osborne House</a> her winter home, and so the island became a fashionable holiday resort for many Victorians, including <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Alfred, Lord Tennyson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julia_Margaret_Cameron" title="Julia Margaret Cameron">Julia Margaret Cameron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> (who wrote much of <i><a href="/wiki/David_Copperfield_(novel)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Copperfield (novel)">David Copperfield</a></i> there), as well as the French painter <a href="/wiki/Berthe_Morisot" title="Berthe Morisot">Berthe Morisot</a> and members of European royalty (many of whom were family relations).<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Until then, the island had been rural, with most people employed in farming, fishing or boat-building.  The boom in tourism, spurred by growing wealth and leisure time, and by Victoria\'s example, led to significant urban development of the island\'s coastal resorts; the island saw significant expansion as many new homes and hotels were built.\n</p><p>Queen Victoria died at Osborne House on 22 January 1901, aged 81.\n</p><p>During her reign, the world\'s first radio station was set up by <a href="/wiki/Guglielmo_Marconi" title="Guglielmo Marconi">Marconi</a> in 1897 at <a href="/wiki/The_Needles_Battery" title="The Needles Battery">the Needles Battery</a>, at the western tip of the island.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 1898 the first paid telegram (called a \'Marconigram\' at the time) was sent from this station, and the island is now the home of the National Wireless Museum, near Ryde.<sup id="cite_ref-Julia_Skinner_2012_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Julia_Skinner_2012-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the mid- to late-nineteenth century, a sizeable <a href="/wiki/Railways_on_the_Isle_of_Wight" title="Railways on the Isle of Wight">network of railways</a> was built on the island, notable for its punishing gradients and numerous tunnels, particularly to reach the town of <a href="/wiki/Ventnor" title="Ventnor">Ventnor</a>.  Since the early twentieth century, these lines were often linked to plans for a tunnel under the Solent, an idea still talked of today.  Most of the rail network closed between 1956 and 1966, and is now a series of <a href="/wiki/Cyclepath" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyclepath">cyclepaths</a>.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> the island was frequently bombed. With its proximity to German-occupied France, the island had a number of observation stations and transmitters, as well as the <a href="/wiki/RAF_Ventnor" title="RAF Ventnor">RAF radar station</a> at Ventnor. It was the starting-point for one of the earlier <a href="/wiki/Operation_Pluto" title="Operation Pluto">Operation Pluto</a> pipelines to feed fuel to Europe after the <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">Normandy landings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ddaymuseum_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ddaymuseum-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Needles_Battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Needles Battery">Needles Battery</a> was used to develop and test the <a href="/wiki/Black_Arrow" title="Black Arrow">Black Arrow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Knight_(rocket)" title="Black Knight (rocket)">Black Knight</a> space rockets, which were subsequently launched from <a href="/wiki/Woomera_Test_Facility" class="mw-redirect" title="Woomera Test Facility">Woomera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Standard_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Standard-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG/220px-Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG" width="220" height="303" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG/330px-Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG/440px-Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2020" data-file-height="2784" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jimi_Hendrix_statue_outside_Dimbola_Lodge.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> outside Dimbola Lodge</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Festival" title="Isle of Wight Festival">Isle of Wight Festival</a> was a very large <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> festival that took place near <a href="/wiki/Afton_Down" title="Afton Down">Afton Down</a>, West Wight in 1970, following two smaller concerts in 1968 and 1969. The 1970 show was notable both as one of the last public performances by <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> and for the number of attendees, reaching by some estimates 600,000.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The festival was revived in 2002 in a different format, and is now an annual event.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first Governor to hold the crown representative title used now of <a href="/wiki/Lord-Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord-Lieutenant">Lord-Lieutenant</a> was <a href="/wiki/Louis_Mountbatten,_1st_Earl_Mountbatten_of_Burma" title="Louis Mountbatten, 1st Earl Mountbatten of Burma">Lord Mountbatten of Burma</a> until his murder in 1979. Lord Mottistone was the last Lord Lieutenant to also hold the title Governor (from 1992 to 1995). Since 1995 there has been no Governor appointed and Mr Christopher Bland has been the Lord Lieutenant.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_history">Political history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Political history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island\'s most ancient borough was <a href="/wiki/Newtown_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Newtown (UK Parliament constituency)">Newtown</a> on the large natural harbour on the island\'s north-western coast. A French raid in 1377, which destroyed much of the town as well as other Island settlements, sealed its permanent decline.  By the middle of the 16th century it was a small settlement long eclipsed by the more easily defended town of <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Isle_of_Wight" title="Newport, Isle of Wight">Newport</a>. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a> breathed some life into the town by awarding two <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">parliamentary seats</a> but this ultimately made it one of the most notorious of the <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotten Borough">Rotten Boroughs</a>.  By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Great Reform Act</a> that abolished the seats, it had just fourteen houses and twenty-three voters.  The Act also disenfranchised the borough of <a href="/wiki/Yarmouth_(Isle_of_Wight)_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Yarmouth (Isle of Wight) (UK Parliament constituency)">Yarmouth</a> and replaced the four lost seats with the first MP for the whole Isle of Wight; <a href="/wiki/Newport_(Isle_of_Wight)_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Newport (Isle of Wight) (UK Parliament constituency)">Newport</a> also retained its two MPs, though these were reduced to one in 1868 and eventually abolished completely in 1885.\n</p><p>Often thought of as part of Hampshire, the Isle of Wight was briefly included in that county when the first <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> were created in 1888. However, a "Home Rule" campaign led to a separate county council being established for the Isle of Wight in 1890, and it has remained separate ever since. Like inhabitants of many islands, Islanders are fiercely jealous of their real (or perceived) independence, and confusion over the Island\'s separate status is a perennial source of friction.\n</p><p>It was planned to merge the county back into Hampshire as a district in the 1974 local government reform, but a last minute change led to it retaining its county council.  However, since there was no provision made in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> for <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authorities</a>, the Island had to retain a two-tier structure, with a county council and two boroughs, <a href="/wiki/Medina,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Medina, England">Medina</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Wight" title="South Wight">South Wight</a>.\n</p><p>The borough councils were merged with the county council on 1 April 1995, to form a single <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Council" title="Isle of Wight Council">Isle of Wight Council</a>. The only significant present-day administrative link with <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> is the police service, the <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_Constabulary" title="Hampshire Constabulary">Hampshire Constabulary</a>, which is joint between Hampshire and the Isle of Wight.\n</p><p>From the closing decades of the 20th century onwards, there has been considerable debate on the Island over whether or not a bridge or tunnel should connect the island with mainland <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isle_of_Wight_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Isle of Wight Party (page does not exist)">Isle of Wight Party</a> campaigned from a positive position, although extensive public debate on the subject revealed a strong body of opinion amongst islanders against such a proposal. In 2002 the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Council" title="Isle of Wight Council">Isle of Wight Council</a> debated the issue and made a policy statement against the proposal.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/History_of_England" title="History of England">History of England</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Rifles" title="Isle of Wight Rifles">Isle of Wight Rifles</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Festival_1970" title="Isle of Wight Festival 1970">Isle of Wight Festival 1970</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight#Notable_residents" title="Isle of Wight">Notable residents of the Isle of Wight</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Wight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-Antoine_et_al._2003-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Antoine_et_al._2003_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation journal">Antoine et al. 2003., Pierre (2003).
