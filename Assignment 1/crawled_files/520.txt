Britain</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Great Britain road numbering scheme</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Numbering_scheme" title="Numbering scheme">numbering scheme</a> used to <a href="/wiki/Categorization" title="Categorization">classify</a> and identify all roads in Great Britain. \nEach road is given a single letter, which represents the road\'s category, and a subsequent number, of 1 to 4 digits. Introduced to arrange funding allocations, the numbers soon became used on maps and as a method of navigation. Two sub-schemes exist: one for <a href="/wiki/List_of_motorways_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of motorways in the United Kingdom">motorways</a>, and another for non-motorway roads.\n</p><p>The scheme applies only to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. Similar systems are used in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Roads_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Roads in Northern Ireland">Roads in Northern Ireland</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Roads_in_the_Isle_of_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Roads in the Isle of Man">Roads in the Isle of Man</a>), <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territories">British overseas territories</a>. These other numbering schemes use identical basic conventions and road-sign designs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Britain_road_numbering_scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Work on classification began in 1913 by the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Government">government</a>\'s Roads Board, with the aim of denoting the quality and usage of British roads. The work was interrupted by the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cbrdhow_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbrdhow-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It did not resume until the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Ministry of Transport</a> was formed in 1919 and given authority to classify highways<sup id="cite_ref-dftfoi20050805_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dftfoi20050805-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and to allocate funding for road maintenance, authority for which was granted by section 17 (2) of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Transport_Act_1919" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Transport Act 1919">Ministry of Transport Act 1919</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cbrdministry_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbrdministry-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> A classification system was created, under which important routes connecting large population centres, or for through traffic, were designated as Class I, and roads of lesser importance were designated as Class II. The definitive list of those roads was published on 1 April 1923, following consultations with <a href="/wiki/Local_authorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Local authorities">local authorities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1922roadslist_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1922roadslist-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Government funding towards the repairs of these roads were set at 60% for the former and 50% for the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Shortly after this, the numbers started to appear in road atlases and on signs on the roads themselves, making them a tool for motorists<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> in addition to their use for determining funding. The numbers of the roads changed quite frequently during the early years of the system, because it was a period of rapid expansion of the network and some numbered routes did not follow the most usual routes taken.<sup id="cite_ref-1920srenumbering_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1920srenumbering-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Trunk Roads Act 1936 gave the Ministry direct control of major routes and a new classification system was created to identify these routes. Originally, those numbers beginning in T were to be made public, but that was eventually deemed unnecessary.<sup id="cite_ref-cbrdhow_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbrdhow-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the introduction of motorways in the late 1950s, a new classification of "M" was introduced. In many cases the motorways duplicated existing stretches of A road, which therefore lost much of their significance and were in some cases renumbered. There was no consistent approach to the renumbering \xe2\x80\x93 some A roads retained their existing number as non-primary roads (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/A40_road" title="A40 road">A40</a> running alongside the <a href="/wiki/M40_motorway" title="M40 motorway">M40</a>), others were given "less significant" numbers (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/A34_road_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="A34 road (England)">A34</a> in Warwickshire became the A3400 after the M40 was built), and the remainder were downgraded to B or unclassified roads (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/A38_road" title="A38 road">A38</a>, which was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5</a> between <a href="/wiki/Tiverton,_Devon" title="Tiverton, Devon">Tiverton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (December 2007)">specify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Occasionally, the new motorway would take the name of the old A road rather than having its own number. The most notable example of that is the <a href="/wiki/A1(M)" class="mw-redirect" title="A1(M)">A1(M)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sykesAm_9-0" class="reference"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Britain_road_numbering_scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-motorway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In England and Wales the road numbering system for all-purpose (i.e. non-motorway) roads is based on a radial pattern centred on <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. In Scotland the same scheme is centred on <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>. title="Mid Wales">Mid Wales</a>.</li>\n<li>Zone 5: North/East of the A5, west of the A6, south of the <a href="/wiki/Solway_Firth" title="Solway Firth">Solway Firth</a>/<a href="/wiki/Eden_Estuary" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>. In Central London, the A40 (Holborn Viaduct, Holborn, High Holborn and Oxford Street) provides a border between the 4 and 5 zones east of Marble Arch. The original A5 (now renumbered A5183) also provides such a border, and north of St Albans the original A6 (now renumbered A1081) provides an Eastern border.</li>\n<li>Zone 6: East of the A6 and A7, west of the A1 covering eastern <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Scottish Borders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lothians" class="mw-redirect" title="Lothians">Lothians</a>. Between St Albans and Luton, the original A6 (now renumbered A1081) provides the Western border of the 6-zone.</li>\n<li>Zone 7: North of the Solway Firth/Eden Estuary, west of the A7, south of the A8 covering <a href="/wiki/Dumfries_and_Galloway" title="Dumfries and Galloway">Dumfries and Galloway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Belt" title="Central Belt">Central Scotland</a></li>\n<li>Zone 8: North of the A8, west of the A9 covering <a href="/wiki/Highland_(council_area)" title="Highland (council area)">Highland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a></li>\n<li>Zone 9: North of the A8, east of the A9 covering <a href="/wiki/North_East_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="North East Scotland">North East Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a></li></ul>\n<p>The first digit in the number of any road should be the number of the furthest-anticlockwise zone entered by that road. For example, the <a href="/wiki/A38_road" title="A38 road">A38 road</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Trunk_road" title="Trunk road">trunk road</a> running from <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mansfield" title="Mansfield">Mansfield</a> starts in Zone 3, and is therefore numbered with an A3x number, even though it passes through Zones 4 and 5 to end in Zone 6. Additionally, the A1 in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> has moved twice. Originally along the Great North Road, it then moved to the <a href="/wiki/Tyne_Tunnel" title="Tyne Tunnel">Tyne Tunnel</a>, causing some of the roads in Zone 1 to lie in Zone 6. The designated A1 later moved to the western bypass around the city, and roads between the two found themselves back in Zone 1. For the most part the roads affected retained their original numbers throughout.\n</p><p>Elsewhere when single-digit roads were bypassed, roads were often re-numbered in keeping with the original zone boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-ARoad_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARoad-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In a limited number of cases road numbering does not necessarily follow the rules with some <a href="/wiki/Anomalously_numbered_roads_in_Great_Britain" title="Anomalously numbered roads in Great Britain">anomalously numbered</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Motorway_number_zones_in_England_and_Wales_map.svg/330px-Motorway_number_zones_in_England_and_Wales_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Motorway_number_zones_in_England_and_Wales_map.svg/440px-Motorway_number_zones_in_England_and_Wales_map.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1038" three decades after the advent of the A-road numbering event, and as a result required a new numbering system. They were given an M prefix, and in England and Wales a numbering system of their own not coterminous with that of the A-road network, though based on the same principle of zones.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Running clockwise from the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1</a> the zones were defined for Zones 1 to 4 based on the proposed <a href="/wiki/M2_motorway_(Great_Britain)" title="M2 motorway (Great Britain)">M2</a>, <a href="/wiki/M3_motorway_(Great_Britain)" title="M3 motorway (Great Britain)">M3</a> and <a href="/wiki/M4_motorway" title="M4 motorway">M4 motorways</a>. The <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5</a> and <a href="/wiki/M6_motorway" title="M6 motorway">M6</a> numbers were reserved for the other two planned long distance motorways.<sup id="cite_ref-sykesnumber_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykesnumber-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Preston_Bypass" class="mw-redirect" title="Preston Bypass">Preston Bypass</a>, the UK\'s first motorway section, should have been numbered A6(M) under the scheme decided upon, but it was decided to keep the number M6 as had already been applied.<sup id="cite_ref-sykesnumber_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykesnumber-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  The first full length motorway in the UK was the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a>.\n</p><p>Shorter motorways typically take their numbers from a parent motorway in contravention of the zone system, explaining the <a href="/wiki/Anomalously_numbered_roads_in_Great_Britain" title="Anomalously numbered roads in Great Britain">apparently anomalous numbers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/M48_motorway" title="M48 motorway">M48</a> and <a href="/wiki/M49_motorway" title="M49 motorway">M49</a> motorways as <a href="/wiki/Spur_route" title="Spur route">spurs</a> of the M4, and <a href="/wiki/M271_motorway" title="M271 motorway">M271</a> and <a href="/wiki/M275_motorway" title="M275 motorway">M275</a> motorways as those of the <a href="/wiki/M27_motorway" title="M27 motorway">M27</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\nThis numbering system was devised in 1958\xe2\x80\x9359 by the then <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Ministry of Transport and Civil Aviation</a>, and applied only in England and Wales. It was decided to reserve the numbers 7, 8 &amp; 9 for Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland, where roads were the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Office" title="Scottish Office">Scottish Office</a> (Scottish Government after 1999), the decision was taken to adopt a scheme whereby motorways took the numbers of the all-purpose routes they replaced. As a result, there is no M7 (as no motorway follows the A7), and when the <a href="/wiki/A90_road" title="A90 road">A90</a> was re-routed to replace the <a href="/wiki/A85_road" title="A85 road">A85</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a>, the short M85 became part of the <a href="/wiki/M90_motorway" title="M90 motorway">M90</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_roads">A roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Britain_road_numbering_scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: A roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single-digit_A_roads">Single-digit A roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Britain_road_numbering_scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Single-digit A roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In England and Wales, the six single-digit numbers reflect the traditionally most important radial routes coming out of London. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These routes are not all centred on London, but as far as possible follow the general principle that their number locates them radially clockwise from the associated single digit route.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/A10_road_(England)" title="A10 road (England)">A10</a> (London to King\'s Lynn) is the first main route clockwise from the A1, the <a href="/wiki/A11_road_(England)" title="A11 road (England)">A11</a> (London to Norwich) is the next, then the <a href="/wiki/A12_road_(England)" title="A12 road (England)">A12</a> (London to Great Yarmouth) and the <a href="/wiki/A13_road_(England)" title="A13 road (England)">A13</a> (London to Shoeburyness); the next radial is the <a href="/wiki/A2_road_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="A2 road (Great Britain)">A2</a>, followed by the <a href="/wiki/A20_road_(England)" title="A20 road (England)">A20</a> (London to Dover), and so on. These roads have been numbered either outwards from or clockwise around their respective hubs, depending on their alignment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (December 2007)">specify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The system continues to three and four digit numbers which further split and criss-cross the radials. Lower numbers originate closer to London than higher numbered ones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (December 2007)">specify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> As roads have been improved since the scheme commenced, some roads with 3 or 4 digit numbers have increased in significance, for example the <a href="/wiki/A127_road" title="A127 road">A127</a>, <a href="/wiki/A1079_road" title="A1079 road">A1079</a> and <a href="/wiki/A414_road" title="A414 road">A414</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. title="A720 road">A720</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Major_Road_Network" title="Major Road Network">Major Road Network</a> is a proposed classification of major local-authority controlled A roads Primary routes include both trunk and non-trunk roads.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorway_sections">Motorway sections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Britain_road_numbering_scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Motorway sections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some sections of A roads have been improved to the same standard as motorways, but do not completely replace the existing road; they form a higher standard part of the route for those which are not excluded.<sup id="cite_ref-sykesAm_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykesAm-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These sections retain the same number but are suffixed with (M), for example the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1(M)</a> and <a href="/wiki/A404(M)_motorway" title="A404(M) motorway">A404(M)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sykesAm_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykesAm-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> There have been occasions where this designation has been used to indicate motorway bypasses of an existing road, but the original retains the A road designation, for example <a href="/wiki/A3_road#A3(M)" title="A3 road">A3(M)</a>, <a href="/wiki/A329(M)_motorway" title="A329(M) motorway">A329(M)</a>, <a href="/wiki/A38(M)" class="mw-redirect" title="A38(M)">A38(M)</a>, <a href="/wiki/A48(M)_motorway" title="A48(M) motorway">A48(M)</a> and <a href="/wiki/A627(M)_motorway" title="A627(M) motorway">A627(M)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sykesAm_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykesAm-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_classifications">Other classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Britain_road_numbering_scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>B roads</b> are numbered <a href="/wiki/Collector_road" title="Collector road">distributor roads</a>, which have lower traffic densities than the main trunk roads, or A roads.  This classification has nothing to do with the width or quality of the physical road, and B roads can range from dual carriageways to single track roads with passing places. B roads follow the same numbering scheme as A roads, but almost always have 3- and 4-digit designations.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Many 3-digit B roads outside the <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> area are former A roads which have been downgraded owing to new road construction; others may link smaller settlements to A
