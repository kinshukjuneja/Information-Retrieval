For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Shipwright_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Shipwright (disambiguation)">Shipwright (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Boatbuilding" class="mw-redirect" title="Boatbuilding">boatbuilding</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg/300px-Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg" width="300" height="244" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg/450px-Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg/600px-Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1625" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pizarroshipbuilding.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An expedition\'s shipwrights building a <a href="/wiki/Brigantine" title="Brigantine">brigantine</a>, 1541</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Shipbuilding</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Watercraft" title="Watercraft">floating vessels</a>.  It normally takes place in a specialized facility known as a <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipyard</a>.  <b>Shipbuilders</b>, also called <b>shipwrights</b>, follow a specialized occupation that traces its roots to before <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>.\n</p><p>Shipbuilding and ship repairs, both commercial and military, are referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Naval_engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval engineering">naval engineering</a>".  The construction of <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boats</a> is a similar activity called <a href="/wiki/Boat_building" title="Boat building">boat building</a>.\n</p><p>The dismantling of ships is called <a href="/wiki/Ship_breaking" title="Ship breaking">ship breaking</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-history">Pre-history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Archaeological evidence indicates that humans arrived on <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> at least 120,000&#160;years ago, probably by sea from the <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> mainland during an <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> period when the sea was lower and distances between islands shorter (See <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea#History" title="Papua New Guinea">History of Papua New Guinea</a>).  The ancestors of <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigine" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Aborigine">Australian Aborigines</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guineans</a> also went across the <a href="/wiki/Lombok_Strait" title="Lombok Strait">Lombok Strait</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sahul_continent" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahul continent">Sahul</a> by boat over 50,000&#160;years ago.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="4th_millennium_BC">4th millennium BC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 4th millennium BC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Evidence from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a> shows that the early <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> knew how to assemble planks of wood into a <a href="/wiki/Hull_(ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hull (ship)">ship hull</a> as early as 3100&#160;BC. Egyptian pottery as old as 4000 BC shows designs of early boats or other means for navigation. The <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_Institute_of_America" title="Archaeological Institute of America">Archaeological Institute of America</a> reports<sup id="cite_ref-AIA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> that some of the oldest ships yet unearthed are known as the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_Boats_(First_Dynasty)_%E2%80%93_Abydos" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Egyptian Boats (First Dynasty) \xe2\x80\x93 Abydos">Abydos boats</a>. These are a group of 14 ships discovered in <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a> that were constructed of wooden planks which were "sewn" together. Discovered by Egyptologist <a href="/wiki/David_O%27Connor_(Egyptologist)" title="David O&#39;Connor (Egyptologist)">David O\'Connor</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AIA2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> woven <a href="/wiki/Strap" title="Strap">straps</a> were found to have been used to lash the planks together,<sup id="cite_ref-AIA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cyperus_papyrus" title="Cyperus papyrus">reeds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grass</a> stuffed between the planks helped to seal the seams.<sup id="cite_ref-AIA_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Because the ships are all buried together and near a mortuary belonging to <a href="/wiki/Khasekhemwy" title="Khasekhemwy">Pharaoh Khasekhemwy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AIA2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> originally they were all thought to have belonged to him, but one of the 14 ships dates to 3000&#160;BC,<sup id="cite_ref-AIA2_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and the associated pottery jars buried with the vessels also suggest earlier dating.<sup id="cite_ref-AIA2_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The ship dating to 3000&#160;BC was about 25&#160;m, 75&#160;feet long<sup id="cite_ref-AIA2_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and is now thought to perhaps have belonged to an earlier pharaoh.<sup id="cite_ref-AIA2_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> According to professor O\'Connor, the 5,000-year-old ship may have even belonged to <a href="/wiki/Hor-Aha" title="Hor-Aha">Pharaoh Aha</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AIA2_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3rd_millennium_BC">3rd millennium BC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 3rd millennium BC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Early Egyptians also knew how to assemble planks of wood with <a href="/wiki/Treenail" title="Treenail">treenails</a> to fasten them together, using <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">pitch</a> for <a href="/wiki/Caulking" class="mw-redirect" title="Caulking">caulking</a> the seams. The "<a href="/wiki/Khufu_ship" title="Khufu ship">Khufu ship</a>", a 43.6-meter vessel sealed into a pit in the <a href="/wiki/Giza_pyramid_complex" title="Giza pyramid complex">Giza pyramid complex</a> at the foot of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Pyramid_of_Giza" title="Great Pyramid of Giza">Great Pyramid of Giza</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_dynasty_of_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth dynasty of Egypt">Fourth Dynasty</a> around 2500 BC, is a full-size surviving example which may have fulfilled the symbolic function of a <a href="/wiki/Solar_barque" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar barque">solar barque</a>.  Early Egyptians also knew how to fasten the planks of this ship together with <a href="/wiki/Mortise_and_tenon" title="Mortise and tenon">mortise and tenon</a> joints.<sup id="cite_ref-AIA_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The oldest known tidal dock in the world was built around 2500 BC during the <a href="/wiki/Harappan_civilisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Harappan civilisation">Harappan civilisation</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lothal" title="Lothal">Lothal</a> near the present day Mangrol harbour on the <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> coast in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. Other ports were probably at <a href="/wiki/Balakot" title="Balakot">Balakot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dwarka" title="Dwarka">Dwarka</a>. However, it is probable that many small-scale ports, and not massive ports, were used for the Harappan maritime trade.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Ships from the harbour at these ancient port cities established trade with <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Shipbuilding and boatmaking may have been prosperous industries in ancient India.<sup id="cite_ref-Tripathi_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tripathi-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Native labourers may have manufactured the flotilla of boats used by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> to navigate across the <a href="/wiki/Hydaspes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydaspes">Hydaspes</a> and even the <a href="/wiki/Indus" class="mw-redirect" title="Indus">Indus</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Nearchos" class="mw-redirect" title="Nearchos">Nearchos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tripathi_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tripathi-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Indians also exported <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> for shipbuilding to ancient <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hourani&amp;Carswel_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hourani&amp;Carswel-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Other references to Indian timber used for shipbuilding is noted in the works of <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Jubayr" title="Ibn Jubayr">Ibn Jubayr</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hourani&amp;Carswel_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hourani&amp;Carswel-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2nd_millennium_BC">2nd millennium BC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2nd millennium BC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The ships of Ancient Egypt\'s <a href="/wiki/Eighteenth_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Eighteenth Dynasty">Eighteenth Dynasty</a> were typically about 25&#160;meters (80&#160;ft) in length, and had a single <a href="/wiki/Mast_(sailing)" title="Mast (sailing)">mast</a>, sometimes consisting of two poles lashed together at the top making an "A" shape.  They mounted a single square <a href="/wiki/Sail" title="Sail">sail</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Yard_(sailing)" title="Yard (sailing)">yard</a>, with an additional <a href="/wiki/Spar_(sailing)" title="Spar (sailing)">spar</a> along the bottom of the sail. These ships could also be <a href="/wiki/Oar" title="Oar">oar</a> propelled.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The ocean and sea going ships of Ancient Egypt were constructed with cedar wood, most likely hailing from Lebanon.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The ships of <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicia</a> seem to have been of a similar design.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1st_millennium_BC">1st millennium BC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1st millennium BC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Naval_history_of_China" title="Naval history of China">naval history of China</a> stems back to the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> (722 BC\xe2\x80\x93481 BC) of the ancient <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Zhou Dynasty">Zhou Dynasty</a>. The Chinese built large rectangular barges known as "castle ships", which were essentially floating fortresses complete with multiple decks with guarded <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">ramparts</a>. There is considerable knowledge regarding shipbuilding and seafaring in the ancient Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malays_(ethnic_group)" title="Malays (ethnic group)">Malay people</a> independently invented <a href="/wiki/Junk_rig" title="Junk rig">junk sails</a>, made from woven mats reinforced with bamboo, at least several hundred years BC.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_1st_millennium_AD">Early 1st millennium AD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early 1st millennium AD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The ancient <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> also built <a href="/wiki/Ramming" title="Ramming">ramming</a> vessels as in the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Trireme" title="Trireme">trireme</a>, although oar-steered ships in China lost favor very early on since it was in the 1st century China that the <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>-mounted <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudder</a> was first developed. This was dually met with the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> <a href="/wiki/Junk_(ship)" title="Junk (ship)">junk ship</a> design in the same century. By the time of this dynasty, the Chinese adopted Malay junk sail for their rig.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg/220px-Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg" width="220" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg/330px-Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg/440px-Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="943" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Djong_Pati_Unus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jong as depicted in 16th century European book.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Malay and <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> people, started building seafaring <a href="/wiki/Djong_(ship)" title="Djong (ship)">jong</a> about 1st century AD.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> These ships used 2 types of sail of their invention, the junk sail and <a href="/wiki/Tanja_sail" title="Tanja sail">tanja sail</a>. Large ships are about 50-60 metres (164-197 ft) long, had 4-7 metres tall <a href="/wiki/Freeboard_(nautical)" title="Freeboard (nautical)">freeboard</a>, each carrying provisions enough for a year, and could carry 200-1000 people. This type of ship favored by Chinese travelers, because they did not built seaworthy ships until around 8-9th century AD.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Archeological investigations done at Portus near Rome have revealed inscriptions indicating the existence of  a \'guild of shipbuilders\' during the time of Hadrian.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span title="Song Yingxing">Song Yingxing</a>, published in 1637</div></div></div>\n<p>Viking <a href="/wiki/Longship" title="Longship">longships</a> were an advancement from the traditional <a id="cite_ref-ZumerchikDanver2010_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZumerchikDanver2010-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Sometime around the 12th century, northern European ships began to be built with a straight <a href="/wiki/Sternpost" title="Sternpost">sternpost</a>, enabling the mounting of a rudder, which was much more durable than a <a href="/wiki/Steering_oar" title="Steering oar">steering oar</a> held over the side. Development in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> favored "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Round_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Round ship (page does not exist)">round ships</a>", with a broad beam and heavily curved at both ends. Another important ship type was the galley which was constructed with both sails and oars.\n</p><p>An insight into ship building in the North Sea/Baltic areas of the early medieval period was found at Sutton Hoo, England, where a ship was buried with  a chieftain. The ship was 26 metres (85&#160;ft) long and, 4.3 metres (14&#160;ft)<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> wide. Upward from the keel, the hull was made by overlapping nine planks on either side with rivets fastening the oaken planks together. It could hold upwards of thirty men.\n</p><p>The first extant treatise on shipbuilding was written c. 1436 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_of_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michael of Rhodes (page does not exist)">Michael of Rhodes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> a man who began his career as an oarsman on a Venetian galley in 1401 and worked his way up into officer positions. He wrote and illustrated a book that contains a treatise on ship building, a treatise on mathematics, much material on astrology, and other materials. His treatise on shipbuilding treats three kinds of galleys and two kinds of round ships.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:422px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Galley_of_Flanders-model.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Galley_of_Flanders-model.gif/420px-Galley_of_Flanders-model.gif" width="420" height="315" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Galley_of_Flanders-model.gif/630px-Galley_of_Flanders-model.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0f/Galley_of_Flanders-model.gif 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Galley_of_Flanders-model.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 3D model of the basic hull structure of a Venetian "<a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">galley</a> of Flanders", a large Mediterranean trading vessel of the 15th century. The shipbuilding industry in <a href="/wiki/Imperial_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial China">Imperial China</a> reached its height during the <a href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a>, and early <a href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>, building commercial vessels that by the end of this period were to reach a size and sophistication far exceeding that of contemporary Europe<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. The mainstay of China\'s merchant and naval fleets was the <a href="/wiki/Junk_(ship)" title="Junk (ship)">junk</a>, which had existed for centuries, but it was at this time that the large ships based on this design were built. During the Sung period (960\xe2\x80\x931279 AD), the establishment of China\'s first official standing navy in 1132 AD and the enormous increase in maritime trade abroad (from <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian</a> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Fatimid" class="mw-redirect" title="Fatimid">Fatimid</a> <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>) allowed the shipbuilding industry in provinces like <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> to thrive as never before. The  largest <a href="/wiki/Seaport" class="mw-redirect" title="Seaport">seaports</a> in the world were in China and included <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quanzhou" title="Quanzhou">Quanzhou</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xiamen" title="Xiamen">Xiamen</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In the Islamic world, shipbuilding thrived at <a href="/wiki/Basra" title="Basra">Basra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dhow" title="Dhow">dhow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felucca" title="Felucca">felucca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baghlah" title="Baghlah">baghlah</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sambuk" title="Sambuk">sambuk</a>, became symbols of successful maritime trade around the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>; from the ports of <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and the ports of <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Al-Hind" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Hind">Hind</a> (India) during the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> period.\n</p><p>At this time islands spread over vast distances across the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> were being colonised by the Melenesians and Polynesians, who built giant canoes and progressed to great catamarans.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern">Early modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>With the development of the <a href="/wiki/Carrack" title="Carrack">carrack</a>, the west moved into a new era of ship construction by building the first regular oceangoing vessels. In a relatively short time, these ships grew to an unprecedented size, complexity and cost.\n</p><p>Shipyards became large industrial complexes and the ships built were financed by consortia of investors.\nThese considerations led to the documentation of design and construction practices in what had previously been a secretive trade run by master shipwrights, and ultimately led to the field of <a href="/wiki/Naval_architecture" title="Naval architecture">naval architecture</a>, where professional designers and draughtsmen played an increasingly important role.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Even so, construction techniques changed only very gradually. The ships of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> were still built more or less to the same basic plan as those of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> of two centuries earlier but there had been numerous subtle improvements in ship design and construction throughout this period. For instance, the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Tumblehome" title="Tumblehome">tumblehome</a>; adjustments to the shapes of sails and hulls; the introduction of the wheel; the introduction of hardened copper fastenings below the waterline; the introduction of copper sheathing as a deterrent to shipworm and fouling; etc.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shipbuilding_England_1858.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shipbuilding_England_1858.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of some shipbuilding methods in England, 1858</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BT_Babbitts_Rotary_Engine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/BT_Babbitts_Rotary_Engine.jpg/220px-BT_Babbitts_Rotary_Engine.jpg" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BT_Babbitts_Rotary_Engine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Babbitt\'s <a href="/wiki/Rotary_engine" title="Rotary engine">rotary engine</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The industrial revolution made possible the use of new materials and designs that radically altered shipbuilding. <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Iron</a> was gradually adopted in ship construction, initially in discrete areas  in a wooden hull needing greater strength, (e.g. as deck knees,  hanging knees, knee riders and the other sharp joints, ones in which a curved, progressive joint could not be achieved).  Then, in the form of plates riveted together and made watertight, it was used to form the hull itself. Initially copying wooden construction traditions with a frame over which the hull was fastened,  <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Britain" title="SS Great Britain"><i>Great Britain</i></a> of 1843 was the first radical new design, being built entirely of wrought iron. Despite her success,  and the great savings in cost and space provided by the iron hull, compared to  a copper sheathed counterpart, there remained problems with fouling due to the adherence of weeds and barnacles. As a result, <a href="/wiki/Composite_construction" title="Composite construction">composite construction</a> remained the dominant approach where fast ships were required, with wooden timbers laid over an iron frame (<i><a href="/wiki/Cutty_Sark" title="Cutty Sark">Cutty Sark</a></i> is a famous example). Later <i>Great Britain</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s iron hull  was sheathed in wood to enable it to  carry a <a href="/wiki/Copper_sheathing" title="Copper sheathing">copper-based sheathing</a>.  Brunel\'s <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Eastern" title="SS Great Eastern"><i>Great Eastern</i></a> represented the next great development in shipbuilding. Built in association with <a href="/wiki/John_Scott_Russell" title="John Scott Russell">John Scott Russell</a>, it used longitudinal <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stringer" class="extiw" title="wikt:stringer">stringers</a> for strength, inner and outer hulls, and <a href="/wiki/Bulkhead_(partition)" title="Bulkhead (partition)">bulkheads</a> to form multiple watertight compartments. <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">Steel</a> also supplanted <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> when it became readily available in the latter half of the 19th century, providing great savings when compared with iron in cost and weight. Wood continued to be favored for the decks.\n</p><p>Sailing ship technology vastly improved during the early Industrial Revolution (between 1760 and 1825), as "the risk of being wrecked for Atlantic shipping fell by one third, and of foundering by two thirds, reflecting improvements in seaworthiness and navigation respectively."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The improvements in seaworthiness have been credited to "replacing the traditional stepped deck ship with stronger flushed decked ones derived from <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian</a> designs, and the increasing use of iron reinforcement."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The design originated from <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> rice ships,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> with Bengal being famous for its shipbuilding industry at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-ray_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ray-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> One study finds that there were considerable improvements in ship speed from 1750 to 1850: "we find that average sailing speeds of British ships in moderate to strong winds rose by nearly a third. Driving this steady progress seems to be continuous evolution of sails and rigging, and improved hulls that allowed a greater area of sail to be set safely in a given wind. By contrast, looking at every voyage between the Netherlands and East Indies undertaken by the Dutch East India Company from 1595 to 1795, we find that journey time fell only by 10 per cent, with no improvement in the heavy mortality, averaging six per cent per voyage, of those aboard."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the need for cargo ships was so great that construction time for <a href="/wiki/Liberty_ship" title="Liberty ship">Liberty ships</a> went from initially eight months or longer, down to weeks or even days. They employed production line and prefabrication techniques such as those used in shipyards today. The total number of dry-cargo ships built in the United States in a 15-year period just before the war was a grand total of two. During the war, thousands of Liberty ships and <a href="/wiki/Victory_ship" title="Victory ship">Victory ships</a> were built, many of them in shipyards that didn\'t exist before the war. And, they were built by a workforce consisting largely of women and other inexperienced workers who had never seen a ship before (or even the ocean).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a Second World War, shipbuilding (which encompasses the shipyards, the marine equipment manufacturers, and many related <a href="/wiki/Service_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Service industry">service</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_economy" title="Knowledge economy">knowledge</a> providers) grew as an important and strategic industry in a number of countries around the world. This importance stems from:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The large number of <a href="/wiki/Skilled_workers" class="mw-redirect" title="Skilled workers">skilled workers</a> required directly by the shipyard, along with supporting industries such as <a href="/wiki/Steel_mills" class="mw-redirect" title="Steel mills">steel mills</a>, railroads and <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> manufacturers; and</li>\n<li>A nation\'s need to manufacture and repair its own <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">navy</a> and vessels that support its <a href="/wiki/Primary_industries" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary industries">primary industries</a></li></ul>\n<p>Historically, the industry has suffered from the absence of <a href="/wiki/International_trade_law" title="International trade law">global rules</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and a tendency towards (<a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">state</a>-<a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">supported</a>) over-investment due to the fact that shipyards offer a wide range of technologies, employ a significant number of workers, and generate income as the shipbuilding market is <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">global</a>.\n</p><p>Shipbuilding is therefore an attractive industry for <a href="/wiki/Developing_nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing nations">developing nations</a>. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> used shipbuilding in the 1950s and 1960s to rebuild its industrial structure; <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> started to make shipbuilding a strategic industry in the 1970s, and China is now in the process of repeating these models with large state-supported investments in this industry. Conversely, Croatia is privatising its shipbuilding industry.\n</p><p>As a result, the world shipbuilding market suffers from over-capacities, depressed prices (although the industry experienced a price increase in the period 2003\xe2\x80\x932005 due to strong demand for new ships which was in excess of actual cost increases), low profit margins, trade distortions and widespread subsidisation. All efforts to address the problems in the OECD have so far failed, with the 1994 international shipbuilding agreement never entering into force and the 2003\xe2\x80\x932005 round of negotiations being paused in September 2005 after no agreement was possible. After numerous efforts to restart the negotiations these were formally terminated in December 2010.  The OECD\'s Council Working Party on Shipbuilding (WP6) will continue its efforts to identify and progressively reduce factors that distort the shipbuilding market.\n</p><p>Where <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">state</a> <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidies</a> have been removed and domestic <a href="/wiki/Industrial_policies" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial policies">industrial policies</a> do not provide support in high labor cost countries, shipbuilding has gone into decline. The British shipbuilding industry is a prime example of this with its industries suffering badly from the 1960s. In the early 1970s British yards still had the capacity to build all types and sizes of merchant ships but today they have been reduced to a small number specialising in defence contracts, luxury yachts and repair work. Decline has also occurred in other European countries, although to some extent this has reduced by protective measures and industrial support policies. In the U.S.A, the <a href="/wiki/Merchant_Marine_Act_of_1920" title="Merchant Marine Act of 1920">Jones Act</a> (which places restrictions on the ships that can be used for moving domestic cargoes) has meant that merchant shipbuilding has continued, albeit at a reduced rate, but such protection has failed to penalise shipbuilding inefficiencies. The consequence of this is that contract prices are far higher than those of any other country building oceangoing ships.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day_shipbuilding">Present day shipbuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Present day shipbuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">China</a> is the world\'s largest shipbuilder.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The country has been an emerging low-cost, high-volume shipbuilder that overtook South Korea during the <a href="/wiki/Late-2000s_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-2000s recession">2008\xe2\x80\x932010 global financial crisis</a> as they won new orders for medium and small-sized container ships.<sup id="cite_ref-news.khan.co.kr_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.khan.co.kr-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>South Korea\'s "big three" shipbuilders, <a href="/wiki/Hyundai_Heavy_Industries" title="Hyundai Heavy Industries">Hyundai Heavy Industries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Heavy_Industries" title="Samsung Heavy Industries">Samsung Heavy Industries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Daewoo_Shipbuilding_%26_Marine_Engineering" title="Daewoo Shipbuilding &amp; Marine Engineering">Daewoo Shipbuilding &amp; Marine Engineering</a>, dominate the global market for large container ships. As freight rates continue to decline into 2016, production delays, and overcapacity in the industry have led South Korean shipbuilders into financial distress. Consequently, significant market share has been ceded to their Chinese and Japanese rivals.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The market share of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> ship builders began to decline in the 1960s as they lost work to Japan in the same way Japan most recently lost their work to China and South Korea. Over the four years from 2007, the total number of employees in the European shipbuilding industry declined from 150,000 to 115,000.<sup id="cite_ref-ifr_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ifr-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  The output of the United States also underwent a similar change.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_31-0" class="reference"><a Group.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable" style="text-align=center; margin:1em auto 1em auto;">\n\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="text-align:center;" colspan="5"><b>Global Shipbuilding class="mw-headline" id="Modern_shipbuilding_manufacturing_techniques">Modern shipbuilding manufacturing techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modern shipbuilding manufacturing techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg/220px-HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg/330px-HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg/440px-HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1840" data-file-height="1232" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Dauntless_D33.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Construction of prefabricated module blocks of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dauntless_(D33)" title="HMS Dauntless (D33)">HMS&#160;<i>Dauntless</i></a> at <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Surface_Ships" class="mw-redirect" title="BAE Systems Surface Ships">BAE\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> Shipyard.</div></div></div>\n<p>Modern shipbuilding makes considerable use of <a href="/wiki/Prefabrication" title="Prefabrication">prefabricated sections</a>. Entire multi-deck segments of the hull or <a href="/wiki/Superstructure_(ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="Superstructure (ship)">superstructure</a> will be built elsewhere in the yard, transported to the building dock or slipway, then lifted into place. This is known as "block construction". The most modern shipyards pre-install equipment, pipes, electrical cables, and any other components within the blocks, to minimize the effort needed to assemble or install components deep within the hull once it is welded together.\n</p><p>Ship design work, also called <a href="/wiki/Naval_architecture" title="Naval architecture">naval architecture</a>, may be conducted using a <a href="/wiki/Ship_model_basin" title="Ship model basin">ship model basin</a>. Previously, loftsmen at the <a href="/wiki/Mould_loft" class="mw-redirect" title="Mould loft">mould lofts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipyards</a> were responsible for taking the dimensions, and details from drawings and plans and translating this information into templates, battens, ordinates, cutting sketches, profiles, margins and other data.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> However, since the early 1970s <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> (CAD) became normal for the shipbuilding design and lofting process.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Modern ships, since roughly 1940, have been produced almost exclusively of <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welded</a> steel. Early welded steel ships used steels with inadequate <a href="/wiki/Fracture_toughness" title="Fracture toughness">fracture toughness</a>, which resulted in some ships suffering catastrophic <a href="/wiki/Brittle_fracture" class="mw-redirect" title="Brittle fracture">brittle fracture</a> structural cracks (see <a href="/wiki/Problems_of_the_Liberty_ship" class="mw-redirect" title="Problems of the Liberty ship">problems of the Liberty ship</a>). Since roughly 1950, specialized steels such as <a href="/wiki/ABS_Steels" title="ABS Steels">ABS Steels</a> with good properties for ship construction have been used. Although it is commonly accepted that modern steel has eliminated brittle fracture in ships, some controversy still exists.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Brittle fracture of modern vessels continues to occur from time to time because grade A and grade B steel of unknown toughness or fracture appearance transition temperature (FATT) in ships\' side shells can be less than adequate for all ambient conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ship_repair_industry">Ship repair industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipbuilding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ship repair industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG/220px-Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG/330px-Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG/440px-Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haj%C3%B3gy%C3%A1r1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>River shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Kom%C3%A1rno" title="Kom\xc3\xa1rno">Kom\xc3\xa1rno</a> (<a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>).</div></div></div>\n<p>All ships need repair work at some point in their working lives. A part of these jobs must be carried out under the supervision of the classification society.\n</p><p>A lot of maintenance is carried out while at sea or in port by ship\'s crew.\nHowever a large number of repair and maintenance works can only be carried out while the ship is out of commercial operation, in a ship repair yard.\n</p><p>Prior to undergoing repairs, a tanker must dock at a deballasting station for completing the tank cleaning operations and pumping ashore its slops (dirty cleaning water and hydrocarbon residues).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
