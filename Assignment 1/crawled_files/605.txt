it does not apply only to highways (or in Scotland a relevant <i>road</i>) but includes other places available for public use, which are not highways.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> the equivalent requirement applies after four years.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The requirement does not apply to vehicles used <i>only</i> on various small islands with no convenient connection "to a road in any part of Great Britain";<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> no similar exemption is listed at the beginning of 2014 for Northern Ireland, which has a single inhabited island, <a href="/wiki/Rathlin_Island" title="Rathlin Island">Rathlin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The name derives from the Ministry of Transport,<sup id="cite_ref-name_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> a defunct <a href="/wiki/Ministry_(government_department)" title="Ministry (government department)">government department</a>, which was one of several ancestors of the current <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Department for Transport</a>, but is still officially used. The MOT test certificates are currently issued in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Driver_and_Vehicle_Standards_Agency" title="Driver and Vehicle Standards Agency">Driver and Vehicle Standards Agency</a> (DVSA) (formed as a result of the merger between the <a href="/wiki/Driving_Standards_Agency" title="Driving Standards Agency">Driving Standards Agency</a> (DSA) and the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_and_Operator_Services_Agency" title="Vehicle and Operator Services Agency">Vehicle and Operator Services Agency</a> (VOSA)), an <a href="/wiki/Executive_agency" title="Executive agency">executive agency</a> of the Department for Transport, and before 1 April 2014 by VOSA. Certificates in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> are issued by the <a href="/wiki/Driver_and_Vehicle_Agency" title="Driver and Vehicle Agency">Driver and Vehicle Agency</a> (DVA). The test and the pass certificate are often referred to simply as the "MOT".\n</p><p>About 20,100<sup id="cite_ref-mot_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> local car repair <a href="/wiki/Automobile_repair_shop" title="Automobile repair shop">garages</a> throughout Great Britain, employing about 53,000 testers,<sup id="cite_ref-mot_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> are authorised to perform testing and to issue certificates. In principle, any individual in Great Britain can apply to run a MOT station, although in order to gain an authorisation from DVSA, both the individual wanting to run the station, as well as the premises, need to meet minimal criteria set out on the government\'s website within the so-called VT01 form.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Northern Ireland tests are performed exclusively at the DVA\'s own test centres, although currently there is an open project investigating bringing Northern Ireland into line with the rest of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The MOT test was first introduced in 1960 under the direction of the Minister of Transport, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Marples" title="Ernest Marples">Ernest Marples</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> under powers in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Road_Traffic_Act_1956&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Road Traffic Act 1956 (page does not exist)">Road Traffic Act 1956</a>. The test was originally a basic test including brakes, lights and steering check which was to be carried out after the vehicle was ten years old and every year thereafter. This became known as the "ten year test", or alternatively the "Ministry of Transport Test". A fee is applicable to the test and the amount involved for a car when testing was introduced in 1960 was fourteen shillings (70 pence) plus one shilling (5 pence) for the certificate. The high failure rate resulted in the age that vehicles became due for testing being reduced to seven years on 31 December 1961. In 1962, the first commercial vehicle exam was created and a valid certificate was required in order to receive a tax disc, and in April 1967 the testable age for an MOT was reduced to three years. On 1 January 1983 the testable age for ambulances, taxis and vehicles with more than eight passenger seats, excluding the driver\'s, was reduced to one year.\n</p><p>The list of items tested has been continually expanded over the years, including:\n</p>\n<ul><li>1968 \xe2\x80\x93 a tyre check</li>\n<li>1977 \xe2\x80\x93 checks of windscreen wipers and washers, direction indicators, brakelights, horns, exhaust system and condition of the body structure and chassis</li>\n<li>1991 \xe2\x80\x93 checks of the <a href="/wiki/Emission_standard" title="Emission standard">emissions test</a> for petrol engine vehicles, together with checks on the anti-lock braking system, rear wheel bearings, rear wheel steering (where appropriate) and rear seat belts</li>\n<li>1992 \xe2\x80\x93 a stricter tyre tread depth requirement for most vehicles</li>\n<li>1994 \xe2\x80\x93 a check of emissions for diesel engine vehicles</li>\n<li>2005 \xe2\x80\x93 introduction of a computerised administration system for issuing non-secure test certificates, and the creation of the \'Automated Test Bay\' which differed from traditional testing by installing equipment in the bay to obviate the need for a tester\'s assistant during the test</li>\n<li>2012 \xe2\x80\x93 checks of secondary restraint systems, battery and wiring, electronic stability control (ESC), speedometers and steering locks.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_classification">Test classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Test classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The test classes are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Class I&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 <a href="/wiki/Motor_bicycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor bicycle">Motor bicycles</a> (with or without side cars) up to 200cc</li>\n<li>Class II&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 All motor bicycles (including Class I) (with or without side cars).</li>\n<li>Class III&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 3-wheeled vehicles not more than 450&#160;kg unladen weight (excluding motor bicycles with side cars).</li>\n<li>Class IV&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">Cars</a>, including 3-wheeled vehicles more than 450&#160;kg unladen weight, taxis, <a href="/wiki/Minibus" title="Minibus">minibuses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ambulance" title="Ambulance">ambulances</a> up to 12 passenger seats, Goods Vehicles not exceeding 3,000&#160;kg Design Gross Weight (DGW), <a href="/wiki/Motorhome" title="Motorhome">motor caravans</a> and Dual Purpose Vehicles.</li>\n<li>Class V&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Private passenger vehicles, ambulances, motor caravans and dual purpose vehicles with 13 or more passenger seats</li>\n<li>Class VII&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Goods vehicles over 3,000&#160;kg up to and including 3,500&#160;kg DGW. If a vehicle is presented with a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s plate and a \'Ministry plate\' the weights to be used are those on the \'Ministry plate\'.</li>\n<li>PSV test (Class VI)&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Public service vehicles used for hire or reward with more than eight passenger seats (test conducted by DVSA/DVA staff their own stations, or at DVSA authorised testing facilities (ATF) or designated premises (DP)).</li>\n<li>HGV test&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Goods vehicles over 3,500&#160;kg GVW and trailers over 1,020&#160;kg unladen weight or 3,500&#160;kg GVW if fitted with overrun brakes (test conducted by DVSA/DVA staff their own stations, or at a DVSA authorised testing facility (ATF) or designated premises (DP)).</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fees">Fees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>All test stations are required to display a "VT9A Fees and Appeals" poster on their premises which must be available to the public. The "MOT" Test will provide an emissions report if applicable.\n</p><p>It is illegal to drive a non-exempt vehicle that requires a test on public roads without a current MOT,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> except when driving to or from (subject to insurance terms and conditions) a booked MOT test or to have remedial work done to rectify failures in a previous test.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Possession of an up-to-date VT20 test certificate is a pre-requisite for obtaining <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_excise_duty" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicle excise duty">vehicle excise duty</a>, and advertisements for used cars frequently say how many months are left to run on the current MOT (i.e., VT20 certificate; although the VT20 points out that it does not, in any way, guarantee road-worthiness at the time of sale). A vehicle could suffer major damage after an MOT has been carried out, but the certificate would still be valid, and obtaining a new one is not required by law (some <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> companies may require a new test, but this is their practice, not law). However, driving a vehicle which is in a dangerous condition on a public road is always illegal, irrespective of its test status.\n</p><p>The official UK MOT inspection manuals are available online.<sup id="cite_ref-manuals_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuals-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_the_test">Overview of the test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Overview of the test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG/170px-Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG" width="170" height="257" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG/255px-Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG/340px-Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1632" data-file-height="2464" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Land_Rover_Series_IIA_MOT_test.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">Land Rover</a> has its chassis inspected as part of its MOT. The vehicle structure is among many of the points covered in an MOT test.</div></div></div>\n<p>In Great Britain MOT testing centres are regulated and licensed by the Department and Transport and DVSA for the purpose, and the individual testers carrying out the inspections also have to be trained and certified. The decision to pass or fail each presented vehicle comes down to the discretion of the tester following the guidelines issued by the DVSA.\n</p><p>The MOT test covers the following aspects:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Lighting and signalling equipment</li>\n<li>Steering (including suspension)</li>\n<li>Brakes</li>\n<li>Tyres and wheels</li>\n<li>Seat belts</li>\n<li>Body, structure and general items. Includes body and components such as spoilers, bumpers and mirror housings.</li>\n<li>Exhaust, fuel and emissions (unless electric)</li>\n<li>Driver\'s view of the road</li></ul>\n<p>The inspection manual can be found here:<sup id="cite_ref-manuals_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuals-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The full details must by law be provided on request by all garages licensed to perform MOT tests, and are currently published in DVSA\'s leaflet <i>The MOT Test and You</i>. An MOT pass certificate indicates that at the time of the test the vehicle met or exceeded the minimum safety standards determined by the DVSA guidelines.\n</p><p>An MOT test certificate confirms that at the time of test, the vehicle has met the minimum acceptable environmental and road safety standards. It does not mean that the vehicle is roadworthy for the life of the certificate. The test does not cover the condition of the engine (other than the emissions), clutch or gearbox. Maintenance that is necessary for the reliable and efficient operation of the vehicle \xe2\x80\x93 but not its safety \xe2\x80\x93 forms part of a <a href="/wiki/Service_(motor_vehicle)" title="Service (motor vehicle)">service</a> inspection that is recommended by manufacturers, but is not a legal requirement for operating the vehicle on the public highway.\n</p><p>Items such as the windscreen, wipers and exhaust systems are tested for condition and operation. Windscreen wipers will fail the test if they do not adequately clear the windscreen when used in conjunction with the washers. The exhaust system, in addition to checks on its condition and security, is tested to ascertain whether it is obviously louder than another vehicle of the same make and model with a standard exhaust system fitted. Dismantling of any part of the vehicle during the MOT test is strictly against test regulations, making the assessment of corrosion or worn components in certain areas on certain car models very difficult to determine accurately. As the MOT is only an inspection for road-worthiness at the time of test, the inspection of most accessories is not included. One exception is tow bars: their condition and their attachment to the vehicle is now included in the MOT.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting_exemption">Lighting exemption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lighting exemption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A vehicle that has no front- and rear-position lights fitted or has had those lights permanently removed, painted or masked-over is exempt from the lighting sections of the MOT test. An advisory note will be provided on the VT32 as a reminder that the vehicle should only be used during daylight hours and not used at times of seriously reduced visibility.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This is sometimes incorrectly referred to as a "Daytime MOT", but there is no official distinction. It is the Road Vehicles Lighting Regulations that prevent an unlit vehicle being used on the road after dark, not the MOT.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Historic_.28Classic.29_Vehicles_Exemption"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_(Classic)_Vehicles_Exemption">Historic (Classic) Vehicles Exemption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historic (Classic) Vehicles Exemption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A vehicle is exempt from the MOT if it was first registered more than 40 years ago and no \xe2\x80\x98substantial changes\xe2\x80\x99 have been made to the vehicle in the last 30 years<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>. This includes vehicles previously exempted on the basis of being first registered before 1960. A vehicle alteration is a \xe2\x80\x98substantial change\xe2\x80\x99 if the technical characteristics of the main components have changed in the previous 30 years, unless these fall into the acceptable alterations category. Different rules apply to large vehicles.\n</p><p>An exempt vehicle must be roadworthy.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-tests">Re-tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Re-tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When a vehicle fails the MOT test it can be re-tested within the end of 10 working days to gain a pass. There may be a charge payable. If the vehicle remains at the test station for repair after failure then it can have a free partial re-test within 10 working days after the original test has been carried out. If it is removed from the premises for repair and then returned before the end of 10 working days it can have a retest at half the original fee paid. If the failed vehicle is taken away but then brought back to the same test station and retested before the end of the next working day on one or more of the following items only then no fee is charged for a retest:\n</p><p>Bonnet, horn, sharp edges, boot lid, lamps, steering wheel, brake pedal anti-slip, loading door, tailboard, direction indicators, mirrors, tailgate, doors rear reflectors, <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_identification_number" title="Vehicle identification number">VIN</a>, drop sides, registration plates, windscreen and glass, fuel filler cap, seat belts, wipers and washers, hazard warning, seats, wheels and tyres.\n</p><p>After the 10-day period a full MOT test will have to be carried out. The full MOT test fee is charged again.\n</p><p>Test stations and the DVSA\'s website provide full and up-to-date information regarding MOT re-tests.\n</p><p>The fee for testing and retesting is at the discretion of the proprietor of the test facility, subject to legal maximum prices. The vehicle owner is only subject to one retest per MOT test. If the vehicle fails the MOT retest it is then subject to a full MOT test at the full MOT test fee.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appeals_against_MOT_inspections">Appeals against MOT inspections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Appeals against MOT inspections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Motorists who recently had a vehicle MOT tested and disagree with the outcome of the inspection are entitled to an appeal against the decision. The appeal must be lodged with DVSA/DVA within 28 days of the original test date if the items in question are mechanical in nature; three months are allowed for corrosion issues, except for corrosion of brake discs, brake lines or the exhaust system. Mileage, or the lack thereof, incurred after the inspection has no relevance to the appeal even if the vehicle has not been used for several months after the test.\n</p><p>If the items in question are repaired, replaced or removed from the vehicle, the right of the owner to appeal becomes null and void. Failure because of items easily replaceable, such as tyres or windscreen wiper blades, may not be appealed against, as it cannot be adequately determined if they were the ones fitted at the time of inspection.\n</p><p>To appeal against an MOT pass is free of charge, but appeals against a failure incur a fee whose value would amount to the normal maximum price of an MOT for that vehicle. This fee is then refunded if the appellate inspection finds in favour of the motorist lodging the appeal. If the appellate inspection finds the vehicle was incorrectly diagnosed, DVSA/DVA takes appropriate action against the station involved. This can range from penalty points being issued for minor infringements, to the station\'s MOT licence being rescinded for more major violations.\n</p><p>DVSA/DVA has only the power to discipline the station involved and cannot pursue compensation of any kind for the complainant; that is the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Trading_Standards" title="Trading Standards">Trading Standards</a>. An MOT station cannot be held responsible for defects that occur to the vehicle several months after the test was conducted. The appeal process is outlined on the reverse of the VT20 pass certificate and the VT30 failure notice.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mileage">Mileage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mileage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It is a common misconception that the MOT inspection provides an irrefutable record of a vehicle\'s mileage. However, although the mileage is recorded during the test, no part of the inspection requires the test station to verify that this is indeed the <i>actual</i> mileage. It is merely recorded, and any <a href="/wiki/Odometer_fraud" title="Odometer fraud">tampering of an odometer</a> would not be discovered as part of the MOT inspection.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_2012">Changes in 2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Changes in 2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2012 the MOT test was radically overhauled with many items not previously included in the test now becoming standard.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It also saw the introduction in Great Britain of \'receipt style\' plain paper certificates that serve as a notification that a \'pass\' entry has been recorded on the DVSA database. The MOT test number contained on the certificate gives access to the vehicle\'s current test status as well as its test history from 2005 onwards, via the DVSA web site. MOT certificates in Northern Ireland continue to be issued on paper, accompanied by a paper \'certificate disc\' which must be displayed on the vehicle. Display of these discs has been mandatory on Northern Ireland tested cars since 2008. However, this was abolished in April 2015.\n</p><p>From 18 November, the MOT<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> certificate shows the vehicle\'s recent mileage history. This has been introduced as part of a government initiative to reduce vehicle crime. Where available, the mileage history will comprise the readings associated with the three most recent VT20s (test passes) along with the date of those readings.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_2018">Changes in 2018</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MOT_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Changes in 2018">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From 20 May 2018, the way that the MOT test works in England, Scotland and Wales changes to comply with EU Directive 2014/45. This involves some changes to the way the test is carried out and reported, with the basic essence of the test remaining the same. After the change date, vehicles first registered 40 or more years prior will not need to undergo an MOT test.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Emissions tests on <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> cars have not been carried out during MOTs in Northern Ireland for 12 years, despite being legally required.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
