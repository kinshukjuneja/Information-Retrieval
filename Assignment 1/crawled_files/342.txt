historiography">Medieval</a></small></dd></dl>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>British Iron Age</b> is a conventional name used in the <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a> of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, referring to the <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protohistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Protohistoric">protohistoric</a> phases of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> of the main island and the smaller islands, typically excluding <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Ireland" title="Prehistoric Ireland">prehistoric Ireland</a>, which had an independent Iron Age culture of its own.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The parallel phase of Irish archaeology is termed the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Age Ireland">Irish Iron Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\nThe Iron Age is not an <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_horizon" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeological horizon">archaeological horizon</a> of common artefacts, but is rather a locally diverse cultural phase.\n</p><p>The British Iron Age lasted in theory from the first significant use of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> for tools and weapons in Britain to the <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romanisation</a> of the southern half of the island. The Romanised culture is termed <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> and is considered to supplant the British Iron Age. The Irish Iron Age was ended by the rise of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Christianity</a>.\n</p><p>The tribes living in Britain during this time are often popularly considered to be part of a broadly <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> culture, but in recent years this has been disputed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> At a minimum, "Celtic" is a linguistic term without an implication of a lasting cultural unity connecting Gaul with the British Isles throughout the Iron Age. The <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic languages</a> spoken in Britain at this time, as well as others including the <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Goidelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaulish_language" title="Gaulish language">Gaulish</a> languages of neighbouring Ireland and Gaul respectively, certainly belong to the group known as <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a>. However it cannot be assumed that particular cultural features found in one Celtic-speaking culture can be extrapolated to the others.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Periodisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At present over 100 large-scale excavations of Iron Age sites have taken place,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> dating from the 8th century BC to the 1st century AD, and extends into the very early <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> under the theory that Romanisation required some time to take effect. In parts of Britain that were not <a href="/wiki/Romanisation_(cultural)" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanisation (cultural)">Romanised</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the period is extended a little longer, say to the 5th century. The geographer closest to AD 100 is perhaps <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> are a bit older (and therefore a bit more contemporary), but Ptolemy gives the most detail (and the least theory).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_evidence">Archaeological evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeological evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. G. Allen">Major George Allen</a> (1891\xe2\x80\x931940).</div></div></div>\n<p>Attempts to understand the human behaviour of the period have traditionally focused on the geographic position of the islands and their <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a>, along with the channels of influence coming from <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a>.\n</p><p>During the later <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> there are indications of new ideas influencing <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">settlement</a>. Extensive <a href="/wiki/Field_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Field systems">field systems</a>, now called <a href="/wiki/Celtic_fields" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic fields">Celtic fields</a>, were being set out and settlements were becoming more permanent and focused on better exploitation of the land. The central organisation to undertake this had been present since the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period but it was now targeted at <a href="/wiki/Economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social" title="Social">social</a> goals, such as taming the landscape rather than the building of large ceremonial structures like <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a>. Long ditches, some many miles in length, were dug with <a href="/wiki/Enclosure_(archaeology)" title="Enclosure (archaeology)">enclosures</a> placed at their ends. These are thought to indicate territorial <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a> and a desire to increase control over wide areas.\n</p><p>By the 8th century BC, there is increasing evidence of Great Britain becoming closely tied to continental Europe, especially in Britain\'s South and East. New weapon types appeared with clear parallels to those on the continent such as the <a href="/wiki/Carp%27s_tongue_sword" class="mw-redirect" title="Carp&#39;s tongue sword">Carp\'s tongue sword</a>, complex examples of which are found all over <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Europe" title="Atlantic Europe">Atlantic Europe</a>. <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> traders probably began visiting Great Britain in search of <a href="/wiki/Minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerals">minerals</a> around this time, bringing with them goods from the Mediterranean. At the same time, Northern European <a href="/wiki/Cultural_artefact" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural artefact">artefact</a> types reached Eastern Great Britain in large quantities from across the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>.  \n</p><p>Defensive structures dating from this time are often impressive, for example the <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">brochs</a> of Northern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> that dotted the rest of the islands. Some of the most well-known hill forts include <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Castle,_Dorset" title="Maiden Castle, Dorset">Maiden Castle, Dorset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Castle,_Somerset" title="Cadbury Castle, Somerset">Cadbury Castle, Somerset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danebury" title="Danebury">Danebury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>. Hill forts first appeared in <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a> in the Late Bronze Age, but only become common in the period between 550 and 400 BC. The earliest were of a simple <a href="/wiki/Univallate" class="mw-redirect" title="Univallate">univallate</a> form, and often connected with earlier enclosures attached to the long ditch systems. Few hill forts have been substantially excavated in the modern era, Danebury being a notable exception, with 49% of its total surface area studied. However, it appears that these "forts" were also used for domestic purposes, with examples of food storage, industry and occupation being found within their earthworks. On the other hand, they may have been only occupied intermittently as it is difficult to reconcile permanently occupied hill forts with the lowland farmsteads and their <a href="/wiki/Roundhouse_(dwelling)" title="Roundhouse (dwelling)">roundhouses</a> found during the 20th century, such as at <a href="/wiki/Little_Woodbury" title="Little Woodbury">Little Woodbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rispain_Camp" title="Rispain Camp">Rispain Camp</a>. Many hill forts are not in fact "forts" at all, and demonstrate little or no evidence of occupation.\n</p><p>The development of hill forts may have occurred due to greater tensions that arose between the better structured and more populous social groups. Alternatively, there are suggestions that, in the latter phases of the Iron Age, these structures simply indicate a greater accumulation of wealth and a higher standard of living, although any such shift is invisible in the archaeological record for the Middle Iron Age, when hill forts come into their own. In this regard, they may have served as wider centres used for markets and social contact. Either way, during the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman occupation</a> the evidence suggests that, as defensive structures, they proved to be of little use against concerted Roman attack. <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a> comments that Vespasian captured more than twenty "towns" during a campaign in the West Country in 43 AD, and there is some evidence of violence from the hill forts of Hod Hill and Maiden Castle in Dorset from this period. Some hill forts continued as settlements for the newly conquered <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historic)">Britons</a>. Some were also reused by later cultures, such as the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxons</a>, in the early <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">Medieval</a> period.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_people_of_Iron_Age_Britain">The people of Iron Age Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The people of Iron Age Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celts" title="Insular Celts">Insular Celts</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Celtic_movement_from_the_continent">Celtic movement from the continent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Celtic movement from the continent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historic)">Britons</a> were descended from people who had arrived from the continent, comparing the <a href="/wiki/Caledonians" title="Caledonians">Caledonians</a> (in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>) to their <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> neighbours; the <a href="/wiki/Silures" title="Silures">Silures</a> of Southern <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> to <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberian</a> settlers; and the inhabitants of Southeast <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannia</a></i> to <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaulish</a> tribes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Migrationism" class="mw-redirect" title="Migrationism">migrationist</a> view long informed later views of the origins of the British Iron Age and the making of the modern nations. <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguistic</a> evidence inferred from the surviving <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a> in Northern and Western Great Britain at first appeared to support this idea, and the changes in material culture which <a href="/wiki/Archaeologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeologist">archaeologists</a> observed during later <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> were routinely ascribed to a new wave of invaders.\n</p><p>From the early 20th century, this "invasionist" scenario was juxtaposed to a <a href="/wiki/Trans-cultural_diffusion" title="Trans-cultural diffusion">diffusionist</a> view. By the 1960s, this latter model seemed to have gained mainstream support,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but, in turn, it came under attack in the 1970s.\n</p><p>There was certainly a large migration of people from <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> westwards during the early Iron Age. The question whether these movements should be described as "invasions", or as "migrations", or as mostly "diffusion" is largely a semantic one. \n</p><p>Examples of events that could be labelled "invasions" include the arrival in Southern Britain of the <a href="/wiki/Belgae" title="Belgae">Belgae</a> from the end of the 2nd century BC, as described in Caesar\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Commentaries_on_the_Gallic_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Commentaries on the Gallic War">Commentaries on the Gallic War</a>.</i> Such sudden events may be invisible in the archaeological record. In this case, it depends on the interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Aylesford-Swarling_pottery" title="Aylesford-Swarling pottery">Aylesford-Swarling pottery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Regardless of the "invasionist" vs. "diffusionist" debate, it is beyond dispute that exchanges with the continent were a defining aspect of the British Iron Age.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> According to Julius Caesar, the Britons further inland than the Belgae believed that they were <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Population estimates vary but the number of people in Iron Age Great Britain could have been three or four million by the first century BC,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (August 2013)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:British_Iron_Age#Dubious" title="Talk:British Iron Age">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> with most concentrated densely in the agricultural lands of the South. Settlement density and a land shortage may have contributed to rising tensions during the period. The average life expectancy at birth would have been around 25, but at the age of five it would have been around 30.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These figures would be slightly lower for women, and slightly higher for men throughout the Middle Iron Age in most areas, on account of the high mortality rate of young women during childbirth; however, the average age for the two sexes would be roughly equal for the Late Iron Age.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This interpretation depends on the view that warfare and social strife increased in the Late Iron Age, which seems to be fairly well attested in the archaeological record, for Southern Britain at least.\n</p><p>Early in the Iron Age, the widespread Wessex pottery of Southern Britain, such as the type style from <a href="/wiki/All_Cannings_Cross" title="All Cannings Cross">All Cannings Cross</a>, may suggest a consolidated socio-economic group in the region. However, by 600 BC this appears to have broken down into differing sub-groups with their own pottery styles.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Between c. 400 and 100 BC there is evidence of emerging regional identities and a significant population increase.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Ptolemy.27s_Albion"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ptolemy\'s_Albion">Ptolemy\'s Albion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ptolemy&#039;s Albion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a> described Britain at the beginning of Roman rule but incorporated material from earlier sources.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Although the name "<a href="/wiki/Pretanic_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Pretanic Isles">Pretanic Isles</a>" had been known since the voyage of <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>" was in use by <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Pliny</a>, Ptolemy used the earlier "<a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a>", known to have been used as early as the <a href="/wiki/Massaliote_Periplus" title="Massaliote Periplus">Massaliote Periplus</a>.\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information&#32;on Iron Age peoples of Britain: <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_tribes_in_Britain" title="Iron Age tribes in Britain">Iron Age tribes in Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Celtic_tribes" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Celtic tribes">List of Celtic tribes</a></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age_beliefs_in_Britain">Iron Age beliefs in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Iron Age beliefs in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Celtic_polytheism" title="Celtic polytheism">Celtic polytheism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Age religion">Iron Age religion</a></div>\n<p>The Romans described a variety of deities worshipped by the people of Northwestern Europe. <a href="/wiki/Barry_Cunliffe" title="Barry Cunliffe">Barry Cunliffe</a> perceives a division between one group of gods relating to masculinity, the sky and individual tribes and a second group of goddesses relating to associations with fertility, the earth and a universality that transcended tribal differences. Wells and springs had female, divine links exemplified by the goddess <a href="/wiki/Sulis" title="Sulis">Sulis</a> worshipped at <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>\' <a href="/wiki/Agricola_(book)" title="Agricola (book)">Agricola</a> (2.21), he notes the similarity between both religious and ritual practices of the pre-Roman British and the <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Religious practices revolved around offerings and sacrifices, sometimes human but more often involving the ritual slaughter of animals or the deposition of metalwork, especially war booty. Weapons and horse trappings have been found in the bog at <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Cerrig_Bach" title="Llyn Cerrig Bach">Llyn Cerrig Bach</a> on <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> and are interpreted as <a href="/wiki/Votive_offering" title="Votive offering">votive offerings</a> cast into a lake. Numerous weapons have also been recovered from rivers, especially the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">Thames</a>, but also the <a href="/wiki/River_Trent" title="River Trent">Trent</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne</a>. Some buried <a href="/wiki/Hoard" title="Hoard">hoards</a> of jewellery are interpreted as gifts to the earth gods.\n</p><p>Disused grain storage pits and the ends of ditches have also produced what appear to be deliberately placed deposits, including a preference for burials of horses, dogs and ravens. The bodies were often mutilated and some human finds at the bottom of pits, such as those found at <a href="/wiki/Danebury" title="Danebury">Danebury</a>, may have had a ritual aspect.\n</p><p>Caesar\'s texts tell us that the priests of Britain were <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">Druids</a>, a religious elite with considerable holy and secular powers. Great Britain appears to have been the seat of the Druidic religion and Tacitus\' account of the later raid on Anglesey led by <a href="/wiki/Suetonius_Paulinus" class="mw-redirect" title="Suetonius Paulinus">Suetonius Paulinus</a> gives some indication of its nature. No archaeological evidence survives of Druidry, although a number of burials made with ritual trappings and found in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> may suggest a religious character to the subjects.\n</p><p>Overall, the traditional view is that religion was practiced in natural settings in the open air. However, several sites interpreted as Iron Age shrines seem to contradict this view which may derive from Victorian and later Celtic romanticism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Sites such as at <a href="/wiki/Hayling_Island" title="Hayling Island">Hayling Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> and that found during construction work at <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathrow airport">Heathrow airport</a> are interpreted as purpose-built shrines. The Hayling Island example was a circular wooden building set within a rectangular precinct and was rebuilt in stone as a <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> temple in the 1st century AD to the same plan. The Heathrow temple was a small <a href="/wiki/Cella" title="Cella">cella</a> surrounded by a ring of <a href="/wiki/Posthole" title="Posthole">postholes</a> thought to have formed an <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatory</a> which is very similar to Romano-Celtic temples found elsewhere in Europe. A rectangular structure at <a href="/wiki/Danebury" title="Danebury">Danebury</a> and a sequence of six-poster structures overlooking calf burials and culminating in a trench-founded rectangular structure at <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Castle,_Somerset" title="Cadbury Castle, Somerset">Cadbury Castle</a>, Somerset, have been similarly interpreted. An example at Sigwells, overlooking Cadbury Castle, was associated with metalwork and whole and partial animal burials to its east.<sup id="cite_ref-Tabor_2008_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tabor_2008-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, evidence of an open-air shrine was found at <a href="/wiki/Hallaton" title="Hallaton">Hallaton</a>, Leicestershire.  Here, a collection of objects known as the <a href="/wiki/Hallaton_Treasure" title="Hallaton Treasure">Hallaton Treasure</a> were buried in a ditch in the early 1st century AD.  The only structural evidence was a wooden palisade built in the ditch.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Death in Iron Age Great Britain seems to have produced different behaviours in different regions. <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">Cremation</a> was a common method of disposing of the dead, although the <a href="/wiki/Chariot_burial" title="Chariot burial">chariot burials</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Inhumation" class="mw-redirect" title="Inhumation">inhumations</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arras_culture" title="Arras culture">Arras culture</a> of East Yorkshire, and the cist burials of Cornwall, demonstrate that it was not ubiquitous. In fact, the general dearth of excavated Iron Age burials makes drawing conclusions difficult. <a href="/wiki/Excarnation" title="Excarnation">Excarnation</a> has been suggested as a reason for the lack of burial evidence with the remains of the dead being dispersed either naturally or through human agency.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_of_Iron_Age_Britain">Economy of Iron Age Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy of Iron Age Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Trade links developed in the Bronze Age and beforehand provided Great Britain with numerous examples of continental craftsmanship. Swords especially were imported, copied and often improved upon by the natives. Early in the period, <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt" title="Hallstatt">Hallstatt</a> slashing swords and daggers were a significant import although, by the mid 6th century, the volume of goods arriving seems to have declined, possibly due to more profitable trade centres appearing in the Mediterranean. <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La T\xc3\xa8ne culture">La T\xc3\xa8ne culture</a> items (usually associated with the <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celts</a>) appeared in later centuries and, again, these were adopted and adapted with alacrity by the locals.\n</p><p>There also appears to have been a collapse in the <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> trade during the early Iron Age, which can be viewed in three ways:\n</p>\n<ol><li>Steady transition: the development of iron parallel to a diminishing bronze system.</li>\n<li>Rapid abandonment; iron undermines bronze and takes over its social function.</li>\n<li>Bronze crisis: severe reduction in the supply of <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> allows the iron to replace it.</li></ol>\n<p>With regard to animal husbandry, cattle represented a significant investment in pre-Roman Britain as they could be used as a source of portable wealth, as well as providing useful domestic by-products such as milk, cheese and leather. In the later Iron Age, an apparent shift is visible, revealing a change in dominance from cattle rearing to that of sheep. Economically, sheep are significantly less labour-intensive, requiring fewer people per animal.\n</p><p>Whilst cattle and sheep dominate the osteo-archaeological record, evidence for pig, ox, dog and, rarely, chicken is widely represented. There is generally an absence from environmental remains of hunted game and wild species as well as fresh and sea water species, even in coastal communities.\n</p><p>A key commodity of the Iron Age was salt, used for preservation and the supplementation of diet. Whilst difficult to find archaeologically, some evidence does exist. <a href="/wiki/Salterns" class="mw-redirect" title="Salterns">Salterns</a>, in which sea water was boiled to produce salt, are prevalent in the <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> fenlands. Additionally, Morris notes that some salt trading networks spanned over 75&#160;km.\n</p><p>Representing an important political and economic medium, the vast number of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_coinage" title="Celtic coinage">Iron Age coins</a> found in Great Britain are of great archaeological value.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Some, such as <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> <a href="/wiki/Stater" title="Stater">staters</a>, were imported from mainland Europe. Others, such as the cast bronze (<a href="/wiki/Potin" title="Potin">potin</a>) coins of Southeast England, are clearly influenced by Roman originals. The <a href="/wiki/British_tribal" class="mw-redirect" title="British tribal">British tribal</a> kings also adopted the continental habit of putting their names on the coins they had minted, with such examples as Tasciovanus from <a href="/wiki/Verulamium" title="Verulamium">Verulamium</a> and Cunobelinos from <a href="/wiki/Camulodunum" title="Camulodunum">Camulodunum</a> identifying regional differentiation. <a href="/wiki/Hoard" title="Hoard">Hoards</a> of Iron Age coins include the <a href="/wiki/Silsden_Hoard" title="Silsden Hoard">Silsden Hoard</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a> found in 1998. A large collection of coins, known as the <a href="/wiki/Hallaton_Treasure" title="Hallaton Treasure">Hallaton Treasure</a>, was found at a Late Iron Age shrine near <a href="/wiki/Hallaton" title="Hallaton">Hallaton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a> in 2000 and consisted of 5294 coins, mostly attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Corieltavi" class="mw-redirect" title="Corieltavi">Corieltavi</a> tribe.  These were buried in 14 separate hoards over several decades in the early 1st century AD.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The expansion of the economy throughout the period, but especially in the later Iron Age, is in large part a reflection of key changes in the expression of social and economic status.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade">Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_in_the_Early_and_Middle_Iron_Age">Trade in the Early and Middle Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Trade in the Early and Middle Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Early Iron Age saw a substantial number of goods belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> imported from the continent, and these came to have a major effect on Middle Iron Age native art.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_in_the_Late_Iron_Age">Trade in the Late Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Trade in the Late Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>From the late 2nd century BC onwards South-central Britain was indirectly linked into Roman trading networks via <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and the Atlantic seaways to southwest France.<sup id="cite_ref-Cunliffe,_B._W_1988_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunliffe,_B._W_1988-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hengistbury_Head" title="Hengistbury Head">Hengistbury Head</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> was the most important trading site and large quantities of Italian wine <a href="/wiki/Amphora" title="Amphora">amphorae</a> have been found there.<sup id="cite_ref-Cunliffe,_B._W_1988_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunliffe,_B._W_1988-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> These Atlantic trade networks were heavily disrupted following <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>\'s conquest of Brittany in the 50s BC.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This fact may support a supposition that the Celts of Britain had an economic interest in supporting their Gallic brethren in their resistance to Roman occupation.\n</p><p>In Southeast Britain, meanwhile, extensive contact with the <a href="/wiki/Belgae" title="Belgae">\xe2\x80\x98Belgic\xe2\x80\x99</a> tribes of Northern France is evidenced by large numbers of imported Gallo-Belgic gold <a href="/wiki/Celtic_coins" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic coins">coins</a> between the mid-2nd century BC and Caesar\'s <a href="/wiki/Gallic_Wars" title="Gallic Wars">conquest of Gaul</a> in the 50s BC.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> These <a href="/wiki/Celtic_coins" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic coins">coins</a> probably did not principally move through trade. In the past, the emigration of Belgic peoples to Southeast Britain has been cited as an explanation for their appearance in that region.  However,  recent work suggests that their presence in Southeast Britain may have occurred due to a kind of political and social patronage that was paid by the North French groups in exchange for obtaining aid from their British counterparts in their warfare with the Romans on the Continent.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After Caesar\'s conquest of Gaul, a thriving trade developed between Southeast Britain and the near Continent. This is archaeologically evidenced through imports of wine and olive oil amphorae and mass-produced Gallo-Belgic <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_pottery" title="Ancient Roman pottery">pottery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, writing in the early 1st century AD, lists ivory chains and necklaces, amber gems, <a href="/wiki/Roman_glass" title="Roman glass">glass vessels</a>, and other petty wares, as articles imported to Britain, whilst he recorded the island\'s exports as grain, cattle, gold, silver, iron, hides, slaves and hunting dogs.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> This trade probably thrived as a result of political links and <a href="/wiki/Roman_client_kingdoms_in_Britain" title="Roman client kingdoms in Britain">client kingship</a> relationships that developed between groups in Southeast Britain and the Roman world.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_end_of_Iron_Age_Britain">The end of Iron Age Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Iron_Age&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The end of Iron Age Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romano-British culture</a></div>\n<p>Historically speaking, the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> in Southern Great Britain ended with the <a href="/wiki/Roman_invasion_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman invasion of Britain">Roman invasion</a>. Clearly the native societies were not instantaneously changed into <a href="/wiki/Toga" title="Toga">toga</a>-wearing, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>-speaking provincials, though some relatively quick change is evident archaeologically. For example, the Romano-Celtic shrine in <a href="/wiki/Hayling_Island" title="Hayling Island">Hayling Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> was constructed in the AD 60s&#8211;70s,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> whilst <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Agricola</a> was still campaigning in Northern Britain (mostly in what is now <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>), and on top of an <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> ritual site. Rectilinear stone structures, indicative of a change in housing to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> style are visible from the mid to late 1st century AD at <a href="/wiki/Brixworth" title="Brixworth">Brixworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quinton,_Northamptonshire" title="Quinton, Northamptonshire">Quinton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In areas where Roman rule was not strong or was non-existent, Iron Age beliefs and practices remained, but not without at least marginal levels of Roman, or Romano-British influence. The survival of place names, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Camulodunum" title="Camulodunum">Camulodunum</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a>), and which derive from the native language, is evidence of this.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
