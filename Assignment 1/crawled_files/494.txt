lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>This is a <b>list of current and former <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> <a href="/wiki/Mascot" title="Mascot">mascots</a></b>, sorted alphabetically.\n</p><p>The tradition in the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> <a href="/wiki/Mascot" title="Mascot">mascot</a> began with <a href="/wiki/Mr._Met" title="Mr. Met">Mr. Met</a>, introduced for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> when <a href="/wiki/Shea_Stadium" title="Shea Stadium">Shea Stadium</a> opened in 1964.  Although some mascots came and went over time, the popularity of mascots increased when <a href="/wiki/The_San_Diego_Chicken" class="mw-redirect" title="The San Diego Chicken">The San Diego Chicken</a> started independently making appearances at <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> games in 1977.  <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> management felt they needed a mascot similar to the Chicken, so they debuted the <a href="/wiki/Phillie_Phanatic" title="Phillie Phanatic">Phillie Phanatic</a> in 1978.\n</p><p>Today, all but three major-league teams have "official" mascots (Dodgers, Yankees, and Angels).  Five team mascots \xe2\x80\x94 Sluggerrr (Kansas City Royals), the San Diego Chicken, the Phillie Phanatic, Mr. Met, and Slider (Cleveland Indians) \xe2\x80\x94 have been inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Mascot_Hall_of_Fame" title="Mascot Hall of Fame">Mascot Hall of Fame</a>.  Several others have been nominated since the Hall\'s creation in 2005.\n</p><p>Mascots in the MLB are often used to help market the team and league to young children. \n</p>\n<div id="toc" The mascot was created by Brantley Bell (who is currently an infielder in the Cincinnati Reds minor league system), the son of <a href="/wiki/Jay_Bell" title="Jay Bell">Jay Bell</a>, one of the players on the Diamondbacks 1998 inaugural season roster. Brantley came up with the name from two sources. "D. Baxter" comes from the team\'s nickname, "the D-Backs".  The bobcat is from the original name of the stadium where the Diamondbacks play. Today called <a href="/wiki/Chase_Field" title="Chase Field">Chase Field</a>, it was once called Bank One Ballpark, or "BOB" for short. 1973, appearing as a cheerful man with a big <a href="/wiki/Mustache" class="mw-redirect" title="Mustache">mustache</a>.  A beer-barreled <a href="/wiki/Chalet" title="Chalet">chalet</a> was built for him inside the stadium where he led the crowd cheering.  Following each <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home run</a> and every victory by the Brewers, he would slide down and plunge himself into a huge <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> mug in celebration.  He was joined by a companion <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Brewer" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonnie Brewer">Bonnie Brewer</a>, who would playfully swat at the backside of the opposing team\'s third base coach with a broom as the field crew swept the base paths.\n</p><p>Bernie Brewer was a fixture at Brewers home games until 1984, when the Brewers re-built the <a href="/wiki/Bleacher" title="Bleacher">bleachers</a>, replacing the chalet with a sound tower and sending Bernie into <a href="/wiki/Retirement" title="Retirement">retirement</a>. By popular demand, Bernie Brewer came out of his retirement in 1993, when the fans voted for his return.  Bernie was brought back not as just a mustachioed man in <a href="/wiki/Lederhosen" title="Lederhosen">lederhosen</a>, but a full-body costume of a man, including large foam head. The chalet was then rebuilt (it had been in storage on the third base side under the box seats) above the left-center field bleachers. Resembling a <a href="/wiki/Marlin" title="Marlin">marlin</a> with limbs, he can be seen at every Marlins home game. He competes in a waterboat race, which is a computer-animated video shown on the screen, during each game.  The name, picked by original team owner <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Huizenga" title="Wayne Huizenga">Wayne Huizenga</a>, is derived from the fact that a marlin is a <a href="/wiki/Billfish" title="Billfish">billfish</a>, and Huizenga wanted a name that was different from the baseball type names of other mascots (like Slider and Sluggerrr) and one that children could remember more easily.  On Mothers Day and Father\'s Day, Billy is joined by his parents, Bill Sr. and Betty the Marlin.  Billy is also seen at games dancing with kids on the field in between innings and making special appearances in the Fan Zone.\n</p><p>On Opening Day of 1997, the year the Marlins won their first World Series Championship, a Navy SEAL who was parachuting into <a href="/wiki/Hard_Rock_Stadium" title="Hard Rock Stadium">Hard Rock Stadium</a> (then known as Pro Player Stadium) as Billy, lost the head in mid-air.  While the crowd was unaware of the problem, media outlets had been alerted to Billy\'s parachute entrance.  When he didn\'t arrive, the media ran with the story, getting national attention and leading to ESPN\'s Dan Patrick\'s nightly quote, "Bring me the head of Billy the Marlin!"\n</p><p>The original Billy The Marlin was John Routh, who spent 10 years (1993\xe2\x80\x932002) entertaining Marlins fans.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Routh A person dressed up as Fredbird can often be found entertaining young children during baseball games at <a href="/wiki/Busch_Stadium_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Busch Stadium III">Busch Stadium</a>. His name is derived from "Redbird", a synonym for the cardinal bird and for the Cardinals themselves.\n</p><p>Fredbird was introduced in 1979 by the Cardinals, then owned by <a href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch" title="Anheuser-Busch">Anheuser-Busch</a>, to entertain younger fans at the games. He quickly became popular with fans for his dancing, habit of "beaking" the heads of supporters, and for throwing T-shirts into the stands. In later years, he has been joined by "Team Fredbird", a group of young women employed by the club who help him with his T-shirt toss and occasionally in other duties. A young fan won two season tickets for submitting the winning name; he is named after the "gap" in the stands in the seats of Great American, which provides a view into and out of the stadium.  The term "gapper" is also a slang phrase for a batted ball which falls into the "gap" between outfielders (generally a ball hit to either left-center or right-center field which rolls to the fence). He can be seen riding around on an <a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">ATV</a> at home games. He is a large green parrot who wears a Pirates jersey and cap. The character of a <a href="/wiki/Parrot" title="Parrot">parrot</a> was derived from the classic story <i><a href="/wiki/Treasure_Island" title="Treasure Island">Treasure Island</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>, most notably the one owned by <a href="/wiki/Long_John_Silver" title="Long John Silver">Long John Silver</a> named "<a href="/wiki/Captain_Flint" title="Captain Flint">Captain Flint</a>". The race debuted in 2006, and the four presidents on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rushmore" title="Mount Rushmore">Mount Rushmore</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> ("George"), <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> ("Tom"), <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> ("Abe"), and <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> ("Teddy") \xe2\x80\x93 have raced in every season since. In <a href="/wiki/2013_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2013 Major League Baseball season">2013</a>, a fifth permanent contestant \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a> ("Bill") \xe2\x80\x93 was added. A sixth contestant was added in <a href="/wiki/2015_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2015 Major League Baseball season">2015</a> as part of a three-year marketing deal with the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Historical_Association" title="White House Historical Association">White House Historical Association</a>, with the sixth slot changing annually based on the president featured by the association on its annual Christmas ornament. <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Calvin Coolidge</a> ("Cal") was the first to fill the sixth slot, making his debut in July 2015. <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> ("Herbie") replaced Coolidge for <a href="/wiki/2016_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2016 Major League Baseball season">2016</a>. Each president has a uniform number corresponding to his place in the order in which they held the office (George \xe2\x80\x93 1; Tom \xe2\x80\x93 3; Abe \xe2\x80\x93 16; Teddy \xe2\x80\x93 26; William \xe2\x80\x93 27; Cal \xe2\x80\x93 30; Herbie \xe2\x80\x93 31).\n</p><p>The Racing Presidents became an instant success upon their 2006 debut and make multiple public appearances every year. Notably, Abraham Lincoln appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> float for President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>\'s first <a href="/wiki/Presidential_inauguration" title="Presidential inauguration">inauguration</a> parade on January 20, 2009.\n</p><p>A running gag with the Racing Presidents from 2006 to 2012 was that Teddy could never win a race, although he came close in 2012, after apparently defeating the other three presidents: While he was "<a href="/wiki/Tebowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Tebowing">Tebowing</a>" near the finish line, George drove up in a car and whacked him in the back of the head with a baseball bat, knocking him out before he could finish the race. In October 2012, however, just before the regular season ended and shortly before the Nationals\' first postseason run began, Teddy finally won his first race, and he then went on to win four straight. \n</p><p>The Nationals scrapped plans to replace Herbie in <a href="/wiki/2017_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2017 Major League Baseball season">2017</a> with the president to be featured on that year\'s White House Historical Association Christmas ornament. Instead, they announced before the 2017 season that Bill, Cal, and Herbie all had retired to <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, where in 2017 they began a new series of Presidents Races among themselves during Nationals <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">spring training</a> games at <a href="/wiki/FITTEAM_Ballpark_of_the_Palm_Beaches" title="FITTEAM Ballpark of the Palm Beaches">FITTEAM Ballpark of the Palm Beaches</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach</a>. Meanwhile, the Presidents Race at <a href="/wiki/Nationals_Park" title="Nationals Park">Nationals Park</a> returned in 2017 to its original format of 2006-2012, featuring only George, Tom, Abe, and Teddy.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Rally_Squirrel_.28St._Louis_Cardinals.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rally_Squirrel_(St._Louis_Cardinals)">Rally Squirrel (St. Louis Cardinals)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Rally Squirrel (St. Louis Cardinals)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rally_Squirrel" title="Rally Squirrel">Rally Squirrel</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="#Fredbird_(St._Louis_Cardinals)"> \xc2\xa7&#160;Fredbird (St. Louis Cardinals)</a></div>\n<p><b>Rally Squirrel</b> is a secondary mascot for the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>. Some of these mascots may still be used, but are not considered "official" mascots.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrojack_and_Astrodillo">Astrojack and Astrodillo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Astrojack and Astrodillo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Astrojack</b>, an anthropomorphic <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a>, and <b>Astrodillo</b>, an anthropomorphic <a href="/wiki/Nine-banded_armadillo" title="Nine-banded armadillo">armadillo</a>, were mascots for the Houston Astros in the 1980s. They wore the Astros\' "rainbow" uniforms of that time, and were also the team\'s first mascots to circulate through the crowd. Before games and during breaks between innings, they would also race around the field on three-wheelers and perform skits with the <a href="/wiki/Astrodome" title="Astrodome">Astrodome</a>\'s house band, The Astronuts. BJ was created and played by the same person, Kevin Shanahan, for his entire 20-year career as the Jays\' mascot.  Shanahan lost 3 toes on his left foot in an automobile accident during the 1991 off season, but managed to return as the Jays mascot, missing only the first home game of the season.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonnie_Brewer">Bonnie Brewer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Bonnie Brewer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Bonnie Brewer</b> is a former official mascot for the Milwaukee Brewers, appearing at Milwaukee County Stadium from 1973 to 1979. Bonnie was portrayed as a young <a href="/wiki/Blonde" class="mw-redirect" title="Blonde">blonde</a> woman in a gold blouse and short blue <a href="/wiki/Lederhosen" title="Lederhosen">lederhosen</a>, wearing a baseball cap and frequently carrying a blue-and-gold broom which she would use to sweep the bases.\n</p><p>Bonnie was first introduced as the female companion to the Brewers\' mascot Bernie Brewer. Bernie and Bonnie were created by then-team vice president Dick Hackett as part of an effort to create a lively atmosphere at County Stadium, which also included hiring organist Frank Charles to play a Wurlitzer during the games. As Hackett remembers it, Bernie and Bonnie were added over the objections of team owner <a href="/wiki/Bud_Selig" title="Bud Selig">Bud Selig</a>.\n</p><p>Bonnie was noted mainly for her colorful antics during the seventh-inning stretch. As the grounds crew swept the infield, Bonnie wielded her signature broom, sweeping off each base in turn. After sweeping third base, she would playfully swat the opposing team\'s third-base coach on the backside with her broom, following it up with a kiss on his cheek.\n</p><p>Bonnie was discontinued after the 1979 season, although no clear reason has ever been given for her "firing". Bernie Brewer was discontinued as a mascot in 1984, although he was brought back as a costumed mascot in 1993, complete with full-body costume and large foam head. Bonnie Brewer returned as part of the nostalgia-heavy final home stand at County Stadium, September 18\xe2\x80\x9328, 2000. As of  2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, Bonnie is part of the Brewers\' "Retro Fridays" promotions at <a href="/wiki/Miller_Park_(Milwaukee)" class="mw-redirect" title="Miller Park (Milwaukee)">Miller Park</a>, incorporating the traditional base sweeping as well as dancing with Bernie on Bernie\'s Dugout during the fans\' singing of the "<a href="/wiki/Beer_Barrel_Polka" title="Beer Barrel Polka">Beer Barrel Polka</a>" in the seventh inning stretch.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Braves_Bleacher_Creature">Braves Bleacher Creature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Braves Bleacher Creature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Braves Bleacher Creature was a mascot for the Atlanta Braves major league baseball team during the late 1970s. It featured green shaggy fur with a Braves cap and logo on top. The word Braves was written across its chest in big red letters. It had a permanent toothless smile. The mascot usually roamed the stands from time to time during home games and was intended more for the entertainment of younger fans.\n</p><p>The mascot was costumed by Alan Stensland, then a student at Georgia Tech. Stensland was working as an usher at Atlanta Fulton County Stadium when he was approached to wear the costume. The outfit required someone who was 5"8" to 5\'10" tall, and Alen met the height and shoe size requirements. Alen recalls having one of his costume\'s eyes removed by a youngster on his first night out. They also attempted to bust his kneecaps on bat night. During the 1977 season, the mascot made some 250 appearances at games, parties, and parades.\n</p><p>Stensland was only 18 at the time he first donned the costume. The most intense problem he had was the heat. With the added humidity, a really "funky smell" permeated the inside of the costume. Once Stensland graduated, he left the Braves organization, and the mascot was discontinued. The other Braves mascot, Chief Noc-A-Homa, continued on for several more years.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charlie-O">Charlie-O</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Charlie-O">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Charlie-O" title="Charlie-O">Charlie-O</a></div>\n<p><b>Charlie-O the Mule</b> was the mascot used by the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Kansas City Athletics and Oakland A\'s</a> from 1963 to 1976. The <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mule</a> was named after their colorful owner at that time, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Finley" title="Charlie Finley">Charles O. Finley</a>.\n</p><p>When the A\'s moved to then heavily Democratic <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, where the official state animal is the mule, <a href="/wiki/Warren_Hearnes" class="mw-redirect" title="Warren Hearnes">Warren Hearnes</a> gave a mule to Finley for his barnyard menagerie at <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Stadium_(Kansas_City)" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipal Stadium (Kansas City)">Municipal Stadium</a> which also include sheep and goats that scampered up the hill behind right field.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The Municipal Stadium menagerie also included <a href="/wiki/Warpaint_(horse)" class="mw-redirect" title="Warpaint (horse)">Warpaint</a>, the horse mascot of the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Chiefs" title="Kansas City Chiefs">Kansas City Chiefs</a>.  As questions swirled about whether Finley would be loyal to Missouri, he embraced the mule and removed the elephant from the A\'s logo and changed the A\'s colors from blue, red and white to green, gold, and white.\n</p><p>Finley took the <a href="/wiki/Sorrel_(horse)" title="Sorrel (horse)">sorrel</a> 5-foot-tall (1.5&#160;m) mule around the country, walking him into cocktail parties and hotel lobbies, and on one occasion even into the press room after a large feeding to annoy reporters.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chester_Charge">Chester Charge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Chester Charge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In April 1977 the Houston Astros introduced their very first mascot, Chester Charge.  Chester Charge was a 45-pound costume of a cartoon Texas cavalry soldier on a horse.  Chester appeared on the field at the beginning of each home game, during the seventh inning stretch and then ran around the bases at the conclusion of each win. At the blast of a bugle, the scoreboard would light up and the audience would yell, "Charge!"  The first Chester Charge was played by Steve Ross who was then an 18-year-old Senior High School student. The creation of Chester Charge and the (incredible for its day) scoreboard graphics were created by Ed Henderson.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chief_Noc-A-Homa">Chief Noc-A-Homa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Chief Noc-A-Homa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chief_Noc-A-Homa" title="Chief Noc-A-Homa">Chief Noc-A-Homa</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Chief_Noc-A-Homa" title="Chief Noc-A-Homa">Chief Noc-A-Homa</a> was the original mascot of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> from the 1950s until 1986. The name was used for the "laughing Indian" sleeve patch worn on Braves jerseys. From at least the early 1960s, while still in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_County_Stadium" title="Milwaukee County Stadium">Milwaukee County Stadium</a>, until the early 1980s at Atlanta\'s <a href="/wiki/Fulton_County_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulton County Stadium">Fulton County Stadium</a>, this mascot "lived" in a <a href="/wiki/Teepee" class="mw-redirect" title="Teepee">teepee</a> in an unoccupied section of the bleacher seats.\n</p><p>The teepee was involved in a bit of controversy in 1982 when the Braves, who were in first place in the National League West at the time, elected to remove the chief\'s home to provide more seating for the fans. The team dropped out of first when they lost 19 of 21 games after the removal. The teepee was returned to its spot and the team won the division.\n</p><p>Opposition to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> mascots caused the Braves to retire Chief Noc-A-Homa and eventually replace him with Homer The Brave.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crazy_Crab">Crazy Crab</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=49" title="Edit section: Crazy Crab">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <b>Crazy Crab</b> was a mascot of the San Francisco Giants for the 1984 season. As opposed to other mascots, Crazy Crab was meant as an "anti-mascot", satirizing on the mascot craze that was going on at the time.  Fans were encouraged to boo the mascot (played by actor Wayne Doba) and manager <a href="/wiki/Frank_Robinson" title="Frank Robinson">Frank Robinson</a> appeared in a commercial with the crustacean where Robinson was restrained from attacking him.  This encouragement may have worked too well, as Giants fans regularly threw various dangerous objects at Crazy Crab, including beer bottles and batteries, and Crazy Crab\'s suit had to be reinforced with a fiberglass shell for protection.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The crab was so hated, players on both the Giants and even the opposition would throw <a href="/wiki/Rosin" title="Rosin">rosin</a> bags and other objects at the mascot.  Doba sued the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> after two of their players (<a href="/wiki/Kurt_Bevacqua" title="Kurt Bevacqua">Kurt Bevacqua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Bochy" title="Bruce Bochy">Bruce Bochy</a>) tackled him, causing injuries. The mascot lasted only one year and the Giants would not have another mascot until Lou Seal in 1996. The crab returned for the last game at <a href="/wiki/Candlestick_Park" title="Candlestick Park">Candlestick Park</a> that the Giants played in 1999, and a bobblehead was given away with its likeness July 18, 2008 as the franchise celebrated its fiftieth anniversary in the Bay Area. There is even a website devoted to bringing back the Crazy Crab called Rehab The Crab <sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and now a Crazy Crab sandwich at AT&amp;T Park. In 2014, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Hanks" title="Colin Hanks">Colin Hanks</a> directed <i>The Anti-Mascot</i>, an ESPN <i><a href="/wiki/30_for_30" title="30 for 30">30 for 30</a></i> short about Crazy Crab.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On July 7, 2018, the Crazy Crab made its AT&amp;T Park debut in honor of the Giants\' Crazy Crab scarf giveaway. <sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dandy">Dandy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=50" title="Edit section: Dandy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dandy_(mascot)" title="Dandy (mascot)">Dandy (mascot)</a></div>\n<p><b>Dandy</b> was a short-lived mascot of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>. He was a large pinstriped bird-like mascot that sported a Yankees hat.  He had a mustache that gave him an appearance similar to that of former Yankee pitcher <a href="/wiki/Sparky_Lyle" title="Sparky Lyle">Sparky Lyle</a>.  His name was a play on the classic American folk song "<a href="/wiki/Yankee_Doodle_Dandy" title="Yankee Doodle Dandy">Yankee Doodle Dandy</a>".  He appeared at the start of the 1980 season and was so unpopular that he was quickly canceled.  Dandy was beaten up by fans who didn\'t want a mascot, and quit, leading to the elimination of the character as the Yankees chose not to replace him.\n</p><p>Along with this experiment, the Yankees briefly had mascots resembling ballpark food (plus Yankees hats on top) during the mid-1990s. Outside of these two occasions, the Yankees have not had an official mascot or cheerleading squad roam the stands or perform on the field, although the late <a href="/wiki/Freddy_Schuman" title="Freddy Schuman">Freddy Schuman</a> has served as an unofficial promoter in the stands for decades, and <a href="/wiki/Rally_Squirrel#Other_Rally_Squirrels" title="Rally Squirrel">a squirrel</a> appearing on the field has brought inspiration as a mascot for the team.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Admission">General Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=51" title="Edit section: General Admission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>General Admission</b> (a pun on the unreserved $4 seating section of the <a href="/wiki/Astrodome" title="Astrodome">Astrodome</a>) was a mascot for the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> in the mid-to-late 1990s.  He was played by a middle-aged white male, who is now the Senior Director of Guest Services for the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>, and wore a traditional <a href="/wiki/U.S._Cavalry" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Cavalry">U.S. Cavalry</a> uniform complete with gold stars he would affix to his uniform for every Astros home run hit in the dome. Whenever an Astro hit a home run, General Admission would fire a cannon from his outfield platform (that would often scare those seated near him). He was "killed off" at the end of the 1999 season when the Astros main mascot, Orbit, had him zapped by an alien ray gun on the penultimate game of the regular season.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homer">Homer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=52" title="Edit section: Homer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Homer</b> was the mascot of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a>.  He had a <a href="/wiki/Baseball_(object)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball (object)">baseball</a> shaped head, and looked a little like <a href="/wiki/Mr._Met" title="Mr. Met">Mr. Met</a>. This was meant to sound like "home of the brave", the last words of the <a href="/wiki/Star-Spangled_Banner" class="mw-redirect" title="Star-Spangled Banner">national anthem</a>. Incidentally, "homer" was also the longtime nickname for a home run.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Junction_Jack">Junction Jack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: Junction Jack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Junction Jack</b> was the mascot character for the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> from 2000 until 2012. The new stadium was originally called "The Ballpark at Union Station" because it was built on the site of the historic railway station in downtown Houston.  In keeping with this new theme for the Astros, Orbit was replaced by the engineer.  The character was designed by Logan Goodson and named by Duone Byars, both former Astros employees.\n</p><p>After the 2012 season, Junction Jack, Julie, and Jesse were retired, with Orbit returning to his former place as the Astros mascot on November 2, 2012. After the Sox were sold in 1981 by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Veeck" title="Bill Veeck">Bill Veeck</a> to an ownership group headed by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Reinsdorf" title="Jerry Reinsdorf">Jerry Reinsdorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Einhorn" title="Eddie Einhorn">Eddie Einhorn</a>, the new owners, who were eager to draw on the 1970s popularity of such mascots as The San Diego Chicken, hired the design firm responsible for creating the Phillie Phanatic to create a new mascot for the Sox.\n</p><p>They debuted the pair of furry mascots in September 1981, but the fans never accepted the two, ridiculing them throughout their tenure with the team\xe2\x80\x94both because of their ludicrous appearance, which had no apparent connection with the team, and also because they were seen as an attempt to eliminate <a href="/wiki/Andy_the_Clown" title="Andy the Clown">Andy the Clown</a>, who had performed unofficially at Sox games since 1960. "Rhubarb" is longtime <a href="/wiki/List_of_baseball_jargon_(R)#rhubarb" class="mw-redirect" title="List of baseball jargon (R)">baseball slang</a> for a heated on-field argument; Ribbie comes from the acronym RBI, for <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">runs batted in</a>. Often reports will say ribbie instead of RBI to describe it.\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>For most of the 1980s, the patrons at Comiskey Park ... were asked to endure the \'antics\' of baseball\'s least appealing mascots, Ribbie and Roobarb. One looked like the dim-witted son of <a href="/wiki/Oscar_the_Grouch" title="Oscar the Grouch">Oscar the Grouch</a>, the other like a chartreuse <a href="/wiki/Anteater" title="Anteater">anteater</a> with a genetic flaw.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>After another failed mascot in the early 1990s was  Waldo the White Sox Wolf. He is an orange furry creature with a white face originally leased in 1979 and designed by <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Erickson" title="Bonnie Erickson">Bonnie Erickson</a>, formerly a designer for some of <a href="/wiki/Jim_Henson" title="Jim Henson">Jim Henson</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Muppets" class="mw-redirect" title="Muppets">Muppets</a> characters. Youppi! was so named resembling the phrase <i>Yippee!</i> or <i>Hooray!</i> in French. Youppi! was the first mascot to be thrown out of a Major League Baseball game: on August 23, 1989, in the 11th inning, while atop the visitors\' dugout, Youppi! took a running leap, landing hard and noisily on its roof, and then sneaked into a front row seat. <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> manager <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lasorda" title="Tommy Lasorda">Tommy Lasorda</a> complained to the umpires and Youppi! was ejected, though he later returned, confined to the home team\'s dugout roof. Youppi! was abandoned as a mascot after the Expos franchise moved to Washington in 2005, but was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">NHL</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a> team on September 16, 2005, as potentially the first sports mascot to switch their allegiance from one sport to another, while remaining in the same city.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teams_without_a_mascot">Teams without a mascot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=64" title="Edit section: Teams without a mascot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The following MLB teams do not currently have an official mascot:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mascot_store_in_various_ballparks">Mascot store in various ballparks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Major_League_Baseball_mascots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=65" title="Edit section: Mascot store in various ballparks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Citizens_Bank_Park#Other_attractions" title="Citizens Bank Park">"Build-A-Bear Workshop" Make-Your-Own-Phanatic store</a>, at Citizens Bank Park, was the first store of its kind in sports. Fans are invited to buy and stuff a <a href="/wiki/Phillie_Phanatic" title="Phillie Phanatic">Phillie Phanatic</a> doll and dress it up. Following the 2010 season, the Build-A- Bear in Philadelphia was discontinued.  Similar shops have since been set up in Cincinnati (<a href="/wiki/Great_American_Ball_Park" title="Great American Ball Park">Great American Ball Park</a>), Cleveland (<a href="/wiki/Progressive_Field" title="Progressive Field">Progressive Field</a>), St. Louis (<a href="/wiki/Busch_Stadium" title="Busch Stadium">Busch Stadium</a>), San Francisco (<a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Park" title="AT&amp;T Park">AT&amp;T Park</a>), and Washington, D.C. (<a href="/wiki/Nationals_Park" title="Nationals Park">Nationals Park</a>). The Milwaukee Brewers also have in their main team store at <a href="/wiki/Miller_Park_(Milwaukee)" class="mw-redirect" title="Miller Park (Milwaukee)">Miller Park</a> a whole section of their store consisting entirely of merchandise featuring the <a href="/wiki/Sausage_Race" title="Sausage Race">Racing Sausages</a>, called The Meat Locker.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
