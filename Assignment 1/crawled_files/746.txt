If the time of ownership can be fixed and determined, it cannot be a freehold.\n</p><p>In England and Wales, before the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Property_Act_1925" title="Law of Property Act 1925">Law of Property Act 1925</a>, a freehold estate transferable to the owner\'s "<a href="/wiki/Heirs" class="mw-redirect" title="Heirs">heirs and assigns</a>" (that is, successors by inheritance, or purchase (including gift), respectively) was a <i><a href="/wiki/Fee_simple" title="Fee simple">fee simple</a></i> estate. When transfer, by inheritance or otherwise, was limited to lineal descendants ("<a href="/wiki/Heirs_of_the_body" title="Heirs of the body">heirs of the body</a>" or "heirs of the blood") of the first person to whom the estate was given, it was a <i><a href="/wiki/Fee_tail" title="Fee tail">fee tail</a></i> estate. There were also freehold estates not of inheritance, such as an <a href="/wiki/Estate_for_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Estate for life">estate for life</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
