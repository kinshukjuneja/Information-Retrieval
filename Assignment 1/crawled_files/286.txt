Wales</b> in terms of human settlements covers the period from about 230,000 years ago, the date attributed to the earliest human remains found in what is now <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, to the year AD 48 when the <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a> began a military campaign against one of the Welsh tribes. Traditionally, historians have believed that successive waves of immigrants brought different cultures into the area, largely replacing the previous inhabitants, with the last wave of immigrants being the <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celts</a>. However, studies of population genetics now suggest that this may not be true, and that immigration was on a smaller scale.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Palaeolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The earliest known human remains discovered in modern-day Wales date from 230,000 years ago. An early <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a> upper jaw fragment containing two teeth, whose owner probably lived during an <a href="/wiki/Interglacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Interglacial period">interglacial period</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Palaeolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower Palaeolithic">Lower Palaeolithic</a>, was found in a cave in the <a href="/wiki/River_Elwy" title="River Elwy">River Elwy</a> valley, at the <a href="/wiki/Bontnewydd_Paleaolithic_site" class="mw-redirect" title="Bontnewydd Paleaolithic site">Bontnewydd Paleaolithic site</a>, near <a href="/wiki/St_Asaph" title="St Asaph">St Asaph</a> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Llanelwy</i>), <a href="/wiki/Denbighshire" title="Denbighshire">Denbighshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jewels_1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jewels_1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Excavations of the site in between 1978 and 1995 revealed a further 17 teeth belonging to five individuals, a total of seven hand axes and some animal bones, some of which show signs of butchery.<sup id="cite_ref-rhagor_1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhagor_1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This site is the most north-westerly in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> at which the remains of early <a href="/wiki/Hominids" class="mw-redirect" title="Hominids">hominids</a> have been found, and is considered to be of international importance. Late Neanderthal <a href="/wiki/Hand_axe" title="Hand axe">hand axes</a> were also found at <a href="/wiki/Coygan_Cave" title="Coygan Cave">Coygan Cave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carmarthenshire" title="Carmarthenshire">Carmarthenshire</a> and have been dated to between 60,000 and 35,000 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-jewels_2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jewels_2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rhagor_2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhagor_2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Paviland" class="mw-redirect" title="Paviland">Paviland</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> caves of the <a href="/wiki/Gower_Peninsula" title="Gower Peninsula">Gower Peninsula</a> in south <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> are by far the richest source of <a href="/wiki/Aurignacian" title="Aurignacian">Aurignacian</a> material in Britain, including <a href="/wiki/Burin_(lithic_flake)" title="Burin (lithic flake)">burins</a> and scrapers dated to about 28,500 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The first remains of modern humans, <i>Homo sapiens sapiens</i> to be found in Wales was the famous <a href="/wiki/Red_Lady_of_Paviland" title="Red Lady of Paviland">Red Lady of Paviland</a>. This was a human skeleton dyed in <a href="/wiki/Red_ochre" class="mw-redirect" title="Red ochre">red ochre</a> discovered in 1823 in one of the Paviland caves.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Despite the name, the skeleton is actually that of a young man who lived about 33,000 years ago at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a> Period (old <a href="/wiki/Stone_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Stone age">stone age</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He is considered to be the oldest known ceremonial burial in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>. The skeleton was found along with fragments of small cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> rods, fragments of ivory <a href="/wiki/Bracelet" title="Bracelet">bracelets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">seashells</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Settlement in Wales was apparently intermittent as periods of cooling and warming led to the ice sheets advancing and retreating. Wales appears to have been abandoned from about 21,000 years ago until after 13,000 years ago, with a burial found at <a href="/wiki/Kendrick%27s_Cave" title="Kendrick&#39;s Cave">Kendrick\'s Cave</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Orme" title="Great Orme">Great Orme</a> dating to about 12,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mesolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Following the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">Ice age</a>, Wales became roughly the shape it is today by about 7000 BC and was inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherers" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter-gatherers">hunter-gatherers</a>. Wales has many sites where Mesolithic material has been found, but securely stratified material is rare.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The earliest dated Mesolithic site in Wales is Nab Head, <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a>, around 9,200 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Many of the sites from this period are coastal, although 9,000 years ago they would have been some distance inland from the sea. There is a particular concentration in <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a>, but there are also a good number of upland sites, most apparently seasonal hunting locations, for example around <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Brenig" title="Llyn Brenig">Llyn Brenig</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Some decorated pebbles found at <a href="/wiki/Rhuddlan" title="Rhuddlan">Rhuddlan</a> represent the earliest art found in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An investigation of post holes at the late Neolithic - Early Bronze Age chambered tomb of <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Celli_Ddu" title="Bryn Celli Ddu">Bryn Celli Ddu</a> on <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>, published in 2006, gave a radiocarbon dating which placed two of the holes in the Mesolithic period.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic">Neolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BrynCelliDdu3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/BrynCelliDdu3.jpg/250px-BrynCelliDdu3.jpg" width="250" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/BrynCelliDdu3.jpg/375px-BrynCelliDdu3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/BrynCelliDdu3.jpg/500px-BrynCelliDdu3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3504" data-file-height="2336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BrynCelliDdu3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bryn_Celli_Ddu" title="Bryn Celli Ddu">Bryn Celli Ddu</a>, a late Neolithic chambered tomb on Anglesey</div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest farming communities are now believed to date from about 4000 BC, marking the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period. Pollen evidence indicates the clearing of forests on an increasing scale during this period. The Neolithic saw the construction of many <a href="/wiki/Megalithic_tomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Megalithic tomb">chambered tombs</a>, the most notable including Bryn Celli Ddu and <a href="/wiki/Barclodiad_y_Gawres" title="Barclodiad y Gawres">Barclodiad y Gawres</a> on Anglesey.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Three main types of megalithic tomb are found in Wales, the <a href="/wiki/Severn-Cotswold_tomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Severn-Cotswold tomb">Severn-Cotswold</a> type in the south-east, the <a href="/wiki/Portal_dolmen" class="mw-redirect" title="Portal dolmen">Portal dolmen</a> type and the <a href="/wiki/Passage_grave" title="Passage grave">Passage graves</a> which are characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> area and the Atlantic fa\xc3\xa7ade of Europe and Morocco. Megalithic tombs are most common in the western lowlands.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> There is evidence of close cultural links with <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, particularly in the Early Neolithic period.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A number of houses from the Neolithic period have also been found in Wales, most notably the settlement at <a href="/wiki/Clegyr_Boia" title="Clegyr Boia">Clegyr Boia</a> near <a href="/wiki/St_David%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="St David&#39;s">St David\'s</a> in Pembrokeshire. Many artefacts have also been found, particularly polished stone axeheads. There were a number of "factories" in Wales producing these axeheads, the largest being the Graig Lwyd factory at <a href="/wiki/Penmaenmawr" title="Penmaenmawr">Penmaenmawr</a> on the north coast which exported its products as far afield as <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and the English midlands.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Pottery finds also indicate a relationship with Ireland.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Bronze_Age">The Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg/180px-British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg" width="180" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg/270px-British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg/360px-British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1263" data-file-height="779" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Museum_gold_thing_501594_fh000035.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Mold_cape" title="Mold cape">Mold cape</a>, now in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Metal tools first appeared in Wales about 2500 BC, initially <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>. The climate during the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a> (c. 2100-1400 BC) is thought to have been warmer than at present, as there are many remains from this period in what are now bleak uplands. Much of the <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> for the production of bronze probably came from the copper mine on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Orme" title="Great Orme">Great Orme</a>, where prehistoric mining on a very large scale dates largely from the middle Bronze Age.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In particular copper from the Great Orme mines appears to have been used for the production of bronze implements of the Acton Park Complex, named after a hoard found at <a href="/wiki/Acton_Park,_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Acton Park, Wales">Acton Park</a> near <a href="/wiki/Wrexham" title="Wrexham">Wrexham</a>. These tools, particularly axeheads, were developed towards the end of the Early Bronze Age and are innovative in both <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a> and design. They were widely exported, with examples being found along the continental coast from <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> to north <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg/140px-Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg" width="140" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg/210px-Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg/280px-Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg 2x" data-file-width="283" data-file-height="337" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Banc_Ty%27ndd%C3%B4l_sun-disc" title="Banc Ty&#39;ndd\xc3\xb4l sun-disc">Banc Ty\'ndd\xc3\xb4l sun-disc</a> Primary <a href="/wiki/Beaker_culture" title="Beaker culture">Bell Beaker</a> Goldwork Tradition</div></div></div>\n<p>Burial practices in the Bronze Age differed from the communal tombs of the Neolithic period, with a change to burial in <a href="/wiki/Round_barrow" title="Round barrow">round barrows</a> and the provision of grave goods. Inhumation was soon replaced by cremation and in Wales the cemetery mound with a number of burials had become the standard form by about 2000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  One of the most striking finds from Bronze Age Wales was the <a href="/wiki/Mold_cape" title="Mold cape">gold cape</a> found in a tomb at Bryn yr Ellyllion, <a href="/wiki/Mold,_Flintshire" title="Mold, Flintshire">Mold, Flintshire</a> dated to 1900-1600 BC, weighing 560 g and produced from a single gold ingot. Very few weapons have been found in Early Bronze Age graves in Wales compared with other objects, and the lack of traces of earlier Bronze Age settlements is thought to indicate that farms or hamlets were undefended.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From about 1250 BC there was a deterioration in the climate which became more marked from about 1000 BC, with higher rainfall and much lower summer temperatures. This led to an increase in peat formation and probably the abandonment of many upland settlements.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that this led to conflict and to changes in social organization, with the earliest hillforts appearing about 800 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Late Bronze Age saw the development of more advanced bronze implements, with weapons becoming increasingly common.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> While the weapons reflect introduced styles, there are pronounced regional variations in the styles of tools, particularly axes. On the basis of tool types, Wales can be divided into four regions, the south-east, south-west, north-west and north-east. These regions show an approximate correspondence to the territories of the tribes later recorded in these areas by the Romans, the <a href="/wiki/Silures" title="Silures">Silures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demetae" title="Demetae">Demetae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ordovices" title="Ordovices">Ordovices</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deceangli" title="Deceangli">Deceangli</a> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Iron_Age">The Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG/220px-Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG/330px-Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG/440px-Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3504" data-file-height="2336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tre%27r_Ceiri.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Entrance through the dry-stone rampart, Tre\'r Ceiri hillfort, Gwynedd</div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest iron implements found in Wales come from <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Fawr" title="Llyn Fawr">Llyn Fawr</a> at the head of the <a href="/wiki/River_Rhondda" title="River Rhondda">Rhondda Valley</a>, where objects apparently deposited as <a href="/wiki/Votive" class="mw-redirect" title="Votive">votive</a> offerings include three made of iron: a sword, a spearhead and a sickle. These items are thought to date to about 650 BC, and while the sword appears to be imported the sickle is an imitation of a native bronze prototype.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">Iron Age</a> saw the building of <a href="/wiki/Hillfort" title="Hillfort">hillforts</a> which are particularly numerous in Wales, examples being <a href="/wiki/Pen_Dinas" title="Pen Dinas">Pen Dinas</a> near  <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tre%27r_Ceiri" title="Tre&#39;r Ceiri">Tre\'r Ceiri</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Ll%C5%B7n_Peninsula" title="Ll\xc5\xb7n Peninsula">Ll\xc5\xb7n Peninsula</a>. The earliest distinctively Iron Age settlement in Wales is considered to be Castell Odo, a small hillfort near the tip of the Ll\xc5\xb7n Peninsula, dated to about 400 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The largest hillforts are most numerous along the eastern border of Wales, with some large examples also found in the lowlands of north-west Wales. In the south-west, by contrast, hillforts are very numerous but mainly small, with an area of under 1.2 hectares.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A particularly significant find from this period was made in 1943 at <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Cerrig_Bach" title="Llyn Cerrig Bach">Llyn Cerrig Bach</a> on Anglesey, when the ground was being prepared for the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> base. The cache included weapons, shields, <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariots</a> along with their fittings and harnesses, and slave chains and tools. Many had been deliberately broken and seem to have been <a href="/wiki/Votive_offering" title="Votive offering">votive offerings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> These finds are considered to be one of the most important collections of <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La T\xc3\xa8ne culture">La T\xc3\xa8ne</a> metalwork discovered in Britain. Pottery on the other hand is fairly rare in Wales during this period and most of what has been found appears to be imported.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The La T\xc3\xa8ne culture is traditionally associated with the <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celts</a>, and the general view until fairly recently was that the appearance of this culture indicated a large-scale invasion by peoples who also brought a Celtic language which later developed into <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The currently more popular view is that any movement of peoples was on a smaller scale, with cultural diffusion responsible for most of the changes. There is some evidence to support the latter model, such as burials associated with earlier religious sites.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that a Celtic language was being spoken in Wales by about 700 BC.\n</p><p>The prehistoric period ended with the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a>, who began their campaigns against the Welsh tribes in 48 AD with an attack on the <a href="/wiki/Deceangli" title="Deceangli">Deceangli</a> in north-east Wales. Wales was divided between a number of tribes, of which the <a href="/wiki/Silures" title="Silures">Silures</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ordovices" title="Ordovices">Ordovices</a> put up the most stubborn resistance. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Wales">Roman</a> conquest of Wales was complete by 79 AD. The reports of Roman historians such as <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> give a little more information about Wales in this period, such as that the island of <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> was apparently a stronghold of the <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">Druids</a>. The impact of the arrival of the Romans may have varied from one part of Wales to another; for example there is evidence that some hillforts, such as Tre\'r Ceiri, continued to be occupied during the Roman period.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Lynch, Aldhose-Green &amp; Davies  <i>Prehistoric Wales</i> p. 6</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-jewels_1-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-jewels_1_2-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.webcitation.org/66bOAOxmp?url=http://www.gtj.org.uk/en/small/item/GTJ27306/">"Gathering the Jewels"</a>. <i>Early Neanderthal jaw fragment, c. 230,000 years old</i>.
