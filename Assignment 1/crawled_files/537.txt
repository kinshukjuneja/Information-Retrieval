The term <b>Post-Roman Britain</b> is also used for the period, mainly in non-archaeological contexts.\n</p><p>The term "sub-Roman" was originally used to describe archaeological remains such as <a href="/wiki/Potsherd" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsherd">potsherds</a> found in sites of the 5th and 6th centuries, and hinted at the decay of locally-made wares from a previous higher standard that had existed under the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. It is now more often used to denote this period of history instead.\n</p><p>Although the culture of Britain in the period was mainly derived from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> sources, there were also <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> settled as <i><a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">foederati</a></i> in the area, originally from <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Saxony</a> in northwestern Germany, although the term \'Saxon\' was used by the British for all Germanic incomers. Gradually the latter assumed more control, creating <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a> in the process. The <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> in northern Scotland were outside the applicable area.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meaning of terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The period of sub-Roman Britain traditionally covers the <a href="/wiki/History_of_England" title="History of England">history of the area which subsequently became England</a> from the end of <a href="/wiki/Roman_withdrawal_from_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman withdrawal from Britain">Roman imperial rule</a> in 410 to the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Canterbury" title="Augustine of Canterbury">Saint Augustine</a> in 597. The date taken for the end of this period is arbitrary in that the sub-Roman culture continued in northern England until the merger of <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a> (the kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Brigantes" title="Brigantes">Brigantes</a>) with <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a> by dynastic marriage in 633, and longer in the <a href="/wiki/West_of_England" title="West of England">West of England</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.\n</p><p>This period has attracted a great deal of academic and popular debate, in part because of the scarcity of the written source material. The term "post-Roman Britain" is also used for the period, mainly in non-archaeological contexts; "sub-Roman" and "post-Roman" are both terms that apply to the old <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannia</a>, i.e. Britain south of the <a href="/wiki/River_Forth" title="River Forth">Forth</a>\xe2\x80\x93<a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">Clyde</a> line. The history of the area between <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a> and the Forth\xe2\x80\x93Clyde line is similar to that of Wales (see <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bernicia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gododdin" title="Gododdin">Gododdin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strathclyde" title="Strathclyde">Strathclyde</a>). North of the line lay a thinly-populated area including the kingdoms of the Maeatae (in <a href="/wiki/Angus,_Scotland" title="Angus, Scotland">Angus</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dalriada" class="mw-redirect" title="Dalriada">Dalriada</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Argyll" title="Argyll">Argyll</a>), and the kingdom whose <i>kaer</i> (castle) near Inverness was visited by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Columba" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Columba">Saint Columba</a>. The Romans referred to these peoples collectively as <i>Picti</i> <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, meaning Painted Ones.\n</p><p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>", implying wider horizons, is finding more use in the academic community, especially when transformations of classical culture common throughout the post-Roman West are examined; it is less successfully applied to Britain at the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The period may also be considered as part of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">early Middle Ages</a>, if continuity with the following periods is stressed. Popular (and some academic) works use a range of more dramatic names for the period: the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>, the Brythonic Age, the Age of Tyrants, or the Age of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">Arthur</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Written_accounts">Written accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Written accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is very little extant written material available from this period, though there is a considerable amount from later periods that may be relevant. A lot of what is available deals with the first few decades of the 5th century only. The sources can usefully be classified into British and continental, and into contemporary and non-contemporary.\n</p><p>Two primary contemporary British sources exist: the <i>Confessio</i> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a>\' <i><a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></i> (<i>On the Ruin and Conquest of Britain</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Patrick\'s <i>Confessio</i> and his <i>Letter to <a href="/wiki/Ceretic_Guletic" title="Ceretic Guletic">Coroticus</a></i> reveal aspects of life in Britain, from where he was abducted to Ireland. It is particularly useful in highlighting the state of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Christianity at the time</a>. Gildas is the nearest to a source of Sub-Roman history but there are many problems in using it. The document represents British history as he and his audience understood it. Though a few other documents of the period do exist, such as Gildas\' letters on monasticism, they are not directly relevant to British history. Gildas\' <i>De Excidio</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Jeremiad" title="Jeremiad">jeremiad</a>: it is written as a polemic to warn contemporary rulers against sin, demonstrating through historical and biblical examples that bad rulers are always punished by God&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 in the case of Britain, through the destructive wrath of the <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxon</a> invaders. The historical section of <i>De Excidio</i> is short, and the material in it is clearly selected with Gildas\' purpose in mind. There are no absolute dates given, and some of the details, such as those regarding the <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Wall" title="Antonine Wall">Antonine</a> Walls are clearly wrong. Nevertheless, Gildas does provide us with an insight into some of the kingdoms that existed when he was writing, and how an educated monk perceived the situation that had developed between the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxons</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historic)">Britons</a>.\n</p><p>There are more continental contemporary sources that mention Britain, though these are highly problematic. The most famous is the so-called <i>Rescript of <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Augustus_Honorius" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavius Augustus Honorius">Honorius</a></i>, in which the Western Emperor Honorius tells the British <i><a href="/wiki/Civitates" class="mw-redirect" title="Civitates">civitates</a></i> to look to their own defence. The first reference to this rescript is written by the 6th century <a href="/wiki/Byzantine" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> scholar <a href="/wiki/Zosimus" title="Zosimus">Zosimus</a> and is found in the middle of a discussion of southern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>; no further mention of Britain is made, which has led some, though not all, modern academics to suggest that the rescript does not apply to Britain, but to <a href="/wiki/Bruttium" class="mw-redirect" title="Bruttium">Bruttium</a> in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Gallic Chronicles, <a href="/wiki/Chronica_Gallica_of_452" title="Chronica Gallica of 452">Chronica Gallica of 452</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chronica_Gallica_of_511" title="Chronica Gallica of 511">Chronica Gallica of 511</a>, say prematurely that "Britain, abandoned by the Romans, passed into the power of the Saxons" and provide information about St <a href="/wiki/Germanus_of_Auxerre" title="Germanus of Auxerre">Germanus</a> and his visit(s) to Britain, though again this text has received considerable academic deconstruction.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The work of <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a>, another 6th-century Byzantine writer, makes some references to Britain, though the accuracy of these is uncertain.\n</p><p>There are numerous later written sources that claim to provide accurate accounts of the period. The first to attempt this was the monk <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>, writing in the early 8th century. He based his account of the Sub-Roman period in his <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum</a></i> (written around 731) heavily on Gildas, though he tried to provide dates for the events Gildas describes. It was written from an anti-Briton point of view. Later sources, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">Historia Brittonum</a></i> often attributed to <a href="/wiki/Nennius" title="Nennius">Nennius</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> (again written from a non-Briton point of view, based on West Saxon sources) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Annales_Cambriae" title="Annales Cambriae">Annales Cambriae</a></i>, are all heavily shrouded in myth and can only be used with caution as evidence for this period.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There are also documents giving <a href="/wiki/Welsh_poetry" title="Welsh poetry">Welsh poetry</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Taliesin" title="Taliesin">Taliesin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aneirin" title="Aneirin">Aneirin</a>) and land deeds (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Llandaff" title="Book of Llandaff">Llandaff charters</a>) that appear to date back to the 6th century.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> there were many books written that purport to give the history of the Sub-Roman period. These have been influenced by the fictionalised account in <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i> (<i>History of the Kings of Britain</i>). Therefore, they can only be regarded as showing how the legends grew. Not until modern times have serious studies of the period been undertaken.\n</p><p>Some "Lives of Saints" relating to Celtic clerics are early, but most are late and unreliable. <a href="/wiki/Jude_the_Apostle" title="Jude the Apostle">St. Thaddaeus</a> is described as visiting a <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villa</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chepstow" title="Chepstow">Chepstow</a>, while St Cuthbert visited deserted <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_evidence">Archaeological evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archaeological evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">Archaeology</a> provides further evidence for this period, though of a different nature from that provided by documents. Archaeology suggested to Richard Reece that the depopulation of Roman towns and the development of villa and estate organization was already occurring in the 4th century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The 5th and 6th centuries in Britain are marked by a sharp discontinuity in town life, perhaps always a somewhat artificial Roman imposition on the landscape, dependent on imperial military requirements and requirements of administration and tax collection; the exceptions are a handful of sites: <a href="/wiki/Londinium" title="Londinium">Londinium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eboracum" title="Eboracum">Eboracum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wroxeter" title="Wroxeter">Wroxeter</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, but the discontinuity in the episcopate, "the institution most responsible for the survival of towns in Gaul", as <a href="/wiki/H._R._Loyn" title="H. R. Loyn">H. R. Loyn</a> observes, speaks against town survival in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villa</a> system, represented by some five hundred archaeological sites, did not survive either; unlike Gaul, in Britain not a single villa name survived into the Germanic period. It may be possible that the villa system did not survive the disastrous <a href="/wiki/Pict" class="mw-redirect" title="Pict">Pictish</a> <a href="/wiki/Great_Conspiracy" title="Great Conspiracy">incursions in 367</a> and following years.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the sub-Roman period, building in stone came to an end; buildings were constructed of less durable materials than during the Roman period. However, <a href="/wiki/Brooch" title="Brooch">brooches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Weapons">weapons</a> from this period have survived. The study of <a href="/wiki/Burial" title="Burial">burials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremations</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Grave_goods" title="Grave goods">grave goods</a> associated with these, has done much to expand the understanding of cultural identities in the period.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Archaeology has shown some evidence of continuity with <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Ancient_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Education in Ancient Rome">Roman education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_commerce" title="Roman commerce">trade with the Mediterranean</a> and with <a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic art</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Excavation_(archaeology)" title="Excavation (archaeology)">Excavations</a> of settlements have revealed possible changes in social structures, and to what extent life in Britain continued unaltered in certain pockets into the early <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> period. Hilltops, the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Hillfort" title="Hillfort">hillforts</a>", <i><a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">castra</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Monasteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Monasteries">monasteries</a> have been excavated. Work on towns has been particularly important. Work on the hill-forts has shown evidence of refurbishment, and also of overseas trade, in this period. One of the earliest major excavations was at <a href="/wiki/Tintagel" title="Tintagel">Tintagel</a> (Radford 1939). This uncovered rectangular structures and much Mediterranean pottery. The buildings were initially interpreted as a monastery, but later as a princely stronghold and trading post. Another important excavation was at <a href="/wiki/Dinas_Powys" title="Dinas Powys">Dinas Powys</a> (Alcock 1963) which showed evidence of metalworking. Alcock also led the excavations at <a href="/wiki/South_Cadbury" title="South Cadbury">South Cadbury</a> (Alcock 1995). Many other sites have now been shown to have been occupied during the sub-Roman period, including <a href="/wiki/Banna_(Birdoswald)" title="Banna (Birdoswald)">Birdoswald</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saxon_Shore" title="Saxon Shore">Saxon Shore</a> forts.\n</p><p>Work on <a href="/wiki/Field_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Field systems">field systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_archaeology" title="Environmental archaeology">environmental archaeology</a> has also highlighted how much <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural practice</a> continued and changed over the period.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Archaeology, however, has its limits, especially in dating. Although <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> can provide a rough estimate, this is not accurate enough to associate archaeological finds with historical events. <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">Dendrochronology</a> is accurate enough to do this, though few suitable pieces of wood have been uncovered.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg/200px-All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg" width="200" height="247" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg/300px-All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg/400px-All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2036" data-file-height="2512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roman coins findings clearly indicate the areas of biggest "romanization" and presence in Roman Britain</div></div></div>\n<p>Coins would normally be the most useful tool for dating, but not for sub-Roman Britain since no newly minted coins are believed to have entered circulation after the very early 5th century.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is some archaeological evidence for <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> and Britons living on the same site. For example, in the cemetery at <a href="/wiki/Wasperton" title="Wasperton">Wasperton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>, one can see a family adopting Anglo-Saxon culture over a long period.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakdown_of_Roman_society">Breakdown of Roman society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breakdown of Roman society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Because of the sparse evidence for the period, many interpretations are possible. Some have taken all the sources at their face value (e.g. Alcock 1971, Morris 1973, Ashe 1985) and others set aside the non-contemporary sources. Any interpretation can only be tentative and dates more so.\n</p><p>At the start of the 5th century, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannia</a> was part of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> under <a href="/wiki/Honorius_(emperor)" title="Honorius (emperor)">Honorius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> But there were already signs of decline, and some <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> may already have been in England as <a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">mercenaries</a>. Some Roman troops were withdrawn by <a href="/wiki/Stilicho" title="Stilicho">Stilicho</a> in 402, and bulk coin payments ceased around then. In 406 the army in Britain revolted, electing three successive "tyrants", the last of whom took further troops to <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>. He established himself briefly as <a href="/wiki/Constantine_III_(western_emperor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine III (western emperor)">Constantine III</a> but was defeated and subsequently executed in 411. Meanwhile, there were barbarian raids on Britain in 408, but these seem to have been defeated. After 410 Honorius apparently sent letters to the cities of Britain telling them to fend for themselves, though this is sometimes disputed.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The withdrawal of most Roman troops did not end the Roman culture of the "lost province", which still remained part of the Roman cultural world, with inhabitants who identified themselves as Roman.\n</p><p>From the middle of the 5th century the Germanic raiders began to settle in the eastern river valleys.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Later civil wars seem to have broken out, which have been interpreted either as being between pro-Roman and independence groups or between "Established Church" and <a href="/wiki/Pelagian" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelagian">Pelagian</a> parties (Myres 1965, Morris 1965), a class struggle between peasants and land owners (Thompson 1977, Wood 1984) and a coup by an urban elite (Snyder 1988). A recent view explored by Laycock<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> sees Britain violently fragmenting into kingdoms based on British tribal identities; \'violently\' is disputable, but clearly most of the <i>civitates</i> gradually transformed into kingdoms. Life seems to have continued much as before in the countryside, and on a reduced scale in the towns as evidenced by the descriptions of <a href="/wiki/Germanus_of_Auxerre" title="Germanus of Auxerre">Germanus</a>\' visits. Feuding kingships replaced the centrally governed <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman provinces</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> says that a "council" was convened by <a href="/wiki/Vortigern" title="Vortigern">Vortigern</a> to find ways of countering the barbarian threat. The council opted to hire Saxon mercenaries, following Roman practice. After a while these turned against the British and plundered the towns. A British leader, <a href="/wiki/Ambrosius_Aurelianus" title="Ambrosius Aurelianus">Ambrosius Aurelianus</a>, fought against them in a number of battles apparently over a long period. Towards the end of this period was the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mons_Badonicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Mons Badonicus">Battle of Mons Badonicus</a>, around 490, which later sources claimed was won by <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>, though Gildas does not identify him. After this there was a long period of peace. The British seem to have been in control of England and Wales roughly west of a line from <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a>. The Saxons had control of eastern areas in an arc from <a href="/wiki/East_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="East Yorkshire">East Yorkshire</a> through <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> and perhaps <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>, to <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_East_England" title="South East England">South East England</a>.\n</p><p>Writing in Latin, perhaps about 540, Gildas gives an account of the history of Britain, but the earlier part (for which other sources are available) is severely muddled. He castigates five rulers in western Britain&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Constantine of <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>, Aurelius Caninus, Vortipor of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Dyfed" title="Kingdom of Dyfed">Demetae</a>, Cuneglasus and Maglocunus (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mailcun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mailcun (page does not exist)">Mailcun</a> or in later spelling <a href="/wiki/Maelgwn_Gwynedd" title="Maelgwn Gwynedd">Maelgwn</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>) &#160;\xe2\x80\x93 for their sins. He also attacks the British clergy. He gives information on the British diet, dress and entertainment. He writes that Britons were killed, emigrated or were enslaved but gives no idea of numbers.\n</p><p>In the late 6th century there was another period of Saxon expansion, starting with the capture of <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum" title="Old Sarum">Searoburh</a> in 552 by the dynasty that later ruled <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>, and including entry into the <a href="/wiki/Cotswolds" title="Cotswolds">Cotswolds</a> area after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Deorham" title="Battle of Deorham">Battle of Deorham</a> (577), though the accuracy of the entries in the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i> for this period has been questioned. These conquests are often said by modern writers, on no clear evidence, to have separated the Britons of <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a> (known later as the West Welsh) from those of Wales. (Just after the period being discussed, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Chester" title="Battle of Chester">Battle of Chester</a> in 611 might have separated the latter from those of the north of England.) Until the 570s, the Britons were still in control of about half of England and Wales.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingdoms">Kingdoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Kingdoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:343px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Britain.circa.540.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Britain.circa.540.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Britain.circa.540.jpg/341px-Britain.circa.540.jpg" width="341" height="469" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Britain.circa.540.jpg/512px-Britain.circa.540.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Britain.circa.540.jpg/682px-Britain.circa.540.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2046" data-file-height="2814" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Britain.circa.540.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (Celtic people)">British</a> kingdoms existed at some point in the period. id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Officially the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> was <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> at the start of the 5th century, but there is evidence of rural <a href="/wiki/Pagan" class="mw-redirect" title="Pagan">pagan</a> temples being refurbished at the start of this period in western England. However, most temples seem to have been replaced eventually by <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Christian churches</a> on the same site or nearby. "Celtic" churches or monasteries seem to have flourished during this period in the British areas, such as that at <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury" title="Glastonbury">Glastonbury</a>, though mostly not until the 6th century; but the "Saxons" were pagan. This reinforced a great antipathy between the peoples. Many Roman cemeteries continued into much later times, such as that at <a href="/wiki/Cannington,_Somerset" title="Cannington, Somerset">Cannington, Somerset</a>. In the east there was a gradual transition among the pagan Saxons from cremation to <a href="/wiki/Inhumation" class="mw-redirect" title="Inhumation">inhumation</a>. Although the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Canterbury" title="Augustine of Canterbury">Saint Augustine</a> is traditionally seen as the significant Christianising event for the Saxons, a bishop had already arrived in Kent with the king\'s <a href="/wiki/Merovingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> wife. Other Saxons remained pagan after this time.\n</p><p>In 429, a British Deacon, <a href="/wiki/Palladius_(bishop_of_Ireland)" title="Palladius (bishop of Ireland)">Palladius</a>, had requested support from the Pope in Rome to combat <a href="/wiki/Pelagianism" title="Pelagianism">Pelagianism</a>. Bishops <a href="/wiki/Germanus_of_Auxerre" title="Germanus of Auxerre">Germanus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lupus_of_Troyes" title="Lupus of Troyes">Lupus of Troyes</a> were sent. Germanus, a former military commander, is reported to have led the British to the "Hallelujah" victory, possibly in Wales or Herefordshire. Germanus is said<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wood_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> to have made a second visit to England later. Participation by a British bishop at a synod in Gaul demonstrates that at least some British churches were in full administrative and doctrinal touch with Gaul as late as 455.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the north, <a href="/wiki/Whithorn" title="Whithorn">Whithorn</a> is said to be the earliest church in Scotland, being founded in 397 by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ninian" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Ninian">Saint Ninian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ceretic_Guletic" title="Ceretic Guletic">Coroticus (or Ceretic)</a> was a Christian king who was the recipient of the letter from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a>. His base may have been Dumbarton Rock on the River Clyde, and his descendant <a href="/wiki/Rhydderch_Hael" title="Rhydderch Hael">Rhydderch Hael</a> is named in the <i>Life of Saint Columba</i>. Rhydderch was a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ed%C3%A1n_mac_Gabr%C3%A1in" title="\xc3\x81ed\xc3\xa1n mac Gabr\xc3\xa1in">\xc3\x81ed\xc3\xa1n mac Gabr\xc3\xa1in</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dal_Riata" class="mw-redirect" title="Dal Riata">Dal Riata</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urien" title="Urien">Urien</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a> in the late 6th century, as well as of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelfrith_of_Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x86thelfrith of Northumbria">\xc3\x86thelfrith</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bernicia</a>. Unlike Columba, <a href="/wiki/Kentigern" class="mw-redirect" title="Kentigern">Kentigern</a>, the supposed apostle to the Britons of the Clyde and alleged founder of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, is a shadowy figure.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Saxon_migration">Anglo-Saxon migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon helmet">Sutton Hoo helmet</a>, 7th century</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_evidence">Linguistic evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Linguistic evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Celtic_language-death_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic language-death in England">Celtic language-death in England</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguistics</a> is useful in the analysis of culture, and to an extent political associations.\n<a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum</a></i> (completed in 731) wrote that "currently, [there are in Britain] the languages of five peoples, namely that of the Angles (<a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">English</a>), the Britons (<a href="/wiki/Old_Welsh" title="Old Welsh">Welsh</a>), the Scots (<a href="/wiki/Old_Gaelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Gaelic">Gaelic</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Pictish_language" title="Pictish language">Picts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Latins</a>" (<i>HE</i> 1.1).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\nA review of the changes in the <a href="/wiki/British_language_(Celtic)" class="mw-redirect" title="British language (Celtic)">Brythonic language</a> during this period is given by <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_H._Jackson" title="Kenneth H. Jackson">Kenneth H. Jackson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Studies of <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">P-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Q-Celtic</a> and Latin have provided evidence for contact among the Britons, the Gaels, and the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a>. The consensus is that Old English has little evidence of linguistic contact. Some scholars have suggested that there is more evidence in the grammar than in the <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a>, though this is challenged by many.<sup id="cite_ref-roberts_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roberts-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ecec_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ecec-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gelderen_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gelderen-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Latin continued to be used for writing but the extent of its use for speech has been much disputed.\n</p><p>Similarly, studies of place names give clues about the linguistic history of an area. England (except <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>) shows patchy evidence now of <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> in its place names. There are scattered Celtic place names throughout, increasing towards the west. There are also Celtic river names and topographical names. An explanation of the toponymic and linguistic evidence is that Anglo-Saxon language and culture became dominant due to their political and social preeminence in the south and east of Britain. Names with a Latin element may suggest continuity of settlement, while some places are named for pagan Germanic deities. Names of British origin may or may not indicate survival of a British population. Names based on the Anglo-Saxon word for the British, <i>wealh</i>, are also taken as indicating British survival. An example is Walton, meaning settlement of the British<sup id="cite_ref-Hamerow_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamerow-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and this name is found in many parts of England, though it sometimes means <i>Wall-town</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Surviving inscriptions on stones provide another source of information on the settlements of Britons and the Anglo-Saxons. Celtic inscribed stones from this period occur in western England, Wales and southern Scotland, and the CISP project has been set up to record these and provide information online. Later inscriptions in some areas are written in <a href="/wiki/Runes" title="Runes">runes</a> and provide information on the settlement of Angles. Inscriptions in parts of Scotland, Wales, and Cornwall, are in <a href="/wiki/Ogham" title="Ogham">ogham</a>, some containing forms which scholars have not been able to understand.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent_of_the_migrations">Extent of the migrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Extent of the migrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Two contrasting models of the end of sub-Roman Britain have been described by Richard Reece as "decline and immigration" and "invasion and displacement".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> It has long been held that the Anglo-Saxons migrated to Britain in large numbers in the 5th and 6th centuries, substantially displacing the British people. The Anglo-Saxon historian <a href="/wiki/Frank_Stenton" title="Frank Stenton">Frank Stenton</a> in 1943, although making considerable allowance for British survival, essentially sums up this view, arguing "that the greater part of southern England was overrun in the first phase of the war".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This interpretation was based on the written sources, particularly <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> but also the later sources such as the Anglo-Saxon historian <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>, that cast the arrival of the Anglo-Saxons as a violent event. The toponymic and linguistic evidence was also considered to support this interpretation, as very few British place names survived in eastern Britain, very few <a href="/wiki/British_language_(Celtic)" class="mw-redirect" title="British language (Celtic)">British Celtic</a> words entered the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> language and the <a href="/wiki/British_language_(Celtic)" class="mw-redirect" title="British language (Celtic)">Brythonic language</a> and peoples migrated from south-western Britain to <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a>, which eventually became <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. This interpretation particularly appealed to earlier English historians, who wanted to further their view that England had developed differently from Europe, with a limited monarchy and love of liberty. This, it was argued, came from the mass Anglo-Saxon invasions. While this view was never universal&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon" title="Edward Gibbon">Edward Gibbon</a> believed that there had been a great deal of British survival&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 it was the dominant paradigm. Though many scholars would now employ this argument<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, the traditional view is still held by many other historians, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_James" title="Lawrence James">Lawrence James</a> writing in 2002 that England was "submerged by an Anglo-Saxon current which swept away the Romano-British."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The traditional view has been partly deconstructed (considerably in some circles) since the 1990s, with a reduction in the numbers of Anglo-Saxons believed to have arrived in Britain. A lower figure is sometimes accepted, which would mean that it is highly unlikely that the existing British population was substantially displaced by the Anglo-Saxons.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> If fewer Anglo-Saxons arrived, it is proposed that they formed a ruling elite, with acculturisation of the local population. Thus some "Saxon" graves may be of Britons, though many scholars disagree.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_Roman_Britain">End of Roman Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: End of Roman Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/End_of_Roman_rule_in_Britain" title="End of Roman rule in Britain">End of Roman rule in Britain</a></div>\n<p>Various dates have been proposed to mark the end of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>, including the end of <a href="/wiki/Roman_currency" title="Roman currency">Roman currency</a> coinage importation in 402, <a href="/wiki/Constantine_III_(usurper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine III (usurper)">Constantine III</a>\'s rebellion in 407, the rebellion mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Zosimus" title="Zosimus">Zosimus</a> in 409, and the Rescript of <a href="/wiki/Honorius_(emperor)" title="Honorius (emperor)">Honorius</a> in 410.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Unlike modern <a href="/wiki/Decolonisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Decolonisation">decolonisation</a>, the dating of the end of Roman Britain is complex, and the exact process is unknown.\n</p><p>There is some controversy as to why Roman rule ended in Britain. The view first advocated by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Mommsen" title="Wolfgang Mommsen">Wolfgang Mommsen</a> was that Rome left Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> This argument was substantiated over time, most recently by A.S. Esmonde-Cleary.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> According to this argument, internal turmoil in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> and the need to withdraw troops to fight off barbarian armies led Rome to abandon Britain. It was the collapse of the imperial system that led to the end of imperial rule in Britain. However, Michael Jones has advanced an alternative thesis that argues that Rome did not leave Britain, but that Britain left Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> He highlights the numerous <a href="/wiki/Usurper" title="Usurper">usurpers</a> who came from Britain in the late 4th and early 5th centuries, and points out that the supply of coinage to Britain had dried up by the early 5th century, so that administrators and troops were not getting paid. All of this, he argues, led the British people to rebel against Rome. Both of these arguments are open to criticism, and the question is still open.\n</p><p>It was a violent period, and there was probably widespread tension, alluded to in all the written sources. This may have led to the deaths of many Britons. There are also references to plagues. Laycock (<i>Britannia the Failed State</i>, 2008) suggests tribal conflict, possibly even starting before 410, may have sliced up much of Britain and helped destroy the economy. The evidence from land use suggests a decline in production, which might be a sign of population decline.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is clear that some British people migrated to Europe, and <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a> in northwest <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> became known as <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. There is also evidence of British migration to <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a>. The dates of these migrations are uncertain, but recent studies suggest that the migration from south western Britain to Brittany may have begun as early as 300 and had largely ended by 500. These settlers, unlikely to be refugees if the date was this early, made their presence felt in the naming of the westernmost, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>-facing provinces of Armorica, Kerne/Cornouaille ("Kernow/<a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>") and Domnonea ("<a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> However, there is clear linguistic evidence for close contact between the south west of Britain and Brittany across the sub-Roman period.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, in the north west corner of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberian peninsula">Iberian peninsula</a>, another region of traditional <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> culture, the <a href="/wiki/Suebian" class="mw-redirect" title="Suebian">Suebian</a> <i>Parochiale</i>, drawn up about 580, includes a list of the principal churches of each diocese in the metropolitanate of <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a> (the <i>ecclesia Britonensis</i>, now <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breto%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Breto\xc3\xb1a (page does not exist)">Breto\xc3\xb1a</a>), which was the seat of a bishop who ministered to the spiritual needs of the British immigrants to northwestern Spain: in 572 the bishop, Mailoc, had a Celtic name.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The settlers had brought their <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Christianity</a> with them but finally accepted the Latin Rite at the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_Toledo" title="Fourth Council of Toledo">Council of Toledo</a> in 633. The diocese stretched from <a href="/wiki/Ferrol,_A_Coru%C3%B1a" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrol, A Coru\xc3\xb1a">Ferrol</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Eo_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Eo River">Eo River</a>. In Spain, the area has sometimes been dubbed "the third Britain" or "the last Britain".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Non-Anglo-Saxon kingdoms began appearing in western Britain, and are first referred to in Gildas\' <i>De Excidio</i>. To an extent these kingdoms may have derived from Roman structures.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> But it is also clear that they drew on a strong influence from <a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a>, which was never part of the Roman Empire. Archaeology has helped further the study of these kingdoms, notably at sites like <a href="/wiki/Tintagel" title="Tintagel">Tintagel</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Hillfort" title="Hillfort">hillfort</a> at <a href="/wiki/South_Cadbury" title="South Cadbury">South Cadbury</a>.\n</p><p>In the north there developed the British kingdoms of the <a href="/wiki/Hen_Ogledd" title="Hen Ogledd">Hen Ogledd</a>, the "Old North", comprising <a href="/wiki/Ebrauc" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebrauc">Ebrauc</a> (probable name), <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bryneich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strathclyde" title="Strathclyde">Strathclyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elmet" title="Elmet">Elmet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gododdin" title="Gododdin">Gododdin</a>. 5th- and 6th-century repairs along <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a> have been uncovered, and at <a href="/wiki/Whithorn" title="Whithorn">Whithorn</a> in south western <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> (possibly the site of <a href="/wiki/Ninian" title="Ninian">Ninian</a>\'s monastery). Chance discoveries have helped document the continuing urban occupation of some Roman towns such as <a href="/wiki/Wroxeter" title="Wroxeter">Wroxeter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caerwent" title="Caerwent">Caerwent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Continued urban use might be associated with an <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">ecclesiastical</a> structure.\n</p><p>Western Britain has attracted those archaeologists who wish to place <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> as a historical figure.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Though there is little contemporary written evidence for this, archaeological evidence does suggest that a <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> king might have wielded considerable power during the sub-Roman period, as demonstrated by the creation of sites such as <a href="/wiki/Tintagel" title="Tintagel">Tintagel</a> and earthworks such as the <a href="/wiki/Wansdyke_(earthwork)" title="Wansdyke (earthwork)">Wansdyke</a>. Such interpretations continue to attract the popular imagination and the scepticism of academics.\n</p><p>While pushed back politically and linguistically, British scholars and ecclesiastics had a significant impact on the Anglo-Saxon newcomers through literacy, ecclesiastical social constructs and historical memory of the Roman period in Britain, particularly after the Christianizing of the Anglo-Saxons. Coming from a fully oral cultural background, the Anglo-Saxons were heavily influenced by the more developed Christianized and literate culture of the Britons. British scholars were often employed at Anglo-Saxon courts to assist in the management of the kingdoms. This reintroduced <a href="/wiki/British_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="British culture">British culture</a> to those parts of Britain lost to the British politically. The epitome of this process is the adoption of the legendary British war leader, <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>, as the national hero of the English, due to the literary work of Welsh historians.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_change_effects">Environmental change effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental change effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is evidence for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> in the 5th century, with conditions turning cooler and wetter. This shortened the growing season and made uplands unsuited to growing <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">Dendrochronology</a> reveals a particular <a href="/wiki/Climate_changes_of_535%E2%80%93536" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate changes of 535\xe2\x80\x93536">climatic event in 540</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Michael Jones suggests that declining agricultural production from land that was already fully exploited had considerable demographic consequences.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_fluctuation">Population fluctuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Population fluctuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_for_reduction">Theories for reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Theories for reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Slaves were important in the economy and the army in the Roman Empire. Estimates for the prevalence of slavery in the Roman Empire vary: some estimate that around 30% of the population of the Empire in the 1st century was enslaved.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\nA more recent study suggests 10-15% even for the early empire "as any greater estimate would require implausible levels of transformation in a pre-modern context."<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The difference the lower percentage in the Later Roman Empire can be attributed to fewer slaves in sub elite households and agricultural estates<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> (replaced by a great expansion in various types of tenancy).<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The Germanic region was one of the main sources of slaves. It was mainly wholesale dealers, who followed the Roman armies, who sold slaves. After the Empire expanded, there were fewer places to obtain slaves. Around 210, <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a> increased around the North Sea and boosted the supply, taken from villages in that area, along with those captured for ransom.  \n</p><p>Britain was not easily defensible. It did not pay completely the costs of occupation. Nevertheless the Romans were forced to keep 3 or 4 legions, 30-40,000 men with auxiliary units in place to defend it. They managed fairly well until the collapse of Roman authority after the garrison was reduced in size by Maximus in 388 and Stilicho in 401. It seems that post-350 the Roman government was having more difficulty in recruiting soldiers. \n</p><p>In an effort to remedy the situation it resorted to payment instead of provision of recruit, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurum_tironicum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aurum tironicum (page does not exist)">aurum tironicum</a>.  Landowners could pay a set fee to prevent any of their tenants from being pressed into the army (slaves were rarely resorted to even at critical moments in exchange for their freedom).<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Not enough men wanted to enter military service.  The gold from the tax led to a greater use of Germanic or other tribal groups who did not need to be expensively equipped, housed and paid pensions, as the tax was used to recruit mercenaries as Foederati, but it also drained the Treasury. Previously foreigners were put into units, the <i>auxilia</i>, officered by Romans. Roman army units, the smaller Later Roman legions, continued to exist but gradually disappeared in the 5th century leaving defense of the Empire to hirelings. \n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Adrianople" title="Battle of Adrianople">Battle of Adrianople</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">Foederati</a>, by the Treaty of 382, were allowed to remain with the Empire intact, which reversed the centuries-old Roman policy of destroying barbarian enemies by killing them all, selling them or incorporating them into the Roman army by scattering them across units. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hospitalitas_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hospitalitas system (page does not exist)">Hospitalitas system</a> granted a third of the land (or fees) of a region to barbarians who had invaded and occupied those lands assigned to them. In return, these people declared loyalty to the Emperor and provided military support, whilst retaining their independence. If the theory is correct, Germanic peoples may have been resident in Britain before and after these reforms. One thing led to another to create a spiral. \n</p><p>The policy of substituting mercenaries who were paid in gold which should have gone to support the professional standing army and accommodation to their presence spelled the doom of the Western Empire. The <i>federates,</i> operating from within the boundaries of the Empire, eventually became the new landlords, as there was no professional Roman army to subdue them. \n</p><p>Ancient authors bitterly comment on this series of policy mistakes. The East which relied far less on mercenaries escaped Britain\'s fate. The Britons in the decades post-410 repeated the mistakes the emperors had done with the Visigoths, Burgundians, Sueves, Vandals and Franks - they invited mercenaries to protect them, then those same mercenaries subsequently took control over Britain.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plague_of_Justinian">Plague of Justinian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Plague of Justinian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Justinian" title="Plague of Justinian">Plague of Justinian</a></div>\n<p>The population of Britain may have decreased by between 1.5 and 3 million after the Roman period, perhaps caused by environmental changes (the <a href="/wiki/Late_Antique_Little_Ice_Age" title="Late Antique Little Ice Age">Late Antique Little Ice Age</a>) and, subsequently, by <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> (around 600, the smallpox spread from India into Europe).<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> It is known that the Plague of Justinian entered the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> world in the 6th century and first arrived in the British Isles in 544 or 545, when it reached Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> It is estimated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2012)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that the <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Justinian" title="Plague of Justinian">Plague of Justinian</a> killed as many as 100 million people across the world. As a result, <a href="/wiki/Medieval_demography" title="Medieval demography">Europe\'s population</a> fell by around 50% between 550 and 700.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The later medieval Welsh <a href="/wiki/Lludd_and_Llefelys" title="Lludd and Llefelys">Lludd and Llefelys</a> mentions a series of three plagues affecting the British of London.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Saxon_settlement">Anglo-Saxon settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Anglo-Saxon settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>According to research led by <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> settlers could have enjoyed a substantial social and economic advantage over the native <a href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (Celtic people)">Celtic Britons</a><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> who lived in what is now <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, for more than 300 years from the middle of the 5th century.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> However, this scenario, assuming that the Anglo-Saxons were just Germanic <a href="/wiki/Ingaevones" title="Ingaevones">Ingaevones</a>, has been questioned.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_migrations_theories">Minor migrations theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Minor migrations theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The traditional view about the numbers of Anglo-Saxons arriving in Britain during this period has been deconstructed. Incoming people uniting with the existing population could explain why the island conquest was as slow and incomplete as it was, and why the island was exposed to external attacks by <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danish pirates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>, as well as other issues about the population composition.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Stephen_Oppenheimer.27s_theory"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Stephen_Oppenheimer\'s_theory">Stephen Oppenheimer\'s theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-Roman_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Stephen Oppenheimer&#039;s theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Oppenheimer" title="Stephen Oppenheimer">Stephen Oppenheimer</a>, basing his research on the Weale and Capelli studies, maintains that none of the invasions since the Romans have had a significant impact on the gene pool of the British Isles, and that the inhabitants from prehistoric times belong to an <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberian</a> genetic grouping. He says that most people in the British Isles are genetically similar to the <a href="/wiki/Basque_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque people">Basque people</a> of northern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, from 90% in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> to 66% in <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>. Oppenheimer suggests that the division between the West and the East of England is not due to the Anglo-Saxon invasion but originates with two main routes of genetic flow&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 one up the Atlantic coast, the other from neighbouring areas of Continental Europe&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 which occurred just after the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>. He reports work on linguistics by Forster and Toth which suggests that <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a> began to fragment some 10,000 years ago, at the end of the last Ice Age. He claims that the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a> split from the Indo-European earlier than previously suspected, some 6000 years ago. He claims that the English language split from the other <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> before the Roman period, and became the English that was spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Belgae" title="Belgae">Belgae</a> tribes of what is now southern and eastern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, northeastern France, and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> prior to their conquest by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>, and long before the arrival of the Anglo-Saxon.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Sykes" title="Bryan Sykes">Bryan Sykes</a> came to fairly similar conclusions as Oppenheimer in his research, which he set forth in his 2006 published book <i>Blood of the Isles: Exploring the Genetic Roots of our Tribal History</i>, published in the United States and Canada as <i>Saxons, Vikings and Celts: The Genetic Roots of Britain and Ireland</i>. In its summary of their article "Who were the Celts?", the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_Cardiff" title="National Museum Cardiff">National Museum of Wales</a> note, "It is possible that future genetic studies of ancient and modern human DNA may help to inform our understanding of the subject. However, early studies have, so far, tended to produce implausible conclusions from very small numbers of people and using outdated assumptions about linguistics and archaeology".<sup
