There are also three <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>: <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.  The United Kingdom comprises <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, each <a href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">country</a> having its own history, with all but Northern Ireland having been independent states at one point. The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_formation_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of the formation of the United Kingdom">history of the formation of the United Kingdom</a> is very complex.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">British monarch</a> was <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> of all of the countries of the British Isles from the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Crowns" title="Union of the Crowns">Union of the Crowns</a> in 1603 until the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Ireland Act">Republic of Ireland Act</a> in 1949, although the term "British Isles" was not used in 1603.  Additionally, since the independence of most of Ireland, historians of the region often avoid the term <i>British Isles</i> due to the complexity of relations between the peoples of the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> (see: <i><a href="/wiki/Terminology_of_the_British_Isles" title="Terminology of the British Isles">Terminology of the British Prehistoric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">Prehistoric Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Ireland" title="Prehistoric Ireland">Prehistoric Ireland</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeolithic_and_Mesolithic_periods">Palaeolithic and Mesolithic periods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Palaeolithic and Mesolithic periods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Palaeolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeolithic">Palaeolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a>, also known as the Old and Middle Stone Ages, were characterised by a <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> economy and a reliance on stone tool technologies.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeolithic">Palaeolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Palaeolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">Lower Palaeolithic period in the British Isles</a> saw the region\'s first known habitation by early hominids, specifically the extinct <a href="/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis" title="Homo heidelbergensis">Homo heidelbergensis</a>.\n</p><p>One of the most prominent archaeological sites dating to this period is that of <a href="/wiki/Boxgrove_Quarry" class="mw-redirect" title="Boxgrove Quarry">Boxgrove Quarry</a> in West Sussex, southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Mesolithic_.2810.2C000_to_4.2C500_BC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesolithic_(10,000_to_4,500_BC)">Mesolithic (10,000 to 4,500 BC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mesolithic (10,000 to 4,500 BC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>By the Mesolithic, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i>, or modern humans, were the only hominid species to still survive in the British Isles.\nBritish Isles were linked to continental Europe by a territory named <a href="/wiki/Doggerland" title="Doggerland">Doggerland</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Neolithic_and_Bronze_Ages_.284500_to_600BC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic_and_Bronze_Ages_(4500_to_600BC)">Neolithic and Bronze Ages (4500 to 600BC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neolithic and Bronze Ages (4500 to 600BC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_British_Isles" title="Neolithic British Isles">Neolithic British Isles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age Britain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronze Age Ireland">Bronze Age Ireland</a></div>\n<p>In the British Isles, the Neolithic and Bronze Ages saw the transformation of British and Irish society and landscape. It saw the adoption of agriculture, as communities gave up their hunter-gatherer modes of existence to begin farming.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Iron_Age_.281200BC_to_600AD.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age_(1200BC_to_600AD)">Iron Age (1200BC to 600AD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iron Age (1200BC to 600AD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">British Iron Age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Iron Age">Irish Iron Age</a></div>\n<p>As its name suggests, the British Iron Age is also characterised by the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, a metal which was used to produce a variety of different tools, ornaments and weapons.\n</p><p>In the course of the first millennium BC, and possibly earlier, some combination of <a href="/wiki/Trans-cultural_diffusion" title="Trans-cultural diffusion">trans-cultural diffusion</a> and immigration from continental Europe resulted in the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a> in the islands, eventually giving rise to the <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic</a> group. What languages were spoken in the islands before is unknown, though they are assumed to have been <a href="/wiki/Pre-Indo-European_languages" title="Pre-Indo-European languages">Pre-Indo-European</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_period">Classical period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Classical period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire" title="Scotland during the Roman Empire">Scotland during the Roman Empire</a></div>\n<p>In 55 and 54 BC, Roman general and future dictator <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Caesar" title="Gaius Julius Caesar">Gaius Julius Caesar</a> launched two <a href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_invasions_of_Britain" title="Caesar&#39;s invasions of Britain">separate invasions</a> of the British Isles, though neither resulted in a full Roman occupation of the island.\n</p><p>From AD 40 to about AD 410, southern Britain was <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">a part of the Roman Empire</a>, with archaeologists referring to this area as "Roman Britain", and this time span the "<a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romano-British</a> period" or the "Roman Iron Age".\n</p><p>In and around the same time period, Ireland was being invaded and settled by <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaels</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_period">Medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Medieval_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval England">Medieval England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval Scotland">Medieval Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval Wales">Medieval Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Early_medieval_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Early medieval Ireland">Early medieval Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Late_medieval_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Late medieval Ireland">Late medieval Ireland</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_medieval">Early medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Early medieval period saw a series of <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">invasions of Britain</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>, beginning in the 5th century. Anglo-Saxon kingdoms were formed and, through wars with British states, gradually came to cover the territory of present-day England. Around 600, seven principal kingdoms had emerged, beginning the so-called period of the <a href="/wiki/Heptarchy" title="Heptarchy">Heptarchy</a>. During that period, the Anglo-Saxon states were <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Christianity" title="Anglo-Saxon Christianity">Christianised</a> (the conversion of the British ones had begun much earlier). In the 9th century, <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> from <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">conquered most of England</a>. Only the Kingdom of Wessex under <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a> survived and even managed to re-conquer and unify England for much of the 10th century, before a new series of Danish raids in the late 10th century and early 11th century culminated in the wholesale subjugation of England to Denmark under <a href="/wiki/Canute_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Canute the Great">Canute the Great</a>. Danish rule was overthrown and the local House of Wessex was restored to power under <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> for about two decades until his death in 1066.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Medieval">Late Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Late Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_WillelmDux.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Bayeux_Tapestry_WillelmDux.jpg/300px-Bayeux_Tapestry_WillelmDux.jpg" width="300" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Bayeux_Tapestry_WillelmDux.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="401" data-file-height="291" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_WillelmDux.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry" title="Bayeux Tapestry">Bayeux Tapestry</a> depicting events leading to the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a>, which defined much of the subsequent history of the British Isles</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1066, <a href="/wiki/William,_Duke_of_Normandy" class="mw-redirect" title="William, Duke of Normandy">William, Duke of Normandy</a> said he was the rightful heir to the English throne, invaded England, and defeated King <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold II</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a>. Proclaiming himself to be King William I, he strengthened his regime by appointing loyal members of the Norman elite to many positions of authority, building a system of castles across the country and ordering a census of his new kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a>. The Late Medieval period was characterised by many battles between England and France, coming to a head in the Hundred Years\' War from which France emerged victorious. The monarchs throughout the Late Medieval period belonged to the houses of Plantaganet, Lancaster and York.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: (1691\xe2\x80\x931801)</a></div>\n<p>Major historical events in the early modern period include the <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance" title="English Renaissance">English Renaissance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Scottish Reformation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, the Restoration of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a> and the formation of the <a href="/wiki/First_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="First British Empire">First British Empire</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Georgian_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian society">Georgian society</a></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Union_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland">Union of Great Britain and Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Union of Great Britain and Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> was a settler state; the monarch was the incumbent monarch of England and later of Great Britain. The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</a> headed the government on behalf of the monarch. He was assisted by the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Secretary_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Secretary of Ireland">Chief Secretary of Ireland</a>. Both were responsible to the government in London rather than to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Parliament of Ireland</a>. Before the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_1782" title="Constitution of 1782">Constitution of 1782</a>, the Irish parliament was also <a href="/wiki/Poynings%27_Law" title="Poynings&#39; Law">severely fettered</a>, and decisions in Irish courts could be overturned on appeal to the British <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> in London.\n</p><p>Ireland gained a degree of independence in the 1780s thanks to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Grattan" title="Henry Grattan">Henry Grattan</a>. During this time the effects of the <a href="/wiki/Penal_laws_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Penal laws (Ireland)">penal laws</a> on the primarily Roman Catholic population were reduced, and some property-owning Catholics were granted the franchise in 1794; however, they were still excluded from becoming members of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Commons" title="Irish House of Commons">Irish House of Commons</a>. This brief period of limited independence came to an end following the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1798" title="Irish Rebellion of 1798">Irish Rebellion of 1798</a>, which occurred during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">British war with revolutionary France</a>. The British government\'s fear of an independent Ireland siding against them with the French resulted in the decision to unite the two countries. This was brought about by <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">legislation in the parliaments of both kingdoms</a> and came into effect on 1 January 1801. The Irish had been led to believe by the British that their loss of legislative independence would be compensated for with <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Emancipation" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic Emancipation">Catholic Emancipation</a>, i.e. by the removal of <a href="/wiki/Civil_disabilities_placed_upon_Roman_Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil disabilities placed upon Roman Catholics">civil disabilities placed upon Roman Catholics</a> in both Great Britain and Ireland. However, <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">King George III</a> was bitterly opposed to any such Emancipation and succeeded in defeating his government\'s attempts to introduce it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Napoleonic_Wars">Napoleonic Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Napoleonic Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a></div>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Second_Coalition" title="War of the Second Coalition">War of the Second Coalition</a> (1799\xe2\x80\x931801), Britain occupied most of the French and Dutch overseas possessions, the Netherlands having become a satellite state of France in 1796, but <a href="/wiki/Tropical_diseases" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical diseases">tropical diseases</a> claimed the lives of over 40,000 troops. When the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Amiens" title="Treaty of Amiens">Treaty of Amiens</a> ended the war, Britain agreed to return most of the territories it had seized. The peace settlement was in effect only a ceasefire, and Napoleon continued to provoke the British by attempting a trade <a href="/wiki/Embargo" class="mw-redirect" title="Embargo">embargo</a> on the country and by occupying the city of <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, capital of the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Electorate of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">Electorate</a>, a German-speaking duchy which was in a <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> with the United Kingdom. In May 1803, war was declared again. <a href="/wiki/Napoleon%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon&#39;s">Napoleon\'s</a> plans to invade Britain failed, chiefly due to the inferiority of his navy, and in 1805 Lord Nelson\'s <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> fleet decisively defeated the French and Spanish at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Trafalgar</a>, ending any hopes Napoleon had to wrest control of the oceans away from the British.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg/220px-Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg/330px-Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg/440px-Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="897" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trafalgar-Auguste_Mayer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The British <a href="/wiki/HMS_Sandwich_(1759)" title="HMS Sandwich (1759)">HMS <i>Sandwich</i></a> fires to the French <a href="/wiki/Flagship" title="Flagship">flagship</a> <i><a href="/wiki/French_ship_Bucentaure_(1803)" class="mw-redirect" title="French ship Bucentaure (1803)">Bucentaure</a></i> (completely dismasted) into battle off <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Trafalgar</a>. The <i>Bucentaure</i> also fights <a href="/wiki/HMS_Victory" title="HMS Victory">HMS <i>Victory</i></a> (behind her) and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Temeraire_(1798)" title="HMS Temeraire (1798)">HMS <i>Temeraire</i></a> (left side of the picture). In fact, HMS <i>Sandwich</i> never fought at Trafalgar, it is a mistake from <a href="/wiki/Auguste_%C3%89tienne_Fran%C3%A7ois_Mayer" title="Auguste \xc3\x89tienne Fran\xc3\xa7ois Mayer">Auguste Mayer</a>, the painter.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1806, Napoleon issued the series of <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Decree" title="Berlin Decree">Berlin Decrees</a>, which brought into effect the <a href="/wiki/Continental_System" title="Continental System">Continental System</a>. This policy aimed to eliminate the threat from the British by closing French-controlled territory to foreign trade. The <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> remained a minimal threat to France; it maintained a standing strength of just 220,000 men at the height of the Napoleonic Wars, whereas France\'s armies exceeded a million men\xe2\x80\x94in addition to the armies of numerous allies and several hundred thousand <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_(France)" title="National Guard (France)">national guardsmen</a> that Napoleon could draft into the French armies when they were needed. Although the Royal Navy effectively disrupted France\'s extra-continental trade\xe2\x80\x94both by seizing and threatening French shipping and by seizing French colonial possessions\xe2\x80\x94it could do nothing about France\'s trade with  the major continental economies and posed little threat to French territory in Europe. France\'s population and agricultural capacity far outstripped that of Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Top French  leaders argued that cutting the British off from the European mainland would end their economic hegemony, but the United Kingdom possessed the greatest industrial capacity in the world, and its mastery of the seas allowed it to build up considerable economic strength through trade to its possessions from its rapidly expanding new Empire. In terms of economic damage to Britain, the blockade was largely ineffective. As Napoleon realized that extensive trade was going through Spain and Russia, he invaded those two countries. He tied down his forces in Spain, and lost very badly in Russia in 1812.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Spanish uprising in 1808 at last permitted Britain to gain a foothold on the Continent. The Duke of Wellington and his army of British and Portuguese gradually pushed the French out of Spain, and in early 1814, as Napoleon was being driven back in the east by the Prussians, Austrians, and Russians, Wellington invaded southern France. After Napoleon\'s surrender and exile to the island of Elba, peace appeared to have returned, but when he escaped back into France in 1815, the British and their allies had to fight him again. The armies of Wellington and Blucher defeated Napoleon once and for all at Waterloo.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_(1812).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_%281812%29.jpg/220px-Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_%281812%29.jpg" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_%281812%29.jpg/330px-Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_%281812%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_%281812%29.jpg/440px-Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_%281812%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="967" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Signing_of_Treaty_of_Ghent_(1812).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Ghent" title="Treaty of Ghent">Treaty of Ghent</a> With the United States (1814), by A. Forestier</div></div></div>\n<p>Simultaneous with the Napoleonic Wars, trade disputes and British impressment of American sailors led to the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> with the United States. A central event in American history, it was little noticed in Britain, where all attention was focused on the struggle with France. The British could devote few resources to the conflict until the fall of Napoleon in 1814. American frigates also inflicted a series of embarrassing defeats on the British navy, which was short on manpower due to the conflict in Europe.\n</p><p>A stepped-up war effort that year brought about some successes such as the burning of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, but the Duke of Wellington argued that an outright victory over the U.S. was impossible because the Americans controlled the western Great Lakes and had destroyed the power of Britain\'s Indian allies. A full-scale British invasion was defeated in upstate New York. Peace was agreed to at the end of 1814, but unaware of this, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a> won a great victory over the British at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_New_Orleans" title="Battle of New Orleans">Battle of New Orleans</a> in January 1815 (news took several weeks to cross the Atlantic before the advent of steam ships). The Treaty of Ghent subsequently ended the war with no territorial changes.  It was the last war between Britain and the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="George_IV_and_William_IV">George IV and William IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: George IV and William IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Britain emerged from the Napoleonic Wars a very different country than it had been in 1793. As industrialisation progressed, society changed, becoming more urban and less rural. The postwar period saw an economic slump, and poor harvests and inflation caused widespread social unrest. Europe after 1815 was on guard against a return of Jacobinism, and even liberal Britain saw the passage of the Six Acts in 1819, which proscribed radical activities. By the end of the 1820s, along with a general economic recovery, many of these repressive laws were repealed  and in 1828 new legislation guaranteed the civil rights of religious dissenters.\n</p><p>A weak ruler as regent (1811\xe2\x80\x9320) and king (1820\xe2\x80\x9330), George IV let his ministers take full charge of government affairs, playing a far lesser role than his father, George III. His governments, with little help from the king, presided over victory in the Napoleonic Wars, negotiated the peace settlement, and attempted to deal with the social and economic malaise that followed.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His brother <a href="/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="William IV of the United Kingdom">William IV</a> ruled (1830\xe2\x80\x9337), but was little involved in politics. His reign saw several reforms: the <a href="/wiki/Poor_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Poor law">poor law</a> was updated, <a href="/wiki/Child_labour" title="Child labour">child labour</a> restricted, <a href="/wiki/Slavery_Abolition_Act_1833" title="Slavery Abolition Act 1833">slavery abolished</a> in nearly all the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, and, most important, the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a> refashioned the British electoral system.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There were no major wars until the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> (1853\xe2\x80\x9356).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> While Prussia, Austria, and Russia, as absolute monarchies, tried to suppress liberalism wherever it might occur, the British came to terms with new ideas. Britain intervened in Portugal in 1826 to defend a constitutional government there and recognising the independence of Spain\'s American colonies in 1824.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> British merchants and financiers, and later railway builders, played major roles in the economies of most Latin American nations.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Whig_reforms_of_the_1830s">Whig reforms of the 1830s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Whig reforms of the 1830s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Whig_(British_political_party)" class="mw-redirect" title="Whig (British political party)">Whig Party</a> recovered its strength and unity by supporting moral reforms, especially the reform of the electoral system, the abolition of slavery and emancipation of the Catholics. <a href="/wiki/Catholic_emancipation" title="Catholic emancipation">Catholic emancipation</a> was secured in the Catholic Relief Act of 1829, which removed the most substantial restrictions on Roman Catholics in Great Britain and Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-L._Woodward,_1938_pp_325\xe2\x80\x9330_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._Woodward,_1938_pp_325\xe2\x80\x9330-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Whigs became champions of Parliamentary reform. They made <a href="/wiki/Charles_Grey,_2nd_Earl_Grey" title="Charles Grey, 2nd Earl Grey">Lord Grey</a> prime minister 1830\xe2\x80\x931834, and the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act of 1832</a> became their signature measure. It broadened the franchise and ended the system of "rotten borough" and "pocket boroughs" (where elections were controlled by powerful families), and instead redistributed power on the basis of population. It added 217,000 voters to an electorate of 435,000 in England and Wales. The main effect of the act was to weaken the power of the landed gentry, and enlarge the power of the professional and business middle-class, which now for the first time had a significant voice in Parliament. However, the great majority of manual workers, clerks, and farmers did not have enough property to qualify to vote. The aristocracy continued to dominate the government, the Army and Royal Navy, and high society.<sup id="cite_ref-L._Woodward,_1938_pp_325\xe2\x80\x9330_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._Woodward,_1938_pp_325\xe2\x80\x9330-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> After parliamentary investigations demonstrated the horrors of child labour, limited reforms were passed in 1833.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartism</a> emerged after the 1832 Reform Bill failed to give the vote  to the working class. Activists denounced the "betrayal" of the working classes and the "sacrificing" of their "interests" by the "misconduct" of the government. In 1838, Chartists issued the People\'s Charter demanding manhood suffrage, equal sized election districts, voting by ballots, payment of Members of Parliament (so that poor men could serve), annual Parliaments, and abolition of property requirements. The <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling class</a> saw the movement as pathological,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> so the Chartists were unable to force serious constitutional debate. Historians see Chartism as both a continuation of the 18th century fight against corruption and as a new stage in demands for democracy in an industrial society.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 1832 Parliament abolished slavery in the Empire with the <a href="/wiki/Slavery_Abolition_Act_1833" title="Slavery Abolition Act 1833">Slavery Abolition Act 1833</a>. The government purchased the slaves for \xc2\xa320,000,000 (the money went to rich plantation owners who mostly lived in England), and freed the slaves, especially those in the Caribbean sugar islands.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Prime Ministers of the period included: <a href="/wiki/William_Pitt_the_Younger" title="William Pitt the Younger">William Pitt the Younger</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Wyndham_Grenville,_1st_Baron_Grenville" class="mw-redirect" title="William Wyndham Grenville, 1st Baron Grenville">Lord Grenville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Portland" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Portland">Duke of Portland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Perceval" title="Spencer Perceval">Spencer Perceval</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Jenkinson,_2nd_Earl_of_Liverpool" title="Robert Jenkinson, 2nd Earl of Liverpool">Lord Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Canning" title="George Canning">George Canning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_John_Robinson,_1st_Viscount_Goderich" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick John Robinson, 1st Viscount Goderich">Lord Goderich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Duke of Wellington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Grey,_2nd_Earl_Grey" title="Charles Grey, 2nd Earl Grey">Lord Grey</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Lamb,_2nd_Viscount_Melbourne" title="William Lamb, 2nd Viscount Melbourne">Lord Melbourne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Peel" title="Robert Peel">Sir Robert Peel</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Victorian_era">Victorian era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Victorian era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg/200px-Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg" width="200" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg/300px-Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg/400px-Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2729" data-file-height="3851" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Queen_Victoria_by_Bassano.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Queen Victoria (1837\xe2\x80\x931901)</div></div></div>\n<p>The Victorian era was the  period of <a href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria\'s</a> rule between 1837 and 1901 which signified the height of the British <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> and the apex of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. Scholars debate whether the Victorian period\xe2\x80\x94as defined by a variety of sensibilities and political concerns that have come to be associated with the Victorians\xe2\x80\x94actually begins with the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a>. The era was preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Regency_era" title="Regency era">Regency era</a> and succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Edwardian period">Edwardian period</a>. Victoria became queen in 1837 at age 18. Her long reign saw Britain reach the zenith of its economic and political power, with the introduction of steam ships, railroads, photography, and the telegraph. Britain again remained mostly inactive in Continental politics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_trade_imperialism">Free trade imperialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Free trade imperialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Great London Exhibition of 1851 clearly demonstrated Britain\'s dominance in engineering and industry; that lasted until the rise of the United States and Germany in the 1890s. Using the imperial tools of free trade and financial investment,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> it exerted major influence on many countries outside Europe, especially in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Thus Britain had both a formal Empire based on British rule as well as an informal one based on the British pound.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Russia.2C_France_and_the_Ottoman_Empire"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia,_France_and_the_Ottoman_Empire">Russia, France and the Ottoman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Russia, France and the Ottoman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>One nagging fear was the possible collapse of the Ottoman Empire. It was well understood that a collapse of that country would set off a scramble for its territory and possibly plunge Britain into war. To head that off Britain sought to keep the Russians from occupying Constantinople and taking over the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bosporous_Straits&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bosporous Straits (page does not exist)">Bosporous Straits</a>, as well as from <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Game" title="The Great Game">threatening India via Afghanistan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1853, Britain and France intervened in the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> against Russia. Despite mediocre generalship, they managed to capture the Russian port of <a href="/wiki/Sevastopol" title="Sevastopol">Sevastopol</a>, compelling <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_I_of_Russia" title="Nicholas I of Russia">Tsar Nicholas I</a> to ask for peace.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%9378)" class="mw-redirect" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877\xe2\x80\x9378)">next Russo-Ottoman war</a> in 1877 led to another European intervention, although this time at the negotiating table. The <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Berlin" title="Congress of Berlin">Congress of Berlin</a> blocked Russia from imposing the harsh Treaty of San Stefano on the Ottoman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Despite its alliance with the French in the Crimean War, Britain viewed the Second Empire of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a> with some distrust, especially as the emperor constructed ironclad warships and began returning France to a more active foreign policy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Civil_War">American Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: American Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> (1861\xe2\x80\x931865), British leaders favoured the Confederacy, a major source of cotton for textile mills. Prince Albert was effective in defusing a <a href="/wiki/Trent_Affair" title="Trent Affair">war scare in late 1861</a>. The British people, however, who depended heavily on American food imports, generally favoured the Union. What little cotton was available came from New York, as the blockade by the US Navy shut down 95% of Southern exports to Britain. In September 1862, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> announced the <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a>. Since support of the Confederacy now meant supporting the institution of slavery, there was no possibility of European intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The British sold arms to both sides, built blockade runners for a lucrative trade with the Confederacy, and surreptitiously allowed warships to be built for the Confederacy. The warships caused a major diplomatic row that was resolved in the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Claims" title="Alabama Claims">Alabama Claims</a> in 1872, in the Americans\' favour.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Empire_expands">Empire expands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Empire expands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1867, Britain united most of its <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> colonies as the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominion of Canada">Dominion of Canada</a>, giving it self-government and responsibility for its own defence, but Canada did not have an independent foreign policy until 1931. Several of the colonies temporarily refused to join the Dominion despite pressure from both Canada and Britain; the last one, <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, held out until 1949. The second half of the 19th century saw a <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">huge expansion</a> of Britain\'s colonial empire, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. A talk of the Union Jack flying "from Cairo to Cape Town" only became a reality at the end of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">Great War</a>. Having possessions on six continents, Britain had to defend all of its empire and did so with a volunteer army, the only <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great power</a> in Europe to have no conscription. Some questioned whether the country was overstretched.\n</p><p>The rise of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> since its creation in 1871 posed a new challenge, for it (along with the United States), threatened to usurp Britain\'s place as the world\'s foremost industrial power. Germany acquired a number of colonies in Africa and the Pacific, but Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a> succeeded in achieving general peace through his balance of power strategy. When <a href="/wiki/William_II,_German_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="William II, German Emperor">William II</a> became emperor in 1888, he discarded Bismarck, began using bellicose language, and planned to build a navy to rival Britain\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ever since Britain had wrested control of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a> from the Netherlands during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, it had co-existed with Dutch settlers who had migrated further away from the Cape and created two republics of their own. The British imperial vision called for control over these new countries, and the Dutch-speaking "Boers" (or "Afrikaners") fought back in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">War in 1899\xe2\x80\x931902</a>. Outgunned by a mighty empire, the Boers waged a guerrilla war (which certain other British territories would later employ to attain independence). This gave the British regulars a difficult fight, but their weight of numbers, superior equipment, and often brutal tactics, eventually brought about a British victory. The war had been costly in human rights and was widely criticised by Liberals in Britain and worldwide. However, the United States gave its support. The Boer republics were merged into the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_South_Africa" title="Union of South Africa">Union of South Africa</a> in 1910; this had internal self-government, but its foreign policy was controlled by London and it was an integral part of the British Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland_and_the_move_to_Home_Rule">Ireland and the move to Home Rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Ireland and the move to Home Rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ireland_(1801%E2%80%931922)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Ireland (1801\xe2\x80\x931922)">History of Ireland (1801\xe2\x80\x931922)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Great Famine (Ireland)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_movement" title="Irish Home Rule movement">Irish Home Rule movement</a></div>\n<p>Part of the agreement which led to the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1800">1800 Act of Union</a> stipulated that the Penal Laws in Ireland were to be repealed and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_emancipation" title="Catholic emancipation">Catholic emancipation</a> granted. However King <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="George III of England">George III</a> blocked emancipation, arguing that to grant it would break his <a href="/wiki/Coronation_oath" class="mw-redirect" title="Coronation oath">coronation oath</a> to defend the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Church</a>. A campaign by the lawyer <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O&#39;Connell">Daniel O\'Connell</a>, and the death of George III, led to the concession of Catholic Emancipation in 1829, allowing Roman Catholics to sit in the Parliament of the United Kingdom. But Catholic Emancipation was not O\'Connell\'s ultimate goal, which was Repeal of the Act of Union with Great Britain. On 1 January 1843 O\'Connell confidently, but wrongly, declared that Repeal would be achieved that year. When <a href="/wiki/Potato_blight" class="mw-redirect" title="Potato blight">potato blight</a> hit the island in 1846, much of the rural population was left without food, because <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crops</a> were being exported to pay rents.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>British politicians such as the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Peel" title="Robert Peel">Robert Peel</a> were at this time wedded to the <a href="/wiki/Economic_policy" title="Economic policy">economic policy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">laissez-faire</a>,  which argued against state intervention. While funds were raised by private individuals and charities, lack of adequate action let the problem become a catastrophe. Cottiers (or farm labourers) were largely wiped out during what is known in Ireland as the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Great Hunger</a>". A significant minority elected <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionists</a>, who championed the Union. A <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> former <a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory</a> barrister turned nationalist campaigner, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Butt" title="Isaac Butt">Isaac Butt</a>, established a new moderate nationalist movement, the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_League" title="Home Rule League">Home Rule League</a>, in the 1870s. After Butt\'s death the Home Rule Movement, or the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a> as it had become known, was turned into a major political force under the guidance of <a href="/wiki/William_Shaw_(Irish_politician)" title="William Shaw (Irish politician)">William Shaw</a> and a radical young Protestant landowner, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Charles Stewart Parnell</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Parnell\'s movement campaigned for "Home Rule", by which they meant that Ireland would govern itself as a region within the United Kingdom. Two Home Rule Bills (1886 and 1893) were introduced by Liberal Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a>, but neither became law, mainly due to opposition from the Conservative Party and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. The issue was a source of contention throughout Ireland, as a significant majority of <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionists</a> (largely but not exclusively based in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>), opposed Home Rule, fearing that a <a href="/wiki/Rome_Rule" title="Rome Rule">Catholic Nationalist</a> ("Rome Rule") Parliament in Dublin would discriminate or retaliate against them, impose Roman Catholic doctrine, and impose tariffs on industry. While most of Ireland was primarily agricultural, six of the counties in Ulster were the location of heavy industry and would be affected by any tariff barriers imposed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_to_present">20th century to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: 20th century to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of the United Kingdom">History of the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="History of the Republic of Ireland">History of the Republic of Ireland</a></div>\n<h3><span id="1900.E2.80.931945"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1900\xe2\x80\x931945">1900\xe2\x80\x931945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: 1900\xe2\x80\x931945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At the turn of the century, Britain was involved in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>, who had reigned since 1837, died in 1901 and was succeeded by her son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">Edward VII</a>, who, in turn, was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> in 1910.\n</p><p>In 1914, Britain entered the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> by declaring war on <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a>. Nearly a million Britons were killed in the war, which lasted until Germany\'s surrender on 11 November 1918.\n</p><p>Home Rule in Ireland, which had been a major political issue since the late 19th century but put on hold by the war, was somewhat resolved after the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a> brought the British Government to a stalemate in 1922. Negotiations led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>. However, in order to appease Unionists in the north, the north-eastern six counties remained as part of the U.K., with its own Parliament at Stormont in Belfast.\n</p><p>Having been in power for much of the early 20th century under Prime Ministers <a href="/wiki/Henry_Campbell-Bannerman" title="Henry Campbell-Bannerman">Campbell-Bannerman</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">Asquith</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">Lloyd George</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal party</a> suffered a sharp decline from 1922; the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour party</a>, whose leader <a href="/wiki/Ramsay_MacDonald" title="Ramsay MacDonald">Ramsay MacDonald</a> led two minority governments, swiftly became the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a>\' main opposition, and Britain\'s largest party of the left.\n</p><p>King <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII" title="Edward VIII">Edward VIII</a> succeeded his father George V in January 1936, but was quickly met with difficulties due to his love affair with <a href="/wiki/Wallis_Simpson" title="Wallis Simpson">Wallis Simpson</a>, an American who had already been married twice. In December, he decided to abdicate in order to be able to marry Simpson, and his brother <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a> was crowned king.\n</p><p>In order to avoid another European conflict, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Neville Chamberlain</a> attempted to appease German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, who was expanding his country\'s territory across Central Europe. Despite proclaiming that he has achieved "peace for our time", Britain declared war on Germany on 3 September 1939, following Hitler\'s invasion of Poland two days earlier. The U.K. thus joined the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a> in opposition to the Axis forces of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861\xe2\x80\x931946)">Fascist Italy</a>. For the first time, civilians were not exempt from the war, as London suffered nightly bombings during the Blitz. At the war\'s end in 1945, however, the U.K. emerged as one of the victorious nations.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1945.E2.80.931997"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1945\xe2\x80\x931997">1945\xe2\x80\x931997</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: 1945\xe2\x80\x931997">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, who had been leader of the wartime coalition government, suffered a surprising landslide defeat to <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>\'s Labour party in 1945 elections. Attlee created a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Welfare State">Welfare State</a> in Britain, which most notably provided free healthcare under the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a>. By the late 1940s, the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> was underway, which would dominate British foreign policy for another 40 years.\n</p><p>In 1951, Churchill and the Tories returned to power; they would govern uninterrupted for the next 13 years. King <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a> died in 1952, and was succeeded by his eldest daughter, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>.\n</p><p>Churchill was succeeded in 1955 by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Anthony_Eden" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Anthony Eden">Sir Anthony Eden</a>, whose premiership was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">Suez Crisis</a>, in which Britain, France and Israel plotted to bomb Egypt after its President <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Nasser</a> nationalised the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez Canal</a>. Eden\'s successor, Harold Macmillan, split the Conservatives when Britain applied to join the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>, but French President <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a> vetoed the application.\n</p><p>Labour returned to power in 1964 under <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>, who brought in a number of social reforms, including the legalisation of abortion, the abolition of capital punishment and the decriminalisation of homosexuality. In 1973, Conservative Prime Minister Edward Heath succeeded in securing U.K. membership in the European Economic Community (EEC), what would later become known as the European Union. Wilson, having lost the 1970 election to Heath, returned to power in 1974; however, Labour\'s reputation was harmed by the <a href="/wiki/Winter_of_discontent" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter of discontent">winter of discontent</a> of 1978-9 under Jim Callaghan, which enabled the Conservatives to re-take control of Parliament in 1979, under <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, Britain\'s first female Prime Minister.\n</p><p>Although Thatcher\'s economic reforms made her initially unpopular, her decision in 1982 to retake the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> from invading <a href="/wiki/Argentine" class="mw-redirect" title="Argentine">Argentine</a> forces, in what would become known as the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>, changed her fortunes and enabled a <a href="/wiki/Landslide_victory" title="Landslide victory">landslide victory</a> in 1983. After winning an unprecedented third election in 1987, however, Thatcher\'s popularity began to fade and she was replaced by former chancellor <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a> in 1990.\n</p><p>Tensions between Protestants and Catholics in Northern Ireland came to a head in the late 1960s, when nationalist participants in a civil rights march were shot by members of the <a href="/wiki/B_Specials" class="mw-redirect" title="B Specials">B Specials</a>, a reserve police force manned almost exclusively by unionists. From this point the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a>, also known as the Provos or simply the IRA, began a bombing campaign throughout the U.K., beginning a period known as <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a>, which lasted until the late 1990s.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Charles">Prince Charles</a>, the Prince of Wales and Elizabeth\'s eldest son married <a href="/wiki/Lady_Diana_Spencer" class="mw-redirect" title="Lady Diana Spencer">Lady Diana Spencer</a> in 1981; the couple had two children, William and Harry, but divorced in 1992, during which year Prince Andrew and Princess Anne also separated from their spouses, leading the Queen to call the year her \'<a href="/wiki/Annus_horribilis" title="Annus horribilis">annus horribilis</a>\'. In 1997, Diana was killed in a car crash in Paris, leading to a mass outpouring of grief across the United Kingdom, and indeed the world.\n</p><p>On the international stage, the second half of the 20th century was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> between the Soviet Union and its socialist allies and the United States and its capitalist allies; the U.K. was a key supporter of the latter, joining the anti-Soviet military alliance <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> in 1949. During this period, the U.K. became involved in several Cold War conflicts, such as the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> (1950\xe2\x80\x931953). In contrast, the Republic of Ireland remained neutral and provided troops to U.N. peace-keeping missions.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1997.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1997\xe2\x80\x93present">1997\xe2\x80\x93present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_British_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: 1997\xe2\x80\x93present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1997, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> was elected prime minister in a landslide victory for the so-called \'<a href="/wiki/New_Labour" title="New Labour">New Labour</a>\', economically following \'<a href="/wiki/Third_Way_(centrism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Way (centrism)">Third Way</a>\' programmes. Blair won re-election in 2001 and 2005, before handing over power to his chancellor <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> in 2007. After a decade of prosperity both the U.K. and the Irish Republic were affected by the global recession, which began in 2008. In 2010, the Conservative party formed a coalition government with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>, with Tory leader <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> as Prime Minister. In 2015 polling suggested a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a> was the most likely outcome in the General Election; however the Conservatives secured a slim majority.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/September_11_Attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11 Attacks">September 11 Attacks</a>, the U.K. supported the U.S. in their "<a href="/wiki/War_on_Terror" title="War on Terror">War on Terror</a>", and joined them in the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001\xe2\x80\x93present)">War in Afghanistan (2001\xe2\x80\x93present)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">invasion of Iraq</a>. London was attacked in <a href="/wiki/7_July_2005_London_bombings" title="7 July 2005 London bombings">July 2005</a>. The U.K. also took a leading role in the <a href="/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya" title="2011 military intervention in Libya">2011 military intervention in Libya</a>. In a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">referendum in 2016</a>, the U.K. voted to leave the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.\n</p><p>After becoming Prime Minister and leader of the Conservative Party shortly after David Cameron\xe2\x80\x99s resignation following the Brexit result, an <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017">election</a>, called by the incumbent Prime Minister <a
