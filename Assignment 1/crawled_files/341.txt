class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Kentucky" title="Governor of Kentucky">Governor of Kentucky</a> is the head of the <a href="/wiki/Executive_branch" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive branch">executive branch</a> of <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Kentucky" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Kentucky">Kentucky\'s state government</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and serves as <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-Chief" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander-in-Chief">commander-in-chief</a> of the state\'s <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_National_Guard" title="Kentucky National Guard">military forces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The governor has a duty to enforce state laws;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the power to either approve or <a href="/wiki/Veto" title="Veto">veto</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_(proposed_law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill (proposed law)">bills</a> passed by the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_General_Assembly" title="Kentucky General Assembly">Kentucky General Assembly</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the power to convene the legislature;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the power to grant <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">pardons</a>, except in cases of <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a> and <a href="/wiki/Impeachment" title="Impeachment">impeachment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He or she is also empowered to reorganize the state government or reduce it in size. Historically, the office has been regarded as one of the most powerful executive positions in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Fifty-eight individuals have held the office of Governor. Prior to a 1992 amendment to the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Constitution" title="Kentucky Constitution">state\'s constitution</a>, the Governor was prohibited from succeeding himself in office, though four men (<a href="/wiki/Isaac_Shelby" title="Isaac Shelby">Isaac Shelby</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_L._Helm" title="John L. Helm">John L. Helm</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_B._McCreary" title="James B. McCreary">James B. McCreary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Happy_Chandler" title="Happy Chandler">Happy Chandler</a>) served multiple non-consecutive terms. <a href="/wiki/Paul_E._Patton" title="Paul E. Patton">Paul E. Patton</a>, the first Kentucky Governor eligible for a second consecutive term under the amendment, won his reelection bid in 1999. <a href="/wiki/James_Garrard" title="James Garrard">James Garrard</a> succeeded himself in 1800, before the constitutional provision existed.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_Goebel" title="William Goebel">William Goebel</a>, who was elected to the office in the disputed <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_gubernatorial_election,_1899" title="Kentucky gubernatorial election, 1899">election of 1899</a>, remains the only Governor of any U.S. state to die from assassination while in office.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martha_Layne_Collins" title="Martha Layne Collins">Martha Layne Collins</a>, who held the office from 1983 to 1987, was the first woman to serve as governor and was only the third woman to serve as governor of any U.S. state who was not the wife or widow of a previous governor.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The 62nd and current Kentucky Governor is <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Matt_Bevin" title="Matt Bevin">Matt Bevin</a>, who took office on December 8, 2015.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governors">Governors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Governors_of_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Governors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Kentucky was initially <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_County,_Virginia" title="Kentucky County, Virginia">Kentucky County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. It achieved statehood and was admitted to the Union on June 1, 1792; see the list of governors of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Virginia" title="List of Governors of Virginia">Virginia</a> for the period before statehood. There have been 57 governors, serving 61 distinct terms.\n</p><p>An unelected group proclaimed Kentucky\'s secession from the <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> on November 20, 1861, and it was annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate States of America</a> on December 10, 1861. The Confederate government elected two governors (listed separately), but it never held much control over the state, and the main line of governors was preserved.\n</p><p>The original 1792 <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Constitution" title="Kentucky Constitution">Kentucky Constitution</a> had the governor chosen by an <a href="/wiki/Electoral_college" title="Electoral college">electoral college</a> for a term of four years.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The second constitution in 1799 changed this to a popular vote, and prevented governors from succeeding themselves within seven years of their terms.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The third constitution in 1850 reduced the succession limitation to four years.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A 1992 amendment to the constitution allowed governors to have a second term before being prevented from succeeding themselves for four years.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable" style="text-align:center;">\n\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>#\n</th>\n<th colspan="3">Governor\n</th>\n<th>Term in office\n</th>\n<th>Party\n</th>\n<th>Election\n</th>\n<th colspan="2"><a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Kentucky" title="Lieutenant Governor of Kentucky">Lt.
