lang="kw"><b>Golowji an Voth</b></i>, <i>the hump lighthouse</i>), in the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> was designed by <a href="/wiki/William_Douglass_(engineer)" title="William Douglass (engineer)">William Tregarthen Douglass</a> for <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a> and completed in 1887. At the time of building it was one of three lights in the Isles of Scilly, the others being the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop Rock">Bishop Rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Agnes, Isles of Scilly">St Agnes lighthouse</a>. The light was modernised in 1966, automated in 1987 and the island designated as a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI) in 1995. It is now managed by the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly_Wildlife_Trust" title="Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust">Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust</a>, and except for the maintenance of the <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">Grade 2 listed lighthouse</a>, landing is not allowed.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wildlife_Trust_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wildlife_Trust-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Wildlife_and_ecology"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Wildlife and ecology</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Round_Island_Light,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairn</a> or burial chamber was destroyed when the lighthouse was built. Cairns on the Isles of Scilly date back to the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age</a> and at that time Round Island was probably a peninsula on the northern shore of the main island in the Isles of Scilly. The <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ashlar" title="Ashlar">ashlar</a>, 19 metres (62&#160;ft) tall tower was designed by William Tregarthen Douglass, chief engineer for the <a href="/wiki/Commissioners_of_Irish_Lights" title="Commissioners of Irish Lights">Commissioners of Irish Lights</a> and is built on a 35 metres (115&#160;ft) tall mass of <a href="/wiki/Hercynian" class="mw-redirect" title="Hercynian">Hercynian granite</a>. At the time of building the only access was up a flight of steps cut out of the rock and supplies were taken up the rock face by an <a href="/wiki/Hoist_(device)" title="Hoist (device)">aerial hoist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Within the walls of the lighthouse the keepers tended a small vegetable garden, for which the soil was transported to the island.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The light has a <a href="/wiki/Focal_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Focal plane">focal plane</a> of 180&#160;feet, and originally had an enormous <a href="/wiki/Hyperradiant_Fresnel_lens" title="Hyperradiant Fresnel lens">hyperradial</a> optic 4.6 metres (15&#160;ft) high and weighing more than 8 tons. It was built by <a href="/wiki/Chance_Brothers" title="Chance Brothers">Chance Brothers &amp; Co</a> of Birmingham and, said at the time, to be \xe2\x80\xb3<i> .... in relation both to size and character .... the most remarkable works of their kind hitherto achieved.</i>\xe2\x80\xb3 A similar optic was installed for the Bishop Rock light the previous year. The optic was replaced in 1966 when the lighthouse was modernised, and again in 1987 when the light was automated. The light now has a 360 millimetres (14&#160;in) optic which emits one white flash every ten seconds, has an intensity of 340,000 <a href="/wiki/Candela" title="Candela">candela</a> and a range of 44.5&#160;km (24 nautical miles). The <a href="/wiki/Fog_signal" class="mw-redirect" title="Fog signal">fog signal</a> sounds four blasts every minute.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Noall_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noall-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Gusts of winds can be ferocious such as in 1954 when there was continuous gales from 29 November to 16 December. Wind velocities of <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">177 km/h</a> (<a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">110&#160;mph</a>) were recorded at the Bishop Rock where seas raced past the window, and on Round Island the <a href="/wiki/Wind_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind gauge">wind gauge</a> was destroyed at 177&#160;km/h.<sup id="cite_ref-Lousley_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lousley-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_and_ecology">Wildlife and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Round_Island_Light,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wildlife and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Round Island was designated a Site of Special Scientific Interest in 1995 as part of the Pentle Bay, Merrick and Round Islands SSSI. The island is important for its breeding seabirds, especially the European storm\xe2\x80\x93petrel (<i><a href="/wiki/Hydrobates_pelagicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrobates pelagicus">Hydrobates pelagicus</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Breeding storm\xe2\x80\x93petrels were unrecorded on Round Island for many years, until one of the lighthouse keepers, mystified by the nightly appearance of black feathers in the living quarters, decided to keep some. When the identity of the bird was discovered, the cat was banished.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Seabird 2000 survey counted 183 occupied nests and a follow\xe2\x80\x93up survey in 2006 found 251 occupied nests on the island; the second highest total in the Isles of Scilly.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Puffins</a> (<i>Fratercula arctica</i>) were first recorded as breeding on Round Island in 1850 by Issac North and during the building of the lighthouse it was said that \xe2\x80\xb3<i>They (puffins) were extremely tame and used to walk in and out of the kitchen of the workmen who built the tower. This tameness, and the edibility of their eggs, proved their undoing, for none survive now</i>\xe2\x80\xb3.<sup id="cite_ref-North_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-North-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Noall_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noall-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The seabird survey in 2000 also recorded 34 occupied nests of <a href="/wiki/Manx_shearwater" title="Manx shearwater">Manx shearwater</a> (<i>Puffinus puffinus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Permission is needed from Trinity House to land on the island and only two botanists are known to have visited. The first, <a href="/wiki/Job_Edward_Lousley" title="Job Edward Lousley">J. E. Lousley</a>, in 1957, only recorded the invasive Hottentot fig (<i><a href="/wiki/Carpobrotus_edulis" title="Carpobrotus edulis">Carpobrotus edulis</a></i>). Thirty years later, in 1987, Rosemary Parslow found much of the ground between the buildings and the cliff edge was covered in a carpet of purple dewplant (<i><a href="/wiki/Disphyma_crassifolium" class="mw-redirect" title="Disphyma crassifolium">Disphyma crassifolium</a></i>) and Hottentot fig. She also recorded a small number of the expected coastal species. They were sea spleenwort (<i><a href="/wiki/Asplenium_marinum" title="Asplenium marinum">Asplenium marinum</a></i>), bird\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x93foot trefoil (<i><a href="/wiki/Lotus_corniculatus" title="Lotus corniculatus">Lotus corniculatus</a></i>), spear\xe2\x80\x93leaved orache (<i><a href="/wiki/Atriplex" title="Atriplex">Atriplex prostrata</a></i>), sea beet (<i><a href="/wiki/Sea_beet" title="Sea beet">Beta vulgaris subsp. maritima</a></i>), rock sea spurrey (<i><a href="/wiki/Spergularia" title="Spergularia">Spergularia rupicola</a></i>), thrift (<i><a href="/wiki/Armeria_maritima" title="Armeria maritima">Armeria maritima</a></i>) and tree mallow (<i><a href="/wiki/Lavatera_arborea" class="mw-redirect" title="Lavatera arborea">Lavatera arborea</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
