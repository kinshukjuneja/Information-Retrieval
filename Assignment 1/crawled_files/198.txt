data-file-height="641" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jarlshof_20080821_-_aisled_roundhouse_and_broch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The href="#cite_note-jarl-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Due to building in stone on virtually treeless islands\xe2\x80\x94a practice dating to at least the early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> Period\xe2\x80\x94Shetland is extremely rich in physical remains of the prehistoric era, and there are over 5,000 archaeological sites.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Midden" title="Midden">midden</a> site at West Voe on the south coast of Mainland, dated to 4320\xe2\x80\x934030 BC, has provided the first evidence of Mesolithic human activity on Shetland.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The same site provides dates for early Neolithic activity, and finds at Scourd of Brouster in <a href="/wiki/Walls,_Shetland_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Walls, Shetland Islands">Walls</a> have been dated to 3400 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;Note 1&#93;</a></sup> "Shetland knives" are stone tools that date from this period made from <a href="/wiki/Felsite" title="Felsite">felsite</a> from <a href="/wiki/Northmavine" title="Northmavine">Northmavine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sch10_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sch10-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viking_expansion">Viking expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Viking expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flateyjarbok_Haraldr_Halfdan.jpg" class="image"><img class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flateyjarbok_Haraldr_Halfdan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Harald H\xc3\xa5rfagre took control of Hjaltland in ca 875. <br />The image is from the Icelandic manuscript <a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k">Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k</a> from the 15th century.</div></div></div>\n<p>By the end of the 9th century the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Scandinavians</a> shifted their attention from plundering to <a href="/wiki/Viking_expansion" title="Viking expansion">invasion</a>, mainly due to the overpopulation of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> in comparison to resources and <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> available there.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Shetland was colonised by Norsemen in the 9th century, the fate of the existing indigenous population being uncertain. The colonists gave it that name and established their laws and language. That language evolved into the <a href="/wiki/West_Norse" class="mw-redirect" title="West Norse">West Nordic</a> language <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn</a>, which survived into the 19th century.\n</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/Harald_Finehair" class="mw-redirect" title="Harald Finehair">Harald Finehair</a> took control of all Norway, many of his opponents fled, some to <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and Shetland. From the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">Northern Isles</a> they continued to raid Scotland and Norway, prompting Harald H\xc3\xa5rfagre to raise a large fleet which he sailed to the islands. In about 875 he and his forces took control of Shetland and Orkney. <a href="/wiki/Ragnvald_Eysteinsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Ragnvald Eysteinsson">Ragnvald, Earl of M\xc3\xb8re</a> received Orkney and Shetland as an <a href="/wiki/Earldom" class="mw-redirect" title="Earldom">earldom</a> from the king as reparation for his son\'s being killed in battle in Scotland. Ragnvald gave the earldom to his brother <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Eysteinsson" title="Sigurd Eysteinsson">Sigurd the Mighty</a>.\n</p><p>Shetland was <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianised</a> in the 10th century.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict_with_Norway">Conflict with Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conflict with Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sverrir_by_Arbo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sverrir_by_Arbo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>King Sverre\'s march over the Vossefjell</i> by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Nicolai_Arbo" title="Peter Nicolai Arbo">Peter Nicolai Arbo</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1194 when king <a href="/wiki/Sverre_Sigurdsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sverre Sigurdsson">Sverre Sigurdsson</a> (ca 1145\xe2\x80\x931202) ruled Norway and <a href="/wiki/Harald_Maddadsson" title="Harald Maddadsson">Harald Maddadsson</a> was <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Orkney" title="Earl of Orkney">Earl of Orkney and Shetland</a>, the Lendmann Hallkjell Jonsson and the Earl\'s brother-in-law Olav raised an army called the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjarskeggjar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" Their pretender king was Olav\'s young foster son <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Magnusson" title="Sigurd Magnusson">Sigurd</a>, son of king <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Erlingsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus Erlingsson">Magnus Erlingsson</a>. The <i>eyjarskeggjar</i> were beaten in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Florv%C3%A5g" title="Battle of Florv\xc3\xa5g">Battle of Florv\xc3\xa5g</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a>. The body of Sigurd Magnusson was displayed for the king in Bergen in order for him to be sure of the death of his enemy, but he also demanded that Harald Maddadsson (Harald jarl) answer for his part in the uprising. In 1195 the earl sailed to Norway to reconcile with King Sverre. As a punishment the king placed the earldom of Shetland under the direct rule of the king, from which it was probably never returned.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Growing_Scottish_interest">Growing Scottish interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Growing Scottish interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. href="/wiki/File:Heladia_Carta_Marina.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Heladia_Carta_Marina.JPG/220px-Heladia_Carta_Marina.JPG" width="220" Marina</a> of 1529</div></div></div>\n<p>When <a href="/wiki/Alexander_III_of_Scotland" title="Alexander III of Scotland">Alexander III of Scotland</a> turned twenty-one in 1262 and became of age he declared his intention of continuing the aggressive policy his father had begun towards the western and northern isles. This had been put on hold when his father had died thirteen years earlier. Alexander sent a formal demand to the Norwegian King <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A5kon_H%C3%A5konsson" class="mw-redirect" title="H\xc3\xa5kon H\xc3\xa5konsson">H\xc3\xa5kon H\xc3\xa5konsson</a>.\n</p><p>After decades of civil war, Norway had achieved stability and grown to be a substantial nation with influence in Europe and the potential to be a powerful force in war. With this as a background, King H\xc3\xa5kon rejected all demands from the Scots. The Norwegians regarded all the islands in the North Sea as part of the Norwegian realm. To add weight to his answer, King H\xc3\xa5kon activated the <i><a href="/wiki/Leidang" title="Leidang">leidang</a></i> and set off from Norway in a fleet which is said to have been the largest ever assembled in Norway. The fleet met up in <i>Breideyarsund</i> in Shetland (probably today\'s <a href="/wiki/Bressay" title="Bressay">Bressay</a> Sound) before the king and his men sailed for Scotland and made landfall on <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a>. The aim was to conduct negotiations with the army as a backup.\n</p><p>Alexander III drew out the negotiations while he patiently waited for the autumn storms to set in. Finally, after tiresome diplomatic talks, King H\xc3\xa5kon lost his patience and decided to attack. At the same time a large storm set in which destroyed several of his ships and kept others from making landfall. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Largs" title="Battle of Largs">Battle of Largs</a> in October 1263 was not decisive and both parties claimed victory, but King H\xc3\xa5kon H\xc3\xa5konsson\'s position was hopeless. On 5 October, he returned to Orkney with a discontented army, and there he died of a fever on 17 December 1263. His death halted any further Norwegian expansion in Scotland.\n</p><p>King <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Lagab%C3%B8te" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus Lagab\xc3\xb8te">Magnus Lagab\xc3\xb8te</a> broke with his father\'s expansion policy and started negotiations with Alexander III. In the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Perth" title="Treaty of Perth">Treaty of Perth</a> of 1266 he surrendered his furthest Norwegian possessions including <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Man</a> and the Sudreyar (<a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>) to Scotland in return for 4,000 <a href="/wiki/Mark_(money)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark (money)">marks</a> sterling and an annuity of 100 marks. The Scots also recognised Norwegian sovereignty over Orkney and Shetland.\n</p><p>One of the main reasons behind the Norwegian desire for peace with Scotland was that trade with England was suffering from the constant state of war. In the new trade agreement between England and Norway in 1223 the English demanded Norway make peace with Scotland. In 1269, this agreement was expanded to include mutual free trade.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pawned_to_Scotland">Pawned to Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pawned to Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg/220px-Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg/330px-Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg/440px-Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg 2x" data-file-width="598" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Christian_1_of_Denmark.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of <a href="/wiki/Christian_I_of_Denmark" title="Christian I of Denmark">King Christian I of Denmark</a> from the  <i>Nordens Historie</i> of 1887</div></div></div>\n<p>In the 14th century Norway still treated Orkney and Shetland as a Norwegian province, but Scottish influence was growing, and in 1379 the Scottish earl <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_Sinclair,_Earl_of_Orkney" title="Henry I Sinclair, Earl of Orkney">Henry Sinclair</a> took control of Orkney on behalf of the Norwegian king <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A5kon_VI_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="H\xc3\xa5kon VI of Norway">H\xc3\xa5kon VI Magnusson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1348 Norway was severely weakened by the <a href="/wiki/Black_Plague" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Plague">Black Plague</a> and in 1397 it entered the <a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Union" title="Kalmar Union">Kalmar Union</a>. With time Norway came increasingly under <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> control. King <a href="/wiki/Christian_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian I of Norway">Christian I</a> of Denmark and Norway was in financial trouble and, when his daughter Margaret became engaged to <a href="/wiki/James_III_of_Scotland" title="James III of Scotland">James III of Scotland</a> in 1468, he needed money to pay her <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a>. Under Norse <a href="/wiki/Udal_law" title="Udal law">udal law</a>, the king had no overall ownership of the land in the realm as in the Scottish feudal system. He was king of his people, rather than king of the land. What the king did not personally own was owned absolutely by others. The King\'s lands represented only a small part of Shetland.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Apparently without the knowledge of the Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Riksr%C3%A5d" title="Riksr\xc3\xa5d">Riksr\xc3\xa5d</a> (Council of the Realm) he entered into a commercial contract on 8 September 1468 with the King of Scots in which he pawned his personal interests in Orkney for 50,000 Rhenish <a href="/wiki/Guilder" title="Guilder">guilders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> On 28 May the next year he also pawned his Shetland interests for 8,000 Rhenish guilders.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He secured a clause in the contract which gave Christian or his successors the right to redeem the islands<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> for a fixed sum of 210 kilograms (460&#160;lb) of gold or 2,310 kilograms (5,090&#160;lb) of silver. There was an obligation to retain the language and laws of Norway, which was not only implicit in the pawning document, but is acknowledged in later correspondence between James III and King Christian\xe2\x80\x99s son John (Hans).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 1470 <a href="/wiki/William_Sinclair,_1st_Earl_of_Caithness" title="William Sinclair, 1st Earl of Caithness">William Sinclair, 1st Earl of Caithness</a> ceded his title to James III and on 20 February 1472, the Northern Isles were directly annexed to the Crown of Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-Macdougall1982_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macdougall1982-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>James and his successors fended off all attempts by the Danes to redeem them (by formal letter or by special embassies were made in 1549, 1550, 1558, 1560, 1585, 1589, 1640, 1660 and other intermediate years) not by contesting the validity of the claim, but by simply avoiding the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hansa_era">Hansa era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hansa era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From the early 15th century on the Shetlanders sold their goods through the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> of German merchantmen. The Hansa would buy shiploads of salted fish, wool and butter and import <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloth" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloth">cloth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> and other goods. The late 16th century and early 17th century was dominated by the influence of the despotic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stewart,_1st_Earl_of_Orkney" title="Robert Stewart, 1st Earl of Orkney">Robert Stewart</a>, Earl of Orkney, who was granted the islands by his half-sister <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary Queen of Scots</a>, and his son <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stewart,_2nd_Earl_of_Orkney" title="Patrick Stewart, 2nd Earl of Orkney">Patrick</a>. The latter commenced the building of <a href="/wiki/Scalloway_Castle" title="Scalloway Castle">Scalloway Castle</a>, but after his execution in 1609 the Crown annexed Orkney and Shetland again until 1643 when <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> granted them to <a href="/wiki/William_Douglas,_7th_Earl_of_Morton" title="William Douglas, 7th Earl of Morton">William Douglas, 7th Earl of Morton</a>. These rights were held on and off by the Mortons until 1766, when they were sold by <a href="/wiki/James_Douglas,_14th_Earl_of_Morton" title="James Douglas, 14th Earl of Morton">James Douglas, 14th Earl of Morton</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sir_Lawrence_Dundas,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Lawrence Dundas, 1st Baronet">Sir Laurence Dundas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_era">British era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: British era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The trade with the North German towns lasted until the 1707 <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1707">Act of Union</a> prohibited the German merchants from trading with Shetland. Shetland then went into an economic depression as the Scottish and local traders were not as skilled in trading with salted fish. However, some local merchant-lairds took up where the German merchants had left off, and fitted out their own ships to export fish from Shetland to the Continent. For the independent farmers of Shetland this had negative consequences, as they now had to fish for these merchant-lairds.<sup id="cite_ref-Visit.Shetland.org_history_page_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visit.Shetland.org_history_page-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> With the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Crofters%27_Holdings_(Scotland)_Act_1886" title="Crofters&#39; Holdings (Scotland) Act 1886">Crofters\' Holdings (Scotland) Act 1886</a> the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> prime minister <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a> emancipated crofters from the rule of the landlords. The Act enabled those who had effectively been landowners\' serfs to become owner-occupiers of their own small farms.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 200 years after the pawning the islands were passed back and forth fourteen times between the Crown and courtiers as a means of extracting income. Laws were changed, weights and measures altered and the language suppressed, a process historians now call "feudalisation" as a means by which Shetland became incorporated into Scotland, particularly during the 17th century. The term is a nonsense because a feudal charter requires ownership by the Crown<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> \xe2\x80\x93 ownership it has never had and has never openly claimed to have had. As late as the 20th century the courts declared that no land in Shetland was under feudal tenure.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Crown might have thought that by prescription (the passage of time) it gave them ownership necessary to give out feudal charters, grants, or licences. It certainly behaved that way. Nevertheless, this was proved wrong by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Breda_(1667)" title="Treaty of Breda (1667)">Treaty of Breda (1667)</a>. Its direct concern was the redistribution of colonial lands throughout the world after the second Anglo-Dutch war. It was signed by \xe2\x80\x98the plenipotentiaries of Europe\xe2\x80\x99 - delegations having full government power.\n</p><p>The Danish delegation tried to have a clause inserted to have the islands returned without delay. Because the overall treaty was too important to Charles II he eventually conceded that the original marriage document still stood, that his and previous monarchs\xe2\x80\x99 actions in granting out the islands under feudal charters were illegal.\n</p><p>In 1669 Charles passed his <a href="/wiki/1669_Act_for_annexation_of_Orkney_and_Shetland_to_the_Crown" title="1669 Act for annexation of Orkney and Shetland to the Crown">1669 Act for annexation of Orkney and Shetland to the Crown</a>, restoring the situation much as it had been in 1469. He abolished the office of Sheriff and "erected Shetland into a Stewartry", having "a direct dependence upon His Majesty and his officers" (what today we would today call a Crown Dependency)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;Note 2&#93;</a></sup>. Charles II also provided that, in the event of a "general dissolution of his majesty\xe2\x80\x99s properties" by which he clearly meant the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Act of Union</a>, Shetland was not to be included. Shetland could not be incorporated into the realm of Scotland or the proposed new union with England. The terms of the marriage document also meant that any Acts of Parliament before or after the pawning could have had no relevance to Shetland.\n</p><p>With the consent of Parliament, Charles was taking the exclusive rights to the islands back to the Crown for all time coming. Furthermore, he was specifically excluding Shetland from the coming Act of Union, even going so far as to say that the Act of Union itself would be null and void if Shetland were to be included.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg" class="image"><img alt="James III and Margaret of Denmark.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg/200px-James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg" width="200" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg/300px-James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2a/James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg 2x" data-file-width="346" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>Several attempts were made<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2018)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> during the 17th and 18th centuries to redeem the islands, without success.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Following a legal dispute with <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_James_Douglas,_10th_Earl_of_Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William James Douglas, 10th Earl of Morton (page does not exist)">William, Earl of Morton</a>, who held the estates of Orkney and Shetland, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> ratified the pawning document by a Scottish <a href="/wiki/1669_Act_for_annexation_of_Orkney_and_Shetland_to_the_Crown" title="1669 Act for annexation of Orkney and Shetland to the Crown">Act of Parliament</a> on 27 December 1669 which officially made the islands a <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependency" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown dependency">Crown dependency</a> and exempt from any "dissolution of His Majesty\xe2\x80\x99s lands". In 1742 a further Act of Parliament returned the estates to <a href="/wiki/James_Douglas,_14th_Earl_of_Morton" title="James Douglas, 14th Earl of Morton">a later Earl of Morton</a>, although the original Act of Parliament specifically ruled that any future act regarding the islands status would be "considered null, void and of no effect".\n</p><p>Nonetheless, Shetland\'s connection with Norway has proven to be enduring. When Norway became independent again in 1906 the Shetland authorities sent a letter to King <a href="/wiki/Haakon_VII_of_Norway" title="Haakon VII of Norway">Haakon VII</a> in which they stated: "Today no \'foreign\' flag is more familiar or more welcome in our voes and havens than that of Norway, and Shetlanders continue to look upon Norway as their mother-land, and recall with pride and affection the time when their forefathers were under the rule of the Kings of Norway."<sup id="cite_ref-Sch13_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sch13-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Napoleonic_wars">Napoleonic wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Napoleonic wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some 3000 Shetlanders served in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleonic wars">Napoleonic wars</a> from 1800 to 1815.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> a Norwegian naval unit nicknamed the "<a href="/wiki/Shetland_bus" title="Shetland bus">Shetland Bus</a>" was established by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Operations_Executive" title="Special Operations Executive">Special Operations Executive</a> Norwegian Section in the autumn of 1940 with a base first at <a href="/wiki/Lunna_House" title="Lunna House">Lunna</a> and later in <a href="/wiki/Scalloway" title="Scalloway">Scalloway</a> in order to conduct operations on the coast of Norway. About 30 fishing vessels used by Norwegian refugees were gathered in Shetland. Many of these vessels were rented, and Norwegian fishermen were recruited as volunteers to operate them.\n</p><p>The Shetland Bus sailed in covert operations between Norway and Shetland, carrying men from <a href="/wiki/Company_Linge" class="mw-redirect" title="Company Linge">Company Linge</a>, intelligence agents, refugees, instructors for the resistance, and military supplies. Many people on the run from the Germans, and much important information on German activity in Norway, were brought back to the Allies this way. Some mines were laid and direct action against German ships was also taken. At the start the unit was under a British command, but later Norwegians joined in the command.\n</p><p>The fishing vessels made 80 trips across the sea. German attacks and bad weather caused the loss of 10 boats, 44 crewmen, and 60 refugees. Because of the high losses it was decided to procure faster vessels. The Americans gave the unit the use of three submarine chasers (<a href="/wiki/HNoMS_Hessa" title="HNoMS Hessa">HNoMS <i>Hessa</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/HNoMS_Hitra" title="HNoMS Hitra">HNoMS <i>Hitra</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HNoMS_Vigra" title="HNoMS Vigra">HNoMS <i>Vigra</i></a>). None of the trips with these vessels incurred loss of life or equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Shetland Bus made over 200 trips across the sea and the most famous of the men, <a href="/wiki/Leif_Andreas_Larsen" class="mw-redirect" title="Leif Andreas Larsen">Leif Andreas Larsen</a> (Shetlands-Larsen) made 52 of them.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_influences">Cultural influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg/220px-Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg/330px-Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg/440px-Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shetland_crofthouse_museum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Shetland <a href="/wiki/Croft_(land)" title="Croft (land)">Crofthouse</a> museum</div></div></div><p>Historical, archaeological, place-name and linguistic evidence indicates Norse cultural dominance of Shetland during the Viking period.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A few place names might have Pictish origin, but this is disputed. Several genetic studies have been conducted investigating the genetic makeup of the islands\' population today in order to establish its origin. Shetlanders are less than half Scandinavian in origin. They have almost identical proportions of Scandinavian matrilineal and patrilineal ancestry (ca 44%), suggesting that the islands were settled by both men and women, as seems to have been the case in Orkney and the northern and western coastline of Scotland, but areas of the British Isles further away from Scandinavia show signs of being colonised primarily by males who found local wives.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> After the islands were transferred to Scotland thousands of Scots families emigrated to Shetland in the 16th and 17th centuries. Contacts with Germany and the Netherlands through the fishing trade brought smaller numbers of immigrants from those countries. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the oil industry have also brought about population growth through immigration.<sup id="cite_ref-Visit.Shetland.org_history_page_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visit.Shetland.org_history_page-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_development">Population development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Shetland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Population development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The population development on Shetland has through history been affected by deaths at sea and epidemics. <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">Smallpox</a> afflicted the islands in the 17th and 18th centuries, but as vaccines became common after 1760 the population increased to 40,000 in 1861. The population increase led to a lack of food, and many young men went away to serve in the British merchant fleet. By 100 years later the islands\' population had been more than halved, mainly due to many Shetland men being lost at sea during the two world wars, and the waves of emigration in the 1920s and 1930s. Now more people of Shetlandic background live in Canada, Australia and New Zealand than in Shetland.\n</p>\n<table class="sortable wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th rowspan="2">District</th>\n<th
