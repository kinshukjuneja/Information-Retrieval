template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Barbary slave trade</b> refers to the slave markets that were lucrative and vast on the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary Coast</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, which included the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> provinces of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Algeria" title="Ottoman Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Tripolitania" title="Ottoman Tripolitania">Tripolitania</a> and the independent sultanate of <a href="/wiki/Saadi_dynasty" title="Saadi dynasty">Morocco</a>, between the 16th and middle of the 18th century. The Ottoman provinces in North Africa were nominally under Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a>, but in reality they were mostly autonomous. The North African slave markets were part of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">Arab slave trade</a>. \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barbary_coast3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Barbary_coast3.jpg/250px-Barbary_coast3.jpg" width="250" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Barbary_coast3.jpg/375px-Barbary_coast3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Barbary_coast3.jpg/500px-Barbary_coast3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="974" data-file-height="582" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Barbary_coast3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Barbary Coast</div></div></div>\n<p>Perpetrated largely on Europeans, and within in-land routes to indigenous European inhabitants. These peoples were systematically preyed upon and turned into slaves, acquired by <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a> during <a href="/wiki/Slave_raid" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave raid">slave raids</a> on ships and by raids on coastal towns from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> to <a href="/wiki/The_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="The Netherlands">the Netherlands</a>, as far north as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and in the eastern shores of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>.\n</p><p>The Ottoman eastern Mediterranean was the scene of intense piracy.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  As late as the 18th century, piracy continued to be a "consistent threat to maritime traffic in the Aegean".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For centuries, large vessels on the Mediterranean relied on <a href="/wiki/Galley_slave" title="Galley slave">galley slaves</a> supplied by North African and <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Slavery in the Ottoman Empire">Ottoman slave traders</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg/310px-Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg" width="310" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg/620px-Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2237" data-file-height="1623" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Purchase_of_Christian_captives_from_the_Barbary_States.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The redemption (buying back) of Christian captives by <a href="/wiki/Mercedarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercedarian">Mercedarian</a> monks in the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary states">Barbary states</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a> history Professor Robert Davis describes the <a href="/wiki/White_slavery" title="White slavery">White Slave Trade</a> as minimized by most modern historians in his book <i>Christian Slaves, Muslim Masters: White Slavery in the Mediterranean, the Barbary Coast and Italy, 1500\xe2\x80\x931800</i>. Davis estimates that 1 million to 1.25 million Europeans were enslaved in North Africa, from the beginning of the 16th century to the middle of the 18th, by <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave trade">slave traders</a> from Tunis, Algiers, and Tripoli alone (these numbers do not include the European people which were enslaved by Morocco and by other raiders and traders of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> coast),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and roughly 700 <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">Americans</a> were held captive in this region as slaves between 1785 and 1815.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  However, to extrapolate his numbers, Davis assumes the number of European slaves captured by Barbary pirates were constant for a 250-year period, stating:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><i>"There are no records of how many men, women and children were enslaved, but it is possible to calculate roughly the number of fresh captives that would have been needed to keep populations steady and replace those slaves who died, escaped, were ransomed, or converted to Islam. On this basis it is thought that around 8,500 new slaves were needed annually to replenish numbers - about 850,000 captives over the century from 1580 to 1680.  By extension, for the 250 years between 1530 and 1780, the figure could easily have been as high as 1,250,000."</i><sup id="cite_ref-Earle_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Earle-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Davis\' numbers have been challenged by other historians, such as David Earle, who cautions that true picture of Europeans slaves is clouded by the fact the corsairs also seized non-Christian whites from eastern Europe and black people from west Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Earle_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Earle-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Purported Middle East expert and  researcher, John Wright, cautions that modern estimates are based on back-calculations from human observation.<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Since no official records were ever kept but the authorities of Ottoman or pre Ottoman sources, observations across the late 1500s and early 1600s observers, estimate that around 35,000 European slaves were held throughout this period on the Barbary Coast, across Tripoli, Tunis, but mostly in Algiers. The majority were sailors (particularly those who were English), taken with their ships, but others were fishermen and coastal villagers.  However, most of these captives were people from lands close to Africa, particularly Spain and Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From bases on the Barbary coast, North Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a> raided ships traveling through the Mediterranean and along the northern and western coasts of Africa, plundering their cargo and enslaving the people they captured. From at least 1500, the pirates also conducted raids along seaside towns of Italy, Spain, France, England, the Netherlands and as far away as Iceland, capturing men, women and children. On some occasions, settlements such as <a href="/wiki/Baltimore,_County_Cork" title="Baltimore, County Cork">Baltimore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> were abandoned following the raid, only being resettled many years later. Between 1609 and 1616, England alone had 466 merchant ships lost to Barbary pirates.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While Barbary corsairs looted the cargo of ships they captured, their primary goal was to capture non-Muslim people for sale as slaves or for ransom. Those who had family or friends who might ransom them were held captive, the most famous of these was the author <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a>, who was held for almost five years. Others were sold into various types of servitude. Captives who converted to Islam were generally freed, since enslavement of Muslims was prohibited; but this meant that they could never return to their native countries.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sixteenth- and 17th-century customs statistics suggest that Istanbul\'s additional slave import from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> may have totaled around 2.5 million from 1450 to 1700.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The markets declined after the loss of the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Wars" title="Barbary Wars">Barbary Wars</a> and finally ended in the 1800s, after a US Navy expedition under Commodore <a href="/wiki/Edward_Preble" title="Edward Preble">Edward Preble</a> engaging gunboats and fortifications in Tripoli, 1804 and later when after a British diplomatic mission led to some confused orders and a massacre; British and Dutch ships delivered a punishing nine-hour <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Algiers_(1816)" title="Bombardment of Algiers (1816)">bombardment of Algiers</a> leading to an acceptance of terms. It ended with the <a href="/wiki/French_conquest_of_Algeria" title="French conquest of Algeria">French conquest of Algeria</a> (1830-1847). The Kingdom of Morocco had already suppressed piracy and recognized the United States as an independent country in 1776.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_slave_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_antiquity" title="Slavery in antiquity">Slavery in antiquity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saqaliba" title="Saqaliba">Saqaliba</a></div>\n<p>The slave trade had existed in North Africa since antiquity, with a supply of African slaves arriving through <a href="/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade" title="Trans-Saharan trade">trans-Saharan trade</a> routes. The towns on the North African coast were recorded in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times for their slave markets, and this trend continued into the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval age</a>. The Barbary Coast increased in influence in the 15th century, when the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> took over as rulers of the area. Coupled with this was an influx of <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish refugees</a>, newly expelled from Spain after the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>.\n</p><p>With Ottoman protection and a host of destitute immigrants, the coastline soon became reputed for <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a>. Crews from the seized ships were either <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">enslaved</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ransom" title="Ransom">ransomed</a>. Between 1580 and 1680, there were in Barbary around 15,000 <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/renegade" class="extiw" title="wikt:renegade">renegades</a>, Christian Europeans who converted to Islam, and half of the corsair captains were in fact renegades. Some of them were slaves that converted to Islam but most had probably never been slaves and had come to North Africa looking for opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_the_Barbary_Pirates">Rise of the Barbary Pirates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_slave_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise of the Barbary Pirates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_slavery_in_the_Muslim_world" title="History of slavery in the Muslim world">History of slavery in the Muslim world</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg/310px-Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg" width="310" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg/465px-Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg/620px-Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1350" data-file-height="1067" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombardement_d%27Alger.1682.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The bombardment of <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a> in 1682, by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Duquesne" title="Abraham Duquesne">Abraham Duquesne</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>After a revolt in the mid-17th century reduced the ruling Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Pasha" title="Pasha">Pashas</a> to little more than figureheads in the region, the towns of <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> and others became independent in all but name. Without a large central authority and its laws, the pirates themselves started to gain much influence.\n</p><p>In 1785 when <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a> went to London to negotiate with Tripoli\'s envoy, Ambassador Sidi Haji Abdrahaman, they asked him what right he had to take slaves in this way. He replied that the "right" was "founded on the Laws of the Prophet, that it was written in their Koran that all nations who should not have answered their authority were sinners, that it was their right and duty to make war upon them wherever they could be found, and to make slaves of all they could take as prisoners, and that every Mussulman who should be slain in battle was sure to go to Paradise".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pirate raids for the acquisition of slaves occurred in towns and villages on the African <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">seaboard</a>, as well as in Europe. Reports of Barbary raids and kidnappings of those in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and as far north as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> exist from between the 16th to the 19th centuries. It is estimated that between 1 million and 1.25 million Europeans were captured by pirates and sold as slaves in Tunis, Algiers and Tripoli during this time period. The slave trade in Europeans in other parts of the Mediterranean is not included in this estimation.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Famous accounts of Barbary slave raids include a mention in the diary of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a> and a raid on the coastal village of <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Baltimore" title="Sack of Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, Ireland, during which pirates left with the entire populace of the settlement. The attack was led by a <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> captain, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Janszoon" title="Jan Janszoon">Jan Janszoon</a> van Haarlem, also known as Murad Reis the Younger.  Janszoon also led the 1627 raid on Iceland. Such raids in the Mediterranean were so frequent and devastating that the coastline between Venice to Malaga<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> suffered widespread depopulation, and settlement there was discouraged. In fact, it was said that this was largely because "there was no one left to capture any longer."<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The power and influence of these pirates during this time was such that nations including the <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="United States of America">United States of America</a> paid tribute in order to stave off their attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Supplies from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> appear to have been even larger. A compilation of partial statistics and patchy estimates indicates that a little fewer than 2 million Russians, Ukrainians, and Poles were seized from 1468 to 1694. Additionally, there were slaves from the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> obtained by a mixture of raiding and trading. 16th- and 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a> statistics suggest that Istanbul\'s slave import from the Black Sea may have totaled around 2.5 million from 1450 to 1700.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_slave_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NH_65536-KN.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/NH_65536-KN.jpg/400px-NH_65536-KN.jpg" width="400" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/NH_65536-KN.jpg/600px-NH_65536-KN.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/NH_65536-KN.jpg/800px-NH_65536-KN.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7021" data-file-height="3680" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NH_65536-KN.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A US Navy expedition under Commodore <a href="/wiki/Edward_Preble" title="Edward Preble">Edward Preble</a> engaging gunboats and fortifications in Tripoli, 1804.</div></div></div>\n<p>In the first years of the 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a> and some European nations fought and won the <a href="/wiki/First_Barbary_War" title="First Barbary War">First Barbary War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Barbary_War" title="Second Barbary War">Second Barbary War</a> against the pirates.  The <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Wars" title="Barbary Wars">Barbary Wars</a> were a direct response of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> states to the raids and white slave trade by the Barbary pirates, which ended in the 1830s when the region was <a href="/wiki/French_conquest_of_Algeria" title="French conquest of Algeria">conquered by France</a>. The white slave trade and markets in the Mediterranean declined and eventually disappeared after the European occupations.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After an <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Algiers_(1816)" title="Bombardment of Algiers (1816)">Anglo-Dutch raid in 1816</a> on <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a> immobilized most of the Pirate fleet, the Dey of Algiers was forced to agree to terms which included a cessation of the practice of enslaving Christians, although slave trading in non-Europeans could still continue. After losing in this period of formal hostilities with European and American powers, the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary states</a> went into decline.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Barbary pirates did not cease their operations, and another British raid on Algiers took place in 1824. France <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Algiers_in_1830" title="Invasion of Algiers in 1830">invaded Algiers in 1830</a>, placing it under colonial rule. <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> was similarly <a href="/wiki/French_conquest_of_Tunisia" title="French conquest of Tunisia">invaded by France in 1881</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a> returned to direct Ottoman control in 1835, before falling into Italian hands in the 1911 <a href="/wiki/Italo-Turkish_War" title="Italo-Turkish War">Italo-Turkish War</a>. As such, the slave traders now found that they had to work in accordance with the laws of their governors, and could no longer look to self-regulation. The slave trade ceased on the Barbary coast in the 19th and 20th centuries or when European governments passed laws granting emancipation to slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The word <b><a href="/wiki/Razzia_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Razzia (disambiguation)">razzia</a></b> was borrowed via Italian and French from <a href="/wiki/Algerian_Arabic" title="Algerian Arabic">Algerian Arabic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ghazw" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghazw">ghaziya</a></i> (\xd8\xba\xd8\xb2\xd9\x8a\xd8\xa9 "raiding"), originally referring to <a href="/wiki/Slave_raid" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave raid">slave raids</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
