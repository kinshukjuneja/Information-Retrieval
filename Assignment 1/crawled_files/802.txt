Braves">History of the Boston Braves</a></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Milwaukee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves#Milwaukee_(1953\xe2\x80\x931965)" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves  \xc2\xa7&#160;Milwaukee (1953\xe2\x80\x931965)</a></div>\n<h3><span id="1953.E2.80.931959"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1953\xe2\x80\x931959">1953\xe2\x80\x931959</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1953\xe2\x80\x931959">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> went wild over the Braves, who were welcomed as genuine heroes. The <a href="/wiki/1953_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1953 Milwaukee Braves season">Braves</a> finished <span class="nowrap">.597</span> in their <a href="/wiki/1953_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1953 Milwaukee Braves season">first season</a> in Milwaukee, and drew a then-NL record <span class="nowrap">1.8 million fans.<sup id="cite_ref-bfsnlmk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bfsnlmk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ftnlre_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ftnlre-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></span> The success of the team was noted by many owners. Not coincidentally, the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles#St._Louis_Browns" title="Baltimore Orioles">St. Louis Browns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> all relocated over the next five years.\n</p><p>As the 1950s progressed, the reinvigorated Braves became increasingly competitive. Sluggers <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Mathews" title="Eddie Mathews">Eddie Mathews</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hank_Aaron" title="Hank Aaron">Hank Aaron</a> drove the offense (they would hit a combined 1,226 home runs as Braves, with 850 of those coming while the franchise was in Milwaukee), while Spahn, <a href="/wiki/Lew_Burdette" title="Lew Burdette">Lew Burdette</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Buhl" title="Bob Buhl">Bob Buhl</a> anchored the rotation.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1957">1957</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1957">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1957_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1957 Milwaukee Braves season">1957 Milwaukee Braves season</a></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/1957_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1957 Milwaukee Braves season">1957</a>, the Braves celebrated their first pennant in nine years spearheaded by Aaron\'s <a href="/wiki/MLB_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="MLB Most Valuable Player Award">MVP season</a>, as he led the National League in home runs and RBI. Perhaps the most memorable of his 44 round-trippers that season came on September 23, a two-run <a href="/wiki/Walk-off_home_run" title="Walk-off home run">walk-off home run</a> that gave the Braves a <span class="nowrap">4\xe2\x80\x932</span> victory over the <a href="/wiki/1957_St._Louis_Cardinals_season" title="1957 St. Louis Cardinals season">St. Louis Cardinals</a> and clinched the league championship. The team drew over <span class="nowrap">2.2 million</span> at home during the regular season, then went on to its first <a href="/wiki/1957_World_Series" title="1957 World Series">World Series</a> win in over 40 years, defeating the <a href="/wiki/1957_New_York_Yankees_season" title="1957 New York Yankees season">New York Yankees</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Berra" title="Yogi Berra">Yogi Berra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mickey Mantle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Whitey_Ford" title="Whitey Ford">Whitey Ford</a> in seven games. Burdette, the <a href="/wiki/World_Series_Most_Valuable_Player" class="mw-redirect" title="World Series Most Valuable Player">Series MVP</a>, threw three complete game victories, giving up only two earned runs, including the seventh game in New York, <span class="nowrap">a 5\xe2\x80\x930 shutout.<sup id="cite_ref-bwwti_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwwti-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bithwrl_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bithwrl-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1958">1958</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1958">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1958_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1958 Milwaukee Braves season">1958 Milwaukee Braves season</a></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/1958_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1958 Major League Baseball season">1958</a>, the Braves again won the National League pennant and jumped out to a three games to one lead in the <a href="/wiki/1958_World_Series" title="1958 World Series">World Series</a> against New York once more, thanks in part to the strength of Spahn\'s and Burdette\'s pitching. But the Yankees stormed back to take the last three games, the last two in Milwaukee, in large part to World Series MVP <a href="/wiki/Bob_Turley" title="Bob Turley">Bob Turley</a>, the winning pitcher in games five and seven.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1959">1959</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1959">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1959_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1959 Milwaukee Braves season">1959 Milwaukee Braves season</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/1959_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1959 Major League Baseball season">1959</a> season saw the Braves finish the season in a tie with the <a href="/wiki/1959_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1959 Los Angeles Dodgers season">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>. Many residents of Chicago and Milwaukee were hoping for a <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/1959_Chicago_White_Sox_season" title="1959 Chicago White Sox season">White Sox</a>\xe2\x80\x93Braves</span> World Series, as the cities are only about 75 miles (120&#160;km) apart along the west shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>. It was not to be, as Milwaukee fell in a best-of-3 playoff for the league title with two straight losses to the Dodgers, ending the Braves\' pennant streak <span class="nowrap">at two.<sup id="cite_ref-cblach_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cblach-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></span> Los Angeles defeated the Sox in six games in the <a href="/wiki/1959_World_Series" title="1959 World Series">World Series</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960-65">1960-65</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1960-65">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The next six years were up-and-down for the Braves. The <a href="/wiki/1960_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1960 Milwaukee Braves season">1960</a> season featured two no-hitters by Burdette and Spahn, and Milwaukee finished in second, seven games behind the <a href="/wiki/1960_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1960 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>, who won the <a href="/wiki/1960_World_Series" title="1960 World Series">World Series</a> that year. Milwaukee\'s home attendance slipped under 1.5 million for the first time since the move from Boston. The <a href="/wiki/1961_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1961 Milwaukee Braves season">1961</a> season saw a drop in the standings for the Braves down to fourth, despite Spahn recording his 300th victory and pitching another no-hitter that year.  The team\'s home attendance continued its decline; the last season exceeding one million was in 1961.\n</p><p>Aaron hit 45 home runs in <a href="/wiki/1962_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1962 Milwaukee Braves season">1962</a>, a Milwaukee career high for him, but this did not translate into wins for the Braves, as they finished fifth. The <a href="/wiki/1963_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1963 Milwaukee Braves season">next season</a>, Aaron again hit 44 home runs and notched 130 RBI, and Spahn was once again the ace of the staff, going .767. However, none of the other Braves produced at that level, and the team finished in the lower half of the league, or "<a href="/wiki/First_division_(baseball)" title="First division (baseball)">second division</a>", for the first time in its short history in Milwaukee.\n</p><p>The Braves were somewhat mediocre as the 1960s began, but fattened up on the expansion <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Houston_Colt_.45s" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston Colt .45s">Houston Colt .45s</a> starting in 1962. The Milwaukee Braves are the only team in the modern era to play more than one season and never had a losing record.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_Atlanta">Move to Atlanta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Move to Atlanta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lou Perini sold the Braves to a <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>-based group led by <a href="/wiki/William_Bartholomay" title="William Bartholomay">William Bartholomay</a> after the 1961 season and the ink was barely dry on the deal when Bartholomay started shopping the Braves to a larger television market. Keen to attract them, the fast-growing city of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, led by Mayor <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Allen,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Ivan Allen, Jr.">Ivan Allen, Jr.</a>, constructed a new $18 million, 52,007-60,606 seat multi-purpose stadium in less than one year, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta%E2%80%93Fulton_County_Stadium" title="Atlanta\xe2\x80\x93Fulton County Stadium">Atlanta Stadium</a>, (later on known as Atlanta\xe2\x80\x93Fulton County Stadium from 1976 until 1996 with its demolition in 1997) which was officially opened in 1965 in hopes of luring an existing <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">major league</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a>/<a href="/wiki/American_Football_League" title="American Football League">AFL</a> team.\n</p><p>After the city failed to lure the <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/1965_Kansas_City_Athletics_season" title="1965 Kansas City Athletics season">Kansas City A\'s</a></span> (moved to <a href="/wiki/Oakland" class="mw-redirect" title="Oakland">Oakland</a> in <a href="/wiki/1968_Oakland_Athletics_season" title="1968 Oakland Athletics season">1968</a>), the Braves announced their intention to move to Atlanta for the <a href="/wiki/1965_Milwaukee_Braves_season" title="1965 Milwaukee Braves season">1965</a> season. However, an injunction filed in Wisconsin kept the Braves in Milwaukee for one final year, but the home attendance was less than 560,000.<sup id="cite_ref-bsfwisb_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bsfwisb-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Braves completed the move to Atlanta prior to the <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1966 Atlanta Braves season">1966</a> season, and drew over 1.5 million in the new stadium that <span class="nowrap">first year.<sup id="cite_ref-btbr_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-btbr-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlanta">Atlanta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Atlanta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="1966.E2.80.931975"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1966\xe2\x80\x931975">1966\xe2\x80\x931975</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1966\xe2\x80\x931975">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Braves were a .500 team in their first few years in Atlanta &#8211; 85-77 in <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1966 Atlanta Braves season">1966</a>, 77-85 in <a href="/wiki/1967_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1967 Atlanta Braves season">1967</a>, and 81-81 in <a href="/wiki/1968_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1968 Atlanta Braves season">1968</a>. The 1967 season was the Braves\' first losing season since <a href="/wiki/1952_Boston_Braves_season" title="1952 Boston Braves season">1952</a>, their last year in Boston. In <a href="/wiki/1969_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1969 Atlanta Braves season">1969</a>, with the onset of divisional play, the Braves won the first-ever <a href="/wiki/National_League_West" title="National League West">National League West</a> pennant, but were swept in three games by the "<a href="/wiki/1969_New_York_Mets_season" title="1969 New York Mets season">Miracle Mets</a>" in the <a href="/wiki/1969_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1969 National League Championship Series">National League Championship Series</a>. Atlanta would not be a factor in the division in the next decade, posting only two winning seasons between 1970 and 1981 \xe2\x80\x93 in some cases, fielding teams as bad as the worst Boston teams.\n</p><p>In the meantime, fans had to be satisfied with the achievements of Hank Aaron. In the relatively hitter-friendly confines of Atlanta Stadium ("The Launching Pad"), he actually increased his offensive production. It also produced batting champions in <a href="/wiki/Rico_Carty" title="Rico Carty">Rico Carty</a> (in 1970) and in <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Garr" title="Ralph Garr">Ralph Garr</a> (in 1974). By the end of the <a href="/wiki/1973_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1973 Atlanta Braves season">1973</a> season, Aaron had hit 713 home runs, one short of Ruth\'s record. Throughout the winter he received racially motivated death threats, but stood up well under the pressure. The <a href="/wiki/1974_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1974 Atlanta Braves season">next season</a>, it was only a matter of time before he set a new record. On April 4 he hit #714 in <a href="/wiki/1974_Cincinnati_Reds_season" title="1974 Cincinnati Reds season">Cincinnati</a>, and on April 8, in front of his home fans, he finally beat Ruth\'s mark.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ted_Turner_era_begins">Ted Turner era begins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ted Turner era begins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1976, the team was purchased by media magnate <a href="/wiki/Ted_Turner" title="Ted Turner">Ted Turner</a>, owner of <a href="/wiki/Superstation" title="Superstation">superstation</a> <a href="/wiki/TBS_(TV_network)" class="mw-redirect" title="TBS (TV network)">WTBS</a>, as a means to keep the team (and one of his main programming staples) in Atlanta. The financially strapped Turner used money already paid to the team for their broadcast rights as a down-payment. It was then that Atlanta Stadium was renamed <a href="/wiki/Atlanta%E2%80%93Fulton_County_Stadium" title="Atlanta\xe2\x80\x93Fulton County Stadium">Atlanta\xe2\x80\x93Fulton County Stadium</a>. Turner quickly gained a reputation as a quirky, hands-on baseball owner.\n</p><p>Turner used the Braves as a major programming draw for his fledgling cable network, a move that made the Braves one of the first Major League Baseball franchise to have a regular, nationwide audience and fanbase, along with the Chicago Cubs (distributed nationally via WGN). WTBS marketed the team as "The Atlanta Braves: <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Team" title="America&#39;s Team">America\'s Team</a>", a nickname that still sticks in some areas of the country, especially <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">the South</a>, today. Among other things, in 1976 Turner suggested the nickname "Channel" for pitcher <a href="/wiki/Andy_Messersmith" title="Andy Messersmith">Andy Messersmith</a> and jersey number 17, in order to promote the television station that aired Braves games. Major League Baseball quickly vetoed the idea.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1976.E2.80.931989"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1976\xe2\x80\x931989">1976\xe2\x80\x931989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1976\xe2\x80\x931989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On May 11, 1977, Turner appointed himself <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a>, but, because MLB passed a rule in the 1950s barring managers from holding a financial stake in their team, Turner was ordered to relinquish that position after one game (the <a href="/wiki/1977_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1977 Atlanta Braves season">Braves</a> lost 2-1 to the <a href="/wiki/1977_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1977 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> to bring their losing streak to 17 games).\n</p><p>After three straight losing seasons, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Cox" title="Bobby Cox">Bobby Cox</a> was hired for his first stint as manager of the franchise for the <a href="/wiki/1969_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1969 Major League Baseball season">1978 season</a>, and he promoted a 22-year-old slugger named <a href="/wiki/Dale_Murphy" title="Dale Murphy">Dale Murphy</a> into the starting lineup. Murphy hit 77 home runs over the next three seasons, but struggled on defense, positioned at either catcher or first base while being unable to adeptly play either. However, in <a href="/wiki/1980_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1980 Major League Baseball season">1980</a>, Murphy was moved to center field and demonstrated excellent range and throwing ability, while the Braves earned their first winning season since 1974.\n</p><p>Cox was fired after the <a href="/wiki/1981_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1981 Major League Baseball season">1981 season</a> and replaced with <a href="/wiki/Joe_Torre" title="Joe Torre">Joe Torre</a>, under whose leadership the <a href="/wiki/1982_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1982 Atlanta Braves season">Braves</a> attained their first divisional title since 1969. However, the Braves were swept by the eventual World Series champion <a href="/wiki/1982_St._Louis_Cardinals_season" title="1982 St. Louis Cardinals season">St. Louis Cardinals</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1982_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1982 National League Championship Series">NLCS</a>. Strong performances from <a href="/wiki/Bob_Horner" title="Bob Horner">Bob Horner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Chambliss" title="Chris Chambliss">Chris Chambliss</a>, pitcher <a href="/wiki/Phil_Niekro" title="Phil Niekro">Phil Niekro</a>, and short relief pitcher <a href="/wiki/Gene_Garber" title="Gene Garber">Gene Garber</a> helped the Braves, but no Brave was more acclaimed than Murphy, who won both a <a href="/wiki/MLB_Most_Valuable_Player_award" class="mw-redirect" title="MLB Most Valuable Player award">Most Valuable Player</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Gold_Glove_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold Glove Award">Gold Glove</a> award. Murphy also won a Most Valuable Player award the following season, but the Braves began a period of decline that defined the team throughout the 1980s. Murphy, excelling in defense, hitting, and running, was consistently recognized as one of the league\'s best players, but the Braves averaged only 65 wins per season between 1985 and 1990. Their lowest point came in 1988, when they lost 106 games.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1986_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1986 Major League Baseball season">1986 season</a> saw the return of Bobby Cox to the Braves organization as <a href="/wiki/General_manager_(baseball)" title="General manager (baseball)">general manager</a>.  Also in 1986, the team stopped using their Native American-themed mascot, <a href="/wiki/Chief_Noc-A-Homa" title="Chief Noc-A-Homa">Chief Noc-A-Homa</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1990.E2.80.932004:_Successes_and_stars"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1990\xe2\x80\x932004:_Successes_and_stars">1990\xe2\x80\x932004: Successes and stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1990\xe2\x80\x932004: Successes and stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1990-94">1990-94</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 1990-94">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Cox returned to the dugout as manager in the middle of the <a href="/wiki/1990_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1990 Major League Baseball season">1990 season</a>, replacing <a href="/wiki/Russ_Nixon" title="Russ Nixon">Russ Nixon</a>. The <a href="/wiki/1990_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1990 Atlanta Braves season">Braves</a> would finish the year with the worst record in baseball, at 65-97, and traded Dale Murphy to the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> after it was clear he was becoming a less dominant player. However, pitching coach <a href="/wiki/Leo_Mazzone" title="Leo Mazzone">Leo Mazzone</a> began developing young pitchers <a href="/wiki/Tom_Glavine" title="Tom Glavine">Tom Glavine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Avery" title="Steve Avery">Steve Avery</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Smoltz" title="John Smoltz">John Smoltz</a> into future stars. That same year, the Braves used the number one overall pick in the <a href="/wiki/1990_Major_League_Baseball_Draft" class="mw-redirect" title="1990 Major League Baseball Draft">1990 MLB Draft</a> to select <a href="/wiki/Chipper_Jones" title="Chipper Jones">Chipper Jones</a>, who would go on to become one of the best hitters in team history. Perhaps the Braves\' most important move, however, was not on the field, but in the front office. Immediately after the season, <a href="/wiki/John_Schuerholz" title="John Schuerholz">John Schuerholz</a> was hired away from the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a> as general manager.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1991_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1991 Major League Baseball season">following season</a>, Glavine, Avery, and Smoltz would be recognized as the best young pitchers in the league, winning 52 games among them. Meanwhile, behind position players <a href="/wiki/David_Justice" title="David Justice">Dave Justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Gant" title="Ron Gant">Ron Gant</a> and unexpected league Most Valuable Player and batting champion <a href="/wiki/Terry_Pendleton" title="Terry Pendleton">Terry Pendleton</a>, the <a href="/wiki/1991_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1991 Atlanta Braves season">Braves</a> overcame a 39-40 start, winning 55 of their final 83 games over the last three months of the season and edging the <a href="/wiki/1991_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1991 Los Angeles Dodgers season">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> by one game in one of baseball history\'s more memorable playoff races. The "Worst to First" Braves, who had not won a divisional title since 1982, captivated the city of Atlanta (and, to a larger degree, the state of Georgia and the entire southeast) during their improbable run to the flag. They defeated the <a href="/wiki/1991_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1991 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> in a very tightly contested seven-game <a href="/wiki/1991_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1991 National League Championship Series">NLCS</a> only to lose the <a href="/wiki/1991_World_Series" title="1991 World Series">World Series</a>, also in seven games, to the <a href="/wiki/1991_Minnesota_Twins_season" title="1991 Minnesota Twins season">Minnesota Twins</a>. The series, considered by many to be one of the greatest ever, was the first time a team that had finished last in its division one year went to the World Series the next; both the Twins and Braves accomplished the feat.\n</p><p>During the Braves\' rise to prominence in the early 1990s, their long-standing ethnic nickname came under much closer scrutiny, even being protested in Minneapolis when the Braves visited the Twins for Game 1 of the 1991 World Series. The team was especially criticized for selling plastic and <a href="/wiki/Foam_tomahawk" class="mw-redirect" title="Foam tomahawk">foam tomahawks</a>, encouraging the so-called <a href="/wiki/Tomahawk_Chop" class="mw-redirect" title="Tomahawk Chop">tomahawk chop</a> and the accompanying war cry emitted by the fans. The war cry and tomahawk chop are similar, if not identical, to what <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_Seminoles" title="Florida State Seminoles">Florida State University</a> fans do at their games. Initially, the war chant music was played by the Braves\' organist, but in recent years, a recording of the FSU band has been used instead.  This tradition can be traced back to the arrival of former Seminole <a href="/wiki/Deion_Sanders" title="Deion Sanders">Deion Sanders</a>, who also played for the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Falcons" title="Atlanta Falcons">Atlanta Falcons</a> at the time (he would go on to play both sports simultaneously in San Francisco for one year).\n</p><p>Despite the World Series loss, the Braves\' success would continue. In the <a href="/wiki/1992_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1992 Major League Baseball season">1992 season</a>, the <a href="/wiki/1992_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1992 Atlanta Braves season">Braves</a> returned to the <a href="/wiki/1992_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1992 National League Championship Series">NLCS</a> and once again defeated the <a href="/wiki/1992_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1992 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pirates</a> in seven games, only to lose in the <a href="/wiki/1992_World_Series" title="1992 World Series">World Series</a> to a dominating <a href="/wiki/1992_Toronto_Blue_Jays_season" title="1992 Toronto Blue Jays season">Toronto Blue Jays</a> team.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1993_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1993 Major League Baseball season">1993</a>, the Braves signed <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a> winning pitcher <a href="/wiki/Greg_Maddux" title="Greg Maddux">Greg Maddux</a> from the Chicago Cubs, leading many baseball insiders to declare the team\'s pitching staff the best at that time. The <a href="/wiki/1993_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1993 Atlanta Braves season">1993 team</a> posted a franchise-best 104 wins after a dramatic pennant race with the <a href="/wiki/1993_San_Francisco_Giants_season" title="1993 San Francisco Giants season">San Francisco Giants</a>, who won 103 games. The Braves needed a stunning 55-19 finish to edge out the Giants, who led the Braves by nine games in the standings as late as August 11. However, the Braves fell in the <a href="/wiki/1993_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1993 National League Championship Series">NLCS</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> in a six-game upset.\n</p><p>In 1994, in a realignment of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>\'s divisions following the 1993 expansion, the Braves moved to the Eastern Division.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This realignment was the main cause of the team\'s <a href="/wiki/Braves%E2%80%93Mets_rivalry" title="Braves\xe2\x80\x93Mets rivalry">heated rivalry</a> with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> during the mid to late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SubwaySeries_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SubwaySeries-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1994\xe2\x80\x9395 Major League Baseball strike">player\'s strike</a> cut short the <a href="/wiki/1994_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1994 Major League Baseball season">1994 season</a>, prior to the division championships, with the Braves six games behind the <a href="/wiki/1994_Montreal_Expos_season" title="1994 Montreal Expos season">Montreal Expos</a> with 48 games left to play.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="1995.E2.80.932004"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1995\xe2\x80\x932004">1995\xe2\x80\x932004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 1995\xe2\x80\x932004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/1995_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="1995 Atlanta Braves season">Braves</a> returned strong the following strike-shortened (teams played 144 games instead of the customary 162) <a href="/wiki/1995_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1995 Major League Baseball season">year</a> and beat the <a href="/wiki/1995_Cleveland_Indians_season" title="1995 Cleveland Indians season">Cleveland Indians</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1995_World_Series" title="1995 World Series">World Series</a>. This squelched claims by many Braves critics that they were the "<a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a> of baseball" (January 1996 issue of <i>Beckett Baseball Card Monthly</i>). With this World Series victory, the Braves became the first team in Major League Baseball to win world championships in three different cities. With their strong pitching being a constant, the Braves would also appear in the 1996 and 1999 World Series (they lost both series to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>, however), and had a streak of division titles from 1991 to 2005 interrupted only in 1994 when the strike ended the season early. Pitching is not the only constant in the Braves organization \xe2\x80\x93 , Schuerholz remained the team\'s GM until after the 2007 season when he was promoted to team president. Pendleton did not finish his playing career in Atlanta, but has returned to the Braves system as the hitting coach.\n</p><p>A 95-67 record in <a href="/wiki/2000_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2000 Major League Baseball season">2000</a> produced a ninth consecutive division title. However, a sweep at the hands of the <a href="/wiki/2000_St._Louis_Cardinals_season" title="2000 St. Louis Cardinals season">St. Louis Cardinals</a> prevented the <a href="/wiki/2000_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="2000 Atlanta Braves season">Braves</a> from reaching the <a href="/wiki/2000_National_League_Championship_Series" title="2000 National League Championship Series">NLCS</a>. In <a href="/wiki/2001_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2001 Major League Baseball season">2001</a>, <a href="/wiki/2001_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="2001 Atlanta Braves season">Atlanta</a> won the <a href="/wiki/National_League_East" title="National League East">National League East</a> division yet again, swept the <a href="/wiki/2001_National_League_Division_Series" title="2001 National League Division Series">NLDS</a> against the <a href="/wiki/2001_Houston_Astros_season" title="2001 Houston Astros season">Houston Astros</a>, then lost to the <a href="/wiki/2001_Arizona_Diamondbacks_season" title="2001 Arizona Diamondbacks season">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2001_National_League_Championship_Series" title="2001 National League Championship Series">National League Championship Series</a> four games to one. One memorable game the Braves played that year came on September 21, when they played <a href="/wiki/Braves%E2%80%93Mets_rivalry" title="Braves\xe2\x80\x93Mets rivalry">rival</a> <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> in the first major professional sporting event held in New York City since <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/2002_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2002 Major League Baseball season">2002</a>, <a href="/wiki/2003_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2003 Major League Baseball season">2003</a> and <a href="/wiki/2004_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2004 Major League Baseball season">2004</a>, the Braves won their division again, but lost in the NLDS in all three years; 3 games to 2 to the <a href="/wiki/2002_San_Francisco_Giants_season" title="2002 San Francisco Giants season">San Francisco Giants</a> and <a href="/wiki/2003_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="2003 Chicago Cubs season">Chicago Cubs</a>, and 3 games to 1 to the <a href="/wiki/2004_Houston_Astros_season" title="2004 Houston Astros season">Houston Astros</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cy_Young_dominance">Cy Young dominance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cy Young dominance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Six National League <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Awards</a> in the 1990s were awarded to three Braves pitchers:\n</p>\n<ul><li>In 1991, left-handed pitcher <a href="/wiki/Tom_Glavine" title="Tom Glavine">Tom Glavine</a> received his first award.</li>\n<li>Right-handed pitcher <a href="/wiki/Greg_Maddux" title="Greg Maddux">Greg Maddux</a> won three in a row with the Braves, from 1993 through 1995. His first award came in 1992 with the Cubs.</li>\n<li>In 1996, right-handed pitcher <a href="/wiki/John_Smoltz" title="John Smoltz">John Smoltz</a> received his only Cy Young Award.</li>\n<li>In 1998, Glavine won his second.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005:_A_new_generation">2005: A new generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2005: A new generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="2005 Atlanta Braves season">2005 Atlanta Braves season</a></div>\n<p>In 2005, the Braves won the Eastern Division championship for the eleventh consecutive year from 1995 to 2005. This followed winning the Western Division championship three times from 1991 to 1993, prior to the 1994 player\'s strike. The 2005 title marked the first time any MLB team made the postseason with more than 4 rookies who each had more than 100 ABs (<a href="/wiki/Wilson_Betemit" title="Wilson Betemit">Wilson Betemit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_McCann_(baseball)" title="Brian McCann (baseball)">Brian McCann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Orr" title="Pete Orr">Pete Orr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Langerhans" title="Ryan Langerhans">Ryan Langerhans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Francoeur" title="Jeff Francoeur">Jeff Francoeur</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Catcher <a href="/wiki/Brian_McCann_(baseball)" title="Brian McCann (baseball)">Brian McCann</a>, right fielder <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Francoeur" title="Jeff Francoeur">Jeff Francoeur</a>, and pitcher <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Davies_(baseball)" title="Kyle Davies (baseball)">Kyle Davies</a> all grew up in the suburbs of Atlanta. The large number of rookies to debut in 2005 were nicknamed the "Baby Braves" by fans and became an Atlanta-area sensation, helping to lead the club to a record of 90-72.\n</p><p>However, the season would end on a sour note as the Braves lost the <a href="/wiki/2005_National_League_Division_Series" title="2005 National League Division Series">National League Division Series</a> to the <a href="/wiki/2005_Houston_Astros_season" title="2005 Houston Astros season">Astros</a> in four games. In Game 4, with the Braves leading by 5 in the eighth inning, the Astros battled back with a <a href="/wiki/Lance_Berkman" title="Lance Berkman">Lance Berkman</a> <a href="/wiki/Grand_slam_(baseball)" title="Grand slam (baseball)">grand slam</a> and a two-out, ninth inning <a href="/wiki/Brad_Ausmus" title="Brad Ausmus">Brad Ausmus</a> home run off of Braves closer <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Farnsworth" title="Kyle Farnsworth">Kyle Farnsworth</a>. The game didn\'t end until the 18th inning, becoming the longest game in playoff history at 5 hours 50 minutes. <a href="/wiki/Chris_Burke_(baseball)" title="Chris Burke (baseball)">Chris Burke</a> ended the marathon with a home run off of <a href="/wiki/Joey_Devine" title="Joey Devine">Joey Devine</a>.\n</p><p>After the 2005 season, the Braves lost their long-time pitching coach <a href="/wiki/Leo_Mazzone" title="Leo Mazzone">Leo Mazzone</a>, who left to go to the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Roger_McDowell" title="Roger McDowell">Roger McDowell</a> took his place in the Atlanta dugout. Unable to re-sign shortstop <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Furcal" title="Rafael Furcal">Rafael Furcal</a>, the Braves acquired shortstop <a href="/wiki/%C3%89dgar_Renter%C3%ADa" title="\xc3\x89dgar Renter\xc3\xada">\xc3\x89dgar Renter\xc3\xada</a> from the Boston Red Sox.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006:_Struggles">2006: Struggles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 2006: Struggles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2006_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="2006 Atlanta Braves season">2006 Atlanta Braves season</a></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/2006_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2006 Major League Baseball season">2006</a>, the Braves did not perform at the level they had grown accustomed to. Due to an offensive slump, injuries to their starting rotation, and subpar bullpen performances, the Braves compiled a 6-21 record for the month of June, the worst month ever in the city of Atlanta, and just percentage points better than the Boston Braves of May 1935 (4-20).\n</p><p>The Braves made their move in July, going 14-10. However, the team remained in the bottom half of the NL East and trailed the <a href="/wiki/2006_New_York_Mets_season" title="2006 New York Mets season">Mets</a> by a double-digit deficit for much of the season (13 games at the <a href="/wiki/2006_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="2006 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">all-star break</a>). However, despite their struggles, the Braves entered the break down by only six and a half games to the Dodgers for the NL Wild Card slot after winning seven of their last ten games.\n</p><p>After the break, the Braves came out with their bats swinging, setting many franchise records. They won five straight, sweeping the <a href="/wiki/2006_San_Diego_Padres_season" title="2006 San Diego Padres season">Padres</a> and taking two from the <a href="/wiki/2006_St._Louis_Cardinals_season" title="2006 St. Louis Cardinals season">Cardinals</a>, tallying a total of 65 runs in that span. The 65 runs in five games is the best by the franchise since 1897, when the Boston Beaneaters totaled 78, including 25 in one game and 21 in another, from May 31-June 3; the 2006 Braves also became the first team since the <a href="/wiki/1930_New_York_Yankees_season" title="1930 New York Yankees season">1930 New York Yankees</a> to score ten runs or more in five straight games. The Braves had a total of 81 hits during their five-game run and 98 hits in their last six games, going back to an 8-3 victory over <a href="/wiki/2006_Cincinnati_Reds_season" title="2006 Cincinnati Reds season">Cincinnati</a> on July 9, the last game before the all-star break. Additionally, Chipper Jones was able to maintain a 20-game hitting streak and tie <a href="/wiki/Paul_Waner" title="Paul Waner">Paul Waner</a>\'s 69-year-old Major League record with a 14-game extra-base hit streak. (<i>The Sporting News Baseball Record Book</i>, 2007, p.&#160;29)\n</p><p>The Braves made their first trade of the season on July 20 to shore up the bullpen, sending Class A Rome catcher Max Ramirez to Cleveland for closer <a href="/wiki/Bob_Wickman" title="Bob Wickman">Bob Wickman</a>. He served as the Braves\' closer for the remainder of the season, taking over for an embattled <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Sosa" title="Jorge Sosa">Jorge Sosa</a>, who was subsequently traded on the July 31 trade deadline for St. Louis minor league pitcher Rich Scalamandre.\n</p><p>On July 29, the Braves traded reserve third baseman/shortstop <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Betemit" title="Wilson Betemit">Wilson Betemit</a> to the Los Angeles Dodgers for reliever <a href="/wiki/Danys_B%C3%A1ez" title="Danys B\xc3\xa1ez">Danys B\xc3\xa1ez</a> and infielder <a href="/wiki/Willy_Aybar" title="Willy Aybar">Willy Aybar</a>. The move came on the night that starting third baseman <a href="/wiki/Chipper_Jones" title="Chipper Jones">Chipper Jones</a> went on the 15-day <a href="/wiki/Disabled_list" title="Disabled list">disabled list</a> with a strained oblique muscle. With Betemit gone, Atlanta called up infielder <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Francisco_Pe%C3%B1a_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tony Francisco Pe\xc3\xb1a (baseball) (page does not exist)">Tony Pe\xc3\xb1a Jr.</a> from AAA Richmond to supplement <a href="/wiki/Pete_Orr" title="Pete Orr">Pete Orr</a>.\n</p><p>Before the expansion of rosters on September 1, the Braves acquired <a href="/wiki/Daryle_Ward" title="Daryle Ward">Daryle Ward</a> from the Washington Nationals for Class A Myrtle Beach pitcher <a href="/wiki/Luis_Atilano" title="Luis Atilano">Luis Atilano</a>, in hopes that he would be a valuable pinch-hitter in the postseason.\n</p><p>However, on September 18, the New York Mets\' win over the <a href="/wiki/2006_Florida_Marlins_season" title="2006 Florida Marlins season">Florida Marlins</a> mathematically eliminated the Braves from winning the NL East, ending the Braves\' eleven-year reign over the NL East. On September 24, the Braves\' loss to the <a href="/wiki/2006_Colorado_Rockies_season" title="2006 Colorado Rockies season">Colorado Rockies</a> mathematically eliminated the Braves from winning the NL Wild Card, making 2006 the first year that the Braves would not compete in the postseason since 1990, not counting the strike-shortened 1994 season.\n</p><p>Also, a loss to the Mets on September 28 guaranteed the Braves their first losing season since 1990. Although the Braves won two of their last three games against the Astros, including rookie <a href="/wiki/Chuck_James" title="Chuck James">Chuck James</a> besting <a href="/wiki/Roger_Clemens" title="Roger Clemens">Roger Clemens</a>, Atlanta finished the season in third place, one game ahead of the Marlins, at 79-83.\n</p><p>After the season, the Atlanta coaching staff underwent a few changes. <a href="/wiki/Brian_Snitker" title="Brian Snitker">Brian Snitker</a> became the third base coach after <a href="/wiki/Fredi_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="Fredi Gonz\xc3\xa1lez">Fredi Gonz\xc3\xa1lez</a> left to become the manager for the Florida Marlins. <a href="/wiki/Chino_Cadahia" title="Chino Cadahia">Chino Cadahia</a> replaced <a href="/wiki/Pat_Corrales" title="Pat Corrales">Pat Corrales</a> as bench coach and former catcher <a href="/wiki/Eddie_P%C3%A9rez_(baseball)" title="Eddie P\xc3\xa9rez (baseball)">Eddie Perez</a> became the new bullpen coach, replacing <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Dews" title="Bobby Dews">Bobby Dews</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_to_Liberty_Media">Sale to Liberty Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sale to Liberty Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In December 2005, team owner <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a>, who inherited the Braves after purchasing TBS in 1996, announced it was placing the team for sale. <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media</a> began negotiations to purchase the team.\n</p><p>In February 2007, after more than a year of negotiations, Time Warner agreed to a deal that would sell the Braves to <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media Group</a> (a company which owned a large amount of stock in <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Warner">Time Warner, Inc.</a>), pending approval by 75 percent of MLB owners and the Commissioner of Baseball, <a href="/wiki/Bud_Selig" title="Bud Selig">Bud Selig</a>. The deal includes the exchange of the Braves, and $1 billion cash, for the large block of Time Warner stock held by Liberty Media. Team President Terry McGuirk anticipated no change in the current front office structure, personnel, or day-to-day operations of the Braves. Liberty Media is not expected to take any type of "active" ownership in terms of day-to-day operations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On May 16, 2007, Major League Baseball\'s owners approved the sale of the Braves from Time Warner to Liberty Media.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007:_A_good_start....">2007: A good start....</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 2007: A good start....">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2007_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="2007 Atlanta Braves season">2007 Atlanta Braves season</a></div>\n<p>The Braves made their first moves by re-signing <a href="/wiki/Bob_Wickman" title="Bob Wickman">Bob Wickman</a> to a one-year deal and picking up <a href="/wiki/John_Smoltz" title="John Smoltz">John Smoltz</a>\'s option in September 2006. Then they traded starting pitcher <a href="/wiki/Horacio_Ram%C3%ADrez" title="Horacio Ram\xc3\xadrez">Horacio Ram\xc3\xadrez</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> for pitcher <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Soriano" title="Rafael Soriano">Rafael Soriano</a>, an American League reliever with a solid 2.20 ERA in 2006. They also denied arbitration to pitcher <a href="/wiki/Chris_Reitsma" title="Chris Reitsma">Chris Reitsma</a> and second baseman <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Giles" title="Marcus Giles">Marcus Giles</a>. Then the Braves signed utility-man <a href="/wiki/Chris_Woodward" title="Chris Woodward">Chris Woodward</a> to fill a spot on the bench. The biggest trade in the offseason involved first baseman <a href="/wiki/Adam_LaRoche" title="Adam LaRoche">Adam LaRoche</a> and a minor league player for <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> closer <a href="/wiki/Mike_Gonz%C3%A1lez_(pitcher)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mike Gonz\xc3\xa1lez (pitcher)">Mike Gonz\xc3\xa1lez</a> and a minor league infielder, <a href="/wiki/Brent_Lillibridge" title="Brent Lillibridge">Brent Lillibridge</a>. Gonzalez, who converted 24 of 24 save opportunities in 2006, joined Soriano as a set-up man for Wickman in the bullpen. The team then signed first baseman <a href="/wiki/Craig_Wilson_(first_baseman)" title="Craig Wilson (first baseman)">Craig Wilson</a> to a one-year deal to platoon with <a href="/wiki/Scott_Thorman" title="Scott Thorman">Scott Thorman</a>. The Braves also had solid relievers in <a href="/wiki/Macay_McBride" title="Macay McBride">Macay McBride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blaine_Boyer" title="Blaine Boyer">Blaine Boyer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tyler_Yates" title="Tyler Yates">Tyler Yates</a>. In addition, the majority of the Braves\' offense, which was second in the NL in runs scored in 2006, returned in 2007.  However, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Hampton" title="Mike Hampton">Mike Hampton</a> was sidelined for the entire 2007 season with yet another surgery. Mike Gonz\xc3\xa1lez was later sidelined for the season while recovering from Tommy John surgery.\n</p><p>The Braves\' bullpen and offense came through in the clutch early on, helping the Braves to a 7-1 start, their best start since winning the World Series in 1995. The team finished April with a 16-9 record, but struggled during May, finishing 14-14. The Braves also struggled during <a href="/wiki/Interleague_play" title="Interleague play">interleague play</a>, finishing with an NL-worst 4-11 record. On June 24, the Braves fell to .500 for the first time in the 2007 season, but rebounded by winning the next 5 games.\n</p><p>On July 5, Chipper Jones surpassed Dale Murphy for the Atlanta club record of 372 home runs by belting two against the <a href="/wiki/2007_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="2007 Los Angeles Dodgers season">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>. On July 31, 2007, the Braves finalized the deal to acquire slugger first baseman <a href="/wiki/Mark_Teixeira" title="Mark Teixeira">Mark Teixeira</a> and LHP Ron Mahay from the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> for <a href="/wiki/Jarrod_Saltalamacchia" title="Jarrod Saltalamacchia">Jarrod Saltalamacchia</a> and four minor-leaguers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  The Braves also acquired <a href="/wiki/Octavio_Dotel" title="Octavio Dotel">Octavio Dotel</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a> for <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Davies_(baseball)" title="Kyle Davies (baseball)">Kyle Davies</a> and also traded LHP <a href="/wiki/Wilfredo_Ledezma" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilfredo Ledezma">Wilfredo Ledezma</a> and RHP Will Startup to the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> for <a href="/wiki/Royce_Ring" title="Royce Ring">Royce Ring</a>. On August 19, 2007 John Smoltz passed <a href="/wiki/Phil_Niekro" title="Phil Niekro">Phil Niekro</a> for 1st place on the Braves\' all-time strikeout list. After struggling during the second half of the 2007 season, Atlanta finished over .500 and missed the post season again. On October 12, 2007, John Schuerholz stepped aside from his General Manager position to take over as the team\'s president.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Schuerholz\'s former Assistant GM Frank Wren will take over as the new Atlanta General Manager.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_off-season_moves">2008 off-season moves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 2008 off-season moves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Wren\'s first major transaction was the announcement that the team would not re-sign center fielder <a href="/wiki/Andruw_Jones" title="Andruw Jones">Andruw Jones</a> (who later would sign with the Dodgers).  The second major move was acquiring OF <a href="/wiki/Gorkys_Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Gorkys Hern\xc3\xa1ndez">Gorkys Hern\xc3\xa1ndez</a> and RHP <a href="/wiki/Jair_Jurrjens" title="Jair Jurrjens">Jair Jurrjens</a> from the Detroit Tigers in exchange for SS <a href="/wiki/%C3%89dgar_Renter%C3%ADa" title="\xc3\x89dgar Renter\xc3\xada">\xc3\x89dgar Renter\xc3\xada</a> and cash considerations.  His third move was signing LHP <a href="/wiki/Tom_Glavine" title="Tom Glavine">Tom Glavine</a> to a one-year contract.\n</p><p>The team\'s first new move for 2008 was acquiring OF <a href="/wiki/Mark_Kotsay" title="Mark Kotsay">Mark Kotsay</a> from the A\'s (to replace Jones) in exchange for RHP <a href="/wiki/Joey_Devine" title="Joey Devine">Joey Devine</a>, RHP <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamie_Richmond&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jamie Richmond (page does not exist)">Jamie Richmond</a> and cash considerations. Days later, Wren traded <a href="/wiki/Willy_Aybar" title="Willy Aybar">Willy Aybar</a>, outfielder <a href="/wiki/Tom_Lindsey" title="Tom Lindsey">Tom Lindsey</a>, and infielder <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chase_Fontaine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chase Fontaine (page does not exist)">Chase Fontaine</a> to the Rays in exchange for left-hand reliever <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Ridgway" title="Jeff Ridgway">Jeff Ridgway</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
