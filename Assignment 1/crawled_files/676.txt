class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dorset_ashmore_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ashmore pond</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Cranborne Chase</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Cranborne_Chase&amp;params=ST970180_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">ST970180</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a> in central southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, straddling the counties <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>. The plateau is part of the English <a href="/wiki/Chalk_Formation" class="mw-redirect" title="Chalk Formation">Chalk Formation</a> and is adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Wiltshire_Downs" title="West Wiltshire Downs">West Wiltshire Downs</a> in the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Dorset_Downs" title="Dorset Downs">Dorset Downs</a> to the south west. The <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">scarp</a> slope of the hills faces the <a href="/wiki/Blackmore_Vale" title="Blackmore Vale">Blackmore Vale</a> to the west, and to some extent the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Wardour" title="Vale of Wardour">Vale of Wardour</a> to the north.  The chalk gently slopes south and dips under clays and gravels.\n</p><p>An area of 379 square miles (981 square kilometres) of Cranborne Chase and the West Wiltshire Downs has been designated an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB), the sixth largest AONB in the country.  The highest point is <a href="/wiki/Win_Green" class="mw-redirect" title="Win Green">Win Green</a>, in Wiltshire, at 910&#160;ft (277 m).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cranborne_Chase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dorset_brings_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Dorset_brings_01.jpg/220px-Dorset_brings_01.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Dorset_brings_01.jpg/330px-Dorset_brings_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Dorset_brings_01.jpg/440px-Dorset_brings_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="525" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dorset_brings_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Badbury Rings</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">downland</a> has a long history with many earthworks and <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Neolithic age">Neolithic age</a> onwards.  The dense woodland originally covering the downs would have gradually been cleared by the first farmers, but would have grown back repeatedly over the centuries as soils became exhausted and the agricultural carrying capacity of the land was exceeded several times over the course of six millennia. Much of the area therefore remained wooded from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.\n</p><p>Analysis of remains found in some of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> burial mounds, by experts at <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth_University" title="Bournemouth University">Bournemouth University</a>, has revealled that many of the bones had small holes drilled in then, enabling them, it is hypothesised, to have been articulated by means of wooden pegs, i.e. the skeletons were prevented from falling apart during repeated removal and re-burial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>There are many Neolithic and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> monuments, including the henge monuments at <a href="/wiki/Knowlton_Circles" title="Knowlton Circles">Knowlton</a> and the remains of a number of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> settlements on the downs, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill fort</a> at <a href="/wiki/Badbury_Rings" title="Badbury Rings">Badbury Rings</a> (<i>Vindocladia</i>).  There is a <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villa</a> which has been dug by archaeological television programme <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Team" title="Time Team">Time Team</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  During the <a href="/wiki/Saxon_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon people">Saxon</a> invasion of England the <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> kept the invaders out of Dorset by building <a href="/wiki/Bokerley_Dyke" title="Bokerley Dyke">Bokerley Dyke</a>, a defensive ditch, across the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Road" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Road">Roman Road</a> that runs across the downs from <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Dorset" title="Dorchester, Dorset">Dorchester</a> to <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum" title="Old Sarum">Old Sarum</a>.\n</p><p>The downs have been sparsely populated since Saxon times, largely preserving archaeology until World War II when the need for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> land outweighed the archaeological importance.  It was here that <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Pitt_Rivers" title="Augustus Pitt Rivers">Augustus Pitt Rivers</a> developed modern archaeological field work in the 19th century.\n</p><p>The downs are named after the village <a href="/wiki/Cranborne" title="Cranborne">Cranborne</a>, founded by the Saxons, which had a <a href="/wiki/Manor_house" title="Manor house">manor house</a> and a small <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a>.  The word "chase" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunts</a>, frequented by royalty (including Kings <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a>), which took place on the downs.\n</p><p>The first known owner of the chase is thought to have been <a href="/wiki/Brictric" title="Brictric">Brictric</a>. His possessions were confiscated by <a href="/wiki/Matilda_of_Flanders" title="Matilda of Flanders">Queen Matilda</a>, wife of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>; on her death, it passed to the Crown, and was granted, with other lands forming the feudal barony of Gloucester, to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fitzhamon" title="Robert Fitzhamon">Robert Fitzhamon</a> in 1083. This passed to his son-in-law <a href="/wiki/Robert,_1st_Earl_of_Gloucester" title="Robert, 1st Earl of Gloucester">Robert, 1st Earl of Gloucester</a>. Ownership of the chase passed through successive <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Gloucester" title="Earl of Gloucester">Earls of Gloucester</a> until the death of <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_de_Clare,_8th_Earl_of_Gloucester" title="Gilbert de Clare, 8th Earl of Gloucester">Gilbert de Clare, 8th Earl of Gloucester</a> in 1314. The chase passed to his sister <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_de_Clare" title="Elizabeth de Clare">Elizabeth de Clare</a> (d. 1360), and thence to her granddaughter <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_de_Burgh,_4th_Countess_of_Ulster" title="Elizabeth de Burgh, 4th Countess of Ulster">Elizabeth de Burgh, 4th Countess of Ulster</a> (d. 1363). The Countess was succeeded by her daughter <a href="/wiki/Philippa,_5th_Countess_of_Ulster" title="Philippa, 5th Countess of Ulster">Philippa, 5th Countess of Ulster</a> (d. 1382), wife of <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Mortimer,_3rd_Earl_of_March" title="Edmund Mortimer, 3rd Earl of March">Edmund Mortimer, 3rd Earl of March</a>. The chase was owned by successive Earls of March until 1461, when the last Earl was crowned as <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV_of_England" title="Edward IV of England">Edward IV of England</a>. Cranborne Chase remained crown property until 1616, when it was granted to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cecil,_1st_Earl_of_Salisbury" title="Robert Cecil, 1st Earl of Salisbury">Robert Cecil, 1st Earl of Salisbury</a>. His great-grandson, the <a href="/wiki/James_Cecil,_3rd_Earl_of_Salisbury" title="James Cecil, 3rd Earl of Salisbury">3rd Earl</a>, sold it to <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Ashley_Cooper,_1st_Earl_of_Shaftesbury" title="Anthony Ashley Cooper, 1st Earl of Shaftesbury">Anthony Ashley Cooper, 1st Earl of Shaftesbury</a> in 1671. His son, the <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Ashley-Cooper,_2nd_Earl_of_Shaftesbury" title="Anthony Ashley-Cooper, 2nd Earl of Shaftesbury">2nd Earl</a>, sold it in 1692 to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Freke_(died_1701)" title="Thomas Freke (died 1701)">Thomas Freke</a> (d. 1701), who bequeathed it to Elizabeth Freke (the wife of his kinsman, also <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Freke_(1660%E2%80%931721)" title="Thomas Freke (1660\xe2\x80\x931721)">Thomas Freke</a>) and her father Thomas Pile, with reversion to <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Pitt_(1663-1735)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="George Pitt (1663-1735) (page does not exist)">George Pitt</a> should she die without children. Pitt inherited Cranborne Chase from her in 1714, and it passed from father to son in the Pitt family to his great-grandson <a href="/wiki/George_Pitt,_2nd_Baron_Rivers" title="George Pitt, 2nd Baron Rivers">George Pitt, 2nd Baron Rivers</a>, after whose death it was disfranchised.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Much of the Chase is still owned by large estates such as <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Lacy" title="Kingston Lacy">Kingston Lacy</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cranborne_Chase_School" title="Cranborne Chase School">Cranborne Chase School</a>, a former boarding school for girls, was based at two locations in Cranborne Chase: at <a href="/wiki/Crichel_House" title="Crichel House">Crichel House</a> near the village of <a href="/wiki/Moor_Crichel" title="Moor Crichel">Moor Crichel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> from 1946 to 1961, and then at <a href="/wiki/New_Wardour_Castle" title="New Wardour Castle">New Wardour Castle</a> in the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Wardour,_Wiltshire" title="Wardour, Wiltshire">Wardour</a> (near the village of <a href="/wiki/Tisbury,_Wiltshire" title="Tisbury, Wiltshire">Tisbury</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, until the School\'s closure in 1990.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cranborne_Chase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_region">Natural region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cranborne_Chase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cranborne Chase is part of a <a href="/wiki/National_Character_Area" title="National Character Area">National Character Area</a> (No. 134), "<a href="/wiki/Dorset_Downs" title="Dorset Downs">Dorset Downs</a> and Cranborne Chase", designated by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hills">Hills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cranborne_Chase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The landscape of Cranborne Chase is remarkably varied, its most dramatic scenery being near the boundary between Dorset and Wiltshire where the chalk downland rises sharply  to the rounded summits of <a href="/wiki/Breeze_Hill" title="Breeze Hill">Breeze Hill</a> (262 m), with the hairpin bends of the B3081 climbing Zig Zag Hill on its northwestern flanks, and <a href="/wiki/Win_Green" class="mw-redirect" title="Win Green">Win Green</a> (277 m). Nearby, another prominent top, <a href="/wiki/Melbury_Hill" title="Melbury Hill">Melbury Hill</a> (263 m) above <a href="/wiki/Melbury_Abbas" title="Melbury Abbas">Melbury Abbas</a>, "appears almost like an island rising above the flat, sea-like expanses of" <a href="/wiki/Compton_Abbas" title="Compton Abbas">Compton Abbas</a> airfield.<sup id="cite_ref-DL_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DL-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Further south are two more summits on an outlier of the Chase that define its southern limits: <a href="/wiki/Hambledon_Hill" title="Hambledon Hill">Hambledon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hod_Hill" title="Hod Hill">Hod Hills</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DL_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DL-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cranborne_Chase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An area of 1,115 acres (451&#160;ha) of Cranborne Chase has been <a href="/wiki/SSSI_notification" class="mw-redirect" title="SSSI notification">notified</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Biological_Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological Site of Special Scientific Interest">biological Site of Special Scientific Interest</a>, notification initially taking place in 1975.\n</p><p>As some of the wooded areas in the chase are centuries old, they possess a diverse ground flora that is associated with such sites. The area is one of the richest in southern England for numbers of <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> species; over 160 have been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cranborne_Chase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bowerchalke" title="Bowerchalke">Bowerchalke</a> - Geological profile of a Lower Greensand inlier on <a href="/wiki/Chalk_grassland" class="mw-redirect" title="Chalk grassland">chalklands</a> of Cranborne Chase</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cranborne_Chase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20050219094735/http://www.channel4.com/history/timeteam/2004_cran_t.html">"Channel4.com - Time Team - Cranborne Chase"</a>. 19 February 2005.
