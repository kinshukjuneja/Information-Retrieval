links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Pertaining to the ability of clear-sightedness, clairvoyance refers to the <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">paranormal</a> ability to see persons and events that are distant in time or space.  It can be divided into roughly three classes: <a href="/wiki/Precognition" title="Precognition">precognition</a>, the ability to perceive or predict future events, <a href="/wiki/Retrocognition" title="Retrocognition">retrocognition</a>, the ability to see past events, and <a href="/wiki/Remote_viewing" title="Remote viewing">remote viewing</a>, the perception of contemporary events happening outside of the range of normal perception.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_history">In history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Throughout history, there have been numerous places and times in which people have claimed themselves or others to be clairvoyant.\n</p><p>A number of Christian saints were said to be able to see or know things that were far removed from their immediate sensory perception as a kind of gift from God, including <a href="/wiki/Columba_of_Iona" class="mw-redirect" title="Columba of Iona">Columba of Iona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Padre_Pio" title="Padre Pio">Padre Pio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Catherine_Emmerich" title="Anne Catherine Emmerich">Anne Catherine Emmerich</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a> in the Gospels is also recorded as being able to know things that were far removed from his immediate human perception.\n</p><p>In other religions, similar stories of certain individuals being able to see things far removed from their immediate sensory perception are commonplace, especially within pagan religions where <a href="/wiki/Oracle" title="Oracle">oracles</a> were used. <a href="/wiki/Prophecy" title="Prophecy">Prophecy</a> often involved some degree of clairvoyance, especially when future events were predicted.\n</p><p>In most of these cases, however, the ability to see things was attributed to a higher power and not thought of as an ability that lay within the person himself.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jainism">Jainism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jainism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jain_epistemology" title="Jain epistemology">Jain epistemology</a></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>, clairvoyance is regarded as one of the five kinds of knowledge. The beings of hell and heaven (<a href="/wiki/Deva_(Jainism)" title="Deva (Jainism)">devas</a>) are said to possess clairvoyance by birth. According to Jain text <a href="/wiki/Sarv%C4%81rthasiddhi" title="Sarv\xc4\x81rthasiddhi">Sarv\xc4\x81rthasiddhi</a>, "this kind of knowledge has been called <i>avadhi</i> as it ascertains matter in downward range or knows objects within limits".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTES._A._Jain199216_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTES._A._Jain199216-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parapsychology">Parapsychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parapsychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_research">Early research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The earliest record of somnambulistic clairvoyance is credited to the <a href="/wiki/Armand-Marie-Jacques_de_Chastenet,_Marquis_of_Puys%C3%A9gur" class="mw-redirect" title="Armand-Marie-Jacques de Chastenet, Marquis of Puys\xc3\xa9gur">Marquis de Puys\xc3\xa9gur</a>, a follower of <a href="/wiki/Franz_Mesmer" title="Franz Mesmer">Franz Mesmer</a>, who in 1784 was treating a local dull-witted peasant named Victor Race. During treatment, Race reportedly would go into trance and undergo a personality change, becoming fluent and articulate, and giving diagnosis and prescription for his own disease as well as those of others.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Clairvoyance was a reported ability of some mediums during the <a href="/wiki/Spiritualism" title="Spiritualism">spiritualist</a> period of the late 19th and early 20th centuries, and psychics of many descriptions have claimed clairvoyant ability up to the present day.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg/220px-Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg/330px-Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg/440px-Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="627" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Character_reader_and_Clairvoyant.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Character reader and clairvoyant in a British travelling show of the 1940s, collected by Arthur James Fenwick (1878\xe2\x80\x931957)</div></div></div>\n<p>Early researchers of clairvoyance included <a href="/wiki/William_Gregory_(chemist)" title="William Gregory (chemist)">William Gregory</a>, Gustav Pagenstecher, and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Tischner" title="Rudolf Tischner">Rudolf Tischner</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Clairvoyance experiments were reported in 1884 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Richet" title="Charles Richet">Charles Richet</a>. Playing cards were enclosed in envelopes and a subject put under hypnosis attempted to identify them. The subject was reported to have been successful in a series of 133 trials but the results dropped to chance level when performed before a group of scientists in Cambridge. J. M. Peirce and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Charles_Pickering" title="Edward Charles Pickering">E. C. Pickering</a> reported a similar experiment in which they tested 36 subjects over 23,384 trials which did not obtain above chance scores.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ivor_Lloyd_Tuckett" title="Ivor Lloyd Tuckett">Ivor Lloyd Tuckett</a> (1911) and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCabe" title="Joseph McCabe">Joseph McCabe</a> (1920) analyzed early cases of clairvoyance and came to the conclusion they were best explained by coincidence or fraud.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1919, the magician <a href="/wiki/P._T._Selbit" title="P. T. Selbit">P. T. Selbit</a> staged a s\xc3\xa9ance at his own flat in <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury" title="Bloomsbury">Bloomsbury</a>. The spiritualist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a> attended the s\xc3\xa9ance and declared the clairvoyance manifestations to be genuine.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A significant development in clairvoyance research came when <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks_Rhine" title="Joseph Banks Rhine">J. B. Rhine</a>, a parapsychologist at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>, introduced a standard methodology, with a standard statistical approach to analyzing data, as part of his research into <a href="/wiki/Extrasensory_perception" title="Extrasensory perception">extrasensory perception</a>. A number of psychological departments attempted to repeat Rhine\'s experiments with failure. W. S. Cox (1936) from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> with 132 subjects produced 25,064 trials in a playing card ESP experiment. Cox concluded "There is no evidence of extrasensory perception either in the \'average man\' or of the group investigated or in any particular individual of that group. The discrepancy between these results and those obtained by Rhine is due either to uncontrollable factors in experimental procedure or to the difference in the subjects."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Four other psychological departments failed to replicate Rhine\'s results.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It was revealed that Rhine\'s experiments contained methodological flaws and procedural errors.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Eileen_Garrett" class="mw-redirect" title="Eileen Garrett">Eileen Garrett</a> was tested by Rhine at Duke University in 1933 with <a href="/wiki/Zener_cards" title="Zener cards">Zener cards</a>. Certain symbols that were placed on the cards and sealed in an envelope, and she was asked to guess their contents. She performed poorly and later criticized the tests by claiming the cards lacked a <a href="/wiki/Energy_(esotericism)" title="Energy (esotericism)">psychic energy</a> called "energy stimulus" and that she could not perform clairvoyance to order.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The parapsychologist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Soal" title="Samuel Soal">Samuel Soal</a> and his colleagues tested Garrett in May, 1937. Most of the experiments were carried out in the Psychological Laboratory at the <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>. A total of over 12,000 guesses were recorded but Garrett failed to produce above chance level.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In his report Soal wrote "In the case of Mrs. Eileen Garrett we fail to find the slightest confirmation of Dr. J. B. Rhine\'s remarkable claims relating to her alleged powers of extra-sensory perception. Not only did she fail when I took charge of the experiments, but she failed equally when four other carefully trained experimenters took my place."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remote_viewing">Remote viewing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Remote viewing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Remote_viewing" title="Remote viewing">Remote viewing</a> also known as remote sensing, remote perception, telesthesia and travelling clairvoyance is the alleged paranormal ability to perceive a remote or hidden target without support of the senses.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A well known study of remote viewing in recent times has been the US government-funded project at the <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">Stanford Research Institute</a> during the 1970s through the mid-1990s. In 1972, <a href="/wiki/Harold_E._Puthoff" title="Harold E. Puthoff">Harold Puthoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russell_Targ" title="Russell Targ">Russell Targ</a> initiated a series of human subject studies to determine whether participants (the <i>viewers</i> or <i>percipients</i>) could reliably identify and accurately describe salient features of remote locations or <i>targets</i>. In the early studies, a human <i>sender</i> was typically present at the remote location, as part of the experiment protocol. A three-step process was used, the first step being to randomly select the target conditions to be experienced by the senders. Secondly, in the viewing step, participants were asked to verbally express or sketch their impressions of the remote scene. Thirdly, in the judging step, these descriptions were matched by separate judges, as closely as possible, with the intended targets. The term <a href="/wiki/Remote_viewing" title="Remote viewing">remote viewing</a> was coined to describe this overall process. The first paper by Puthoff and Targ on remote viewing was published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> in March 1974; in it, the team reported some degree of remote viewing success.<sup id="cite_ref-nat251_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nat251-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> After the publication of these findings, other attempts to replicate the experiments were carried out <sup id="cite_ref-hast1_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hast1-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> with remotely linked groups using computer conferencing.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The psychologists <a href="/wiki/David_Marks_(psychologist)" title="David Marks (psychologist)">David Marks</a> and Richard Kammann attempted to replicate Targ and Puthoff\'s remote viewing experiments that were carried out in the 1970s at the Stanford Research Institute. In a series of 35 studies, they were unable to replicate the results so investigated the procedure of the original experiments. Marks and Kammann discovered that the notes given to the judges in Targ and Puthoff\'s experiments contained clues as to which order they were carried out, such as referring to yesterday\'s two targets, or they had the date of the session written at the top of the page. They concluded that these clues were the reason for the experiment\'s high hit rates.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Marks was able to achieve 100 per cent accuracy without visiting any of the sites himself but by using cues.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Randi" title="James Randi">James Randi</a> has written controlled tests by several other researchers, eliminating several sources of cuing and extraneous evidence present in the original tests, produced negative results. Students were also able to solve Puthoff and Targ\'s locations from the clues that had inadvertently been included in the transcripts.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1980, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Tart" title="Charles Tart">Charles Tart</a> claimed that a rejudging of the transcripts from one of Targ and Puthoff\'s experiments revealed an above-chance result.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Targ and Puthoff again refused to provide copies of the transcripts and it was not until July 1985 that they were made available for study when it was discovered they still contained <a href="/wiki/Sensory_cue" title="Sensory cue">sensory cues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Marks and Christopher Scott (1986) wrote "considering the importance for the remote viewing hypothesis of adequate cue removal, Tart\'s failure to perform this basic task seems beyond comprehension. As previously concluded, remote viewing has not been demonstrated in the experiments conducted by Puthoff and Targ, only the repeated failure of the investigators to remove sensory cues."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1982 <a href="/wiki/Robert_G._Jahn" title="Robert G. Jahn">Robert Jahn</a>, then Dean of the School of Engineering at Princeton University wrote a comprehensive review of psychic phenomena from an engineering perspective. His paper included numerous references to remote viewing studies at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Statistical flaws in his work have been proposed by others in the parapsychological community and within the general scientific community.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffers2006_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffers2006-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_reception">Scientific reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scientific reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to scientific research, clairvoyance is generally explained as the result of <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">confirmation bias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Observer-expectancy_effect" title="Observer-expectancy effect">expectancy bias</a>, fraud, <a href="/wiki/Hallucination" title="Hallucination">hallucination</a>, self-<a href="/wiki/Delusion" title="Delusion">delusion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sensory_leakage" title="Sensory leakage">sensory leakage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subjective_validation" title="Subjective validation">subjective validation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wishful_thinking" title="Wishful thinking">wishful thinking</a> or failures to appreciate the base rate of chance occurrences and not as a paranormal power.<sup id="cite_ref-skepdic1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skepdic1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Parapsychology is regarded by the scientific community as a <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscience</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 1988, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> concluded "The committee finds no scientific justification from research conducted over a period of 130 years, for the existence of parapsychological phenomena."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Scientific_skepticism" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific skepticism">Skeptics</a> say that if clairvoyance were a reality it would have become abundantly clear. They also contend that those who believe in <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">paranormal</a> phenomena do so for merely psychological reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/David_Myers_(academic)" title="David Myers (academic)">David G. Myers</a> (<i>Psychology,</i> 8th ed.):\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The search for a valid and reliable test of clairvoyance has resulted in thousands of experiments. One controlled procedure has invited \'senders\' to telepathically transmit one of four visual images to \'receivers\' deprived of sensation in a nearby chamber (Bem &amp; Honorton, 1994). The result? A reported 32 percent accurate response rate, surpassing the chance rate of 25 percent. But follow-up studies have (depending on who was summarizing the results) failed to replicate the phenomenon or produced mixed results (Bem &amp; others, 2001; Milton &amp; Wiseman, 2002; Storm, 2000, 2003).<br /><br />One skeptic, magician <a href="/wiki/James_Randi" title="James Randi">James Randi</a>, has a longstanding offer\xe2\x80\x94now <a href="/wiki/One_Million_Dollar_Paranormal_Challenge" title="One Million Dollar Paranormal Challenge">U.S. $1 million</a>\xe2\x80\x94"to anyone who proves a genuine psychic power under proper observing conditions" (Randi, 1999). French, Australian, and Indian groups have parallel offers of up to 200,000 euros to anyone with demonstrable paranormal abilities (CFI, 2003). Large as these sums are, the scientific seal of approval would be worth far more to anyone whose claims could be authenticated. To refute those who say there is no ESP, one need only produce a single person who can demonstrate a single, reproducible ESP phenomenon. So far, no such person has emerged. Randi\'s offer has been publicized for three decades and dozens of people have been tested, sometimes under the scrutiny of an independent panel of judges. Still, nothing.  "People\'s desire to believe in the paranormal is stronger than all the evidence that it does not exist." <a href="/wiki/Susan_Blackmore" title="Susan Blackmore">Susan Blackmore</a>, "Blackmore\'s first law", 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Clairvoyance is considered <a href="/wiki/Hallucination" title="Hallucination">hallucination</a> by mainstream <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clairvoyance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 20em; -webkit-column-width: 20em; column-width: 20em;">\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Astral_projection" title="Astral projection">Astral projection</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Aura_(paranormal)" title="Aura (paranormal)">Aura</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/C._W._Leadbeater#Clairvoyance" class="mw-redirect" title="C.
