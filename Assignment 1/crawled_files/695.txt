class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cross_-_panoramio_(15).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Cross_-_panoramio_%2815%29.jpg/220px-Cross_-_panoramio_%2815%29.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Cross_-_panoramio_%2815%29.jpg/330px-Cross_-_panoramio_%2815%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Cross_-_panoramio_%2815%29.jpg/440px-Cross_-_panoramio_%2815%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2155" data-file-height="2873" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cross_-_panoramio_(15).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Padstow war memorial</div></div></div>\n<p>Padstow was originally named <i>Petroc-stow</i>, Petroc-stowe, or \'Petrock\'s Place\', after the Welsh missionary <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petroc" title="Saint Petroc">Saint Petroc</a>, who landed at <a href="/wiki/Trebetherick" title="Trebetherick">Trebetherick</a> around <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">AD</a> 500. After his death a monastery (Lanwethinoc, the church of Wethinoc, an earlier holy man) was established here which was of great importance until "Petroces stow" (probably Padstow) was raided by the Vikings in 981, according to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Whether as a result of this attack or later, the monks moved inland to <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a>, taking with them the relics of St Petroc.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The cult of St Petroc was important both in Padstow and Bodmin.\n</p><p>Padstow is recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> (1086) when it was held by Bodmin Monastery. There was land for 4 ploughs, 5 villeins who had 2 ploughs, 6 smallholders and 24 acres of pasture. It was valued at 10/- (10 shillings or 50p).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the medieval period Padstow was commonly called <b>Aldestowe</b> (\'old place\' in contrast to Bodmin, the \'new place\').<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> or <b>Hailemouth</b> ("hayle" being Cornish for estuary). The modern Cornish form <i>Lannwedhenek</i> derives from <i>Lanwethinoc</i> and in a simpler form appears in the name of the Lodenek Press, a publisher based in Padstow.\n</p><p>The seal of the borough of Padstow was a ship with three masts, the sails furled and an anchor hanging from the bow, with the legend "Padstow." <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Time_Team" title="Time Team">Time Team</a></i> visited Padstow for the episode "From Constantinople to Cornwall," broadcast on 9 March 2008.\n</p><p>There are two Cornish crosses in the parish: one is built into a wall in the old vicarage garden and another is at <a href="/wiki/Prideaux_Place" title="Prideaux Place">Prideaux Place</a> (consisting of a four-holed head and part of an ornamented cross shaft). There is also part of a decorated cross shaft in the churchyard.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The church of <a href="/wiki/St_Petroc" class="mw-redirect" title="St Petroc">St Petroc</a> is one of four said to have been founded by the saint, the others being <a href="/wiki/Little_Petherick" title="Little Petherick">Little Petherick</a>, Parracombe and <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a>. It is quite large and mostly of 13th and 14th century date. There is a fine 15th century font of <a href="/wiki/Catacleuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Catacleuse">Catacleuse</a>; the pulpit of c. 1530 is also of interest. There are two fine monuments to members of the Prideaux family (Sir  \nNicholas, 1627 and Edmund, 1693): there is also a monumental brass of 1421.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant,_Padstow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant%2C_Padstow.jpg/220px-Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant%2C_Padstow.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant%2C_Padstow.jpg/330px-Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant%2C_Padstow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant%2C_Padstow.jpg/440px-Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant%2C_Padstow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rick_Stein%27s_Seafood_Restaurant,_Padstow.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rick Stein\'s Seafood Restaurant, Padstow</div></div></div>\n<p>Traditionally a <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> port, Padstow is now a popular <a href="/wiki/Tourist_destination" class="mw-redirect" title="Tourist destination">tourist destination</a>. Although some of its former fishing fleet remains, it is mainly a yachting haven on a dramatic coastline with few easily navigable harbours. The influence of restaurateur <a href="/wiki/Rick_Stein" title="Rick Stein">Rick Stein</a> can be seen in the port, and tourists travel from long distances to eat at his restaurant and caf\xc3\xa9s. This has led to the town being dubbed "Padstein", by food writers in the British media.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, the boom in the popularity of the port has caused house price inflation both in the port and surrounding areas, as people buy homes to live in, or as second or holiday homes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This has meant significant numbers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of locals cannot afford to buy property in the area, with prices often well over 10 times the average salary of around \xc2\xa315,000. This has led to a population decline.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Plans to build a skatepark in Padstow have been proposed and funds are being raised to create this at the Recreation Ground (Wheal Jubilee Parc).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_traffic">Maritime traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Maritime traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the mid-19th century, ships carrying timber from <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a>) would arrive at Padstow and offer cheap travel to passengers wishing to emigrate. Shipbuilders in the area would also benefit from the quality of their cargoes. Among the ships that sailed were the <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">barques</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Clio_(barque)" title="Clio (barque)">Clio</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belle_(barque)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Belle (barque) (page does not exist)">Belle</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voluna_(barque)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Voluna (barque) (page does not exist)">Voluna</a></i>; and the <a href="/wiki/Brig" title="Brig">brig</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalusia_(brig)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dalusia (brig) (page does not exist)">Dalusia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG/220px-Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG/330px-Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG/440px-Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1593" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Padstow-Rock_Ferry.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Padstow-Rock ferry</div></div></div>\n<p>The approach from the sea into the River Camel is partially blocked by the <a href="/wiki/Doom_Bar" title="Doom Bar">Doom Bar</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sand_bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Sand bank">bank of sand</a> extending across the estuary which is a significant hazard to shipping and the cause of many <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwrecks</a>.\n</p><p>For ships entering the estuary, the immediate loss of wind due to the cliffs was a particular hazard, often resulting in ships being swept onto the Doom Bar. A manual capstan was installed on the west bank of the river (its remains can still be seen) and rockets were fired to carry a line to ships so that they could be winched to safety.\n</p><p>There have been ferries across the Camel estuary for centuries and the current service, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Tor_Ferry" title="Black Tor Ferry">Black Tor Ferry</a>, carries pedestrians between Padstow and <a href="/wiki/Rock,_Cornwall" title="Rock, Cornwall">Rock</a> daily throughout the year.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From 1899 until 1967 <a href="/wiki/Padstow_railway_station,_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Padstow railway station, Cornwall">Padstow railway station</a> was the westernmost point of the former <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern Railway (Great Britain)">Southern Railway</a>. The railway station was the terminus of an extension from <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge_railway_station" title="Wadebridge railway station">Wadebridge</a> of the former <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_and_Wadebridge_Railway" title="Bodmin and Wadebridge Railway">Bodmin and Wadebridge Railway</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall_Railway" title="North Cornwall Railway">North Cornwall Railway</a>. These lines were part of the <a href="/wiki/London_and_South_Western_Railway" title="London and South Western Railway">London and South Western Railway</a> (LSWR), then incorporated into the Southern Railway in 1923 and <a href="/wiki/British_Railways" class="mw-redirect" title="British Railways">British Railways</a> in 1948, but were proposed for closure during the <a href="/wiki/Beeching_Axe" class="mw-redirect" title="Beeching Axe">Beeching Axe</a> of the 1960s.\n</p><p>The LSWR (and Southern Railway) promoted Padstow as a holiday resort; these companies were rivals to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> (which was the larger railway in the West of England). Until 1964, Padstow was served by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Express" title="Atlantic Coast Express">Atlantic Coast Express</a> &#8211; a direct train service to/from <a href="/wiki/London_Waterloo_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="London Waterloo railway station">London (Waterloo)</a> &#8211; but the station was closed in 1967. The old railway line is now the <a href="/wiki/Camel_Trail" title="Camel Trail">Camel Trail</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> a footpath and cycle path which is popular owing to its picturesque route beside the River Camel. One of the railway mileposts is now embedded outside the Shipwright\'s Arms public house on the Harbour Front.\n</p><p>Today, the nearest railway station is at <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Parkway_railway_station" title="Bodmin Parkway railway station">Bodmin Parkway</a>, a few miles south of Bodmin. Plymouth Bus operate buses to the station.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Footpaths">Footpaths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Footpaths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/South_West_Coast_Path" title="South West Coast Path">South West Coast Path</a> runs on both sides of the River Camel estuary and crosses from Padstow to Rock via the Black Tor ferry. The path gives walking access to the coast with <a href="/wiki/Stepper_Point" title="Stepper Point">Stepper Point</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trevose_Head" title="Trevose Head">Trevose Head</a> within an easy day\'s walk of Padstow.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Saints%27_Way" title="Saints&#39; Way">Saints\' Way</a> long-distance footpath runs from Padstow to <a href="/wiki/Fowey" title="Fowey">Fowey</a> on the south coast of Cornwall.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Camel_Trail" title="Camel Trail">Camel Trail</a> cycleway follows the course of the former railway (<i>see above</i>) from Padstow. It is open to walkers, cyclists and horse riders and suitable for disabled access. The 17.3-mile (27.8&#160;km) long route leads to <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge" title="Wadebridge">Wadebridge</a> and on to <a href="/wiki/Wenford_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Wenford Bridge">Wenford Bridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a>, and is used by an estimated 400,000 users each year<sup id="cite_ref-NCM_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCM-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> generating an income of approximately \xc2\xa33 million a year.<sup id="cite_ref-NCM_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCM-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id=".27Obby_.27Oss_festival"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="\'Obby_\'Oss_festival">\'Obby \'Oss festival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: &#039;Obby &#039;Oss festival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/%27Obby_%27Oss_festival" title="&#39;Obby &#39;Oss festival">\'Obby \'Oss festival</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg/220px-Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg/330px-Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg/440px-Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_obby_oss_maiden_20050502.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The \'Old Oss\' capturing a passing maiden during the Mayday festival</div></div></div>\n<p>Padstow is best known for its "\'Obby \'Oss" festival. Although its origins are unclear, it most likely stems from an ancient <a href="/wiki/Fertility_rite" title="Fertility rite">fertility rite</a>, perhaps the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic festival</a> of <a href="/wiki/Beltane" title="Beltane">Beltane</a>. The festival starts at midnight on <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Eve</a> when townspeople gather outside the Golden Lion Inn to sing the "Night Song." By morning, the town has been dressed with greenery and flowers placed around the <a href="/wiki/Maypole" title="Maypole">maypole</a>. The excitement begins with the appearance of one of the \'Obby \'Osses. Male dancers cavort through the town dressed as one of two \'Obby \'Osses, the "Old" and the "Blue Ribbon" \'Obby \'Osses; as the name suggests, they are stylised kinds of horses. Prodded on by <a href="/wiki/Acolyte" title="Acolyte">acolytes</a> known as "Teasers," each wears a mask and black frame-hung cape under which they try to catch young maidens as they pass through the town. Throughout the day, the two parades, led by the "Mayer" in his top hat and decorated stick, followed by a band of accordions and drums, then the \'Oss and the Teaser, with a host of people - all singing the "Morning Song."<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.lyricsty.com/rankin-family-padstow-the-may-morning-song-lyrics.html">[1]</a> - pass along the streets of the town. Finally, late in the evening, the two \'osses meet, at the maypole, before returning to their respective stables where the crowd sings of the \'Obby \'Oss death, until its resurrection the following May Eve.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg/220px-Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg/330px-Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg/440px-Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_obby_oss_party_20050502.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The \'Old Oss\' party attending the Oss with dozens of accordions and drums</div></div></div>\n<h3><span id="Mummers.27_or_Darkie_Day"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mummers\'_or_Darkie_Day">Mummers\' or Darkie Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mummers&#039; or Darkie Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On <a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day" title="Boxing Day">Boxing Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year&#39;s Day">New Year\'s Day</a>, it is a tradition for some residents to don <a href="/wiki/Blackface" title="Blackface">blackface</a> and parade through the town singing \'<a href="/wiki/Minstrel" title="Minstrel">minstrel</a>\' songs. This is an ancient <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">midwinter celebration</a> that occurs every year in Padstow and was originally part of the pagan heritage of midwinter celebrations that were regularly celebrated all over Cornwall where people would <a href="/wiki/Guise_dancing" title="Guise dancing">guise dance</a> and disguise themselves by blackening up their faces or wearing masks. Recently (since 2007), the people of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> have revived its midwinter celebration with the <a href="/wiki/Montol_Festival" title="Montol Festival">Montol Festival</a> which like Padstow at times would have had people darkening or painting their skin to disguise themselves as well as masking.)\n</p><p>Folklorists associate the practice with the widespread British custom of blacking up for mumming and morris dancing, and suggest there is no record of slave ships coming to Padstow. Once an unknown local charity event, the day has recently become controversial, perhaps since a description was published.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> \nAlso some now suggest it is racist for white people to "black up" for any reason.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  \nAlthough "outsiders" have linked the day with racism, Padstonians insist that this is not the case and are incredulous at both description and allegations. Long before the controversy Charlie Bate, noted Padstow folk advocate, recounted that in the 1970s the content and conduct of the day were carefully reviewed to avoid potential offence.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> \nThe Devon and Cornwall Constabulary have taken video evidence twice and concluded there were no grounds for prosecution.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> \nNonetheless protests resurface annually. The day has now been renamed <i>Mummers\' Day</i> in an attempt to avoid offence and identify it more clearly with established Cornish tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> \nThe debate has now been subject to academic scrutiny.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other similar traditions that use the black-face disguise and are still celebrated within the United Kingdom are the <a href="/wiki/Border_Morris" title="Border Morris">Border Morris</a> dancers, and <a href="/wiki/Molly_dance" title="Molly dance">Molly dancers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East Midlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_residents">Notable residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Padstow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Notable residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Donald_Rawe" title="Donald Rawe">Donald Rawe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish</a> <a href="/wiki/Publisher" class="mw-redirect" title="Publisher">publisher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dramatist" class="mw-redirect" title="Dramatist">dramatist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novelist" title="Novelist">novelist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">poet</a>, was born in Padstow.
