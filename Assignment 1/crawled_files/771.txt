Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Priscillian</b> (died <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;385</span>) was a wealthy nobleman of Roman Hispania who promoted a strict form of Christian <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">asceticism</a>. He became <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_%C3%81vila" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of \xc3\x81vila">bishop of \xc3\x81vila</a> in 380. Certain practices of his followers (such as meeting at country villas instead of attending church) were denounced at the <a href="/wiki/Councils_of_Saragossa" title="Councils of Saragossa">Council of Zaragoza</a> in 380. Tensions between Priscillian and bishops opposed to his views continued, as well as political maneuvering by both sides. Around 385, Priscillian was charged with sorcery and executed by authority of the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Maximus" title="Magnus Maximus">Emperor Maximus</a>. The ascetic movement <a href="/wiki/Priscillianism" title="Priscillianism">Priscillianism</a> is named after him, and continued in <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> until the late 6th century.  <a href="/wiki/Tract_(literature)" title="Tract (literature)">Tractates</a> by Priscillian and close followers, which had seemed lost, were discovered in 1885 and published in 1889.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The principal and almost contemporary source for the career of Priscillian is the Gallic chronicler <a href="/wiki/Sulpicius_Severus" title="Sulpicius Severus">Sulpicius Severus</a>, who characterized him (<i>Chronica</i> II.46) as noble and rich, a layman who had devoted his life to study, and was vain of his classical pagan education.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Priscillian was born around 340 A.D, into the nobility, possibly in western Hispania, and was well-educated. About 370, he initiated a movement in favour of asceticism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcGuckin2004284_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcGuckin2004284-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Priscillian advocated studying not only the Bible, but also apocryphal books. His followers, who were won over by his eloquence and his severely ascetic example, included the bishops Instantius and Salvianus.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealy1911_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealy1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs">Beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to Priscillian, apostles, prophets, and "doctors" (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "teachers") are the divinely appointed orders of the Church, preeminence being due the doctors, among whom Priscillian reckoned himself. The "spiritual" comprehend and judge all things, being "children of wisdom and light"; and the distinction between flesh and spirit, darkness and light, Moses and Christ, and the "prince of this world" and Christ, are emphasised. In asceticism Priscillian distinguished three degrees, though he did not deny hope of pardon to those who were unable to attain full perfection. The perfect in body, mind, and spirit were celibate, or, if married, continent.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Certain practices of the Priscillianists are known through the condemnatory canons issued by the 580 synod, such as receiving the Eucharist in the church but eating it at home or in the conventicle;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> women joining with men during the time of prayer; <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a> even on Sunday; and meditating at home or in the mountains instead of attending church during <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a>.\n</p><p>According to Ana Maria C.M. Jorge, "He played the role of a catalyst among Lusitanian Christians and crystallized a variety of ascetic, monastic and intellectual aspirations that were either fairly, or even entirely, incompatible with Christianity as it was lived by the great majority of the bishops of the day."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJorge2006_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJorge2006-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>His notable opponents in Hispania were <a href="/wiki/Hyginus_of_Cordoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyginus of Cordoba">Hyginus</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="C\xc3\xb3rdoba, Spain">Cordoba</a>, and Hydatius, bishop of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Spain" title="M\xc3\xa9rida, Spain">M\xc3\xa9rida</a>. They accused Priscillian\'s teachings of being <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">gnostic</a> in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-wlc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wlc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Through his intolerant severity Hydatius promoted rather than prevented the spread of the sect.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWace1911_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWace1911-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\nHydatius convened a synod held at <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a> in 380. Ten bishops were present at this synod from Spain, and two from Aquitaine, Delphinus of Bordeaux, and Ph\xc5\x93badus of Agen.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Although neither Priscillian nor any of his followers attended, he wrote in reply his third tract justifying the reading of apocryphal literature, without denying that their contents were partly spurious.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzogHauckJacksonSherman1910-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Neither Priscillian nor any of his disciples is mentioned in the decrees. The synod forbade certain practices. It forbade assumption of the title of "doctor", and forbade <a href="/wiki/Cleric" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleric">clerics</a> from becoming monks on the motivation of a more perfect life; women were not to be given the title of "<a href="/wiki/Virginity" title="Virginity">virgins</a>" until they had reached the age of forty. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kulikowski" title="Michael Kulikowski">Michael Kulikowski</a> characterizes the concern at Zaragoza as the relationship between town and country, and the authority of the urban episcopacy over religious practice in outlying rural areas.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulikowski2010244-250_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulikowski2010244-250-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the immediate aftermath of the synod, Priscillian was elected bishop of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81vila,_Spain" title="\xc3\x81vila, Spain">\xc3\x81vila</a>, and was consecrated by Instantius and Salvianus.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWace1911_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWace1911-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Priscillian was now a <a href="/wiki/Suffragan" class="mw-redirect" title="Suffragan">suffragan</a> of Ithacius of <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Ossonuba</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_bishop" title="Metropolitan bishop">metropolitan bishop</a> of Lusitania, whom he attempted to oust, but who then obtained from the emperor <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> an edict against "false bishops and <a href="/wiki/Manichaeism" title="Manichaeism">Manichees</a>". This was a threat against the Priscillianists, since the Roman Empire had banned Manichaeism long before it legalized Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, the three bishops, Instantius, Salvianus and Priscillian, went in person to Rome, to present their case before <a href="/wiki/Pope_Damasus_I" title="Pope Damasus I">Pope Damasus I</a>, himself a native of Hispania. Neither the Pope nor <a href="/wiki/Ambrose" title="Ambrose">Ambrose</a>, bishop of Milan, where the emperor resided, granted them an audience. Salvianus died in Rome, but through the intervention of Macedonius, the imperial <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_officiorum" title="Magister officiorum">magister officiorum</a></i> and an enemy of Ambrose, they succeeded in procuring the withdrawal of Gratian\'s edict, and an order for the arrest of Ithacius. Instantius and Priscillian, returning to Spain, regained their sees and churches.\n</p><p>A sudden change occurred in 383, when the governor of Britain, <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Maximus" title="Magnus Maximus">Magnus Maximus</a>, rebelled against Gratian, who marched against him but was assassinated. Maximus was recognized as emperor of Britain, Gaul and Spain, and made <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> his residence.\n</p><p>There Ithacius presented his case against Priscillian, and Maximus ordered a <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a> convened at <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> in 384. After this, the matter was transferred to the secular court at Treves. Ithacius and Hydatius of M\xc3\xa9rida both went there for the trial. Sulpicius Severus notes that <a href="/wiki/Martin_of_Tours" title="Martin of Tours">Martin of Tours</a> protested to the Emperor against the ruling, which said that the accused who went to Treves should be imprisoned.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJorge2006_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJorge2006-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Maximus, a Spaniard by birth, treated the matter not as one of ecclesiastical rivalry but as one of morality and society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWace1911_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWace1911-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He is also said to have wished to enrich his treasury by confiscation of the property of the condemned.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealy1911_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealy1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At Trier, Priscillian was tried by a secular court on criminal charges that included sorcery, a capital offence. Priscillian was questioned and forced to make the confession that he studied obscene doctrines, held nocturnal meetings with shameful women, and prayed while naked.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBosmajian199920_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBosmajian199920-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKronenfeld199819_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKronenfeld199819-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, he was charged with practicing magic (maleficium), for which he was convicted and sentenced to death.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESalzmanS\xc3\xa1ghyTesta2015167_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESalzmanS\xc3\xa1ghyTesta2015167-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Ithacius was his chief accuser. Priscillian was condemned and, with five of his companions, executed by the sword in 385.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrus,_p._97_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrus,_p._97-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Priscillian\xe2\x80\x99s execution is seen as the first example of secular justice intervening in an ecclesiastical matter.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJorge2006_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJorge2006-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_to_the_execution">Reactions to the execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactions to the execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Pope_Siricius" title="Pope Siricius">Pope Siricius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_of_Milan" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambrose of Milan">Ambrose of Milan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Martin_of_Tours" title="Martin of Tours">Martin of Tours</a> protested against the execution, largely on the jurisdictional grounds that an ecclesiastical case should not be decided by a civil tribunal, and worked to reduce the persecution. Pope Siricius censured not only Ithacius but the emperor himself. On receiving information from Maximus, he excommunicated Ithacius and his associates. On an official visit to Trier, Ambrose refused to give any recognition to Ithacius, "not wishing to have anything to do with bishops who had sent heretics to their death".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Before the trial, Martin had obtained from Maximus a promise not to apply a death penalty. After the execution, Martin broke off relations with <a href="/wiki/Felix_of_Trier" title="Felix of Trier">Felix</a>, bishop of Trier, and all others associated with the enquiries and the trial, and restored communion only when the emperor promised to stop the persecution of the Priscillianists.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Maximus was killed in his attempted invasion of Italy in 388. Under the new ruler, Ithacius and Hydatius were deposed and exiled. The remains of Priscillian were brought from Trier to Spain, where he was honoured as a martyr, especially in the west of the country, where Priscillianism did not die out until the second half of the 6th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHughes197927-28-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Continued_Priscillianism">Continued Priscillianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Continued Priscillianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The heresy, notwithstanding the severe measures taken against it, continued to spread in Gaul as well as in Hispania. A letter dated 20 February 405, from Pope Innocent I to Bishop Exuperius of Toulouse, opposed the Priscillianists\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of the Apocrypha.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJorge2006_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJorge2006-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 412 Lazarus, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Aix-en-Provence" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Aix-en-Provence">bishop of Aix-en-Provence</a>, and Herod, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Arles" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Arles">bishop of Arles</a>, were expelled from their sees on a charge of Manichaeism. Proculus, the metropolitan of <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, and the metropolitans of <a href="/wiki/Vienne" title="Vienne">Vienne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Narbonensis" title="Gallia Narbonensis">Narbonensis Secunda</a> were also followers of the rigorist tradition of Priscillian.                                                                                                                                                                                     Something was done for its repression by a synod held by Turibius of <a href="/wiki/Astorga_(Spain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Astorga (Spain)">Astorga</a> in 446, and by that of <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a> in 447; as an openly professed creed it had to be declared heretical once more by the second synod of <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a> in 563, a sign that Priscillianist asceticism was still strong long after his execution. "The official church," says <a href="/wiki/F._C._Conybeare" class="mw-redirect" title="F. C. Conybeare">F. C. Conybeare</a>, "had to respect the <a href="/wiki/Ascetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascetic">ascetic</a> spirit to the extent of enjoining <a href="/wiki/Clerical_celibacy" title="Clerical celibacy">celibacy</a> upon its priests, and of recognizing, or rather immuring, such of the laity as desired to live out the old ascetic ideal. \n</p><p>It is not always easy to separate the genuine assertions of Priscillian himself from those ascribed to him by his enemies, nor from the later developments taken by groups who were labelled Priscillianist. The long prevalent estimation of Priscillian as a heretic and <a href="/wiki/Manichaean" class="mw-redirect" title="Manichaean">Manichaean</a> rested upon <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turibius_of_Astorga" title="Turibius of Astorga">Turibius of Astorga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_I" title="Pope Leo I">Leo the Great</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paulus_Orosius" class="mw-redirect" title="Paulus Orosius">Orosius</a> (who quotes a fragment of a letter of Priscillian\'s), although at the Council of Toledo in 400, fifteen years after Priscillian\'s death, when his case was reviewed, the most serious charge that could be brought was the error of language involved in a misrendering of the word <i>innascibilis</i> ("unbegettable").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrieve1911361_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrieve1911361-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Augustine criticized the Priscillianists, who he said were like the Manicheans in their habit of fasting on Sundays.\n</p><p>Priscillianism continued in the north of Hispania and the south of Gaul. Priscillian was honored as a martyr, especially in <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norte,_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Norte, Portugal">northern</a> <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>), where his body was reverentially returned from Trier.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings_and_rediscovery">Writings and rediscovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priscillian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Writings and rediscovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some writings by Priscillian were accounted orthodox and were not burned. For instance he divided the Pauline epistles (including the <i><a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Hebrews" title="Epistle to the Hebrews">Epistle to the Hebrews</a></i>) into a series of texts on their theological points and wrote an introduction to each section. These canons survived in a form edited by <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia_(1913)/Peregrinus" class="extiw" title="s:Catholic Encyclopedia (1913)/Peregrinus">Peregrinus</a>. They contain a strong call to a life of personal piety and asceticism, including celibacy and abstinence from meat and wine. The <a href="/wiki/Charisma" title="Charisma">charismatic</a> gifts of all believers are equally affirmed. Study of scripture is urged. Priscillian placed considerable weight on <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal books</a>, not as being inspired but as helpful in discerning truth and error.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealy1911_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealy1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was long thought that all his writings had perished, but in 1885, Georg Schepss discovered at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="University of W\xc3\xbcrzburg">University of W\xc3\xbcrzburg</a> eleven genuine tracts, published in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vienna_Corpus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vienna Corpus (page does not exist)">Vienna Corpus</a> in 1886. <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrieve1911361_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrieve1911361-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>Though they bear Priscillian\'s name, four describing Priscillian\'s trial appear to have been written by a close follower.\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Raymond_E._Brown" title="Raymond E. Brown">Raymond E. Brown</a> (1995), the source of the <i><a href="/wiki/Comma_Johanneum" class="mw-redirect" title="Comma Johanneum">Comma Johanneum</a></i>, a brief <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_of_John" title="First Epistle of John">First Epistle of John</a>, known since the fourth century, appears to be the Latin <i>Liber Apologeticus</i> by Priscillian.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown1995Introduction_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown1995Introduction-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The modern assessment of Priscillian is summed up by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Chadwick_(theologian)" title="Henry Chadwick (theologian)">Henry Chadwick</a> (1976).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChadwick1976_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChadwick1976-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
