class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>International volunteering</b> is when volunteers <a href="/wiki/Volunteering" title="Volunteering">contribute their time</a> to work for organisations or causes outside their respective home countries. In most such cases, volunteers work in developing countries on <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a> programmes with local volunteer organisations that conduct activities such as <a href="/wiki/Health_promotion" title="Health promotion">health promotion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental conservation">environmental conservation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Palmer_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Trends show that international volunteering has become increasingly popular across many countries over the past few decades.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> International volunteering is a broad term which is used to capture multi-year, skilled placements as well as short term roles, recently termed <i>voluntourism</i>, and a range of activities in between conducted by <a href="/wiki/Governments" class="mw-redirect" title="Governments">governments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charities" class="mw-redirect" title="Charities">charities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Travel_agents" class="mw-redirect" title="Travel agents">travel agents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SMUC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMUC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Formal overseas volunteering can be traced back over one hundred years to when the <a href="/wiki/British_Red_Cross" title="British Red Cross">British Red Cross</a> set up the <i>Voluntary Aid Detachment</i> (VAD) scheme in 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The VAD volunteers, as well as volunteers from many other national Red Cross organisations, worked in battlefields across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> to treat soldiers and civilians regardless of the side they fought for.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eleanor_Roosevelt_%26_JFK_Peace_Corps.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Eleanor_Roosevelt_%26_JFK_Peace_Corps.jpg/300px-Eleanor_Roosevelt_%26_JFK_Peace_Corps.jpg" width="300" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/df/Eleanor_Roosevelt_%26_JFK_Peace_Corps.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="323" data-file-height="272" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eleanor_Roosevelt_%26_JFK_Peace_Corps.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eleanor Roosevelt and President John F. Kennedy discuss the <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">Peace Corps</a>, 1961.</div></div></div>\n<p>Up to the mid-20th century overseas volunteering projects were mainly undertaken by people with direct connections to a particular cause and were considered more as short term in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The more formal inception of international volunteering organisations can be linked to organisations such as <a href="/wiki/Australian_Volunteers_International" title="Australian Volunteers International">Australian Volunteers International</a> (formerly the <i>Volunteer Graduate Scheme</i>) which formed in 1951, <a href="/wiki/International_Voluntary_Services" title="International Voluntary Services">International Voluntary Services</a> in 1953 in the United States, and <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_Services_Overseas" class="mw-redirect" title="Voluntary Services Overseas">Voluntary Services Overseas</a> (VSO) in 1958 the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IVS_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IVS-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These services and that of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">Peace Corps</a>, established in 1961 during the Kennedy administration, paved the way for broader recognition of overseas volunteering in later years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During the 1960s and 1970s a movement of volunteerism and study abroad programs became popular among university students and graduates and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> launched the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Volunteers" title="United Nations Volunteers">UN Volunteers</a> programme for young professionals to take part in a long-term (two year plus) overseas programme.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In recent years the accessibility of international volunteering has increased significantly with many smaller charities connecting volunteers with non-governmental organisations in developing countries. Travel companies have also increasingly been offering paid-for volunteering opportunities, this growth coincided with the increasing number of young people taking <a href="/wiki/Gap_year" title="Gap year">gap years</a> and has been termed <i>volunteer tourism</i> and <i>voluntourism</i> to denote shorter-term voluntary work that is not necessarily the sole purpose of the trip.<sup id="cite_ref-SMUC_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMUC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, many opportunities medium- and long-term opportunities for skilled international volunteers remain, for example, the publicised role of volunteers in addressing the <a href="/wiki/Ebola_virus_epidemic_in_West_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebola virus epidemic in West Africa">Ebola virus epidemic in West Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volunteer_base">Volunteer base</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Volunteer base">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>International volunteering appeals to a broad cross-section of society, but the majority of volunteers are in their twenties and thirties, potentially due to perceptions of volunteering abroad being a more risky activity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The average age of VSO volunteers, however, is 38, showing a broad range of participation across age groups.<sup id="cite_ref-VSO_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VSO-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Many participants use these trips to boost their resumes, travel with friends, gain world experience, and see new countries. Recently there has been an increase in <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomer" class="mw-redirect" title="Baby boomer">baby boomer</a> volunteers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> One possible explanation for the increase is that these people are transitioning into a new stage of life and their focus may shift toward finding activities that give their life new meaning. Shorter-term voluntourism is therefore appealing to some, as it is targeted at travellers who want to make a positive change in the world, while still providing a touristic experience.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  People generally volunteer in order to increase their international awareness, to contextualize poverty and its effects, as an education opportunity, and to help people while having a morally rewarding experience. Many believe that the trip will change the way they think when they return home. However, others are just looking to give to others and do not believe that their experience will cause them to think twice about their lives back home.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critiques_and_challenges">Critiques and challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critiques and challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Volunteering#Difficulties_in_cross-national_aid" title="Volunteering">Volunteering \xc2\xa7&#160;Difficulties in cross-national aid</a></div>\n<p>Certain critiques and challenges are associated with international volunteering.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outcomes">Outcomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Outcomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Measuring the outcomes of international volunteering is an ongoing challenge. Sometimes the costs invested in these partnerships are high. The intangible nature of impact and outcomes is hard to measure and research has been proposed in this area.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, how to measure the success of a volunteer and the supporting organisation\'s performance is complicated.<sup id="cite_ref-Dev_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dev-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> To allow volunteers to integrate properly into the community, it is essential that volunteers have some useful skills and are reasonably well-informed and trained before the placement.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shannon_O%27Donnell_(writer)" title="Shannon O&#39;Donnell (writer)">Shannon O\'Donnell</a>, a vocal critic of poorly designed international programs, contends that many volunteer organizations compromise the dignity of local populations\xe2\x80\x94these programs often foster a cyclical dependency international volunteers within the communities the programs are designed to serve.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs">Costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Costs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Related to the impact of international volunteering, the cost of having an international volunteer has been cited as another area of concern, especially costs for air tickets, allowances, insurance, training and logistics. Local staff do require such costs, and the local organisations could put these funds into more important issues; however, many volunteers pay these expenses personally.<sup id="cite_ref-Palmer_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Some institutions provide scholarships for international volunteering.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Volunteers are far cheaper than other forms of long-term technical assistance because they live and work under local conditions. Expatriates who work in the same capacity can be paid multiple times more than any allowances volunteers receive (if any).<sup id="cite_ref-Dev_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dev-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The cost-benefit of international volunteers is hard to quantify, though studies have highlighted improvements in well-being and inter-cultural understanding in communities and schools as a result of international exchanges and volunteers.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Integration_in_the_workplace">Integration in the workplace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Integration in the workplace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A consideration is that volunteers may dominate the workplace, undermine local management and work culture especially in small organisations. This is due to volunteers often being considered more highly educated than local staff, even if they do not have direct experience. Coming from a different culture can also lead to volunteers imposing their values on organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-Dev_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dev-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Indeed, volunteers can have a strong influence on organisations, especially those who deal with <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> and management. However, volunteers are often trained to respect the local working culture and ethics.<sup id="cite_ref-VSO_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VSO-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Also, since they report directly to local organisations, they can have their contracts terminated if they break any local regulations, which further minimises the fear of domination.<sup id="cite_ref-Dev_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dev-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Skills.2C_experience_and_understanding_of_local_context"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Skills,_experience_and_understanding_of_local_context">Skills, experience and understanding of local context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Skills, experience and understanding of local context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>International volunteers from outside the host community can lack an understanding of the local context and sometimes may not have the correct skill-set to achieve their project goal.<sup id="cite_ref-Dev_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dev-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There is sometimes a vetting or selection process for volunteers before they are recruited to serve in developing countries, but this process has at times been found wanting.<sup id="cite_ref-Mangold,_K._2012_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mangold,_K._2012-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> According to some,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2018)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> international volunteers today receive significant training before and often during their placement which can address this deficit.<sup id="cite_ref-VSO_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VSO-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Others counter that there are countless multinational organizations offering unskilled volunteer placements to any participant willing to pay the placement fee. In these circumstances, there is conflict about whether the fees volunteers pay justify the time spent supervising and revising their work, and if a sufficient portion of the fees make it into the local communities hosting volunteers.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EVS_Training_Group.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/EVS_Training_Group.JPG/300px-EVS_Training_Group.JPG" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/EVS_Training_Group.JPG/450px-EVS_Training_Group.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/EVS_Training_Group.JPG/600px-EVS_Training_Group.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EVS_Training_Group.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A group of <a href="/wiki/European_Voluntary_Service" title="European Voluntary Service">European Voluntary Service</a> volunteers during training</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivations_of_volunteers">Motivations of volunteers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Motivations of volunteers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>People volunteer for many reasons, but seldom does anyone volunteer strictly for monetary reasons, as very few organisations offer a stipend for volunteering.<sup id="cite_ref-Palmer_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> More compelling motives include experiencing another culture, meeting new people, and advancing one\'s career prospects. Such motivations are common among younger volunteers who are looking for experience or direction in their careers.<sup id="cite_ref-Mangold,_K._2012_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mangold,_K._2012-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A common motivation is to "make a difference"<sup id="cite_ref-Palmer_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and to "achieve something positive for others"<sup id="cite_ref-Reh_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reh-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> who are less fortunate than the volunteer. Many volunteers tend to concur that there are disadvantaged people in their home countries, but the scale of disadvantage outside their home countries is felt to be greater. Volunteering at home may elicit images of helping the less fortunate, or campaigning with a local pressure group.<sup id="cite_ref-Reh_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reh-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Volunteering abroad has tended to be associated with <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a> and bridging the divide between the rich and poor worlds. Volunteering abroad often seems a more worthy contribution in this context to the volunteers than work in their own country. This perspective is particularly true of volunteers who are older and looking for something more value-based as they near the end of their professional careers or after their children have left home.<sup id="cite_ref-Palmer_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neo-colonialism">Neo-colonialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Neo-colonialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There have been allegations from some quarters of neo-colonial advances disguised as an effort to tackle poverty, as some volunteer organisations are connected to national governments, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">Peace Corps</a>, which was set up by the American government.<sup id="cite_ref-SMUC2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMUC2-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Despite this challenge, most volunteer organisations are non-governmental (<a href="/wiki/NGOs" class="mw-redirect" title="NGOs">NGOs</a>) and are not influenced by government policies.<sup id="cite_ref-Dev_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dev-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The present structures of international volunteering are also often aimed at impacts on a local, community scale which is sharply in contrast with the macro-political government strategies of the colonial era.<sup id="cite_ref-SMUC2_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMUC2-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, many academic journals elaborate that volunteers often have little knowledge or expertise in the work they do when volunteering abroad. This has raised concerns of its value. Frances Brown and Derek Hall write that this creates a neo-colonial narrative. They say the volunteer perspective is framed around the idea that Westerners with minimal experience can effect change in the Global South, just by nature of being from the West.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This perpetuates the narrative of Western domination in a post-colonial world, and the need to "save" and "help" the Global South.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntourism:_criticisms">Voluntourism: criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_volunteering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Voluntourism: criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Volunteer tourism, also known as "voluntourism", is a specific kind of international volunteering.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It is characterized as being conducted by profit-making companies rather than charities, by the age of the participants, and by the length of time they volunteer abroad.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Participants are often young adults (ages 15\xe2\x80\x9330), the length of the trip is under a month, and the volunteering is regularly packaged with adventure and travel activities. Voluntourism has undergone intense scrutiny over the course of the 2000s, and an increasing number of academic papers question volunteer tourists\' motivations and experiences.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Volunteer-sending organizations, such as <a href="/wiki/WE_Charity" title="WE Charity">Free The Children</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Me_to_We" title="Me to We">Me to We</a> trips and the British company Projects-abroad, have been critiqued as furthering the aforementioned neo-colonial narrative to youth. The increased prevalence of promotional material regarding trips to "help" the Global South has "increased media exposure in the Global North to poverty in the Global South."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Critics argue that the way in which these organizations advertise their trips stigmatizes and frames the developing world as helpless. This plays into Maria Eriksson Baaz\'s theories in the book <i>Paternalism of Partnership: a Postcolonial Reading of Identity in Development Aid</i>, in which she discusses discourse that frames the volunteer as a developed, paternalistic individual and the donor as underdeveloped.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The framing and "othering" of cultures outside the West and Global North can also be found in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Said" title="Edward Said">Edward Said</a>\'s text, <i><a href="/wiki/Orientalism_(book)" title="Orientalism (book)">Orientalism</a></i>. His theory is rooted in the same idea, in which he describes West\'s patronizing portrayals of the East.\n</p><p>Other criticisms of the voluntourism industry are that not only are short-term volunteers often untrained in the projects they participate in (building schools, health centres, wells),<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> but that projects can fuel conflict among communities,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> offer bandaid solutions, replace work locals could be doing, and reinforce neoliberal policies. Children in these communities may become dependent and commodified when volunteers are constantly arriving and departing every couple weeks. The rhetoric of such volunteer-sending organizations has also been argued to inform a "consumer-capitalist"<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> culture that plays to the wants and needs of the privileged North, at the disadvantage of the Global South.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
