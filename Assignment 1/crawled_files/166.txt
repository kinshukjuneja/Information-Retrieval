4</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>BBC Radio 4</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio station</a> owned and operated by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">British Broadcasting Corporation</a> (BBC) that broadcasts a wide variety of spoken-word programmes including news, drama, comedy, science and history. It replaced the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Home_Service" title="BBC Home Service">BBC Home Service</a> in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-history1960s_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1960s-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The station controller is <a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Williams" title="Gwyneth Williams">Gwyneth Williams</a>, and the station is part of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC Radio</a> and the <i>BBC Radio</i> department. The station is <a href="/wiki/Broadcast" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadcast">broadcast</a> from the BBC\'s headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting_House" title="Broadcasting House">Broadcasting House</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.\n</p><p>It is the second most popular domestic radio station in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, broadcast throughout the UK, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> on <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcast_band" title="FM broadcast band">FM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">LW</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Broadcasting" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Audio Broadcasting">DAB</a>, and can be received in eastern and south eastern counties of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the north of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a>. It is also available through <a href="/wiki/Freeview_(UK)" title="Freeview (UK)">Freeview</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sky_(UK_%26_Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sky (UK &amp; Ireland)">Sky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Media" title="Virgin Media">Virgin Media</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. Its sister station, <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4_Extra" title="BBC Radio 4 Extra">BBC Radio 4 Extra</a> (formerly <i>BBC Radio 7</i>), complements the main channel by broadcasting repeats from the Radio 4 archive, extended versions of Radio 4 programmes and supplements to series such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Archers" title="The Archers">The Archers</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Desert_Island_Discs" title="Desert Island Discs">Desert Island Discs</a></i>.\n</p><p>It is notable for its news bulletins and programmes such as <i><a href="/wiki/Today_(BBC_Radio_4)" title="Today (BBC Radio 4)">Today</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_at_One" title="The World at One">The World at One</a></i>, heralded on air by the <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Time_Signal" title="Greenwich Time Signal">Greenwich Time Signal</a> "pips" or the chimes of <a href="/wiki/Big_Ben" title="Big Ben">Big Ben</a>. Radio 4 broadcasts the <a href="/wiki/Shipping_Forecast" title="Shipping Forecast">Shipping Forecast</a>, which reached 150 years old in August 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The pips are only accurate on FM, LW &amp; MW as there is a delay on DAB and digital radio of approximately 3 to 5 seconds, even longer online (up to 23 seconds).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" Kingdom">British</a> domestic <a href="/wiki/List_of_most-listened-to_radio_programs" title="List of most-listened-to radio programs">radio station</a> by total hours,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> after <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">Radio 2</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 and the most popular in London and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">South of England</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It recorded its highest audience, of 11 million listeners, in May 2011<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and was "UK Radio Station of the Year" at the 2003, 2004 and 2008 <a href="/wiki/Sony_Radio_Academy_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Radio Academy Awards">Sony Radio Academy Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It also won a <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a> in 2002 for <i>File On 4:  Export Controls</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Costing \xc2\xa371.4 million (2005/6),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> it is the BBC\'s most expensive national radio network and is considered by many to be its flagship. There is no comparable British commercial network: <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> abandoned plans to launch its own speech-based digital radio station in October 2008 as part of a \xc2\xa3100m cost cutting review.<sup id="cite_ref-radio4rival_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radio4rival-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2010 <a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Williams" title="Gwyneth Williams">Gwyneth Williams</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> replaced <a href="/wiki/Mark_Damazer" title="Mark Damazer">Mark Damazer</a> as Radio 4 controller. Damazer became Master of <a href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_College,_Oxford" title="St Peter&#39;s College, Oxford">St Peter\'s College, Oxford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Music and sport are the only fields that largely fall outside the station\'s remit. It broadcasts occasional concerts, and documentaries related to various forms of both <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular</a> and classical music, and the long-running music-based <i><a href="/wiki/Desert_Island_Discs" title="Desert Island Discs">Desert Island Discs</a></i>. Prior to the creation of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_5_(former)" title="BBC Radio 5 (former)">BBC Radio 5</a> it broadcast sports-based features, notably <a href="/wiki/Sport_on_Four" title="Sport on Four">Sport on Four</a>, and since the creation of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_5_Live" title="BBC Radio 5 Live">BBC Radio 5 Live</a> has become the home of <a href="/wiki/Test_Match_Special" title="Test Match Special">ball-by-ball commentaries</a> of most <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test cricket</a> matches played by England, broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">long wave</a>. As a result, for around 70 days a year listeners have to rely on FM broadcasts or increasingly DAB for mainstream Radio 4 broadcasts \xe2\x80\x93 the number relying solely on long wave is now a small minority.\n</p><p>The cricket broadcasts take precedence over on-the-hour news bulletins, but not the Shipping Forecast, carried since its move to long wave in 1978 because that can be received clearly at sea.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The station is the UK\'s national broadcaster in times of national emergency such as war, due to the wide coverage of the Droitwich signal: if all other radio stations were forced to close, it would carry on broadcasting.<sup id="cite_ref-radio4rival_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radio4rival-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It has been claimed that the commanders of nuclear-armed submarines believing that Britain had suffered nuclear attack were required to check if they could still receive Radio 4 on 198 long wave, and if they could not they would open <a href="/wiki/Letters_of_last_resort" title="Letters of last resort">sealed orders that might authorise a retaliatory strike</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-today_facts_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-today_facts-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As well as news and drama, the station has a strong reputation for comedy, including experimental and <a href="/wiki/Alternative_comedy" title="Alternative comedy">alternative comedy</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_BBC_Radio_4_programmes#Comedy" title="List of BBC Radio 4 programmes">many successful comedians and comedy shows</a> first appearing on the station. Following the six o\'clock news from Monday to Friday, the station normally broadcasts a thirty-minute comedy programme.\n</p><p>The station is available on <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a> in most of Great Britain, parts of Ireland and the north of France; <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">LW</a> throughout the UK and in parts of Northern Europe, and the Atlantic north of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> to about 20 degrees west; <a href="/wiki/Medium_wave" title="Medium wave">MW</a> in some areas; <a href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Broadcasting" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Audio Broadcasting">DAB</a>; Digital TV including <a href="/wiki/Freeview_(UK)" title="Freeview (UK)">Freeview</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freesat" title="Freesat">Freesat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sky_(UK_%26_Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sky (UK &amp; Ireland)">Sky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Media" title="Virgin Media">Virgin Media</a>, and on the Internet. Freesat, Sky and Virgin have a separate channel which broadcasts the Radio 4 LW output in mono, in addition to the FM output.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BBC_Radio_4_(logo_until_2007).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/38/BBC_Radio_4_%28logo_until_2007%29.jpg/100px-BBC_Radio_4_%28logo_until_2007%29.jpg" width="100" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/38/BBC_Radio_4_%28logo_until_2007%29.jpg/150px-BBC_Radio_4_%28logo_until_2007%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/38/BBC_Radio_4_%28logo_until_2007%29.jpg/200px-BBC_Radio_4_%28logo_until_2007%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="203" data-file-height="380" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BBC_Radio_4_(logo_until_2007).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Logo of Radio 4 until 2007</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/BBC_Home_Service" title="BBC Home Service">BBC Home Service</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_BBC_Radio_4" title="Timeline of BBC Radio 4">Timeline of BBC Radio 4</a></div>\n<p>The BBC Home Service was the predecessor of Radio 4 and broadcast between 1939 and 1967. It had <a href="/wiki/Regional_variations" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional variations">regional variations</a> and was broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Medium_wave" title="Medium wave">medium wave</a> with a network of <a href="/wiki/VHF" class="mw-redirect" title="VHF">VHF</a> FM transmitters being added from 1955. Radio 4 replaced it on 30 September 1967, when the BBC renamed many of its domestic radio stations,<sup id="cite_ref-history1960s_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1960s-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> in response to the challenge of <a href="/wiki/Offshore_radio" title="Offshore radio">offshore radio</a>. It moved to long wave in November 1978, taking over the 200&#160;kHz frequency (1500 meters) previously held by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">Radio 2</a>, and later moved to 198&#160;kHz as a result of <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Frequency_Plan_of_1975" title="Geneva Frequency Plan of 1975">international agreements</a> aimed at avoiding interference (all UK AM/MW/LW frequencies are divisible by 9) and to mark the station becoming a fully national service for the first time the station officially became known as Radio 4 UK, a title that remained until mid 1984. \n</p><p>For a time during the 1970s Radio 4 carried regional news bulletins Monday to Saturday. These were broadcast twice at breakfast, at lunchtime and an evening bulletin was aired at 5.55pm. There were also programme variations for the parts of England not served by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Local_Radio" title="BBC Local Radio">BBC Local Radio</a> stations. These included <i><a href="/wiki/Roundabout_East_Anglia" title="Roundabout East Anglia">Roundabout East Anglia</a></i>, a VHF opt-out of the <i>Today</i> programme broadcast from <a href="/wiki/BBC_East" title="BBC East">BBC East</a>\'s studios in Norwich each weekday from 6.45&#160;am to 8.45&#160;am.<sup id="cite_ref-round_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-round-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i>Roundabout East Anglia</i> came to an end in mid-1980, when local radio services were introduced to <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> with the launch of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Norfolk" title="BBC Radio Norfolk">BBC Radio Norfolk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-round_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-round-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>All regional news bulletins broadcast from BBC regional news bases around England ended in August 1980, apart from in the south west. Until January 1983 there was no <a href="/wiki/BBC_Local_Radio" title="BBC Local Radio">BBC Local Radio</a> in the south west so these news bulletins and its weekday morning regional programme, <i><a href="/wiki/Morning_Sou%27West" title="Morning Sou&#39;West">Morning Sou\'West</a></i>, continued to be broadcast from the BBC studios in Plymouth on VHF and on the Radio 4 medium wave Plymouth relay until 31 December 1982.\n</p><p>The launch of Radio 5 on 27 August 1990 saw the removal of <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a>, schools programming and the <i>Study on 4</i>/<i>Options</i> adult education slot to the new station resulting in the full Radio 4 schedule being available on FM for the first time.\n</p><p>Between 17 January 1991 and 2 March 1991 FM broadcasts were replaced by a continuous news service devoted to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_4_News_FM" title="Radio 4 News FM">Radio 4 News FM</a>, with the main Radio 4 service being exclusively on long wave.\n</p><p>In September 1991 it was decided that the main Radio 4 service would be on FM as FM coverage had now extended to cover almost all of the UK - Radio 4 didn\'t become available on FM in much of Scotland and Wales until the early 1990s. Opt-outs were transferred to long wave: currently <i><a href="/wiki/Test_Match_Special" title="Test Match Special">Test Match Special</a></i>, extra shipping forecasts, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Service" title="The Daily Service">The Daily Service</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Today_in_Parliament" title="Today in Parliament">Yesterday in Parliament</a></i>. Long wave very occasionally opts out at other times, such as to broadcast special services, the most recent being when <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI%27s_visit_to_the_United_Kingdom" title="Pope Benedict XVI&#39;s visit to the United Kingdom">visited Britain in 2010</a>.\n</p><p>The longwave signal is part of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>\'s system of <a href="/wiki/Letters_of_last_resort" title="Letters of last resort">Last Resort Letters</a>. In the event of a suspected catastrophic attack on Britain, <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_class_submarine" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanguard class submarine">submarine</a> captains, in addition to other checks, check for a broadcast signal from Radio 4 on 198 long wave to verify the annihilation of organised society in Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programmes_and_schedules">Programmes and schedules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Programmes and schedules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daily_schedule">Daily schedule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Daily schedule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An online schedule page lists the running order of programmes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many programmes are pre-recorded. Programmes transmitted live include <i>Today</i>, magazine programme <i>Woman\'s Hour</i>, consumer affairs programme <i><a href="/wiki/You_and_Yours" title="You and Yours">You and Yours</a></i>, and (often) the music, film, books, arts and culture programme <i><a href="/wiki/Front_Row_(radio)" title="Front Row (radio)">Front Row</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Continuity_(broadcasting)" title="Continuity (broadcasting)">Continuity</a> is managed from <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting_House" title="Broadcasting House">Broadcasting House</a> with news bulletins, including the hourly summaries and longer programmes such as the <i>Six O\'Clock News</i> and <i>Midnight News</i>, and news programmes such as <i>Today</i>, <i>The World at One</i> and <i>PM</i>, which by early 2013 had returned to Broadcasting House after 15 years at <a href="/wiki/BBC_Television_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC Television Centre">BBC Television Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/White_City,_London" title="White City, London">White City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The news returning to Broadcasting House has also meant that newsreaders can provide cover for continuity, which regularly occurs at 23:00 each night and 16:00 on a Sunday. This has reduced the total number of continuity announcers required each day down from four to three.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Time_Signal" title="Greenwich Time Signal">Time Signal</a>, known as \'the pips\', is broadcast every hour to herald the news bulletin, except at midnight and 18:00, where the chimes of <a href="/wiki/Big_Ben" title="Big Ben">Big Ben</a> are played. There is no Greenwich Time Signal during the Saturday Afternoon Drama at 15:00 on Radio 4 on Saturdays either. Only pips broadcast on FM/AM/MW/LW are accurate. On all digital platforms they are in delay by between 3 and 5 seconds and even longer online (up to 23 seconds).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programmes">Programmes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Radio_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Programmes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_BBC_Radio_4_programmes" title="List of BBC Radio 4 programmes">List of BBC Radio 4 programmes</a></div>\n<p>Radio 4 programmes cover a wide variety of genre including news and current affairs, history, culture, science, religion, arts, drama and light entertainment. A number of the programmes on Radio 4 take the form of a "magazine" show, featuring numerous small contributions over the course of the programme\xe2\x80\x94<i><a href="/wiki/Woman%27s_Hour" title="Woman&#39;s Hour">Woman\'s Hour</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/From_Our_Own_Correspondent" title="From Our Own Correspondent">From Our Own Correspondent</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/You_and_Yours" title="You and Yours">You and Yours</a></i>. The rise of these magazine shows is primarily due to the work of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Whitby" title="Tony Whitby">Tony Whitby</a>, controller of Radio 4 from 1970 to 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The station hosts a number of long-running programmes, many of which have been broadcast for over 40 years.\n</p><p>Most programmes are available for four weeks after broadcast as streaming audio from Radio 4\'s <i>listen again</i> page<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and via <a href="/wiki/BBC_iPlayer" title="BBC iPlayer">BBC iPlayer</a>. A selection of programmes is also available as <a href="/wiki/Podcasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Podcasts">podcasts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">downloadable</a> audio files.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Many comedy and drama programmes from the Radio 4 archives are rebroadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4_Extra" title="BBC Radio 4 Extra">BBC Radio 4 Extra</a> (formerly BBC Radio 7).\n</p><p>Due to the capacity limitations of DAB and increasing sport broadcasts on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_5_Live_Sports_Extra" title="BBC Radio 5 Live Sports Extra">BBC Radio 5 Live Sports Extra</a>, BBC Radio 4 DAB has to reduce its bit rate most evenings, such that after 7pm its DAB output is usually in mono, even though many of its programmes are made in stereo (including its flagship drama "The Archers"), these can be heard in stereo only on FM, Digital TV on Freeview &amp; Freesat (Ch. 704), Sky, Virgin and on line via BBC iPlayer radio. BBC World Service, which uses BBC Radio 4 FM &amp; DAB frequencies between 01:00am and 05:20am, is in stereo, but only on Radio 4 FM &amp; DAB and not on its own dedicated DAB channel. BBC Radio 4 Extra broadcasts in mono on DAB, but has always been in stereo on Digital TV (Freeview / Freesat Ch 708), Sky, Virgin and online.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" voiced by centre-right newspapers in recent years over a perceived left political bias across a range of issues, such as the EU and the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" magazine <i>Today</i><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> as part of a reported perception of a general "malaise" at the BBC. Conversely, the journalist <a href="/wiki/Mehdi_Hasan" title="Mehdi Hasan">Mehdi Hasan</a> has criticised the station for an overtly "<a href="/wiki/Social_conservatism" title="Social conservatism">socially</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_conservatism" title="Cultural conservatism">culturally</a> conservative" approach.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There has been frequent criticism of Radio 4\xe2\x80\x94and <i>Today</i> in particular\xe2\x80\x94for a lack of female broadcasters.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In September 1972, Radio 4 employed the first female continuity announcers\xe2\x80\x94Hylda Bamber and Barbara Edwards (an event which caused the <i>Daily Mail</i> to proclaim that Radio 4 had "fallen" to women\'s liberation).
