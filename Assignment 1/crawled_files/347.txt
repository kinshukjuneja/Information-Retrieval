Nationals</b>, sometimes referred to as the Washington Statesmen, was a professional baseball team in the mid to late 1880s. They existed for a period of four years as a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (NL) from <a href="/wiki/1886_in_baseball" title="1886 in baseball">1886</a> to <a href="/wiki/1889_in_baseball" title="1889 in baseball">1889</a>. During their four-year tenure they had six different <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">managers</a> and compiled a record of 163-337, for a .326 <a href="/wiki/Winning_percentage" title="Winning percentage">winning percentage</a>. The franchise played their home games at <a href="/wiki/Swampoodle_Grounds" title="Swampoodle Grounds">Swampoodle Grounds</a>.\n</p><p>Their most notable player was <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">catcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Connie_Mack" title="Connie Mack">Connie Mack</a>, who went on to a <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Hall of Fame</a> career as <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a> from 1901 to 1950. Outfielder <a href="/wiki/Dummy_Hoy" title="Dummy Hoy">Dummy Hoy</a>, notable for being deaf, played for the 1888 and 1889 Washington teams. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Donnelly_(baseball)" title="Jim Donnelly (baseball)"> Jim Donnelly</a> also spent time with the Nationals.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
