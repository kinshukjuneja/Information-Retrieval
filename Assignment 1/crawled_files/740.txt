Office</b> (officially the <b>Meteorological Office</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is the United Kingdom\'s national weather service. It is an <a href="/wiki/Executive_agency" title="Executive agency">executive agency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trading_fund" title="Trading fund">trading fund</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Business,_Energy_and_Industrial_Strategy" title="Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy">Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy</a> led by acting CEO<sup id="cite_ref-CEO2018_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CEO2018-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>, Nick Jobling and chief scientist, Stephen Belcher. <a href="/wiki/Penelope_Endersby" title="Penelope Endersby">Penelope Endersby</a> will take over as Chief Executive in December 2018, the first woman to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-Endersby_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endersby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Met Office makes meteorological predictions across all timescales from weather forecasts to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Fitzroy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Robert_Fitzroy.jpg/100px-Robert_Fitzroy.jpg" width="100" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Robert_Fitzroy.jpg/150px-Robert_Fitzroy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Robert_Fitzroy.jpg/200px-Robert_Fitzroy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="235" data-file-height="313" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Fitzroy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vice-Admiral Robert FitzRoy founder of the Met Office</div></div></div><p> The Met Office was established in 1854 as a small department within the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a> under Vice-Admiral <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a> as a service to <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">mariners</a>. The loss of the passenger vessel, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter_(ship)" title="Royal Charter (ship)"><i>Royal Charter</i></a>, and 459 lives off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> in a violent storm in October 1859 led to the first gale warning service. FitzRoy established a network of 15 coastal stations from which visual gale warnings could be provided for ships at sea.\n</p><p>The new <a href="/wiki/Electric_telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric telegraph">electric telegraph</a> enabled rapid dissemination of warnings and also led to the development of an observational network which could then be used to provide synoptic analysis. The Met Office started in 1861 to provide weather forecasts to newspapers.  FitzRoy requested the daily traces of the photo-<a href="/wiki/Barograph" title="Barograph">barograph</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kew_Observatory" class="mw-redirect" title="Kew Observatory">Kew Observatory</a> (invented by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ronalds" title="Francis Ronalds">Francis Ronalds</a>) to assist in this task and similar barographs and as well as instruments to continuously record other meteorological parameters were later provided to stations across the observing network.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Publication of forecasts ceased in May 1866 after FitzRoy\xe2\x80\x99s death but recommenced in April 1879.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_with_the_Ministry_of_Defence">Connection with the Ministry of Defence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Connection with the Ministry of Defence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Meteorological_Office,_Bracknell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_489065.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Meteorological_Office%2C_Bracknell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_489065.jpg/220px-The_Meteorological_Office%2C_Bracknell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_489065.jpg" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Meteorological_Office%2C_Bracknell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_489065.jpg/440px-The_Meteorological_Office%2C_Bracknell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_489065.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="320" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Meteorological_Office,_Bracknell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_489065.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former Met Office building in Bracknell, Berkshire before relocation to Exeter, since demolished</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, the Met Office became part of the <a href="/wiki/Air_Ministry" title="Air Ministry">Air Ministry</a> in 1919, the weather observed from the top of <a href="/wiki/Adastral_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Adastral House">Adastral House</a> (where the Air Ministry was based) giving rise to the phrase "The weather on the Air Ministry roof". As a result of the need for weather information for aviation, the Met Office located many of its observation and data collection points on <a href="/wiki/List_of_Royal_Air_Force_stations" title="List of Royal Air Force stations">RAF airfields</a>, and this accounts for the large number of military airfields mentioned in weather reports even today. In 1936 the Met Office split with services to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> being provided by its own forecasting services.\n</p><p>It became an <a href="/wiki/Executive_agency" title="Executive agency">executive agency</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> in April 1990, a quasi-governmental role, being required to act commercially.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_in_ownership">Change in ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Change in ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following a <a href="/wiki/Machinery_of_government" title="Machinery of government">machinery of government</a> change, the Met Office became part of the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Business,_Innovation_and_Skills" title="Department for Business, Innovation and Skills">Department for Business, Innovation and Skills</a> on 18 July 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and subsequently part of the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Business,_Energy_and_Industrial_Strategy" title="Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy">Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy</a> following the merger of BIS and the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Energy_and_Climate_Change" title="Department of Energy and Climate Change">Department of Energy and Climate Change</a> on 14 July 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although no longer part of the MOD, the Met Office maintains strong links with the military through its front line offices at RAF and Army bases both in the UK and overseas and its involvement in the Joint Operations Meteorology and Oceanography Centre (JOMOC) with the Royal Navy.  The <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Meteorological_Unit" title="Mobile Meteorological Unit">Mobile Met Unit</a> (MMU) are a unit consisting of Met Office staff who are also <a href="/wiki/RAF_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="RAF Reserve">RAF reservists</a> who accompany forward units in times of conflict advising the armed forces of the conditions for battle, particularly the RAF.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations">Locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG/220px-Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG" width="220" height="80" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG/330px-Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG/440px-Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ukmo-exeter-panorama.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 2003 headquarters building on the edge of Exeter</div></div></div>\n<p>In September 2003 the Met Office moved its headquarters from <a href="/wiki/Bracknell" title="Bracknell">Bracknell</a> in Berkshire to a purpose-built \xc2\xa380m structure at <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> Business Park, near junction 29 of the <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5 motorway</a>. The new building was officially opened on 21 June 2004 \xe2\x80\x93 a few weeks short of the Met Office\'s 150th anniversary \xe2\x80\x93 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_May,_Baron_May_of_Oxford" title="Robert May, Baron May of Oxford">Robert May, Baron May of Oxford</a>. \n</p><p>It has a worldwide presence&#160;&#8211;&#32;including a forecasting centre in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, and offices in <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Falklands" class="mw-redirect" title="Falklands">Falklands</a>. href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Weather forecasting and warnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Met Office issues Severe Weather Warnings for the United Kingdom through the <a href="/wiki/National_Severe_Weather_Warning_Service" title="National Severe Weather Warning Service">National Severe Weather Warning Service</a> (NSWWS). These warn of weather events that may affect transport infrastructure and endanger people\'s lives.  In March 2008, the system was improved and a new stage of warning was introduced, the \'Advisory\'.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2015 the Met Office established a "name our storms" project, the aim is to provide a single authoritative naming system for the storms that affect the UK and Ireland by asking the public to suggest names. On 10 November, the first named storm was <a href="/wiki/Storm_Abigail" title="Storm Abigail">Abigail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_prediction_models">Weather prediction models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Weather prediction models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The main role of the Met Office is to produce forecast models by gathering information from <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellites</a> in space and observations on earth, then processing it with a variety of models, based on a software package known as the <a href="/wiki/Unified_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Unified model">unified model</a>. The principal weather products for UK customers are 36-hour forecasts from the operational 1.5&#160;km resolution <a href="/wiki/Unified_Model#UKV_model" title="Unified Model">UKV model</a> covering the UK and surroundings<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> (replacing the 4&#160;km model), 48-hour forecasts from the 12&#160;km resolution NAE model covering Europe and the North Atlantic, and 144-hour forecasts from the 25&#160;km resolution global model (replacing the 40&#160;km global model).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Met Office\'s Global Model forecast has consistently been in the top 3 for global weather forecast performance (in the decades up to 2010) in independent verification to WMO standards.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2013)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Products for other regions of the globe are sold to customers abroad, provided for <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">MOD</a> operations abroad or provided free to developing countries in Africa. If necessary, forecasters may make adjustments to the computer forecasts. Data is stored in the Met Office\'s own <a href="/wiki/PP-format" title="PP-format">PP-format</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flood_Forecasting_Centre">Flood Forecasting Centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Flood Forecasting Centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flood_Forecasting_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Flood Forecasting Centre">Flood Forecasting Centre</a></div>\n<p>Formed in 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Flood_Forecasting_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Flood Forecasting Centre">Flood Forecasting Centre</a> (FFC) is a joint venture between the <a href="/wiki/Environment_Agency" title="Environment Agency">Environment Agency</a> and the Met Office to provide flood risk guidance for England and Wales. The Centre is jointly staffed from both parent organisations and is based in the Operations Centre at the Met Office headquarters in Exeter.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland this role is performed by the Scottish Flood Forecasting Service, a joint venture between the <a class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_forecasts">Seasonal forecasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span forecasts by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Meteorological Organisation">World Meteorological Organisation</a> and continues to provide At the BBC Weather Centre, they are continuously updated on the information by computer, <a href="/wiki/Fax" title="Fax">fax</a> and email.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/BBC_Weather" title="BBC Weather">BBC\'s new graphics</a> are used on all of their television weather broadcasts. <a href="/wiki/ITV_Weather" title="ITV Weather">ITV Weather</a> use animated weather symbols. The data provides details of wind speed and direction, air temperature, cloud type and tops, and other features.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_Ash_Advisory_Centre">Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Met_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Ash_Advisory_Center" title="Volcanic Ash Advisory Center">Volcanic Ash Advisory Center</a></div>\n<p>As part of its aviation forecast operation the Met Office operates the London <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Ash_Advisory_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre">Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre</a> (VAAC).<sup id="cite_ref-LVAAC_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LVAAC-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This provides forecasts to the aviation industry of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a> clouds that could enter aircraft flight paths and impact aviation safety. The London VAAC, one of nine worldwide, is responsible for the area covering the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, the north east Atlantic and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. The VAAC were set up by the <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="International Civil Aviation Organisation">International Civil Aviation Organisation</a> (ICAO), an agency of the United Nations, as part of the International Airways Volcano Watch (IAVW).<sup id="cite_ref-IAVW_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAVW-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The London VAAC makes use of satellite images, plus seismic, radar and visual observation data from <a href="/wiki/Volcanism_of_Iceland" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanism of Iceland">Iceland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Witham_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witham-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> the location of all of the active volcanoes in its area of responsibility. Some stations produce manual observations during business hours and revert to automatic observations outside these times. Many stations feature "present weather" sensors, <a href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_television" title="Closed-circuit television">CCTV</a>, etc.  There is also a network of \'upper air\' stations, using <a href="/wiki/Radiosondes" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiosondes">Radiosondes</a>.\n</p><p>Some stations have limited reporting times, while other report continuously, mainly <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a> and <a href="/wiki/Army_Air_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="Army Air Corps (United Kingdom)">Army Air Corps</a> stations where a manned met office is provided for military operations. The "standard" is a once-hourly reporting schedule, but automatic stations can often be "polled" as required, while stations at airfields report twice-hourly, with additional (often frequent in times of bad weather) special reports as necessary to inform airfield authorities of changes to the weather that may affect aviation operations.\n</p><p>Some stations report only <a href="/wiki/CLIMAT" title="CLIMAT">CLIMAT</a> data (e.g. maximum and minimum temperatures, rainfall totals over a period, etc.) and these are usually recorded at 0900 and 2100 hours daily. Weather reports are often performed by observers not specifically employed by the Met Office, such as <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">Air traffic control</a> staff, coastguards, university staff and so on.\n</p>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">\n<ul><li><a
