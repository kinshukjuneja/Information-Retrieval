class="mw-headline" id="National_identity">National identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: National identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Identity_Northern_Ireland_Census_2011.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/National_Identity_Northern_Ireland_Census_2011.png/300px-National_Identity_Northern_Ireland_Census_2011.png" width="300" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/National_Identity_Northern_Ireland_Census_2011.png/450px-National_Identity_Northern_Ireland_Census_2011.png 1.5x, data-file-height="17495" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Identity_Northern_Ireland_Census_2011.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of predominant national identity in the 2011 census in Northern Ireland. Stronger blue is more British. Stronger green is more Irish.</div></div></div>\n<p>In Northern Ireland, national identity is complex and diverse. The question of national identity<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> was asked in the 2011 census with the three most common identities given being <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a>, Northern Irish and <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a>. Most people of Protestant background consider themselves British, while a majority of people of Catholic background consider themselves Irish. This has origins in the 17th century <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a>, when mainly-Catholic <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> was colonized by Protestant settlers from Great Britain.\n</p><p>In the early 20th century, most <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestants" title="Ulster Protestants">Ulster Protestants</a> and Catholics saw themselves as Irish, although Protestants tended to have a much stronger sense of Britishness.<sup id="cite_ref-walker_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> With the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_Crisis" title="Home Rule Crisis">Home Rule Crisis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_revolutionary_period" title="Irish revolutionary period">events that followed</a>, Protestants gradually began to abandon Irish identity,<sup id="cite_ref-walker_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> as Irishness and Britishness became more and more to be seen as mutually exclusive. In 1968 \xe2\x80\x93 just before the onset of <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> \xe2\x80\x93 39% of Protestants described themselves as British and 20% described themselves as Irish, while 32% chose an <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> identity.<sup id="cite_ref-moxon-browne_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moxon-browne-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By 1978, following the worst years of the conflict, there had been a large shift in identity amongst Protestants, with the majority (67%) now calling themselves British and only 8% calling themselves Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-moxon-browne_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moxon-browne-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-conflict-consensus_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conflict-consensus-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This shift has not been reversed.<sup id="cite_ref-conflict-consensus_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conflict-consensus-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, the majority of Catholics have continued to see themselves as Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-moxon-browne_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moxon-browne-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 1989, \'Northern Irish\' began to be included as an identity choice in surveys, and its popularity has grown since then.<sup id="cite_ref-conflict-consensus_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conflict-consensus-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Some organizations have promoted \'Northern Irish\' identity as a way of overcoming sectarian division. In a 1998 survey of students, this was one of the main reasons they gave for choosing that identity, along with a desire to appear \'neutral\'.<sup id="cite_ref-mckeown_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mckeown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> However, surveys show that \'Northern Irish\' identity tends to have different meanings for Catholics and Protestants.<sup id="cite_ref-mckeown_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mckeown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Surveys also show that those choosing \'Northern Irish\' regard their national identity as less important than those choosing British and Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-mckeown_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mckeown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect"
