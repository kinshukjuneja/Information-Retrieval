<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (UK) comprises four <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">countries</a>: <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Within the United Kingdom, a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary</a> <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a>, Northern Ireland, Scotland and Wales have gained a degree of autonomy through the process of <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">UK Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">British Government</a> deal with all <i><a href="/wiki/Reserved_matters" class="mw-redirect" title="Reserved matters">reserved matters</a></i> for Northern Ireland, Scotland and Wales, but not in general matters that have been <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>. Additionally, devolution in Northern Ireland is conditional on co-operation between the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> (see <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a>) and the British Government consults with the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> to reach agreement on some non-devolved matters for Northern Ireland (see <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a>). England, comprising the majority of the population and area of the United Kingdom,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ONSCOUNTRYPROFILES_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONSCOUNTRYPROFILES-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> remains fully the responsibility of the UK Parliament centralised in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.\n</p><p>England, Northern Ireland, Scotland and Wales are not themselves listed in the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO) <a href="/wiki/Countries_in_International_Organization_for_Standardization" class="mw-redirect" title="Countries in International Organization for Standardization">list of countries</a>. However the ISO list of the subdivisions of the UK, compiled by <a href="/wiki/British_Standards" title="British Standards">British Standards</a> and the UK\'s <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a>, uses "country" to describe England, Scotland and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-ISO_Newsletter_ii-3-2011-12-13_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO_Newsletter_ii-3-2011-12-13-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Northern Ireland, in contrast, is described as a "province" in the same lists.<sup id="cite_ref-ISO_Newsletter_ii-3-2011-12-13_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO_Newsletter_ii-3-2011-12-13-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Each has separate <a href="/wiki/Sport_governing_body" class="mw-redirect" title="Sport governing body">national governing bodies for sports</a> and compete separately in many international sporting competitions, including the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Games" title="Commonwealth Games">Commonwealth Games</a>.  Northern Ireland also forms joint <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Island of Ireland">All-Island</a> sporting bodies with the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> for most sports, including <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> are <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">dependencies</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a> and are not part of the UK. Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territories">British overseas territories</a>, remnants of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, are not part of the UK.\n</p><p>Historically, from 1801, following the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union</a>, until 1921 the whole island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> was a country within the UK.  Ireland was <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">split into two separate jurisdictions</a> in 1921: <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.  Southern Ireland left the United Kingdom under the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State_Constitution_Act_1922" title="Irish Free State Constitution Act 1922">Irish Free State Constitution Act 1922</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" Wales was described (in varying combinations) as the "country", "principality", and "dominion" of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-LWA1535I_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LWA1535I-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LWA1542_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LWA1542-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Outside Wales, England was not given a specific name or term. The Laws in Wales Acts have subsequently been repealed.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a> refer to both England and Scotland as a "part" of a united <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">kingdom of Great Britain</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union 1800</a> use "part" in the same way to refer to England and Scotland. However, they use the word "country" to describe Great Britain and Ireland respectively, when describing trade between them<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a> described Great Britain, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> and Northern Ireland as "countries" in provisions relating to taxation.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Act_1998" title="Northern Ireland Act 1998">Northern Ireland Act 1998</a>, which repealed the Government of Ireland Act 1920, does not use any term to describe Northern Ireland.</li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_legal_terminology">Current legal terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Current legal terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Interpretation_Act_1978" title="Interpretation Act 1978">Interpretation Act 1978</a> provides statutory definitions of the terms "England", "Wales" and the "United Kingdom", but neither that Act nor any other current statute defines "Scotland" or "Northern Ireland". Use of the first three terms in other legislation is interpreted following the definitions in the 1978 Act. The definitions in the 1978 Act are listed below:\n</p>\n<ul><li>"England" means, "subject to any alteration of boundaries under Part IV of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, the area consisting of the counties established by section 1 of that Act, Greater London and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>." This definition applies from 1 April 1974.</li>\n<li>"United Kingdom" means "Great Britain and Northern Ireland." This definition applies from 12 April 1927.</li>\n<li>"Wales" means the combined area of 13 <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_Wales" title="Historic counties of Wales">historic counties</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> including <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire_(historic)" title="Monmouthshire (historic)">Monmouthshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972#Wales_2" title="Local Government Act 1972">re-formulated</a> into 8 new counties under section 20 of the Local Government Act 1972, as originally enacted, but subject to any alteration made under section 73 of that Act (consequential alteration of boundary following alteration of watercourse). In 1996 these 8 <a href="/wiki/Preserved_counties_of_Wales" title="Preserved counties of Wales">new counties</a> were redistributed into the current <a href="/wiki/Wales#Local_government" title="Wales">22 unitary authorities</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a> there is no delineation of Scotland, with the definition in section 126 simply providing that Scotland includes "so much of the internal waters and territorial sea of the United Kingdom as are adjacent to Scotland".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Voting_System_and_Constituencies_Act_2011" title="Parliamentary Voting System and Constituencies Act 2011">Parliamentary Voting System and Constituencies Act 2011</a> refers to England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland as "<i>parts</i>" of the United Kingdom in the following clause: "Each constituency shall be wholly in one of the four parts of the United Kingdom (England, Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland)."\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_official_usage">Other section: Other official usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Fine_Art_Commission_(1840s)" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Fine Art href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Current">Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>"Regions": For purposes of <a href="/wiki/NUTS_1" class="mw-redirect" title="NUTS 1">NUTS 1</a> collection of statistical data in a format that is compatible with similar data that is collected elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the United Kingdom has been divided into twelve regions of approximately equal size.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland are regions in their own right while England has been divided into nine regions.\n</p><p>The official term <b>rest of the UK</b> (RUK or rUK) is used in Scotland, for example in export statistics<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and in legislating for <a This term is also used in the context of potential <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a> to mean the UK without Scotland.\n</p><p>The alternative term <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Nations" title="Home Nations">Home Nations</a></i> is sometimes used in sporting contexts and may include all of the island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_nationality">Identity and nationality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Identity and nationality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Demography_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Demography of the United Kingdom">Demography of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/British_Social_Attitudes_Survey" title="British Social Attitudes Survey">British Social Attitudes Survey</a>, there are broadly two interpretations of British identity, with ethnic and civic dimensions:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The first group, which we term the ethnic dimension, contained the items about birthplace, ancestry, living in Britain, and sharing British customs and traditions. The second, or civic group, contained the items about feeling British, respecting laws and institutions, speaking English, and having British citizenship.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Of the two perspectives of British identity, the civic definition has become the dominant idea and in this capacity, <a href="/wiki/Britishness" title="Britishness">Britishness</a> is sometimes considered an institutional or overarching <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a> identity.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This has been used to explain why first-, second- and third-generation immigrants are more likely to describe themselves as British, rather than English, Northern Irish, Scottish or Welsh, because it is an "institutional, inclusive" identity, that can be acquired through <a href="/wiki/Naturalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalisation">naturalisation</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British nationality law</a>; the vast majority of people in the United Kingdom who are from an ethnic minority feel British.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, this attitude is more common in England than in Scotland or Wales; "white English people perceived themselves as English first and as British second, and most people from ethnic minority backgrounds perceived themselves as British, but none identified as English, a label they associated exclusively with white people".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Contrariwise, in Scotland and Wales "there was a much stronger\nidentification with each country than with Britain."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Studies and surveys have reported that the majority of the Scots and Welsh see themselves as both Scottish/Welsh and British though with some differences in emphasis. The <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Racial_Equality" title="Commission for Racial Equality">Commission for Racial Equality</a> found that with respect to notions of <a href="/wiki/Nationality" title="Nationality">nationality</a> in Britain, "the most basic, objective and uncontroversial conception of the British people is one that includes the English, the Scots and the Welsh".<sup id="cite_ref-CRE22_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRE22-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> However, "English participants tended to think of themselves as indistinguishably English or British, while both Scottish and Welsh participants identified themselves much more readily as Scottish or Welsh than as British".<sup id="cite_ref-CRE22_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRE22-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Some people opted "to combine both identities" as "they felt Scottish or Welsh, but held a <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passport</a> and were therefore British", whereas others saw themselves as exclusively Scottish or exclusively Welsh and "felt quite divorced from the British, whom they saw as the English".<sup id="cite_ref-CRE22_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRE22-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Commentators have described this latter phenomenon as "<a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>", a rejection of British identity because some Scots and Welsh interpret it as "cultural imperialism imposed" upon the United Kingdom by "English ruling elites",<sup id="cite_ref-Ward2-3_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ward2-3-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> or else a response to a historical misappropriation of equating the word "English" with "British",<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> which has "brought about a desire among Scots, Welsh and Irish to learn more about their heritage and distinguish themselves from the broader British identity".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The propensity for nationalistic feeling varies greatly across the UK, and can rise and fall over time.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The state-funded Northern Ireland Life and Times Survey,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> part of a joint project between the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ulster" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Ulster">University of Ulster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University_Belfast" title="Queen&#39;s University Belfast">Queen\'s University Belfast</a>, has addressed the issue of identity in since it started polling in 1998. It reported that 37% of people identified as British, whilst 29% identified as Irish and 24% identified as Northern Irish. 3% opted to identify themselves as Ulster, whereas 7% stated \'other\'. Of the two main religious groups, 68% of Protestants identified as British as did 6% of Catholics; 60% of Catholics identified as Irish as did 3% of Protestants. 21% of Protestants and 26% of Catholics identified as Northern Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For Northern Ireland, however, the results of the Life &amp; Times Survey are not the whole story. The poll asks for a single preference, whereas many people easily identify as any combination of British and Irish, or British, Northern Irish and Irish, or Irish and Northern Irish. The 2014 Life &amp; Times Survey addressed this to an extent by choosing two of the options from the identity question: British and Irish. It found that, while 28% of respondents stated they felt "British <i>not</i> Irish" and 26% felt "Irish <i>not</i> British", 39% of respondents felt some combination of both identities. Six percent chose \'other description\'.<sup id="cite_ref-ark.ac.uk_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ark.ac.uk-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The identity question is confounded further by identity with politics and religion, and particularly by a stance on the constitutional status of Northern Ireland. Again in 2014, the Life &amp; Times Survey asked what respondents felt should be the "long term future for Northern Ireland". 66% of respondents felt the future should be as a part of the UK, with or without devolved government. 17% felt that Northern Ireland should unify with the Republic of Ireland. 50% of specifically Roman Catholics considered that the long-term future should be as part of the UK, with 32% opting for separation. 87% of respondents identifying as any Protestant denomination opted for remaining part of the UK, with only 4% opting for separation. Of those respondents who declared no religion, 62% opted for remaining part of the UK, with 9% opting for separation.<sup id="cite_ref-ark.ac.uk_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ark.ac.uk-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following <a href="/wiki/Devolution#United_Kingdom" title="Devolution">devolution</a> and the significant broadening of autonomous governance throughout the UK in the late 1990s, debate has taken place across the United Kingdom on the relative value of full <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> an option that was rejected<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> by the Scottish people in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum, 2014</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> is administered as a county of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a> are a recognised national minority, included under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Framework_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_National_Minorities" title="Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities">Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities</a> in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-govcornish_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-govcornish-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
