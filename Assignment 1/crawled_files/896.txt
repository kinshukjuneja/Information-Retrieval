Helen\'s</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw"><b>Enys Elidius</b></i>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is one of the fifty or so uninhabited islands in the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> and has an approximate <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a> of  0.1885 square kilometres (46.6 acres). On the south side of the island is one of the earliest Christian sites in Scilly, an early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> religious complex, which is thought to be the remains of St Elidius Hermitage, an 8th-century chapel lived in by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lide" title="Saint Lide">Saint Lide</a>, (also known as Elid or Elidius). There are also the remains of an isolation hospital used to quarantine sailors with <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a>. The island is the major part of a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and some features have been given the designation of <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_ancient_monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled ancient monument">scheduled ancient monument</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Access to the island is through chartered or private boat,<sup id="cite_ref-StHelens_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StHelens-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> although there are some season trips throughout the summer.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> St Helen\'s is currently managed by the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly_Wildlife_Trust" title="Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust">Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s is the third largest of the uninhabited islands and is situated in the northern part, between <a href="/wiki/Tresco,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Tresco, Isles of Scilly">Tresco</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Martin&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Martin\xe2\x80\x99s</a>. It has an area of 18.85 hectares (47 acres) and rises to 42&#160;m (138&#160;ft). Some of the nearby islands are considered to be part of the St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s group, these are; Foreman\xe2\x80\x99s Island, Men-a-vaur, Northwethel, <a href="/wiki/Round_Island_Light,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Round Island Light, Isles of Scilly">Round Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Te%C3%A4n" title="Te\xc3\xa4n">Te\xc3\xa4n</a>. The island mostly consists of a hill of coarse-grained, highly jointed <a href="/wiki/Hercynian" class="mw-redirect" title="Hercynian">Hercynian</a> <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>. The landing site for the island is on a sandy beach on the south side where there are the remains of a granite quay and low <a href="/wiki/Dune#Coastal_dunes" title="Dune">sand dunes</a> at the top of the beach. The ruined pest house is situated on a flat area behind the dunes and further inland the religious complex is based at the foot of the hill. To the east of the complex is a <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">well</a> and the walls of several fields.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-history">Pre-history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cairns">Cairns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cairns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The platform <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a> on St Helen\'s make up a large amount of the total 387 surviving cairns on the Isles of Scilly, and so have been registered as an ancient monument. The remaining <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> cairn field on the island consists of four ring cairns on the slight north-west facing slope. The first cairn is 4 m in diameter with a ring of earth with protruding spaced stones, 1m wide and 0.2 m high. The second cairn is oval 4 m by 3 m with a ring 0.4 m wide and 0.2 m high, incorporate two contiguous slabs embedded on edge. The third cairn is 6 m in diameter with a ring with spaced stones averaging 1m wide and 0.3 m high. The fourth cairn is 4 m in diameter with a ring of spaced stones which averages 0.7 m wide and 0.2 m high.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Christian_Chapel">Early Christian Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Christian Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>\nOn the south slope of St Helen\'s there is an early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> religious complex that is thought to be the remains of St Elidius Hermitage, an 8th-century chapel lived in by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lide" title="Saint Lide">Saint Lide</a> (also known as Elid or Elidius) which is also Saint Lide alleged burial place. <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">Archaeological</a> <a href="/wiki/Excavation_(archaeology)" title="Excavation (archaeology)">excavations</a> suggest that the site developed from a solitary <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_(religious_retreat)" title="Hermitage (religious retreat)">hermit</a> site, with a single round hut and oratory, to a communal hermitage with a number of rectangular huts for individual accommodation, surrounding the church and oratory buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Five graves from the early hermitage were also excavated east of the living section. A field system was also present providing food for the Hermitage/Chapel, and later the Pest House residents.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the early 11th-century a small church was built on the north east side of the living section and around 1120 AD the church was granted to <a href="/wiki/Tavistock_Abbey" title="Tavistock Abbey">Tavistock Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This increased the numbers of pilgrims visiting the site due to it being the shrine of Elidius, and many alterations and remodeling were made to the complex.<sup id="cite_ref-StHelens_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StHelens-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, further excavations of the site have uncovered decorated ridge-tiles and parts of the 12th century Purbeck Marble shrine that is thought to have once housed Saint Elidius <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  It is also said that there was once a causeway from abbey church at <a href="/wiki/Tresco,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Tresco, Isles of Scilly">Tresco</a> across the downs to the church on St Helen\'s Isle.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Currently there are yearly open air service on 8 August to celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">feast of St Elidius</a> at the small ruined church.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-0" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: St Helen&#039;s Pool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>St Helen\'s Pool is a stretch of water just off St Helen\'s island which provided sheltered anchorage for shipping,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> though there is a chance ships may experience <a href="/wiki/Swell_(ocean)" title="Swell (ocean)">swells</a> near <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">high water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\nThere is  evidence to show that during the later medieval period the <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a> of Tresco may have collected <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">tolls</a> from ships for anchorage in St Helen\'s Pool, as it is considered that it was the main harbour of the islands in medieval times.<sup id="cite_ref-Assessment_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Assessment-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pest_house">The Pest house</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Pest house">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>St Helen\'s Isolation hospital, also known as the <i>Pest House</i>, was a <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">quarantine</a> station built in 1764<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> to house <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> cases from visiting ships calling at <a href="/wiki/Old_Grimsby" title="Old Grimsby">Old Grimsby</a> and St Helen\'s Pool. It was constructed after an <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Parliament in the United Kingdom">Act of Parliament</a> in 1754 decreed that any plague-ridden ship north of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Finisterre" title="Cape Finisterre">Cape Finisterre</a> heading for <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> should anchor off this island.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The station included the building of an isolation hospital as well as a <a href="/wiki/Slipway" title="Slipway">slipway</a> and an extensive <a href="/wiki/Wharf" title="Wharf">quay</a> to serve it.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The pest house was still open to receive patients from quarantined vessels.<sup id="cite_ref-north_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-north-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The hospital building is a rectangular, roofless building, 7 m by 5.5 m externally with a two\xe2\x80\x93roomed extension on its east side. The walls of the building are 0.6 m wide and 3 m high, and are constructed of mortared rubble with other details picked out in larger stones. The building has recently been repaired and stabilized to prevent its collapse with the help of <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> funding through the Isles of Scilly (IOS) Grant Scheme, administered by Cornwall and Isles of Scilly Community Grants Programme (CIOS).<sup id="cite_ref-Assessment_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Assessment-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> There is also a graveyard associated with the pest house which is known to include the grave of a 27-year-old naval <a href="/wiki/Surgeon" title="Surgeon">surgeon</a> who was sent to treat the sick and died within a week himself, and various passengers from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> who also died at the station.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_and_ecology">Wildlife and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wildlife and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island is part of the <b>St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s (with Northwethel and Men-a-vaur)</b> Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI) and is designated for its rare flora and breeding seabirds. The SSSI was first notified in 1971, re-notified in 1986 and covers all land above the mean high-water mark.<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1940 the highest land on St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s was covered in maritime heath with ling (<i><a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">Calluna vulgaris</a></i>) and bell heather (<i><a href="/wiki/Erica_cinerea" title="Erica cinerea">Erica cinerea</a></i>) which was destroyed by fires from <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">incendiary</a> bombs from German aircraft during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. There were further fires during the dry weather of 1949, and when <a href="/wiki/Job_Edward_Lousley" title="Job Edward Lousley">J E Lousley</a> surveyed the vegetation in 1957 the soil was only one or two inches deep, grey and with granite chips. The most abundant plant was buck\xe2\x80\x99s-horn plantain (<i><a href="/wiki/Plantago_coronopus" title="Plantago coronopus">Plantago coronopus</a></i>) and English stonecrop (<i><a href="/wiki/Sedum_anglicum" title="Sedum anglicum">Sedum anglicum</a></i>). The fires probably also affected the frequency and cover of the <a href="/wiki/Lichens" class="mw-redirect" title="Lichens">lichens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryophytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Bryophytes">bryophytes</a>. At the time of the publication of Lousley\xe2\x80\x99s flora (1971) the vegetation had still not reverted to heath and by 1987 Hottentot fig (<i><a href="/wiki/Carpobrotus_edulis" title="Carpobrotus edulis">Carpobrotus edulis</a></i>) had spread over much of the area. By 2002 much of this plant had disappeared possibly because of salt spray, or because of unusually cold weather. At the last SSSI assessment in 2009, vegetation on the island including the heath was considered to be in \xe2\x80\xb3<i>a favourable condition</i>\xe2\x80\xb3. Lousley found large areas of cliffs and rocks were also covered with Hottentot fig which is spread by gulls carrying the plant to their nests. It can then take root and spread over bare ground and vegetation.\n</p><p>The lower lying ground to the south of the hill, has deeper soils with black <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a> and white sand grains. The flora is dominated by a tangle of bracken (<i><a href="/wiki/Pteridium_aquilinum" title="Pteridium aquilinum">Pteridium aquilinum</a></i>) and bramble (<i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_ulmifolius" title="Rubus ulmifolius">Rubus ulmifolius</a></i>) and is comparatively rich compared with many of the other islands; probably because St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s has less salt spay due to its comparatively sheltered position being surrounded by other islands. The long history of human settlement has also allowed for the establishment of additional species, either by <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">human introduction</a>, or by <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> which has helped to develop deeper soils on the lower ground. Around the pest house there is rank grassland and scrub with flowering plants such as hemlock (<i><a href="/wiki/Conium_maculatum" title="Conium maculatum">Conium maculatum</a></i>), hogweed (<i><a href="/wiki/Heracleum_sphondylium" title="Heracleum sphondylium">Heracleum sphondylium</a></i>) and good populations of balm-leaved figwort (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrophularia_scorodonia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Scrophularia scorodonia (page does not exist)">Scrophularia scorodonia</a></i>) which is spreading in one area. Unusually for a small Scillonian island there is also grey willow (<i><a href="/wiki/Salix_cinerea" title="Salix cinerea">Salix cinerea</a></i>), wood spurge (<i><a href="/wiki/Euphorbia_amygdaloides" title="Euphorbia amygdaloides">Euphorbia amygdaloides</a></i>) and small reed (<i><a href="/wiki/Calamagrostis_epigejos" title="Calamagrostis epigejos">Calamagrostis epigejos</a></i>). In the past the areas around the buildings would have been cultivated or grazed and North reported the island was uncultivated during his visit in 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-north_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-north-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> To allow access for visitors to the August church service, the tall vegetation is cut around the ruined buildings and on the path from the landing place.\n</p><p>On the north-west side of the island there is an area of maritime grassland with abundant thrift (<i><a href="/wiki/Armeria_maritima" title="Armeria maritima">Armeria maritima</a></i>), sea beet (<i><a href="/wiki/Sea_beet" title="Sea beet">Beta vulgaris subsp. maritima</a></i>) and tree mallow (<i><a href="/wiki/Lavatera_arborea" class="mw-redirect" title="Lavatera arborea">Lavatera arborea</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lousley_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lousley-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Webber_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webber-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Also on the north side and directly opposite Round Island, Issac North (1850) found a chasm richly covered in sea spleenwort (<i><a href="/wiki/Asplenium_marinum" title="Asplenium marinum">Asplenium marinum</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-north_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-north-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shore_dock">Shore dock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Shore dock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Shore dock  (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rumex_rupestris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rumex rupestris (page does not exist)">Rumex rupestris</a></i>)  is one of the rarer <a href="/wiki/Rumex" title="Rumex">docks</a> and a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="United Kingdom Biodiversity Action Plan">BAP</a> species; it is one of the reasons why the Isles of Scilly is a <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> (SAC). It was recorded in 1957 by Lousley, but has not been seen since and is thought to be extinct on St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s. It was recorded nearby on Te\xc3\xa4n in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-SAC_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAC-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> of the outer islands of the Isles of Scilly is generally poorly recorded because of, both the difficulty of reaching them, and the length of the time spent.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mammals">Mammals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mammals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s has a long history of habitation and when Issac North visited the islands in 1850 he reported goats (<i><a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">Capra aegagrus hircus</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-north_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-north-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The deer were still present in 1865, and in 1870 sheep (<i><a href="/wiki/Ovis_aries" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovis aries">Ovis aries</a></i>) were said to run wild.<sup id="cite_ref-Lousley_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lousley-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A 1919 guide book recorded St Helen\'s only inhabitants as goats, deer, rabbits and sea fowl.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The Scilly shrew (<i><a href="/wiki/Lesser_white-toothed_shrew" title="Lesser white-toothed shrew">Crocidura suaveolens</a></i>) was seen on low cliffs in 1964 and rabbit (<i><a href="/wiki/Oryctolagus_cuniculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Oryctolagus cuniculus">Oryctolagus cuniculus</a></i>) has not been recorded since 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Birds">Birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Cooper (2006) recorded a few great black-backed gull <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">(<i>Larus marinus</i>)</a> nesting, a scattering of herring gull <a href="/wiki/European_herring_gull" title="European herring gull">(<i>Larus argentatus</i>)</a> and an overflow colony of kittiwake <a href="/wiki/Black-legged_kittiwake" title="Black-legged kittiwake">(<i>Rissa tridactyla</i>)</a> on the eastern cliffs, while Parslow (2007) records a large colony of lesser black-backed gull <a href="/wiki/Lesser_black-backed_gull" title="Lesser black-backed gull">(<i>Larus fuscus</i>)</a> and a small colony of puffin (<i><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Fratercula arctica</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cooper_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The Seabird 2000 breeding seabird survey recorded five occupied nests of Manx shearwater (<i><a href="/wiki/Puffinus_puffinus" class="mw-redirect" title="Puffinus puffinus">Puffinus puffinus</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Other_islands_in_the_St_Helen.E2.80.99s_group"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_islands_in_the_St_Helen\xe2\x80\x99s_group">Other islands in the St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other islands in the St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Foreman.E2.80.99s_Island"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreman\xe2\x80\x99s_Island">Foreman\xe2\x80\x99s Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Foreman\xe2\x80\x99s Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Foreman\xe2\x80\x99s Island (<a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;params=SV900160_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">SV900160</a></span>) is a 0.37 hectares (0.91 acres) low-lying island on the south side of St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s Pool between Te\xc3\xa4n and Tresco. It is one of a group of rocks and islets of importance for breeding seabirds.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The common froghopper (<i><a href="/wiki/Philaenus_spumarius" title="Philaenus spumarius">Philaenus spumarius</a></i>) has been recorded on Foreman\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Men-a-vaur">Men-a-vaur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Men-a-vaur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Men-a-vaur (<a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;params=SV893175_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">SV893175</a></span>) is to the north-west of St Helen\'s and consists of three granite slabs covering an area of 0.55 hectares (1.4 acres) and rising to 38 metres (125&#160;ft). It is part of the SSSI for its seabirds with eight breeding species. The razorbill (<i><a href="/wiki/Alca_torda" class="mw-redirect" title="Alca torda">Alca torda</a></i>) population is of national importance and together with fulmar (<i><a href="/wiki/Fulmaris_glacialis" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulmaris glacialis">Fulmaris glacialis</a></i>) and guillemot (<i><a href="/wiki/Uria_aalge" class="mw-redirect" title="Uria aalge">Uria aalge</a></i>) are the largest in the Isles of Scilly; the other species are kittiwake, great black-backed gull, lesser black-backed gull, herring gull, shag (<i><a href="/wiki/Phalacrocorax_aristotelis" class="mw-redirect" title="Phalacrocorax aristotelis">Phalacrocorax aristotelis</a></i>) and puffin.<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Peregrine falcon (<i><a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">Falco peregrinus</a></i>) and raven (<a href="/wiki/Common_raven" title="Common raven"><i>Corvus corax</i></a>) breed in some years.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lousley recorded common scurvygrass (<i><a href="/wiki/Cochlearia_officinalis" title="Cochlearia officinalis">Cochlearia officinalis</a></i>), tree mallow and a species of orache (<a href="/wiki/Atriplex" title="Atriplex"><i>Atriplex</i> spp</a>) although flowering plants are not a permanent feature.<sup id="cite_ref-Lousley_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lousley-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The rare <a href="/wiki/Rove_beetle" title="Rove beetle">rove beetle</a>, (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omalium_allardi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Omalium allardi (page does not exist)">Omalium allardi</a></i>) has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northwethel">Northwethel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Northwethel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Northwethel (<a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;params=SV895163_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">SV895163</a></span>) is a 4.63 hectares (11.4 acres) and 15.2&#160;m (50&#160;ft) high island in Old Grimsby Channel, off Grimble Porth, Tresco. The island consists of two hills connected by a low stretch of land. There is a sandy beach at the landing area on the south and a small brackish pool behind the sea bank.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hunt_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Human habitation dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age</a> with a cairn field of up to nine cairns including one that has a chamber 5.5&#160;m (18&#160;ft) in diameter with two cover stones still in place. The chamber is oriented east to west and 3.0&#160;m (10&#160;ft) long, 1.36&#160;m (4.5&#160;ft) wide at the centre and 0.45&#160;m (18&#160;in) at the entrance. There is also a prehistoric field system and a settlement with (probably) six <a href="/wiki/Hut_circle" title="Hut circle">hut circles</a>. Some of the hut circles are integrated into the field walls of which there are three or four irregular shaped fields 30.0&#160;m (98&#160;ft) to 50.0&#160;m (164&#160;ft) across. The hut circles have an internal width of 4.0&#160;m (13&#160;ft) to 6.0&#160;m (20&#160;ft). A number of finds of flint, bone and pottery of unknown date have been deposited with the Isles of Scilly Museum on <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Mary&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Mary\xe2\x80\x99s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> It is said that 1,500 <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Parliamentarians</a> landed on the island in the mistaken belief they were on Tresco and a roughly rectangular enclosure with two sides of drystone walling is a possible \xe2\x80\xb3Parliamentary army shelter\xe2\x80\xb3.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> On the west side of the island are the remains of a rusting ship.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunt_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The botanist <a href="/wiki/Job_Edward_Lousley" title="Job Edward Lousley">J E Lousley</a> visited the island one afternoon in May 1957 and recorded fifty-four species of plant, despite finding most of the island covered in a tangle of bracken and bramble to 1&#160;m (3&#160;ft) high and difficult to penetrate.<sup id="cite_ref-Lousley_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lousley-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Unusual plants (for the Isles of Scilly) in the brackish pool are sea-milkwort (<i><a href="/wiki/Glaux" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaux">Glaux maritima</a></i>), fennel-leaved pondweed (<i><a href="/wiki/Stuckenia_pectinata" title="Stuckenia pectinata">Stuckenia pectinata</a></i>) and red goosefoot (<i><a href="/wiki/Chenopodium_rubrum" class="mw-redirect" title="Chenopodium rubrum">Chenopodium rubrum</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_13-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A visit by John Hunt in the 1980s found a \xe2\x80\xb3healthy\xe2\x80\xb3 growth of bracken.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunt_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The fern soft shield-fern (<i><a href="/wiki/Polystichum_setiferum" title="Polystichum setiferum">Polystichum setiferum</a></i>) once thought to be very rare on the islands has now been found on all the inhabited islands (except Bryher) and curiously also on Northwethel.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Round_Island">Round Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Round Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Round_Island_Light,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Round Island Light, Isles of Scilly">Round Island Light, Isles of Scilly</a></div>\n<p>Round Island (<a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;params=SV901177_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">SV901177</a></span>) is a 4.09 hectares (10.1 acres) island to the north of St Helen\xe2\x80\x99s with an unmanned <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a> on the summit. It is part of the <i>Pentle Bay, Merrick and Round Islands Site of Special Scientific Interest</i> (SSSI).\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Te.C3.A4n"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Te\xc3\xa4n">Te\xc3\xa4n</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Te\xc3\xa4n">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Te%C3%A4n" title="Te\xc3\xa4n">Te\xc3\xa4n</a></div>\n<p>Te\xc3\xa4n (<a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;params=SV908164_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">SV908164</a></span>) is a 16.13 hectares (39.9 acres) island between St Helen\'s, to the north-west and St Martin\'s to the east. The island has a long history of habitation and was designated as the <i>Te\xc3\xa4n Site of Special Scientific Interest</i> (SSSI) in 1971. It is included in the Isles of Scilly <a href="/wiki/Geological_Conservation_Review" title="Geological Conservation Review">Geological Conservation Review</a> for the linking of islands by a sandy bar or <a href="/wiki/Tombolo" title="Tombolo">tombolo</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Weatherhill, C (1995). <i>Cornish Placenames and Language</i>.
