War">Naval</a></li></ul>\n</div></td></tr></tbody></table></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War</b> was the central <a href="/wiki/Theater_(warfare)" title="Theater (warfare)">area</a> of operations in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in the second half of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. During the first three years of the conflict, the largest military encounters were in the north, focused on campaigns around the cities of <a href="/wiki/Boston_campaign" title="Boston campaign">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_and_New_Jersey_campaign" title="New York and New Jersey campaign">New York</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_campaign" title="Philadelphia campaign">Philadelphia</a>. After the failure of the <a href="/wiki/Saratoga_campaign" title="Saratoga campaign">Saratoga campaign</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> largely abandoned operations in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Colonies" title="Middle Colonies">Middle Colonies</a> and pursued peace through subjugation in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Colonies" title="Southern Colonies">Southern Colonies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Before 1778, the southern colonies were largely dominated by <a href="/wiki/Patriot_(American_Revolution)" title="Patriot (American Revolution)">Patriot</a>-controlled governments and militias, although there was also a <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> presence that played a role in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sullivan%27s_Island" title="Battle of Sullivan&#39;s Island">defense of Charleston</a> in 1776, suppression of <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Loyalist</a> militias, and attempts to drive the British from strongly Loyalist <a href="/wiki/East_Florida" title="East Florida">East Florida</a>.\n</p><p>The British "southern strategy" commenced in late 1778 with the <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Savannah" title="Capture of Savannah">capture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>, which was followed in 1780 by operations in <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> that included the defeat of two Continental Armies at <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Charleston" title="Siege of Charleston">Charleston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Camden" title="Battle of Camden">Camden</a>.  General <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a>, who took over as Continental Army commander after Camden, engaged in a strategy of avoidance and attrition against the British. The two forces fought a string of battles, most of which were tactical victories for the British. In almost all cases, however, <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhic_victory" title="Pyrrhic victory">the "victories" strategically weakened the British army by the high cost in casualties</a>, while leaving the Continental Army intact to continue fighting. This was best exemplified by the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guilford_Courthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Guilford Courthouse">Battle of Guilford Courthouse</a>. Several American victories, such as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ramseur%27s_Mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Ramseur&#39;s Mill">Battle of Ramseur\'s Mill</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cowpens" title="Battle of Cowpens">Battle of Cowpens</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kings_Mountain" title="Battle of Kings Mountain">Battle of Kings Mountain</a>, also served to weaken the overall British military strength. The culminating engagement, the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown" title="Siege of Yorktown">Siege of Yorktown</a>, ended with the British army\'s surrender. It essentially marked the end of <a href="/wiki/British_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="British colonization of the Americas">British power in the Colonies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" The map is oriented with North to the bottom and South to the top.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia">Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In most colonies British officials quickly departed as the Patriots took control. In Virginia, the royal governor resisted. In the <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder_Incident" title="Gunpowder Incident">Gunpowder Incident</a> of April 20, 1775, <a href="/wiki/John_Murray,_4th_Earl_of_Dunmore" title="John Murray, 4th Earl of Dunmore">Lord Dunmore</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_government_in_the_Thirteen_Colonies" title="Colonial government in the Thirteen Colonies">Royal Governor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Province of Virginia">Virginia</a>, removed gunpowder stored in <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Virginia" title="Williamsburg, Virginia">Williamsburg</a> to a British warship in the <a href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" class="mw-redirect" title="James River (Virginia)">James River</a>. Dunmore saw rising unrest in the colony and was trying to deprive Virginia <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> of supplies needed for insurrection. Patriot militia led by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Henry" title="Patrick Henry">Patrick Henry</a> forced Dunmore to pay for the gunpowder.  Dunmore continued to hunt for caches of military equipment and supplies in the following months, acts that were sometimes anticipated by Patriot militia, who would move supplies before his arrival.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Dunmore issued an <a href="/wiki/Dunmore%27s_Proclamation" title="Dunmore&#39;s Proclamation">emancipation proclamation</a> in November 1775, promising freedom to runaway slaves who fought for the British. After an <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kemp%27s_Landing" title="Battle of Kemp&#39;s Landing">incident at Kemp\'s Landing</a> in November where Dunmore\'s troops killed and captured Patriot militiamen, Patriot forces defeated Loyalist troops (which included runaway slaves Dunmore had formed into his <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Regiment" title="Ethiopian Regiment">Ethiopian Regiment</a>) at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Great_Bridge" title="Battle of Great Bridge">Battle of Great Bridge</a> on December 9. Dunmore and his troops retreated to <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> ships anchored off <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a>; these naval forces <a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Norfolk" title="Burning of Norfolk">bombarded and burned the town</a> on January 1, 1776. Patriot forces in the town completed the destruction of the former Loyalist stronghold. Dunmore was driven from an island in <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> that summer, and never returned to Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Georgia">Georgia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Georgia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U._S._state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgia (U. S. state)">Georgia</a>\'s royal governor, <a href="/wiki/James_Wright_(governor)" title="James Wright (governor)">James Wright</a>, nominally remained in power until January 1776, when the unexpected arrival of British ships near <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah</a> prompted the local <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Safety_(American_Revolution)" class="mw-redirect" title="Committee of Safety (American Revolution)">Committee of Safety</a> to order his arrest. Georgia Patriots and Loyalists alike believed the fleet had arrived to provide military support to the governor; it had been sent from the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">besieged</a> British forces in <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a> to acquire rice and other provisions. Wright escaped captivity and reached the fleet. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Rice_Boats" title="Battle of the Rice Boats">Battle of the Rice Boats</a> in early March, the British successfully left Savannah with a number of merchant vessels containing the desired rice supplies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Carolina">South Carolina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: South Carolina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Snow_Campaign" title="Snow Campaign">Snow Campaign</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sullivan%27s_Island" title="Battle of Sullivan&#39;s Island">Battle of Sullivan\'s Island</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SirHenryClinton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/SirHenryClinton.jpg/250px-SirHenryClinton.jpg" width="250" height="285" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/SirHenryClinton.jpg/375px-SirHenryClinton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/SirHenryClinton.jpg/500px-SirHenryClinton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="526" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SirHenryClinton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Sir Henry Clinton</a> led the British land forces in the failed attack on Charleston.</div></div></div>\n<p>South Carolina\'s population was politically divided when the war began. The lowland communities, dominated by <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston</a>, were strongly Patriot in their views, while the back country held a large number of Loyalist sympathizers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> By August 1775, both sides were recruiting militia companies.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In September, Patriot militia seized <a href="/wiki/Fort_Johnson_(South_Carolina)" title="Fort Johnson (South Carolina)">Fort Johnson</a>, Charleston\'s major defense works, and Governor <a href="/wiki/Lord_William_Campbell" title="Lord William Campbell">William Campbell</a> fled to a Royal Navy ship in the harbor.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The seizure by Loyalists of a shipment of gunpowder and ammunition intended for the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Cherokee people">Cherokee</a> caused an escalation in tensions that led to the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Savage%27s_Old_Fields" title="Siege of Savage&#39;s Old Fields">First Siege of Ninety Six</a> in western South Carolina late November.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Patriot recruiting was by then outstripping that of the Loyalists, and a major campaign (called the <a href="/wiki/Snow_Campaign" title="Snow Campaign">Snow Campaign</a> due to unusually heavy snowfall) involving as many as 5,000 Patriots led by Colonel <a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Richardson_(military_officer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Richard Richardson (military officer) (page does not exist)">Richard Richardson</a> succeeded in capturing or driving away most of the Loyalist leadership. Loyalists fled, either to <a href="/wiki/East_Florida" title="East Florida">East Florida</a> or to the Cherokee lands. A faction of the Cherokee, known as the <a href="/wiki/Chickamauga_Cherokee" title="Chickamauga Cherokee">Chickamauga</a>, rose up in support of the British and Loyalists in 1776. They were finally defeated by militia forces from North and South Carolina.\n</p><p>Crucial in any British attempt to gain control of <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">the South</a> was the possession of a port to bring in supplies and men. To this end, the British organized an expedition to establish a strong post somewhere in the southern colonies, and sent military leaders to recruit Loyalists in North Carolina. The expedition\'s departure from Europe was significantly delayed, and the Loyalist force that was recruited to meet it was decisively defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moore%27s_Creek_Bridge" title="Battle of Moore&#39;s Creek Bridge">Battle of Moore\'s Creek Bridge</a> in late February 1776. When General <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Henry Clinton</a> arrived at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_(headland)" title="Cape Fear (headland)">Cape Fear</a>, North Carolina, in May, he found conditions there unsuitable for a strong post. Scouting by the Royal Navy identified Charleston, whose defenses were unfinished and seemed vulnerable, as a more suitable location. In June 1776, Clinton and Admiral <a href="/wiki/Sir_Peter_Parker,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Peter Parker, 1st Baronet">Sir Peter Parker</a> led an <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sullivan%27s_Island" title="Battle of Sullivan&#39;s Island">assault on Fort Sullivan</a>, which guarded the Charleston harbor.\n</p><p>Clinton had failed to order a complete reconnaissance of the area. His 2,200 men force was landed on Long Island (adjacent to Sullivan\'s Island on which the fort was positioned), and they found the channel dividing the two islands too deep to ford.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Instead of re-embarking on his boats, he relied on the expedition\'s naval forces to reduce the fort, which became known after the war as <a href="/wiki/Fort_Moultrie" title="Fort Moultrie">Fort Moultrie</a>. However, the firepower of the British ships was unable to make an impression on the spongy palmetto logs that formed the majority of the fort\'s defenses, and the bombardment failed in its objective.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It was a humiliating failure, and Clinton called off his campaign in the Carolinas.<sup id="cite_ref-Jstor_link_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jstor_link-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Clinton and Parker argued after the engagement, each blaming the other for the failure of the assault.<sup id="cite_ref-Jstor_link_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jstor_link-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It is debated that the South was lost by this failure to take Charleston in 1776, as it left the Loyalists unsupported for three years, while allowing the port of Charleston to serve the American cause until 1780.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_attempts_at_British_East_Florida">Failed attempts at British East Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Failed attempts at British East Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Patriots in Georgia attempted several times to defeat the British garrison that was based at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Augustine, Florida">Saint Augustine</a> in British <a href="/wiki/East_Florida" title="East Florida">East Florida</a>.  This garrison actively supported the activities of Loyalists who fled there from Georgia and other southern states, and were responsible for raiding cattle and other supplies in southern Georgia. The first attempt was organized by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lee_(general)" title="Charles Lee (general)">Charles Lee</a> after he took command of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>\'s Southern Department, but sputtered out when he was recalled to the main army. The second attempt was organized by Georgia Governor <a href="/wiki/Button_Gwinnett" title="Button Gwinnett">Button Gwinnett</a> with minimal help from the new commander of the Southern Department, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Howe_(Continental_Army_officer)" title="Robert Howe (Continental Army officer)">Robert Howe</a>, in 1777. This expedition also failed. Gwinnett and his militia commander, <a href="/wiki/Lachlan_McIntosh" title="Lachlan McIntosh">Lachlan McIntosh</a>, could not agree on anything. Some Georgia militia companies made it into East Florida, but they were checked in the May <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thomas_Creek" title="Battle of Thomas Creek">Battle of Thomas Creek</a>. The last expedition was in early 1778. More than 2,000 Continentals and state militia were raised for the effort, but it also failed due to issues of command between Howe and Georgia governor <a href="/wiki/John_Houstoun" title="John Houstoun">John Houstoun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  A <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alligator_Bridge" title="Battle of Alligator Bridge">brief skirmish at Alligator Bridge</a> in late June, combined with tropical diseases and command issues in the Patriot forces, left East Florida firmly in British hands for the war\'s duration.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_campaign_in_the_South">British campaign in the South</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: British campaign in the South">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Loyalist_question">The Loyalist question</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Loyalist question">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1778, the British again turned their attention to the South, where they hoped to regain control by recruiting thousands of Loyalists. Their belief in widespread Loyalist support was based on the accounts of Loyalist exiles in London who had direct access to the American Secretary, <a href="/wiki/George_Germain" class="mw-redirect" title="George Germain">George Germain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Keen to recover their lands and be rewarded for their loyalty to the crown, these men realized that the best way to convince the British to undertake a major operation in the South would be to exaggerate the level of potential Loyalist support. As a group, they had great influence on the British ministers in London.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In addition, there were strong business, trading and family ties among some Loyalists and the British in London. The British operated under the expectation that they would find substantial support for their actions, if only they liberated the right areas. While in South Carolina, Cornwallis wrote in a letter to Clinton that "Our assurances of attachment from our poor distressed friends in North Carolina are as strong as ever."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> For the most part, this assumption was incorrect, as Cornwallis soon realized as the campaign progressed.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_take_Savannah">British take Savannah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: British take Savannah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Benjamin_lincoln_by_charles_wilson_peale.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Benjamin_lincoln_by_charles_wilson_peale.jpg/220px-Benjamin_lincoln_by_charles_wilson_peale.jpg" width="220" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Benjamin_lincoln_by_charles_wilson_peale.jpg/330px-Benjamin_lincoln_by_charles_wilson_peale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dc/Benjamin_lincoln_by_charles_wilson_peale.jpg 2x" data-file-width="432" data-file-height="530" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Benjamin_lincoln_by_charles_wilson_peale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of General <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Lincoln" title="Benjamin Lincoln">Benjamin Lincoln</a>; by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Willson_Peale" title="Charles Willson Peale">Charles Willson Peale</a></div></div></div>\n<p>On April 19, 1778, three row galleys of the Georgia Navy engaged, defeated, and captured a Royal Navy <a href="/wiki/Brigantine" title="Brigantine">brigantine</a>, an armed British East Florida provincial sloop, and an armed brig.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On December 29, 1778, a British expeditionary corps of 3,500 men from New York, under the command of Lieutenant Colonel <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Campbell_(British_Army_officer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Archibald Campbell (British Army officer)">Archibald Campbell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Savannah" title="Capture of Savannah">captured Savannah, Georgia</a>.  He was joined in mid-January 1779 by Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Augustine_Prevost" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustine Prevost">Augustine Prevost</a>, leading troops that marched up from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Augustine, Florida">Saint Augustine</a>, taking over outposts along the way. Prevost assumed command of the forces in Georgia; and dispatched Campbell with 1,000 men toward <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta</a> with the goals of gaining control of that town and the recruitment of Loyalists.<sup id="cite_ref-Morill46_7_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morill46_7-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The remnants of the defense of Savannah had retreated to <a href="/wiki/Purrysburg,_South_Carolina" title="Purrysburg, South Carolina">Purrysburg, South Carolina</a>, about 12 miles (19&#160;km) upriver from Savannah, where they were met by Major General <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Lincoln" title="Benjamin Lincoln">Benjamin Lincoln</a>, commander of Continental Army forces in the South. He marched most of the army from Charleston, South Carolina in a move intended to monitor and oppose Prevost. Early in February, Prevost sent a few hundred men to occupy <a href="/wiki/Beaufort,_South_Carolina" title="Beaufort, South Carolina">Beaufort</a> in a move probably intended to divert Lincoln\'s attention from Campbell\'s movements; Lincoln responded by sending General Moultrie and 300 men to drive them out. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Beaufort" title="Battle of Beaufort">Battle of Beaufort</a> was largely indecisive, and both contingents eventually returned to their bases.\n</p><p>In the meantime, Campbell had taken control of Augusta without much resistance, and Loyalists were beginning to turn out. While he enrolled more than 1,000 men over a two-week period, he was powerless to prevent the defeat of a sizable number of Loyalists by Patriot militia under <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Pickens_(congressman)" title="Andrew Pickens (congressman)">Andrew Pickens</a> in the February 14 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kettle_Creek" title="Battle of Kettle Creek">Battle of Kettle Creek</a>, 50 miles (80&#160;km) from Augusta.  This demonstrated to everyone in the area the limits of the British Army\'s ability to protect Loyalists. Campbell suddenly left Augusta, apparently in response to the arrival of <a href="/wiki/John_Ashe_(general)" title="John Ashe (general)">John Ashe</a> and more than 1,000 North Carolina militia Lincoln sent to add to the 1,000 militia that were already across the river from Augusta in South Carolina. On the way back to Savannah, Campbell turned over command of his men to Augustine Prevost\'s brother, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Prevost" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark Prevost">Mark</a>. The younger Prevost turned the tables on Ashe, who was following him south, surprising and very nearly destroying his force of 1,300 in the March 3 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brier_Creek" title="Battle of Brier Creek">Battle of Brier Creek</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Morrill48_0_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morrill48_0-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_attack_on_Charleston">Second attack on Charleston</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Second attack on Charleston">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>By April, Lincoln had been reinforced by large numbers of South Carolina militia and received additional military supplies through <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch</a> shipments to Charleston. He decided to move toward Augusta. Leaving 1,000 men under the command of General Moultrie at Purrysburg to monitor Augustine Prevost, he began the march north on April 23. Prevost\'s reaction was to lead 2,500 men from Savannah toward Purrysburg on April 29. Moultrie fell back toward Charleston rather than engaging, and Prevost was within 10 miles (16&#160;km) on May 10 before he began to see resistance. Two days later he intercepted a message indicating that Lincoln, alerted to Prevost\'s advance, was hurrying back from Augusta to assist in the defense of Charleston. Prevost retreated to the islands southwest of Charleston, leaving an entrenched guard at Stono Ferry (near present-day <a href="/wiki/Rantowles,_South_Carolina" class="mw-redirect" title="Rantowles, South Carolina">Rantowles, South Carolina</a>) to cover his retreat. When Lincoln got back to Charleston he led about 1,200 men, mostly untried militia, after Prevost. This force was repulsed by the British on June 20 in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stono_Ferry" title="Battle of Stono Ferry">Battle of Stono Ferry</a>. The rear guard, having succeeded in its objective, abandoned that post a few days later.<sup id="cite_ref-Morrill53_4_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morrill53_4-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Prevost\'s foray against Charleston was notable for his troop\'s arbitrary looting and pillaging, which enraged friend and foe alike in the South Carolina low country.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_of_Savannah">Defense of Savannah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Defense of Savannah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In October 1779, an attempt by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> and Continental Army forces to <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Savannah" title="Siege of Savannah">retake Savannah</a>. Under the leadership of General Lincoln, and with the assistance of a French naval <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(naval)" title="Squadron (naval)">squadron</a> commanded by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hector,_comte_d%27Estaing" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Hector, comte d&#39;Estaing">Comte d\'Estaing</a>, it was a spectacular failure, with the combined French-American forces suffering as many as 901 casualties, to the British 54.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The French Navy found Savannah\'s fortifications similar to those that had defied Admiral <a href="/wiki/Sir_Peter_Parker,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Peter Parker, 1st Baronet">Peter Parker</a> at Charleston in 1776. The artillery bombardment had little effect on the defenses, but unlike Charleston\xe2\x80\x94where Clinton decided against attacking <a href="/wiki/Fort_Moultrie" title="Fort Moultrie">Fort Moultrie</a> by land\xe2\x80\x94Estaing decided to press the assault after the naval bombardment had failed.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In this assault, Count <a href="/wiki/Kazimierz_Pu%C5%82aski" class="mw-redirect" title="Kazimierz Pu\xc5\x82aski">Kazimierz Pu\xc5\x82aski</a>, the Polish commander of American <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a>, was fatally wounded.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> With Savannah secured, Clinton could launch a new assault on Charleston, South Carolina, where he had failed in 1776. Lincoln moved his remaining troops to Charleston to assist in the construction of its defenses.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span 1780</div></div></div>\n<p>Clinton moved against Charleston in 1780, blockading the harbor in March and building up about 10,000 troops in the area. His advance on the city was uncontested; the American naval commander, Commodore <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Whipple" title="Abraham Whipple">Abraham Whipple</a>, scuttled five of his eight frigates in the harbor to make a boom for its defense.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Inside the city, General Lincoln commanded about 2,650 Continentals and 2,500 militiamen. When British <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> <a href="/wiki/Banastre_Tarleton" title="Banastre Tarleton">Banastre Tarleton</a> cut off the city\'s supply lines in victories at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monck%27s_Corner" title="Battle of Monck&#39;s Corner">Moncks Corner</a> in April and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lenud%27s_Ferry" title="Battle of Lenud&#39;s Ferry">Lenud\'s Ferry</a> in early May,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Charleston was surrounded.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Clinton began constructing siege lines. On March 11 he commenced the bombardment of the town.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On May 12, 1780, General Lincoln surrendered his 5,000 men\xe2\x80\x94the largest surrender of U.S. troops until the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> With relatively few casualties, Clinton had seized the South\'s biggest city and seaport, winning perhaps the greatest British victory of the war. This victory left the American military structure in the South in ruins. It was only after <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a> slipped past Cornwallis after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guilford_Courthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Guilford Courthouse">Battle of Guilford Courthouse</a> in 1781 that the British finally lost this advantage in the South.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lord_Cornwallis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Lord_Cornwallis.jpg/220px-Lord_Cornwallis.jpg" width="220" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Lord_Cornwallis.jpg/330px-Lord_Cornwallis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Lord_Cornwallis.jpg/440px-Lord_Cornwallis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="657" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lord_Cornwallis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Lord Cornwallis</a> took command when Sir Henry Clinton sailed for New York.</div></div></div>\n<p>The remnants of the southern Continental Army began to withdraw toward North Carolina, but were pursued by Colonel Tarleton, who defeated them at the <a href="/wiki/Waxhaw_Massacre" class="mw-redirect" title="Waxhaw Massacre">Battle of Waxhaws</a> on May 29. Historians have debated accounts spread after the battle that Tarleton\'s forces had massacred many Patriots after they had surrendered. As a consequence, "Bloody Tarleton" or "Bloody Ban" became a hated name, and the phrase, "Tarleton\'s quarter"\xe2\x80\x94referring to his reputed lack of mercy, or "<a href="/wiki/No_quarter" title="No quarter">quarter</a>"\xe2\x80\x94soon became a rallying cry for the Patriots. Whether or not the battle was the massacre that it was claimed, its ramifications were felt throughout the campaign. When a Loyalist militia surrendered at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kings_Mountain" title="Battle of Kings Mountain">Battle of Kings Mountain</a>, many of them were killed when Patriot marksmen continued to fire while shouting "Tarleton\'s Quarters!".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Tarleton later published an account of the war that glossed over accusations of misconduct towards American militia, and portrayed him in an unabashedly positive light.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornwallis_takes_command">Cornwallis takes command</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cornwallis takes command">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After Charleston, organized American military activity in the South virtually collapsed. The states carried on their governmental functions, and the war was carried on by <a href="/wiki/Partisan_(military)" title="Partisan (military)">partisans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Francis_Marion" title="Francis Marion">Francis Marion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Sumter" title="Thomas Sumter">Thomas Sumter</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_R._Davie" class="mw-redirect" title="William R. Davie">William R. Davie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Pickens_(congressman)" title="Andrew Pickens (congressman)">Andrew Pickens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Clarke" title="Elijah Clarke">Elijah Clarke</a>. General Clinton turned over British operations in the South to Lord Cornwallis. The <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> dispatched General <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Gates" title="Horatio Gates">Horatio Gates</a>, the victor of Saratoga, to the South with a new army, but Gates promptly suffered one of the worst defeats in U.S. military history at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Camden" title="Battle of Camden">Battle of Camden</a> (August 16, 1780). Cornwallis prepared to invade North Carolina.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cornwallis\' attempts to raise Loyalists in large numbers in North Carolina were effectively crushed when Patriot militia defeated a larger force of Loyalists in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kings_Mountain" title="Battle of Kings Mountain">Battle of Kings Mountain</a> on October 7, 1780. Many of the Patriot men had crossed the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Washington_District,_North_Carolina" title="Washington District, North Carolina">Washington District of North Carolina</a> to fight the British, and were so named the <a href="/wiki/Overmountain_Men" title="Overmountain Men">Overmountain Men</a>. The British plan to raise large Loyalist armies failed\xe2\x80\x94not enough Loyalists enlisted, and those who did were at high risk once the British army moved on. The defeat at Kings Mountain and the continuing harassment of his communications and supply lines by militia forces in South Carolina forced Cornwallis to withdraw and winter in South Carolina.\n</p><p>Gates was replaced by Washington\'s most dependable subordinate, General <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a>. Greene assigned about 1,000 men to General <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Morgan" title="Daniel Morgan">Daniel Morgan</a>, a superb tactician who crushed Tarleton\'s troops at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cowpens" title="Battle of Cowpens">Battle of Cowpens</a> on January 17, 1781. As after Kings Mountain, Cornwallis was later criticized for detaching part of his army without adequate support.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Greene proceeded to wear down his opponents in a series of skirmishes and military movements referred to as the "Race to the Dan" (so named because the <a href="/wiki/Dan_River" title="Dan River">Dan River</a> flows close to the border between North Carolina and Virginia); each encounter resulted in a tactical victory for the British but gave them no strategic advantage, while attrition took its toll.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cornwallis knew that Greene had divided his forces and wanted to face either Morgan\'s or Greene\'s contingent before they could rejoin. He stripped his army of all excess baggage in an effort to keep up with the fast-moving Patriots. When Greene learned of this decision, his gleeful response was "Then, he is ours!"<sup id="cite_ref-Morrill140_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morrill140-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  Cornwallis\' lack of provisions as a consequence played a role in his later difficulties.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greene_portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Greene_portrait.jpg/170px-Greene_portrait.jpg" width="170" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Greene_portrait.jpg/255px-Greene_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7f/Greene_portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="323" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greene_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of General <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a>; by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Willson_Peale" title="Charles Willson Peale">Charles Willson Peale</a></div></div></div>\n<p>General Greene first engaged Cornwallis at Cowan\'s Ford, where he sent <a href="/wiki/William_Lee_Davidson" title="William Lee Davidson">William Lee Davidson</a> with 900 men. When Davidson was killed in the river, the Americans retreated. Greene was weakened, but he continued his delaying tactics, fighting a dozen more skirmishes in North and South Carolina against Cornwallis\' forces. About 2,000 British troops died in these engagements. Greene summed up his approach in a motto that would become famous: "We fight, get beat, rise, and fight again." His tactics have been likened to the <a href="/wiki/Fabian_strategy" title="Fabian strategy">Fabian strategy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Fabius_Maximus_Verrucosus" title="Quintus Fabius Maximus Verrucosus">Quintus Fabius Maximus Verrucosus</a>, the Roman general who wore down the superior forces of the Carthaginian <a href="/wiki/Hannibal_Barca" class="mw-redirect" title="Hannibal Barca">Hannibal</a> by a slow <a href="/wiki/War_of_Attrition" title="War of Attrition">war of attrition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Greene eventually felt strong enough to face Cornwallis directly\xe2\x80\x94near New Garden, North Carolina (modern day <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro, North Carolina</a>).  Although Cornwallis was the tactical victor in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House" title="Battle of Guilford Court House">Battle of Guilford Court House</a>, the casualties his army suffered forced him to retreat to <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington</a> for resupply and reinforcements.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif/300px-Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif" width="300" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif/450px-Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d2/Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif/600px-Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif 2x" data-file-width="966" data-file-height="737" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Revolutionary_War_-_Major_Operations_in_the_South_1781.Dean.USMA.edu.history.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Major operations in the South during 1781</div></div></div>\n<p>While Cornwallis was unable to completely destroy Greene, he recognized that most of the supplies that the American forces were relying on were coming from Virginia, a state that up to this point in the war had been relatively untouched. Against the wishes of Clinton, Cornwallis resolved to invade Virginia in the hopes that cutting the supply lines to the Carolinas would make American resistance there impossible.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> This theory was supported by <a href="/wiki/Lord_George_Germain" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord George Germain">Lord George Germain</a> in a series of letters that left Clinton out of the decision-making process for the Southern Army, despite his nominally being its overall commander.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Without informing Clinton, Cornwallis marched north from Wilmington into Virginia to engage in raiding operations,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> where he eventually met the army commanded by <a href="/wiki/William_Phillips_(British_Army_officer)" title="William Phillips (British Army officer)">William Phillips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a>, which had engaged in raiding activities there.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When Cornwallis left Greensboro for Wilmington, he left the road open for Greene to begin the American reconquest of South Carolina. This he achieved by the end of June, in spite of a reverse sustained at <a href="/wiki/Francis_Rawdon-Hastings,_1st_Marquess_of_Hastings" title="Francis Rawdon-Hastings, 1st Marquess of Hastings">Lord Rawdon</a>\'s hands at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hobkirk%27s_Hill" title="Battle of Hobkirk&#39;s Hill">Hobkirk\'s Hill</a> (2 miles north of <a href="/wiki/Camden,_South_Carolina" title="Camden, South Carolina">Camden</a>) on April 25. From May 22 to June 19, 1781 Greene led the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Ninety-Six" title="Siege of Ninety-Six">Siege of Ninety-Six</a>, which he was only forced to abandon when word arrived that Rawdon was bringing troops to relieve the siege.  However, the actions of Greene and militia commanders like Francis Marion drove Rawdon to eventually abandon the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ninety_Six_District&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ninety Six District (page does not exist)">Ninety Six District</a> and Camden, effectively reducing the British presence in South Carolina to the port of Charleston. Augusta, Georgia was <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Augusta" title="Siege of Augusta">also besieged</a> on May 22, and fell to Patriot forces under <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Pickens_(congressman)" title="Andrew Pickens (congressman)">Andrew Pickens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lee_III" title="Henry Lee III">Harry "Light Horse" Lee</a> on June 6, reducing the British presence in that state to the port of Savannah.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Greene then gave his forces a six weeks\' rest on the High Hills of the <a href="/wiki/Santee_River" title="Santee River">Santee River</a>. On September 8, with 2,600 men, he engaged British forces under Lieutenant Colonel Alexander Stewart at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Eutaw_Springs" title="Battle of Eutaw Springs">Eutaw Springs</a>. Americans who fell in this battle were immortalized by American author <a href="/wiki/Philip_Freneau" title="Philip Freneau">Philip Freneau</a> in his 1781 poem "To the Memory of Brave Americans." The battle, although tactically a draw, so weakened the British that they withdrew to Charleston, where Greene penned them in for the remaining months of the war.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yorktown">Yorktown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span in Washington.</div></div></div>\n<p>Upon arrival in Virginia, Cornwallis took command of the existing British forces in the region, which had been commanded first by turncoat <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a>, and then by <a href="/wiki/Major_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Major General">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Phillips_(British_Army_officer)" title="William Phillips (British Army officer)">William Phillips</a>. Phillips, a good friend of Cornwallis, died two days before Cornwallis reached his position at <a href="/wiki/Petersburg,_Virginia" title="Petersburg, Virginia">Petersburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Having marched without informing Clinton of his movements (communications between the two British commanders was by sea and extremely slow, sometimes up to three weeks),  Cornwallis sent word of his northward march and set about destroying American supplies in the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 1781, in response to the threat of Arnold and Phillips, General Washington had dispatched the <a href="/wiki/Marquis_de_Lafayette" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquis de Lafayette">Marquis de Lafayette</a> to defend Virginia. The young Frenchman had 3,200 men at his command, but British troops in the state totaled 7,200. Lafayette skirmished with Cornwallis, avoiding a decisive battle while gathering reinforcements. It was during this period that Cornwallis received orders from Clinton to choose a position on the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Peninsula" title="Virginia Peninsula">Virginia Peninsula</a>\xe2\x80\x94referred to in contemporary letters as the "Williamsburg Neck"\xe2\x80\x94and construct a fortified naval post to shelter <a href="/wiki/Ship_of_the_line" title="Ship of the line">ships of the line</a>. In complying with this order, Cornwallis put himself at risk to become trapped. With the arrival of the French fleet under the <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Joseph_Paul_de_Grasse" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois Joseph Paul de Grasse">Comte de Grasse</a> and General Washington\'s combined French-American army, Cornwallis found himself cut off. When the Royal Navy fleet, under Admiral <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Graves,_1st_Baron_Graves" title="Thomas Graves, 1st Baron Graves">Thomas Graves</a>, was defeated by the French at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Chesapeake" title="Battle of the Chesapeake">Battle of the Chesapeake</a>, and a French <a href="/wiki/Siege_train" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege train">siege train</a> arrived from <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, his position became untenable. Cornwallis surrendered to General Washington and the French commander the <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Donatien_de_Vimeur,_comte_de_Rochambeau" title="Jean-Baptiste Donatien de Vimeur, comte de Rochambeau">Comte de Rochambeau</a> on October 19, 1781.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cornwallis reported this disaster to Clinton in a letter that opened:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>I have the mortification to inform Your Excellency that I have been forced to give up the posts of York and Gloucester and to surrender the troops under my command by capitulation, on the 19th instant, as prisoners of war to the combined forces of America.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With the surrender at Yorktown, the full participation of French forces in that battle, and the resulting loss of Cornwallis\' army, the British war effort ground to a halt. The sole remaining British army of any size remaining in America was that under Sir Henry Clinton in New York. Clinton, paralyzed by the defeat, made no further action and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Guy_Carleton,_1st_Baron_Dorchester" title="Guy Carleton, 1st Baron Dorchester">Guy Carleton</a> in 1782.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Such a shocking reversal in fortune, coming as it had on the back of a rare naval defeat, served to increase the shift in British popular opinion against the war. The <a href="/wiki/North_Ministry" class="mw-redirect" title="North Ministry">North Ministry</a> collapsed, a peace-oriented government took power, and no further major operation on the American continent occurred for the rest of the war.
