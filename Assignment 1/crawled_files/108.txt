England</b> are <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local authorities</a> that are responsible for the provision of all <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">local government services</a> within a district. They are constituted under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1992" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Government Act 1992">Local Government Act 1992</a>, which amended the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> to allow the existence of counties that do not have multiple districts. They typically allow large towns to have separate local authorities from the less urbanised parts of their counties and provide a single authority for small counties where division into districts would be impractical. Unitary authorities do not cover all of England. Most were <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1992)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1992)">established during the 1990s</a> and a further tranche were <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">created in 2009</a>. Unitary authorities have the powers and functions that are elsewhere separately administered by councils of <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">non-metropolitan counties</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">non-metropolitan districts</a> within them.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Unitary_authorities_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitary_authorities_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a>" was first used in the <a href="/wiki/Redcliffe-Maud_Report" title="Redcliffe-Maud Report">Redcliffe-Maud Report</a> in 1969 in its current sense of a local government authority which combines the functions of a county council and a district council.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Strictly speaking, the term does not necessarily mean a single level of local government within an area, because in some cases there are also <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish councils</a> in the same area.\n</p><p>Although the term was not applied to them, <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a> between 1889 and 1974 were effectively unitary authorities, that is, single-tier administrative units.  Before 1889, local government authorities had different powers and functions, but from medieval times some cities and towns had a high degree of autonomy as <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">counties corporate</a>.  Some smaller settlements also enjoyed some degree of autonomy from regular administration as <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">boroughs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(division)" title="Liberty (division)">liberties</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> created areas for local government where large towns and their rural hinterlands were administered together. The concept of unitary units was abandoned with a two-tier arrangement of county and district councils in all areas of England, except the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> where the small size and distance from the mainland made it impractical. In 1986 a broadly unitary system of local government was introduced in the six <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan counties</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>, where the upper-tier authorities were abolished and their functions were split between central government, the borough councils and joint boards.<sup id="cite_ref-atkinson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atkinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s_reform">1990s reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitary_authorities_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1990s reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1992)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1992)">Local Government Commission for England (1992)</a></div>\n<p>A review in the 1990s was initiated to select non-metropolitan areas where new unitary authorities could be created.<sup id="cite_ref-politics_uk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-politics_uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The resulting structural changes were implemented between 1995 and 1998. Bristol, Herefordshire, the Isle of Wight and Rutland were established as counties of a single district; the district councils of Berkshire became unitary; the counties of Avon, Humberside and Cleveland were broken up to create several unitary authorities; and a number of districts were split off from their associated counties.<sup id="cite_ref-atkinson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atkinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The changes caused the <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial counties</a> to be defined separately, as they had been before 1974. The review caused 46 unitary authorities to be created.<sup id="cite_ref-atkinson_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atkinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009_changes">2009 changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitary_authorities_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2009 changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">2009 structural changes to local government in England</a></div>\n<p>A further review was initiated in 2007 and was enacted in 2009. The review established Cornwall and Northumberland as counties of a single district; established unitary authorities in County Durham, Shropshire and Wiltshire covering the part of the county that was not already split off in the 1990s review; and divided the remainder of Bedfordshire and Cheshire into two unitary authorities. The review caused nine unitary authorities to be created.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_reform">Further reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitary_authorities_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Further reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2017, it was proposed that two unitary authorities be formed to cover the ceremonial county of <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>. One of the authorities would consist of the existing unitary authorities of <a href="/wiki/Borough_of_Bournemouth" class="mw-redirect" title="Borough of Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borough_of_Poole" class="mw-redirect" title="Borough of Poole">Poole</a> and the non-metropolitan district of <a href="/wiki/Christchurch,_Dorset" title="Christchurch, Dorset">Christchurch</a>, the other would be composed of the remainder of the county.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In November 2017, Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government, <a href="/wiki/Sajid_Javid" title="Sajid Javid">Sajid Javid</a> stated that he was "minded to approve the proposals" and a final decision to implement the two unitary authority model was confirmed in February 2018. Statutory instruments for the creation of two unitary authorities, to be named <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth,_Christchurch_and_Poole_Council" title="Bournemouth, Christchurch and Poole Council">Bournemouth, Christchurch and Poole Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorset_Council_(UK)" title="Dorset Council (UK)">Dorset Council</a>, have been made and shadow authorities for the new council areas have been formed.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two competing plans were drawn up for <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>. One plan would see the abolition of the four district councils resulting in the existing county council becoming a unitary authority. The other plan would see the formation of two unitary authorities, one authority would be formed through the merger of the three existing districts of <a href="/wiki/Chiltern_District" title="Chiltern District">Chiltern</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Bucks" title="South Bucks">South Bucks</a> <a href="/wiki/Wycombe_District" title="Wycombe District">Wycombe</a> with the other formed by the existing <a href="/wiki/Aylesbury_Vale" title="Aylesbury Vale">Aylesbury Vale</a> district becoming a unitary authority.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In March 2018, Communities Secretary Sajid Javid indicated that he was minded to consider a single unitary authority option in preference to the two unitary authority model.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2018, an independent report commissioned by the Secretary of State for Housing, Communities and Local Government, proposed structural changes to local government in <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>. These changes would see the existing county council and district councils abolished and two new unitary authorities created in their place.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> One authority would consist of the existing districts of <a href="/wiki/Daventry_District" title="Daventry District">Daventry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northampton" title="Northampton">Northampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Northamptonshire" title="South Northamptonshire">South Northamptonshire</a> and the other authority would consist of <a href="/wiki/Corby_(borough)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corby (borough)">Corby</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Northamptonshire" title="East Northamptonshire">East Northamptonshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borough_of_Kettering" title="Borough of Kettering">Kettering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borough_of_Wellingborough" title="Borough of Wellingborough">Wellingborough</a> districts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016, Oxfordshire County Council put forward a \'One Oxfordshire\' proposal which would see Oxford City Council and the four other district councils in Oxfordshire abolished and replaced with a single unitary county council for Oxfordshire. In 2017, Oxford City Council voiced their opposition to the proposal. A decision on whether the proposal will go ahead was to have been announced in March 2017.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitary_authorities_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Unitary authorities combine the powers and functions that are normally delivered separately by the councils of non-metropolitan counties and non-metropolitan districts. These functions are housing, waste management, waste collection, council tax collection, education, libraries, social services, transport, planning, consumer protection, licensing, cemeteries and crematoria. of the Isles of Scilly">Council of the Isles of Scilly</a> is a <i><a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a></i> single-tier authority, created in 1890 and since 1930 has held the "powers, duties and liabilities" of a county council.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It thus is not a Unitary Authority as those are such authorities created under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1992" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Government Act 1992">Local Government Act 1992</a>. The 36 <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a> councils are also the sole elected local government units in their areas (except for parish councils in a few locations), but share strategic functions with <a href="/wiki/Joint-board" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint-board">joint boards</a> and arrangements. On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a> and the 32 <a href="/wiki/London_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="London borough">London borough</a> councils, although they have a high degree of autonomy, share strategic functions with the directly elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of
