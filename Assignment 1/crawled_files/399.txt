src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c6/Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg/220px-Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1783 map of Cornwall</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>hundreds of Cornwall</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Keverangow Kernow</i>) were administrative divisions (<a href="/wiki/Hundred_(administrative_division)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred (administrative division)">hundreds</a>) into which <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, the present day <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">administrative county</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, was divided between <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;925</span> and 1894, when they were replaced with local government <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">districts</a>\n</p><p>Some of the names of the hundreds ended with the suffix <i>shire</i> as in Pydarshire, East and West Wivelshire and Powdershire which were first recorded as names between 1184 and 1187.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> the word <i>keverang</i> (<i>pl. keverangow</i>) is the equivalent for English "hundred" and the Welsh <a href="/wiki/Cantref" title="Cantref">cantref</a>. The word, in its plural form, appears in place names like Meankeverango (i.e. stone of the hundreds) in 1580 (now The Enys, north of <a href="/wiki/Prussia_Cove" title="Prussia Cove">Prussia Cove</a> and marking the southern end of the boundary between the hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerrier" title="Kerrier">Kerrier</a>), and Assa Govranckowe 1580, Kyver Ankou <i>c.</i> 1720, also on the Penwith \xe2\x80\x93 Kerrier border near <a href="/wiki/Scorrier" title="Scorrier">Scorrier</a>. It is also found in the singular form at Buscaverran, just south of <a href="/wiki/Crowan" title="Crowan">Crowan</a> churchtown and also on the Penwith-Kerrier border. The hundred of <i>Trigg</i> is mentioned by name during the 7th century, as <i>"Pagus Tricurius"</i>, <i>"land of three war hosts"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear: right; margin-bottom: .5em; float: right; padding: .5em 0 .8em 1.4em; background: none; width: auto;"><div id="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundreds_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The division of <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a> into hundreds is thought to date from the reign of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelstan" title="\xc3\x86thelstan">King Athelstan</a>, and in the Geld Inquest of 1083, only seven hundreds are found in Cornwall, identified by the names of the chief manors of each: <a href="/wiki/Gwithian" title="Gwithian">Connerton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winnianton" class="mw-redirect" title="Winnianton">Winnianton</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Breock#Manor_of_Pawton" title="St Breock">Pawton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tybesta" title="Tybesta">Tybesta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratton,_Cornwall#Government" title="Stratton, Cornwall">Stratton</a>, Fawton and Rillaton (corresponding to Penwith, Kerrier, Pydar, Powder, Trigg, West Wivel and East Wivel). At the time of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Survey" class="mw-redirect" title="Domesday Survey">Domesday Survey</a> of 1086, the internal order of the Cornish <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manors</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Liber_Exoniensis" title="Liber Exoniensis">Exeter Domesday Book</a> is in most cases based on the hundreds to which they belonged, although the hundred names are not used.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>All of the lordships of the Hundreds of Cornwall belonged, and still belong, to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a>, apart from Penwith which belonged to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arundell_family_of_Lanherne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arundell family of Lanherne (page does not exist)">Arundells of Lanherne</a>. The Arundells sold their lordship to the Hawkins family in 1813 and the Hawkinses went on to sell it to the Paynters in 1832. The Lordship of Penwith came with a great number of rights over the entire hundred. These included: rights to try certain cases of <a href="/wiki/Trespass" title="Trespass">trespass</a>, trespass on the law, <a href="/wiki/Detinue" title="Detinue">debt and detinue</a>, to appoint a jailor for the detention of persons apprehended, to receive high-rent from the lords of the principal <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manors</a> and to claim the <a href="/wiki/Regalia" title="Regalia">regalia</a> of the navigable rivers and havens, the profits of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mines_Act_1424" title="Royal Mines Act 1424">royal gold and silver mines</a>, and all <a href="/wiki/Receiver_of_Wreck" title="Receiver of Wreck">wrecks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Escheat" title="Escheat">escheats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deodand" title="Deodand">deodands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Treasure_trove" title="Treasure trove">treasure trove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waif" title="Waif">waifs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estray" title="Estray">estrays</a>, goods of <a href="/wiki/Felony#Convicted_Felon" title="Felony">felons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Droit" title="Droit">droits of admiralty</a> happening within the hundred.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_hundred_names">Origin of the hundred names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundreds_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin of the hundred names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The origins of the names have puzzled some earlier writers on the subject: Penwith is certainly the name of Land\'s End in Cornish (earliest occurrence in the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> for 997); Kerrier (sometimes Kirrier) is thought by Thomas to be derived from an obsolete name (ker hyr = long fort) of Castle Pencaire on <a href="/wiki/Tregonning_Hill" title="Tregonning Hill">Tregonning Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breage,_Cornwall" title="Breage, Cornwall">Breage</a>; Lesnewth denotes a place where a \'new court\' has been established (the \'old court\' having been at Henlis(-ton): <a href="/wiki/Helstone" title="Helstone">Helstone</a>, formerly Helston-in-Trigg); Powder has no certain derivation: \'pou\' must mean \'territory\' in Cornish; Pydar (or Pyder) has been variously explained: perhaps it derives from a Cornish word meaning \'a fourth part\'; <a href="/wiki/Stratton,_Cornwall" title="Stratton, Cornwall">Stratton</a> was at the time of Domesday an important manor and 200 years earlier it is mentioned as \'Str\xc3\xa6tneat\' (<i>etym. dub.</i>); <a href="/wiki/Triggshire" title="Triggshire">Trigg</a> is explained in the separate article; East and West (<a href="/wiki/Wivelshire" title="Wivelshire">Wivelshire</a>) must have originally had a Cornish name but it is not recorded (Wivel may be from an Anglo-Saxon personal name \'Wifel\').<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_hundreds_in_1841">List of hundreds in 1841</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
