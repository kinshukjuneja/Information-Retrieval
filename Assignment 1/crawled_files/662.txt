the Rhine</a>, often considered to be an archetypal Cavalier.</div></div></div>\n<p>The term <b>Cavalier</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x8c/: secondary stress follows">\xcb\x8c</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/\xc3\xa6/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">\xc3\xa6</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/\xc9\x99/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">\xc9\x99</span><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/\xc9\xaa\xc9\x99r/: &#39;ear&#39; in &#39;near&#39;">\xc9\xaa\xc9\x99r</span></span>/</a></span></span>) was first used by <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Roundheads</a> as a term of abuse for the wealthier Royalist supporters of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a> during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Interregnum_(England)" title="Interregnum (England)">Interregnum</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration</a> (1642 \xe2\x80\x93 c. 1679).  It was later adopted by the Royalists themselves. Although it referred originally  to political and social attitudes and behaviour, of which clothing was a very small part, it has subsequently become strongly identified with the fashionable clothing of the court at the time.  <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert_of_the_Rhine" title="Prince Rupert of the Rhine">Prince Rupert</a>, commander of much of Charles I\'s cavalry, is often considered to be an archetypal Cavalier.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManganiello2004476_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManganiello2004476-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input reading</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cavalier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cavalier derives from the same Latin root as the French word <i>chevalier</i> (as well as the Spanish word <i>caballero</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caballarius" class="extiw" title="wikt:caballarius">caballarius</a></i>, meaning "horseman". Shakespeare used the word <i>cavaleros</i> to describe an overbearing swashbuckler or swaggering gallant in <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Part_2" title="Henry IV, Part 2">Henry IV, Part 2</a>, in which Shallow says "I\'ll drink to Master Bardolph, and to all the cavaleros about London".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOED1989&quot;Cavalier&quot;_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOED1989&quot;Cavalier&quot;-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Civil_War">English Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cavalier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: English Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:King_Charles_I_and_his_adherents.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/King_Charles_I_and_his_adherents.jpg/170px-King_Charles_I_and_his_adherents.jpg" width="170" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/King_Charles_I_and_his_adherents.jpg/255px-King_Charles_I_and_his_adherents.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e2/King_Charles_I_and_his_adherents.jpg 2x" data-file-width="318" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King_Charles_I_and_his_adherents.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An engraving depicting <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> and his adherents.</div></div></div>\n<p>"Cavalier" is chiefly associated with the Royalist supporters of King Charles I in his struggle with Parliament in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. It first appears as a term of reproach and contempt, applied to the followers of King Charles I in June 1642:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>1642 (June 10) <i>Propositions of Parlt</i>. in Clarendon v. (1702) I. 504 Several sorts of malignant Men, who were about the King; some whereof, under the name of Cavaliers, without having respect to the Laws of the Land, or any fear either of God or Man, were ready to commit all manner of Outrage and Violence. 1642 <i>Petition Lords &amp; Com.</i> 17 June in Rushw. Coll. III. (1721) I. 631 That your Majesty..would please to dismiss your extraordinary Guards, and the Cavaliers and others of that Quality, who seem to have little Interest or Affection to the publick Good, their Language and Behaviour speaking nothing but Division and War.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOED1989&quot;Cavalier&quot;_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOED1989&quot;Cavalier&quot;-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Charles, in the Answer to the Petition 13 June 1642 speaks of Cavaliers as a "word by what mistake soever it seemes much in disfavour".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911562_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911562-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It was soon <a href="/wiki/Reappropriation" title="Reappropriation">reappropriated</a> (as a title of honour) by the king\'s party, who in return applied <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Roundhead</a> to their opponents, and at the Restoration the court party preserved the name, which survived until the rise of the term <a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911562_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911562-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_perceptions">Social perceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cavalier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social perceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cavalier was not understood at the time as primarily a term describing a style of dress, but a whole political and social attitude. However, in modern times the word has become more particularly associated with the <a href="/wiki/1600%E2%80%9350_in_Western_European_fashion" class="mw-redirect" title="1600\xe2\x80\x9350 in Western European fashion">court fashions of the period</a>, which included <a href="/wiki/Long_hair" title="Long hair">long flowing hair</a> in ringlets, brightly coloured clothing with elaborate trimmings and lace collars and cuffs, and plumed <a href="/wiki/Cavalier_hat" title="Cavalier hat">hats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This contrasted with the dress of at least the most extreme Roundhead supporters of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a>, with their preference for shorter hair and plainer dress, although neither side conformed to the <a href="/wiki/Stereotypical" class="mw-redirect" title="Stereotypical">stereotypical</a> images entirely.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir-Anthony-van-Dyck-Lord-John-Stuart-and-His-Brother-Lord-Bernard-Stuart.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" Sir <a href="/wiki/Anthony_van_Dyck" title="Anthony van Dyck">Anthony van Dyck</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Most Parliamentarian generals wore their hair at much the same length as their Royalist counterparts,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="No proof for this assertion and many of the puritan major generals came from other social backgrounds (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> though <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Cromwell</a> was something of an exception. The best patrons in the nobility of Charles I\'s court painter Sir <a href="/wiki/Anthony_van_Dyck" title="Anthony van Dyck">Anthony van Dyck</a>, the archetypal recorder of the Cavalier image, all took the Parliamentary side in the Civil War. Probably the most famous image identified as of a "cavalier", <a href="/wiki/Frans_Hals" title="Frans Hals">Frans Hals</a>\' <i><a href="/wiki/Laughing_Cavalier" title="Laughing Cavalier">Laughing Cavalier</a></i>, shows a gentleman from the strongly <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinist</a> Dutch town of <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a>, and is dated 1624. These derogatory terms (for at the time they were so intended) also showed what the typical Parliamentarian thought of the Royalist side \xe2\x80\x93 capricious men who cared more for vanity than the nation at large.\n</p><p>The chaplain to King Charles I, Edward Simmons described a Cavalier as "a Child of Honour, a Gentleman well borne and bred, that loves his king for conscience sake, of a clearer countenance, and bolder look than other men, because of a more loyal Heart".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarlton200252_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarlton200252-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There were many men in the Royalist armies who fit this description since most of the Royalist field officers were typically in their early thirties, married with rural estates which had to be managed.\n</p><p>Although they did not share the same outlook on how to worship God as the English <a href="/wiki/Independent_(religion)" title="Independent (religion)">Independents</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a>, God was often central to their lives.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolrych2002249_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolrych2002249-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This type of Cavalier was personified by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Astley,_1st_Baron_Astley_of_Reading" title="Jacob Astley, 1st Baron Astley of Reading">Jacob Astley, 1st Baron Astley of Reading</a>, whose prayer at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Edgehill" title="Battle of Edgehill">Battle of Edgehill</a> has become famous "O Lord, Thou knowest how busy I must be this day. If I forget Thee, do not forget me".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHume1841&#91;httpsbooksgooglecombooksidrBcUAAAAYAAJpgPA216_216&#93;_See_footnote_r._cites_Warwick_229_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHume1841[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidrBcUAAAAYAAJpgPA216_216]_See_footnote_r._cites_Warwick_229-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At the end of the <a href="/wiki/First_English_Civil_War" title="First English Civil War">First Civil War</a> Astley gave his word that he would not take up arms again against Parliament and having given his word he felt duty bound to refuse to help the Royalist cause in the <a href="/wiki/Second_English_Civil_War" title="Second English Civil War">Second Civil War</a>.\n</p><p>However, the word was coined by the Roundheads as a pejorative propaganda image of a licentious, hard drinking and frivolous man, who rarely, if ever, thought of God. It is this image which has survived and many Royalists, for example <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wilmot,_1st_Earl_of_Rochester" title="Henry Wilmot, 1st Earl of Rochester">Henry Wilmot, 1st Earl of Rochester</a>, fitted this description to a tee.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarratt2005177_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarratt2005177-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Of another Cavalier, <a href="/wiki/George_Goring,_Lord_Goring" title="George Goring, Lord Goring">George Goring, Lord Goring</a>, a general in the Royalist army,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMemegalos2007_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMemegalos2007-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the principal advisor to <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hyde,_1st_Earl_of_Clarendon" title="Edward Hyde, 1st Earl of Clarendon">Edward Hyde, 1st Earl of Clarendon</a>, said:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[He] would, without hesitation, have broken any trust, or done any act of treachery to have satisfied an ordinary passion or appetite; and in truth wanted nothing but industry (for he had wit, and courage, and understanding and ambition, uncontrolled by any fear of God or man) to have been as eminent and successful in the highest attempt of wickedness as any man in the age he lived in or before. Of all his qualifications dissimulation was his masterpiece; in which he so much excelled, that men were not ordinarily ashamed, or out of countenance, with being deceived but twice by him".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClarendon18393_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClarendon18393-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911a259_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911a259-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>This sense has developed into the modern English use of "cavalier" to describe a recklessly nonchalant attitude, although still with a suggestion of stylishness.\n</p><p>Cavalier remained in use as a description for members of the party that supported the monarchy up until the <a href="/wiki/Exclusion_Crisis" title="Exclusion Crisis">Exclusion Crisis</a> of 1678\xe2\x80\x931681 when the term was superseded by "<a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory</a>" which was another term initially with pejorative connotations. Likewise during Exclusion Bill crisis the term Roundhead was replaced with "<a href="/wiki/Whigs_(British_political_party)" title="Whigs (British political party)">Whig</a>", a term introduced by the opponents of the Whigs and also was initially a pejorative term.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorden20094_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorden20094-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cavaliers_in_the_arts">Cavaliers in the arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
