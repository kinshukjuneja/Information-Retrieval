<b>prehistory of Ireland</b> has been pieced together from <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_history_of_the_British_Isles" title="Genetic class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and finishes with the start of the <a href="/wiki/Historical_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Historical record">historical record</a> around 400 <a href="/wiki/AD" class="mw-redirect" title="AD">AD</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric</a> period covers the <a href="/wiki/Palaeolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeolithic">Palaeolithic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> societies of Ireland. For much of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the historical record begins when the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> invaded; as Ireland was not invaded by the Romans its historical record starts later, with the coming of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Palaeolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_settlement_of_the_British_Isles" title="Prehistoric settlement of the British Isles">Prehistoric settlement of the British Isles</a></div>\n<p>During the most recent <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> more than 3,000&#160;m (9,800&#160;ft) thick scoured the landscape of Ireland, pulverising rock and bone, and eradicating any possible evidence of early human settlements during the Glenavian warm period<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>(human remains pre-dating the last <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a> have been uncovered in the extreme south of <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">Britain</a>, which largely escaped the advancing ice sheets).\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> (ca. 26,000\xe2\x80\x9319,000 years ago),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Ireland was an Arctic wasteland, or <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>. The Midland General Glaciation (<a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period#Devensian_&amp;_Midlandian_glaciation,_in_Britain_and_Ireland" title="Last glacial period">"Midlandian period"</a>) was originally thought to have covered two thirds of the island with ice.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Subsequent evidence from the past 50 years has shown this to be untrue and recent publications (Greenwood and Clark, 2009) suggest that ice went off the southern coast of Ireland. The early part of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> had a climate that was inhospitable to most European animals and plants. Human occupation was unlikely, although fishing was possible.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif/220px-Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif" width="220" height="274" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif/330px-Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif/440px-Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif 2x" data-file-width="466" data-file-height="581" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Www.wesleyjohnston.com-users-ireland-maps-historical-ice_age.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Ireland during the last ice age.</div></div></div>\n<p>During the period between 17,500 and 12,000 years ago, a warmer period, the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B8lling-Aller%C3%B8d" class="mw-redirect" title="B\xc3\xb8lling-Aller\xc3\xb8d">B\xc3\xb8lling-Aller\xc3\xb8d</a>, allowed the rehabitation of northern areas of Europe by roaming <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a>. Genetic evidence suggests this reoccupation began in southwestern Europe and faunal remains suggest a <a href="/wiki/Refugium_(population_biology)" title="Refugium (population biology)">refugium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a> that extended up into <a href="/wiki/Southern_France" title="Southern France">southern France</a>. The original attraction to the north during the pre-<a href="/wiki/Boreal_(period)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boreal (period)">boreal period</a> would be species like <a href="/wiki/Reindeer" title="Reindeer">reindeer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aurochs" title="Aurochs">aurochs</a>. Some sites as far north as <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> inhabited earlier than 10,000 years ago suggest that humans might have used <a href="/wiki/Glacier_terminus" title="Glacier terminus">glacial termini</a> as places from which they hunted migratory game.\n</p><p>These factors and ecological changes brought humans to the edge of the northernmost ice-free zones of Europe by the onset of the Holocene and this included regions close to Ireland.\n</p><p>Britain and Ireland were joined by a land bridge, but because this link was cut so early into the warm period few temperate terrestrial flora or fauna crossed into Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-drowning_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drowning-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The bridge was possibly gone by 14,000 BC, which is before the most recent <a href="/wiki/Stadial" title="Stadial">stadial</a> (cold period), the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-drowning_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drowning-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The lowered sea level also joined Britain to continental Europe; this persisted much longer, probably until around 5600 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Cunliffe56_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunliffe56-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The date for the earliest known modern humans in Ireland was pushed back some 2,500 years, and into the late <a href="/wiki/Palaeolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeolithic">Palaeolithic</a>, by a <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> performed in 2016 on a bear bone excavated in 1903 in the "Alice and Gwendoline Cave", <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a>.  This has cut marks showing it was butchered when fresh and gave a date of around 10,500 BC, showing humans were in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A British site on the eastern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>, dated to 11,000 BC, indicated people were in the area eating a marine diet including <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>. These modern humans may have also colonised Ireland after crossing the southern, now ice-free, land bridge that linked south-east Ireland and Cornwall. This land bridge existed until about 14,000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These people may have found few resources outside of coastal shellfishing and <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorns</a> and so may not have continually occupied the region.\n</p><p>As the northern glaciers retreated, sea levels rose with water draining into an inland sea where the Irish Sea currently stands; the outflow of freshwater and eventual rise in sea level between the Irish and <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Sea" title="Celtic Sea">Celtic Seas</a> inhibited the entry of flora and fauna from Europe via Britain. To this day snakes (and most other reptiles) have not made their way back to Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The return of freezing conditions in the Younger Dryas, that lasted from 10,900 BC to 9700 BC, may have depopulated Ireland. During the Younger Dryas, sea-levels continued to rise and no ice-free land bridge between Great Britain and Ireland ever returned.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Mesolithic_.288000.E2.80.934000_BC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesolithic_(8000\xe2\x80\x934000_BC)">Mesolithic \xe2\x80\x93 Irish National Heritage Park.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a> came to an end in Ireland about 10\xe2\x80\x9312 thousand years ago. Until the single 2016 Palaeolithic dating described above, the earliest evidence of human occupation after the retreat of the ice was dated to the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a>, around <a href="/wiki/8th_millennium_BC" title="8th millennium BC">8000 BC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lithicsireland.ie_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lithicsireland.ie-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Evidence for Mesolithic <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherers" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter-gatherers">hunter-gatherers</a> has been found throughout the island: a number of the key early Mesolithic excavations are the settlement site at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sandel_Mesolithic_site" title="Mount Sandel Mesolithic site">Mount Sandel</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a> (Coleraine); the cremations at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hermitage,_County_Limerick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hermitage, County Limerick (page does not exist)">Hermitage, County Limerick</a> on the bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">River Shannon</a>; and the campsite at <a href="/wiki/Lough_Boora" class="mw-redirect" title="Lough Boora">Lough Boora</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Offaly" title="County Offaly">County Offaly</a>. As well as these, early Mesolithic <a href="/wiki/Lithic_core" title="Lithic core">lithic</a> scatters have been noted around the island, from the north in <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a> to the south in <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lithicsireland.ie_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lithicsireland.ie-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Although sea levels were still lower than they are today, Ireland was already  an island by the time the first settlers arrived. This was by boat from continental Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Most of the Mesolithic sites in Ireland are coastal settlements. The earliest inhabitants of the island were seafarers who depended for much of their livelihood upon the sea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A DNA study of the first humans into Ireland concluded that Ireland was first settled around 9,000 years ago by people who travelled by land and sea up the coast from northern Spain and southern France.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (May 2017)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This has since been challenged. (See <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_of_the_Irish" title="Blood of the Irish">Blood of the Irish</a></i>).\n</p><p>The hunter-gatherers of the Mesolithic era lived on a varied diet of seafood, birds, <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnuts</a>. There is no evidence for <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> in the Irish Mesolithic and it is likely that the first <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> were introduced in the early stages of the Neolithic.<sup id="cite_ref-urlKerry_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlKerry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The human population hunted with <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arrow" title="Arrow">arrows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harpoon" title="Harpoon">harpoons</a> tipped with small stone blades called <a href="/wiki/Microliths" class="mw-redirect" title="Microliths">microliths</a>, while supplementing their diet with gathered <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, fruit and <a href="/wiki/Berries" class="mw-redirect" title="Berries">berries</a>. They lived in seasonal shelters, which they constructed by stretching <a href="/wiki/Animal_skin" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal skin">animal skins</a> over wooden frames. They had outdoor <a href="/wiki/Hearth" title="Hearth">hearths</a> for cooking their food. During the Mesolithic the population of Ireland was probably never more than a few thousand.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Neolithic_.284000.E2.80.932500_BC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" \xe2\x80\x93 Irish National Heritage Park.</div></div></div>\n<p>Many areas of Europe entered the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> with a \'package\' of cereal cultivars, pastoral animals (domesticated oxen/cattle, sheep, goats), pottery, weaving, housing and burial cultures, which arrive simultaneously, a process that begins in central Europe as LBK (<a href="/wiki/Linear_Pottery_culture" title="Linear Pottery culture">Linear Pottery culture</a>) about 6000 BC.  Within several hundred years this culture is observed in northern France. An alternative Neolithic culture, <a href="/wiki/La_Hoguette" title="La Hoguette">La Hoguette</a> culture, that arrived in France\'s north-western region appears to be a derivative of the Ibero Italian-Eastern Adriatic Impressed Cardial Ware culture (<a href="/wiki/Cardium_Pottery" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardium Pottery">Cardium Pottery</a>). The La Hoguette culture, like the western Cardial culture, raised sheep and goats more intensely. By 5100 BC there is evidence of dairy practices in south England and modern English cattle appear to be derived from "T1 Taurids" that were domesticated in the Aegean region shortly after the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>. These animals were probably derived from the LBK cattle. Around 4300 BC cattle arrived in northern Ireland during the late Mesolithic period. The red deer was introduced from Britain about this time.<sup id="cite_ref-urlKerry_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlKerry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From around 4500 BC a Neolithic package that included cereal cultivars, housing culture (similar to those of the same period in Scotland) and stone monuments arrived in Ireland. Sheep, goats, cattle and cereals were imported from southwest continental Europe, and the population then rose significantly. The earliest clear proof of farmers in Ireland or Great Britain is from Ferriter\'s Cove on the Dingle Peninsula, where a flint knife, cattle bones and a sheep\'s tooth were found and dated to c.4350 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9ide_Fields" title="C\xc3\xa9ide Fields">C\xc3\xa9ide Fields</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Mayo" title="County Mayo">County Mayo</a>, an extensive Neolithic field system (arguably the oldest known in the world) has been preserved beneath a blanket of <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>. Consisting of small fields separated from one another by dry-stone walls, the C\xc3\xa9ide Fields were farmed for several centuries between 3500 and 3000 BC. Wheat and <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> were the principal crops cultivated. Pottery made its appearance around the same time as agriculture. Ware, similar to that found in northern Great Britain, has been excavated in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> (Lyle\'s Hill pottery) and in <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>. Typical of this ware are wide-mouthed, round-bottomed bowls.\n</p><p>This follows a pattern similar to western Europe or gradual onset of Neolithic, such as seen in La Hoguette Culture of France and Iberia\'s Impressed Cardial Ware Culture. Cereal culture advance markedly slows north of France; certain cereal strains such as wheat were difficult to grow in cold climates\xe2\x80\x94however, barley and <a href="/w/index.php?title=German_rye&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="German rye (page does not exist)">German rye</a> were suitable replacements. It can be speculated that the <a href="/wiki/HLA-DQ2#DQ2.5" title="HLA-DQ2">DQ2.5</a> aspect of the <a href="/wiki/AH8.1" class="mw-redirect" title="AH8.1">AH8.1</a> haplotype may have been involved in the slowing of cereal culture into Ireland, Scotland and Scandinavia since this haplotype confers susceptibility to a <a href="/wiki/Triticeae" title="Triticeae">Triticeae</a> protein induced disease as well as Type I diabetes and other autoimmune diseases that may have arisen as an indirect result of Neolithisation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional" style="max-width: 326px;_width: 326px;float:right;">\n\t<li class="gallerycaption">Different class="gallerytext">\n<p><a href="/wiki/Glantane_East" title="Glantane East">Glantane East</a> <b>wedge tomb</b>.\n</p>\n\t\t\t</div>\n\t\t</div></li>\n</ul>\n<p>The most striking characteristic of the Neolithic in Ireland was the sudden appearance and dramatic proliferation of <a href="/wiki/List_of_megalithic_monuments_in_Ireland" title="List of megalithic monuments in Ireland">megalithic monuments</a>. The largest of these tombs were clearly places of religious and ceremonial importance to the Neolithic population. In most of the tombs that have been excavated, human remains\xe2\x80\x94usually, but not always, cremated\xe2\x80\x94have been found. Grave goods\xe2\x80\x94pottery, arrowheads, beads, pendants, axes, etc.\xe2\x80\x94have also been uncovered. These <a href="/wiki/Megalithic_tomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Megalithic tomb">megalithic tombs</a>, more than 1,200 of which are now known, can be divided for the most part into four broad groups:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Court_tomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Court tomb">Court cairns</a> \xe2\x80\x93 These are characterised by the presence of an entrance courtyard. They are found almost exclusively in the north of the island and are thought to include the oldest specimens.  North Mayo has many examples of this type of megalith \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Faulagh" title="Faulagh">Faulagh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kilcommon" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilcommon">Kilcommon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erris" title="Erris">Erris</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Passage_tomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Passage tomb">Passage tombs</a> \xe2\x80\x93 These constitute the smallest group in terms of numbers, but they are the most impressive in terms of size and importance. They are distributed mainly throughout the north and east of the island, the biggest and most impressive of them being found in the four great Neolithic "cemeteries" of the Boyne, Loughcrew (both in <a href="/wiki/County_Meath" title="County Meath">County Meath</a>), Carrowkeel and Carrowmore (both in <a href="/wiki/County_Sligo" title="County Sligo">County Sligo</a>). The most famous of them is <a href="/wiki/Newgrange" title="Newgrange">Newgrange</a>, a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> and one of the oldest astronomically aligned monuments in the world. It was built around 3200 BC. At the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a> the first rays of the rising sun still shine through a light-box above the entrance to the tomb and illuminate the burial chamber at the centre of the monument. Another of the Boyne megaliths, Knowth, contains the world\'s earliest map of the Moon carved into stone.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Portal_dolmen" class="mw-redirect" title="Portal dolmen">Portal tombs</a> \xe2\x80\x93 These tombs include the well known <a href="/wiki/Dolmen" title="Dolmen">dolmens</a>. They consist of three or more upright stones supporting a large flat horizontal capstone (table). Most of them are to be found in two main concentrations, one in the southeast of the island and one in the north. The Knockeen and Gaulstown Dolmens in County Waterford are exceptional examples.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Wedge-shaped_gallery_grave" class="mw-redirect" title="Wedge-shaped gallery grave">Wedge tombs</a> \xe2\x80\x93 The largest and most widespread of the four groups, the wedge tombs are particularly common in the west and southwest. <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a> is exceptionally rich in them. They are the latest of the four types and belong to the end of the Neolithic. They are so called from their wedge-shaped burial chambers.</li></ul>\n<p>The theory that these four groups of monuments were associated with four separate waves of invading colonists still has its adherents today, but the growth in population that made them possible need not have been the result of colonisation: it may simply have been the natural consequence of the introduction of agriculture.\n</p><p>Some regions of Ireland showed patterns of pastoralism that indicated that some Neolithic peoples continued to move and indicates that pastoral activities dominated agrarian activities in many regions or that there was a division of labour between pastoral and agrarian aspects of the Neolithic.\n</p><p>At the height of the Neolithic the population of the island was probably in excess of 100,000, and perhaps as high as 200,000. But there appears to have been an economic collapse around 2500 BC, and the population declined for a while.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Copper_and_Bronze_Ages_.282500.E2.80.93500_BC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Copper_and_Bronze_Ages_(2500\xe2\x80\x93500_BC)">Copper and Bronze Ages (2500\xe2\x80\x93500 BC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Copper and Bronze Ages (2500\xe2\x80\x93500 BC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Metallurgy arrived in Ireland with new people, generally known as the <a href="/wiki/Beaker_culture" title="Beaker culture">Bell Beaker</a> People, from their characteristic pottery, in the shape of an inverted bell.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This was quite different from the finely made, round-bottomed pottery of the Neolithic. It is found, for example, at <a href="/wiki/Ross_Island,_Killarney" title="Ross Island, Killarney">Ross Island</a>, and associated with copper mining there. There is some disagreement about when the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> (and thus <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_migrations" title="Indo-European migrations">Indo-Europeans</a>) first arrived in Ireland. It is thought by some scholars to be associated with the Beaker People of the Bronze Age, but the more mainstream view is that the Celts arrived much later at the beginning of the Iron Age.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> began once copper was <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloyed</a> with <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> to produce true bronze artefacts, and this took place around 2000 BC, when some <a href="/wiki/Ballybeg" title="Ballybeg">Ballybeg</a> flat axes and associated metalwork were produced. The period preceding this, in which Lough Ravel and most Ballybeg axes were produced, and which is known as the <a href="/wiki/Copper_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Copper Age">Copper Age</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a>, commenced about 2500 BC.\n</p><p>Bronze was used for the manufacture of both weapons and tools. Swords, axes, daggers, hatchets, halberds, awls, drinking utensils and horn-shaped trumpets are just some of the items that have been unearthed at <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> sites. Irish craftsmen became particularly noted for the horn-shaped trumpet, which was made by the <i>cire perdue</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Lost_wax" class="mw-redirect" title="Lost wax">lost wax</a>, process.\n</p><p>Copper used in the manufacture of bronze was mined in Ireland, chiefly in the southwest of the island, while the tin was imported from <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in Britain. The earliest known copper mine in these islands was located at <i>Ross Island</i>, at the <a href="/wiki/Lakes_of_Killarney" title="Lakes of Killarney">Lakes of Killarney</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a>; mining and metalworking took place there between 2400 and 1800 BC. Another of Europe\'s best-preserved copper mines has been discovered at Mount Gabriel in <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a>, which was worked for several centuries in the middle of the second millennium.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Mines in Cork and Kerry are believed to have produced as much as 370 tonnes of copper during the Bronze Age. As only about 0.2% of this can be accounted for in excavated bronze artefacts, it is surmised that Ireland was a major exporter of copper during this period.\n</p><p>Ireland was also rich in native gold, and the Bronze Age saw the first <a href="/wiki/Gold_working_in_the_Bronze_Age_British_Isles" title="Gold working in the Bronze Age British Isles">extensive working of this precious metal</a> by Irish craftsmen. More Bronze Age gold hoards have been discovered in Ireland than anywhere else in Europe. Irish gold ornaments have been found as far afield as Germany and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. In the early stages of the Bronze Age these ornaments consisted of simple but finely decorated <a href="/wiki/Gold_lunula" title="Gold lunula">gold lunulae</a>, a distinctively Irish type of object later made in Britain and the continent, and disks of thin gold sheet. Later the familiar thin twisted <a href="/wiki/Torc" title="Torc">torc</a> made its appearance; this was a collar consisting of a bar or ribbon of metal, twisted into a spiral. Gold earrings, sun disks, bracelets, clothes fasteners, large "gorgets" and in the Late Bronze Age, the distinctively Irish <a href="/wiki/Bulla_(amulet)" title="Bulla (amulet)"><i>bullae</i> amulets</a> were also made in Ireland during the Bronze Age.\n</p><p>Smaller wedge tombs continued to be built throughout the Bronze Age, and while the previous tradition of large scale monument building was much reduced, existing earlier megalithic monuments continued in use in the form of secondary insertions of funerary and ritual artefacts. Towards the end of the Bronze Age the single-grave cist made its appearance. This consisted of a small rectangular stone chest, covered with a stone slab and buried a short distance below the surface. Numerous stone circles were also erected at this time, chiefly in Ulster and Munster.\n</p><p>During the Bronze Age, the climate of Ireland deteriorated and extensive deforestation took place. The population of Ireland at the end of the Bronze Age was probably in excess of 100,000, and may have been as high as 200,000. It is possible that it was not much greater than it had been at the height of the Neolithic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  In Ireland the Bronze Age lasted until c. 500BC, later than the continent and also Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Iron_Age_.28500_BC_.E2.80.93_AD_400.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age_(500_BC_\xe2\x80\x93_AD_400)">Iron Age (500 BC \xe2\x80\x93 AD 400)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Iron Age (500 BC \xe2\x80\x93 AD 400)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><span id="Iron_Age"></span>\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Protohistory_of_Ireland" title="Protohistory of Ireland">Protohistory of Ireland</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_settlement_of_the_British_Isles" title="Prehistoric settlement of the British Isles">Prehistoric settlement of the British Isles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_substrate_hypothesis" title="Goidelic substrate hypothesis">Goidelic substrate hypothesis</a></div>\n<div style="float:right; position: relative;border: solid #CCCCCC 1px;padding:5px;margin:5px;">\n<p><a href="/wiki/File:Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png" class="image"><img alt="Island of Ireland NASA.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png/200px-Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png" width="200" height="253" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png/300px-Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png/400px-Island_of_Ireland_NASA.png 2x" data-file-width="746" data-file-height="945" /></a>\n</p>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:144px; top:32px"><b>Robogdii</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:164px; top:62px"><b>Darini</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:134px; top:72px"><b>Voluntii</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:64px; top:82px"><b>Nagnatae</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:124px; top:102px"><b>Ebdani</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:134px; top:122px"><b>Cauci</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:144px; top:152px"><b>Manapii</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:144px; top:172px"><b>Coriondi</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:134px; top:192px"><b>Brigantes</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:94px; top:172px"><b>Usdiae</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:54px; top:182px"><b>Gangani</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:54px; top:152px"><b>Auteini</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:34px; top:202px"><b>Vellabori</b></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:44px; top:232px"><b>Iverni</b></div>\n<p>Tribes of Ireland according to <br /><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Geographia_(Ptolemy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographia (Ptolemy)">Geographia</a> (written c. 150 AD).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</div>\n<p>The Irish <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> began around 500 BC and continued until the Christian era in Ireland, which brought some written records and therefore the end of prehistoric Ireland. This is still often seen as the beginning of the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> (i.e. speakers of the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language" title="Proto-Celtic language">Proto-Celtic language</a>) and thus Indo-European speakers, to the island, though it is debatable to what extent, if any, the likely Indo-European speaking bearers of the Bell Beaker culture had colonized the island during the earlier stage of the Bronze Age.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>   The Iron Age includes the period in which the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> ruled the neighbouring island of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>. Roman interest in the area led to some of the earliest written evidence about Ireland. The names of its tribes were recorded by the geographer Ptolemy in the 2nd century AD.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a> of Britain and Ireland, also known as <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic</a>, can be divided into two groups, <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Goidelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">Brittonic</a>. When primary written records of Celtic first appear in about the fifth century, Gaelic or Goidelic, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Primitive_Irish" title="Primitive Irish">Primitive Irish</a>, is found in Ireland, while Brittonic, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a>, is found in Britain.\n</p><p>The recorded tribes of Ireland included at least three with names identical or similar to British or Gaulish tribes: the <a href="/wiki/Brigantes" title="Brigantes">Brigantes</a> (also the name of the largest tribe in northern and midland Britain), the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manapii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Manapii (page does not exist)">Manapii</a> (possibly the same people as the <a href="/wiki/Menapii" title="Menapii">Menapii</a>, a Belgic tribe of northern <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Coriondi" title="Coriondi">Coriondi</a> (a name similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Cirencester" title="Cirencester">Corinion</a>, later Cirencester and the <a href="/wiki/Corionototae" title="Corionototae">Corionototae</a> of northern Britain).\n</p><p>The late Iron Age saw sizeable changes in human activity. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles-Edwards" title="Thomas Charles-Edwards">Thomas Charles-Edwards</a> coined the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Irish_Dark_Age" title="Irish Dark Age">Irish Dark Age</a>" to refer to a period of apparent economic and cultural stagnation in late prehistoric Ireland, lasting from c. 100 BC to c. AD 300.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">Pollen data</a> extracted from Irish <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a> indicate that "the impact of human activity upon the flora around the bogs from which the pollen came was less between c. 200 BC and c. AD 300 than either before or after."<sup id="cite_ref-Charles-Edwards,_p._148_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles-Edwards,_p._148-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The third and fourth centuries saw a rapid recovery.<sup id="cite_ref-Charles-Edwards,_p._148_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles-Edwards,_p._148-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The reasons for the decline and recovery are uncertain, but it has been suggested that recovery may be linked to the purported "Golden Age" of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> in the third and fourth centuries. The archaeological evidence for trade with, or raids on, Roman Britain is strongest in northern <a href="/wiki/Leinster" title="Leinster">Leinster</a>, centred on modern <a href="/wiki/County_Dublin" title="County Dublin">County Dublin</a>, followed by the coast of <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a>, with lesser concentrations in <a href="/wiki/The_Rosses" title="The Rosses">the Rosses</a> on the north coast of <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Carlingford_Lough" title="Carlingford Lough">Carlingford Lough</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Inhumation" class="mw-redirect" title="Inhumation">Inhumation</a> burials may also have spread from Roman Britain, and had become common in Ireland by the fourth and fifth centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Examples from Iron Age Ireland of <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_style" class="mw-redirect" title="La T\xc3\xa8ne style">La T\xc3\xa8ne style</a>, the term for Iron Age <a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic art</a>, are very few. Some of these are of very high quality, such as a number of scabbards from Ulster and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Petrie_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Petrie Crown (page does not exist)">Petrie Crown</a>, apparently dating to the 2nd century AD. This was well after Celtic art elsewhere had been subsumed into <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallo-Roman">Gallo-Roman</a> art and its British equivalent.  Despite this it was in Ireland that the style seemed to revive in the early Christian period, to form the <a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular art</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Kells" title="Book of Kells">Book of Kells</a> and other well-known masterpieces, perhaps under influence from Late Roman and post-Roman <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> styles.  The 1st century BC <a href="/wiki/Broighter_Gold" title="Broighter Gold">Broighter Gold</a> hoard, from Ulster, includes a small model boat, a spectacular <a href="/wiki/Torc" title="Torc">torc</a> with relief decoration influenced by classical style, and other gold jewellery probably imported from the Roman world, perhaps as far away as <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was also during this time that some <a href="/wiki/Protohistory_of_Ireland" title="Protohistory of Ireland">protohistoric</a> records begin to appear.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://irisharchaeology.ie/2016/03/new-discovery-pushes-back-date-of-human-existence-in-ireland-by-2500-years/">"New Discovery Pushes Back Date of Human Existence in Ireland by 2500 years"</a>, Irish Archaeology</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation journal">Clark, Peter U.; Dyke, Arthur S.; Shakun, Jeremy D.; Carlson, Anders E.; Clark, Jorie; <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Wohlfarth" title="Barbara Wohlfarth">Wohlfarth, Barbara</a>; Mitrovica, Jerry X.; Hostetler, Steven W.; McCabe, A.
