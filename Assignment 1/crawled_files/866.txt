class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="/\xc9\xa1/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">\xc9\xa1</span><span title="/j/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;yes&#39;">j</span><span title="/u\xcb\x90/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">u\xcb\x90</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw"><b>Keow</b></i>, meaning "hedge banks")<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> could be described as the sixth inhabited island of the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, but is usually included with <a href="/wiki/St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Agnes, Isles of Scilly">St Agnes</a> with which it is joined by a sandy <a href="/wiki/Tombolo" title="Tombolo">tombolo</a> known as "The Bar" when exposed at low tide. The island is only about 1&#160;km (0.62&#160;mi) long and about 0.5&#160;km (0.31&#160;mi) wide, with the highest point, Kittern Hill at 34&#160;m (112&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The geology consists of <a href="/wiki/Hercynian" class="mw-redirect" title="Hercynian">Hercynian</a> granite with shallow <a href="/wiki/Podzolic" class="mw-redirect" title="Podzolic">podzolic</a> soils on the higher ground and deeper sandy soils on the lower ground. The former Gugh farm is just north of the neck across the middle of the island between the two hills. The two houses were designed and built in the 1920s by Charles Hamlet Cooper.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The name is often mispronounced as "Goo", "Guff" or even "Gogh".\n</p><p>The island lies within the Isles of Scilly <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Heritage Coast">Heritage Coast</a>, is in the Isles of Scilly <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> and is managed by the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly_Wildlife_Trust" title="Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust">Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust</a>. Vegetation cover is mainly wind-pruned heath or dense bracken and bramble with a small area of coastal grassland formed over blown sand which has accumulated near the bar.\n</p><p>In 2013 the Isles of Scilly Seabird Recovery Project was set up by a number of organisations including the RSPB  and the Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust. The five-year project aims to keep the islands of <a href="/wiki/St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Agnes, Isles of Scilly">St Agnes</a> and Gugh <a href="/wiki/Brown_rat" title="Brown rat">brown rat</a> (<i>Rattus norvegicus</i>) free, to help breeding sea birds, which lost 25% of their populations between 1983 and 2006. The rats eat eggs and kill the chicks of those birds that nest in burrows or on the ground. Rat removal began in October 2013 by a team of 30 volunteers led by Wildlife Management International Limited (WMIL) of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, and there has been no signs of rats on St Agnes and Gugh since December 2013. WMIL will return to the islands to do a final check for rats in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-pearson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Man_of_Gugh,_Isles_of_Scilly_-_geograph.org.uk_-_84368.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Old_Man_of_Gugh%2C_Isles_of_Scilly_-_geograph.org.uk_-_84368.jpg/220px-Old_Man_of_Gugh%2C_Isles_of_Scilly_-_geograph.org.uk_-_84368.jpg" width="220" height="320" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Old_Man_of_Gugh%2C_Isles_of_Scilly_-_geograph.org.uk_-_84368.jpg/330px-Old_Man_of_Gugh%2C_Isles_of_Scilly_-_geograph.org.uk_-_84368.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Old_Man_of_Gugh%2C_Isles_of_Scilly_-_geograph.org.uk_-_84368.jpg 2x" data-file-width="440" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Man_of_Gugh,_Isles_of_Scilly_-_geograph.org.uk_-_84368.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Old Man of Gugh</div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest signs of occupation on Gugh are two groups each, of <a href="/wiki/Scillonian_entrance_grave" title="Scillonian entrance grave">entrance graves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a>. Entrance graves are either burial or ritual monuments and cairns are burial mounds. A lack of finds, most likely because of acid soils destroying any evidence, makes the dating of the monuments difficult but a few pottery remains date them to late <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> or early Bronze Age.  On Kittern Hill there are five entrance graves, one of which <a href="/wiki/Obadiah%27s_Barrow" title="Obadiah&#39;s Barrow">Obadiah\'s Barrow</a> was excavated in 1901, by <a href="/wiki/George_Edward_Bonsor_Saint_Martin" title="George Edward Bonsor Saint Martin">George Bonsor</a>, and \xe2\x80\xb3disarticulate unburnt bones\xe2\x80\xb3 found.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  There is also a cluster of fourteen cairns which are linked by prehistoric field walls or banks but the relationship between the two is not established. The only <a href="/wiki/Menhir" title="Menhir">menhir</a> to be excavated on Scilly is the <i>Old Man of Gugh</i>, a 2.7&#160;m (9&#160;ft) tall standing stone which lies at the base of Kittern Hill, but there was no features or finds.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  There is also a cluster of nineteen cairns and a field system on the south part of Gugh along with a further two entrance graves. An <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> <a href="/wiki/Artillery_battery" title="Artillery battery">battery</a> was built over one on Carn of Works and its chamber re-used as a <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(artillery)" title="Magazine (artillery)">magazine</a>. The Civil War defences are concentrated around the Scillonian coast to defend the deep-water approaches. Part of Gugh is a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_Monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled Monument">Scheduled Monument</a> and the whole island is recommended for scheduling.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St._Agnes_map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/St._Agnes_map.png/220px-St._Agnes_map.png" width="220" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/St._Agnes_map.png/330px-St._Agnes_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9d/St._Agnes_map.png 2x" data-file-width="413" data-file-height="358" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St._Agnes_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The islands of Gugh and St Agnes</div></div></div>\n<p>For centuries Gugh seemed to have been uninhabited and used by the residents of St Agnes for cattle grazing.<sup id="cite_ref-fire1933_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fire1933-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Two kelp pits have been recognised, one on the north-east side of Kittern Hill and the second at Tol Tuppens. Burning seaweed was introduced in 1684 by Mr Nance on <a href="/wiki/Te%C3%A4n" title="Te\xc3\xa4n">Te\xc3\xa4n</a> to provide <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> for glass making and continued until 1835. Kelp burning only produces 2\xe2\x80\x933 percent <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> and during the 19th-century more efficient commercial and industrial methods ended the practice locally.\n</p><p>In the 1920s, a retired surveyor and former consulting engineer of the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Borough_of_Wimbledon" title="Municipal Borough of Wimbledon">Corporation of Wimbledon</a>, William Hamlet Cooper (died 10 September 1932), formerly of <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a>, secured the lease of the island, built the two buildings that can be seen today and started a farm. He lived on the island, along with his housekeeper<sup id="cite_ref-fire1933_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fire1933-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In a 1925 letter to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Morning_News" title="Western Morning News">Western Morning News</a> he wrote of his attempts to control black-backed gulls (greater or lesser is not recorded), which included constant shooting and the destruction of nearly 2,000 eggs. In 1924, none of his animals could graze on the northern part of the island during the nesting season due to attacks on his cattle and sheep, which included the loss of a valuable ram which never recovered from the injuries received.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In Cooper\'s will it states that if he died on Gugh he should be buried on the island at Kitten Hill (place of death is not mentioned).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> An auction of the animals and crops owned by Cooper occurred on Gugh on 21 October 1932. Animals listed were 80 fowls (mostly white leghorn), two Kerry cows, a heifer, two farm horses and 69 pigs. Also for sale was 5 tons of potatoes, 50 cartloads of mangold wurzels, 5 tons of hay, 3 tons of barley grain, 35 cwt of bran and 10 tons of lime. The buyer also had the right to harvest 2 acres of <a href="/wiki/Narcissus_(plant)" title="Narcissus (plant)">narcissus</a> bulbs (Soleil d\'Or, Scilly White and Princeps).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A fire on the island In September 1933 burnt for a week with the island said to be ablaze  from end to end. The fire was put out by the staff of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Algernon_Dorrien-Smith" title="Arthur Algernon Dorrien-Smith">Major Dorrien-Smith</a> and the farmhouse and farm buildings owned by Mr Theo Bond and his wife, the only inhabitants, were saved. The Bonds formerly lived on <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Martin&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Martin\'s</a>, spent their honeymoon on the Gugh and decided to live there and continue flower production.<sup id="cite_ref-fire1933_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fire1933-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_history">Natural history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>\nThe island was first notified as a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> in 1976 and re-notified under the 1981 Act in 1986. The SSSI covers an area of 36.6 hectares (90 acres), of which 35.3 hectares (87 acres) was assessed as "unfavourable recovering" when it was last reviewed on 30 July 2010. </p><blockquote><p>"Although the vascular plant assemblage (VPA) is favourable the notified heathland habitat is recorded as unfavourable recovering. \nThe VPA species are all present and occurring in suitable habitat except for small adder\'s-tongue (<i><a href="/wiki/Ophioglossum_azoricum" title="Ophioglossum azoricum">Ophioglossum azoricum</a></i>) which has not been recorded on this site since 1986, however the former site appears suitable for the species and is therefore recorded as favourable".</p></blockquote>\n<p>The reasons for the unfavourable assessment is because there is too much ground cover of bramble (<i><a href="/wiki/Blackberry" title="Blackberry">Rubus fruiticosus</a></i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Pittosporum" title="Pittosporum">Pittosporum</a></i>, and the heath on Kittern Hill is less interesting than on the rest of the island. The reason is probably because of a fire on the hill in 1972 and subsequently there is less <i><a href="/wiki/Cladonia" title="Cladonia">Cladonia</a></i> <i>sp</i>. (a lichen). The <i>Pittosporum</i> requires urgent control and the island needs grazing to return it to a favourable condition. There is also a problem with the recent appearance of stone mazes which should be discouraged.<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The notifiable habitats for Gugh are the heath communities; <a href="/wiki/British_NVC_community_H7" title="British NVC community H7">H7</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_NVC_community_H8&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="British NVC community H8 (page does not exist)">H8</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_NVC_community_H11&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="British NVC community H11 (page does not exist)">H11</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bell_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GughSandbar.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/GughSandbar.JPG/180px-GughSandbar.JPG" width="180" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/GughSandbar.JPG/270px-GughSandbar.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/GughSandbar.JPG/360px-GughSandbar.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GughSandbar.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The tombolo looking from St. Agnes towards Gugh</div></div></div>\n<p>Much of the vegetation of Gugh is either wind-pruned, dry, waved maritime heath or dense gorse and bracken.  The three dominant species on the heath are heather (<i><a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">Calluna vulgaris</a></i>), bell heather (<i><a href="/wiki/Erica" title="Erica">Erica cinerea</a></i>) and western gorse (<i><a href="/wiki/Ulex_gallii" title="Ulex gallii">Ulex gallii</a></i>). Immediately above the bar is a small area of dune grassland merging into maritime grassland around the coastal fringe. On the small dune system grows western clover (<i><a href="/wiki/Trifolium_occidentale" title="Trifolium occidentale">Trifolium occidentale</a></i>), sea holly (<i><a href="/wiki/Eryngium_maritimum" title="Eryngium maritimum">Eryngium maritimum</a></i>), sea spurge (<i><a href="/wiki/Euphorbia_paralias" title="Euphorbia paralias">Euphorbia paralias</a></i>) and Portland spurge (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Euphorbia_portlandica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Euphorbia portlandica (page does not exist)">Euphorbia portlandica</a></i>), wild thyme (<i><a href="/wiki/Thymus_polytrichus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thymus polytrichus">Thymus polytrichus</a></i>) and sea bindweed (<i><a href="/wiki/Calystegia_soldanella" title="Calystegia soldanella">Calystegia soldanella</a></i>). The neck of the island between the two hills where the farm was located has an unusual flora. Amongst dense bracken is balm-leaved figwort (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrophularia_scorodonia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Scrophularia scorodonia (page does not exist)">Scrophularia scorodonia</a></i>), common here but not found elsewhere on the island and an unidentified yellow, cultivated rose. A second alien, Argentine dock (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rumex_frutescens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rumex frutescens (page does not exist)">Rumex frutescens</a></i>) grows on the edge of a sand pit which was originally intended to be a reservoir. The fields below the two houses were, before 1933, fertilised with "<a href="/wiki/Shoddy" class="mw-redirect" title="Shoddy">shoddy</a>" \xe2\x80\x93 a high nitrate manure derived from the woollen industry. Within these fields can be found viper\xe2\x80\x99s-bugloss (<i><a href="/wiki/Echium_vulgare" title="Echium vulgare">Echium vulgare</a></i>), common melilot (<i><a href="/wiki/Melilotus_officinalis" title="Melilotus officinalis">Melilotus officinalis</a></i>) and wild mignonette (<i><a href="/wiki/Reseda_lutea" title="Reseda lutea">Reseda lutea</a></i>). All three are thought to be imported arable weeds. In the 1960s the "neck" was a closely cropped sward of grass but after <a href="/wiki/Myxomatosis" title="Myxomatosis">myxomatosis</a> the area became overgrown with bracken and bramble.\n</p><p>In October, 1972 a fire on Kittern Hill burnt though the shallow peaty soil to the granite. Bleached stones and blackened gorse stems can still be seen and the vegetation has not recovered sufficiently to equal the waved heath elsewhere on Scilly. Heath is on the hills on both sides of \xe2\x80\x9cthe neck\xe2\x80\x9d and in the south of the island the nationally rare orange bird\xe2\x80\x99s-foot (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithopus_pinnatus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ornithopus pinnatus (page does not exist)">Ornithopus pinnatus</a></i>) can be found as can rare lichen species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lobaria_pulmonaria" title="Lobaria pulmonaria">Lobaria pulmonaria</a></i> and golden-hair lichen (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teloschistes_flavicans&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Teloschistes flavicans (page does not exist)">Teloschistes flavicans</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rare_plants">Rare plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rare plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li>Shore dock (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rumex_rupestris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rumex rupestris (page does not exist)">Rumex rupestris</a></i>) first discovered here in 1893 by <a href="/wiki/John_Ralfs" title="John Ralfs">John Ralfs</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> still extant in the 1960s but now extinct.</li>\n<li>Small adder\'s-tongue (<i><a href="/wiki/Ophioglossum_azoricum" title="Ophioglossum azoricum">Ophioglossum azoricum</a></i>) has not been seen since at least the mid-1980s. The site was on the east coast between Carn Kimbra and Point Witcher.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The only area of the SSSI that was classified as "favourable".</li>\n<li>Four-leaved allseed (<i><a href="/wiki/Polycarpon_tetraphyllum" title="Polycarpon tetraphyllum">Polycarpon tetraphyllum</a></i>) was recorded by Lousley in 1939 and 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In July 1924, W. N. Blair caught a shrew which he did not recognise and sent a specimen to the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> for the attention of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Hinton" title="Martin Hinton">Martin Hinton</a>, who identified it as the lesser white-toothed shrew (<i><a href="/wiki/Crocidura_suaveolens" class="mw-redirect" title="Crocidura suaveolens">Crocidura suaveolens</a></i>). Known as the "Scilly shrew", the type specimen is held by the British Museum.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The other mammals found on Gugh are feral cats, rabbit (<i><a href="/wiki/Oryctolagus_cuniculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Oryctolagus cuniculus">Oryctolagus cuniculus</a></i>) and possibly the house mouse (<i><a href="/wiki/Mus_musculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mus musculus">Mus musculus</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Permanent monitoring stations, consisting of boxes with chocolate wax inside, have been set up around the coasts of both Gugh and St Agnes and a 50&#160;m (164&#160;ft) baiting grid will be set up around any signs of rats.<sup id="cite_ref-pearson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As of 2015 the brown rat is most likely eradicated on the island.<sup id="cite_ref-pearson_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Rabbits are currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2016)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the only grazing animal and in the 1960s myxomatosis decimated the population and led to an increase of scrub on parts of the island, especially "the neck" where in some years cuckoos (<i><a href="/wiki/Cuculus_canorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuculus canorus">Cuculus canorus</a></i>) were attracted by the large numbers of garden tiger moth (<i><a href="/wiki/Arctia_caja" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctia caja">Arctia caja</a></i>) and other large caterpillars. In one year the number of garden tiger larvae was 90 per square metre. The last grazing animals left in 1974 and <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> would like grazing animals back on Gugh to counteract the effects of the scrub and dense sward of grass covering parts of the island. A <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> deficiency in the soil means grazing animals need supplements.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the southern part of the island large colonies of lesser black-blacked (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_fuscus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus fuscus">Larus fuscus graellsii</a></i>) and herring gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_argentatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus argentatus">Larus argentatus</a></i>) breed, as do a small number of greater black-backed gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_marinus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus marinus">Larus marinus</a></i>). Storm petrel (<i><a href="/wiki/Hydrobates_pelagicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrobates pelagicus">Hydrobates pelagicus</a></i>) and kittiwake (<i><a href="/wiki/Rissa_tridactyla" class="mw-redirect" title="Rissa tridactyla">Rissa tridactyla</a></i>) no longer breed there.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> To protect and enhance the islands\' seabirds, and to protect <a href="/wiki/Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Annet, Isles of Scilly">Annet</a> (an important breeding site) from re-invasion, a feasibility study was carried out to see if it was possible to eradicate rats from the Isles of Scilly. A winter trapping survey on St Agnes and Gugh indicated that those islands had a population of 3,300 brown rats. It was found the rats foraged on a variety of food including Scilly shrew which were found in the stomach contents of 18% of the rats trapped. Furthermore, numbers of the shrew were higher in areas where the rats were controlled; an indication that rats are having an effect on their numbers. The survey showed that it was both feasible, and there are significant benefits, to remove the rats as they are preventing Manx shearwater and storm petrel from establishing on St Agnes and Gugh.<sup id="cite_ref-Bell_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In 2009 lesser black-backed gull bred on Gugh but with low chick productivity, and the small colony of kittiwake nested, but failed for at least the fourth year.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Following the rat eradication programme 12 Manx shearwater chicks fledged on Gugh and St Agnes in September 2014.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vagrant_birds">Vagrant birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vagrant birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_eagle" title="White-tailed eagle">white-tailed eagle</a> (<i>Haliaetus albicilla</i>) with a wingspan of 7&#160;ft 6&#160;in (2.29 m) was shot on Gugh in November 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>A white-tailed eagle (<i>Haliaetus albicilla</i>) was seen on the Isles of Scilly (and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bosigran&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bosigran (page does not exist)">Bosigran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zennor" title="Zennor">Zennor</a>) from April 1947 onwards. It was thought to roost on Gugh where it was once seen eating a shag (<i><a href="/wiki/Phalacrocorax_aristotelis" class="mw-redirect" title="Phalacrocorax aristotelis">Phalacrocorax aristotelis</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>A <a href="/wiki/Greenish_warbler" title="Greenish warbler">two\xe2\x80\x93barred greenish warbler</a> was found by <a href="/wiki/Colin_Bradshaw" title="Colin Bradshaw">Colin Bradshaw</a> and Tom Bradshaw on Gugh from 21\xe2\x80\x9327 October 1987.
