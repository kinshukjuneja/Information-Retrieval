style="height: 0.2em;">\n<td colspan="2">\n</td></tr>\n\n\n\n</tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Quantock Hills</b> is a range of hills west of <a href="/wiki/Bridgwater" title="Bridgwater">Bridgwater</a> in <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, England. The Quantock Hills were England\'s first <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>, being designated in 1956,<sup id="cite_ref-Somerset_County_Council_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Somerset_County_Council-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and consist of heathland, oak woodlands, ancient parklands and agricultural land.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> have designated the Quantock Hills as <a href="/wiki/National_character_area" class="mw-redirect" title="National character area">national character area</a> 144. They are entirely surrounded by NCA 146: the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Taunton_and_Quantock_Fringes" title="Vale of Taunton and Quantock Fringes">Vale of Taunton and Quantock Fringes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The hills run from the <a href="/wiki/Taunton_Deane" title="Taunton Deane">Vale of Taunton Deane</a> in the south, for about 15 miles (24&#160;km) to the north-west, ending at <a href="/wiki/Kilve" title="Kilve">Kilve</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Quantoxhead" title="West Quantoxhead">West Quantoxhead</a> on the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel" title="Bristol Channel">Bristol Channel</a>. They form the western border of <a href="/wiki/Sedgemoor" title="Sedgemoor">Sedgemoor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Somerset_Levels" title="Somerset Levels">Somerset Levels</a>. From the top of the hills on a clear day, it is possible to see <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Tor" title="Glastonbury Tor">Glastonbury Tor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mendip_Hills" title="Mendip Hills">Mendips</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> as far as the <a href="/wiki/Gower_Peninsula" title="Gower Peninsula">Gower Peninsula</a> to the north, the <a href="/wiki/Brendon_Hills" title="Brendon Hills">Brendon Hills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exmoor" title="Exmoor">Exmoor</a> to the west, and the <a href="/wiki/Blackdown_Hills" title="Blackdown Hills">Blackdown Hills</a> to the south. The highest point on the Quantocks is <a href="/wiki/Wills_Neck" title="Wills Neck">Wills Neck</a>, at 1,261 feet (384&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-curio_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-curio-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Soil types and weather combine to support the hills\' plants and animals. In 1970 an area of 6,194.5 acres (2,506.8&#160;ha) was designated as a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Biological Site of Special Scientific Interest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Archaeological landscape features include <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Round_barrow" title="Round barrow">round barrows</a>, extensive ancient field systems and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> hill forts. Evidence from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times includes <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> coins discovered in <a href="/wiki/West_Bagborough" title="West Bagborough">West Bagborough</a>. The hills are now a peaceful area popular with walkers, mountain bikers, horse riders and tourists. They explore paths such as the <a href="/wiki/Coleridge_Way" title="Coleridge Way">Coleridge Way</a> (the poet <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a> lived in <a href="/wiki/Nether_Stowey" title="Nether Stowey">Nether Stowey</a> from 1797 to 1799) or visit places of interest in the surrounding villages.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">13</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The name first appears in <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxon</a> charters in around AD 880 as <i>Cantuctun</i> and two centuries later in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> as <i>Cantoctona</i> and <i>Cantetone</i>. The name means <i>settlement by a rim or circle of hills</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-dunning_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dunning-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Cantuc</i> is <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> for a rim or circle, and <i>-ton</i> or <i>-tun</i> is <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> for a settlement. The highest point of the hills is called Will\'s Neck meaning <i>ridge of the Welshman</i>, probably referring to a time when the hills marked the boundary between the expanding Saxon kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a> and the lands of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Britons" title="Celtic Britons">Britons</a> or \'Welsh\' to the West. A battle was fought locally at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-curio_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-curio-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Quantocksbeach.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Gently sloping rock slab beach. In the distance are cliffs showing lines of striation." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Quantocksbeach.jpg/220px-Quantocksbeach.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Quantocksbeach.jpg/330px-Quantocksbeach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Quantocksbeach.jpg/440px-Quantocksbeach.jpg 2x" data-file-width="586" data-file-height="388" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Quantocksbeach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beach at Quantock\'s Head. The wave cut platform is visible at low tide below the short "cliff" exposing the rock strata.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Quantock Hills are largely formed by rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> period, which consist of sediments originally laid down under a shallow sea and slowly compressed into solid rock. In the higher north-western areas older Early Devonian rocks known as <a href="/wiki/Exmoor_Group" title="Exmoor Group">Hangman Grits</a> (or, more formally, the Hangman Sandstone Formation) predominate<sup id="cite_ref-dunning_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dunning-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and can be seen in the exposed rock at <a href="/wiki/West_Quantoxhead" title="West Quantoxhead">West Quantoxhead</a> quarry, which was worked for road building.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Hangman Grits are described in three divisions: the lowest are the Little Quantock Beds, which are located near <a href="/wiki/Crowcombe" title="Crowcombe">Crowcombe</a>, and made up of siltstones and slates. Between <a href="/wiki/Triscombe" class="mw-redirect" title="Triscombe">Triscombe</a> and West Quantoxhead is a layer of the Triscombe Beds which is around 500 metres (1,600&#160;ft) thick and is made up of green sandstone and mudstones. The uppermost division is the Hodders Combe Beds of sandstone and conglomerate and is approximately 300 metres (980&#160;ft) thick.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Further south there are newer Middle and Late Devonian rocks, known as <a href="/wiki/Exmoor_Group" title="Exmoor Group">Ilfracombe beds and Morte Slates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These include sandstone and limestone, which have been quarried near <a href="/wiki/Aisholt" class="mw-redirect" title="Aisholt">Aisholt</a>. At Great Holwell, south of Aisholt, is the only limestone cave in the Devonian limestone of North <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and West Somerset.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The lower fringes around the hills are composed of younger <a href="/wiki/New_Red_Sandstone" title="New Red Sandstone">New Red Sandstone</a> rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> period.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> These rocks were laid down in a shallow sea and often contain irregular masses or veins of <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, which was mined on the foreshore at <a href="/wiki/Watchet" title="Watchet">Watchet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several areas have outcrops of slates. Younger rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> period can be found between <a href="/wiki/St_Audries" class="mw-redirect" title="St Audries">St Audries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kilve" title="Kilve">Kilve</a>. This area falls within the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Anchor_to_Lilstock_Coast_SSSI" title="Blue Anchor to Lilstock Coast SSSI">Blue Anchor to Lilstock</a> <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI) and is considered to be of international geological importance.\n</p><p>Kilve has the remains of a red-brick <a href="/wiki/Retort" title="Retort">retort</a> built in 1924 after the shale in the cliffs was found to be rich in oil. Along this coast, the cliffs are layered with compressed strata of oil-bearing shale and <a href="/wiki/Blue_Lias" title="Blue Lias">blue</a>, yellow and brown <a href="/wiki/Early_Jurassic" title="Early Jurassic">Lias</a> embedded with <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>. The Shaline Company was founded in 1924 to exploit these strata but was unable to raise sufficient capital. The company\'s retort house is thought to be the first structure erected here for the conversion of shale to oil and is all that remains of the anticipated Somerset oil boom.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At <a href="/wiki/Blue_Anchor" title="Blue Anchor">Blue Anchor</a> the coloured <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">alabaster</a> found in the cliffs gave rise to the name of the colour "Watchet Blue".<sup id="cite_ref-curio_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-curio-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The village has the only updraught brick <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a> known to have survived in Somerset. It was built around 1830 and was supplied by small vessels carrying limestone to the small landing <a href="/wiki/Jetty" title="Jetty">jetty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-somharbours_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-somharbours-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Now used as a garage, the kiln is thought to have operated until the 1870s, when the large-scale production of bricks in <a href="/wiki/Bridgwater" title="Bridgwater">Bridgwater</a> rendered small brickyards uneconomic.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cockercombe_tuff" title="Cockercombe tuff">Cockercombe tuff</a> is a greenish-grey, hard <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_rock" title="Pyroclastic rock">pyroclastic rock</a> formed by the compression of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a> and is found almost exclusively in the south-eastern end of the Quantock Hills.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Quantocks,_aerial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Quantocks%2C_aerial.jpg/300px-Quantocks%2C_aerial.jpg" width="300" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Quantocks%2C_aerial.jpg/450px-Quantocks%2C_aerial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Quantocks%2C_aerial.jpg/600px-Quantocks%2C_aerial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="2756" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Quantocks,_aerial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view of the Quantock Hills</div></div></div>\n<p>Along with the rest of <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a>, the Quantock Hills has a <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperateness">temperate climate</a> that is generally wetter and milder than the rest of England. The mean temperature is approximately 10&#160;\xc2\xb0C (50&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and shows a <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasonal</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_motion" title="Diurnal motion">diurnal</a> variation, but because of the modifying effect of the sea the range is less than in most other parts of the United Kingdom. January is the coldest month with mean minimum temperatures between 1&#160;\xc2\xb0C (34&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and 2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (36&#160;\xc2\xb0F). July and August are the warmest months, with mean daily maxima around 21&#160;\xc2\xb0C (70&#160;\xc2\xb0F). December is normally the most cloudy month and June the sunniest. High pressure over the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> often brings clear skies to south-west England, particularly in summer.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">Cloud</a> often forms inland especially near hills, and acts to reduce sunshine. The average annual sunshine totals around 1,600&#160;hours. Rainfall tends to be associated with Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">depressions</a> or with convection. In summer, convection caused by solar surface heating sometimes forms shower clouds, and a large proportion of rain falls from showers and thunderstorms at this time of year. Average rainfall is around 31 to 35 inches (790 to 890&#160;mm). About 8 to 15&#160;days of snowfall is typical. From November to March, mean wind speeds are highest; winds are lightest from June to August. The predominant wind direction is from the south-west.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1970 an area of 6,194.5 acres (2,506.8&#160;ha) in the Quantocks was designated as a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Biological Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI).<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This a <a href="/wiki/Conservation_designation" title="Conservation designation">conservation designation</a> denoting a <a href="/wiki/Protected_area" title="Protected area">protected area</a> in the United Kingdom, selected by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>, for areas with particular landscape and ecological characteristics. It provides some protection from development, from other damage, and (since 2000) also from neglect, under the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000</a>.\n</p><p>The streams and open water such as <a href="/wiki/Hawkridge_Reservoir" title="Hawkridge Reservoir">Hawkridge Reservoir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ashford_Reservoir" title="Ashford Reservoir">Ashford Reservoir</a> on <a href="/wiki/Cannington_Brook" title="Cannington Brook">Cannington Brook</a> also provide habitats for a range of species.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Quantockheather.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Ground covered with purple heather. In the distance are hills and coastline." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Quantockheather.jpg/220px-Quantockheather.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Quantockheather.jpg/330px-Quantockheather.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Quantockheather.jpg/440px-Quantockheather.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="454" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Quantockheather.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Northern Flank of Beacon Hill (Quantocks). In late summer the northern Quantocks are ablaze with heather and gorse. <a href="/wiki/Minehead" title="Minehead">Minehead</a> can be seen in the distance.</div></div></div>\n<p>The hilltops are covered in heathland of gorse, heather, bracken and thorn with plantations of conifer. The western side of the Quantocks are steep <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">scarp</a> slopes of pasture, woods and parkland. Deep stream-cut <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">combes</a> to the north-east contain extensive oak-woods with small flower-rich bogs above them. The areas where there is limited drainage are dominated by <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a> (<i>Calluna vulgaris</i>), with significant populations of <a href="/wiki/Erica_tetralix" title="Erica tetralix">cross-leaved heath</a> (<i>Erica tetralix</i>), <a href="/wiki/Molinia_caerulea" title="Molinia caerulea">purple moor-grass</a> (<i>Molinia caerulea</i>), <a href="/wiki/Bilberry" title="Bilberry">bilberry</a> (<i>Vaccinium myrtillus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Deschampsia" title="Deschampsia">wavy hair-grass</a> (<i>Deschampsia flexuosa</i>). Drier areas are covered with <a href="/wiki/Erica_cinerea" title="Erica cinerea">bell heather</a> (<i>Erica cinerea</i>), <a href="/wiki/Ulex_gallii" title="Ulex gallii">western gorse</a> (<i>Ulex gallii</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Agrostis" title="Agrostis">bristle bent</a> (<i>Agrostis curtisii</i>), while <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a> (<i>Pteridium aquilinum</i>) is common on well-drained deeper soils. The springs and streams provide a specialist environment that supports <a href="/wiki/Anagallis_tenella" title="Anagallis tenella">bog pimpernel</a> (<i>Anagallis tenella</i>). The woodland is generally birch/sessile oak woodland, valley alder woodland and ash/wych elm woodland, which support a rich <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> flora. <a href="/wiki/Alfoxton" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfoxton">Alfoxton</a> Wood is one of only three British locations where the lichen <i>Tomasellia lectea</i> is present.<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The various habitats, together with the wide range of slopes and aspects, provide ideal conditions\nfor a rich fauna. <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">Amphibians</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Palmate_newt" title="Palmate newt">palmate newt</a> (<i>Triturus helveticus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Common_frog" title="Common frog">common frog</a> (<i>Rana</i>\ntemporaris<i>), and <a href="/wiki/Common_toad" title="Common toad">common toad</a> (</i>Bufo bufo<i>) are represented in the damper environments. Reptiles</i>\npresent include adder (<i><a href="/wiki/Vipera_berus" title="Vipera berus">Vipera berus</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Grass_snake" title="Grass snake">grass snake</a> (<i>Natrix natrix</i>), <a href="/wiki/Anguis_fragilis" title="Anguis fragilis">slowworm</a> (<i>Anguis fragilis</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Viviparous_lizard" title="Viviparous lizard">common lizard</a> (<i>Lacerta vivipara</i>). Many bird species breed on the Quantocks, including the <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper_warbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Grasshopper warbler">grasshopper warbler</a> (<i>Locustella naevia</i>), <a href="/wiki/Nightjar" title="Nightjar">nightjar</a> (<i>Caprimulgus europaeus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Raven" title="Raven">raven</a> (<i>Corvus corax</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/European_pied_flycatcher" title="European pied flycatcher">European pied flycatcher</a> (<i>Ficedula hypoleuca</i>). The Quantocks are also an important site for <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> (<i>Cervus elaphus</i>). Invertebrates of note include the <a href="/wiki/Silver-washed_fritillary" title="Silver-washed fritillary">silver-washed fritillary</a> butterfly (<i>Argynnis paphia</i>), and three nationally rare dead-wood beetles: <i>Thymalus limbatus</i>, <i>Orchesia undulata</i> and <i>Rhinosimus ruficollis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-SSSI_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSSI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Evidence of activity in the Quantocks from prehistoric times includes finds of <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> flints at <a href="/wiki/North_Petherton" title="North Petherton">North Petherton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broomfield,_Somerset" title="Broomfield, Somerset">Broomfield</a><sup id="cite_ref-AoS_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AoS-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and many <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Round_barrow" title="Round barrow">round barrows</a> (marked on maps as <i><a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">tumulus</a></i>, plural <i>tumuli</i>), such as Thorncombe Barrow above <a href="/wiki/Bicknoller" title="Bicknoller">Bicknoller</a>. Several ancient stones can be seen, such as the <a href="/wiki/Triscombe" class="mw-redirect" title="Triscombe">Triscombe</a> Stone and the Long Stone above <a href="/wiki/Holford" title="Holford">Holford</a>. Many of the tracks along ridges of the Quantocks probably originated as ancient <a href="/wiki/Ridgeway_(track)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ridgeway (track)">ridgeways</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dunning_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dunning-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A Bronze Age <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill fort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norton_Camp" title="Norton Camp">Norton Camp</a>, was built to the south at <a href="/wiki/Norton_Fitzwarren" title="Norton Fitzwarren">Norton Fitzwarren</a>, close to the centre of bronze making in <a href="/wiki/Taunton" title="Taunton">Taunton</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruborough_Camp_Somerset_Map.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Plan drawing with shading showing the position of earthworks." Camp">Ruborough Camp</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> sites in the Quantocks include major hill forts at <a href="/wiki/Dowsborough" title="Dowsborough">Dowsborough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruborough" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruborough">Ruborough</a>, as well as several smaller earthwork enclosures, such as <a href="/wiki/Trendle_Ring" title="Trendle Ring">Trendle Ring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plainsfield_Camp" title="Plainsfield Camp">Plainsfield Camp</a>. Ruborough near <a href="/wiki/Broomfield,_Somerset" title="Broomfield, Somerset">Broomfield</a> is on an easterly spur from the main Quantock ridge, with steep natural slopes to the north and south east. The fort is triangular in shape, with a single rampart and ditch (<a href="/wiki/Univallate" class="mw-redirect" title="Univallate">univallate</a>), enclosing 4 acres (1.6&#160;ha). A linear outer work about 131 yards (120&#160;m) away, parallel to the westerly rampart, encloses another 4 acres (16,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>). The name <i>Ruborough</i> comes from <i>Rugan beorh</i> or <i>Ruwan-beorge</i> meaning <i>Rough Hill</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Dowsborough fort has an oval shape, with a single rampart and ditch (<i>univallate</i>) following the contours of the hill top, enclosing an area of 7 acres (2.8&#160;ha). The main entrance is to the east, towards <a href="/wiki/Nether_Stowey" title="Nether Stowey">Nether Stowey</a>, with a simpler opening to the north west, aligned with a ridgeway leading down to Holford. A <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pass" title="Mountain pass">col</a> to the south connects the hill to the main Stowey ridge, where a linear earthwork known as Dead Woman\'s Ditch cuts across the spur.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Little evidence exists of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> influence on the Quantock region beyond isolated finds and hints of transient forts. A Roman port was at <a href="/wiki/Combwich" title="Combwich">Combwich</a>, and it is possible that a <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads" title="Roman roads">Roman road</a> ran from there to the Quantocks, because the names Nether Stowey and <a href="/wiki/Over_Stowey" title="Over Stowey">Over Stowey</a> come from the Old English <i>stan wey</i>, meaning <i>stone way</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-curio_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-curio-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In October 2001 the <a href="/wiki/West_Bagborough_Hoard" title="West Bagborough Hoard">West Bagborough Hoard</a> of 4th-century <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> was discovered in <a href="/wiki/West_Bagborough" title="West Bagborough">West Bagborough</a>. The 681&#160;coins included two <a href="/wiki/Denarius" title="Denarius">denarii</a> from the early 2nd century, and eight <a href="/wiki/Miliarense" title="Miliarense">miliarense</a> and 671&#160;<a href="/wiki/Siliqua" title="Siliqua">siliqua</a> dating to 337\xe2\x80\x93367&#160;AD. The majority were struck in the reigns of emperors <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Didius_Julianus" title="Didius Julianus">Julian</a> and derive from a range of mints including <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyons</a> in France, <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> in Germany and Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dark_Ages_and_Anglo-Saxon">Dark Ages and Anglo-Saxon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dark Ages and Anglo-Saxon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The area remained under Romano-British <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> control until 681\xe2\x80\x93685&#160;AD, when <a href="/wiki/Centwine_of_Wessex" title="Centwine of Wessex">Centwine of Wessex</a> pushed west from the <a href="/wiki/River_Parrett" title="River Parrett">River Parrett</a>, conquered the Welsh <a href="/wiki/Cadwaladr" title="Cadwaladr">King Cadwaladr</a>, and occupied the rest of Somerset north to the Bristol Channel.<sup id="cite_ref-ASC681_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASC681-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\nSaxon rule was later consolidated under <a href="/wiki/Ine_of_Wessex" title="Ine of Wessex">King Ine</a>, who established a fort at Taunton in about 700&#160;AD.<sup id="cite_ref-VCH_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCH-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first documentary evidence of the village of <a href="/wiki/Crowcombe" title="Crowcombe">Crowcombe</a> is by <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelwulf_of_Wessex" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x86thelwulf of Wessex">\xc3\x86thelwulf of Wessex</a> in 854, where it was spelt \'Cerawicombe\'.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> At that time the manor belonged to <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Abbey" title="Glastonbury Abbey">Glastonbury Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the later Saxon period, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">King Alfred</a> led the resistance to <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> invasion from <a href="/wiki/Athelney" title="Athelney">Athelney</a>, south-east of the Quantocks.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>, the early port at <a href="/wiki/Watchet" title="Watchet">Watchet</a> was plundered by Danes in 987 and 997.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Alfred established a series of forts and lookout posts linked by a military road, or <a href="/wiki/Herepath" title="Herepath">herepath</a>, so his army could cover Viking movements at sea. The herepath has a characteristic form that is familiar on the Quantocks: a regulation 66-foot (20&#160;m) wide track between avenues of trees growing from <a href="/wiki/Hedge_laying" class="mw-redirect" title="Hedge laying">hedge laying</a> embankments. The herepath ran from the ford on the River Parrett at Combwich, past <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cynwit" title="Battle of Cynwit">Cannington hill fort</a> to Over Stowey, where it climbed the Quantocks along the line of the current Stowey road, to Crowcombe Park Gate. Then it went south along the ridge, to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Triscombe_Stone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Triscombe Stone (page does not exist)">Triscombe Stone</a>. One branch may have led past Lydeard Hill and Buncombe Hill, back to Alfred\'s base at Athelney. The main branch descended the hills at Triscombe, then along the avenue to Red Post Cross, and west to the <a href="/wiki/Brendon_Hills" title="Brendon Hills">Brendon Hills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exmoor" title="Exmoor">Exmoor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gres_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gres-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> There is some evidence that an area of the hills known as Quantock Common may have been a Saxon Royal Forest.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a> in 1066 <a href="/wiki/William_de_Moyon" title="William de Moyon">William de Moyon</a> was given land at <a href="/wiki/Dunster" title="Dunster">Dunster</a>, Broomfield and <a href="/wiki/West_Quantoxhead" title="West Quantoxhead">West Quantoxhead</a>, his son becoming <a href="/wiki/William_de_Mohun_of_Dunster,_1st_Earl_of_Somerset" title="William de Mohun of Dunster, 1st Earl of Somerset">William de Mohun of Dunster, 1st Earl of Somerset</a>, while <a href="/wiki/William_Malet_(Norman_conquest)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Malet (Norman conquest)">William Malet</a> received <a href="/wiki/Enmore,_Somerset" title="Enmore, Somerset">Enmore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/East_Quantoxhead" title="East Quantoxhead">East Quantoxhead</a> was given to the Luttrells (previously spelled de Luterel), who passed the manor down through descendants into the 20th century. A Luttell also became the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Carhampton" title="Earl of Carhampton">Earl of Carhampton</a> and acquired <a href="/wiki/Dunster_Castle" title="Dunster Castle">Dunster Castle</a> in 1376, holding it until it became a <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a> property in 1976.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Stowey_Castle" title="Stowey Castle">Stowey Castle</a> at Nether Stowey was built in the 11th century. The castle is sited on a small isolated knoll, about 390&#160;ft (119&#160;m) high. It consisted of a square <a href="/wiki/Keep" title="Keep">keep</a> (which may have been stone, or a wooden superstructure on stone foundations) and its defences and an outer and an inner <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="Motte-and-bailey">bailey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The mount is 29&#160;ft (9&#160;m) above the 6&#160;ft (2&#160;m) wide ditch which itself is 7&#160;ft (2&#160;m) deep. The <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="Motte-and-bailey">motte</a> has a flat top with two large and two small mounds, which may be sites of towers, at the edge.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Blue_Lias" title="Blue Lias">blue lias</a> rubble walling is the only visible structural remains of the castle, which stand on a conical earthwork with a ditch approximately 820&#160;ft (250&#160;m) in circumference.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The castle was destroyed in the 15th century, which may have been as a penalty for the local <a href="/wiki/James_Tuchet,_7th_Baron_Audley" title="James Tuchet, 7th Baron Audley">Lord Audley\'s</a> involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Cornish_Uprising_of_1497" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Cornish Uprising of 1497">Second Cornish Uprising of 1497</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Perkin_Warbeck" title="Perkin Warbeck">Perkin Warbeck</a> against the taxes of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Some of the stone was used in the building of Stowey Court in the village.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There was very little action on the Quantocks during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>.  Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Stawell" title="John Stawell">John Stawell</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cothelstone" title="Cothelstone">Cothelstone</a> was a leading Royalist. When <a href="/wiki/Taunton" title="Taunton">Taunton</a> fell to parliamentary troops and was held by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Blake_(admiral)" title="Robert Blake (admiral)">Robert Blake</a>, he attacked Stawell at <a href="/wiki/Bishops_Lydeard" title="Bishops Lydeard">Bishops Lydeard</a> and imprisoned him. After the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">restoration</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> conferred the title of <a href="/wiki/Baron_Stawell" title="Baron Stawell">Baron Stawell</a> on Blake\'s son Ralph.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A group of <a href="/wiki/Clubmen" title="Clubmen">Clubmen</a> met at Triscombe in 1645 and petitioned parliament calling for peace through negotiation.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of the <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_Rebellion" title="Monmouth Rebellion">Monmouth Rebellion</a> of 1685, (also known as the Pitchfork Rebellion), many participants were executed in the Quantocks. The rebellion was an attempt to overthrow the <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_monarchs" title="List of English monarchs">King of England</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II</a>, who became king when his elder brother, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, died on 6 February 1685. James II was unpopular because he was <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>, and many people were opposed to a "<a href="/wiki/Papist" title="Papist">papist</a>" king. <a href="/wiki/James_Scott,_1st_Duke_of_Monmouth" title="James Scott, 1st Duke of Monmouth">James Scott, 1st Duke of Monmouth</a>, claimed to be rightful heir to the throne and attempted to displace James II.<sup id="cite_ref-foard_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foard-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The rebellion ended with the defeat of Monmouth\'s forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sedgemoor" title="Battle of Sedgemoor">Battle of Sedgemoor</a> on 6 July 1685. Monmouth was <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">executed</a> for <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a> on 15 July,<sup id="cite_ref-foard_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foard-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and many of his supporters were executed, including some by hanging at Nether Stowey and Cothelstone,<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transported</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Assizes" title="Bloody Assizes">Bloody Assizes</a> of <a href="/wiki/George_Jeffreys,_1st_Baron_Jeffreys" title="George Jeffreys, 1st Baron Jeffreys">Judge Jeffreys</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dodington,_Somerset" class="mw-redirect" title="Dodington, Somerset">Dodington</a> was the site of the Buckingham Mine where <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> was extracted.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The mine was established before 1725 and followed earlier exploration at Perry Hill, <a href="/wiki/East_Quantoxhead" title="East Quantoxhead">East Quantoxhead</a>. It was financed by the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Buckingham" title="Duke of Buckingham">Marquis of Buckingham</a> until 1801 when it was closed, until various attempts were made to reopen it during the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Stone building with square tower. In the foreground are stone crosses, gravestones and trees." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg/220px-Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg/330px-Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg/440px-Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crowcombe_church_and_cross.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Crowcombe" title="Crowcombe">Crowcombe</a> Church</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1724 the 14th century spire of the Church of the Holy Ghost in Crowcombe was damaged by a lightning strike. The top section of the spire was removed and is now planted in the churchyard,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and stone from the spire was used in the flooring of the church. Inside the church, carved bench-ends dating from 1534<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> depict such pagan subjects as the <a href="/wiki/Green_Man" title="Green Man">Green Man</a> and the legend of the men of Crowcombe fighting a two-headed <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">dragon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Norton Fitzwarren was the site of a boat lift on the now unused section of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Western_Canal" title="Grand Western Canal">Grand Western Canal</a> from 1839 to 1867. A 300-person prisoner of war camp built here during World War II housed Italian prisoners from the <a href="/wiki/Western_Desert_Campaign" title="Western Desert Campaign">Western Desert Campaign</a> and German prisoners from the <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Normandy" title="Invasion of Normandy">Battle of Normandy</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Walking_routes">Walking routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Walking routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Poet <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a> lived in <a href="/wiki/Nether_Stowey" title="Nether Stowey">Nether Stowey</a> in the Quantocks from 1797 to 1799. In his memory a footpath, The <a href="/wiki/Coleridge_Way" title="Coleridge Way">Coleridge Way</a>, was set up by the <a href="/wiki/Exmoor" title="Exmoor">Exmoor</a> park authorities. The 36-mile (58&#160;km) route begins in Nether Stowey and crosses the Quantocks, the Brendon Hills and Exmoor before finishing in <a href="/wiki/Porlock" title="Porlock">Porlock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Quantock_Greenway" title="Quantock Greenway">Quantock Greenway</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Trail" title="Trail">footpath</a> that opened in 2001. The route of the path follows a figure of eight centred on <a href="/wiki/Triscombe" class="mw-redirect" title="Triscombe">Triscombe</a>. The northern loop, taking in <a href="/wiki/Crowcombe" title="Crowcombe">Crowcombe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holford" title="Holford">Holford</a>, is 19 miles (31&#160;km) long, and the southern loop to <a href="/wiki/Broomfield,_Somerset" title="Broomfield, Somerset">Broomfield</a> extends for 18 miles (29&#160;km). The path travels through many types of landscape, including deciduous and coniferous woodland, private parkland, grazed pasture and cropped fields.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Macmillan_Way_West" title="Macmillan Way West">Macmillan Way West</a> follows the Quantocks ridge for several miles.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantock_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Quantock Hills were designated as an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) in 1956, the first such designation in England under the <a href="/wiki/National_Parks_and_Access_to_the_Countryside_Act_1949" title="National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949">National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Somerset_County_Council_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Somerset_County_Council-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Notice of the intention to create the AONB under <i>The Quantock Hills Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty (Designation) Order, 1956</i> was published in the <a href="/wiki/London_Gazette" class="mw-redirect" title="London Gazette">London Gazette</a> on 7 February 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since responsibility for the Quantock AONB is shared between the County Council and three District Councils The Quantock Hills Joint Advisory Committee was set up in 1973. The JAC represents County, District and Parish councils along with representatives from <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_Quantock" title="Friends of Quantock">Friends of Quantock</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Country_Land_and_Business_Association" title="Country Land and Business Association">Country Land and Business Association</a> (CLA), Somerset Local Access Forum,\nthe Quantock Commoners Association, the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Small_Businesses" title="Federation of Small Businesses">Federation of Small Businesses</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="National Trust">National Trust</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Farmers%27_Union_of_England_and_Wales" title="National Farmers&#39; Union of England and Wales">National Farmers Union</a>. The series, which began in 2009, is known as <b>Music on the Quantocks</b>. Run by local volunteers, it has featured some of the world\'s finest musicians, including <a href="/wiki/Sir_James_Galway" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir James Galway">Sir James Galway</a>, The Sixteen, <a href="/wiki/The_Hilliard_Ensemble" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hilliard Ensemble">The Hilliard Ensemble</a>, violinist Tasmin Little and also poets Roger McGough and <a href="/wiki/John_Cooper_Clarke" title="John Cooper Clarke">John Cooper Clarke</a>. The series begins each spring and continues throughout the year culminating at Christmas with a performance by a  choir at <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Mary,_Bishops_Lydeard" class="mw-redirect" title="Church of St Mary, Bishops Lydeard">St Mary\'s, Bishops Lydeard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the most popular <a href="/wiki/Coleridge_Cottage" title="Coleridge Cottage">Coleridge Cottage</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Cottage" title="Cottage">cottage</a> situated in <a href="/wiki/Nether_Stowey" title="Nether Stowey">Nether Stowey</a>. It was constructed in the 17th century as a building containing a parlour, kitchen and service room on the ground floor and three corresponding bed chambers above.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> It has been designated by <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> as a grade II* <a Moore\'s Coleridge Cottage Inn, the building was acquired for the nation in 1908, and the following year it was handed over to the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" The line then turns inland to Washford, and returns to the coast for the run to <a href="/wiki/Minehead" title="Minehead">Minehead</a>. At 23 miles (37&#160;km), it is the longest privately owned passenger rail line in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Halsway_Manor" title="Halsway Manor">Halsway Manor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Halsway" class="mw-redirect" title="Halsway">Halsway</a>, is now used as England\'s National Centre for Traditional Music, Dance and Song. It is the only residential <a href="/wiki/Folk_music_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Folk music of England">folk</a> centre in the UK. The eastern end of the building dates from the 15th century and the western end was a 19th-century addition.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> The manor, which is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a>, was at one time used by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Beaufort" title="Henry Beaufort">Cardinal Beaufort</a> as a hunting lodge and thereafter as a family home until the mid-1960s<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> when it became the folk music centre. It has been designated by English Heritage as a grade II* listed building.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Halswell_House" title="Halswell House">Halswell House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Goathurst" title="Goathurst">Goathurst</a> has Tudor origins but was purchased by the Tynte family and rebuilt in 1689.<sup id="cite_ref-waite_6-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waite-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The surrounding park and 17 acres (6.9&#160;ha) pleasure garden was developed between 1745 and 1785. The grounds contain many fish ponds, cascades, bridges and fanciful buildings,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> including the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Harmony" title="Temple of Harmony">Temple of Harmony</a>, which stands in Mill Wood<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> and has
