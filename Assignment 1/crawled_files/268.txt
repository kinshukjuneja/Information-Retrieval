class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Falmouth,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Miss_Susan_Gay%27s_Falmouth_chronology" title="Miss Susan Gay&#39;s Falmouth chronology">Miss Susan Gay\'s Falmouth chronology</a></div>\n<p>The name Falmouth is of <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> origin. (Present-day speakers of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> use <i>Aberfal</i> or <i>Aberfala</i> based on <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> precedents.) It is claimed that an earlier Celtic name for the place was <i>Peny-cwm-cuic</i> (which translates to English as \'head of the creek\') which is the same as the anglicised "Pennycomequick" district in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Falmouth,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG/220px-King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG/330px-King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG/440px-King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King_Charles_Church_Falmouth.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Church_of_King_Charles_the_Martyr,_Falmouth" title="Church of King Charles the Martyr, Falmouth">Falmouth Parish Church</a>, Church Street, dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">"King Charles the Martyr"</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Falmouth was where <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> built <a href="/wiki/Pendennis_Castle" title="Pendennis Castle">Pendennis Castle</a> to defend <a href="/wiki/Carrick_Roads" title="Carrick Roads">Carrick Roads</a> in 1540. The main town of the district was then at <a href="/wiki/Penryn,_Cornwall" title="Penryn, Cornwall">Penryn</a>. Sir John Killigrew created the town of Falmouth shortly after 1613.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the late 16th century, under threat from the Spanish Armada, the defences at Pendennis were strengthened by the building of angled ramparts. During the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a>, Pendennis Castle was the second to last fort to surrender to the Parliamentary Army.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG/170px-Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG/255px-Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG/340px-Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1224" data-file-height="1632" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Killigrew_monument_Falmouth.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Killigrew monument in Arwenack Street</div></div></div>\n<p>After the Civil War, Sir <a href="/wiki/Peter_Killigrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Killigrew">Peter Killigrew</a> received royal patronage when he gave land for the building of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_King_Charles_the_Martyr,_Falmouth" title="Church of King Charles the Martyr, Falmouth">Church of King Charles the Martyr</a>, dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I, "the Martyr"</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The seal of Falmouth was blazoned as "An eagle displayed with two heads and on each wing with a tower" (based on the arms of Killigrew). The arms of the borough of Falmouth were "Arg[ent]. a double-headed eagle displayed Sa[ble]. each wing charged with a tower Or. in base issuant from the water barry wavy a rock also Sa. thereon surmounting the tail of the eagle a staff also proper flying therefrom a pennant Gu[les]".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Falmouth <a href="/wiki/Post_Office_Packet_Service" title="Post Office Packet Service">Packet Service</a> operated out of Falmouth for over 160 years between 1689 and 1851. Its purpose was to carry mail to and from Britain\'s growing <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">empire</a>. As the most south-westerly good harbour in Great Britain, Falmouth was often the first port for returning <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> ships. Captain John Bullock who worked in the Packet Service built <a href="/wiki/Penmere_Manor_Hotel,_Cornwall" title="Penmere Manor Hotel, Cornwall">Penmere Manor</a> in 1825\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_and_20th_centuries">19th and 20th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Falmouth,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 19th and 20th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Falmouth_Lifeboat_(DSCN0324).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Falmouth_Lifeboat_%28DSCN0324%29.jpg/220px-Falmouth_Lifeboat_%28DSCN0324%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Falmouth_Lifeboat_%28DSCN0324%29.jpg/330px-Falmouth_Lifeboat_%28DSCN0324%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Falmouth_Lifeboat_%28DSCN0324%29.jpg/440px-Falmouth_Lifeboat_%28DSCN0324%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1632" data-file-height="1224" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Falmouth_Lifeboat_(DSCN0324).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_Lifeboat_Station" title="Falmouth Lifeboat Station">Falmouth Lifeboat</a> moored by the docks with the old town and The Penryn River in the background</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1805 news of Britain\'s victory and Admiral Nelson\'s death at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Trafalgar</a> was landed here from the schooner <i>Pickle</i> and taken to London by stagecoach. On 2 October 1836 <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS&#160;<i>Beagle</i></a> anchored at Falmouth at the end of her <a href="/wiki/Second_voyage_of_HMS_Beagle" title="Second voyage of HMS Beagle">noted survey voyage</a> around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> That evening, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> left the ship and took the <a href="/wiki/Mail_coach" title="Mail coach">Mail coach</a> to his family home at <a href="/wiki/The_Mount,_Shrewsbury" title="The Mount, Shrewsbury">The Mount, Shrewsbury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The ship stayed a few days and Captain <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a> visited the <a href="/wiki/Fox_family_of_Falmouth" title="Fox family of Falmouth">Fox family</a> at nearby <a href="/w/index.php?title=Penjerrick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Penjerrick (page does not exist)">Penjerrick</a> Gardens. Darwin\'s shipmate Sulivan later made his home in the nearby waterside village of <a href="/wiki/Flushing,_Cornwall" title="Flushing, Cornwall">Flushing</a>, then home to many naval officers.\n</p><p>In 1839 Falmouth was the scene of a <a href="/wiki/Gold_dust_robbery" title="Gold dust robbery">gold dust robbery</a> when \xc2\xa347,600 worth of gold dust from Brazil was stolen on arrival at the port.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_Docks" title="Falmouth Docks">Falmouth Docks</a> were developed from 1858,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution" title="Royal National Lifeboat Institution">Royal National Lifeboat Institution</a> (RNLI) opened <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_Lifeboat_Station" title="Falmouth Lifeboat Station">Falmouth Lifeboat Station</a> nearby in 1867. The present building dates from 1993 and also houses <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Coastguard" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Coastguard">Her Majesty\'s Coastguard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The RNLI operates two <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboats</a> from Falmouth: <i>Richard Cox Scott</i>, a 17-metre (56&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Severn-class_lifeboat" title="Severn-class lifeboat">Severn-class</a> all-weather boat, and <i>Eve Park</i>, an <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_75-class_lifeboat" title="Atlantic 75-class lifeboat">Atlantic 75</a> inshore lifeboat.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Near the town centre is Kimberley Park. The land pre-dates 1877,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and is named after the Earl of Kimberley who leased the park\'s land to the borough of Falmouth. Today the park has exotic and ornate plants and trees.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway" title="Cornwall Railway">Cornwall Railway</a> reached Falmouth on 24 August 1863. The railway brought new prosperity to Falmouth, as it made it easy for tourists to reach the town. It also allowed the swift transport of the goods recently disembarked from the ships in the port. The town now has three railway stations. <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_Docks_railway_station" title="Falmouth Docks railway station">Falmouth Docks railway station</a> is the original terminus and is close to Pendennis Castle and Gyllyngvase beach. <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_Town_railway_station" title="Falmouth Town railway station">Falmouth Town railway station</a> was opened on 7 December 1970 and is convenient for the <a href="/wiki/National_Maritime_Museum" title="National Maritime Museum">National Maritime Museum</a> Cornwall, the waterfront, and town centre. <a href="/wiki/Penmere_railway_station" title="Penmere railway station">Penmere railway station</a> opened on 1 July 1925 towards the north of Falmouth and within easy walking distance of the top of The Moor. All three stations are served by regular trains from <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Line" title="Maritime Line">Maritime Line</a>. Penmere Station was renovated in the late 1990s, using the original sign and materials.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, 31 people were killed in Falmouth by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">bombing</a>. It was also the launching point for the <a href="/wiki/St_Nazaire_Raid" title="St Nazaire Raid">noted commando raid</a> on <a href="/wiki/Saint-Nazaire" title="Saint-Nazaire">Saint-Nazaire</a>. An anti-submarine net was laid from Pendennis to St Mawes, to prevent enemy <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boats</a> entering the harbour.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" parish">civil parish</a> within Cornwall, formed in 1974 from the historic Falmouth Borough Council. Falmouth received its Order of Charter in 1661.\n</p><p>As of 2017, it is governed by sixteen councillors (four represent the Boslowick Ward, three each for the Arwenack, Penwerris, Smithick and Trescobeas). Each of them serves a four-year term. The majority of everyday services are provided by <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a> which is a unitary authority governing the entirety of mainland Cornwall. Falmouth elects five councillors to Cornwall Council.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Economy.2C_industry_and_tourism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy,_industry_and_tourism">Economy, industry and tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Falmouth,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy, industry and tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg/220px-Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg/330px-Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg/440px-Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Falmouth_Cornwall_Harbour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Falmouth Harbour, <a href="/wiki/National_Maritime_Museum_Cornwall" title="National Maritime Museum Cornwall">National Maritime Museum, Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pendennis_Castle" title="Pendennis Castle">Pendennis Castle</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>While Falmouth\'s maritime activity has much declined from its heyday, the docks are still a major contributor to the town\'s economy. It is the largest port in Cornwall. Falmouth remains a cargo port and the bunkering of vessels and the transfer of cargoes also keep the port\'s facilities busy. The port is popular with cruise ship operators.\n</p><p>Further up the sheltered reaches of the Fal there are several ships laid up, awaiting sailing orders and/or new owners/charterers.\n</p><p>Falmouth is a popular holiday destination and it is now primarily a tourist resort. The five main beaches starting next to Pendennis Castle and moving along the coast towards the Helford river are Castle, Tunnel, <a href="/wiki/Gyllyngvase" title="Gyllyngvase">Gyllyngvase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swanpool,_Cornwall" title="Swanpool, Cornwall">Swanpool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maenporth" title="Maenporth">Maenporth</a> beaches. The <a href="/wiki/National_Maritime_Museum_Cornwall" title="National Maritime Museum Cornwall">National Maritime Museum Cornwall</a> opened in February 2003. The building was designed by the architect M. J. Long.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Falmouth &amp; Penryn Packet</i>, first published in 1858, is still based in the town as the lead title in a series of <a href="/wiki/Packet_Newspapers" title="Packet Newspapers">Packet Newspapers</a> for central and western <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-britishpapers1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britishpapers1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_West_Briton" title="The West Briton">The West Briton</a> newspaper, first published in 1810, is a weekly tabloid newspaper which has a <i>Falmouth &amp; Penryn</i> edition reporting on the area.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Falmouth,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FalmouthObservatory2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/FalmouthObservatory2.jpg/170px-FalmouthObservatory2.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/FalmouthObservatory2.jpg/255px-FalmouthObservatory2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/FalmouthObservatory2.jpg/340px-FalmouthObservatory2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FalmouthObservatory2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Meteorological Observation Tower, built by the "<a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Polytechnic_Society" title="Royal Cornwall Polytechnic Society">Poly</a>"</div></div></div>\n<p>Falmouth has many literary connections. The town was the birthplace of Toad, Mole and Rat: <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Grahame" title="Kenneth Grahame">Kenneth Grahame</a>\'s classic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wind_in_the_Willows" title="The Wind in the Willows">The Wind in the Willows</a></i> began as a series of letters sent to his son. The first two were written at the Greenbank Hotel whilst Grahame was a guest in May 1907. Reproductions of the letters are currently on display in the hotel. <i><a href="/wiki/Poldark" title="Poldark">Poldark</a></i> author <a href="/wiki/Winston_Graham" title="Winston Graham">Winston Graham</a> knew the town well and set his novel <i>The Forgotten Story</i> (1945) in Falmouth.\n</p><p>The town has been the setting for several films and television programmes. British film star <a href="/wiki/Will_Hay" title="Will Hay">Will Hay</a> was a familiar face in Falmouth in 1935 whilst filming his comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Windbag_the_Sailor" title="Windbag the Sailor">Windbag the Sailor</a></i>.  The film had many scenes of the docks area. The docks area was featured in some scenes with <a href="/wiki/John_Mills" title="John Mills">John Mills</a> for the 1948 film <i><a href="/wiki/Scott_of_the_Antarctic_(film)" title="Scott of the Antarctic (film)">Scott of the Antarctic</a></i>.  <a href="/wiki/Robert_Newton" title="Robert Newton">Robert Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Driscoll" title="Bobby Driscoll">Bobby Driscoll</a> and other cast members of the 1950 Walt Disney film <i><a href="/wiki/Treasure_Island_(1950_film)" title="Treasure Island (1950 film)">Treasure Island</a></i> (some scenes were filmed along the <a href="/wiki/River_Fal" title="River Fal">River Fal</a>) were visitors to the town.  Stars from the BBC TV serial <i><a href="/wiki/The_Onedin_Line" title="The Onedin Line">The Onedin Line</a></i> stayed in the town during filming in the late 1970s.  In 2011 <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> filmed parts of the film <i><a href="/wiki/World_War_Z" title="World War Z">World War Z</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a> in Falmouth Docks and off the coast.\n</p><p>Falmouth has the first "Polytechnic": <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Polytechnic_Society" title="Royal Cornwall Polytechnic Society">Royal Cornwall Polytechnic Society</a> which went into administration briefly in 2010 but is now a feature of the town with frequent art exhibitions, stage performances and an art house cinema.\n</p><p>Falmouth is home to many theatre groups, including Falmouth Theatre Company, Falmouth Young Generation and Amity Theatre. Falmouth Theatre Company, also known as FTC, is the oldest local company with performances dating back to 1927.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Falmouth_Art_Gallery" title="Falmouth Art Gallery">Falmouth Art Gallery</a> is a public gallery with a diverse 19th and 20th century art collection including many notable modern Cornish artists exhibited in four to five seasonal exhibitions a year, as well as a "family friendly and free" community and schools education programme.\n</p><p>The Anglican parish churches are dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Church_of_King_Charles_the_Martyr,_Falmouth" title="Church of King Charles the Martyr, Falmouth">King Charles the Martyr</a> and to <a href="/wiki/All_Saints%27_Church,_Falmouth" title="All Saints&#39; Church, Falmouth">All Saints</a>. A third church is <a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Church,_Penwerris" class="mw-redirect" title="St Michael&#39;s Church, Penwerris">St Michael\'s Church, Penwerris</a>. The Roman Catholic church of St Mary Immaculate is in Killigrew Street. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/J._A._Hansom" class="mw-redirect" title="J. A. Hansom">J. A. Hansom</a> and built in 1868; the tower and spire (1881) are by J. S. Hansom; the baptistery and porch were added in 1908 to the original designs. The style is a blend of Gothic and Burgundian Romanesque, creating a very French effect. Two of the stained glass windows are early works of <a href="/wiki/Dom_Charles_Norris" title="Dom Charles Norris">Dom Charles Norris</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Falmouth Methodist Church is also in Killigrew Street; the street fa\xc3\xa7ade is "one of the grandest expressions of Methodism in Cornwall". The United Reformed Church (originally Bible Christian) is in Berkeley Vale. The former synagogue (1816) is one of the earliest surviving synagogues in England; it was in use until 1879.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Falmouth has its own community radio station <a href="/wiki/Source_fm" class="mw-redirect" title="Source fm">Source fm</a> broadcasting on 96.1 FM and online.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016, Falmouth won the "Great British High Street 2016" award, in the \'Coastal Community\' category.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Falmouth,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg/220px-J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg/330px-J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg/440px-J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:J79154_Yul_cdg_20140624-044343.15_FalmouthAngleterre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view of Falmouth: Penryn River centre left; part of Carrick Roads top; part of Falmouth Bay right</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Falmouth_harbour">Falmouth harbour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Falmouth,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Falmouth harbour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Falmouth is famous for its harbour.  Together with <a href="/wiki/Carrick_Roads" title="Carrick Roads">Carrick Roads</a>, it forms the third deepest natural harbour in the world, and the deepest in Western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It has been the start or finish point of various round-the-world record-breaking voyages, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Robin_Knox-Johnston" title="Robin Knox-Johnston">Robin Knox-Johnston</a> and Dame <a href="/wiki/Ellen_MacArthur" title="Ellen MacArthur">Ellen MacArthur</a>.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> had a large base in Falmouth harbour as well as an army base in the town.  Some of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/D-day" class="mw-redirect" title="D-day">D-day</a> landings originated from Falmouth harbour and the surrounding rivers and creeks.\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/SS_Flying_Enterprise" title="SS Flying Enterprise">SS Flying Enterprise</a></i>, a cargo vessel that had sailed from <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> on 21 December 1951, ran into a storm on the Western Approaches to the English Channel. A crack appeared on her deck and the cargo shifted. A number of vessels went to her aid including the tug <i>Turmoil</i> which was stationed in Falmouth, but they found it impossible to take the <i>Flying Enterprise</i> in tow. The ship was finally taken in tow on 5 January 1952 by the <i>Turmoil</i> when she was some 300 nautical miles (560&#160;km) from Falmouth. It took several days to reach port. On 10 January the tow line parted when the ship was still 41 nautical miles (76&#160;km) from Falmouth. Two other tugs joined the battle to save the ship and cargo, but the <i>Flying Enterprise</i> finally sank later that day. Captain Carlsen and the tug\'s mate Kenneth Dancy, the only crew members still on board, were picked up by the <i>Turmoil</i> and taken to Falmouth to a hero\'s welcome.\n</p>\n<h3><span
