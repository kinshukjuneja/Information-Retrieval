class="toclevel-1 tocsection-26"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">17</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Kammbronn is Cornish for \'crooked hill\'.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The word \'kamm\', crooked, is the same in the Breton language, and the Welsh, Gaelic and Irish Gaelic word is \'kam\',<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>. \'Hill\' in Welsh is \'Bryn\'.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Camborne is in the western part of the largest urban and industrial area in Cornwall with the town of <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">Redruth</a> 3 miles (4.8&#160;km) to the east. It is the ecclesiastical centre of a large <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> and has a <a href="/wiki/Town_council" title="Town council">town council</a>. Camborne-Redruth is on the northern side of the <a href="/wiki/Carn_Brea,_Redruth" title="Carn Brea, Redruth">Carn Brea</a>/<a href="/wiki/Carnmenellis" title="Carnmenellis">Carnmenellis</a> granite upland which slopes northwards to the sea. The two towns are linked by the A3047 road which was <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">turnpiked</a> in 1839 and the villages along the road (from the west) were Roskear, Tuckingmill, Pool and Illogan. Running north-south are a number of small streams with narrow river valleys which have been deeply-cut following centuries of <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor_tin-mining#Streaming" title="Dartmoor tin-mining">tin streaming</a> and other industrial processes. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Red_River_(Koner)" title="Red River (Koner)">Red River</a> valley which crosses the A3047 at Tuckingmill. To the north, the <a href="/wiki/A30_(road)" class="mw-redirect" title="A30 (road)">A30 (road)</a> forms a boundary between the urban area and the agricultural land on the other side.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cahill_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cahill-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first mention of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> Camborne churchtown is in 1181 although in 1931 the ruins of a probable <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romano-British</a> <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">villa</a> were found at Magor Farm, Illogan, near Camborne, and excavated that year under the guidance of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_of_Cornwall" title="Royal Institution of Cornwall">Royal Institution of Cornwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cahill_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cahill-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It is the only Roman villa to be found in the whole of Cornwall. There are also early Christian sites such as an inscribed <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a> stone, (now in the Church of <a href="/wiki/Martin_of_Tours" title="Martin of Tours">St Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Meriadoc" class="mw-redirect" title="St Meriadoc">St Meriadoc</a>), and dated to the 10th or 11th centuries, which attests to the existence of a settlement then.<sup id="cite_ref-Elisabeth_Okasha_1993_pp.82-84_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elisabeth_Okasha_1993_pp.82-84-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Langdon (1896) records seven stone crosses in the parish of which two are at Pendarves. By the late Middle Ages <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manorial holdings</a> developed in the surrounding area, and church-paths linked the churchtown to the outlying hamlets. <a href="/wiki/Mystery_play#Miracle_play" title="Mystery play">Cornish medieval mystery plays</a> were held in a <a href="/wiki/Plen-an-gwary" title="Plen-an-gwary">playing place</a> and the chuchyard is said to have had a pilgrimage chapel and holy well. <a href="/wiki/John_Norden" title="John Norden">John Norden</a> visited in 1584 and described Camborne as \xe2\x80\xb3<i>A churche standinge among the barrayne hills\'</i>\xe2\x80\xb3. At this time there would have been moors and rough grazing as well as small fields in the surrounding countryside.<sup id="cite_ref-cahill_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cahill-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 1708 Camborne had rights to hold markets and three fairs a year which may be an indication of tin mining in the area; Camborne\'s was inland and in an unfavourable location for trading. Mining is first recorded locally in the 1400s with early exploitation of the small streams cutting through the mineralised area and from shallow mines following lodes. <a href="/wiki/Adit" title="Adit">Adit mining</a> was first recorded in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-cahill_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cahill-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A sign of increasing industrial activity and increasing industrial population is the first chapel built in 1806 and the development of a local <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> community. In 1823 the population was around 2,000 and in 1841 it was 4,377, with 75 <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">smiths</a> recorded and over two-thirds of the working population employed in the mining industry. In the expanding town <a href="/wiki/Gasworks" title="Gasworks">gasworks</a> were opened in 1834, the <a href="/wiki/Hayle_Railway" title="Hayle Railway">Hayle Railway</a> was built (1834\xe2\x80\x9337) and <a href="/wiki/Holman_Brothers" title="Holman Brothers">Holmans</a> opened a small foundry in 1839.<sup id="cite_ref-cahill_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cahill-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg/220px-Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg" width="220" height="99" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg/330px-Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg/440px-Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1283" data-file-height="579" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tin-mining-cornwall-c1890.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View east from <a href="/wiki/Dolcoath_Mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolcoath Mine">Dolcoath Mine</a>, 1893</div></div></div>\n<p>Camborne is best known as a centre for the former Cornish <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and copper mining industry, having its working heyday during the later 18th and early 19th centuries. Camborne was just a village until transformed by the mining boom which began in the late 18th century and saw the Camborne and Redruth district become the richest mining area in the world. Although a considerable number of ruinous stacks and engine houses remain, they cannot begin to convey the scenes of 150 years ago when scores of mines transfigured the landscape.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harriets.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Harriets.jpg/220px-Harriets.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Harriets.jpg/330px-Harriets.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Harriets.jpg/440px-Harriets.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Harriets.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Harriets Pumping Engine house, part of <a href="/wiki/Dolcoath_Mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolcoath Mine">Dolcoath Mine</a>, built in 1860</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Dolcoath_Mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolcoath Mine">Dolcoath Mine</a>, (English: <i>Old Ground Mine</i>), the \'Queen of Cornish Mines\' was, at a depth of 3,500 feet (1,067 m), for many years the deepest mine in the world, not to mention one of the oldest before its closure in 1921. The <i>last</i> working tin mine in Europe, <a href="/wiki/South_Crofty" title="South Crofty">South Crofty</a>, which closed in 1998, is also to be found in Camborne.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining_related">Mining related</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mining related">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Holmans_drill_1955.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5a/Holmans_drill_1955.jpg/220px-Holmans_drill_1955.jpg" width="220" height="325" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5a/Holmans_drill_1955.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="260" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Holmans_drill_1955.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Holmans Rock Drill from 1955 (taken from the 55 vol. of <a href="/wiki/Camborne_School_of_Mines" title="Camborne School of Mines">CSM</a> Magazine)</div></div></div>\n<p>Apart from the mines themselves, Camborne was also home to many important related industries, including the once world-renowned <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Holman_Bros_Ltd" class="mw-redirect" title="Holman Bros Ltd">Holman Bros Ltd</a> (<a href="/wiki/CompAir" title="CompAir">CompAir</a>). Holmans, a family business founded in 1801, was for generations, Camborne\'s, and indeed Cornwall\'s largest manufacturer of industrial equipment, even making the famous <a href="/wiki/Sten" title="Sten">Sten</a> <a href="/wiki/Submachine_gun" title="Submachine gun">submachine gun</a> for a stint during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Holman_Projector" title="Holman Projector">Holman Projector</a> was used by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. At its height Holmans was spread over three sites within Camborne, employing some three and half thousand men. Despite Britain\'s <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialization" title="Deindustrialization">industrial decline</a>, Compair Holmans Camborne factory finally closed in 2001. On the afternoon of Tuesday 5 December 2006, a wall of the Holmans factory was leaning towards the railway line, as a result the line west of <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a> was closed for the afternoon and night and disrupting railway services, as it was feared the wall could collapse onto the mainline, part of the derelict factory was later demolished that night.\n</p><p>A modest quantity of South Crofty tin was purchased by a local enterprise and this gradually dwindling stock is used to make specialist tin jewellery, branded as the South Crofty Collection. Tin originally mined at South Crofty was used to form the bronze medals awarded in the 2012 London Olympics<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Camborne_School_of_Mines">Camborne School of Mines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Camborne School of Mines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Because of the prior importance of metal mining to the Cornish economy, the <a href="/wiki/Camborne_School_of_Mines" title="Camborne School of Mines">Camborne School of Mines</a> (CSM) developed as the only specialist hard rock education establishment in the United Kingdom, until the <a href="/wiki/Royal_School_of_Mines" title="Royal School of Mines">Royal School of Mines</a> was established in 1851. Plans for the school were laid out in 1829, leading to the current school in 1888. It now forms part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Exeter" title="University of Exeter">University of Exeter</a>; it moved to the University\'s Tremough campus (now known as <a href="/wiki/Penryn_Campus" title="Penryn Campus">Penryn Campus</a>) in 2004. CSM graduates work in the mining industry all over the world. It has a fine collection of minerals in its museum of geology.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_locomotion">Steam locomotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="479" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a title="Puffing Devil">Puffing Devil</a> \xe2\x80\x93 a steam-powered road locomotive built by Camborne engineer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Trevithick" title="Richard Trevithick">Richard Trevithick</a> The events have been turned into a local song:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>Going up Camborne Hill, coming down,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>Going up Camborne Hill, coming down,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>The horses stood still,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>The wheels turn around,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>Going up Camborne Hill, coming down.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Trevithick was born in Penponds, in 1771, a miner\'s son, and was educated at Camborne School. His achievements (not to mention steam power, mining and Cornish culture as a whole) are celebrated every last Saturday of April as the town\'s \'<a href="/w/index.php?title=Trevithick_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trevithick Day (page does not exist)">Trevithick Day</a>\', and by his statue standing outside Camborne Public Library.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_language">Cornish language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cornish language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> was the language of the area around Camborne until the beginning of the 18th century and it is recorded that everyone living west of Truro spoke Cornish in 1644.<sup id="cite_ref-Spriggs,_2003._15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spriggs,_2003.-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Boson" title="Nicholas Boson">Nicholas Boson</a> wrote that Cornish was spoken as far east as Redruth and Falmouth circa 1700.<sup id="cite_ref-Spriggs,_2003._15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spriggs,_2003.-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1700 the pioneering Celtic linguist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lhuyd" title="Edward Lhuyd">Edward Lhuyd</a> came to Cornwall to study the language and visited Camborne, detailing many aspects of the parish.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams,_1993_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams,_1993-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the most important surviving works of medieval Cornish literature is <a href="/wiki/Beunans_Meriasek" title="Beunans Meriasek">Beunans Meriasek</a>, the Life of <a href="/wiki/Meriasek" title="Meriasek">St Meriadoc</a> the patron saint of Camborne.<sup id="cite_ref-Berresford_Ellis,_1974._17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berresford_Ellis,_1974.-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In the 19th century the nickname for Camborne people was Mera-jacks, or Merry-geeks, and those who washed in St Meriasek\'s well were called Merrasicks, Merrasickers, Moragicks or Mearagaks.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball,_1990_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball,_1990-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 20th century several Cornish words and phrases were noted as still in use amongst the inhabitants of Camborne. These include <i>taw tavas</i> (silent tongue) and <i>allycumpoester</i> (all in order).<sup id="cite_ref-Berresford_Ellis,_1974._17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berresford_Ellis,_1974.-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Glossary_of_Celtic_Words_in_Cornish_Dialect_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glossary_of_Celtic_Words_in_Cornish_Dialect-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although a limited amount of Cornish was taught in some schools in west Cornwall during the 19th and early 20th centuries the first school to properly dedicate itself to teaching revived Cornish was the Mount Pleasant House school run by <a href="/wiki/E._G._Retallack_Hooper" title="E. G. Retallack Hooper">E. G. Retallack Hooper</a> in the post-<a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> period.<sup id="cite_ref-Berresford_Ellis,_1974._17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berresford_Ellis,_1974.-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> By 1984 Cornish was being taught in Troon and Camborne primary schools as well as Camborne secondary school and there was a Cornish language playgroup. In 2000 Roskear and Weeth schools were teaching Cornish.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/2011_UK_census" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 UK census">2011 UK census</a>, although there was no specific Cornish language question, thirty people living in the parish of Camborne declared that Cornish was their main language at home, thirteen in Troon and Beacon.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_representation">Parliamentary representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Parliamentary representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Camborne_and_Redruth_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Camborne and Redruth (UK Parliament constituency)">Camborne and Redruth</a> <a href="/wiki/County_constituency" class="mw-redirect" title="County constituency">constituency</a> was created for the <a title="St Meriadoc">St Meriadoc</a>: it is entirely of granite, of 15th century date, but incorporating earlier structural features, including a Norman chevron stone in the west wall of the north aisle found in 2009 and is <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed</a> Grade I. St Martin was added to the original dedication to St Meriadoc in the 15th century. There is a western tower about 60 feet high containing eight bells (with a clock before 1882) and the aisles are identical in design: the building was gutted and restored in 1861-62 and an outer south aisle was added in 1878\xe2\x80\x9379 to a design by <a href="/wiki/James_Piers_St_Aubyn" title="James Piers St Aubyn">James Piers St Aubyn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The church was re-opened on 7 August 1879 by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Benson_(bishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Benson (bishop)">Edward Benson</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Truro" title="Bishop of Truro">Bishop of Truro</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An inscribed <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a> stone found at Chapel Ia, Troon (now set up as the Lady Chapel altar in the parish church), and dated to the 10th or 11th centuries, attests to the existence of a settlement then.<sup id="cite_ref-Elisabeth_Okasha_1993_pp.82-84_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elisabeth_Okasha_1993_pp.82-84-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is inscribed \'Leuiut iusit hec altare pro anima sua\'. The chapel of St Ia was recorded in 1429 and a holy well was nearby. The site was called Fenton-ear (i.e. the well of Ia). The stone is very similar to one now used as the mensa of the Lady Chapel altar at Treslothan Parish Church, formerly used from c.1841 to 1955 as the base for a sundial in the grounds of Pendarves House.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_p._71_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_p._71-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Camborne churchyard contains a number of crosses collected from nearby sites: the finest is one found in a well at Crane in 1896 but already known from <a href="/wiki/William_Borlase" title="William Borlase">William Borlase</a>\'s account of it when it was at Fenton-ear. Arthur Langdon (1896) records six crosses in the parish, including two at <a href="/wiki/Troon,_Cornwall" title="Troon, Cornwall">Pendarves</a>, two at Trevu and one outside the Institute.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two other chapels are known to have existed in the medieval period: one not far from the parish church was dedicated to Our Lady and St Anne and one at Menadarva (derived from Merther-Derwa) was one of Celtic origin dedicated to St Derwa, Virgin, but mentioned in 1429.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_p._71_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_p._71-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30</a> <a href="/wiki/Trunk_road" title="Trunk road">trunk road</a> now by-passes the town around its northern edge. The old A30 through the town has become the A3047. There is a small bus station halfway along and to the south of Trelowarren Street (the main high street), which has featured in tales by Cornish comedian <a href="/wiki/Jethro_(comedian)" title="Jethro (comedian)">Jethro</a>.\n</p><p>The railway station is a half-mile south from the town centre, with a <a href="/wiki/Level_crossing" title="Level crossing">level crossing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Footbridge" title="Footbridge">footbridge</a> at its eastern end. <a href="/wiki/Camborne_railway_station" title="Camborne railway station">Camborne railway station</a> used to be famous for its short platforms, which meant that passengers on main line services between <a href="/wiki/Paddington_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington station">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penzance_railway_station" title="Penzance railway station">Penzance</a> could only board and alight from certain carriages. Partly because of this not all services stopped at Camborne, preferring nearby <a href="/wiki/Redruth_railway_station" title="Redruth railway station">Redruth railway station</a> (which is also classed by <a href="/wiki/First_Great_Western" class="mw-redirect" title="First Great Western">First Great Western</a> (FGW) trains as a short station stop). The platforms have been upgraded but the memory lives on, again partly in stories by the comedian Jethro. Camborne railway station is served by <a href="/wiki/CrossCountry" title="CrossCountry">CrossCountry</a> and FGW trains.\n</p><p>Camborne was, for a quarter of a century, one of the termini of <a href="/wiki/Camborne_and_Redruth_Tramways" title="Camborne and Redruth Tramways">Cornwall\'s only tram service</a>. CPRR is tasked with driving the regeneration of former industrial land, attracting businesses and helping them create sustainable jobs; supporting local business growth ambitions and fostering employment growth through increasing the skills of those in and out of work. To date, as well as working on supporting businesses in the area\xe2\x80\x94especially those in the town centres, CPRR has been engaged in the process of assembling sites, securing agreements with <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_development" title="Real estate development">developers</a> and doing enabling works for major projects such as the east-west link road between Redruth and Camborne.\n</p><p>A challenge faced by CPRR has been to work collaboratively with the owners of the South Crofty mine (which occupies a central position in the Pool regeneration area) to both allow mine development operations to continue and secure the re-development of the wider area around the mine. Stories did appear in the press<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> regarding alleged illegal in-fill of ventilation shafts by CPRR. The truth\xe2\x80\x94that <a href="/wiki/English_Partnerships" title="English Partnerships">English Partnerships</a> had found old unmarked shafts on development sites which were in danger of collapse and made them good with concrete caps (removable if needed later by the mine company)--was lost. CPRR has continued to advance major projects in the area, such as a range of housing and infrastructure schemes, and will help SWERDA and the <a href="/wiki/Homes_and_Communities_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Homes and Communities Agency">Homes and Communities Agency</a> bring these forward shortly. Some of the work of the URC is becoming apparent, with works on the Pool Innovation Centre and the Trevenson Road area both advancing well.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_Regional_Development_Agency">Criticism of the Regional Development Agency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Criticism of the Regional Development Agency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Local MPs have criticised SWERDA for interfering in the private sector, and said there may be ulterior motives. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_George_(politician)" title="Andrew George (politician)">Andrew George</a>, MP for <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="St Ives (UK Parliament constituency)">St Ives</a>, said, "The RDA\xe2\x80\x99s antics are at odds with the claims made to me by the Minister in Parliament and in a letter that the RDA \'will be informed by the outcome of public consultation. I am astounded that a public body can be acting in such a predatory manner. The RDA seems to want to jump in where it is not wanted and yet it doesn\xe2\x80\x99t intervene where it is. There are places like the Union Hotel in Penzance where the owner and local applicants would be grateful if the RDA were able to step in and purchase but the RDA says that it must be market tested first. Yet when they are faced with a Mine where the owners want to do something constructive, the RDA seem keen to intervene. The public sector has a role in supporting the private sector when projects are not able to be self sustaining. Public money and resources should not be used to undermine the efforts of the private sector".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. It has performed on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC Radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Television" title="BBC Television">BBC Television</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Holman_Climax_Male_Voice_Choir" title="Holman Climax Male Voice Choir">Holman Climax Male Voice Choir</a>, based in Camborne, was formed in 1940 by Edgar S. Kessell MBE (1910\xe2\x80\x931981).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ben_Salfield" title="Ben Salfield">Ben Salfield</a> the lutenist lives just outside of the town.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A Church of England National School was built in College Street in 1844 (replaced by a newer building further up the street towards the parish church in 1896-now demolished); in the following year a school for four hundred boys was opened in the Centenary Methodist Chapel and in 1847 the Basset Road British (Methodist) School was opened. A <a href="/wiki/Camborne_School_of_Mines" title="Camborne School of Mines">School of Mines</a> started in 1872 with the <a href="/wiki/Basset_family" title="Basset family">Basset family</a> paying for chemistry laboratories.<sup id="cite_ref-cahill_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cahill-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The town now has a number of schools covering all age ranges, notably the main secondary school, <a href="/wiki/Camborne_Science_and_International_Academy" title="Camborne Science and International Academy">Camborne Science and International Academy</a>, and a campus of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_College" title="Cornwall College">Cornwall College</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction">Fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Alan_M._Kent" title="Alan M. Camborne was twinned with Pachuca at a ceremony in Mexico on 3 July 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The town name inspired the name of <a href="/wiki/Camborne,_New_Zealand" title="Camborne, New Zealand">Camborne, New Zealand</a>, a seaside suburb of <a href="/wiki/Porirua" title="Porirua">Porirua</a> City developed by an investment company headed by an Arthur Cornish.
