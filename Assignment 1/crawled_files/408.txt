role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the disputed use of the English toponym "British Isles". For the confusion between different geopolitical terminology within the British Isles, see <a href="/wiki/Terminology_of_the_British_Isles" title="Terminology of the British Isles">Terminology of the British Isles</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Britain_and_Ireland_satellite_image_bright.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Britain_and_Ireland_satellite_image_bright.png/300px-Britain_and_Ireland_satellite_image_bright.png" width="300" photo: <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> is the island on the left and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> is on the right</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a> usage, the <a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">toponym</a> "<a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>" refers to a <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and adjacent islands.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, the word "<a href="/wiki/British_(adjective)" class="mw-redirect" title="British (adjective)">British</a>" is also an adjective and <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a> referring to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and more historically associated with the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. For this reason, the name British Isles is avoided by some in <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</a>, as such usage could be construed to imply continued <a href="/wiki/Territorial_claim" class="mw-redirect" title="Territorial claim">territorial claims</a> or political overlordship of the Republic of Ireland by the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-reformationBI_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformationBI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Myers_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>More neutral <a href="/wiki/Geographical_renaming" title="Geographical renaming">proposed alternatives</a> the British Isles include "Britain and Ireland",<sup id="cite_ref-reformationBI_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformationBI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-postwarculture_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-postwarculture-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GuardianMOS01_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianMOS01-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> "Atlantic Archipelago",<sup id="cite_ref-Pocock01_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pocock01-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Anglo-Celtic" title="Anglo-Celtic">Anglo-Celtic</a> Isles",<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> the "British-Irish Isles"<sup id="cite_ref-Oakland01_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oakland01-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Islands_of_the_North_Atlantic" title="Islands of the North Atlantic">Islands of the North Atlantic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TLEL_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TLEL-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In documents drawn up jointly between the British and Irish governments, the archipelago is referred to simply as "these islands".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To some, the dispute is partly <a href="/wiki/Semantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Semantic">semantic</a>, and the term is a value-free geographic one, while, to others, it is a <a href="/wiki/Value-laden" class="mw-redirect" title="Value-laden">value-laden</a> political one.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> may also for <a href="/wiki/Political_geography" title="Political geography">geo-political</a> reasons be included in the British Isles, despite not being <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">geographically</a> part of the archipelago.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Law of the United Kingdom">United Kingdom law</a> uses the term <a href="/wiki/British_Islands" title="British Islands">British Islands</a> to refer to the UK, Channel Islands, and <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> as a single collective entity.\n</p><p>An early variant of the term British Isles dates back to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greek</a> times, when they were known as the Pretanic Islands; this however fell into disuse for over a millennium, and was introduced as the British Isles into English in the late 16th or early 17th centuries by English and Welsh writers, whose writings have been described as propaganda and politicised.<sup id="cite_ref-kenm_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kenm-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MayhewGRev_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MayhewGRev-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MayhewBJHS_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MayhewBJHS-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The term became more resisted after the breakup of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a> in 1922.\n</p><p>The names of the archipelago\'s two sovereign states were themselves the subject of a long <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_Irish_state#Name_dispute_with_the_UK" title="Names of the Irish state">dispute between the Irish and British governments</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-18"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perspectives_in_Great_Britain">Perspectives in Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Perspectives in Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In general, the use of the term <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> to refer to the archipelago is common and uncontroversial within <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> at least since the concept of "<a href="/wiki/Britishness" title="Britishness">Britishness</a>" was gradually accepted in Britain after the 1707 Act of Union. In Britain it is commonly understood as being a politically neutral geographical term, although it is sometimes used to refer to the UK or Great Britain alone.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2003, Irish newspapers reported a British Government internal briefing that advised against the use of "British Isles".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> There is evidence that its use has been increasingly avoided in recent years in fields like cartography and in some academic work, such as <a href="/wiki/Norman_Davies" title="Norman Davies">Norman Davies</a><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s history of Britain and Ireland <i><a href="/wiki/The_Isles:_A_History" title="The Isles: A History">The Isles: A History</a></i>. As a purely geographical term in technical contexts (such as geology and natural history), there is less evidence of alternative terms being chosen.  Recent histories of Great Britain and Ireland (published by major British academic publishers such as the Oxford and Cambridge University presses) have discussed the acceptability of the term "British Isles" in Ireland, although one study continues to use the term "for convenience".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Recognition of issues with the term (as well as problems over definitions and terminology) was also discussed by the columnist <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Berlins" title="Marcel Berlins">Marcel Berlins</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> in 2006. Beginning with "At last, someone has had the sense to abolish the British Isles", he opines that "although purely a geographical definition, it is frequently mixed up with the political entities Great Britain, or the United Kingdom. Even when used geographically, its exact scope is widely misunderstood". He also acknowledges that some view the term as representing Britain\'s imperial past, when it ruled the whole of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Berlins_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berlins-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Another historian of British and Irish history has described the term as "politically loaded".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perspectives_in_Ireland">Perspectives in Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Perspectives in Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_of_Ireland">Republic of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Republic of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From the Irish perspective, some consider "The British Isles" as a political term rather than a geographical name for the archipelago. The term "British" had also never applied to Ireland until at least the late 16th century<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and onwards. This period coincided with the Tudor conquest of Ireland, the subsequent Cromwellian activities in Ireland, the Williamite accession in Britain and the <a href="/wiki/Williamite_War_in_Ireland" title="Williamite War in Ireland">Williamite War in Ireland</a>\xe2\x80\x94all of which resulted in severe impact on the Irish people, landowners and native aristocracy. From that perspective, the term "British Isles" is not a neutral geographical term but an unavoidably political one. Use of the name "British Isles" is often rejected in the Republic of Ireland,  because some claim its use implies a primacy of British identity over all the islands outside the United Kingdom, including the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Irish state</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> that was historically dominating and is currently inaccurate, since Ireland is neither Britain nor British.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many political bodies, including the Irish government, avoid describing Ireland as being part of the British Isles; <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a> corrected <a href="/wiki/John_Gunther" title="John Gunther">John Gunther</a> when the journalist used the term during a private meeting in the mid-1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-gunther1940_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gunther1940-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> However, the term "British Isles" has been used by individual ministers, as did cabinet minister <a href="/wiki/S%C3%ADle_de_Valera" title="S\xc3\xadle de Valera">S\xc3\xadle de Valera</a> when delivering a speech including the term at the opening of a drama festival in 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and is used by government departments in relation to geographic topics.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The term was formally disavowed in September 2005 by the Irish Government when Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Dermot_Ahern" title="Dermot Ahern">Dermot Ahern</a> stated: "The British Isles is not an officially recognised term in any legal or inter-governmental sense. It is without any official status. The Government, including the Department of Foreign Affairs, does not use this term."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>"British Isles" has been used in a geographical sense in Irish parliamentary debates by government ministers,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> although it is often used in a way that defines the British Isles as excluding the Republic of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In October 2006, Irish educational publisher Folens announced that it was removing the term from its popular school atlas effective in January 2007. The decision was made after the issue was raised by a geography teacher. Folens stated that no parent had complained directly to them over the use of "British Isles" and that they had a policy of acting proactively, upon the appearance of a "potential problem".<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> This attracted press attention in the UK and Ireland, during which a spokesman for the Irish Embassy in London said, "\'The British Isles\' has a dated ring to it, as if we are still part of the Empire".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> A bilingual dictionary website maintained by <a href="/wiki/Foras_na_Gaeilge" title="Foras na Gaeilge">Foras na Gaeilge</a> translates "British Isles" into Irish as <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">\xc3\x89ire agus an Bhreatain Mh\xc3\xb3r</i> "Ireland and Great Britain".<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Different views on terminology are probably most clearly seen in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> (which covers six of the thirty-two counties in Ireland), where the political situation is difficult and national identity contested.  A survey in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> found that <a href="/wiki/Unionism_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Unionism (Ireland)">Unionists</a> generally considered the British Isles to be a natural geographical entity, considering themselves British and are mainly descendants of British settlers in Ireland. Another survey highlighted the British and Irish identity of the Protestant community, showing that 51% of Protestants felt "not at all Irish" and 41% only "weakly Irish".<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Nationalists</a> considered their community to be that of the Irish nation\xe2\x80\x94a distinct cultural and political community extending across the whole of Ireland. Identities were diverse and multi-layered, and Irishness was a highly contested identity; Nationalists expressed difficulty in understanding Unionist descriptions of Britishness.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The overall opinion of the Northern Irish people about the term (like the opinion of those in the Republic of Ireland and Great Britain) has never been formally gauged. Politicians from the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Unionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Unionist">Irish Unionist</a> tradition readily use the term "British Isles";<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> the contrast between Unionist and Nationalist approaches to the term was shown in December 1999 at a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Irish Cabinet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> in <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a>. The <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="First Minister of Northern Ireland">First Minister of Northern Ireland</a>, David Trimble, told the meeting:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>This represents the Irish government coming back into a relationship with the rest of the British Isles. We are ending the cold war that has divided not just Ireland but the British Isles. That division is now going to be transformed into a situation where all parts work together again in a way that respects each other.<sup id="cite_ref-Irish_Independent_1999-12-14_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_Independent_1999-12-14-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In contrast, the <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a>, did not use the term in his address to the meeting.<sup id="cite_ref-Irish_Independent_1999-12-14_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_Independent_1999-12-14-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At a gathering of the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Inter-Parliamentary_Body" class="mw-redirect" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Inter-Parliamentary Body">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Inter-Parliamentary Body</a> in 1998, sensitivity about the term became an issue. Referring to plans for the proposed <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Council" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council</a> (supported by both Nationalists and Unionists), British MP for Falkirk West <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Canavan" title="Dennis Canavan">Dennis Canavan</a> was paraphrased by official note-takers as having said in a caveat:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>He understood that the concept of a Council of the Isles had been put forward by the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster Unionist">Ulster Unionists</a> and was referred to as a "Council for the British Isles" by <a href="/wiki/David_Trimble" title="David Trimble">David Trimble</a>. This would cause offence to Irish colleagues; he suggested as an acronym IONA-Islands of the North Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In a series of documents issued by the United Kingdom and Ireland, from the <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street_Declaration" title="Downing Street Declaration">Downing Street Declaration</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> (Belfast Agreement), relations in the British Isles were referred to as the "East\xe2\x80\x93West strand" of the tripartite relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="More_alternative_terms">More alternative terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: More alternative terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="rquote pullquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">\n\n<tbody><tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">\n<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">\xe2\x80\x9c\n</td>\n<td style="padding:0 10px;">And [de Valera] set out to explain the difference between Ireland and the "British Isles." Some moments later, having again necessity to describe my field of operations, I sought a phrase and said, after a slight pause, "a group of islands in the northern part of Europe." Mr. de Valera sat back and laughed heartily.\n</td>\n<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">\xe2\x80\x9d\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:normal; text-align:right;"><cite>\xe2\x80\x94&#160;John Gunther<sup id="cite_ref-gunther1940_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gunther1940-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></cite>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>There are several terms used as alternatives for the term "British Isles" other than those cited above:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Isles_and_Ireland">British Isles and Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: British Isles and Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A term sometimes used is "British Isles and Ireland".  Similar to "Great Britain and Ireland", this has been used in a variety of contexts\xe2\x80\x94among others religious,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> medical,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> zoologic,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> academic<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> and others. This form is also used in some book titles<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> and legal publications.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Islands_of_the_North_Atlantic_.28or_IONA.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Islands_of_the_North_Atlantic_(or_IONA)">Islands of the North Atlantic (or IONA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Islands of the North Atlantic (or IONA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the context of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a>, the term "<a href="/wiki/Islands_of_the_North_Atlantic" title="Islands of the North Atlantic">Islands of the North Atlantic</a>" (and its acronym, IONA) was a term created by then-<a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> MP <a href="/wiki/John_Biggs-Davison" title="John Biggs-Davison">Sir John Biggs-Davison</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> It has been used as a term to denote either all the islands, or the two main islands, without referring to the two states.\n</p><p>IONA has been used by (among others) the former Irish <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> (Prime Minister), <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a>:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Government are, of course, conscious of the emphasis that is laid on the East-West dimension by Unionists, and we are, ourselves, very mindful of the unique relationships that exist within these islands \xe2\x80\x93 islands of the North Atlantic or IONA as some have termed them.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Others have interpreted the term more narrowly to mean the "Council of the Isles" or "<a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Council">British-Irish Council</a>".  <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Luff" title="Peter Luff">Peter Luff</a> told the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">British House of Commons</a> in 1998 that\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In the same context, there will be a council of the isles. I think that some people are calling it IONA \xe2\x80\x93 the islands of the north Atlantic, from which England, by definition, will be excluded.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>His interpretation is not widely shared, particularly in Ireland.  In 1997 the leader of the Irish <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Ireland)" title="Green Party (Ireland)">Green Party</a> <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Sargent" title="Trevor Sargent">Trevor Sargent</a>, discussing the <i>Strand Three</i> (or <i>East\xe2\x80\x93West</i>) talks between the Republic of Ireland and the United Kingdom, commented in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann">D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann</a> (the Irish House of Representatives):\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I noted with interest the naming of the islands of the north Atlantic under the acronym IONA which the Green Party felt was extremely appropriate.<sup id="cite_ref-D\xc3\xa1ilDebates_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D\xc3\xa1ilDebates-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>His comments were echoed by <a href="/wiki/Proinsias_De_Rossa" title="Proinsias De Rossa">Proinsias De Rossa</a>, then leader of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Left_(Ireland)" title="Democratic Left (Ireland)">Democratic Left</a> and later President of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Ireland)" title="Labour Party (Ireland)">Irish Labour Party</a>, who told the D\xc3\xa1il, "The acronym IONA is a useful way of addressing the coming together of these two islands."<sup id="cite_ref-D\xc3\xa1ilDebates_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D\xc3\xa1ilDebates-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This name is ambiguous, because of the other islands in the North Atlantic which have never been considered part of the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northwest_European_Archipelago">Northwest European Archipelago</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Northwest European Archipelago">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some academics in the 1990s and early 2000s also used the term "Northwest European archipelago";<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> however, its use appears sporadically in historiography and rare outside it.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_European_Isles">West European Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: West European Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The name "West European Isles" is one translation of the islands\' name in the <a href="/wiki/Goidelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Goidelic">Gaelic</a> languages of Irish<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> with equivalent terms for "British Isle".<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> In Irish, <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">\xc3\x89ire agus an Bhreatain Mh\xc3\xb3r</i> (literally "Ireland and Great Britain") is the more common term.<sup id="cite_ref-tearma_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tearma-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A somewhat similar usage exists in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. "Westman" is the Icelandic name for a person from the Gaelic areas of Britain and Ireland (Ireland, Scotland and the Isle of Man), and "the Western Lands" is the translation of the name for the islands in Icelandic.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pretanic_Isles">Pretanic Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pretanic Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A return to the Greek term "Pretan(n)ic Isles" has been suggested and has seen some use in academic contexts, particularly in reference to the islands in the pre-Roman era.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insular">Insular</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Insular">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insular_script" title="Insular script">Insular script</a> are uncontroversial terms in art history and <a href="/wiki/Palaeography" title="Palaeography">palaeography</a> for the early medieval art and writing of all the islands.  <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Insular Celtic">Insular Celtic</a> is a similar term in linguistics. However, this adjective is used only in relation to artefacts originating over a thousand years ago.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_the_islands_through_the_ages">Names of the islands through the ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Names of the islands through the ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Terminology_of_the_British_Isles" title="Terminology of the British Isles">Terminology of the British Isles</a></div>\n<p>In classical times, several <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> geographers used derivatives of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a>\' term <i>Pretani</i> (such as "Brit-" or "Prit-" with various endings) to refer to the islands northwest of the European mainland; several included islands not currently viewed as part of the "British Isles"\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">Thule</a>, for example. During the Roman era, the word "<a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>" came to mean the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman province of Britain">Roman province of Britain</a> in particular.\n</p><p>Other early classical geographers (and native sources in the post-Roman period) used the general term <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">oceani insulae</i>, which meant "islands of the ocean". Great Britain was called "Britannia"; Ireland was known as "Hibernia" and, between about the 5th and 11th centuries, "<a href="/wiki/Scotia" title="Scotia">Scotia</a>". The <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a> ("Orcades") and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> were typically also included in descriptions of the islands. No collective term for the islands was used other than "islands of the Ocean".\n</p><p>The Latin version of "British Isles" came to be used again in Europe with the rediscovery of Ptolemy\'s Geographica in the 14th Century.  By the middle of the 16th Century the term appears on maps made by geographers including George Lily<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> and Sebastien Munster<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>.\n</p><p>The term "British Isles" entered the English language in the late 16th century to refer to Great Britain, Ireland and the surrounding islands.  In general, the modern notion of "Britishness" evolved after the 1707 Act of Union.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> While it is probably the most common term for the islands, this use is not universally accepted and is generally rejected in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Pocock01_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pocock01-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pretanic_Islands_and_Britanniae">Pretanic Islands and Britanniae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pretanic Islands and Britanniae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The earliest known names for the islands come from <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> writings. Sources included the <a href="/wiki/Massaliote_Periplus" title="Massaliote Periplus">Massaliote Periplus</a> (a merchants\' handbook from around 500 BC describing sea routes)<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> and the travel writings of the Greek, <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a>, from around 320 BC. Although the earliest texts have been lost, excerpts were quoted or paraphrased by later authors. The main islands were called "Ierne", equal to the term <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89riu" title="\xc3\x89riu">\xc3\x89riu</a></i> for Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> and "<a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a>" for present-day Great Britain. These later writers referred to the inhabitants as the <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xce\xa0\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xbf\xce\xb9</span>, "Priteni" or "Pretani", probably from a <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> term meaning "people of the forms";<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> "Pretannia" as a place-name was <a href="/wiki/Diodorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Diodorus">Diodorus</a>\' rendering (in Greek) of this self-description. It is often taken as a reference to the practice by the inhabitants of painting (or <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">tattooing</a>) their skin; since it is unusual for an <a href="/wiki/Autological_word" title="Autological word">autonym</a> (a self-description) to describe appearance, this name may have been used by <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armoricans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> There is considerable confusion about early use of these terms and the extent to which similar terms were used as self-description by the inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> From this name a collective term for the islands was used, appearing as <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xce\xb1\xe1\xbc\xb1 \xce\xa0\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xb9\xce\xba\xce\xb1\xce\xb9 \xce\xbd\xce\xb7\xcf\x83\xce\xbf\xce\xb9</span> ("Pretanic Islands")<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xce\xb1\xe1\xbc\xb1 \xce\x92\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xb9\xce\xb1\xce\xb9</span> ("Brittanic Isles").<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Cognates of these terms are still in use.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The island group had long been known collectively as the Pretanic or Britanic isles. As explained by Pliny the Elder, this included the Orcades (Orkney), the H\xc3\xa6budes (Hebrides), Mona (Anglesey), Monopia (Isle of Man), and a number of other islands less certainly identifiable from his names. The deduced Celtic name for Ireland - Iverio - from which its present name was derived, was known to the Greeks by the 4th century BC at least, possibly as early as the 6th century BC. The name meant "the fertile land". It was Latinised to Hiernia or Hibernia. Its people were the Iverni.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 55 and 54 BC, <a href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_invasions_of_Britain" title="Caesar&#39;s invasions of Britain">Caesar\'s invasions of Britain</a> brought firsthand knowledge; in his <i><a href="/wiki/Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico" title="Commentarii de Bello Gallico">Commentarii de Bello Gallico</a></i>, he introduced the term <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Around AD 70, <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, in Book 4 of his <i><a href="/wiki/Pliny%27s_Natural_History" class="mw-redirect" title="Pliny&#39;s Natural History">Naturalis Historia</a></i>, describes the islands he considers to be "Britanniae" as including Great Britain, Ireland, Orkney, smaller islands such as the Hebrides, the Isle of Man, Anglesey, possibly one of the Frisian Islands, and islands which have been identified as <a href="/wiki/Ushant" title="Ushant">Ushant</a> and Sian. He refers to Great Britain as the island called "Britannia", noting that its former name was "Albion". The list also includes the island of Thule, most often identified as Iceland\xe2\x80\x94although some express the view that it may have been the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>\xe2\x80\x94the coast of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> or <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, or possibly <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bostock_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bostock-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The classical writer <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a>, referred to the larger island as <i>great Britain</i> (<i>megale Bretannia</i>) and to Ireland as <i>little Britain</i> (<i>mikra Brettania</i>) in his work, <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i> (147\xe2\x80\x93148 AD).<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> In his later work, <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i> (c. 150 AD), he gave these islands the names<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Alwion</a></i>[sic], <i><a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Iwernia</a></i>, and <i>Mona</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>), suggesting these may have been native names of the individual islands not known to him at the time of writing <i>Almagest</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>Albion</i> appears to have fallen out of use sometime after the <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">Roman conquest of Great Britain</a>, after which <i>Britain</i> became the more commonplace name for the island called Great Britain.\n</p><p>Ptolemy included essentially the same main islands in the Britannias. He wrote around AD 150, although he used the now-lost work of <a href="/wiki/Marinus_of_Tyre" title="Marinus of Tyre">Marinus of Tyre</a> from about fifty years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-ohi_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ohi-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> His first description is of Ireland, which he called "Hibernia". Second was the island of Great Britain, which he called "Albion". Book II, Chapters 1 and 2 of his <i>Geography</i> are respectively titled as <i>Hibernia, Island of Britannia</i> and <i>Albion, Island of Britannia</i>. Ptolemy included Thule in the chapter on Albion, although the coordinates he gives have been mapped to the area around modern Kristiansund in western Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> The famous <i>Geography</i> of Claudius Ptolemy, written in Greek c. 150 AD, included the British Isles. Ptolemy relied on the work of an earlier geographer, Marinos of Tyre. So the <i>Geography</i> generally reflects the situation c. 100 AD and he retained the names used for the islands by Pliny the Elder: Albion for Britain, and Ierne (Latinised as Hibernia) for Ireland.\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">conquest of AD 43</a> the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a> was established,<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> expanded to cover much of the island of Great Britain. An invasion of Ireland was considered but never undertaken, and Ireland remained outside the Roman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> The Romans failed to consolidate their hold on the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>; the northern extent of the area under their control (defined by the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Wall" title="Antonine Wall">Antonine Wall</a> across central Scotland) stabilised at <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a> across the north of England by about AD 210.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> Inhabitants of the province continued to refer to themselves as "Brittannus" or "Britto", and gave their <i>patria</i> (homeland) as "Britannia" or as their tribe.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> The vernacular term "Priteni" came to be used for the barbarians north of the Antonine Wall, with the Romans using the tribal name "<a href="/wiki/Caledonians" title="Caledonians">Caledonii</a>" more generally for these peoples who (after AD 300) they called <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The post-conquest Romans used Britannia or Britannia Magna (Large Britain) for Britain and Hibernia or Britannia Parva (Small Britain) for Ireland. The post-Roman era saw <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Brythonic</a> kingdoms established in all areas of Britain except the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>, but coming under increasing attacks from <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scoti" title="Scoti">Scotti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a>. At this time Ireland was dominated by the Gaels or Scotti, who subsequently gave their names to Ireland and Scotland.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceani_insulae">Oceani insulae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Oceani insulae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In classical geography, the Mediterranean world was thought to be surrounded by a fast-flowing river, personified as the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titan</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a>. As a result, islands off the north and west shores of continental Europe were termed (in Latin) the <i>Oceani Insulae</i> or "islands of the Ocean." In AD 43, various islands (including Britain, Ireland, and Thule) were referred to as <i>Septemtrionalis Oceani Insulae</i> ("islands of the Northern Ocean") by <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a>, one of the earliest Roman geographers.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This term was also used by indigenous sources during the post-Roman period, who also used the term <i>Oceani Insulae</i> as a term for the islands in the Atlantic and elsewhere. One such example is the <i>Life of Saint Columba</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiography</a> recording the <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> activities of the 6th century Irish monk <a href="/wiki/Saint_Columba" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Columba">Saint Columba</a> among the peoples of modern-day Scotland. It was written in the late 7th century by <a href="/wiki/Adomn%C3%A1n_of_Iona" class="mw-redirect" title="Adomn\xc3\xa1n of Iona">Adomn\xc3\xa1n of Iona</a>, an Irish monk living on the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Hebridean" class="mw-redirect" title="Inner Hebridean">Inner Hebridean</a> island.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> No <i>Priteni</i>-derived collective reference is made.  <a href="/wiki/Jordanes" title="Jordanes">Jordanes</a>, writing in his AD 551 <i><a href="/wiki/Getica_(Jordanes)" class="mw-redirect" title="Getica (Jordanes)">Getica</a></i>, describes the islands (particularly in the Western Ocean) as "islands of the Ocean"; he named various islands in the North Atlantic, and believed Scandinavia to be one of them.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> He described Britain, but does not mention Ireland.\n</p><p>Another native source to use the term is the <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>, written in the early 8th century. Bede\'s work does not have a collective term for the archipelago, referring to "Brittania" solely as the island "formerly called Albion" and treating Ireland separately. As with Jordanes and Columba, he refers to Britain as being <i>Oceani insula</i> or "island of the Ocean".<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">Isidore of Seville</a>\'s <i>Etymology</i>, written in the early 7th century and one of the most popular textbooks in Europe during the Middle Ages,<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> similarly lists Britain ("Britannia"), Ireland ("Scotia" or "Hibernia"), Thule, and many other islands simply as "islands" or "islands of the Ocean" and uses no collective term.\n</p><p>In the 17th century, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Heylin" title="Peter Heylin">Peter Heylin</a>, in his <i>Microcosmus</i>, described the classical conception of the Ocean and included in the "Iles of the Ocean" all the classically known offshore islands\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Zealand" title="Zealand">Zealand</a>, the British Isles, and those in the "Northerne Sea".<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Isles">British Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Isles_naming_dispute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: British Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Empire_1897.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/British_Empire_1897.jpg/220px-British_Empire_1897.jpg" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Empire_1897.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The term <i>British Isles</i> came into use in English at the same time as the term <i>British Empire.</i>  This map shows the British Isles (red) at the centre of the empire (pink) in 1897 where England, Ireland, Scotland and Wales are referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Home_Nations" title="Home Nations">Home Nations</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In his <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i> of around 1136, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> responded to the slights of English historians with his theme of the <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>\xe2\x80\x94exalting Welsh national history, portraying a once-unified <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a> (founded by <a href="/wiki/Brutus_of_Troy" title="Brutus of Troy">Brutus of Troy</a>) defended by <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">Britons</a> against <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> invasion. Arthur (who was now sleeping) would one day return to the rescue. By the end of the twelfth century this adaptation of myths common to <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, Cornwall and <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> had been adapted in the service of England\xe2\x80\x94with <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a> enthusiastically taking up <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_legend" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian legend">Arthurian legend</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a> putting on pageantry to show the Welsh that he was Arthur\'s heir. The Welsh (and the Scots\' <a href="/wiki/Edward_Bruce" title="Edward Bruce">Edward Bruce</a>) used the legends to find common cause as one "kin and nation" in driving the English out of Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> Both Welsh rebels and English monarchs (particularly <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry Tudor</a>) continued such claims; Henry had Welsh ancestry, and claimed descent from Arthur. His son <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> incorporated Wales into England; he also claimed to be an heir of Arthur, as did his successor <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The rediscovery of <a href="/wiki/Geographia_(Ptolemy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographia (Ptolemy)">Ptolemy\'s <i>Geographia</i></a> by <a href="/wiki/Maximus_Planudes" title="Maximus Planudes">Maximus Planudes</a> in 1300 brought new insight, and circulation of copies widened when it was translated into Latin in 1409.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> This spread Ptolemy\'s naming of Hibernia and Albion as "Island[s] of Britannia".<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> The Latin equivalents of terms equating to "British Isles" started to be used by mapmakers from the mid-16th century onwards; <a href="/wiki/George_Lily" title="George Lily">George Lily</a> published a map in 1548 entitled "Britannicae Insulae"<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup>; <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_M%C3%BCnster" title="Sebastian M\xc3\xbcnster">Sebastian M\xc3\xbcnster</a> in <i>Geographia Universalis</i> (a 1550 reissue of Ptolemy\'s <i>Geography</i>) uses the heading <i>De insulis Britannicis, Albione, qu\xc3\xa6 est Anglia, &amp; Hibernia, &amp; de cuiutatibus carum in genere.</i><sup id="cite_ref-biom_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biom-116">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">Gerardus Mercator</a> produced much more accurate maps, including one of "the British Isles" in 1564.<sup id="cite_ref-biom_116-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biom-116">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ortelius" class="mw-redirect" title="Ortelius">Ortelius</a>, in his <i>Atlas</i> of 1570, uses the title <i>Angliae, Scotiae et Hiberniae, sive Britannicar. insularum descriptio</i>. This translates as "A Representation of England, Scotland and Ireland, or Britannica\'s Islands".<sup id="cite_ref-GenMaps_118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GenMaps-118">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The geographer and occultist <a href="/wiki/John_Dee_(mathematician)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Dee (mathematician)">John Dee</a> (of Welsh ancestry)<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> was an adviser to Queen Elizabeth I of England and prepared maps for several explorers. He helped to develop legal justifications for colonisation by <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Protestant England</a>, breaking the <a href="/wiki/Duopoly" title="Duopoly">duopoly</a> the Pope <a href="/wiki/Inter_caetera" title="Inter caetera">had granted</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empires</a>. Dee coined the term "<a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>" and built his case, in part, on the claim of a "British Ocean"; including Britain, Ireland, Iceland, Greenland and (possibly) North America, he used alleged Saxon precedent to claim territorial and trading rights.<sup id="cite_ref-kenm_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kenm-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Current scholarly opinion is generally that "his imperial vision was simply propaganda and antiquarianism, without much practical value and of limited interest to the English crown and state."<sup id="cite_ref-kenm_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kenm-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Ireland" title="Lordship of Ireland">Lordship of Ireland</a> had come under tighter English control as the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a>, and diplomatic efforts (<a href="/wiki/Rough_Wooing" title="Rough Wooing">interspersed with warfare</a>) tried to bring Scotland under the English monarch as well. Dee used the term "Brytish Iles" in his writings of 1577, which developed his arguments claiming these territories.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> This appears to be the first use of a recognisable version of the modern term.\n</p><p>Elizabeth was succeeded by her cousin, King <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a>, who brought the English throne under his personal rule as King James I of England\xe2\x80\x94proclaiming himself as "King of Great Brittaine, France and Ireland".<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> However, the states remained separate until the monarchy was overthrown in the <a href="/wiki/The_Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" class="mw-redirect" title="The Wars of the Three Kingdoms">civil wars of the Three Kingdoms</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a> briefly ruled all, before the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">restoration of the monarchy</a> restored separate states.\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> asserts that the first published use in English of "British Isles" was in 1621 (before the civil wars) by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Heylin" title="Peter Heylin">Peter Heylin</a> (or Heylyn) in his <i>Microcosmus: a little description of the great world</i><sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> (a collection of his lectures on historical geography). Writing from his English political perspective, he grouped Ireland with Great Britain and the minor islands with these three arguments:<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>The inhabitants of Ireland must have come from Britain as it was the nearest land</li>\n<li>He notes that ancient writers (such as Ptolemy) called Ireland a <i>Brtti\xca\x83h Iland</i></li>\n<li>He cites the observation of the first-century Roman writer <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> that the habits and disposition of the people in Ireland were not much unlike the <i>Brittaines</i><sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Modern scholarly opinion<sup id="cite_ref-MayhewGRev_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MayhewGRev-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MayhewBJHS_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MayhewBJHS-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> is that Heylyn "politicised his geographical books Microcosmus ... and, still more, Cosmographie" in the context of what geography meant at that time. Heylyn\'s geographical work must be seen as political expressions concerned with proving (or disproving) constitutional matters, and "demonstrated their authors\' specific political identities by the languages and arguments they deployed." In an era when "politics referred to discussions of dynastic legitimacy, of representation, and of the Constitution ... [Heylyn\'s] geography was not to be conceived separately from politics."\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_(1707)" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union (1707)">Acts of Union of 1707</a> the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> and conflict with France brought a new popular enthusiasm for Britishness (mostly in Britain itself),<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> and the term <i>British Isles</i> came into common use despite the persistent stirrings of <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Irish nationalism</a>. A desire for some form of Irish independence had been active throughout the centuries, with <a href="/wiki/Poynings%27_Law" title="Poynings&#39; Law">Poynings\' Law</a> a common focus of resentment. After the hugely turbulent 16th and 17th centuries, nationalism surfaced among the Irish Protestant population and eventually led to the legislative independence of the Irish Parliament under <a href="/wiki/Henry_Grattan" title="Henry Grattan">Henry Grattan</a>\xe2\x80\x94followed after the Act of Union (1800) by the renewed assertiveness of Irish Catholics, who first agitated for <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Emancipation" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic Emancipation">Catholic Emancipation</a> and later for Repeal of the Union under <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O&#39;Connell">Daniel O\'Connell</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Irish_Famine" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Irish Famine">Great Irish Famine</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Land_War" title="Land War">Land War</a> and the failure of <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stuart_Parnell" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Stuart Parnell">Charles Stuart Parnell</a> to get partial independence (a <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Home Rule Bill">Bill for Home Rule</a>) through the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">Westminister Parliament</a> led to the secession of most of Ireland from the United Kingdom.
