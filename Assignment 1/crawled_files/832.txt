algae</a> <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweeds</a> that make up the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <b>Laminariales</b>. There are about 30 different <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Kelp grows in "underwater <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Kelp_forest" title="Kelp forest">kelp forests</a>) in shallow oceans, and is thought to have appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>, 23 to 5 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (August 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The organisms require nutrient-rich water with temperatures between 6 and 14&#160;\xc2\xb0C (43 and 57&#160;\xc2\xb0F). They are known for their high growth rate\xe2\x80\x94the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Macrocystis" title="Macrocystis">Macrocystis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nereocystis" title="Nereocystis">Nereocystis</a></i> can grow as fast as half a metre a day, ultimately reaching 30 to 80 metres (100 to 260&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas_02_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas_02-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Through the 19th century, the word "kelp" was closely associated with seaweeds that could be burned to obtain <a href="/wiki/Soda_ash" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda ash">soda ash</a> (primarily sodium carbonate). The seaweeds used included species from both the orders Laminariales and <a href="/wiki/Fucales" title="Fucales">Fucales</a>. The word "kelp" was also used directly to refer to these processed ashes.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input id="Growth_and_reproduction">Growth and reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Growth and reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Growth occurs at the base of the <a href="/wiki/Meristem" title="Meristem">meristem</a>, where the blades and stipe meet. Growth may be limited by grazing.  <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchins" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea urchins">Sea urchins</a>, for example, can reduce entire areas to <a href="/wiki/Urchin_barrens" class="mw-redirect" title="Urchin barrens">urchin barrens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The kelp life cycle involves a <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">diploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Sporophyte" title="Sporophyte">sporophyte</a> and haploid <a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">gametophyte</a> stage. The haploid phase begins when the mature organism releases many spores, which then germinate to become male or female gametophytes. <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">Sexual reproduction</a> then results in the beginning of the diploid sporophyte stage, which will develop into a mature individual.\n</p><p>The parenchymatous thalli are generally covered with a mucilage layer, rather than cuticle.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kelp_forests">Kelp forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kelp forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kelp_forest" title="Kelp forest">Kelp forest</a></div>\n<p>Kelp may develop dense forests with high production,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> biodiversity and ecological function. Along the Norwegian coast these forests cover 5800&#160;km<sup>2</sup>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and they support large numbers of animals.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Numerous <a href="/wiki/Sessile_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Sessile animals">sessile animals</a> (sponges, bryozoans and ascidians) are found on kelp stipes and mobile invertebrate fauna are found in high densities on epiphytic algae on the kelp stipes and on kelp holdfasts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> More than 100,000 mobile invertebrates per square meter are found on kelp stipes and holdfasts in well-developed kelp forests (Christie et al., 2003). While larger invertebrates and in particular sea urchins <i>Strongylocentrotus droebachiensis</i> (O.F. M\xc3\xbcller) are important secondary consumers controlling large barren ground areas on the Norwegian coast, they are scarce inside dense kelp forests.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_uses">Commercial uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg/220px-Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg/330px-Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg/440px-Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chowiet_Island_beach_kelp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaskan</a> beach kelp</div></div></div>\n<p>Giant kelp can be harvested fairly easily because of its surface canopy and growth habit of staying in deeper water.\n</p><p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bongo_kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bongo kelp (page does not exist)">Bongo kelp</a> ash is rich in <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a>. In great amount, kelp ash can be used in <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> production. Until the <a href="/wiki/Leblanc_process" title="Leblanc process">Leblanc process</a> was commercialized in the early 19th century, burning of kelp in Scotland was one of the principal industrial sources of soda ash (predominantly <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Clow_52_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clow_52-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alginate" class="mw-redirect" title="Alginate">Alginate</a>, a kelp-derived carbohydrate, is used to thicken products such as <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jelly_(fruit_preserves)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jelly (fruit preserves)">jelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salad_dressing" class="mw-redirect" title="Salad dressing">salad dressing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toothpaste" title="Toothpaste">toothpaste</a>, as well as an ingredient in exotic <a href="/wiki/Dog_food" title="Dog food">dog food</a> and in manufactured goods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Alginate powder is also used frequently in general dentistry and orthodontics for making impressions of the upper and lower arches. These impressions are subsequently poured up in stone and the stone models are used in diagnosis and treatment <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kombu" title="Kombu">Kombu</a> (\xe6\x98\x86\xe5\xb8\x83 in Japanese, and \xe6\xb5\xb7\xe5\xb8\xa6 in Chinese, <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharina_japonica" title="Saccharina japonica">Saccharina japonica</a></i> and others), several Pacific species of kelp, is a very important ingredient in Chinese, Japanese, and Korean cuisines. Kombu is used to flavor broths and stews  (especially <i><a href="/wiki/Dashi" title="Dashi">dashi</a></i>), as a savory garnish (<i>tororo konbu</i>) for rice and other dishes, as a vegetable, and a primary ingredient in popular snacks (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tsukudani" title="Tsukudani">tsukudani</a></i>). Transparent sheets of kelp (<i>oboro konbu</i>) are used as an edible decorative wrapping for rice and other foods.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Kombu can be used to soften beans during cooking, and to help convert indigestible sugars and thus reduce flatulence.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because of its high concentration of iodine, brown kelp (<i><a href="/wiki/Laminaria" title="Laminaria">Laminaria</a></i>) has been used to treat <a href="/wiki/Goiter" class="mw-redirect" title="Goiter">goiter</a>, an enlargement of the thyroid gland caused by a lack of iodine, since medieval times.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2010, a group of researchers in the <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University" title="Newcastle University">University of Newcastle</a> found that a fibrous material called <a href="/wiki/Alginate" class="mw-redirect" title="Alginate">alginate</a> in sea kelp was better at preventing fat absorption than most over-the-counter slimming treatments in laboratory trials. As a food additive, it may be used to reduce fat absorption and thus obesity.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Kelp in its natural form has not yet been proven to have such effects.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_production">Commercial production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Commercial production of kelp harvested from its natural habitat took place in Japan for over a century. Many countries today produce and consume laminaria products, but the largest is China. <i><a href="/wiki/Laminaria_japonica" class="mw-redirect" title="Laminaria japonica">Laminaria japonica</a></i>, the important commercial seaweed, was first introduced into China in the late 1920s from Hokkaido, Japan.  Yet <a href="/wiki/Mariculture" title="Mariculture">mariculture</a> of this algae on a very large commercial scale was realized in China only in the 1950s. Between the 1950s and the 1980s kelp production in China increased from about 60 to over 250,000 dry weight metric tons annually.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy_source">Renewable energy source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Renewable energy source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biomass_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomass energy">Biomass energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algae_fuel" title="Algae fuel">Algae fuel</a></div>\n<p>Kelp has a high rate of growth and its decay is quite efficient in yielding <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, as well as sugars that can be converted to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. It has been proposed that large open-ocean kelp farms could serve as a source of renewable energy.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Unlike some biofuels such as <a href="/wiki/Corn_ethanol" title="Corn ethanol">corn ethanol</a>, kelp energy avoids "<a href="/wiki/Food_vs_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Food vs fuel">food vs fuel</a>" issues<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and does not require irrigation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_history_and_culture">In history and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In history and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some of the earliest evidence for human use of marine resources, coming from Middle Stone Age sites in South Africa, includes the harvesting of foods such as <a href="/wiki/Abalones" class="mw-redirect" title="Abalones">abalones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limpets" class="mw-redirect" title="Limpets">limpets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mussels" class="mw-redirect" title="Mussels">mussels</a> associated with kelp forest habitats.\n</p><p>In 2007, Erlandson et al. suggested that <a href="/wiki/Kelp_forest" title="Kelp forest">kelp forests</a> around the Pacific Rim may have facilitated the dispersal of anatomically modern humans following a coastal route from Northeast Asia to the Americas. This "kelp highway hypothesis" suggested that highly productive kelp forests supported rich and diverse marine food webs in nearshore waters, including many types of fish, shellfish, birds, marine mammals, and seaweeds that were similar from Japan to California, Erlandson and his colleagues also argued that coastal kelp forests reduced wave energy and provided a linear dispersal corridor entirely at sea level, with few obstacles to maritime peoples. Archaeological evidence from California\'s Channel Islands confirms that islanders were harvesting kelp forest shellfish and fish beginning as much as 12,000 years ago.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">Highland Clearances</a>, many Scottish Highlanders were moved on to areas of estates known as <a href="/wiki/Crofting" title="Crofting">crofts</a>, and went to industries such as fishing and kelping (producing soda ash from the ashes of kelp). At least until the 1840s, when there were steep falls in the price of kelp, landlords wanted to create pools of cheap or virtually free labour, supplied by families subsisting in new crofting townships. Kelp collection and processing was a very profitable way of using this labour, and landlords petitioned successfully for legislation designed to stop emigration. But the economic collapse of the kelp industry in northern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> led to further emigration, especially to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.\n</p><p>Natives of the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> are sometimes <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknamed</a> "<a href="/wiki/Kelpers" title="Kelpers">Kelpers</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This designation is primarily <a href="/wiki/Exonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonym">applied by outsiders rather than the natives themselves</a>.\n</p><p>In Chinese <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a>, "kelp" (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">\xe6\xb5\xb7\xe5\xb8\xa6</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">\xe6\xb5\xb7\xe5\xb8\xb6</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">h\xc7\x8ei d\xc3\xa0i</span></i>), is used to describe an unemployed returnee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It has negative overtones, implying the person is drifting aimlessly, and is also a homophonic expression (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">\xe6\xb5\xb7\xe5\xbe\x85</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">h\xc7\x8eid\xc3\xa0i</span></i>, literally "sea waiting"). This expression is contrasted with the employed returnee, having a dynamic ability to travel across the ocean: the "sea turtle" (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">\xe6\xb5\xb7\xe9\xbe\x9f</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">\xe6\xb5\xb7\xe9\xbe\x9c</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">h\xc7\x8ei g\xc5\xabi</span></i>) and is also homophonic with another word (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">\xe6\xb5\xb7\xe5\xbd\x92</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">\xe6\xb5\xb7\xe6\xad\xb8</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">h\xc7\x8ei g\xc5\xabi</span></i>, literally "sea return").\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Overfishing nearshore ecosystems leads to the degradation of kelp forests. Herbivores are released from their usual population regulation, leading to over-grazing of kelp and other algae. This can quickly result in barren landscapes where only a small number of species can thrive.<sup id="cite_ref-Dayton_1985a_23-0" class="reference"><a
