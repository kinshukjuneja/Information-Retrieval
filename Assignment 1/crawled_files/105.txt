class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonel_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Insignia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Colonel_William_Few.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d4/Colonel_William_Few.jpg/220px-Colonel_William_Few.jpg" width="220" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d4/Colonel_William_Few.jpg/330px-Colonel_William_Few.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d4/Colonel_William_Few.jpg 2x" data-file-width="425" data-file-height="568" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Colonel_William_Few.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Few" title="William Few">William Few</a> in the uniform of a Continental Army colonel</div></div></div>\n<p>The insignia for a colonel is a <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> which is a stylized representation of the eagle dominating the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_the_United_States" title="Great Seal of the United States">Great Seal of the United States</a> (which is the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> of the United States). As on the Great Seal, the eagle has a U.S. shield superimposed on its chest and is holding an <a href="/wiki/Olive_branch" title="Olive branch">olive branch</a> and bundle of <a href="/wiki/Arrow" title="Arrow">arrows</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Talon_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Talon (anatomy)">talons</a>. However, in simplification of the Great Seal image, the insignia lacks the scroll in the eagle\'s mouth and the <a href="/wiki/Rosette_(design)" title="Rosette (design)">rosette</a> above its head. On the Great Seal, the olive branch is always clutched in the eagle\'s right-side talons, while the bundle of arrows is always clutched in the left-side talons.  The head of the eagle faces towards the olive branch, rather than the arrows, advocating peace rather than war.  As a result, the head of the eagle always faces towards the viewer\'s left.\n</p><p>However, when worn as a single insignia with no matching pair, such as on the <a href="/wiki/Patrol_cap" title="Patrol cap">patrol cap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garrison_cap" class="mw-redirect" title="Garrison cap">garrison cap</a>/<a href="/wiki/Flight_cap" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight cap">flight cap</a>, or the front of the Army ACU, there is a split between the services on which mirror image of the eagle should be worn.  In the United States Army and United States Air Force, the eagle is always worn with "the head of the eagle to the wearer\'s right," with the olive branch clutched in the eagle\'s right hand talons (see Department of the Army Pamphlet 670-1, paragraph 19-6 (a)(1)). In the United States Marine Corps, United States Navy, United States Coast Guard and NOAA, the eagle is worn with "the head facing forward" on the wearer\'s right side of the garrison cover (see Marine Corps Order P1020.34G, Uniform Regulation, paragraph 4005d(1)).  Since respective service\'s officer insignia is worn on the left side and the rank insignia is worn on the right hand side of the Marine, Navy, Coast Guard and NOAA garrison caps, the eagle is facing to the eagle\'s left with the olive branch clutched in the eagle\'s left hand talons, which is a mirror opposite to the wear of the single eagle for Army and Air Force officers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonel_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The United States rank of colonel is a direct successor to the <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_Kingdom)" title="Colonel (United Kingdom)">same rank</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>.  The first colonels in the U.S. were appointed from <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">Colonial</a> <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militias</a> maintained as <a href="/wiki/Military_reserve_force" title="Military reserve force">reserves</a> to the British Army in the North American colonies. Upon the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, colonial legislatures would grant commissions to men to raise a <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiment</a> and serve as its colonel. Thus, the first U.S. colonels were usually respected men with ties in local communities and active in politics. \n</p><p>With the post-war reduction of the U.S. Army, the rank of colonel disappeared, and was not re-introduced until 1802.\n</p><p>The first insignia for the rank of colonel consisted of gold <a href="/wiki/Epaulette" title="Epaulette">epaulettes</a> worn on the blue uniform of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>.  The first recorded use of the eagle insignia was in 1805 as this insignia was made official in uniform regulations by 1810.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonel_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UCWColonel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="202" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UCWColonel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A shoulder strap from an infantry colonel in the Union Army</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Union_Army_colonel_rank_insignia.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Union_Army_colonel_rank_insignia.png/170px-Union_Army_colonel_rank_insignia.png" width="170" height="68" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Union_Army_colonel_rank_insignia.png/255px-Union_Army_colonel_rank_insignia.png 1.5x, class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Union_Army_colonel_rank_insignia.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A shoulder strap from an infantry colonel in the Union Army</div></div></div>\n<p>The rank of colonel was relatively rare in the early 19th century, partly because the U.S. Army was very small, and the rank was usually obtained only after long years of service. During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> the Army grew rapidly and many colonels were appointed, but most of these colonels were discharged when their regiments were disbanded at the war\'s conclusion.  A number of other colonels were appointed by brevet - an honorary promotion usually for distinguished service in combat.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> saw a large influx of colonels as the rank was commonly held in both the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_Army" title="Confederate States Army">Confederate army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> by those who commanded a regiment. Since most regiments were state formations and were quickly raised, the colonels in command of the regiments were known by the title "Colonel of Volunteers," in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Regular_Army_(United_States)" title="Regular Army (United States)">Regular Army</a> colonels who held permanent commissions.  \n</p><p>During the Civil War, the Confederate Army maintained a unique insignia for colonel, three stars worn on the collar of a uniform. <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee" title="Robert E. Lee">Robert E. Lee</a> wore this insignia due to his former rank in the United States Army and refused to wear the <a href="/wiki/Ranks_and_insignia_of_the_Confederate_States" title="Ranks and insignia of the Confederate States">insignia of a Confederate general</a>, stating that he would only accept permanent promotion when the South had achieved independence.\n</p><p>After the Civil War, the rank of colonel again became rare as the forces of the United States Army became extremely small. However, many colonels were appointed in the volunteers during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War">Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War</a>, prominent among them <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Grant_Colson" title="David Grant Colson">David Grant Colson</a>.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Jeanette_Sustad" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeanette Sustad">Jeanette Sustad</a> as Director of Women Marines.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> saw the largest numbers of colonels ever appointed in the U.S. military. This was mostly due to the temporary ranks of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States_Army#World_War_I" title="History of the United States Army">National Army</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_United_States" title="Army of the United States">Army of the United States</a>, where those who would normally hold the rank of <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States_O-3)" title="Captain (United States O-3)">Captain</a> in the peacetime Regular Army were thrust into the rank of colonel during these two wars.\n</p><p>The Military Promotion System was revised and standardized for all the services in 1980 as a result of passage of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Officer_Personnel_Management_Act" title="Defense Officer Personnel Management Act">Defense Officer Personnel Management Act</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonel_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Modern U.S. colonels usually command Army infantry, artillery, armor, aviation or other types of <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">brigades</a>, USMC <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_Expeditionary_Unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine Expeditionary Unit">Marine Expeditionary Units</a> or Marine Aircraft Groups, and USAF <a href="/wiki/Group_(air_force_unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Group (air force unit)">groups</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wing_(air_force_unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing (air force unit)">wings</a>. An Army colonel typically commands brigade-sized units (4,000 to 6,000 soldiers), with another colonel or a lieutenant colonel as deputy commander, a major as <a href="/wiki/Executive_officer" title="Executive officer">executive officer</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Command_sergeant_major" class="mw-redirect" title="Command sergeant major">command sergeant major</a> as a senior <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">non-commissioned officer</a> (NCO) advisor. An Air Force colonel typically commands a <a href="/wiki/Wing_(air_force_unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing (air force unit)">wing</a> consisting of 1,000 to 4,000+ airmen with another colonel as the vice commander and a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Command_chief_master_sergeant&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Command chief master sergeant (page does not exist)">command chief master sergeant</a> as principal senior NCO enlisted adviser. Some USAF colonels are commanders of <a href="/wiki/Group_(air_force)" class="mw-redirect" title="Group (air force)">groups</a>, which are the four major components of wings.  Colonels are also found as the chief of staff at divisional level-(Army) or <a href="/wiki/Numbered_Air_Force" title="Numbered Air Force">Numbered Air Force</a>-level staff agencies.\n</p><p>In the modern armed forces, the colonel\'s eagle is worn facing forward with head and beak pointing towards the wearer\'s front. Of all U.S. military commissioned officer rank, only the colonel\'s eagle has a distinct right and left insignia.  All other commissioned officer rank insignia can be worn on either the right or left side.\n</p><p>Colonels are sometimes referred to (but not addressed as) <b>full colonels</b>, <b>bird colonels</b>, or <b>full bird colonels</b> because lieutenant colonels are also referred to and addressed in correspondence as "colonel." Referring to an "O-6", a colonel\'s pay grade, also may differentiate colonels and <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant colonel (United States)">lieutenant colonels</a>.\n</p><p>Almost all Army colonels have attended a war college or a senior staff college equivalent to study joint warfare and war itself. Most Army colonels receive postgraduate level senior joint professional military education (JPME) at the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army_War_College" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army War College">Army War College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Pennsylvania" title="Carlisle, Pennsylvania">Carlisle, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> prior to promotion. The 2009 graduating class was 336 including 198 army officers and the rest divided among other military branches, domestic inter-agency representatives and other foreign military leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The highest concentration of USAF colonels graduate from the <a href="/wiki/Air_War_College" title="Air War College">Air War College</a> at <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_AFB" class="mw-redirect" title="Maxwell AFB">Maxwell AFB</a>, Alabama via in-residence at Maxwell AFB. This program will include participation by officers from various other branches. Marine colonels may graduate from the Marine War College or, like all other branches, may receive credit via non-resident attendance at another installation, via correspondence, or will be graduates of an equivalent senior JPME program sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_University" title="National Defense University">National Defense University</a> or one of the other U.S. military services.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorary_colonels">Honorary colonels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonel_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Honorary colonels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(title)" title="Colonel (title)">Colonel (title)</a></div>\n<p>Some people known as "colonels" are actually recipients of honorary colonel ranks from a state governor and are not officers of the U.S. military. In the 19th century the honorary colonels were military appointments and they still are nominally appointed to a governor\'s staff, but without military rights or duties. Famous honorary colonels include Colonel <a href="/wiki/Colonel_Sanders" title="Colonel Sanders">Harland Sanders</a> of <a href="/wiki/KFC" title="KFC">KFC</a> fame, a <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_colonel" class="mw-redirect" title="Kentucky colonel">Kentucky colonel</a>; <a href="/wiki/Colonel_Tom_Parker" title="Colonel Tom Parker">Colonel Tom Parker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>\'s manager, who received the honor from a <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> governor; and <a href="/wiki/Edward_M._House" title="Edward M. House">Edward M. House</a>, known as Colonel House, a Texas honorary colonel and adviser to President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Famous_colonels">Famous colonels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonel_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Famous colonels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<dl><dt></dt>\n<dt>Famous U.S. Army colonels</dt></dl>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Alvin_Beckwith" title="Charles Alvin Beckwith">Charlie Beckwith</a> \xe2\x80\x93 Founder of <a href="/wiki/Delta_Force" title="Delta Force">1st Special Forces Operational Detachment-Delta</a>, the Army\'s elite top-secret <a href="/wiki/Special_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Forces">special forces</a> detachment.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Anthony_G._Brown" title="Anthony G.
