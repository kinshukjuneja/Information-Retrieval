The early ball club, which featured numerous future <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Hall of Famers</a>, finished in first place three consecutive years (1894\xe2\x80\x931895\xe2\x80\x931896) and won the "<a href="/wiki/Temple_Cup" title="Temple Cup">Temple Cup</a>" national championship series in 1896 and 1897. Despite their success, the dominant Orioles were contracted out of the League after the 1899 season, when the N.L. reduced its number of teams and franchises from 12 to 8, with a list of teams and cities limited to just the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">northeastern United States</a> which endured for the next half-century. This controversial action resulting in the elevation of the former <a href="/wiki/Western_League_(original)" class="mw-redirect" title="Western League (original)">Western League</a> by leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson" title="Ban Johnson">Ban Johnson</a> (1864-1931), into a newly-organized <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> in 1901 of which the new reorganized <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees#Origins_in_Baltimore_.281901.E2.80.931902.29" title="New York Yankees">Baltimore Orioles</a> were a prominent member for its first two seasons which "waged war" on the elder "Nationals".\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" also</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltimore_Orioles_(1882%E2%80%9399)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The team was founded in <a href="/wiki/1882_in_baseball" title="1882 in baseball">1882</a> as a charter member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a>, which was then a major league. After several years of mediocrity, the team dropped out of the league in <a href="/wiki/1889_in_baseball" title="1889 in baseball">1889</a>, but re-joined in <a href="/wiki/1890_in_baseball" title="1890 in baseball">1890</a> to replace the last-place <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Gladiators" title="Brooklyn Gladiators">Brooklyn Gladiators</a> club which had dropped out during the season. After the Association folded, the Orioles joined the National League in <a href="/wiki/1892_in_sports" title="1892 in sports">1892</a>. The beginnings of what was to become a legendary team can be traced to June 1892, when <a href="/wiki/Harry_Von_der_Horst" title="Harry Von der Horst">Harry Von der Horst</a> hired <a href="/wiki/Ned_Hanlon_(baseball)" title="Ned Hanlon (baseball)">Ned Hanlon</a> to manage the Orioles, giving him stock in the team and full authority over baseball operations. Ned moved his growing family to a house that stood a block away from Union Park.\n</p><p>After two years finishing near the bottom of the league, the Orioles won three consecutive pennants with several future <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Hall of Famers</a> under player/manager Ned Hanlon from <a href="/wiki/1894_in_baseball" title="1894 in baseball">1894</a> to <a href="/wiki/1896_in_baseball" title="1896 in baseball">1896</a>. They followed up the title run with two consecutive second-place finishes. Accordingly, they participated in all four editions of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Cup" title="Temple Cup">Temple Cup</a> series, winning the final two in 1896 and 1897. After the team\'s 1898 second-place finish, Hanlon and most of the team\'s stars (though not <a href="/wiki/John_McGraw_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="John McGraw (baseball)">John McGraw</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Robinson" title="Wilbert Robinson">Wilbert Robinson</a>) were moved across to the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> by the joint ownership of the clubs.\n</p><p>Following a fourth-place finish in 1899, the National League eliminated four teams from the circuit, the Orioles among them. First-year player/manager <a href="/wiki/John_McGraw_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="John McGraw (baseball)">John McGraw</a> followed through on his threats to abandon the NL and form a club in the rival <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> (being formed by new president <a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson" title="Ban Johnson">Ban Johnson</a> out of the former minor <a href="/wiki/Western_League_(original)" class="mw-redirect" title="Western League (original)">Western League</a>), doing so beginning in <a href="/wiki/1901_in_baseball" title="1901 in baseball">1901</a>. (Those newly formed <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(1901-02)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Orioles (1901-02)">A.L. Orioles</a> only stayed in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> for two seasons before being moved to <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> as "the price of peace" as agreement was established in 1903 between the older circuit and its new upstart rival allowing the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">"Americans"</a> to have a representative also in the "Big Apple" as a sign of respectability. The old Oriole franchise under McGraw became known as the "New York Highlanders" or occasionally the "New York Americans", later becoming renamed in 1913 as the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>.)\n</p><p>A high-minor league franchise in the old <a href="/wiki/International_League" title="International League">Eastern League</a> filled the void left by the Orioles in 1903, including local product and future baseball icon <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lefty_Grove" title="Lefty Grove">Lefty Grove</a>, even winning an unbroken string of six straight titles, 1919\xe2\x80\x931925 in the "Triple A" (AAA) level of <a href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" class="mw-redirect" title="Minor league baseball">minor league baseball</a> in the reorganized <a href="/wiki/International_League" title="International League">International League</a> (after 1911), but top-level professional baseball would not return to <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> until the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Browns" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis Browns">St. Louis Browns</a> relocated to the City in <a href="/wiki/1954_in_baseball" title="1954 in baseball">1954</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ballpark">Ballpark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltimore_Orioles_(1882%E2%80%9399)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ballpark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Orioles played briefly at the old <a href="/wiki/Oriole_Park" title="Oriole Park">Oriole Park</a>, in <a href="/wiki/List_of_Baltimore_neighborhoods" title="List of Baltimore neighborhoods">Harwood</a>, south of the <a href="/wiki/Waverly,_Baltimore" title="Waverly, Baltimore">Waverly</a> neighborhood at 29th and Barclay Streets, (just a block west from <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Route_45" title="Maryland Route 45">Greenmount Avenue</a>) from 1890 to 1891. (The 1901 AL Orioles-turned-Highlanders would play at the site a decade later.) During the 1891 season, the Orioles moved a few blocks away to <a href="/wiki/Union_Park_(Baltimore)" title="Union Park (Baltimore)">Union Park</a> on Huntington Avenue (later renamed 25th Street) and <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Route_45" title="Maryland Route 45">Greenmount Avenue</a>, where they would play and win their famous three straight championships for the old "<a href="/wiki/Temple_Cup" title="Temple Cup">Temple Cup</a>" in 1894\xe2\x80\x931895-1896. Unfortunately they were removed from the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">N.L.</a> roster after the 1899 season when the League was controversially reduced from 12 team franchises to 8, which endured for the next half-century. For further info see <a href="/wiki/List_of_baseball_parks_in_Baltimore,_Maryland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of baseball parks in Baltimore, Maryland">List of baseball parks in Baltimore, Maryland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stars">Stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltimore_Orioles_(1882%E2%80%9399)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mcgraw_jennings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/76/Mcgraw_jennings.jpg/400px-Mcgraw_jennings.jpg" width="400" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/76/Mcgraw_jennings.jpg/600px-Mcgraw_jennings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/76/Mcgraw_jennings.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="284" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mcgraw_jennings.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_McGraw_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="John McGraw (baseball)">John McGraw</a> (left) and <a href="/wiki/Hughie_Jennings" title="Hughie Jennings">Hughie Jennings</a> (right) anchored the left side of the infield for Orioles teams that won three straight <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> pennants (1894\xe2\x80\x931896). Later, both were successful managers. (Note: In the middle image, McGraw is shaking hands with Athletics captain Harry Davis, right).</div></div></div>\n<p>The original Orioles were one of the most storied teams in the history of the game. Managed by <a href="/wiki/Ned_Hanlon_(baseball)" title="Ned Hanlon (baseball)">Ned Hanlon</a>, they won NL pennants in <a href="/wiki/1894_in_sports" title="1894 in sports">1894</a>, <a href="/wiki/1895_in_sports" title="1895 in sports">1895</a> and <a href="/wiki/1896_in_sports" title="1896 in sports">1896</a>, and sported some of the most colorful players in history including <a href="/wiki/John_McGraw_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="John McGraw (baseball)">John McGraw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Keeler" title="Willie Keeler">Wee Willie Keeler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hughie_Jennings" title="Hughie Jennings">Hughie Jennings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Kelley" title="Joe Kelley">Joe Kelley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Robinson" title="Wilbert Robinson">Wilbert Robinson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dan_Brouthers" title="Dan Brouthers">Dan Brouthers</a>.\n</p><p>They were rough characters who practically invented "scientific" baseball, the form of baseball played before the <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home run</a> became the norm in the 1920s.  Like the style known today as "<a href="/wiki/Small_ball_(baseball)" title="Small ball (baseball)">small ball</a>", the "<a href="/wiki/Inside_Baseball_(strategy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inside Baseball (strategy)">inside baseball</a>" strategy of Orioles featured tight <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitching</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hit_and_run_(baseball)" title="Hit and run (baseball)">hit and run</a> tactics, <a href="/wiki/Stolen_base" title="Stolen base">stolen bases</a>, and precise <a href="/wiki/Bunt_(baseball)" title="Bunt (baseball)">bunting</a>. One such play, where the batter deliberately strikes the pitched ball downward onto the infield surface with sufficient force such that the ball rebounds skyward, allowing the batter to reach first base safely before the opposing team can field the ball, remains known as a <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Chop" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Chop">Baltimore Chop</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Matt_Kilroy" title="Matt Kilroy">Matt Kilroy</a> pitched a <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitter</a> for the Orioles on October 6, 1886.  <a href="/wiki/Bill_Hawke" title="Bill Hawke">Bill Hawke</a> threw one on August 16, 1893, the first from the modern pitching distance of 60 feet, 6&#160;inches.  <a href="/wiki/Jay_Hughes" title="Jay Hughes">Jay Hughes</a> threw a no-hitter for the Orioles on April 22, 1898.\n</p><p>Instead of "flying spikes," it was really "flying mouths" that most made the 1890s Orioles stand out.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_United_States_soccer_champions">First United States soccer champions</span><span One solution was to launch a National soccer league containing the same teams names as, and even some players from its Baseball parent. Soccer was growing rapidly in popularity in the United States at the time but a combination of poor advertising, low media coverage, midweek kick off times and most importantly, the failure of the Baseball stars of the day turning up, as promised, to try their hand at the kicking game, led to attendances rarely growing above 1,000 per game.
