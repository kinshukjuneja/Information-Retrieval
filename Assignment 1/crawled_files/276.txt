fifteen of the <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a>, 120\xe2\x80\x93134 (119\xe2\x80\x93133 in the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>), each starting with the <a href="/wiki/Psalms#Superscriptions_and_attributions_(of_authorship)" title="Psalms">superscription</a> Shir Hama\'aloth (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span lang="he" dir="rtl">\xd7\xa9\xd6\xb4\xd7\x81\xd7\x99\xd7\xa8 \xd7\x94\xd7\x9e\xd6\xb7\xd7\xa2\xd6\xb2\xd7\x9c\xd7\x95\xd6\xb9\xd7\xaa</span>&#8206;, meaning "Song of the Ascents"). They are also variously called <b>Gradual Psalms</b>, <b>Songs of Degrees</b>, <b>Songs of Steps</b>  or <b>Pilgrim Songs</b>.\n</p><p>Four of them (Psalms <a href="/wiki/Psalm_122" title="Psalm 122">122</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psalm_124" title="Psalm 124">124</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psalm_131" title="Psalm 131">131</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psalm_133" title="Psalm 133">133</a>) are linked in their ascriptions to <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a>, and one (<a href="/wiki/Psalm_127" title="Psalm 127">127</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a>. Three of them (Psalms 131, 133, and <a href="/wiki/Psalm_134" title="Psalm 134">134</a>) have only three verses.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The longest is <a href="/wiki/Psalm_132" title="Psalm 132">psalm 132</a> (18 verses).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Many scholars believe the title indicates that these psalms were sung by worshippers as they ascended the road to <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> to attend the <a href="/wiki/Three_pilgrim_festivals" class="mw-redirect" title="Three pilgrim festivals">three pilgrim festivals</a> <sup id="cite_ref-Zenger_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zenger-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0516.htm#16">Deuteronomy 16:16</a>). Others think they were sung by the <a href="/wiki/Levite" title="Levite">Levite</a> singers as they ascended the fifteen steps to minister at the <a href="/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem" title="Temple in Jerusalem">Temple in Jerusalem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> One view says the Levites first sang the Songs at the dedication of Solomon\'s temple during the night of the fifteenth of Tishri 959 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Another study suggests that they were composed for a celebration after <a href="/wiki/Nehemiah" title="Nehemiah">Nehemiah</a>\'s rebuilding of Jerusalem\'s walls in 445 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Others consider that they may originally have been individual poems which were later collected together and given the title linking them to pilgrimage after the <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_captivity" title="Babylonian captivity">Babylonian captivity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zenger_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zenger-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>They were well suited for being sung by their poetic form and the sentiments they express. "They are characterized by brevity, by a key-word, by <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_rhetoric_terms" class="mw-redirect" title="Glossary of rhetoric terms">epanaphora</a> [<i>i.e.</i>, repetition], and by their <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">epigrammatic</a> style.... More than half of them are cheerful, and all of them hopeful."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As a collection, they contain a number of repeated formulaic phrases, as well as an emphasis on Zion.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical">Historical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are two references to the Songs of Ascents in the <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a>, noting the correspondence between the fifteen songs and the temple\'s fifteen steps between the Israelite\'s court and the women\'s court.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a> refers to a Talmudic legend that King David composed or sang the fifteen songs to calm rising waters at the foundation of the temple.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day">Present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Present day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Psalm_126" title="Psalm 126">Psalm 126</a>, eponymously called "<i>Shir Hamaalot</i>" due to its common use, is traditionally recited before the <a href="/wiki/Birkat_Hamazon" title="Birkat Hamazon">Grace After Meals</a> on <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jewish_holidays" title="Jewish holidays">Jewish holidays</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Seudat_mitzvah" title="Seudat mitzvah">festive occasions</a> in keeping with its themes of joy and redemption.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The psalm is sung to a wide variety of tunes both liturgical and secular. The most well-known melody in the Ashkenazi Jewish world is that by Cantor Pinchas Minkowsky which was recorded by Cantor <a href="/wiki/Yossle_Rosenblatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Yossle Rosenblatt">Yossle Rosenblatt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is traditional for some Jews to place a copy of <a href="/wiki/Psalm_121" title="Psalm 121">Psalm 121</a> in the labor and delivery room to promote an easy labor by asking God for mercy. As well, it is placed on the baby\'s carriage and in the baby\'s room to protect the child and surround them in learning and with holy objects.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_liturgy">Christian liturgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Christian liturgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgical</a> use of these psalms came into Christianity through its Jewish roots. The form of the <a href="/wiki/Scriptures" class="mw-redirect" title="Scriptures">Scriptures</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Church">Early Church</a>, at least so far as the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> was concerned, was primarily the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>. In the Septuagint, these psalms are numbered 119&#8211;133.\n</p><p>Many early <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermits</a> observed the practice of reciting the entire <a href="/wiki/Psalter" title="Psalter">Psalter</a> daily, <a href="/wiki/Coenobitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Coenobitic">coenobitic</a> communities would chant the entire Psalter through in a week, so these psalms would be said on a regular basis, during the course of the <a href="/wiki/Canonical_hours" title="Canonical hours">Canonical hours</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Christianity">Eastern Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and those <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a> which follow the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a>, the Songs of Degrees (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <i>anabathmoi</i>) make up the Eighteenth <a href="/wiki/Kathisma" title="Kathisma">Kathisma</a> (division of the Psalter), and are read on Friday evenings at <a href="/wiki/Vespers" title="Vespers">Vespers</a> throughout the <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">liturgical year</a>. The Kathisma is divided into three sections (called <i>stases</i>) of five psalms each.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Great_Lent" title="Great Lent">Great Lent</a> the Eighteenth Kathisma is read every weekday (Monday through Friday evening) at Vespers, and on Monday through Wednesday of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">Holy Week</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Slavic usage</a> this Kathisma is also read from the <a href="/wiki/Afterfeast" title="Afterfeast">apodosis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Exaltation_of_the_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Exaltation of the Cross">Exaltation of the Cross</a> up to the <a href="/wiki/Forefeast" class="mw-redirect" title="Forefeast">forefeast</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Nativity of Christ</a>, and from the apodosis of <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Theophany</a> up to the <a href="/wiki/Sunday_of_the_Prodigal_Son" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday of the Prodigal Son">Sunday of the Prodigal Son</a>. The reason for this is that the nights are longer in winter, especially in the northern latitudes, so during this season three Kathismata will be chanted at Matins instead of two, so in order to still have a reading from the Psalter at Vespers, the Eighteenth Kathisma is repeated.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anabathmoi">Anabathmoi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anabathmoi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>At <a href="/wiki/Matins" title="Matins">Matins</a> on Sundays and <a href="/wiki/Feast_day" class="mw-redirect" title="Feast day">feast days</a> throughout the year, special hymns called <i>anabathmoi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">\xce\xac\xce\xbd\xce\xb1\xce\xb2\xce\xb1\xce\xb8\xce\xbc\xce\xbf\xce\xaf</span>, from \xce\xb2\xce\xb1\xce\xb8\xce\xbc\xcf\x8c\xcf\x82, \'step\'; <a href="/wiki/Church_Slavonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Church Slavonic">Slavonic</a>: <i>step\xc3\xa9nnyi</i>) are chanted immediately before the <a href="/wiki/Prokeimenon" title="Prokeimenon">prokeimenon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matins_Gospel" title="Matins Gospel">Matins Gospel</a>. These anabathmoi are compositions based upon the Songs of Ascents, and are written in the <a href="/wiki/Eight_tones" class="mw-redirect" title="Eight tones">eight tones</a> of <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_chant" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine chant">Byzantine chant</a>. The Anabathmoi for each tone consists of three <i>stases</i> or sets of verses (sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Antiphon" title="Antiphon">antiphons</a>), except for Tone 8 which has four stases.  On Sundays, the anabathmoi are chanted according to the tone of the week; on feast days which do not fall on Sunday, the Anabathmoi almost always consist of the first stasis in Tone 4 (based on Psalm 128).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Symbolically, the anabathmoi are chanted as a reminder that Christians are ascending to the <a href="/wiki/Heavenly_Jerusalem" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavenly Jerusalem">Heavenly Jerusalem</a>, and that the spiritual intensity of the service is rising as they approach the reading of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nassar_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nassar-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Christianity">Western Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Western Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Western <a href="/wiki/Daily_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily Office">Daily Office</a> was strongly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_St._Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Rule of St. Benedict">Rule of St. Benedict</a>, where these psalms are assigned to <a href="/wiki/Third_Hour" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Hour">Terce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sext" title="Sext">Sext</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ninth_Hour" class="mw-redirect" title="Ninth Hour">None</a> on weekdays. Over the centuries, however, various schedules have been used for reciting the psalms. In the modern <a href="/wiki/Liturgy_of_the_Hours" title="Liturgy of the Hours">Liturgy of the Hours</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, the Gradual Psalms are used in several ways:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Psalms 120-127 and 129-131 are scheduled throughout the four-week Psalter for use at Vespers; 119, 128, and 132 are scheduled for use for <a href="/wiki/Little_Hours#Reform" title="Little Hours">Daytime Prayer</a>, and 133 is scheduled for Night Prayer.</li>\n<li>Psalms 119-127 are broken into three parts, to be used as the complementary Psalmody for those who pray three daytime offices separately as Terce, Sext, and None, rather than one office of Daytime Prayer.</li>\n<li>They are used as the sole Psalmody at daytime prayer on <a href="/wiki/Solemnity" title="Solemnity">solemnities</a>, except for certain solemnities of the Lord and during the <a href="/wiki/Octave_(liturgical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Octave (liturgical)">octave</a> of <a href="/wiki/Octave_of_Easter" title="Octave of Easter">Easter</a> and those solemnities falling on Sunday.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_of_Ascents&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
