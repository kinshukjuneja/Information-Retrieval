The same logo was later used by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> of <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> originated in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> as the <b>New York Gothams</b> in 1883 and were known as the <b>New York Giants</b> from 1885 until the team <a href="/wiki/Relocation_of_professional_sports_teams" title="Relocation of professional sports teams">relocated</a> to San Francisco after the <a href="/wiki/1957_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1957 Major League Baseball season">1957</a> season. During most of their 75 seasons in New York City, the Giants played home games at various incarnations of the <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Manhattan" title="Upper Manhattan">Upper Manhattan</a>.\n</p><p>Numerous inductees of the <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cooperstown,_New_York" title="Cooperstown, New York">Cooperstown, New York</a> played for the New York Giants, including <a href="/wiki/John_McGraw" title="John McGraw">John McGraw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ott" title="Mel Ott">Mel Ott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Terry" title="Bill Terry">Bill Terry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monte_Irvin" title="Monte Irvin">Monte Irvin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Travis_Jackson" title="Travis Jackson">Travis Jackson</a>. During the club\'s tenure in New York, it won five of the franchise\'s eight World Series wins and 17 of its 24 National League pennants. Famous moments in the Giants\' New York history include the <a href="/wiki/1922_World_Series" title="1922 World Series">1922 World Series</a>, in which the Giants swept the Yankees in four games, the 1951 home run known as the <a href="/wiki/Shot_Heard_%27Round_the_World_(baseball)" title="Shot Heard &#39;Round the World (baseball)">"Shot Heard \'Round the World"</a>, and the defensive feat by <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a> during the first game of the <a href="/wiki/1954_World_Series" title="1954 World Series">1954 World Series</a> known as <a href="/wiki/The_Catch_(baseball)" title="The Catch (baseball)">"the Catch"</a>.\n</p><p>The Giants had intense rivalries with their fellow New York teams the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a>, facing the Yankees in six World Series and playing the league rival Dodgers multiple times per season. Games between any two of these three teams were known collectively as the <a href="/wiki/Subway_Series" title="Subway Series">Subway Series</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dodgers-Giants_rivalry" class="mw-redirect" title="Dodgers-Giants rivalry">rivalry with the Dodgers</a> continues to be played as the Dodgers joined the Giants in moving also to along the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> on the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> after the 1957 season when they relocated to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants" title="New York Giants">New York Giants</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> are named after the team.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" Gothams season">1883 Gothams</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Giants_black_NY.png" class="image"><img alt="" baseball club founded by millionaire <a href="/wiki/Tobacconist" title="Tobacconist">tobacconist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_B._Day" title="John B. Day">John B. Day</a> and veteran amateur baseball player <a href="/wiki/Jim_Mutrie" title="Jim Mutrie">Jim Mutrie</a>. The <b>Gothams</b>, as the Giants were originally known, entered the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> seven years after its 1876 formation, in <a href="/wiki/1883_New_York_Gothams_season" title="1883 New York Gothams season">1883</a>, while their other club, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Metropolitans" title="New York Metropolitans">Metropolitans</a> played in the rival <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a> (1882-1891). Nearly half of the original Gothams players were members of the disbanded <a href="/wiki/Troy_Trojans_(MLB_team)" title="Troy Trojans (MLB team)">Troy Trojans</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upstate_New_York" title="Upstate New York">upstate New York</a>, whose place in the National League the Gothams inherited. While the <a href="/wiki/1883_New_York_Metropolitans_season" title="1883 New York Metropolitans season">Metropolitans</a> were initially the more successful club, after they won the <a href="/wiki/1884_New_York_Metropolitans_season" title="1884 New York Metropolitans season">1884 AA championship</a>, Day and Mutrie began moving star players to the NL Gothams, whose fortunes improved while the Metropolitans\' afterwards slumped.\n</p><p>It is said that after one particularly satisfying victory over the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a>, Mutrie (who was also the team\'s manager) stormed into the dressing room and exclaimed, "My big fellows! My giants!"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> From then on (<a href="/wiki/1885_New_York_Giants_season" title="1885 New York Giants season">1885</a>), the club was known as the Giants.\n</p><p>The team won its first National League <a href="/wiki/Pennant_(sports)" title="Pennant (sports)">pennant</a> in <a href="/wiki/1888_New_York_Giants_season" title="1888 New York Giants season">1888</a>, as well as a victory over the <a href="/wiki/1888_St._Louis_Browns_season" title="1888 St. Louis Browns season">St. Louis Browns</a> in an early incarnation of the pre-modern-era <a href="/wiki/1888_World_Series" title="1888 World Series">World Series</a>. They repeated as champions <a href="/wiki/1889_New_York_Giants_season" title="1889 New York Giants season">the next year</a> with a pennant and world championship victory over the <a href="/wiki/1889_Brooklyn_Bridegrooms_season" title="1889 Brooklyn Bridegrooms season">Brooklyn Bridegrooms</a>.\n</p><p>The Giants\' original home stadium, the <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a>, also dates from this early era. It was originally located north of <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a> adjacent to Fifth and Sixth Avenues and 110th and 112th Streets, in <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> in upper <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. After their eviction from that first incarnation of the Polo Grounds after the 1888 season, they moved further uptown to various fields which they also named the "Polo Grounds" located between 155th and 159th Streets in <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Heights,_Manhattan" title="Washington Heights, Manhattan">Washington Heights</a>, playing in the Washington Heights located famous Polo Grounds until the end of the 1957 season, when they moved to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.\n</p><p>The Giants were a powerhouse in the late 1880s, winning their first two National League Pennants and World Championships in <a href="/wiki/1888_World_Series" title="1888 World Series">1888</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/1889_World_Series" title="1889 World Series">1889</a>. But nearly all of the Giants\' stars jumped to the upstart newly-organized rival loop, the <a href="/wiki/Players%27_League" title="Players&#39; League">Players\' League</a>, whose New York franchise was also named <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(PL)" title="New York Giants (PL)">the Giants</a>, in 1890. The new team even built a stadium next door to the NL Polo Grounds. With a decimated roster, the NL Giants finished a distant sixth. Attendance took a nosedive, and the financial strain affected Day\'s tobacco business as well. The Players\' League dissolved after the single season, and Day sold a minority interest in his NL Giants to the defunct PL Giants\' principal backer, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Talcott" title="Edward Talcott">Edward Talcott</a>. As a condition of the sale, Day had to fire Mutrie as manager. Although the Giants rebounded to third place in 1891, Day was forced to sell a controlling interest to Talcott at the end of the \'91 season.\n</p><p>Four years later, Talcott sold the Giants to <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Freedman" title="Andrew Freedman">Andrew Freedman</a>, a real estate developer with ties to the <a href="/wiki/Tammany_Hall" title="Tammany Hall">Tammany Hall</a> political machine/of the city/state <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> running <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Freedman was one of the most detested owners in baseball history, getting into heated disputes with other owners, writers and his own players, most famously with star pitcher <a href="/wiki/Amos_Rusie" title="Amos Rusie">Amos Rusie</a>, author of the first Giants no-hitter. When Freedman offered Rusie only $2,500 for 1896, the disgruntled hurler sat out the entire season. Attendance fell off throughout the league without Rusie, prompting the other owners to chip in $50,000 to get him to return for 1897. Freedman even hired former owner Day as manager for part of the 1899 season.\n</p><p>In 1902, after a series of disastrous moves that left the Giants 53\xc2\xbd games behind the front-runner, Freedman signed <a href="/wiki/John_McGraw_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="John McGraw (baseball)">John McGraw</a> as player-manager, convincing him to jump in mid-season from the <a href="/wiki/1902_Baltimore_Orioles_season" title="1902 Baltimore Orioles season">Baltimore Orioles</a> (1901-1902) of the fledgling <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> and bring with him several of his teammates. McGraw went on to manage the Giants for three decades until 1932, one of the longest and most successful tenures in professional sports. Hiring "Mr. McGraw," as his players referred to him, was one of Freedman\'s last significant moves as owner of the Giants, since after that 1902 season he was forced to sell his interest in the club to <a href="/wiki/John_T._Brush" title="John T. Brush">John T. Brush</a>. McGraw went on to manage the Giants to nine National League pennants (in 1904-1905, 1911\xe2\x80\x931913, 1914) and three <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> championships (in 1905 &amp; 1921-1922), with a tenth pennant and fourth world championship as Giants owner in 1933 under his handpicked player-manager successor, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Terry" title="Bill Terry">Bill Terry</a>.\n</p><p>The Giants already had their share of stars beginning in the 1880s &amp; 1890s, such as <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Welch" title="Mickey Welch">"Smiling" Mickey Welch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Connor" title="Roger Connor">Roger Connor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Keefe" title="Tim Keefe">Tim Keefe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_O%27Rourke_(baseball)" title="Jim O&#39;Rourke (baseball)">Jim O\'Rourke</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Montgomery_Ward" title="John Montgomery Ward">John Montgomery Ward</a>, the player-lawyer who formed the renegade <a href="/wiki/Players%27_League" title="Players&#39; League">Players\' League</a> in 1890 to protest unfair player contracts. McGraw, a veteran of the infamous 1890s <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(19th_century)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Orioles (19th century)">Baltimore Orioles</a>,  in his three decades managing the Giants, cultivated a new crop of baseball heroes with names like <a href="/wiki/Christy_Mathewson" title="Christy Mathewson">Christy Mathewson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_McGinnity" title="Joe McGinnity">"Iron Man" Joe McGinnity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Thorpe" title="Jim Thorpe">Jim Thorpe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Ames" title="Red Ames">Red Ames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casey_Stengel" title="Casey Stengel">Casey Stengel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art_Nehf" title="Art Nehf">Art Nehf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edd_Roush" title="Edd Roush">Edd Roush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Hornsby" title="Rogers Hornsby">Rogers Hornsby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Terry" title="Bill Terry">Bill Terry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ott" title="Mel Ott">Mel Ott</a>.\n</p><p>The Giants under McGraw famously snubbed their first modern <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> chance in <a href="/wiki/1904_World_Series" title="1904 World Series">1904</a>, established  as the price of peace with the rival Americans, first played the year before, by refusing the invitation to play the <a href="/wiki/1903_World_Series" title="1903 World Series">reigning world champion</a> <a href="/wiki/1904_Boston_Americans_season" title="1904 Boston Americans season">Boston Americans</a> (now known as the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a>) because McGraw considered the newly established <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> of 1901, as little more than a <a href="/wiki/Minor_league" title="Minor league">minor league</a> and disliked its firebrand president, <a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson" title="Ban Johnson">Ban Johnson</a>. He also resented his Giants\' new intra-city rival <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Highlanders</a>, (from the old relocated charter AL franchise <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(1901-02)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Orioles (1901-02)">Baltimore Orioles</a>), who almost won the pennant but lost to Boston on the last day, and stuck by his refusal to play whoever won the 1904 AL pennant. Of note, McGraw had managed the relocated Highlanders franchise in their first two seasons (1901\xe2\x80\x931902), when they were playing in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>.\n</p><p>The ensuing criticism resulted in Brush\'s taking the lead to formalize the rules and format of the World Series. The <a href="/wiki/1905_New_York_Giants_season" title="1905 New York Giants season">Giants</a> won the <a href="/wiki/1905_World_Series" title="1905 World Series">1905 World Series</a> over <a href="/wiki/Connie_Mack" title="Connie Mack">Connie Mack</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/1905_Philadelphia_Athletics_season" title="1905 Philadelphia Athletics season">Philadelphia Athletics</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Christy_Mathewson" title="Christy Mathewson">Christy Mathewson</a> nearly winning the series single-handedly with a still-standing record three complete-game shutouts and 27 consecutive scoreless innings in that one <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>, a feat unlikely ever to be duplicated.\n</p><p>The Giants then had several frustrating years. In 1908, they finished in a tie with the <a href="/wiki/1908_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1908 Chicago Cubs season">Chicago Cubs</a> due to a late-season home tie game with the Cubs resulting from the <a href="/wiki/Merkle%27s_Boner" title="Merkle&#39;s Boner">Fred Merkle baserunning "boner"</a>. They lost the postseason replay of the tie game (ordered by NL president <a href="/wiki/Harry_Pulliam" title="Harry Pulliam">Harry Pulliam</a>) to the Cubs (after disgruntled Giants fans had set fire to the stands the morning of the game), who would go on to win their second (consecutive, and their last for the next 108 years!!) World Series. That post-season game was further darkened by a story that someone on the Giants had attempted to bribe umpire <a href="/wiki/Bill_Klem" title="Bill Klem">Bill Klem</a>. This could have been a disastrous scandal for baseball, but because Klem was honest and the Giants lost the duel between <a href="/wiki/Christy_Mathewson" title="Christy Mathewson">Christy Mathewson</a> and Mordecai "Three-Fingered" Brown 4\xe2\x80\x932, it faded over time.\n</p><p>The Giants experienced a mixture of success and hard luck in the early 1910s, losing three straight World Series in 1911\xe2\x80\x931913 to the A\'s, Red Sox and A\'s again (two seasons later, both the Giants and the A\'s, decimated by the short-lived rival third loop, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_League" title="Federal League">Federal League</a> of 1914-1915, with the "jumping ship"  signings of many of their stars, finished in eighth [last] place). After losing the <a href="/wiki/1917_World_Series" title="1917 World Series">1917 Series</a> to the <a href="/wiki/1917_Chicago_White_Sox_season" title="1917 Chicago White Sox season">Chicago White Sox</a> (the last "Chisox Series" win until 2005), the Giants played in four straight World Series in the early 1920s, winning the first two over their <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a> fellow tenants, the Yankees (after winning the first two of their many pennants, led by their new young slugger and former Red Sox and Orioles pitcher  <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">George Herman "Babe" Ruth</a>), then losing to the <a href="/wiki/1923_New_York_Yankees_season" title="1923 New York Yankees season">Yankees</a> in <a href="/wiki/1923_World_Series" title="1923 World Series">1923</a> after old <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium_(1923)" title="Yankee Stadium (1923)">Yankee Stadium</a> had opened that May. They also lost in 1924, when the <a href="/wiki/1924_Washington_Senators_season" title="1924 Washington Senators season">Washington Senators</a> won their only World Series while  in D.C. (prior to their move to <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis</a> - <a href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins" title="Minnesota Twins">Minnesota Twins</a> before the 1961 season and their later 1987 &amp; 1991 Series wins there).\n</p>\n<h2><span id="1930.E2.80.9357:_Five_pennants_in_28_seasons"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1930\xe2\x80\x9357:_Five_pennants_in_28_seasons">1930\xe2\x80\x9357: Five pennants in 28 seasons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1930\xe2\x80\x9357: Five pennants in 28 seasons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>McGraw handed over the team to Bill Terry after the 1932 season, and Terry played for and managed the Giants for ten years, winning three pennants, defeating the <a href="/wiki/1933_Washington_Senators_season" title="1933 Washington Senators season">Senators</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1933_World_Series" title="1933 World Series">1933 World Series</a> but swept by the Yankees in consecutive fall classics, <a href="/wiki/1936_World_Series" title="1936 World Series">1936</a> and <a href="/wiki/1937_World_Series" title="1937 World Series">1937</a>. Aside from Terry himself, the other stars of the era were slugger <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ott" title="Mel Ott">Mel Ott</a> and southpaw hurler <a href="/wiki/Carl_Hubbell" title="Carl Hubbell">Carl Hubbell</a>. Known as "King Carl" and "The Meal Ticket", Hubbell gained fame in the first two innings of the <a href="/wiki/1934_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1934 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">1934 All-Star Game</a> (played at the Polo Grounds) by striking out five future AL Hall of Famers in a row: <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Lou Gehrig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Foxx" title="Jimmie Foxx">Jimmie Foxx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Simmons" title="Al Simmons">Al Simmons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Cronin" title="Joe Cronin">Joe Cronin</a>.\n</p><p>Ott succeeded Terry as manager in 1942, but the war years proved to be difficult for the Giants. Midway during the 1948 season <a href="/wiki/1948_Brooklyn_Dodgers_season" title="1948 Brooklyn Dodgers season">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> <a href="/wiki/Leo_Durocher" title="Leo Durocher">Leo Durocher</a> left as Dodgers skipper to manage the Giants, not without controversy. Not only was such a midseason managerial switch unprecedented, but Durocher had been accused of gambling in 1947 and subsequently suspended for that whole season by Baseball Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Happy_Chandler" title="Happy Chandler">Albert "Happy" Chandler</a>. Durocher\'s ensuing eight full seasons managing the Giants proved some of the most memorable for their fans, particularly because of the arrival of five-tool superstar <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a>, their two pennants in 1951 &amp; 1954, their unexpected sweep of the powerful (111-43) <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1954_World_Series" title="1954 World Series">1954 World Series</a> and arguably the two most famous plays in Giants history.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1951:_The_.22Shot_Heard_.27Round_the_World.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1951:_The_&quot;Shot_Heard_\'Round_the_World&quot;">1951: The "Shot Heard \'Round the World"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1951: The &quot;Shot Heard &#039;Round the World&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shot_Heard_%27Round_the_World_(baseball)" title="Shot Heard &#39;Round the World (baseball)">Shot Heard \'Round the World (baseball)</a></div>\n<p>The "Shot Heard \'Round the World," or <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Thomson" title="Bobby Thomson">Bobby Thomson</a>\'s come-from-behind ninth-inning <a href="/wiki/Walk-off_home_run" title="Walk-off home run">walk-off home run</a> that won the National League pennant for the Giants over their bitter rivals, the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a>, in the deciding game of a three-game playoff series ended one of baseball\'s most memorable pennant races. The <a href="/wiki/1951_New_York_Giants_(MLB)_season" title="1951 New York Giants (MLB) season">Giants</a> had been 13 1/2 games behind the league-leading <a href="/wiki/1951_Brooklyn_Dodgers_season" title="1951 Brooklyn Dodgers season">Dodgers</a> in August, but under Durocher\'s guidance and with a 16-game winning streak, got hot and caught the Dodgers to tie for the lead on the next-to-last day of the season.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Mays.27s_catch_and_the_1954_Series"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mays\'s_catch_and_the_1954_Series">Mays\'s catch and the 1954 Series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mays&#039;s catch and the 1954 Series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Catch_(baseball)" title="The Catch (baseball)">The Catch (baseball)</a></div>\n<p>In Game 1 of the <a href="/wiki/1954_World_Series" title="1954 World Series">1954 World Series</a> at the Polo Grounds against the <a href="/wiki/1954_Cleveland_Indians_season" title="1954 Cleveland Indians season">Cleveland Indians</a>, Willie Mays made "The Catch," a dramatic over-the-shoulder catch of a fly ball by <a href="/wiki/Vic_Wertz" title="Vic Wertz">Vic Wertz</a> after sprinting with his back to the plate on a dead run to deepest center field. At the time the game was tied 2\xe2\x80\x932 in the eighth inning, with men on first and second and nobody out. Mays caught the ball 450&#160;ft (140&#160;m) from the plate, whirled and threw the ball to the infield, keeping the lead runner, <a href="/wiki/Larry_Doby" title="Larry Doby">Larry Doby</a>, from scoring. Although Doby took third after the catch, he was stranded there and the Giants won on <a href="/wiki/Dusty_Rhodes_(outfielder)" title="Dusty Rhodes (outfielder)">Dusty Rhodes</a>\' tenth-inning pinch-hit walk-off home run with two aboard, 5-2.\n</p><p>The underdog Giants went on to sweep the series in four straight, despite the Indians\' <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> 111-43 regular season. The <a href="/wiki/1954_World_Series" title="1954 World Series">1954 World Series</a> title would be their last appearance in the World Series as the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants" title="New York Giants">New York Giants</a>, with the team moving to San Francisco to start the 1958 season.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_Giants_of_the_1950s">New York Giants of the 1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New York Giants of the 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In addition to <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Thomson" title="Bobby Thomson">Bobby Thomson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a>, other memorable <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants" title="New York Giants">New York Giants</a> of the 1950s include <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Hall of Fame</a> manager <a href="/wiki/Leo_Durocher" title="Leo Durocher">Leo Durocher</a>, coach <a href="/wiki/Herman_Franks" title="Herman Franks">Herman Franks</a>, Hall of Fame outfielder <a href="/wiki/Monte_Irvin" title="Monte Irvin">Monte Irvin</a>, outfielder and runner-up for the 1954 NL batting championship (won by Willie Mays) <a href="/wiki/Don_Mueller" title="Don Mueller">Don Mueller</a>, Hall of Fame <a href="/wiki/Knuckleball" title="Knuckleball">knuckleball</a> relief pitcher <a href="/wiki/Hoyt_Wilhelm" title="Hoyt Wilhelm">Hoyt Wilhelm</a>, starting pitchers <a href="/wiki/Larry_Jansen" title="Larry Jansen">Larry Jansen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sal_Maglie" title="Sal Maglie">Sal Maglie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Hearn" title="Jim Hearn">Jim Hearn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marv_Grissom" title="Marv Grissom">Marv Grissom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Koslo" title="Dave Koslo">Dave Koslo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Liddle" title="Don Liddle">Don Liddle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Lanier" title="Max Lanier">Max Lanier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rub%C3%A9n_G%C3%B3mez_(baseball)" title="Rub\xc3\xa9n G\xc3\xb3mez (baseball)">Rub\xc3\xa9n G\xc3\xb3mez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Worthington" title="Al Worthington">Al Worthington</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Antonelli" title="Johnny Antonelli">Johnny Antonelli</a>, catcher <a href="/wiki/Wes_Westrum" title="Wes Westrum">Wes Westrum</a>, catchers <a href="/wiki/Ray_Katt" title="Ray Katt">Ray Katt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sal_Yvars" title="Sal Yvars">Sal Yvars</a>, shortstop <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Dark" title="Alvin Dark">Alvin Dark</a>, third baseman <a href="/wiki/Hank_Thompson_(baseball)" title="Hank Thompson (baseball)">Hank Thompson</a>, first baseman <a href="/wiki/Whitey_Lockman" title="Whitey Lockman">Whitey Lockman</a>, second basemen <a href="/wiki/Davey_Williams" title="Davey Williams">Davey Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Stanky" title="Eddie Stanky">Eddie Stanky</a>, outfielder-pitcher <a href="/wiki/Clint_Hartung" title="Clint Hartung">Clint Hartung</a> and utility men <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Mize" title="Johnny Mize">Johnny Mize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Rigney" title="Bill Rigney">Bill Rigney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Spencer" title="Daryl Spencer">Daryl Spencer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Hofman" title="Bobby Hofman">Bobby Hofman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joey_Amalfitano" title="Joey Amalfitano">Joey Amalfitano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tookie_Gilbert" title="Tookie Gilbert">Tookie Gilbert</a>, and 1954 Series hero <a href="/wiki/Dusty_Rhodes_(outfielder)" title="Dusty Rhodes (outfielder)">Dusty Rhodes</a>, among others. In the late 1950s and after the move to San Francisco two Hall of Fame first basemen, <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Cepeda" title="Orlando Cepeda">Orlando Cepeda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willie_McCovey" title="Willie McCovey">Willie McCovey</a>, joined the team.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1957:_Move_to_California">1957: Move to California</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1957: Move to California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Giants\' final three years in New York City were unmemorable. They stumbled to third place the year after their World Series win, and attendance fell off precipitously. While seeking a new stadium to replace the crumbling Polo Grounds, the Giants began to contemplate a move from New York, initially considering <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Stadium" title="Metropolitan Stadium">Metropolitan Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Minnesota" title="Bloomington, Minnesota">Bloomington, Minnesota</a>, which was home to their top farm team, the <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis_Millers" title="Minneapolis Millers">Minneapolis Millers</a>. Under the rules of the time, the Giants\' ownership of the Millers gave them priority rights to a major league team in the area (the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="History of the Washington Senators (1901\xe2\x80\x9360)">Washington Senators</a> wound up there as the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins" title="Minnesota Twins">Minnesota Twins</a> in 1961).\n</p><p>At this time, the Giants were approached by San Francisco mayor <a href="/wiki/George_Christopher" title="George Christopher">George Christopher</a>. Despite objections from shareholders such as <a href="/wiki/Joan_Whitney_Payson" title="Joan Whitney Payson">Joan Whitney Payson</a>, majority owner <a href="/wiki/Horace_Stoneham" title="Horace Stoneham">Horace Stoneham</a> entered into negotiations with San Francisco officials around the same time the Dodgers\' owner <a href="/wiki/Walter_O%27Malley" title="Walter O&#39;Malley">Walter O\'Malley</a> was courting the city of Los Angeles. O\'Malley had been told that the Dodgers would not be allowed to move to Los Angeles unless a second team moved to California as well. He pushed Stoneham toward relocation, and so in the summer of 1957 both the Giants and <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> announced their moves to California, ending the three-team golden age of baseball in New York City.\n</p><p>New York would remain a one-team town with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> until 1962, when Joan Payson founded the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> and brought National League baseball back to the city (as part of MLB\'s first wave of expansion). Owners Payson and <a href="/wiki/M._Donald_Grant" title="M. Donald Grant">M. Donald Grant</a>, who became the Mets\' chairman, had been the only Giants board members to vote against the Giants\' move to California. The "NY" script on the Giants\' caps and the orange trim on their uniforms, along with the blue background used by the Dodgers, would be adopted by the Mets, honoring their New York NL forebears with a blend of Giants orange and Dodgers blue.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(baseball)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sports.nyhistory.org/jim-mutrie/">"Jim Mutrie"</a>. <i>nyhistory.org</i><span class="reference-accessdate">.
