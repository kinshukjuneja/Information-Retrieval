class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> survey at the end of the 11th century records manors at Penventinue and Trenant, and a <a href="/wiki/Priory" title="Priory">priory</a> was soon established nearby at <a href="/wiki/Tywardreath" title="Tywardreath">Tywardreath</a>. <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1300</span> the prior granted a charter to people living in Fowey itself. This medieval town ran from a north gate near Boddinick Passage to a south gate at what is now Lostwithiel Street; the town extended a little way up the hillside and was bounded on the other side by the river where merchants had their houses backing onto the waterfront. The natural harbour allowed trade to develop with Europe and local ship owners often hired their vessels to the king to support various wars, although the town also developed a reputation for <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a>, as did many others at this time. A group of <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a> known as the \'<a href="/wiki/Fowey_Gallants" title="Fowey Gallants">Fowey Gallants</a>\' were given licence to seize French vessels during the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years\' War</a>. In the 14th century the harbour was defended by 160 <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archers</a>; after these were withdrawn, two blockhouses were built on either side of the harbour entrance. Despite these defences the town was attacked by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> forces in 1457. <a href="/wiki/Place_House" title="Place House">Place House</a>, by the church, was successfully defended against the French but subsequently strengthened. This building still exists, but much remodelled. A small castle was built on St Catherine\xe2\x80\x99s Point, the western side of the harbour entrance, around 1540.  The defences proved their worth when a <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> attack was beaten off in 1667.<sup id="cite_ref-story_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-story-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The people of Fowey generally sided with the Royalists during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, but in 1644 the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Essex" title="Earl of Essex">Earl of Essex</a> brought a Parliamentarian army to <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel" title="Lostwithiel">Lostwithiel</a> and occupied the peninsula around Fowey. In August, a Royalist army surrounded Essex\xe2\x80\x99s troops and <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a> himself viewed Fowey from Hall Walk above Polruan, where he came close to being killed by a <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">musket</a> shot. On 31 August, the Parliamentarian cavalry forced their way through the Royalist lines and retreated towards <a href="/wiki/Saltash" title="Saltash">Saltash</a>, leaving the foot soldiers to be evacuated by sea from Fowey. Essex and some officers did indeed escape, but the majority of the force surrendered a few days later near <a href="/wiki/Golant" title="Golant">Golant</a> and were then marched to <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a>, but most died before reaching there.<sup id="cite_ref-story_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-story-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> and elsewhere instead.  Fishing became more important, but local merchants were often appointed as <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a> and did some smuggling on the side.  Tin, copper  and iron mines, along with quarries and china clay pits became important industries in the area, which led to improvements at rival harbours.  West Polmear beach was dug out to become <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Cornwall" title="Charlestown, Cornwall">Charlestown</a> harbour circa 1800, as was <a href="/wiki/Pentewan" title="Pentewan">Pentewan</a> in 1826.<sup id="cite_ref-shipbuilders_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shipbuilders-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Treffry" title="Joseph Treffry">Joseph Austen</a> shipped copper from Caffa Mill Pill above Fowey for a while before starting work on the new <a href="/wiki/Par,_Cornwall#Harbour" title="Par, Cornwall">Par harbour</a> in 1829.<sup id="cite_ref-king_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Fowey had to wait another forty years before it saw equivalent development, but its natural deep-water anchorage and a rail link soon gave it an advantage over the shallow artificial harbours nearer to the mines and china clay works.  Meanwhile, a beacon tower was erected on the <a href="/wiki/Gribben_Head" title="Gribben Head">Gribben Head</a> by <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a> to improve navigation into Fowey and around Par bay.<sup id="cite_ref-shipbuilders_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shipbuilders-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div established by an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> in 1869, to develop and improve the harbour.<sup id="cite_ref-shipbuilders_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shipbuilders-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  On 1 June in that year, the <span class="nowrap">7&#160;ft</span> (<span class="nowrap">2,134&#160;mm</span>) <a href="/wiki/Broad_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Broad gauge">broad gauge</a> <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel_and_Fowey_Railway" title="Lostwithiel and Fowey Railway">Lostwithiel and Fowey Railway</a> was opened to new jetties situated above Carne Point, and in 1873, the <span class="nowrap">4&#160;ft&#160;<span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in</span> (<span class="nowrap">1,435&#160;mm</span>) <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a> <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Minerals_Railway" title="Cornwall Minerals Railway">Cornwall Minerals Railway</a> (CMR) opened a line from <a href="/wiki/Newquay,_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Newquay, Cornwall">Newquay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Par,_Cornwall" title="Par, Cornwall">Par</a> to further jetties between Caffa Mill Pill and Carne Point.  Both of these railways initially carried just goods, but on 20 June 1876, a passenger station was opened on the CMR on land reclaimed from Caffa Mill Pill.  The Lostwithiel line closed at the end of 1879 but was reopened by the CMR as a standard gauge line in 1895, and the short gap between the two lines at Carne Point was eliminated.  Passenger trains from Par were withdrawn after 1934 and from Lostwithiel in 1965.  The Par line was subsequently converted to a dedicated roadway for lorries bringing <a href="/wiki/China_clay" class="mw-redirect" title="China clay">china clay</a> from Par after which all trains had to run via <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel_railway_station" title="Lostwithiel railway station">Lostwithiel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution" title="Royal National Lifeboat Institution">Royal National Lifeboat Institution</a> established <a href="/wiki/Fowey_Lifeboat_Station" title="Fowey Lifeboat Station">Fowey Lifeboat Station</a> near the Town Quay in 1922 to replace an earlier station at <a href="/wiki/Polkerris" title="Polkerris">Polkerris</a>. This was replaced in 1997, by a new facility in Passage Street.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Two <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboats</a> are stationed at Fowey: <i>Maurice and Joyce Hardy</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Trent_class_lifeboat" class="mw-redirect" title="Trent class lifeboat">Trent Class</a> all weather boat that is kept afloat opposite the lifeboat station, and <i>Olive Two</i>, an <a href="/wiki/IB1_class_lifeboat" class="mw-redirect" title="IB1 class lifeboat">IB1</a> inshore lifeboat kept inside the station and launched by <a href="/wiki/Davit" title="Davit">davit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Fowey was the main port for loading ammunition for the US 29th Division that landed on <a href="/wiki/Omaha_Beach" title="Omaha Beach">Omaha Beach</a> on <a href="/wiki/D_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="D Day">D Day</a> during the Second World War.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> There was a munitions siding at Woodgate Pill just north of Fowey,  originally built for the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">Great War</a> conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span Local government responsibilities are shared by the town council and Cornwall Council. Besides the town of Fowey itself, the parish includes the coastal area between the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Fowey" title="River Fowey">River Fowey</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Austell_Bay" small <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> and cargo port at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Fowey" title="River Fowey">River Fowey</a> in south <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, England. It is at the entrance to a large flooded valley created after the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> by the melt waters that caused the sea level to rise dramatically, creating a large natural harbour which is navigable for its last seven miles.<sup id="cite_ref-Bird_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Fowey is in the South Coast (Eastern Section) of the Cornwall <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>. It lies at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Saints%27_Way" title="Saints&#39; Way">Saints\' Way</a> and has ferries across the river to <a href="/wiki/Polruan" title="Polruan">Polruan</a> (foot) and <a href="/wiki/Bodinnick" title="Bodinnick">Bodinnick</a> (vehicle). There are many historic buildings in the town, including the ruins of <a href="/wiki/St_Catherine%27s_Castle" title="St Catherine&#39;s Castle">St Catherine\'s Castle</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Readymoney_Cove" title="Readymoney Cove">Readymoney Cove</a> possesses a local <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beach</a>.\n</p><p>At the time of the 2001 census, Fowey had a population of 2,273. This had increased slightly at the 2011 census to 2,395<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  The Fowey electoral ward had a population of 4,690 in href="/wiki/Joseph_of_Arimathea" title="Joseph of Arimathea">Joseph of Arimathea</a> who was a merchant visiting local tin mines in which he had a commercial interest.<sup class="noprint This cross is marked on very early charts and was maintained by monks from <a href="/wiki/Tywardreath" title="Tywardreath">Tywardreath</a>. The cross is known locally as "Punches Cross", supposedly derived from the name of <a href="/wiki/Pontius_Pilate" title="Pontius Pilate">Pontius Pilate</a>.\n</p><p>One hundred yards west of the lighthouse on the west of the harbour entrance, about thirty feet below the top of the cliff edge and broadly concealed, is a small grass area known as "Johnny May\'s Chapel". This name is believed to be that of a <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist</a> preacher at the time when Nonconformism was persecuted.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fowey_Parish_Church">Fowey Parish Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fowey Parish Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The church is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Finbarr" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Finbarr">Saint Finbarr</a> and is listed Grade I. It was built in the early 14th century and rededicated in 1336, replacing a previous Norman church. The church was damaged by the French in 1457, and repaired in 1460 by the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Warwick" title="Earl of Warwick">Earl of Warwick</a>, when the clerestory and the north and south aisles were rebuilt. There is a nave and two aisles with a clerestory, and the aisles are unusually wide; the aisles and the clerestory may be additions of the 15th century. The tower, of the 16th century, is of four stages and has buttresses and bands of ornament. There is an exceptionally fine 15th-century carved wagon roof. The south porch has open arches to the west and east and an eight-ribbed vaulted roof. The font is Norman, of <a href="/wiki/Elvan" title="Elvan">Catacleuze</a> stone, and similar to those of Ladock, Feock and St Mewan. The hexagonal pulpit was made in 1601. The monuments include two brasses of the mid 15th century and those of John Rashleigh, 1582, and Alice Rashleigh, 1602. The most interesting are two later Rashleigh monuments: John Rashleigh, c. 1610, and another of 1683.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The church was used as a town hall for a period up to 1684. Sir <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Quiller-Couch" title="Arthur Quiller-Couch">Arthur Quiller-Couch</a> is buried in the churchyard.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG/220px-Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG/330px-Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG/440px-Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tonne_Fowey_P8272189-1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Loading <a href="/wiki/China_clay" class="mw-redirect" title="China clay">china clay</a> at Carne Point</div></div></div>\n<p>Fowey has thrived as a <a href="/wiki/Harbor" title="Harbor">port</a> for hundreds of years, initially as a trading and <a href="/wiki/Naval" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval">naval</a> town, then as the centre for <a href="/wiki/China_clay" class="mw-redirect" title="China clay">china clay</a> exports.  Today Fowey is busy with <a href="/wiki/Commercial_trawler" class="mw-redirect" title="Commercial trawler">trawlers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yacht" title="Yacht">yachts</a>.  Tourism is also an important source of income, contributing \xc2\xa314m to the local economy and accounting for more than half of the jobs in the town.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Fowey_railway_station" title="Fowey railway station">Fowey railway station</a> closed to passengers in 1965, the <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel_and_Fowey_Railway" title="Lostwithiel and Fowey Railway">Lostwithiel to Fowey branch line</a> remains open for goods traffic, carrying bulk <a href="/wiki/China_clay" class="mw-redirect" title="China clay">china clay</a> to the jetties at Carne Point.  The nearest passenger station is at <a href="/wiki/Par_railway_station" title="Par railway station">Par</a>, whence there are trains to <a href="/wiki/Penzance_railway_station" title="Penzance railway station">Penzance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newquay_railway_station" title="Newquay railway station">Newquay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_railway_station" title="Plymouth railway station">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Temple_Meads_railway_station" title="Bristol Temple Meads railway station">Bristol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a>. <a href="/wiki/First_Kernow" title="First Kernow">First Kernow</a> operate regular bus services, numbered 25, 524 and 525, between Fowey, Par station and <a href="/wiki/St_Austell" title="St Austell">St Austell</a>. The combined frequency varies from one bus per hour on Sundays to four buses per hour on weekdays. From <a href="/wiki/St_Austell_bus_station" class="mw-redirect" title="St Austell bus station">St Austell bus station</a> connecting buses operate to other places in Cornwall. Town Bus is a frequent and regular service running from outside the church in the town centre to the main car park on Hanson Drive.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Both vehicle and foot <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> services cross the river to Bodinnick and Polruan.  A ship to shore <a href="/wiki/Water_taxi" title="Water taxi">water taxi</a> service operates from Easter until the end of October and a foot ferry to the fishing village of Mevagissey runs from 1 May to 1 October, weather permitting.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Fowey has two schools: Fowey Primary School and <a href="/wiki/Fowey_River_Academy" title="Fowey River Academy">Fowey River Academy</a>, both of which are in Windmill Road. Fowey Grammar School, for which its architect <a href="/wiki/Silvanus_Trevail" title="Silvanus Trevail">Silvanus Trevail</a> received a silver medal, was demolished in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Fowey has been the inspiration for many authors, including <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Quiller-Couch" title="Arthur Quiller-Couch">Sir Arthur Quiller-Couch (\'Q\')</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daphne_du_Maurier" title="Daphne du Maurier">Daphne du Maurier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Walmsley" title="Leo Walmsley">Leo Walmsley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Grahame" title="Kenneth Grahame">Kenneth Grahame</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Fowey was Quiller-Couch\'s main residence from 1892 onwards, and a number of his stories are set in \'Troy Town\', a thinly disguised Fowey.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The du Maurier Festival Society runs the Fowey Festival of Arts and Literature each May,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> the month of her birth. \n</p><p>Various visual artists have had close connection with Fowey and lived there, including painters <a href="/wiki/Fred_Yates" title="Fred Yates">Fred Yates</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Litten" title="Andrew Litten">Andrew Litten</a><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and Amanda Hoskin who primarily paints the local coastline.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>. Fowey holds an annual Christmas craft market.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The surrounding coastline of Fowey is popular with fishermen and spear-fishermen. Many sea creatures can be seen all around the Cornish shoreline, including <a href="/wiki/Mullet_(fish)" title="Mullet (fish)">mullet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bass_(fish)" title="Bass (fish)">bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mackerel" title="Mackerel">mackerel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homarus_gammarus" title="Homarus gammarus">lobsters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuttlefish" title="Cuttlefish">cuttlefish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Many of the species can be seen in the Fowey Aquarium in the heart of the town, which includes a very rare Albino Bull Huss.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Fowey_Yacht_Club" title="Royal Fowey Yacht Club">Royal Fowey Yacht Club</a> is based on the harbour front.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A Pilot Gig Rowing Club races in and around Cornwall, with an event at Fowey being held the same week as the Regatta. The club launches from Caffa Mill slip.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Fowey Golf Club was founded in 1894 and continued until the late 1940s.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_services">Public services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Public services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A doctors\' surgery called the \'Fowey River Practice\' is situated in Rawlings Lane, and is part of a group including two other surgeries in the Fowey River Practice group, which are situated at Par and Polruan.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit Elizabethan poet and clergyman was the son of the Rector of Fowey.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hugh_Peters" class="mw-redirect" title="Hugh Peters">Hugh Peters</a> (or Peter), a 17th-century preacher, was born at Fowey.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mary_Bryant" title="Mary Bryant">Mary Bryant</a> (born 1765) was born in Fowey before being <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transported</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Convict" title="Convict">convict</a> to the colony of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, where she became one of the first escapees.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Grahame" title="Kenneth Grahame">Kenneth Grahame</a> (1859\xe2\x80\x931932) most famous for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wind_in_the_Willows" title="The Wind in the Willows">The Wind in the Willows</a></i> (1908) lived for part of the year in Fowey during the 1890s and into the early part of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Quiller-Couch" title="Arthur She settled in Fowey, dying here in 1964.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Leo_Walmsley" title="Leo Walmsley">Leo Walmsley</a> (1892 \xe2\x80\x93 1966) was an English writer. He died in Fowey, Cornwall, on 8 June and his house 21 Passage Street was named <i>Bramblewick</i> after his popular book series.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Clarence_F._Leary" title="Clarence F. Leary">Clarence F. Leary</a> a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> officer and <a href="/wiki/Navy_Cross_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Navy Cross (United States)">Navy Cross</a> winner was born here on 11 January 1894.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Daphne_du_Maurier" title="Daphne du Maurier">Daphne du Maurier</a> (1907\xe2\x80\x931989) English author and playwright; lived in Fowey. His family maintains a lifelong association with the village.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>A number of wealthy entertainers have second homes around the town including <a href="/wiki/Richard_and_Judy" title="Richard and Judy">Richard Madeley and Judy Finnigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dawn_French" title="Dawn French">Dawn French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Hunniford" title="Gloria Hunniford">Gloria Hunniford</a>, and former <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Peter" title="Blue Peter">Blue Peter</a></i> presenter <a href="/wiki/Janet_Ellis" title="Janet Ellis">Janet Ellis</a>\n<sup id="cite_ref-2faces_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2faces-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20140729194902/http://www.magakernow.org.uk/pdf/placename_masterlist.pdf">"List of Place-names agreed by the MAGA Signage Panel"</a> <span class="cs1-format">(PDF)</span>.
