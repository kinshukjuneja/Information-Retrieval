section of the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennine range</a> of hills which runs north\xe2\x80\x93south through northern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. It lies between <a href="/wiki/Carlisle" title="Carlisle">Carlisle</a> to the west and <a href="/wiki/Darlington" title="Darlington">Darlington</a> to the east. It is bounded to the north by the <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne Valley</a> and to the south by the <a href="/wiki/Stainmore" title="Stainmore">Stainmore</a> Gap.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Overview"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Overview</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Visitor_centre"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Visitor centre</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The North Pennines was designated as an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) in 1988 for its <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a> scenery, the product of centuries of farming and lead-mining. At almost 770 square miles (2,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) it is the second largest of the 49 AONBs in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The landscape of the North Pennines AONB is one of open heather moors between deep dales, upland rivers, hay meadows and stone-built villages, some of which contain the legacies of a mining and industrial past. The area has previously been mined and quarried for minerals such as barytes, coal fluorspar, iron, lead, witherite and zinc.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 2013, a Canadian mining company were allowed to test drill for zinc around <a href="/wiki/Allenheads" title="Allenheads">Allenheads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nenthead" title="Nenthead">Nenthead</a>. They said the region was sitting on a "world-class" deposit of zinc and predicted that a new mine in the area could produce 1,000,000 tonnes (980,000 long tons; 1,100,000 short tons) of zinc ore per year.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the North Pennines are: 40% of the UK\'s upland hay meadows; 30% of England\'s upland heathland and 27% of its <a href="/wiki/Blanket_bog" title="Blanket bog">blanket bog</a>; 80% of England\'s <a href="/wiki/Black_grouse" title="Black grouse">black grouse</a> (and also breeding <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_owl" title="Short-eared owl">short-eared owl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_snipe" title="Common snipe">common snipe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_redshank" title="Common redshank">common redshank</a>); 36% of the AONB designated as <a href="/wiki/Sites_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" class="mw-redirect" title="Sites of Special Scientific Interest">Sites of Special Scientific Interest</a>; <a href="/wiki/Red_squirrel" title="Red squirrel">red squirrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otter" title="Otter">otters</a> and rare arctic alpine plants; 22,000 pairs of breeding <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a> and one of England\'s biggest waterfalls \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/High_Force" title="High Force">High Force</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The area shares a boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales_National_Park" title="Yorkshire Dales National Park">Yorkshire Dales National Park</a> in the south and extends as far as the Tyne Valley, just south of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a> in the north.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the many walking routes in the North Pennines is <a href="/wiki/Isaac%27s_Tea_Trail" class="mw-redirect" title="Isaac&#39;s Tea Trail">Isaac\'s Tea Trail</a>, a circular route of 37 miles (60&#160;km) around the area, running from <a href="/wiki/Ninebanks" title="Ninebanks">Ninebanks</a> via <a href="/wiki/Allendale,_Northumberland" title="Allendale, Northumberland">Allendale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nenthead" title="Nenthead">Nenthead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alston,_Cumbria" title="Alston, Cumbria">Alston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  In addition to this, a large section of the <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Way" title="Pennine Way">Pennine Way</a> falls in the AONB,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> including one of the most celebrated stretches through <a href="/wiki/Teesdale" title="Teesdale">Teesdale</a>, a lush valley with dramatic river scenery including the twin attractions of High Force and <a href="/wiki/Cauldron_Snout" title="Cauldron Snout">Cauldron Snout</a>.\n</p><p>The AONB is notable for rare flora and fauna, including wild alpine plants not found elsewhere in Britain. It is also home to red squirrels and diverse birds of prey. The impressive landscape of the North Pennines \xe2\x80\x93 from High Force on the River Tees to the sweeping valley of High Cup Gill above <a href="/wiki/Dufton" title="Dufton">Dufton</a> \xe2\x80\x93 are the product of millions of years of geological processes. The worldwide significance of the geology found in the area was recognised in 2003 when the AONB became Britain\'s first <a href="/wiki/European_Geopark" class="mw-redirect" title="European Geopark">European Geopark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> A year later the area become one of the founding members of the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>-assisted <a href="/wiki/Global_Geoparks_Network" title="Global Geoparks Network">Global Geopark</a> family and in 2015 it was accorded official status as a UNESCO Global Geopark.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Geoparks are areas with outstanding geological heritage where this is being used to support sustainable development.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another of the North Pennines\' oddities is that it is home to England\'s only named wind, the <a href="/wiki/Helm_Wind" title="Helm Wind">Helm Wind</a>. It has caught out many walkers traversing the plateaux around <a href="/wiki/Cross_Fell" title="Cross Fell">Cross Fell</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Eden,_Cumbria" title="River Eden, Cumbria">Eden Valley</a> fellside, and the valleys between <a href="/wiki/Alston,_Cumbria" title="Alston, Cumbria">Alston</a> and Dufton.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The great English poet <a href="/wiki/W._H._Auden" title="W. H. Auden">W. H. Auden</a> spent much time in this area and some forty poems and two plays are set here. Auden visited the area in 1919 and "five years later was writing poems about Alston Moor and Allendale."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He referred to the region as his "Mutterland", his "great good place", and equated it with his idea of Eden.
