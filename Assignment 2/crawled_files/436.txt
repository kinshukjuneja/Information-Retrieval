/>.uk</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Plymouth City Council</b> is the unitary authority for <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>. It has traditionally been controlled by Labour or the Conservatives, with <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> rarely winning seats. \n</p><p>The council is run by the "leader and cabinet" model, where the leader of the council \xe2\x80\x93 normally leader of the majority party \xe2\x80\x93 is selected by fellow councillors, who also select the executive, commonly referred to as the cabinet. The leader of the council since May 2018 is <a href="/wiki/Tudor_Evans" title="Tudor Evans">Tudor Evans</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, and the opposition group leader is Ian Bowyer of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Plymouth_City_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Plymouth was recorded as a <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">borough</a> from 1276 and was incorporated in 1439. In April 1889, as a result of the reform of <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>, Plymouth became a self-administering <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a>. In 1914, the Borough of Plymouth was united with the adjoining boroughs of <a href="/wiki/Devonport,_Plymouth" title="Devonport, Plymouth">Devonport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stonehouse,_Plymouth" title="Stonehouse, Plymouth">Stonehouse</a> and in 1928, became a <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">city</a> by <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1971, a Local Government White Paper was published which would have left Plymouth, a town of 250,000 people, being administered from a council based at Exeter, a smaller city on the other side of Devon. This led to Plymouth lobbying for the creation of a new county of "Tamarside", to include Plymouth, Torpoint, Saltash, and their rural hinterland. This campaign was unsuccessful, and on 1 April 1974, Plymouth surrendered control of several areas to Devon County Council.\n</p><p>This continued until 1 April 1998, when, under the recommendations of the Banham Commission, Plymouth was designated to become a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a>, and Plymouth City Council was established.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plymouth_City_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:City_of_Plymouth_coat_of_arms.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> of the City of Plymouth</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> of the City of Plymouth show the four <a href="/wiki/Fortified_tower" title="Fortified tower">towers</a> of the old <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Castle" title="Plymouth Castle">Plymouth Castle</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Saltire" title="Saltire">saltire</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Andrew">Saint Andrew</a>, who is the patron of Plymouth\'s oldest church. The <a href="/wiki/Crest_(heraldry)" title="Crest (heraldry)">crest</a> is a blue <a href="/wiki/Naval_crown" title="Naval crown">naval crown</a> with a red <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">anchor</a> held in a lion\'s paw. The crown and anchor were part of the crest of the former County Borough of Devonport and represent the importance of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> in the life of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a>, <i>Turris Fortissima est Nomen Jehova</i>, means "The strongest tower is the name of the Lord".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers_and_functions">Powers and functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plymouth_City_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Powers and functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Plymouth City Council appoints four members to the <a href="/wiki/Devon_and_Somerset_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Devon and Somerset Fire and Rescue Service">Devon and Somerset Combined Fire Authority</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is also responsible for arranging the elections for local <a href="/wiki/Member_of_parliament" title="Member of parliament">Members of Parliament</a>; in 2017 it was criticised after more than six thousand votes were not included in the electoral result for one constituency,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and hundreds of applications for postal votes were not dealt with by polling day.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plymouth_City_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_City_Council_election,_2018" title="Plymouth City Council election, 2018">Plymouth City Council election, 2018</a></div>\n<p>Elections to Plymouth City Council happen once every year in May, for three years out of every four. Elected councillors serve terms of four years. This is because the council is split into thirds, with one third elected each year except the "fallow" year.\n</p><p>The council is traditionally dominated by the Labour and Conservative parties, with independents and the Liberal Democrats rarely winning seats. The <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">UK Independence Party</a> gained three seats at the local elections in 2014.\n</p><p>At present, Labour has 31 councillors and the Conservatives have 28.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_Mayoralty">Lord Mayoralty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plymouth_City_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lord Mayoralty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Lord_Mayors_of_Plymouth" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Lord Mayors of Plymouth">List of Lord Mayors of Plymouth</a></div>\n<p>Plymouth has had a mayor in some form since 1439, and this tradition continued until 1934, when the king granted Plymouth the honour of having a Lord Mayor.\n</p><p>The role of the Lord Mayor is largely ceremonial, and has evolved into a figurehead position which is the public, non-political image of Plymouth City Council. The Lord Mayor chairs council meetings in the Council Chamber. The position usually rotates between the Conservatives and Labour, and is chosen on the third Friday of May. He then chooses the Deputy Lord Mayor.\n</p><p>The Lord Mayor\'s official residence is 3 Elliot Terrace, located on the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Hoe" title="Plymouth Hoe">Hoe</a>. Once a home of Waldorf and <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Astor" class="mw-redirect" title="Nancy Astor">Nancy Astor</a>, it was given by <a href="/wiki/Lady_Astor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lady Astor">Lady Astor</a> to the City of Plymouth as an official residence for future Lord Mayors and is also used today for civic hospitality, as lodgings for visiting dignitaries and High Court judges, and it is also available to hire for private events. The <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Civic_Centre" title="Plymouth Civic Centre">Civic Centre</a> municipal office building in Armada Way became a listed building in June 2007 because of its quality and period features. The Council has sold the building to a private developer, and staff have moved into new accommodation elsewhere in the city.
