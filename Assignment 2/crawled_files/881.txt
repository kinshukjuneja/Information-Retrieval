class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Writer of children\'s literature</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This is a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">family name</a> is <a href="/wiki/Huang_(surname)" title="Huang (surname)">Huang (Wong)</a>.</div>\n<p><b>Huang Qingyun</b> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">\xe9\xbb\x83\xe6\x85\xb6\xe9\x9b\xb2</span>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade\xe2\x80\x93Giles">Wade\xe2\x80\x93Giles</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile"><b>Huang Ch\'ing-y\xc3\xbcn</b></span></i>; 1920 \xe2\x80\x93 20 September 2018), or <b>Wong Hing-wan</b> in <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a>, was a Hong Kong-Chinese writer of <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_literature" title="Children&#39;s literature">children\'s literature</a> and magazine publisher. She published the only Chinese-language children\'s magazine in China and Hong Kong during World War II, and later served as Vice President of the <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> Writers Association. Considered a pioneer of children\'s literature in Hong Kong, she received the Best Artist Award from the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Arts_Development_Council" title="Hong Kong Arts Development Council">Hong Kong Arts Development Council</a> in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-Link1984_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Link1984-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huang_Qingyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Huang was born in <a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">British Hong Kong</a> in 1920. She studied at <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen_University" title="Sun Yat-sen University">Sun Yat-sen University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a> (Canton), <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>, China, and received her master\'s degree from the <a href="/wiki/Teachers_College,_Columbia_University" title="Teachers College, Columbia University">Teachers College, Columbia University</a> in New York, the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Link1984_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Link1984-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1941, she established the bi-monthly magazine <i>Modern Children</i> (\xe6\x96\xb0\xe5\x85\x92\xe7\xab\xa5), the only Chinese-language children\'s magazine in China and Hong Kong that was published during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In "Letters to Big Sister Wan", a distinguished feature in the magazine, Huang, using the pen name <b>Sister Wan</b> (\xe9\x9b\xb2\xe5\xa7\x90\xe5\xa7\x90), corresponded with young readers. She encouraged them to study and helped them cope with life in wartime, when Hong Kong and much of China fell under Japanese occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hkpl_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hkpl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the end of World War II, the British authorities closed Huang\'s magazine in 1948 for its alleged sympathy for the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Chinese Communist</a> cause. She moved to Guangzhou and stayed in mainland China after the Communist takeover in 1949. She served as vice-president of the Guangdong Writers Association in the People\'s Republic of China.<sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Huang moved back to Hong Kong in the late 1980s. Still active in the literary circle, she also became widely known as the mother-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Lo_Hoi-sing" title="Lo Hoi-sing">Lo Hoi-sing</a>, the political activist who helped Chinese dissidents escape China after the <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_protests_of_1989" title="Tiananmen Square protests of 1989">Tiananmen Square protests of 1989</a>. Lo was the husband of <a href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%91%A8%E8%9C%9C%E8%9C%9C" class="extiw" title="zh:\xe5\x91\xa8\xe8\x9c\x9c\xe8\x9c\x9c">Chow Mat-mat</a> (Zhou Mimi), who also became a well known children\'s writer under her mother\'s influence.<sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Huang died on 20 September 2018, at the age of 98.<sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_works_and_awards">Selected works and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huang_Qingyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Selected works and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Huang published more than 60 books over a career spanning more than seven decades. Her works include:<sup id="cite_ref-hkpl_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hkpl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>The Strange Red Star</i> (\xe5\xa5\x87\xe7\x95\xb0\xe7\x9a\x84\xe7\xb4\x85\xe6\x98\x9f, 1956)</li>\n<li><i>Flowers in Full Bloom</i> (\xe8\x8a\xb1\xe5\x85\x92\xe6\x9c\xb5\xe6\x9c\xb5\xe9\x96\x8b, 1966)</li>\n<li><i>Daughter of the Moon</i> (\xe6\x9c\x88\xe4\xba\xae\xe7\x9a\x84\xe5\xa5\xb3\xe5\x85\x92, 1982)</li>\n<li><i>A Golden Childhood</i> (\xe9\x87\x91\xe8\x89\xb2\xe7\xab\xa5\xe5\xb9\xb4, 1985)</li></ul>\n<p>She twice won the Hong Kong Chinese Literature Prize for Children\'s Literature, for <i>The Smart Dog and the Ever-Changing Cat</i> (\xe8\x81\xb0\xe6\x98\x8e\xe7\x8b\x97\xe5\x92\x8c\xe7\x99\xbe\xe8\xae\x8a\xe8\xb2\x93) and <i>Strange Adventure of the Cat QQ</i> (\xe8\xb2\x93\xe5\x92\xaaQQ\xe7\x9a\x84\xe5\xa5\x87\xe9\x81\x87).<sup id="cite_ref-hkpl_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hkpl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, she received the Best Artist Award from the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Arts_Development_Council" title="Hong Kong Arts Development Council">Hong Kong Arts Development Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ng_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
