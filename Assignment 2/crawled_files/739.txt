is a country in <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_legend" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian legend">Arthurian legend</a>, particularly in the story of <a href="/wiki/Tristan_and_Iseult" title="Tristan and Iseult">Tristan and Iseult</a>. Said to border <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, it is most notable as the home of the hero <a href="/wiki/Tristan" title="Tristan">Tristan</a>, whose father was king. In later traditions Lyonesse is said to have sunk beneath the waves some time after the Tristan stories take place, making it similar to <a href="/wiki/Ys" title="Ys">Ys</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Lost_lands" title="Lost lands">lost lands</a> in medieval <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic</a> tales, and perhaps connecting it with the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyonesse_in_Arthurian_legend">Lyonesse in Arthurian legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lyonesse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lyonesse in Arthurian legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In medieval Arthurian legend, there are no references to the sinking of Lyonesse, because the name originally referred to a still-existing place. Lyonesse is an English alteration of French <i>L\xc3\xa9oneis</i> or <i>L\xc3\xa9onois</i> (earlier <i>Lo\xc3\xb6nois</i>), a development of <i>Lodonesia</i>, the Latin name for <a href="/wiki/Lothian" title="Lothian">Lothian</a> in Scotland. Continental writers of Arthurian romances were often puzzled by the internal geography of Great Britain; thus it is that the author French <a href="/wiki/Prose_Tristan" title="Prose Tristan">Prose <i>Tristan</i></a> appears to place L\xc3\xa9onois contiguous, by land, to <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. \n</p><p>In English adaptations of the French tales, L\xc3\xa9onois, now "Lyonesse", becomes a kingdom wholly distinct from Lothian, and closely associated with the Cornish region, though its exact geographical location remained unspecified. The name was not attached to Cornish legends of lost coastal lands until the reign of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>, however.<sup id="cite_ref-Bivar_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bivar-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, the legendary lost land between Land\'s End and Scilly has a distinct Cornish name: <i>Lethowsow</i>. This derives from the Cornish name for the Seven Stones reef, on the reputed site of the lost land\'s capital and the site of the notorious wreck of the <a href="/wiki/SS_Torrey_Canyon" title="SS Torrey Canyon"><i>Torrey Canyon</i></a>. The name means "the milky ones", from the constant white water surrounding the reef.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Alfred, Lord Tennyson</a>\'s Arthurian epic <i><a href="/wiki/Idylls_of_the_King" title="Idylls of the King">Idylls of the King</a></i> describes Lyonesse as the site of the final battle between Arthur and <a href="/wiki/Mordred" title="Mordred">Mordred</a>. One passage in particular references legends of Lyonesse as a land fated to sink beneath the ocean:\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>Then rose the King and moved his host by night<br />\nAnd ever pushed Sir Mordred, league by league,<br />\nBack to the sunset bound of Lyonesse\xe2\x80\x94<br />\nA land of old upheaven from the abyss<br />\nBy fire, to sink into the abyss again;<br />\nWhere fragments of forgotten peoples dwelt,<br />\nAnd the long mountains ended in a coast<br />\nOf ever-shifting sand, and far away<br />\nThe phantom circle of a moaning sea.\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p>Deriving from a false etymology of Lyonesse, the \'City of Lions\' was said in some later traditions to be the capital of the legendary kingdom, situated on what is today the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Stones_reef" class="mw-redirect" title="Seven Stones reef">Seven Stones reef</a>, some eighteen miles west of Land\'s End and eight miles north-east of the Isles of Scilly.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogues_in_Celtic_mythology">Analogues in Celtic mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lyonesse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Analogues in Celtic mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The legend of a sunken kingdom appears in both <a href="/wiki/Cornish_mythology" title="Cornish mythology">Cornish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breton_mythology" title="Breton mythology">Breton mythology</a>. In Christian times it came to be viewed as a sort of Cornish <a href="/wiki/Sodom_and_Gomorrah" title="Sodom and Gomorrah">Sodom and Gomorrah</a>, an example of divine wrath provoked by unvirtuous living.\n</p><p>There is a Breton parallel in the tale of the Cit\xc3\xa9 d\'<a href="/wiki/Ys" title="Ys">Ys</a>, similarly drowned as a result of its debauchery with a single virtuous survivor escaping on a horse, in this case King <a href="/wiki/Gradlon" title="Gradlon">Gradlon</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_mythology" title="Welsh mythology">Welsh</a> equivalent to Lyonesse and <a href="/wiki/Ys" title="Ys">Ker Ys</a> is <a href="/wiki/Cantre%27r_Gwaelod" title="Cantre&#39;r Gwaelod">Cantre\'r Gwaelod</a>, a legendary drowned kingdom in <a href="/wiki/Cardigan_Bay" title="Cardigan Bay">Cardigan Bay</a>.\n</p><p>It is often suggested that the tale of Lyonesse represents an extraordinary survival of folk memory of the flooding of the Isles of Scilly and <a href="/wiki/Mount%27s_Bay" title="Mount&#39;s Bay">Mount\'s Bay</a> near Penzance.<sup id="cite_ref-Hind_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hind-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> name of <a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Mount" title="St Michael&#39;s Mount">St Michael\'s Mount</a> is <i>Karrek Loos y\'n Koos</i> - literally, "the grey rock in the wood". <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a> around Penzance still get occasional glimpses at extreme low water of a sunken forest in Mount\'s Bay, where petrified tree stumps become visible. The importance of the maintenance of this memory can be seen in that it came to be associated with the legendary <a href="/wiki/Brython" class="mw-redirect" title="Brython">Brython</a> hero Arthur, although the date of its inundation is actually c. 2500 BC.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyonesse_in_modern_fiction">Lyonesse in modern fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lyonesse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lyonesse in modern fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Walter_de_la_Mare" title="Walter de la Mare">Walter de la Mare</a>\'s "Sunk Lyonesse" (1922) evokes it as a lost world:\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>In sea-cold Lyonesse,<br />\nWhen the Sabbath eve shafts down<br />\nOn the roofs, walls, belfries<br />\nOf the foundered town,<br />\nThe Nereids pluck their lyres<br />\nWhere the green translucency beats,<br />\nAnd with motionless eyes at gaze<br />\nMake ministrely in the streets.<br />\n&#160;<br />\nAnd the ocean water stirs<br />\nIn salt-worn casement and porch<br />\nPlies the blunt-nosed fish<br />\nWith fire in his skull for torch.<br />\nAnd the ringing wires resound;<br />\nAnd the unearthly lovely weep,<br />\nIn lament of the music they make<br />\nIn the sullen courts of sleep:<br />\n<br />\nWhose marble flowers bloom for aye:<br />\nAnd\xe2\x80\x94lapped by the moon-guiled tide\xe2\x80\x94<br />\nMock their carver with heart of stone,<br />\nCaged in his stone-ribbed side.\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p>Lyonesse has been used as a setting for many modern fantasy stories, including:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jack_Vance" title="Jack Vance">Jack Vance</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Lyonesse_Trilogy" title="Lyonesse Trilogy"><i>Lyonesse</i> trilogy</a>.</li>\n<li>Lyonesse makes a small appearance in <a href="/wiki/Gordon_R._Dickson" title="Gordon R. Dickson">Gordon R. To this day the family\'s shield bears a white horse fully bridled with one foot over the waves. The horse leapt, with Vyvyan aboard, and where it landed, there the Vyvyan family set its roots (<a href="/wiki/Mawgan-in-Meneage" title="Mawgan-in-Meneage">Trelowarren</a>).</li>\n<li>Both <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Plath" title="Sylvia Plath">Sylvia Plath</a> published poems referring to Lyonnesse, the latter taking the mythical land\'s name as its title. English composer <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Finzi" title="Gerald Finzi">Gerald Finzi</a> set the Hardy poem, "When I Set Out for Lyonnesse", to music in his song cycle <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_and_Air_and_Rain" title="Earth and Air and Rain">Earth and Air and Rain</a></i> (published 1936).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Sam_Llewellyn" title="Sam Llewellyn">Sam Llewellyn</a> wrote two children\'s books set in the sinking Lyonesse, with original Celtic names for the cast of <a href="/wiki/Arthurian" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian">Arthurian</a> legend: <i>Lyonesse: The Well Between The Worlds</i> (2009) and <i>Lyonesse: Dark Solstice</i> (2010).</li>\n<li>"Lyonesse" is a song, by Cornish folk composer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Gendall" title="Richard Gendall">Richard Gendall</a>, which appears as the title track of the 1982 album by <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Wootton" title="Brenda Wootton">Brenda Wootton</a>.</li>\n<li>In <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Brideshead_Revisited" title="Brideshead Revisited">Brideshead Revisited</a></i>, the narrator describes the <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> of his youth as being "submerged now and obliterated, irrecoverable as Lyonnesse, so quickly have the waters come flooding in..."</li>\n<li>In the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Age_of_Camelot" title="Dark Age of Camelot">Dark Age of Camelot</a></i>, Lyonesse is a partially inundated zone at one end of the land of Albion, filled with ruins and dangerous monsters, many of them undead.</li>\n<li>In the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicles_of_the_Sword" title="Chronicles of the Sword">Chronicles of the Sword</a></i>, Lyonesse is the forebodding domain of the evil Morgana.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_name_Lyonesse">Other uses of the name Lyonesse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
