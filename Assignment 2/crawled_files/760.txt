href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, England, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.\n</p><p>It <a href="/wiki/Source_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Source (river)">rises</a> at <b>Fowey Well</b> (originally <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Fenten Fowi</i>, meaning <i>spring of the river Fowey</i>) about 1-mile (1.6&#160;km) north-west of <a href="/wiki/Brown_Willy" title="Brown Willy">Brown Willy</a> on <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Moor" title="Bodmin Moor">Bodmin Moor</a>, not far from one of its tributaries rising at <a href="/wiki/Dozmary_Pool" title="Dozmary Pool">Dozmary Pool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colliford_Lake" title="Colliford Lake">Colliford Lake</a>, passes <a href="/wiki/Lanhydrock_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Lanhydrock House">Lanhydrock House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restormel_Castle" title="Restormel Castle">Restormel Castle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel" title="Lostwithiel">Lostwithiel</a>, then broadens at <a href="/wiki/Milltown,_Cornwall" title="Milltown, Cornwall">Milltown</a> before joining the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> at <a href="/wiki/Fowey" title="Fowey">Fowey</a>. The estuary is called <b>Uzell</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Usel</i>, meaning <i>howling place</i>). It is only navigable by larger craft for the last 7 miles (11&#160;km). There is a <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> between Fowey and <a href="/wiki/Bodinnick" title="Bodinnick">Bodinnick</a>. The first road crossing going upstream is in <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel" title="Lostwithiel">Lostwithiel</a>. The river has seven tributaries, the largest being the <a href="/wiki/Lerryn" title="Lerryn">River Lerryn</a>. The section of the Fowey Valley between <a href="/wiki/Doublebois" title="Doublebois">Doublebois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Parkway_railway_station" title="Bodmin Parkway railway station">Bodmin Parkway railway station</a> is known as the <b>Glynn Valley</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Glyn</i>, meaning <i>deep wooded valley</i>). The valley is the route of both the A38 trunk road and the railway line (built by the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway" title="Cornwall Railway">Cornwall Railway</a> in 1859). The railway line is carried on eight stone viaducts along this stretch (see <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway_viaducts" title="Cornwall Railway viaducts">Cornwall Railway viaducts</a>).\n</p>\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King-donierts-stone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/King_Doniert%27s_Stone" title="King Doniert&#39;s Stone">King Doniert\'s Stone</a> (2007)</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Donyarth</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la"><b>Doniert</b></i>) or <b>Dungarth</b> (died 875) was the last recorded king of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Cornwall" title="History of Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. He is thought to be the \'Doniert\' recorded on an inscription on <a href="/wiki/King_Doniert%27s_Stone" title="King Doniert&#39;s Stone">King Doniert\'s Stone</a>, a 9th-century cross shaft which stands in <a href="/wiki/St_Cleer" title="St Cleer">St Cleer</a> <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">parish</a> in Cornwall, although he is not given any title in the inscription.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-P56_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P56-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\nAccording to the <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Annales_Cambriae" title="Annales Cambriae">Annales Cambriae</a></i></i>, he drowned in 875. His death may have been an accident, but it was recorded in Ireland as a punishment for collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>, who were harrying the West Saxons and briefly occupied <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> in 876 before being driven out by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CharlesEdwards_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CharlesEdwards-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philip_Payton" title="Philip Payton">Philip Payton</a> states that one must imagine that he drowned in the River Fowey, near King Doniert\'s Stone.<sup id="cite_ref-P56_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P56-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology_and_hydrology">Geology and hydrology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology and hydrology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The upper reaches of the Fowey are mainly moorland giving way to woodland and farmland, predominantly livestock. This means that 63.6% of the catchment is grassland, with a further 18.3% woodland and 10.7% arable land. Of the remaining 7.5%, 2.6% is urban or built-up areas, 2.5% is mountain, heath and bog and the remainder is inland waters.<sup id="cite_ref-NERC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NERC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The catchment area of the River Fowey covers a total of  41,800 acres (65.3 sq. miles)<sup id="cite_ref-NERC2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NERC2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> consisting of kaolinised granite on Bodmin Moor, Devonian slates and grits, and valley gravels.<sup id="cite_ref-NERC2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NERC2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Data collected by the National Water Archive shows that average flow at the Restormel monitoring station is 4.78 cubic m/s and is affected by the reservoirs at <a href="/wiki/Colliford" class="mw-redirect" title="Colliford">Colliford</a> and Sibleyback and by abstraction of water for public supply.<sup id="cite_ref-NERC2_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NERC2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The former quarry of the Glynn Valley China Clay Works has closed down and is now flooded. It was in operation from the 1940s but since 2015 the site has been used for camping.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span The Upper Fowey SSSI is a floodplain on the southern slopes of Bodmin Moor and is designated for its wet <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heath</a> vegetation and <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbaceous">herbaceous</a> valley-floor <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">mire</a> communities.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Lower down the river at Golitha Falls part of the woodland is designated a NNR and is within the Draynes Wood SSSI. At this point the river runs through a gorge and is of particular importance for \xe2\x80\xb3lower plants\xe2\x80\xb3 such as <a href="/wiki/Marchantiophyta" title="Marchantiophyta">liverworts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Golitha (pronounced <i>Goleetha</i>) is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> word for <i>obstruction</i>. There is a 1-3-mile (4.8&#160;km) riverside walk, from the visitor car park.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Golitha Falls is the site of Wheal Victoria copper mine<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Fowey valley is designated as an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> so many hikers, holidaymakers and tourists visit the places of interest and eat freshly caught fish. The river has very pleasant sites and special paths made for hiking and walking along the banks and in the countryside surrounding the towns.\n</p><p>The River Fowey is famous for its sailing because of its natural harbour. In the past it has been visited by up to 7,000 yachts in one season. Almost all sections of the river have been paddled by <a href="/wiki/Kayakers" class="mw-redirect" title="Kayakers">kayakers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canoeists" class="mw-redirect" title="Canoeists">canoeists</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Whitewater" title="Whitewater">whitewater</a> sections high up on the moor, all the way down to the estuary.  Fowey has an excellent local chandlery.\n</p><p>Many fish can be caught in the River Fowey so many fishermen come to enjoy the excellent fishing conditions.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_on_the_river">Transport on the river</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transport on the river">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ferry_services">Ferry services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fowey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ferry services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Fowey to Polruan Passenger and Cycle Ferry</b>. The summer service runs 1 May \xe2\x80\x93 30 September from Whitehouse Slip until 1815 hrs. From 1830 hrs the service operates from Town Quay until 2300 hrs. Continuous service from 0715 hrs Monday \xe2\x80\x93 Saturday and 0900 hrs on Sunday. Please check notice board for further information during August.\nWinter service runs 1 October \xe2\x80\x93 30 April leaving Town Quay. Monday \xe2\x80\x93 Saturday 0715 hrs until 1900 hrs continuous service. Sundays 1000 hrs until 1700 hrs continuous service. Ferry services are subject to weather conditions.\n</p><p><b>Fowey to Bodinnick Vehicle Ferry</b>. The summer service operates 1 April \xe2\x80\x93 31 October, starting at 0700 hrs Monday \xe2\x80\x93 Saturday and 0800 hrs on Sunday and running until 1900 hrs. The winter service operates 1 November \xe2\x80\x93 31 March, and runs until 2045 hrs or dusk (whichever comes first). The service starts 0700 hrs Monday \xe2\x80\x93 Friday, 0800 hrs on Saturday and 0900 hrs on Sunday. N.B. Ferry services are subject to weather conditions.\n</p><p><b>Fowey to Mevagissey Passenger Ferry</b> is a timetabled summer service between Fowey and Mevagissey leaving Whitehouse Slip. Journey time is around 40 minutes, please see leaflet or notice board for sailings.
