the period 1536\xe2\x80\x931691</b> saw the first full conquest of the island by <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and its colonization with <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> settlers from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. This established two central themes in future Irish history: subordination of the country to London-based governments and <a href="/wiki/Sectarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Sectarian">sectarian</a> animosity between Catholics and Protestants. The period saw Irish society transform from a locally driven, intertribal, <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic structure</a> to a centralised, monarchical, state-governed society, similar to those found elsewhere in Europe. The period is bounded by the dates 1536, when <a href="/wiki/King_Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Henry VIII">King Henry VIII</a> deposed the <a href="/wiki/FitzGerald_dynasty" title="FitzGerald dynasty">FitzGerald dynasty</a> as Lords Deputies of Ireland (the new <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> was declared by Henry VIII in 1541), and 1691, when the Irish Catholic <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobites</a> surrendered at Limerick, thus confirming British Protestant dominance in Ireland. This is sometimes called the <b>early modern period</b>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>, by which Henry VIII broke with <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Papal</a> authority in 1536, was to change Ireland totally. While Henry VIII broke English Catholicism from Rome, his son <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">Edward VI of England</a> moved further, breaking with Papal doctrine completely. While the English, the Welsh and, later, the Scots accepted <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, the Irish remained Catholic. <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Queen Mary I</a> then reverted the state to Catholicism in 1553\xe2\x80\x9358, and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a> broke again with Rome after 1570. These confusing changes determined their relationship with the British state for the next four hundred years, as the Reformation coincided with a determined effort on behalf of the English state to re-conquer and colonise Ireland thereafter. The religious schism meant that the native Irish and the (Roman Catholic) <a href="/wiki/Old_English_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English (Ireland)">Old English</a> were to be excluded from power in the new settlement unless they converted to Protestantism.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Re-conquest_and_rebellion_(1536\xe2\x80\x931607)"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Re-conquest and rebellion (1536\xe2\x80\x931607)</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Colonization_and_the_religious_question"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Colonization and the religious question</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#A_new_order?_(1607\xe2\x80\x931641)"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">A new order? id="Re-conquest_and_rebellion_(1536\xe2\x80\x931607)">Re-conquest and rebellion (1536\xe2\x80\x931607)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Re-conquest and rebellion (1536\xe2\x80\x931607)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Tudor_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Tudor conquest of Ireland">Tudor conquest of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" 2x" data-file-width="2994" data-file-height="4203" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hans_Holbein,_the_Younger,_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Henry VIII</b> <br />King of England and Ireland, who founded the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> and began the English re-conquest of the country, by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Younger" title="Hans Holbein the Younger">Hans Holbein the Younger</a></div></div></div>\n<p>There is some debate about why Henry VIII decided to re-conquer Ireland. However the most immediate reason was that the Fitzgerald dynasty of <a href="/wiki/Kildare" title="Kildare">Kildare</a>, who had become the effective rulers of Ireland in the 15th century, had become very unreliable allies of the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor</a> monarchs. Most seriously, they had invited <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">Burgundian</a> troops into Dublin to crown the <a href="/wiki/Yorkist" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkist">Yorkist</a> pretender, <a href="/wiki/Lambert_Simnel" title="Lambert Simnel">Lambert Simnel</a> as <a href="/wiki/King_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King of England">King of England</a> in 1487. In 1535, <a href="/wiki/Silken_Thomas" class="mw-redirect" title="Silken Thomas">Silken Thomas Fitzgerald</a> went into open rebellion against the crown. Henry VIII put down this rebellion and then set about to pacify Ireland and bring it all under English government control, perhaps to prevent it from becoming a base for foreign invasions of England (a concern that was to be sustained for another 400 or more years).\n</p><p>Ireland was changed from a lordship to a full Kingdom under Henry VIII. From the period of the original lordship in the 12th century onwards, Ireland had retained its own bicameral <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Parliament of Ireland</a>, consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Commons" title="Irish House of Commons">House of Commons</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Lords" title="Irish House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. It was restricted for most of its existence in terms both of membership \xe2\x80\x93 Gaelic Irishmen were barred from membership \xe2\x80\x93 and of powers, notably by <a href="/wiki/Poynings%27_Law" title="Poynings&#39; Law">Poynings\' Law</a> of 1494, which required the approval of the English <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a> before any draft bills might be introduced to the Parliament. After 1541, Henry VIII admitted native Irish lords into both houses and recognised their land titles, in return for their submission to him as <a href="/wiki/King_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Ireland">King of Ireland</a>. However, the real power in Ireland throughout this period lay not with the Parliament, but with the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Deputy_of_Ireland" title="Lord Deputy of Ireland">Lord Deputy of Ireland</a>, who was nominated by the King of England to govern Ireland. The Parliament met only when called by the Lord Deputy, when he wanted to pass new laws or raise new taxes. The Lord Deputy\'s permanent advisors were the Irish <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a>.\n</p><p>With the institutions of government in place, the next step was to extend the control of the English Kingdom of Ireland over all of its claimed territory. Henry VIII\'s officials were tasked with extending the rule of this new Kingdom throughout Ireland by the policy of "<a href="/wiki/Surrender_and_regrant" title="Surrender and regrant">surrender and regrant</a>". They either negotiated or fought with the autonomous Irish Kings and lords. This took nearly a century to achieve, and the re-conquest was accompanied by a great deal of bloodshed, as it led to the assimilation \xe2\x80\x93 sometimes abolition \xe2\x80\x93 of lordships that had been independent for several hundred years.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg/150px-Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg" width="150" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg/225px-Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg/300px-Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="554" data-file-height="661" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ireland-Cahir_Castle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cahir Castle \xe2\x80\x93 besieged repeatedly in this period</div></div></div>\n<p>The re-conquest was completed during the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a>, after several bloody conflicts. The <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Rebellions" title="Desmond Rebellions">Desmond Rebellions</a> (1569\xe2\x80\x931573 and 1579\xe2\x80\x931583) took place in the southern province of <a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a>, when the Fitzgerald <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Desmond" title="Earl of Desmond">Earl of Desmond</a> dynasty resisted the imposition of an English governor into the province. The second of these rebellions was put down by means of a forced famine, which may have killed up to a third of Munster\'s population. The most serious threat to English rule in Ireland came during the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years_War_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine Years War (Ireland)">Nine Years War</a> 1594\xe2\x80\x931603, when <a href="/wiki/Hugh_O%27Neill,_Earl_of_Tyrone" title="Hugh O&#39;Neill, Earl of Tyrone">Hugh O\'Neill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Roe_O%27Donnell" title="Hugh Roe O&#39;Donnell">Hugh O\'Donnell</a> the most powerful chieftain\'s in the northern province of <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> rebelled against English government. This war developed into a nationwide revolt where O\'Neill and O\'Donnell successfully obtained military aid from Spain, which was then in conflict with England during the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585)">Anglo-Spanish War</a>. A Spanish expeditionary force was defeated by English forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kinsale" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Kinsale">Battle of Kinsale</a> in 1601. O\'Neill and his allies eventually surrendered to the new <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">Stuart</a> King, James I, in 1603. After this point, the English authorities in Dublin established real control over Ireland for the first time, bringing a centralised form of justice to the entire island, and successfully disarmed the various lordships, both Irish and Old English. O\'Neill, O\'Donnell and their allies subsequently fled Ireland for good in the <a href="/wiki/Flight_of_the_Earls" title="Flight of the Earls">Flight of the Earls</a> in 1607. This removed the last major obstacle to English government in Ireland.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonization_and_the_religious_question">Colonization and the religious question</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Colonization and the religious question">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Reformation_in_Ireland" title="Reformation in Ireland">Reformation in Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plantations_of_Ireland" title="Plantations of Ireland">Plantations of Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a></div>\n<p>The English had little success in converting either the native elite or the Irish people to the Protestant religion. It is an enduring question, why the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant reformation">Protestant reformation</a> failed to take hold among the Irish (among many) lies in the fact that brutal methods were used by crown authority to pacify the country and exploit its resources, which heightened resentment of English rule. An additional reason was a determined proselytising campaign carried out in Ireland by <a href="/wiki/Counter-reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter-reformation">counter-reformation</a> Catholic clergy, many of whom had been educated in <a href="/wiki/Seminaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Seminaries">seminaries</a> on the continent. <a href="/wiki/Irish_College" title="Irish College">Irish Colleges</a> had been established in many countries in Catholic Europe for the training of Irish Catholic priests and the education of the Irish Catholic gentry. Finally, the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>, which had played a major role in disseminating Protestant ideas in Europe, came to Ireland very late.\n</p><p>From the mid-16th and into the early 17th century, crown governments carried out a policy of colonisation known as <a href="/wiki/Plantation_(settlement_or_colony)" title="Plantation (settlement or colony)">Plantations</a>. Scottish and English Protestants were sent as colonists to the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a>, Ulster and the counties of <a href="/wiki/Laois" class="mw-redirect" title="Laois">Laois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Offaly" class="mw-redirect" title="Offaly">Offaly</a> (<i>see also <a href="/wiki/Plantations_of_Ireland" title="Plantations of Ireland">Plantations of Ireland</a></i>). The largest of these projects, the <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a>, had settled up to 80,000 English and Scots in the north of Ireland by 1641. The so-called <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_people" title="Ulster Scots people">Ulster Scots</a> were predominantly <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterian</a>, which distinguished them from the <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> English colonists.\n</p><p>These settlers, who had a British and Protestant identity, would form the ruling class of future British administrations in Ireland. A series of <a href="/wiki/Penal_Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Penal Laws">Penal Laws</a> discriminated against all Christian faiths other than the <a href="/wiki/State_religion" title="State religion">established</a> (Anglican) <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a>. The principal victims of these laws were Roman Catholics and also, from the late 17th century on, adherents of Presbyterianism. From 1607, Catholics were barred from public office and from serving in the army. In 1615, the constituencies of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Irish Parliament</a> were altered so that Protestants might form the majority of 108\xe2\x80\x93102 in any given vote in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Commons" title="Irish House of Commons">Irish House of Commons</a>. The Catholic majority in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Lords" title="Irish House of Lords">Irish House of Lords</a> persisted until the <a href="/wiki/Patriot_Parliament" title="Patriot Parliament">Patriot Parliament</a> of 1689, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth</a> period (1650\xe2\x80\x9360).\n</p>\n<h2><span id="A_new_order.3F_.281607.E2.80.931641.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="A_new_order?_(1607\xe2\x80\x931641)">A new order? (1607\xe2\x80\x931641)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: A new order? (1607\xe2\x80\x931641)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the early years of the 17th century, it looked possible for a time that, because of immigration of English and Scottish settlers, Ireland could be peacefully integrated into British society. However, this was prevented by the continued discrimination by the English authorities against Irish Catholics on religious grounds.\n</p><p>The pre-Elizabethan Irish population is usually divided into the "Old (or <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a>) Irish", and the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English (Ireland)">Old English</a>, or descendants of medieval <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Norman" class="mw-redirect" title="Hiberno-Norman">Hiberno-Norman</a> settlers. These groups were historically antagonistic, with English settled areas such as <a href="/wiki/The_Pale" title="The Pale">the Pale</a> around <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, south <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a>, and other walled towns being fortified against the rural Gaelic clans. However, by the 17th century, the cultural divide between these groups, especially at elite social levels, was declining. For example, most Old English lords not only spoke the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Gaelic language</a>, but extensively patronised <a href="/wiki/Irish_poetry" title="Irish poetry">Irish poetry</a> and music. Intermarriage was also common. Moreover, in the wake of the Elizabethan conquest, the native population became defined by their shared religion, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>, in distinction to the new Protestant British settlers and the officially Protestant British government of Ireland. During the decades in between the end of the Elizabethan wars of conquest in 1603 and the outbreak of rebellion in 1641, Irish Catholics felt themselves to be increasingly threatened by and discriminated against by the English government of Ireland.\n</p><p>Most of the Irish upper classes, however, were not ideologically opposed to the sovereignty of the King of England over Ireland, but wanted to be full subjects of the triple Stuart monarchy and maintain their pre-eminent position in Irish society. This was prevented by their religious dissidence and the threat posed to them by the extension of the Plantations. The Protestant settler-dominated Government of Ireland tried to confiscate more land from the native landowners by questioning their medieval land titles and as punishment for non-attendance at Protestant services. In response, Irish Catholics appealed directly to the King, first to James I and then <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>, for full rights as subjects and toleration of their religion: a programme known as <a href="/wiki/The_Graces_(Ireland)" title="The Graces (Ireland)">The Graces</a>. On several occasions, the Monarchs appeared to have reached an agreement with them, granting their demands in return for raising taxes. However, Irish Catholics were disappointed when, on paying the increased levies, the King postponed the implementation of their demands. What was more, by the late 1630s, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wentworth,_1st_Earl_of_Strafford" title="Thomas Wentworth, 1st Earl of Strafford">Thomas Wentworth</a>, Charles\'s representative in Ireland, was proposing further widespread confiscations of native land to break the power of the Irish Catholic upper classes. It is likely that this would eventually have provoked armed resistance from Irish Catholics at some point, but the actual rebellion was sparked by a political crisis in Scotland and England that led to <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Wars of the Three Kingdoms">civil war in the three Kingdoms</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Civil_wars.2C_land_confiscations_and_Penal_Laws_.281641.E2.80.931691.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_wars,_land_confiscations_and_Penal_Laws_(1641\xe2\x80\x931691)">Civil wars, land confiscations and Penal Laws (1641\xe2\x80\x931691)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Civil wars, land confiscations and Penal Laws (1641\xe2\x80\x931691)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg/220px-Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg" width="220" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg/330px-Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg/440px-Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="2916" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oliver_Cromwell_by_Samuel_Cooper.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>After Irish Catholic rebellion and civil war, Oliver Cromwell, on behalf of the English Commonwealth, re-conquered Ireland between 1649 and 1651. Under his government, landownership in Ireland passed overwhelmingly to Protestant colonists</div></div></div>\n<p>The fifty years from 1641 to 1691 saw two catastrophic periods of civil war in Ireland 1641\xe2\x80\x9353 and 1689\xe2\x80\x9391, which killed hundreds of thousands of people and left others in permanent exile. The wars, which pitted Irish Catholics against British forces and Protestant settlers, ended in the almost complete dispossession of the Catholic landed elite.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Confederate_War_and_Cromwellian_conquest">The Confederate War and Cromwellian conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Confederate War and Cromwellian conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederate_Wars" title="Irish Confederate Wars">Irish Confederate Wars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">Cromwellian conquest of Ireland</a></div>\n<p>In the mid-17th century, Ireland was convulsed by <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederate_Wars" title="Irish Confederate Wars">eleven years of warfare</a>, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">Rebellion of 1641</a>, when Irish Catholics, threatened by expanding power of the anti-Catholic <a href="/wiki/Long_Parliament" title="Long Parliament">English Parliament</a> and Scottish <a href="/wiki/Covenanters" class="mw-redirect" title="Covenanters">Covenanters</a> at the expense of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">the King</a>, rebelled against English and Protestant domination. The Rising, launched in Ulster by <a href="/wiki/Phelim_O%27Neill" class="mw-redirect" title="Phelim O&#39;Neill">F\xc3\xa9ilim \xc3\x93 N\xc3\xa9ill</a>, provoked an outbreak of anarchic violence around the country, after which it was joined by most Irish Catholic lords and their followers. In some respects, the rebellion was the end product of the long term alienation of Irish Catholics with English policies in Ireland. However, it was sparked off by the fear of impending civil war in the British Isles as a whole.\n</p><p>The rebellion was marked by a number of <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641#Massacres" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">massacres of Protestant settlers</a>, particularly in Ulster, an event which scarred communal relations in Ireland for centuries afterwards.\n</p><p>As a result of the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> in 1642, no English troops were available to put down the uprising and the rebels were left in control of most of Ireland. The Catholic majority briefly ruled the country as <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Ireland" title="Confederate Ireland">Confederate Ireland</a> (1642\xe2\x80\x931649) during the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Wars of the Three Kingdoms</a> in Britain and Ireland. The Confederate regime allied themselves with <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> and the English Royalists, though they did not sign a formal treaty with them until 1649. Had the Royalists won the English Civil War, the result could have been an autonomous Catholic ruled Ireland. However, the Royalists were defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliamentarians</a>, Charles I was executed and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">re-conquered</a> Ireland in 1649\xe2\x80\x931653 on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/English_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="English Commonwealth">English Commonwealth</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">Cromwellian conquest of Ireland</a> was marked by atrocities, such as the massacre of the Royalist garrison at the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Drogheda" title="Siege of Drogheda">Siege of Drogheda</a> in 1649. Another policy implemented by the Cromwellian regime was the deportation of prisoners of war to the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>. Even worse was a scorched earth policy carried out by Parliamentarian commanders to subdue <a href="/wiki/Rapparee" title="Rapparee">Irish guerrilla fighters</a>, which caused famine throughout the country.\n</p><p>As punishment for the rebellion of 1641, almost all lands owned by Irish Catholics were confiscated and <a href="/wiki/Plantations_of_Ireland#Cromwellian_land_confiscation_(1652)" title="Plantations of Ireland">given to British settlers</a>. The remaining Catholic landowners were transplanted to <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a>. <i>See also <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1652" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Settlement 1652">Act of Settlement 1652</a></i>. In addition, Catholics were barred from the Irish Parliament altogether, forbidden to live in towns and from marrying Protestants (although not all of these laws were strictly enforced). It has been calculated that up to a third of Ireland\'s population (4-600,000 people) died in these wars, either in fighting, or in the accompanying famine and plague. The Cromwellian conquest therefore left <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Drogheda" title="Siege of Drogheda">bitter memories</a> - to say the least -  in Irish popular culture.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">Restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">English Restoration</a></div>\n<p>An uneasy peace returned with the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">Restoration of the monarchy</a> in England and <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> made some efforts to conciliate Irish Catholics with compensation and land grants. (See also <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1662" title="Act of Settlement 1662">Act of Settlement 1662</a>). Most Catholics, however were disappointed that the Cromwellian land confiscations were, on the whole, allowed to stand. Protestants, on the other hand, felt that Irish Catholics had been treated far too leniently by Charles, and deserved to be punished for their massacres of Protestant civilians in 1641. In 1678, there was another brief burst of anti-Catholic repression during the <a href="/wiki/Popish_Plot" title="Popish Plot">Popish Plot</a>, when it was rumoured that Irish Catholics were planning another rebellion with French help. Two Catholic Bishops, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_Peter_Talbot" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop Peter Talbot">Peter Talbot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Plunkett" title="Oliver Plunkett">Oliver Plunkett</a> were arrested. Talbot died in prison and Plunkett was hanged, drawn and quartered.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller,_Bt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller%2C_Bt.jpg/200px-King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller%2C_Bt.jpg" width="200" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller%2C_Bt.jpg/300px-King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller%2C_Bt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller%2C_Bt.jpg/400px-King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller%2C_Bt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="3963" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King_James_II_by_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller,_Bt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>James VII and II</b> <br />Irish Catholics, known as <b>Jacobites</b>, fought for James in 1689-91, but they failed to restore him to the throne of Ireland, England and Scotland</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_War_of_the_Two_Kings">The War of the Two Kings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The War of the Two Kings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Williamite_war_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Williamite war in Ireland">Williamite war in Ireland</a></div>\n<p>However, within a generation of the Restoration, Ireland was at war again. In the reign of the Catholic King <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II of England</a>, Irish Catholics briefly looked like recovering their pre-eminent position in Irish society. James repealed much of the anti-Catholic legislation, allowed Catholics into the Irish Parliament and the Army and appointed a Catholic, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Talbot,_1st_Earl_of_Tyrconnell" title="Richard Talbot, 1st Earl of Tyrconnell">Richard Talbot, 1st Earl of Tyrconnell</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Deputy_of_Ireland" title="Lord Deputy of Ireland">Lord Deputy of Ireland</a>. Protestants in Ireland could do little about this turn of events.\n</p><p>However, with the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> of 1688, <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II</a> was deposed by the <a href="/wiki/Convention_Parliament_(1689)" title="Convention Parliament (1689)">English Parliament</a> and replaced by <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William of Orange</a>, with the help of a Dutch invasion force. Irish Catholics backed James to try to reverse the Penal Laws and land confiscations, whereas Irish and British Protestants supported William to preserve their dominance in the country. Richard Talbot, the Lord Deputy, raised a <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> army from among Irish Catholics and seized all the strong points around the country, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, which was <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Derry" title="Siege of Derry">besieged</a> by his men. James, backed by the French King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>, arrived in Ireland in 1689 with French troops. The Siege of Derry was broken when General <a href="/wiki/Percy_Kirke" title="Percy Kirke">Percy Kirke</a> arrived with a relief force.\n</p><p>The same year <a href="/wiki/Marshal_Schomberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshal Schomberg">Marshal Schomberg</a> landed with a major Williamite expedition and <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Carrickfergus_(1689)" title="Siege of Carrickfergus (1689)">captured Carrickfergus</a>. He then advanced south to <a href="/wiki/Dundalk" title="Dundalk">Dundalk</a> where the two armies <a href="/wiki/Dundalk_Camp" title="Dundalk Camp">took part in a long stand-off</a> before retreating into winter quarters. The following year William III landed at Carrickfergus with a multi-national force of reinforcements, including British, Dutch and Danish troops. The two Kings fought for the English, Scottish and Irish thrones in the <a href="/wiki/Williamite_war_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Williamite war in Ireland">Williamite War</a>, most famously at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Boyne" title="Battle of the Boyne">Battle of the Boyne</a> in 1690, where James\'s forces were defeated. Although not militarily decisive, this battle is remembered as a great Williamite victory because James fled Ireland for France after the battle, effectively conceding defeat to William. <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> resistance in Ireland continued for another year however, winning a success at the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Limerick_(1690)" title="Siege of Limerick (1690)">Siege of Limerick</a>, but was finally ended after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Aughrim" title="Battle of Aughrim">Battle of Aughrim</a> in July 1691, when their main army was destroyed. They surrendered at <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a> shortly afterwards. The Jacobite army left the country under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Limerick" title="Treaty of Limerick">Treaty of Limerick</a>, negotiated by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Sarsfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrick Sarsfield">Patrick Sarsfield</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Flight_of_the_Wild_Geese" title="Flight of the Wild Geese">enter French service</a>. The war, while not as destructive as that of the 1640s and 1650s, was nevertheless a shattering defeat for the old Irish Catholic landed classes, who never recovered their former position in Irish society. The Protestant victory in the war is still celebrated today by the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Orange Institution">Orange Order</a> and by many people in Ulster and across the world.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(1536%E2%80%931691)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Protestant Ascendancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Penal_laws_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Penal laws (Ireland)">Penal Laws</a> (which had been allowed to lapse somewhat after the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">English Restoration</a>) were re-applied with great harshness after this war, as the Protestant elite wanted to ensure that the Irish Catholic landed classes would not be in a position to repeat their rebellions of the 17th century. In fact, many new Penal Laws were introduced, which put restrictions on Catholics inheriting property. As a result of these laws, Catholic landownership fell from around 14% in 1691 to around 5% in the course of the next century.\n</p><p>In addition, as of 1704, <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterians</a> were also barred from holding public office, bearing arms and entering certain professions. This was in part due to the distrust the mostly English Anglican establishment had for the mostly Scottish Presbyterian community, which by now had become a majority in Ulster. By the end of the 17th century, Ireland\'s population was about 25% Protestant (including all denominations) of whom Anglicans (about 13%) formed the ruling <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a>.\nFor the 18th century see <a href="/wiki/Ireland_1691-1801" class="mw-redirect" title="Ireland 1691-1801">Ireland 1691-1801</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
