The eastern branch has its source near <a href="/wiki/St_Cleer" title="St Cleer">St Cleer</a> only 0.31 miles (0.5&#160;km) away from the <a href="/wiki/Golitha_Falls" class="mw-redirect" title="Golitha Falls">Golitha Falls</a> on the <a href="/wiki/River_Fowey" title="River Fowey">River Fowey</a>, and flows south, passing close to the western outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Liskeard" title="Liskeard">Liskeard</a>. The western branch has its source near <a href="/wiki/Dobwalls" title="Dobwalls">Dobwalls</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:OS:_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:OS:-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>South of Liskeard, the <a href="/wiki/Looe_Valley_Line" title="Looe Valley Line">Looe Valley Line</a> railway follows the course of the East Looe River to Looe. The railway is so close to the river that flooding is a common occurrence.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The lowest stretch of the rivers combine to form the tidal harbour and <a href="/wiki/Looe_Estuary" title="Looe Estuary">estuary</a> of Looe. The combined length of the two rivers (including the tidal confluence) is 30.48 miles (49.05&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The river is tidal to Watergate on the West Looe and <a href="/wiki/Sandplace" title="Sandplace">Sandplace</a> on the East Looe. After Sandplace, the river was connected to the <a href="/wiki/Liskeard_and_Looe_Union_Canal" title="Liskeard and Looe Union Canal">Liskeard and Looe Union Canal</a> so that minerals and stone could be transported from the workings and quarries above <a href="/wiki/Liskeard" title="Liskeard">Liskeard</a> to the harbour at Looe.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  The tidal estuarine section is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">Ria</a> (a drowned river valley) and at low tide, the rivers almost dry up to a very small natural level.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Rivers_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Rivers of the United Kingdom">Rivers of the United Kingdom</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Ekwall, Eilert (1960). <i>The concise Oxford dictionary of English place-names</i> (4 ed.).
