<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Britannia~Brittania</i>, via <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i><i lang="fro" title="Old French language text">Bretaigne</i></i> and Middle English <i><i lang="enm" title="Middle English language text">Breteyne</i></i>, possibly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a> <i><i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">Bryten(lond)</i></i>, probably also from Latin <i>Brittania</i>, ultimately an adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a> name for the island, <i>*Pritan\xc4\xab</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An early written reference to the British Isles may derive from the works of the Greek explorer <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Massalia</a>; later Greek writers such as <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_of_Sicily" class="mw-redirect" title="Diodorus of Sicily">Diodorus of Sicily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> who quote Pytheas\' use of variants such as <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xce\xa0\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xb9\xce\xba\xce\xae</span> (<i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">Prettanik\xc4\x93</i>), "The Britannic [land, island]", and <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">n\xc4\x93soi brettaniai</i>, "Britannic islands", with <i>&#42;<i lang="cel" title="Proto-Celtic language text">Pretani</i></i> being a Celtic word that might mean "the painted ones" or "the tattooed folk", referring to body decoration (see below).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The modern <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> name for the island is <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">(Ynys) Prydain</i></i>. This may demonstrate that the original <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a> form had initial P- not B- (which would give **<i>Brydain</i>) and -t- not -tt- (else **<i>Prythain</i>). This may be explained as containing a stem *<i>prit-</i> (Welsh <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">pryd</i></i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> <i><i lang="sga" title="Old Irish language text">cruith</i></i>; &lt; <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Celtic">Proto-Celtic</a> <i>&#42;<i lang="cel" title="Proto-Celtic language text">kwrit-</i></i>), meaning "shape, form", combined with an adjectival suffix. This leaves us with *<i>Pritan\xc4\xab</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chadwick_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chadwick-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Daibhi_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daibhi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunbavin_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunbavin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oman15-16_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oman15-16-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-snyder_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snyder-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britain_(place_name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Written_record">Written record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britain_(place_name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Written record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first known written use of the word was an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliteration</a> of the original P-Celtic term. It is believed to have appeared within a <a href="/wiki/Periplus" title="Periplus">periplus</a> written in about 325 BC by the geographer and explorer <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Massalia</a>, but no copies of this work survive. The earliest existing records of the word are quotations of the periplus by later authors, such as those within <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus of Sicily</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_historica" title="Bibliotheca historica">history</a> (c. 60 BC to 30 BC), <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Geographica" title="Geographica">Geographica</a> (c. 7 BC to AD 19) and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Naturalis_Historia" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalis Historia">Natural History</a> (AD 77).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to Strabo, Pytheas referred to Britain as <i>Bretannik\xc4\x93</i>, which is treated a feminine noun.<sup id="cite_ref-LSJBrettanike_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSJBrettanike-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Strabo(1.4.2)_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo(1.4.2)-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Strabo(4.2.1)_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo(4.2.1)-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Strabo(4.4.1)_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo(4.4.1)-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Although technically an adjective (<i>the Britannic</i> or <i>British</i>) it may have been a case of <a href="/wiki/Noun_ellipsis" title="Noun ellipsis">noun ellipsis</a>, a common mechanism in ancient Greek. This term along with other relevant ones, subsequently appeared inter alia in the following works:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Pliny referred to the main island as <i>Britannia</i>, with <i>Britanniae</i> describing the island group.<sup id="cite_ref-PlinyE(4.41)_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PlinyE(4.41)-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BritanniaeLewisShort_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritanniaeLewisShort-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a> also used the plural <i>Britanniae</i> in his <i>Carmina</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Catullus(29.20)_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catullus(29.20)-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Catullus(45.22)_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catullus(45.22)-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Avienus" title="Avienus">Avienus</a> used <i>insula <a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albionum</a></i> in his <i>Ora Maritima</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Orosius" title="Orosius">Orosius</a> used the plural <i>Britanniae</i> to refer to the islands and <i>Britanni</i> to refer to the people thereof.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Diodorus referred to Great Britain as <i>Prettanik\xc4\x93 n\xc4\x93sos</i> and its inhabitants as <i>Prettanoi</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Diodorus(5.21.1)_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diodorus(5.21.1)-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Diodorus(5.21.2)_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diodorus(5.21.2)-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, in his <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a>, used <i>Brettania</i> and <i>Brettanikai n\xc4\x93soi</i> to refer to the island group and the terms <i>megale Brettania</i> (Great Britain) and <i>mikra Brettania</i> (little Britain) for the islands of Great Britain and Ireland, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> However, in his <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a>, he referred to both <i><a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Alwion</a></i> (Great Britain) and <i><a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Iwernia</a></i> (Ireland) as a <i>n\xc4\x93sos Bretanik\xc4\x93</i>, or British island.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Marcian_of_Heraclea" title="Marcian of Heraclea">Marcian of Heraclea</a>, in his <i>Periplus maris exteri</i>, described the island group as <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xce\xb1\xce\xaf \xce\xa0\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xb9\xce\xba\xce\xb1\xce\xaf \xce\xbd\xce\xae\xcf\x83\xce\xbf\xce\xb9</span> (the Prettanic Isles).<sup id="cite_ref-MarcianPeriplus_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MarcianPeriplus-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stephanus_of_Byzantium" title="Stephanus of Byzantium">Stephanus of Byzantium</a> used the term \xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbb\xce\xb2\xce\xaf\xcf\x89\xce\xbd (Albion) to refer to the island, and \xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbb\xce\xb2\xce\xb9\xcf\x8e\xce\xbd\xce\xb9\xce\xbf\xce\xb9 (Albionioi) to refer to its people.<sup id="cite_ref-SBEthnika_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SBEthnika-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Aristotle" title="Pseudo-Aristotle">Pseudo-Aristotle</a> used <i>n\xc4\x93soi Brettanikai</i>, <i>Albion</i> and <i>Ierne</i> to refer to the island group, Great Britain, and Ireland, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-ArOntheCosmos_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArOntheCosmos-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a>, in the 6th century AD, used the terms <i><a href="/wiki/Brittia" title="Brittia">Brittia</a></i> and <i>Brettania</i> though he considered them to be different islands, the former being located between the latter and <a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">Thule</a>. Moreover, according to him on Brittia lived three different nations, the homonymous <i>Brittones</i> (Britons), the <i>Angiloi</i> (English) and the <i>Phrissones</i> (<a href="/wiki/Frisians" title="Frisians">Frisians</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Procopius_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Procopius-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>As seen above, the original spelling of the term is disputed. Ancient manuscripts alternated between the use of the <i>P-</i> and the <i>B-</i>, and many linguists believe Pytheas\'s original manuscript used <i>P-</i> (<i>Prettania</i>) rather than <i>B-</i>. Although <i>B-</i> is more common in these manuscripts, many modern authors quote the Greek or Latin with a <i>P-</i> and attribute the <i>B-</i> to changes by the Romans in the time of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> the relevant, attested sometimes later, change of the spelling of the word(s) in Greek, as is also sometimes done in modern Greek, from being written with a double <a href="/wiki/Tau" title="Tau">tau</a> to being written with a double <a href="/wiki/Nu_(letter)" title="Nu (letter)">nu</a>, is likewise also explained by Roman influence, from the aforementioned change in the spelling in Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguist</a> Karl Schmidt states that the "name of the island was originally transmitted as \xce\xa0\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1 (with \xce\xa0 instead of \xce\x92) ... as is confirmed by its etymology".<sup id="cite_ref-schmidt68_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt68-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Barry_Cunliffe" title="Barry Cunliffe">Barry Cunliffe</a>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>It is quite probable that the description of Britain given by the Greek writer Diodorus Siculus in the first century BC derives wholly or largely from Pytheas. What is of particular interest is that he calls the island "Pretannia" (Greek "Prettanik\xc4\x93"), that is "the island of the Pretani, or Priteni". "Pretani" is a Celtic word that probably means "the painted ones" or "the tattooed folk", referring to body decoration - a reminder of Caesar\'s observations of woad-painted barbarians. In all probability the word "Pretani" is an ethnonym (the name by which the people knew themselves), but it remains an outside possibility that it was their continental neighbours who described them thus to the Greek explorers.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_period">Roman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britain_(place_name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>\'s expeditions to the island in 55 and 54 BC, <i>Brit(t)an(n)ia</i> was predominantly used to refer simply to the island of Great Britain. After the Roman conquest under the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> in AD 43, it came to be used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman province of Britain</a> (later two provinces), which at one stage consist of part of the island of Great Britain south of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_wall" class="mw-redirect" title="Hadrian&#39;s wall">Hadrian\'s wall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snyder_pg12_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snyder_pg12-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britain_(place_name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> or Anglo-Saxon, the Graeco-Latin term referring to Britain entered in the form of <i>Bryttania</i>, as attested by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>\'s translation of <a href="/wiki/Orosius" title="Orosius">Orosius</a>\' <i>Seven Books of History Against the Pagans</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name <i><a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a></i> re-entered the language through the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i><i lang="fro" title="Old French language text">Bretaigne</i></i>. The use of <i><a href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historic)">Britons</a></i> for the inhabitants of Great Britain is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>bretun</i>, the term for the people and language of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, itself derived from Latin and Greek, e.g. the <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xce\x92\xcf\x81\xce\xaf\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xcf\x89\xce\xbd\xce\xb5\xcf\x82</span> of <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Procopius_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Procopius-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> It was introduced into <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> as <i><i lang="enm" title="Middle English language text">brutons</i></i> in the late 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britain_(place_name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is much conflation of the terms <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, Great Britain, Britain, and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. In many ways accepted usage allows some of these to overlap, but some common usages are incorrect.\n</p><p>The term <i>Britain</i> is widely used as a common name for the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> of the <i>United Kingdom</i>, or UK for short. The United Kingdom includes three countries on the largest island, which can be called <i>the island of Britain</i> or <i>Great Britain</i>: these are England, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. However the United Kingdom also includes <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> on the neighbouring island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the remainder of which is not part of the United Kingdom. <i>England</i> is not synonymous with <i>Britain</i>, <i>Great Britain</i>, or <i>United Kingdom</i>.\n</p><p>The classical writer, <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, referred to the larger island as <i>great Britain</i> (<i>megale Bretannia</i>) and to Ireland as <i>little Britain</i> (<i>mikra Brettania</i>) in his work, <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i> (147\xe2\x80\x93148 AD).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In his later work, <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i> (c. 150 AD), he gave these islands the names<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">\xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbb\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\xaf\xcf\x89\xce\xbd\xce\xbf\xcf\x82</a> (<i>Alwi\xc5\x8dnos</i>), <a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">\xe1\xbc\xb8\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\xb5\xcf\x81\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1\xcf\x82</a> (<i>Iwernias</i>), and <i>Mona</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>), suggesting these may have been native names of the individual islands not known to him at the time of writing <i>Almagest</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>Albion</i> appears to have fallen out of use sometime after the <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">Roman conquest of Great Britain</a>, after which <i>Britain</i> became the more commonplace name for the island called Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-snyder_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snyder-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the Anglo-Saxon period, <i>Britain</i> was used as a historical term only.  <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">pseudohistorical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i> (<i>c.</i>&#160;1136) refers to the\nisland of Great Britain as <i>Britannia major</i> ("Greater Britain"), to distinguish it from <i>Britannia minor</i> ("Lesser Britain"), the continental region which approximates to modern Brittany, which had been settled in the fifth and sixth centuries by Celtic migrants from the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>Great Britain</i> was first used officially in 1474, in the instrument drawing up the proposal for a marriage between <a href="/wiki/Cecily_of_York" title="Cecily of York">Cecily</a> the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV_of_England" title="Edward IV of England">Edward IV of England</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James</a> the son of <a href="/wiki/James_III_of_Scotland" title="James III of Scotland">James III of Scotland</a>, which described it as "this Nobill Isle, callit Gret Britanee". As noted above it was used again in 1604, when <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">King James VI and I</a> styled himself "King of Great Brittaine, France and Ireland".\n</p><p>The term <i>Great Britain</i> later served to distinguish the large island of Britain from the French region of Brittany (in French <i>Grande-Bretagne</i> and <i>Bretagne</i> respectively). With the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a> it became the official name of the new state created by the union of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> (which then included Wales) with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a>, forming the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kingdom_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kingdom-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In 1801, the name of the country was changed to <i>United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</i>, recognising that Ireland had ceased to be a distinct kingdom and was incorporated into the union. After <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Irish independence</a> in the early 20th century, the name was changed to <i>United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland</i>, which is still the official name. In contemporary usage therefore, <i>Great Britain</i>, while synonymous with the island of Britain, and capable of being used to refer politically to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> in combination, is sometimes used as a loose synonym for the United Kingdom as a whole. For example, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Team_GB" title="Team GB">Team GB</a></i> and <i>Great Britain</i> were used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain_at_the_Olympics" title="Great Britain at the Olympics">United Kingdom\'s Olympic team</a> in 2012 and this usage created controversy as some interpreted it as excluding <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The demonym for a citizen of the United Kingdom is <i>British</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britain_(place_name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
