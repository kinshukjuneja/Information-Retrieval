class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Vehicle Excise Duty</b> (<b>VED</b>) (also known as "vehicle tax", "car tax" or "road tax", and formerly as a "tax disc") is a tax that is levied as an <a href="/wiki/Excise" title="Excise">excise</a> duty and which must be paid for most types of <a href="/wiki/Vehicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicles">vehicles</a> which are to be used (or parked) on <a href="/wiki/Public_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Public road">public roads</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Registered vehicles that are not being used or parked on public roads and which has been taxed since 31 January 1998, must be covered by a <b>Statutory Off Road Notification</b> (SORN) to avoid VED. In 2016, VED generated approximately \xc2\xa36 billion for the <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">Exchequer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rac-revenue_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rac-revenue-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dft-stats-2017_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dft-stats-2017-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A vehicle tax was first introduced in Britain in 1888. In 1920, an excise duty was introduced that was specifically applied to motor vehicles; initially it was <a href="/wiki/Hypothecated_tax" title="Hypothecated tax"><i>hypothecated</i></a> (ring-fenced or earmarked) for road construction and paid directly into a special <a href="/wiki/Road_Fund" title="Road Fund">Road Fund</a>. After 1937, this reservation of vehicle revenue for roads was ended, and instead the revenue was paid into the <i><a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Fund" title="Consolidated Fund">Consolidated Fund</a></i> \xe2\x80\x94 the general pot of money held by government. Since then, maintenance of the UK road network has been funded out of general taxation, of which VED is a part.<sup id="cite_ref-ved-briefing_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ved-briefing-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" enforced by the <a href="/wiki/Driver_and_Vehicle_Licensing_Agency" title="Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency">Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency</a> (DVLA). Until 2014, VED in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> was collected by the <a href="/wiki/Driver_and_Vehicle_Agency" title="Driver and Vehicle Agency">Driver and Vehicle Agency</a> there; responsibility has since been transferred to the DVLA.<sup id="cite_ref-dva_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dva-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The licence is issued upon payment of the appropriate VED amount (which may be zero). Owners of registered vehicles which have been licensed since 31 January 1998 and who do not now wish to use or store a vehicle on the public highway are not required to pay VED, but are required to submit an annual Statutory Off-Road Notification (SORN).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Failure to submit a SORN is punishable in the same manner as failure to pay duty when using the vehicle on public roads.\n</p><p>Until 1 October 2014 a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_licence" title="Vehicle licence">vehicle licence</a> (tax disc) had to be displayed on a vehicle (usually adhered inside the windscreen on the nearside thus easily visible to officials patrolling roads on foot) as evidence of having paid the duty. Since that date, the circular paper discs have not been issued and there is no longer a requirement to display a disc as the records are now stored in a centralised database and accessible using the vehicle <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_registration_plate" title="Vehicle registration plate">registration plate</a> details.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a class="mw-headline" id="Registered_after_1_April_2017">Registered after 1 April 2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vehicle_Excise_Duty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Registered after 1 April 2017">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The biggest changes are that hybrid vehicles will no longer be rated at \xc2\xa30 and that cars with a retail price of \xc2\xa340,000 and over will pay a supplement for the first five years of the standard rate (e.g. any subsequent renewal, even if the change of owner is within the first year).\n</p><p>For example, the 2016 Range Rover Under the previous rates, VED was \xc2\xa3620 for the first year and then \xc2\xa3280 for each subsequent year. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vehicle_Excise_Duty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Policy objective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>The pre-2017 VED structure based on CO<sub>2</sub> bands was introduced in 2001 when average UK new car emissions ratings were 178 gCO<sub>2</sub>/km. The Band A threshold of 100 gCO<sub>2</sub>/km below which cars pay no VED was introduced in 2003 when average new car emissions ratings were 173 gCO<sub>2</sub>/km. Since then, to meet EU emissions ratings targets average new car emissions ratings have fallen to 125 gCO<sub>2</sub>/km. This means that an increasingly large number of ordinary cars fell into the zero- or lower-rated VED bands, creating a sustainability challenge and weakening the environmental signal in VED. This is set to continue as manufacturers meet further EU targets of 95 gCO<sub>2</sub>/km set for 2020.\n</p><p>The reformed VED system retains and strengthens the CO<sub>2</sub>-based First-Year-Rates to incentivise uptake of the very cleanest cars whilst moving to a flat Standard Rate in order to make the tax fairer, simpler and sustainable. To ensure those who can afford the most expensive cars make a fair contribution, a supplement of \xc2\xa3310 will be applied to the Standard Rate of cars with a list price (not including VED) over \xc2\xa340,000, for the first five years in which a Standard Rate is paid.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_goods_vehicles">Heavy goods vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vehicle_Excise_Duty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Heavy goods vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Taxation for use of <a href="/wiki/Large_goods_vehicle" title="Large goods vehicle">heavy goods vehicles</a> on UK roads are based on the size, weight per axle. class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Churchill_and_Chamberlain.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hypothecation of vehicle tax was opposed by <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Churchill</a> and ended by <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Chamberlain</a>\'s government in 1937<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Following the 1888 <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_budget" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom budget">budget</a>, two new vehicle duties were introduced \xe2\x80\x94 the <i>locomotive duty</i> and the <i>trade cart duty</i> (a general <i>wheel-tax</i> also announced in the same budget was abandoned). The <i>locomotive duty</i> was levied at <a href="/wiki/%C2%A3" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc2\xa3">\xc2\xa3</a>5 (equivalent to \xc2\xa3511.14 as of 2016),<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>for each locomotive used on the public roads and the <i>trade cart duty</i> was introduced for all trade vehicles (including those which were mechanically powered) not subject to the existing <i>carriage duty</i>, with the exception of those used in agriculture and those weighing less than 10 <a href="/wiki/Hundredweight" title="Hundredweight">cwt-imperial</a>, at the rate of 5<a href="/wiki/%C2%A3sd" title="\xc2\xa3sd">s.</a> (<a href="/wiki/%C2%A3" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc2\xa3">\xc2\xa3</a>0.25) per wheel.<sup id="cite_ref-t_18880327_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t_18880327-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-t_18880507_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t_18880507-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Road_Fund">The Road Fund</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vehicle_Excise_Duty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The Road Fund">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the budget of 1909, the then <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> announced that the roads system would be self-financing,<sup id="cite_ref-hcl_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcl-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and so from 1910 the proceeds of road vehicle excise duties were dedicated to fund the building and maintenance of the road system.<sup id="cite_ref-hceac_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hceac-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Even during this period the majority of the cost of road building and improvement came from general and local taxation owing to the tax being too low for the upkeep of the roads.<sup id="cite_ref-Plowden_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plowden-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Roads_Act_1920" title="Roads Act 1920">Roads Act 1920</a> required councils to \'register all new vehicles and to allocate a separate number to each vehicle\' and \'make provision for the collection and application of the excise duties on mechanically propelled vehicles and on carriages\'. The <a href="/wiki/Finance_Act_1920" class="mw-redirect" title="Finance Act 1920">Finance Act 1920</a> introduced a \'Duty on licences for mechanically propelled vehicles\' which was to be <i><a href="/wiki/Hypothecation_(taxation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypothecation (taxation)">hypothecated</a></i> \xe2\x80\x94 that is, the revenue would be exclusively dedicated to a particular expenditure, namely the newly established <i><a href="/wiki/Road_Fund" title="Road Fund">Road Fund</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-buchanan_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-buchanan-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Excise_duty" class="mw-redirect" title="Excise duty">Excise duties</a> specifically for <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor vehicles">mechanically propelled vehicles</a> were first imposed in 1921, along with the requirement to display a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_licence" title="Vehicle licence">vehicle licence (tax disc)</a> on the vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-hceac_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hceac-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_hypothecation">End of hypothecation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vehicle_Excise_Duty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: End of hypothecation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The accumulated <i>Road Fund</i> was never fully spent on roads (most of it was spent on resurfacing, not the building of new roads), and became notorious for being used for other government purposes, a practice introduced by <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, when he was Chancellor of the Exchequer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1926, by which time the direct use of taxes collected from motorists to fund the road network was already opposed by many in government, the Chancellor Winston Churchill is reported to have said in a memo: "Entertainments may be taxed; public houses may be taxed...and the yield devoted to the general revenue. But motorists are to be privileged for all time to have the tax on motors devoted to roads? This is an outrage upon...common sense."<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Hypothecation came to an end in 1937 under the 1936 Finance Act, and the proceeds of the vehicle road taxes were paid directly into the Exchequer. The <i>Road Fund</i> itself, then funded by government grants, was not abolished until 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-hcl_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcl-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vehicle_Excise_Duty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since 1998, keepers of registered vehicles which had been licensed since 1998, but which were not currently using the public roads, have been required to submit an annual <i>Statutory Off-Road Notification</i> (SORN).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Failure to submit a SORN is punishable in the same manner as failure to pay duty when using the vehicle on public roads. It was announced in the 2013 Budget that SORN declarations would become perpetual, thus removing the need for annual renewal after the  initial declaration has been made. In June 1999, a reduced VED band was introduced for cars with an engine capacity up to 1100cc.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The cost of 12 months tax for cars up to 1100cc was \xc2\xa3100, and for those above 1100cc was \xc2\xa3155.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions_ratings_tax">Emissions ratings tax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vehicle_Excise_Duty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Emissions ratings tax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the 1990s, political arguments were put forward for the abolition of VED. Among the proposals was a suggestion that VED could be replaced by increased <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon_Oil_Duty" title="Hydrocarbon Oil Duty">fuel duty</a> as an incentive for consumers to purchase vehicles with lower emission ratings. The proposal was politically unappealing, as it would increase costs for businesses and for people  living in rural areas. Rather than abolish VED, the <a href="/wiki/Blair_ministry" title="Blair ministry">Labour government under Tony Blair</a> introduced a new system for calculating of VED that was linked explicitly with a vehicle\'s <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emission" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emission">carbon emissions</a> ratings, as a means for <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_emissions_control" title="Vehicle emissions control">vehicle emissions control</a>. Since then, VED was levied in a system of tax bands based on <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> ratings.<sup id="cite_ref-ved-briefing_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ved-briefing-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the pre-budget report of 27 November 2001 the Government announced that VED for HGVs could be replaced, by a new tax based on distance travelled, the Lorry Road-User Charge (LRUC).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  At the same time, the rate of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon_Oil_Duty" title="Hydrocarbon Oil Duty">fuel duty</a> would be cut for such vehicles. As at the start of 2007 this scheme is still at a proposal stage and no indicated start date has been given. The primary aim of the proposed change was that HGVs from the UK and the continent would pay exactly the same to use British roads (removing the ability of foreign vehicles to pay no UK tax). However, it was also expected that the tax would be used to influence routes taken (charging lower rates to use <a href="/wiki/Motorway" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorway">motorways</a>), reduce congestion (by varying the charge with time of day), and encourage low emissions ratings vehicles.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Tax_year" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax year">tax year</a> 2002\xe2\x80\x932003, it is estimated that evasion of the tax equated to a loss to the <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">Exchequer</a> of \xc2\xa3206 million. In an attempt to reduce this, from 2004 an automatic \xc2\xa380 penalty (halved if paid within 28 days) is issued by the DVLA computer for failure to pay the tax within one month of expiry. A maximum fine of \xc2\xa31,000 applies for failure to pay the tax, though in practice fines are normally much lower.\n</p><p>In June 2005 the government announced plans to adopt a road user charging scheme for all road vehicles, which would work by tracing the movement of vehicles using a <a href="/wiki/Telematics" title="Telematics">telematics</a> system. The idea raised objections on <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> grounds that it would amount to <a href="/wiki/Mass_surveillance" title="Mass surveillance">mass surveillance</a>. An online petition protesting this was started and reached over 1.8 million signatures by the closing date of 20 February 2007.\n</p><p>In April 2009 there was a reclassification to the CO<sub>2</sub> rating based bandings with the highest set at \xc2\xa3455 per year and the lowest at \xc2\xa30, the bandings have also been backdated to cover vehicles registered on or after 1 March 2001, meaning that vehicles with the highest emissions ratings registered after this date pay the most. Vehicles registered before 1 March 2001 will still continued to be charged according to engine size, above or below 1549cc.\n</p><p>In 2009 a consultation document from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> raised the possibility of a VED on all road users including <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cyclists</a>, but there was a strong consensus against this.<sup id="cite_ref-caps_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-caps-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 2010 a new first year rate is to be introduced \xe2\x80\x93 dubbed a <i>showroom tax</i>. This new tax was announced in the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Budget" title="Budget">budget</a>, and the level of tax payable will be based on the vehicle excise duty band, ranging from <a href="/wiki/%C2%A3" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc2\xa3">\xc2\xa3</a>0 for vehicles in the lower bands, up to \xc2\xa3950 for vehicles in the highest band.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
