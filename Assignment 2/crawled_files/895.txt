title="Robert Gsellman">Robert Gsellman</a> pitching for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, the <b>pitcher</b> is the player who throws the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_(ball)" title="Baseball (ball)">baseball</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Pitcher%27s_mound" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitcher&#39;s mound">pitcher\'s mound</a> toward the <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">catcher</a> to begin each play, with the goal of <a href="/wiki/Out_(baseball)" title="Out (baseball)">retiring</a> a <a href="/wiki/Batter_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Batter (baseball)">batter</a>, who attempts to either make contact with the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(baseball)" title="Pitch (baseball)">pitched</a> ball or draw a <a href="/wiki/Base_on_balls" title="Base on balls">walk</a>. In the numbering system used to record defensive plays, the pitcher is assigned the number 1. The pitcher is often considered the most important player on the defensive side of the game, and as such is situated at the right end of the <a href="/wiki/Defensive_spectrum" title="Defensive spectrum">defensive spectrum</a>. There are many different types of pitchers, such as the starting pitcher, relief pitcher, middle reliever, lefty specialist, setup man, and the closer.\n</p><p>Traditionally, the pitcher also bats. Starting in 1973 with the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> and spreading to further leagues throughout the 1980s and 1990s, the hitting duties of the pitcher have generally been given over to the position of <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a>, a cause of some controversy. The <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> and the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Central_League" title="Central League">Central League</a> are among the remaining leagues that have not adopted the designated hitter position.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input href="/wiki/Batter_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Batter (baseball)">batter</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hit_(baseball)" title="Hit (baseball)">hit</a> the ball with the bat. A successful pitch is delivered in such a way that the batter either allows the pitch to pass through the <a href="/wiki/Strike_zone" title="Strike zone">strike zone</a>, swings the bat at the ball and misses it, or hits the ball poorly (resulting in a pop fly or ground out). If the batter elects not to swing at the pitch, it is called a <i>strike</i> if any part of the ball passes through the strike zone and a <i>ball</i> when no part of the ball passes through the strike zone.  A check swing is when the batter begins to swing, but then stops the swing short. If the batter successfully checks the swing and the pitch is out of the strike zone, it is called a ball.\n</p><p>There are two legal <a href="/wiki/Pitching_position" title="Pitching position">pitching positions</a>, the <i>windup</i> and the set position or <i>stretch</i>. Either position may be used at any time; typically, the windup is used when the bases are empty, while the set position is used when at least one runner is on base. Each position has certain procedures that must be followed.  A balk can be called on a pitcher from either position.  A <a href="/wiki/Power_pitcher" title="Power pitcher">power pitcher</a> is one who relies on the velocity of his pitches to succeed.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Generally, power pitchers record a high percentage of <a href="/wiki/Strikeouts" class="mw-redirect" title="Strikeouts">strikeouts</a>.  A <a href="/wiki/Control_pitcher" title="Control pitcher">control pitcher</a> succeeds by throwing accurate pitches and thus records few walks.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Baseball_P.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Baseball_P.svg/220px-Baseball_P.svg.png" width="220" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Baseball_P.svg/330px-Baseball_P.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Baseball_P.svg/440px-Baseball_P.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="612" data-file-height="511" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Baseball_P.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The position of the pitcher</div></div></div>\n<p>Nearly all action during a game is centered on the pitcher for the defensive team. A pitcher\'s particular style, time taken between pitches, and skill heavily influence the dynamics of the game and can often determine the victor. Starting with the pivot foot on the <i>pitcher\'s rubber</i> at the center of the pitcher\'s mound, which is 60&#160;feet 6&#160;inches (18.44&#160;m) from <i>home plate</i>, the pitcher throws the baseball to the <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">catcher</a>, who is positioned behind home plate and catches the ball. Meanwhile, a <a href="/wiki/Batter_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Batter (baseball)">batter</a> stands in the <a href="/wiki/Batter%27s_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Batter&#39;s box">batter\'s box</a> at one side of the plate, and attempts to bat the ball <a href="/wiki/Hit_(baseball)" title="Hit (baseball)">safely</a> into fair play.\n</p><p>The type and sequence of pitches chosen depend upon the particular situation in a game. Because pitchers and catchers must coordinate each pitch, a system of hand signals is used by the catcher to communicate choices to the pitcher, who either vetoes or accepts by shaking his head or nodding. The relationship between pitcher and catcher is so important that some teams select the starting catcher for a particular game based on the starting pitcher. Together, the pitcher and catcher are known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Battery_(baseball)" title="Battery (baseball)">battery</a></i>.\n</p><p>Although the object and mechanics of pitching remain the same, pitchers may be classified according to their roles and effectiveness. The <a href="/wiki/Starting_pitcher" title="Starting pitcher">starting pitcher</a> begins the game, and he may be followed by various <a href="/wiki/Relief_pitcher" title="Relief pitcher">relief pitchers</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Long_reliever" title="Long reliever">long reliever</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Left-handed_specialist" title="Left-handed specialist">left-handed specialist</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_relief_pitcher" title="Middle relief pitcher">middle reliever</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Setup_pitcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Setup pitcher">setup man</a>, and/or the <a href="/wiki/Closer_(baseball)" title="Closer (baseball)">closer</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>, every team uses <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Rubbing_Mud" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Rubbing Mud">Baseball Rubbing Mud</a> to rub game balls in before their pitchers use them in games.<sup id="cite_ref-florida_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florida-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitching_in_a_game">Pitching in a game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span catcher</div></div></div>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A skilled pitcher often throws a variety of different pitches to prevent the batter from hitting the ball well. The most basic pitch is a <a href="/wiki/Fastball" title="Fastball">fastball</a>, where the pitcher throws the ball as hard as he can. Some pitchers are able to throw a fastball at a <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> over 100 miles per hour (160&#160;km/h; 150&#160;ft/s), ex., <a href="/wiki/Aroldis_Chapman" title="Aroldis Chapman">Aroldis Chapman</a>. Other common types of pitches are the <a href="/wiki/Curveball" title="Curveball">curveball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slider" title="Slider">slider</a>, <a href="/wiki/Changeup" title="Changeup">changeup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cutter_(baseball)" title="Cutter (baseball)">cutter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinker_(baseball)" title="Sinker (baseball)">sinker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screwball" title="Screwball">screwball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forkball" title="Forkball">forkball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Split-fingered_fastball" class="mw-redirect" title="Split-fingered fastball">split-fingered fastball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slurve" title="Slurve">slurve</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Knuckleball" title="Knuckleball">knuckleball</a>. These generally are intended to have unusual movement or to deceive the batter as to the rotation or velocity of the ball, making it more difficult to hit. Very few pitchers throw all of these pitches, but most use a subset or blend of the basic types. Some pitchers also release pitches from different arm angles, making it harder for the batter to pick up the flight of the ball. (See <a href="/wiki/List_of_baseball_pitches" class="mw-redirect" title="List of baseball pitches">List of baseball pitches</a>.) A pitcher who is throwing well on a particular day is said to have brought his "good stuff."\n</p><p>There are a number of distinct throwing styles used by pitchers. The most common style is a three-quarters delivery in which the pitcher\'s arm snaps downward with the release of the ball. Some pitchers use a sidearm delivery in which the arm arcs laterally to the torso. Some pitchers use a <a href="/wiki/Submarine_(baseball)" title="Submarine (baseball)">submarine</a> style in which the pitcher\'s body tilts sharply downward on delivery, creating an exaggerated sidearm motion in which the pitcher\'s knuckles come very close to the mound.\n</p><p>Effective pitching is vitally important in baseball. In <a href="/wiki/Baseball_statistics" title="Baseball statistics">baseball statistics</a>, for each game, one pitcher will be credited with <a href="/wiki/Win_(baseball_statistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Win (baseball statistics)">winning</a> the game, and one pitcher will be charged with losing it. This is not necessarily the starting pitchers for each team, however, as a reliever can get a win and the starter would then get a no-decision.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation_and_specialization">Rotation and specialization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rotation and specialization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pitcher&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small><i>(November 2015)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table> \n<p>Pitching is physically demanding, especially if the pitcher is throwing with maximum effort. A full game usually involves 120\xe2\x80\x93170 pitches thrown by each team, and most pitchers begin to tire before they reach this point. As a result, the pitcher who starts a game often will not be the one who finishes it, and he may not be recovered enough to pitch again for a few days. The act of throwing a baseball at high speed is very unnatural to the body and somewhat damaging to human muscles; thus pitchers are very susceptible to injuries, soreness, and general pain.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_(4)-edit3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_%284%29-edit3.jpg/150px-20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_%284%29-edit3.jpg" width="150" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_%284%29-edit3.jpg/225px-20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_%284%29-edit3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_%284%29-edit3.jpg/300px-20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_%284%29-edit3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1510" data-file-height="2310" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:20070616_Chris_Young_visits_Wrigley_(4)-edit3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chris_Young_(pitcher)" title="Chris Young (pitcher)">Chris Young</a> throws a <a href="/wiki/Four-seam_fastball" title="Four-seam fastball">four-seam fastball</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bullpen" title="Bullpen">bullpen</a> pregame.</div></div></div>\n<p>Teams have devised two strategies to address this problem: rotation and specialization. To accommodate playing nearly every day, a team will include a group of pitchers who start games and rotate between them, allowing each pitcher to rest for a few days between starts. A team\'s roster of starting pitchers are usually not even in terms of skill. Exceptional pitchers are highly sought after and in the professional ranks draw large salaries, thus teams can seldom stock each slot in the rotation with top-quality pitchers.\n</p><p>The best starter in the team\'s rotation is called the <a href="/wiki/Ace_(baseball)" title="Ace (baseball)">ace</a>. He is usually followed in the rotation by 3 or 4 other starters before he would be due to pitch again. Barring injury or exceptional circumstances, the ace is usually the pitcher that starts on Opening Day. Aces are also preferred to start crucial games late in the season and in the playoffs; sometimes they are asked to pitch on shorter rest if the team feels he would be more effective than the 4th or 5th starter. Typically, the further down in the rotation a starting pitcher is, the weaker he is compared with the others on the staff. The "5th starter" is seen as the cut-off between the starting staff and the <i><a href="/wiki/Bullpen" title="Bullpen">bullpen</a></i>. A team may have a designated 5th starter, sometimes known as a <a href="/wiki/Spot_starter" class="mw-redirect" title="Spot starter">spot starter</a> or that role may shift cycle to cycle between members of the bullpen or <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_(baseball)" title="Triple-A (baseball)">Triple-A</a> starters. Differences in rotation setup could also have tactical considerations as well, such as alternating right- or left-handed pitchers, in order to throw off the other team\'s hitting game-to-game in a series.\n</p><p>Teams have additional pitchers reserved to replace that game\'s starting pitcher if he tires or proves ineffective. These players are called <i><a href="/wiki/Relief_pitcher" title="Relief pitcher">relief pitchers</a></i>, <i>relievers</i>, or collectively the <i>bullpen</i>. Once a starter begins to tire or is starting to give up hits and runs a call is made to the bullpen to have a reliever start to warm up. This involves the reliever starting to throw practice balls to a coach in the bullpen so as to be ready to come in and pitch whenever the manager wishes to pull the current pitcher. Having a reliever warm up does not always mean he will be used; the current pitcher may regain his composure and retire the side, or the manager may choose to go with another reliever if strategy dictates. Commonly, pitching changes will occur as a result of a <a href="/wiki/Pinch_hitter" title="Pinch hitter">pinch hitter</a> being used in the late innings of a game, especially if the pitcher is in the batting lineup due to not having the designated hitter. A reliever would then come out of the bullpen to pitch the next inning.\n</p><p>When making a pitching change a manager will come out to the mound. He will then call in a pitcher by the tap of the arm which the next pitcher throws with. The manager or pitching coach may also come out to discuss strategy with the pitcher, but on his second trip to the mound with the same pitcher in the same inning, the pitcher has to come out. The relief pitchers often have even more specialized roles, and the particular reliever used depends on the situation. Many teams designate one pitcher as the <i><a href="/wiki/Closer_(baseball)" title="Closer (baseball)">closer</a></i>, a relief pitcher specifically reserved to pitch the final inning or innings of a game when his team has a narrow lead, in order to preserve the victory. Other relief roles include <a href="/wiki/Set-up_pitcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Set-up pitcher">set-up men</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_relief_pitcher" title="Middle relief pitcher">middle relievers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Left-handed_specialist" title="Left-handed specialist">left-handed specialists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Long_reliever" title="Long reliever">long relievers</a>. Generally, relievers pitch fewer innings and throw fewer pitches than starters, but they can usually pitch more frequently without the need for several days of rest between appearances. Relief pitchers are typically guys with "special stuff". Meaning that they have really effective pitches or a very different style of delivery. This makes the batter see a very different way of pitching in attempt to get them out. One example is a sidearm or submarine pitcher.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Position_player" title="Position player">Position players</a> are eligible to pitch in a game as well, this however is rare as these players are not truly trained as pitchers and risk injury. (For instance, in a 1993 game, <a href="/wiki/Jose_Canseco" title="Jose Canseco">Jose Canseco</a> suffered a season ending arm injury after pitching 2 innings.) Plus, they tend to throw with less velocity. For these reasons, managers will typically only use a position player as a pitcher in a blowout loss, or if they have run out of available pitchers in order to avoid a forfeit (the latter typically only happens in extra-inning games). <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Pennington_(baseball)" title="Cliff Pennington (baseball)">Cliff Pennington</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a>, who pitched 1/3 of an inning in game 4 of the <a href="/wiki/2015_American_League_Championship_Series" title="2015 American League Championship Series">2015 American League Championship Series</a> en route to a 14-2 loss, is the only documented position player to pitch during the <a href="/wiki/Postseason" class="mw-redirect" title="Postseason">postseason</a>. The only regulation game in which <i>both</i> pitchers of record were position players occurred on May 6, 2012, when the Baltimore Orioles\' designated hitter Chris Davis was the winner in a 16-inning game against Boston while Red Sox outfielder Darnell McDonald took the loss.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_ball_is_pitched">After the ball is pitched</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After the ball is pitched">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The pitcher\'s duty does not cease after he pitches the ball. Unlike the other fielders, a pitcher and catcher must start every play in a designated area. The pitcher must be on the <a href="/wiki/Pitcher%27s_mound" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitcher&#39;s mound">pitcher\'s mound</a>, with one foot in contact with the <a href="/wiki/Pitcher%27s_rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitcher&#39;s rubber">pitcher\'s rubber</a>, and the catcher must be behind <a href="/wiki/Home_plate" class="mw-redirect" title="Home plate">home plate</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Catcher%27s_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Catcher&#39;s box">catcher\'s box</a>. Once the ball is in play, however, the pitcher and catcher, like the other fielders, can respond to any part of the field necessary to make or assist in a defensive play.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> At that point, the pitcher has several standard roles. The pitcher must attempt to field any balls coming up the middle, and in fact a <a href="/wiki/Gold_Glove_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold Glove Award">Gold Glove Award</a> is reserved for the pitcher with the best fielding ability. He must head over to <a href="/wiki/First_base" class="mw-redirect" title="First base">first base</a>, to be available to cover it, on balls hit to the right side, since the first baseman might be fielding them too far to the baseman\'s right to reach first base before the batter-runner can. Except for the first baseman, the pitcher ordinarily has the shortest run to first base of anyone other than the first baseman, and is the second-most-likely person to make a putout at first base by retrieving a fielded ball thrown by an infielder (typically a first baseman). On <a href="/wiki/Passed_ball" title="Passed ball">passed balls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wild_pitch" title="Wild pitch">wild pitches</a>, he covers home-plate when there are runners on. Also, he generally backs up throws to home plate. When there is a throw from the outfield to third base, he has to back up the play to third base as well.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitching_biomechanics">Pitching biomechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pitching biomechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The physical act of overhand pitching is complex and unnatural to the human anatomy. Most major league pitchers throw at speeds between 70 and 100 mph, with fastballs, the most common pitch, generally ranging from 86-102mph, putting high amounts of stress on the pitching arm. Pitchers are by far the most frequently injured players and many professional pitchers will have multiple surgeries to repair damage in the elbow and shoulder by the end of their careers.\n</p><p>As such, the <a href="/wiki/Biomechanics" title="Biomechanics">biomechanics</a> of pitching are closely studied and taught by coaches at all levels and are an important field in <a href="/wiki/Sports_medicine" title="Sports medicine">sports medicine</a>. Glenn Fleisig, a biomechanist who specializes in the analysis of baseball movements, says that pitching is "the most violent human motion ever measured."<sup id="cite_ref-Dean,_J._2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dean,_J._2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  He claims that the pelvis can rotate at 515\xe2\x80\x93667\xc2\xb0/sec, the trunk can rotate at 1,068\xe2\x80\x931,224\xc2\xb0/s, the elbow can reach a maximal angular velocity of 2,200\xe2\x80\x932,700\xc2\xb0/s and the force pulling the pitcher\'s throwing arm away from the shoulder at ball release is approximately 280 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(force)" title="Pound (force)">pounds-force</a> (1,200&#160;<a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">N</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Dean,_J._2010_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dean,_J._2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><br />\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Overhead_throwing_motion" class="mw-redirect" title="Overhead throwing motion">overhead throwing motion</a> can be divided into phases which include windup, early cocking, late cocking, early acceleration, late acceleration, deceleration, and follow-through.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Training for pitchers often includes targeting one or several of these phases. Biomechanical evaluations are sometimes done on individual pitchers to help determine points of inefficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Mechanical measurements that are assessed include, but are not limited to, foot position at stride foot contact (SFC), elbow flexion during arm cocking and acceleration phases, maximal external rotation during arm cocking, horizontal abduction at SFC, arm abduction, lead knee position during arm cocking, trunk tilt, peak angular velocity of throwing arm and angle of wrist.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some players begin intense mechanical training at a young age, a practice that has been criticized by many coaches and doctors, with some citing an increase in <a href="/wiki/Tommy_John_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tommy John surgery">Tommy John surgeries</a> in recent years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  Fleisig lists nine recommendations for preventative care of children\'s arms.<sup id="cite_ref-Fleisig,_G._S._2009_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fleisig,_G._S._2009-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> 1) Watch and respond to signs of fatigue. 2) Youth pitchers should not pitch competitively in more than 8 months in any 12-month period. 3) Follow limits for pitch counts and days of rest. 4) Youth pitchers should avoid pitching on multiple teams with overlapping seasons. 5) Youth pitchers should learn good throwing mechanics as soon as possible: basic throwing, fastball pitching and change-up pitching. 6) Avoid using radar guns. 7) A pitcher should not also be a catcher for his/her team. The pitcher catcher combination results in many throws and may increase the risk of injury. 8) If a pitcher complains of pain in his/her elbow, get an evaluation from a sports medicine physician. 9) Inspire youth to have fun playing baseball and other sports. Participation and enjoyment of various activities will increase the youth\'s athleticism and interest in sports.<sup id="cite_ref-Fleisig,_G._S._2009_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fleisig,_G._S._2009-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To counteract shoulder and elbow injury, coaches and trainers have begun utilizing "jobe" exercises, named for Dr. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Jobe" title="Frank Jobe">Frank Jobe</a>, the pioneer of the Tommy John procedure.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Jobes are exercises that have been developed to isolate, strengthen and stabilize the rotator cuff muscles. Jobes can be done using either resistance bands or lightweight dumbbells.  Common jobe exercises include shoulder external rotation, shoulder flexion, horizontal abduction, prone abduction and scaption (at 45\xc2\xb0, 90\xc2\xb0 and inverse 45\xc2\xb0).\n</p><p>In addition to the Jobes exercises, many pitching coaches are creating lifting routines that are specialized for pitchers. Pitchers should avoid exercises that deal with a barbell. The emphasis on the workout should be on the legs and the core. Other body parts should be worked on but using lighter weights. Over lifting muscles, especially while throwing usually ends up in a strain muscle or possible a tear.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Other than the catcher, pitchers and other fielders wear very few pieces of equipment. In general the ball cap, <a href="/wiki/Baseball_glove" title="Baseball glove">baseball glove</a> and cleats are equipment used. Pitchers may also keep with them at the mound a bag of powdered rosin. Handling the bag applies a small layer of the rosin to the pitcher\xe2\x80\x99s fingers in order to increase his grip on the ball. \n</p><p>Currently there is a new trend of introducing a pitcher helmet to provide head protection from batters hitting <a href="/wiki/Line_drive" class="mw-redirect" title="Line drive">line drives</a> back to the pitcher. As of  January&#160;2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pitcher&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, MLB approved a protective pitchers cap which can be worn by any pitcher if they choose.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> San Diego Padres relief pitcher, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Torres_(baseball)" title="Alex Torres (baseball)">Alex Torres</a> was the first player in MLB to wear the protective cap.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One style of helmet is worn on top of the ballcap to provide protection to the forehead and sides.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters.com_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Softball" title="Softball">softball</a>, a full face helmet is available to all players including pitchers.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> These fielder\'s masks are becoming increasingly popular in younger fast pitch leagues, some leagues even requiring them.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
