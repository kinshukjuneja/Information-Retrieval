Day">All Saints\' Day</a>, at around 09:40 local time.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In combination with subsequent <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fires</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a>, the earthquake almost totally destroyed <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> and adjoining areas. <a href="/wiki/Seismologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Seismologist">Seismologists</a> today estimate the Lisbon earthquake had a magnitude in the range 8.5\xe2\x80\x939.0<sup id="cite_ref-Gutscher_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gutscher-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Moment_magnitude_scale" title="Moment magnitude scale">moment magnitude scale</a>, with its <a href="/wiki/Epicentre" class="mw-redirect" title="Epicentre">epicentre</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> about 200&#160;km (120&#160;mi) west-southwest of <a href="/wiki/Cape_St._Vincent" title="Cape St. Vincent">Cape St. Vincent</a>. Estimates place the death toll in Lisbon alone between 10,000 and 100,000 people,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> making it one of the <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_earthquakes#Deadliest_earthquakes" title="Lists of earthquakes">deadliest earthquakes in history</a>.\n</p><p>The earthquake accentuated political tensions in the Kingdom of Portugal and profoundly disrupted the country\'s <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese empire">colonial ambitions</a>. The event was widely discussed and dwelt upon by European <a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Enlightenment" class="mw-redirect" title="The Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophers</a>, and inspired major developments in <a href="/wiki/Theodicy" title="Theodicy">theodicy</a>. As the first earthquake studied scientifically for its effects over a large area, it led to the birth of modern <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_engineering" title="Earthquake engineering">earthquake engineering</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1755_Lisbon_earthquake.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1755_Lisbon_earthquake.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1755 copper engraving showing <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> in flames and a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> overwhelming the ships in the harbor</div></div></div>\n<p>The earthquake struck on the morning of 1 November 1755, the holy day of <a href="/wiki/All_Saints%27_Day" title="All Saints&#39; Day">All Saints\' Day</a>. Contemporary reports state that the earthquake lasted between three and a half and six minutes, causing fissures 5 metres (16 feet) wide to open in the city centre. Survivors rushed to the open space of the docks for safety and watched as the water receded, revealing a sea floor littered with lost cargo and shipwrecks. Approximately 40 minutes after the earthquake, a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> engulfed the harbour and downtown area, rushing up the <a href="/wiki/Tagus" title="Tagus">Tagus</a> river<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> "so fast that several people riding on horseback&#160;... were forced to gallop as fast as possible to the upper grounds for fear of being carried away." It was followed by two more waves. Candles had been lit all around the city, in homes and churches alike, for All Saints\' Day, and had then been knocked dangerously over in the earthquake\'s commotion. As the tsunami waters receded, the city began to burn so drastically a true <a href="/wiki/Firestorm" title="Firestorm">firestorm</a> burned for hours in the city, asphyxiating people up to a hundred feet from the blaze.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lissabon-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Lissabon-2.jpg/220px-Lissabon-2.jpg" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Lissabon-2.jpg/330px-Lissabon-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Lissabon-2.jpg/440px-Lissabon-2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="780" data-file-height="636" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lissabon-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A depiction of the 1755 Lisbon earthquake as seen from across the Tagus River</div></div></div>\n<p>Lisbon was not the only <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> city affected by the catastrophe. Throughout the south of the country, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a>, destruction was rampant. The <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> destroyed some coastal fortresses in the Algarve and, in the lower levels, it razed several houses. Almost all the coastal towns and villages of the Algarve were heavily damaged, except <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a>, which was protected by the sandy banks of <a href="/wiki/Ria_Formosa" title="Ria Formosa">Ria Formosa</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Lagos,_Portugal" title="Lagos, Portugal">Lagos</a>, the waves reached the top of the city walls. Other towns of different Portuguese regions, such as <a href="/wiki/Peniche,_Portugal" title="Peniche, Portugal">Peniche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cascais" title="Cascais">Cascais</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Covilh%C3%A3" title="Covilh\xc3\xa3">Covilh\xc3\xa3</a>, which is located near the <a href="/wiki/Serra_da_Estrela" title="Serra da Estrela">Serra da Estrela</a> mountain range in central inland Portugal, were affected. The shock waves of the earthquake destroyed part of Covilh\xc3\xa3\'s castle walls and its large towers. On the island of <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funchal" title="Funchal">Funchal</a> and many smaller settlements suffered significant damage. Almost all of the ports in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> archipelago suffered most of their destruction from the tsunami, with the sea penetrating about 150 m inland. In Spain, the tsunamis swept the <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusian</a> Atlantic Coast, nearly destroying the city of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C\xc3\xa1diz">Cadiz</a>, killing at least 1/3 of its population.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lisbon_1755_tsunami_travel_times.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" November 1755</div></div></div>\n<p>Shocks from the earthquake were felt throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> as far as <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and according to some sources even in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lyell_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lyell-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Tsunamis as tall as 20&#160;metres (66&#160;ft) swept the coast of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and struck <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>. A three-metre (ten-foot) <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> hit <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> on the southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> coast. <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a>, on the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, was also hit, resulting in partial destruction of the "<a href="/wiki/Spanish_Arch" title="Spanish Arch">Spanish Arch</a>" section of the city wall. At <a href="/wiki/Kinsale" title="Kinsale">Kinsale</a>, several vessels were whirled round in the harbor, and water poured into the marketplace.<sup id="cite_ref-Lyell_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lyell-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2015, it was revealed that the tsunami waves may have reached the coast of <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">Brazil</a>, then a colony of Portugal. Such a hypothesis was raised by reviewing letters sent by Brazilian authorities at the time of the earthquake. These letters describe damage and destruction caused by gigantic waves.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although seismologists and geologists have always agreed that the epicentre was in the Atlantic to the West of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, its exact location has been a subject of considerable debate. Early hypotheses had proposed the <a href="/wiki/Gorringe_Ridge" title="Gorringe Ridge">Gorringe Ridge</a> until <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_simulation" title="Earthquake simulation">simulations</a> showed that a source closer to the shore of Portugal was required to comply with the observed effects of the tsunami. A seismic reflection survey of the ocean floor along the <a href="/wiki/Azores%E2%80%93Gibraltar_Transform_Fault" title="Azores\xe2\x80\x93Gibraltar Transform Fault">Azores\xe2\x80\x93Gibraltar Transform Fault</a> has revealed a 50&#160;km-long thrust structure southwest of <a href="/wiki/Cape_St._Vincent" title="Cape St. Vincent">Cape St. Vincent</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Dip-slip" class="mw-redirect" title="Dip-slip">dip-slip</a> throw of more than 1&#160;km. This structure may have created the primary tectonic event.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Casualties_and_damage">Casualties and damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1755_Lisbon_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Casualties and damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg/220px-Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg/330px-Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg/440px-Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1704" data-file-height="2272" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Convento_do_Carmo_ruins_in_Lisbon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Ruins" title="Ruins">ruins</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Carmo_Convent_(Lisbon)" title="Carmo Convent (Lisbon)">Carmo Convent</a>, which was destroyed in the Lisbon earthquake.</div></div></div>\n<p>Economic historian \xc3\x81lvaro Pereira estimated that of Lisbon\'s population at the time, of approximately 200,000 people, some 30,000\xe2\x80\x9340,000 were killed; another 10,000 may have lost their lives in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. However, a 2009 study of contemporary reports relating to the 1 November event found them vague and difficult to separate from reports of another local series of earthquakes on 18\xe2\x80\x9319 November.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Pereira estimated the total death toll in Portugal, Spain and Morocco from the earthquake and the resulting fires and tsunami at 40,000 to 50,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Eighty-five percent of Lisbon\'s buildings were destroyed, including famous palaces and libraries, as well as most examples of Portugal\'s distinctive 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Manueline" title="Manueline">Manueline</a> architecture. Several buildings that had suffered little earthquake damage were destroyed by the subsequent fire. The new Lisbon opera house (the "<a href="/wiki/%C3%93pera_do_Tejo" title="\xc3\x93pera do Tejo">\xc3\x93pera do Tejo</a>"), opened just six months before, burned to the ground. The <a href="/wiki/Ribeira_Palace" title="Ribeira Palace">Royal Ribeira Palace</a>, which stood just beside the <a href="/wiki/Tagus" title="Tagus">Tagus</a> river in the modern square of <i><a href="/wiki/Terreiro_do_Pa%C3%A7o" class="mw-redirect" title="Terreiro do Pa\xc3\xa7o">Terreiro do Pa\xc3\xa7o</a></i>, was destroyed by the earthquake and tsunami. Inside, the 70,000-volume royal library as well as hundreds of works of art, including paintings by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Rubens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antonio_da_Correggio" title="Antonio da Correggio">Correggio</a>, were lost. The royal archives disappeared together with detailed historical records of explorations by <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> and other early navigators. The palace of <a href="/wiki/Henrique_de_Meneses,_3rd_Marquis_of_Louri%C3%A7al" title="Henrique de Meneses, 3rd Marquis of Louri\xc3\xa7al">Henrique de Meneses, 3rd Marquis of Louri\xc3\xa7al</a>, which housed an invaluable library of 18,000 books, was also destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-Li&amp;Lin_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li&amp;Lin-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The earthquake damaged several major churches in Lisbon, namely the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Cathedral" title="Lisbon Cathedral">Lisbon Cathedral</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">Basilicas</a> of S\xc3\xa3o Paulo, Santa Catarina, <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_S%C3%A3o_Vicente_de_Fora" title="Monastery of S\xc3\xa3o Vicente de Fora">S\xc3\xa3o Vicente de Fora</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Nossa_Senhora_da_Concei%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Velha" title="Church of Nossa Senhora da Concei\xc3\xa7\xc3\xa3o Velha">Miseric\xc3\xb3rdia Church</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Real_de_Todos_os_Santos" title="Hospital Real de Todos os Santos">Royal Hospital of All Saints</a> (the largest public hospital at the time) in the <a href="/wiki/Rossio" class="mw-redirect" title="Rossio">Rossio</a> square was consumed by fire and hundreds of patients burned to death. The tomb of national hero <a href="/wiki/Nuno_%C3%81lvares_Pereira" title="Nuno \xc3\x81lvares Pereira">Nuno \xc3\x81lvares Pereira</a> was also lost. Visitors to Lisbon may still walk the ruins of the <a href="/wiki/Carmo_Convent_(Lisbon)" title="Carmo Convent (Lisbon)">Carmo Convent</a>, which were preserved to remind Lisboners of the destruction.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relief_and_reconstruction_efforts">Relief and reconstruction efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1755_Lisbon_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relief and reconstruction efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Pombaline_style" title="Pombaline style">Pombaline style</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lisbon1755hangingdetail.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Lisbon1755hangingdetail.JPG/220px-Lisbon1755hangingdetail.JPG" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Lisbon1755hangingdetail.JPG/330px-Lisbon1755hangingdetail.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/ba/Lisbon1755hangingdetail.JPG 2x" data-file-width="420" data-file-height="256" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lisbon1755hangingdetail.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Detail from above</i>: Executions in the aftermath of the Lisbon earthquake.  At least 34 looters were hanged in the chaotic aftermath of the disaster.  As a warning against looting, King <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I_of_Portugal" title="Joseph I of Portugal">Joseph I of Portugal</a> ordered <a href="/wiki/Gallows" title="Gallows">gallows</a> to be constructed in several parts of the city.</div></div></div>\n<p>The royal family escaped unharmed from the catastrophe: King <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I_of_Portugal" title="Joseph I of Portugal">Joseph I of Portugal</a> and the court had left the city, after attending mass at sunrise, fulfilling the wish of one of the king\'s daughters to spend the holiday away from Lisbon. After the catastrophe, Joseph I developed a fear of living within walls, and the court was accommodated in a huge complex of tents and pavilions in the hills of <a href="/wiki/Ajuda" title="Ajuda">Ajuda</a>, then on the outskirts of Lisbon. The king\'s <a href="/wiki/Claustrophobia" title="Claustrophobia">claustrophobia</a> never waned, and it was only after Joseph\'s death that his daughter <a href="/wiki/Maria_I_of_Portugal" title="Maria I of Portugal">Maria I of Portugal</a> began building the royal <a href="/wiki/Ajuda_Palace" class="mw-redirect" title="Ajuda Palace">Ajuda Palace</a>, which still stands on the site of the old tented camp. Like the king, the prime minister <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Carvalho_e_Melo,_1st_Marquis_of_Pombal" title="Sebasti\xc3\xa3o Jos\xc3\xa9 de Carvalho e Melo, 1st Marquis of Pombal">Sebasti\xc3\xa3o de Melo</a> (1st Marquis of Pombal) survived the earthquake. When asked what was to be done, Pombal reportedly replied "Bury the dead and heal the living",<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and set about organizing relief and rehabilitation efforts. Firefighters were sent to extinguish the raging flames, and teams of workers and ordinary citizens were ordered to remove the thousands of corpses before disease could spread. Contrary to custom and against the wishes of the Church, many corpses were loaded onto <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burial_at_sea" title="Burial at sea">buried at sea</a> beyond the mouth of the Tagus. To prevent disorder in the ruined city, the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Army" title="Portuguese Army">Portuguese Army</a> was deployed and <a href="/wiki/Gallows" title="Gallows">gallows</a> were constructed at high points around the city to deter looters; more than thirty people were publicly executed.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The army prevented many able-bodied citizens from fleeing, pressing them into relief and reconstruction work.\n</p><p>The king and the prime minister immediately launched efforts to rebuild the city. On 4 December 1755, little more than a month after the earthquake, Manuel da Maia, chief engineer to the realm, presented his plans for the re-building of Lisbon. Maia presented four options from abandoning Lisbon to building a completely new city. The first plan was to rebuild the old city using re-cycled materials; this was the cheapest option. The second and third plans proposed widening certain streets. The fourth option boldly proposed razing the entire Baixa quarter and "laying out new streets without restraint". This last option was chosen by the king and his minister.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaiola_pombalina.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Gaiola_pombalina.jpg/220px-Gaiola_pombalina.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Gaiola_pombalina.jpg/330px-Gaiola_pombalina.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Gaiola_pombalina.jpg/440px-Gaiola_pombalina.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="474" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaiola_pombalina.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Model of the <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismically</a> protective wooden structure called "<a href="/wiki/Gaiola_(construction)" title="Gaiola (construction)">gaiola</a> pombalina" (pombaline cage), developed for the reconstruction of <a href="/wiki/Pombaline_Lower_Town" class="mw-redirect" title="Pombaline Lower Town">Pombaline Lower Town</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In less than a year, the city was cleared of debris. Keen to have a new and perfectly ordered city, the king commissioned the construction of big squares, rectilinear, large avenues and widened streets&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the new <i>mottos</i> of Lisbon.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pombaline_style" title="Pombaline style">Pombaline</a> buildings are among the earliest <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismically</a> protected constructions in Europe. Small wooden models were built for testing, and earthquakes were simulated by marching troops around them. Lisbon\'s "new" Lower Town, known today as the <a href="/wiki/Pombaline_Lower_Town" class="mw-redirect" title="Pombaline Lower Town">Pombaline Lower Town</a> (<i>Baixa Pombalina</i>), is one of the city\'s famed attractions. Sections of other Portuguese cities, such as the <a href="/wiki/Vila_Real_de_Santo_Ant%C3%B3nio_Municipality" class="mw-redirect" title="Vila Real de Santo Ant\xc3\xb3nio Municipality">Vila Real de Santo Ant\xc3\xb3nio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a>, were also rebuilt along Pombaline principles.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Casa_Pia" title="Casa Pia">Casa Pia</a>, a Portuguese institution founded by Maria I (known as <i>A Pia</i>, "Maria the Pious"), and organized by Police Intendant Pina Manique in 1780, was founded following the social disarray of the 1755 Lisbon earthquake.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Effect_on_society.2C_economy_and_philosophy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_society,_economy_and_philosophy">Effect on society, economy and philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1755_Lisbon_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effect The earthquake had struck on an important religious holiday and had destroyed almost every important church in the city, causing anxiety and confusion amongst the citizens of a staunch and devout <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> country. Theologians and philosophers focused and speculated on the religious cause and message, seeing the earthquake as a manifestation of <a href="/wiki/Divine_judgment" title="Divine judgment">divine judgment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kendrick_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kendrick-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1755_Lisbon_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A 2009 study estimated that the earthquake cost between 32 and 48 percent of Portugal\'s GDP.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Also, "in spite of strict controls, prices and wages remained volatile in the years after the tragedy. The recovery from the earthquake also led to a rise in the wage premium of construction workers. More significantly, the earthquake became an opportunity to reform the economy and to reduce the economic semi-dependency vis-\xc3\xa0-vis Britain."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1755_Lisbon_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:O_Terramoto_de_1755_(1756-92)_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_(MNAA).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/O_Terramoto_de_1755_%281756-92%29_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_%28MNAA%29.png/300px-O_Terramoto_de_1755_%281756-92%29_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_%28MNAA%29.png" width="300" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/O_Terramoto_de_1755_%281756-92%29_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_%28MNAA%29.png/450px-O_Terramoto_de_1755_%281756-92%29_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_%28MNAA%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/O_Terramoto_de_1755_%281756-92%29_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_%28MNAA%29.png/600px-O_Terramoto_de_1755_%281756-92%29_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_%28MNAA%29.png 2x" data-file-width="4434" data-file-height="2728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:O_Terramoto_de_1755_(1756-92)_-_Jo%C3%A3o_Glama_(MNAA).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Allegory of the 1755 Earthquake</i>, by Jo\xc3\xa3o Glama Strob\xc3\xabrle (who depicted himself standing on a pile of rubble on the lower-right corner). The painting depicts, on the upper-left corner, an angel holding a fiery sword (a personification of <a href="/wiki/Divine_judgement" class="mw-redirect" title="Divine judgement">divine judgement</a>).</div></div></div>\n<p>The earthquake and its fallout strongly influenced the intelligentsia of the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Enlightenment" class="mw-redirect" title="The Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>. The noted writer-philosopher <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a> used the earthquake in <i><a href="/wiki/Candide" title="Candide">Candide</a></i> and in his <i><a href="/wiki/Po%C3%A8me_sur_le_d%C3%A9sastre_de_Lisbonne" title="Po\xc3\xa8me sur le d\xc3\xa9sastre de Lisbonne">Po\xc3\xa8me sur le d\xc3\xa9sastre de Lisbonne</a></i> ("Poem on the Lisbon disaster"). Voltaire\'s <i>Candide</i> attacks the notion that all is for the best in this, "<a href="/wiki/The_best_of_all_possible_worlds" class="mw-redirect" title="The best of all possible worlds">the best of all possible worlds</a>", a world closely supervised by a benevolent deity.  The Lisbon disaster provided a counterexample. As <a href="/wiki/Theodor_W._Adorno" title="Theodor W. Adorno">Theodor Adorno</a> wrote, "[t]he earthquake of Lisbon sufficed to cure Voltaire of the <a href="/wiki/Theodicy" title="Theodicy">theodicy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Leibniz</a>" (<i><a href="/wiki/Negative_Dialectics" title="Negative Dialectics">Negative Dialectics</a></i> 361).  In the later twentieth century, following <a href="/wiki/Theodor_W._Adorno" title="Theodor W. Adorno">Adorno</a>, the 1755 earthquake has sometimes been compared to the <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a> as a catastrophe that transformed European culture and philosophy. <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a> was also influenced by the devastation following the earthquake, whose severity he believed was due to too many people living within the close quarters of the city. Rousseau used the earthquake as an argument against cities as part of his desire for a more naturalistic way of life.<sup id="cite_ref-Csmonitor.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Csmonitor.com-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> published three separate texts on the Lisbon earthquake. As a younger man, fascinated with the earthquake, he collected all the information available to him in news pamphlets, and used it to formulate a theory of the causes of earthquakes. Kant\'s theory, which involved the shifting of huge subterranean caverns filled with hot gases, was (though ultimately shown to be incorrect) one of the first systematic modern attempts to explain earthquakes by positing natural, rather than supernatural, causes. According to <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Walter Benjamin</a>, Kant\'s slim early book on the earthquake "probably represents the beginnings of scientific geography in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. And certainly the beginnings of seismology."\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Werner_Hamacher" title="Werner Hamacher">Werner Hamacher</a> has claimed that the earthquake\'s consequences extended into the vocabulary of philosophy, making the common metaphor of firm "grounding" for philosophers\' arguments shaky and uncertain: "Under the impression exerted by the Lisbon earthquake, which touched the European mind in one [of] its more sensitive epochs, the metaphor of ground and tremor completely lost their apparent innocence; they were no longer merely figures of speech" (263).  Hamacher claims that the foundational certainty of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="Ren\xc3\xa9 Descartes">Descartes</a>\' philosophy began to shake following the Lisbon earthquake.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1755_Lisbon_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb e Melo, 1st Marquis of Pombal</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The earthquake had a major impact on <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_politics" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese politics">Portuguese politics</a>. The prime minister, <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Carvalho_e_Melo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebasti\xc3\xa3o Jos\xc3\xa9 de Carvalho e Melo">Sebasti\xc3\xa3o Jos\xc3\xa9 de Carvalho e Melo</a> (better known today by the title of <a href="/wiki/Marquis_of_Pombal" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquis of Pombal">Marquis of Pombal</a>, which was only granted in 1770), was the favorite of the king, but the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_nobility" title="Portuguese nobility">aristocracy</a> despised him as an upstart son of a country squire. The prime minister, in turn, disliked the old nobles, whom he considered corrupt and incapable of practical action. Before 1 November 1755 there was a constant struggle for power and royal favor, but the competent response of the Marquis of Pombal effectively severed the power of the old aristocratic factions. However, silent opposition and resentment of King Joseph I began to rise, which would culminate with the attempted assassination of the king in 1758 and the subsequent elimination of the powerful <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Aveiro" title="Duke of Aveiro">Duke of Aveiro</a> and the <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1vora_affair" title="T\xc3\xa1vora affair">T\xc3\xa1vora family</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_seismology">Development of seismology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1755_Lisbon_earthquake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Development of seismology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parochial_Memories_of_1758" title="Parochial Memories of 1758">Parochial Memories of 1758</a></div>\n<p>The prime minister\'s response was not limited to the practicalities of reconstruction. He ordered a query sent to all <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parishes</a> of the country regarding the earthquake and its effects. Questions included:\n</p>\n<ul><li>At what time did the earthquake begin, and how long did the earthquake last?</li>\n<li>Did you perceive the shock to be greater from one direction than another? Example, from north to south? Did buildings seem to fall more to one side than the other?</li>\n<li>How many people died and were any of them distinguished?</li>\n<li>Did the sea rise or fall first, and how many <a href="/wiki/Hand_(unit)" title="Hand (unit)">hands</a> did it rise above the normal?</li>\n<li>If fire broke out, how long did it last and what damage did it cause?<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The answers to these and other questions are still archived in the <a href="/wiki/Torre_do_Tombo" class="mw-redirect" title="Torre do Tombo">Torre do Tombo</a>, the national historical archive. Studying and cross-referencing the priests\' accounts, modern scientists were able to reconstruct the event from a scientific perspective. Without the questionnaire designed by the <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Carvalho_e_Melo,_1st_Marquess_of_Pombal" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebasti\xc3\xa3o Jos\xc3\xa9 de Carvalho e Melo, 1st Marquess of Pombal">Marquis of Pombal</a>, this would have been impossible. Because the marquis was the first to attempt an objective scientific description of the broad causes and consequences of an earthquake, he is regarded as a forerunner of modern seismological scientists.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> causes of this earthquake and the seismic activity in the region continue to be discussed and debated by contemporary scientists.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
