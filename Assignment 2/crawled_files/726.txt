href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Chilterns cover an area of 833&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (322&#160;sq&#160;mi). They are 18&#160;km (11&#160;mi) wide at their widest, and stretch 74&#160;km (46&#160;mi) in a south west to north east diagonal from <a href="/wiki/Goring-on-Thames" title="Goring-on-Thames">Goring-on-Thames</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>, via <a href="/wiki/Dunstable_Downs" title="Dunstable Downs">Dunstable Downs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pegsdon_Hills" class="mw-redirect" title="Pegsdon Hills">Deacon Hill</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire" title="Bedfordshire">Bedfordshire</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> to near <a href="/wiki/Hitchin" title="Hitchin">Hitchin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-changelandscape_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-changelandscape-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The boundary of the hills is clearly defined on the north west side by the <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">scarp</a> slope. The <a href="/wiki/Dip_slope" title="Dip slope">dip slope</a> is by definition more gradual, and merges with the landscape to the south east.<sup id="cite_ref-HeppleDoggett_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeppleDoggett-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a> is a clear end point to the south west, whereas north east of <a href="/wiki/Luton" title="Luton">Luton</a> the hills decline slowly in prominence.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg/220px-Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg/330px-Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg/440px-Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3200" data-file-height="2399" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shirburn_chalk_field.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chalk soil at the foot of the escarpment of the Chiltern Hills near <a href="/wiki/Shirburn" title="Shirburn">Shirburn</a> Hill</div></div></div>\n<p>The chalk <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpment</a> of the Chiltern Hills overlooks the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Aylesbury" class="mw-redirect" title="Vale of Aylesbury">Vale of Aylesbury</a>, and roughly coincides with the southernmost extent of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a> during the Anglian <a href="/wiki/Glacial_maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial maximum">glacial maximum</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Chilterns are part of a system of chalk downlands throughout eastern and southern England, formed between 65 and 95 million years ago,<sup id="cite_ref-changelandscape_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-changelandscape-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> comprising rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Chalk_Group" title="Chalk Group">Chalk Group</a>; this also includes <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cranborne_Chase" title="Cranborne Chase">Cranborne Chase</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Downs" title="South Downs">South Downs</a> in the south. In the north, the chalk formations continue northeastwards across north <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire_Wolds" title="Lincolnshire Wolds">Lincolnshire Wolds</a>, finally ending as the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Wolds" title="Yorkshire Wolds">Yorkshire Wolds</a> in a prominent escarpment, south of the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Pickering" title="Vale of Pickering">Vale of Pickering</a>. The beds of the Chalk Group were deposited over the buried northwestern margin of the Anglo-Brabant Massif during the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Cretaceous" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Cretaceous">Upper Cretaceous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawson_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> During this time, sources for <a href="/wiki/Siliciclastic" title="Siliciclastic">siliciclastic</a> sediment had been eliminated due to the exceptionally high sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-Anderson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The formation is thinner through the Chiltern Hills than the chalk strata to the north and south and deposition was <a href="/wiki/Tectonically" class="mw-redirect" title="Tectonically">tectonically</a> controlled, with the <a href="/wiki/Lilley,_Hertfordshire" title="Lilley, Hertfordshire">Lilley</a> Bottom structure playing a significant role at times.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawson_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Chalk Group, like the underlying <a href="/wiki/Gault_Clay" class="mw-redirect" title="Gault Clay">Gault Clay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Upper_Greensand" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Greensand">Upper Greensand</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Diachronous" title="Diachronous">diachronous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Anderson_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the late stages of the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Orogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpine Orogeny">Alpine Orogeny</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">African Plate</a> collided with <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> extensional structures, such as the <a href="/wiki/Weald_Basin" title="Weald Basin">Weald Basin</a> of southern England, underwent <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(geology)" title="Inversion (geology)">structural inversion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawson_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This phase of deformation tilted the chalk strata to the southeast in the area of the Chiltern Hills. The gently dipping beds of rock were <a href="/wiki/Eroded" class="mw-redirect" title="Eroded">eroded</a>, forming an escarpment.\n</p><p>The chalk <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> are frequently interspersed with layers of <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> <a href="/wiki/Nodule_(geology)" title="Nodule (geology)">nodules</a> which apparently replaced chalk and infilled pore spaces early in the <a href="/wiki/Diagenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Diagenetic">diagenetic</a> history. Flint has been mined for millennia from the Chiltern Hills.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> They were first extracted for fabrication into <a href="/wiki/Flint_axe" title="Flint axe">flint axes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period, then for <a href="/wiki/Knapping" title="Knapping">knapping</a> into <a href="/wiki/Flintlocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Flintlocks">flintlocks</a>. Nodules are to be seen everywhere in the older houses as a construction material for walls.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg/220px-Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg/330px-Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg/440px-Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1461" data-file-height="917" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ivinghoe_Beacon_seen_from_The_Ridgeway.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ivinghoe_Beacon" title="Ivinghoe Beacon">Ivinghoe Beacon</a> (the eastern trailhead) seen looking north from The Ridgeway</div></div></div>\n<p>The highest point is at 267&#160;m (876&#160;ft) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> at <a href="/wiki/Haddington_Hill" title="Haddington Hill">Haddington Hill</a> near <a href="/wiki/Wendover" title="Wendover">Wendover</a> in Buckinghamshire; a stone monument marks the <a href="/wiki/Summit_(topography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Summit (topography)">summit</a>. The nearby <a href="/wiki/Ivinghoe_Beacon" title="Ivinghoe Beacon">Ivinghoe Beacon</a> is a more prominent hill, although its altitude is only 249&#160;m (817&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It is the starting point of the <a href="/wiki/Icknield_Way_Path" title="Icknield Way Path">Icknield Way Path</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Ridgeway" title="The Ridgeway">the Ridgeway</a> <a href="/wiki/Long_distance_path" class="mw-redirect" title="Long distance path">long distance path</a>, which follows the line of the Chilterns for many miles to the west, where they merge with the <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> downs and southern <a href="/wiki/Cotswolds" title="Cotswolds">Cotswolds</a>. To the east of Ivinghoe Beacon is Dunstable Downs, a steep section of the Chiltern scarp. Near Wendover is <a href="/wiki/Coombe_Hill,_Buckinghamshire" title="Coombe Hill, Buckinghamshire">Coombe Hill</a>, 260&#160;m (853&#160;ft) above sea level. The more gently sloping country \xe2\x80\x93 the dip slope \xe2\x80\x93 to the southeast of the Chiltern scarp is also generally referred to as part of the Chilterns; it contains much <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a><sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and many villages.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landscape_and_land_use">Landscape and land use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Landscape and land use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">Enclosed</a> fields account for almost 66% of the "<a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>" (AONB) area. The next most important, and archetypal, landscape form is <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>, covering 21% of the Chilterns, which is thus one of the most heavily wooded areas in England. Built-up areas (settlements and industry) make up over 5% of the land area; parks and gardens nearly 4%, open land (commons, heaths and downland) is 2%, and the remaining 2% includes a variety of uses, including communications, military, open land, recreation, utilities and water.<sup id="cite_ref-changelandscape_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-changelandscape-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivers">Rivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Chilterns are almost entirely located within the <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a> <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a>, and also drain towards several major Thames tributaries, most notably the <a href="/wiki/River_Lea" title="River Lea">Lea</a>, which rises in the eastern Chilterns, the <a href="/wiki/River_Colne,_Hertfordshire" title="River Colne, Hertfordshire">Colne</a> to the south, and the <a href="/wiki/River_Thame" title="River Thame">Thame</a> to the north and west. Other rivers arising near the Chilterns include the <a href="/wiki/River_Mimram" title="River Mimram">Mimram</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Ver" title="River Ver">Ver</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Gade" title="River Gade">Gade</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Bulbourne" title="River Bulbourne">Bulbourne</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Chess" title="River Chess">Chess</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Misbourne" title="River Misbourne">Misbourne</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Wye,_Buckinghamshire" title="River Wye, Buckinghamshire">Wye</a>. These are classified as <a href="/wiki/Chalk_stream" title="Chalk stream">chalk streams</a>, although the Lea is degraded by water from road drains and sewage treatment works.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> flows through a gap between the <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Downs" title="Berkshire Downs">Berkshire Downs</a> and the Chilterns. Portions of the northern and north-eastern Chilterns around <a href="/wiki/Leighton_Buzzard" title="Leighton Buzzard">Leighton Buzzard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hitchin" title="Hitchin">Hitchin</a> are drained by the <a href="/wiki/River_Ouzel" title="River Ouzel">Ouzel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Flit" title="River Flit">Flit</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hiz" class="mw-redirect" title="Hiz">Hiz</a>, all of which ultimately flow into the <a href="/wiki/River_Great_Ouse" title="River Great Ouse">River Great Ouse</a> (the last two via the <a href="/wiki/River_Ivel" title="River Ivel">Ivel</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_routes">Transport routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A number of transport routes pass through the Chilterns in natural or man-made corridors. There are also over 2,000&#160;km (1,200&#160;mi) of public footpaths in the Chilterns, including long-distance trackways such as the <a href="/wiki/Icknield_Way" title="Icknield Way">Icknield Way</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Ridgeway" title="The Ridgeway">The Ridgeway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-didyouknow_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-didyouknow-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/M40_motorway" title="M40 motorway">M40 motorway</a> passes through the Chilterns in Buckinghamshire and Oxfordshire sections, with a deep cutting through the <a href="/wiki/Stokenchurch_Gap" title="Stokenchurch Gap">Stokenchurch Gap</a>. The <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> crosses the Bedfordshire section near Luton. Other major roads include the <a href="/wiki/A413_road" title="A413 road">A413</a>, the <a href="/wiki/A41_road" title="A41 road">A41</a> and the A5.\n</p><p>Railways include the <a href="/wiki/Chiltern_Main_Line" title="Chiltern Main Line">Chiltern Main Line</a> via <a href="/wiki/High_Wycombe_railway_station" title="High Wycombe railway station">High Wycombe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princes_Risborough_railway_station" title="Princes Risborough railway station">Princes Risborough</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/London_to_Aylesbury_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="London to Aylesbury Line">London to Aylesbury Line</a>  via <a href="/wiki/Amersham_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Amersham railway station">Amersham</a>. Nearby the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Main_Line" title="West Coast Main Line">West Coast Main Line</a> runs through <a href="/wiki/Berkhamsted_railway_station" title="Berkhamsted railway station">Berkhamsted</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Midland_Main_Line" title="Midland Main Line">Midland Main Line</a> with <a href="/wiki/Thameslink" title="Thameslink">Thameslink</a> calls at <a href="/wiki/Luton_railway_station" title="Luton railway station">Luton</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Main_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Western Main Line">Great Western Main Line</a> and its branches such as the <a href="/wiki/Henley_Branch_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Henley Branch Line">Henley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marlow_Branch_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Marlow Branch Line">Marlow</a> branch lines link the southern side of the Chilterns with <a href="/wiki/London_Paddington" class="mw-redirect" title="London Paddington">London Paddington</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chinnor_and_Princes_Risborough_Railway" title="Chinnor and Princes Risborough Railway">Chinnor and Princes Risborough Railway</a> is a preserved line. In 2012 the UK Government announced that the proposed <a href="/wiki/High_Speed_2" title="High Speed 2">High Speed 2</a> rail route would pass through the Chilterns near Amersham, partly through tunnels and partly at surface level.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Air corridors from <a href="/wiki/Luton_Airport" title="Luton Airport">Luton Airport</a> pass over the Chilterns to the east and west of the airport.\n</p><p>There are no navigable rivers (apart from the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>), though the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Union_Canal" title="Grand Union Canal">Grand Union Canal</a> passes through the Chilterns between Berkhamsted and Marsworth following the course of the <a href="/wiki/River_Colne,_Hertfordshire" title="River Colne, Hertfordshire">Colne</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Gade" title="River Gade">Gade</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Bulbourne" title="River Bulbourne">Bulbourne</a> and (after crossing a watershed) the <a href="/wiki/River_Ouzel" title="River Ouzel">Ouzel</a>. This canal has branches to <a href="/wiki/Wendover_Arm_Canal" title="Wendover Arm Canal">Wendover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aylesbury_Canal_Society" title="Aylesbury Canal Society">Aylesbury</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In pre-<a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> times, the Chiltern ridge provided a relatively safe and easily navigable route across southern <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">Iron Age England</a>, thus the <a href="/wiki/Icknield_Way" title="Icknield Way">Icknield Way</a> (one of England\'s ancient <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> trackways) follows the line of the hills.\n</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Tribal_Hidage" title="Tribal Hidage">Tribal Hidage</a> suggests the tribe gave its name to the hills, the truth must be the reverse since the <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">toponym</a> is of <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brittonic</a> origin. <a href="/wiki/Eilert_Ekwall" title="Eilert Ekwall">Eilert Ekwall</a> suggested that "Chiltern" is possibly related to the ethnic name "Celt" ("Celt\xc3\xa6" in early Celtic). An adjective <i>celto-</i> ="high" with suffix <i>-erno-</i> could be the origin of Chiltern.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the principal <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Human_settlement" title="Human settlement">settlements</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Britannia_Superior" title="Britannia Superior">Britannia Superior</a> was sited at <a href="/wiki/Verulamium" title="Verulamium">Verulamium</a> (now <a href="/wiki/St_Albans" title="St Albans">St Albans</a>) and there are significant Roman and <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> remains in the area.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudors</a> had a <a href="/wiki/Hunting_lodge" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunting lodge">hunting lodge</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hemel_Hempstead" title="Hemel Hempstead">Hemel Hempstead</a> area.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement">Settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Prior to the 18th century the population was dispersed across the predominantly <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural</a> landscape of the Chilterns, in remote villages, hamlets and farmsteads and small market towns along the main long-distance turnpike routes which funnelled through convenient valleys. The coming of canals in the 18th century and railways in the 19th century encouraged settlement and growth of many towns, e.g. <a href="/wiki/High_Wycombe" title="High Wycombe">High Wycombe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tring" title="Tring">Tring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luton" title="Luton">Luton</a>. There was significant housing and industrial development in the first half of the 20th century and continued throughout the 20th century, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Amersham" title="Amersham">Amersham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beaconsfield" title="Beaconsfield">Beaconsfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berkhamsted" title="Berkhamsted">Berkhamsted</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hitchin" title="Hitchin">Hitchin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chesham" title="Chesham">Chesham</a>. The growth in motor car use and improved rail transport to and from London has latterly put a premium on the availability and price of <a href="/wiki/Commuting" title="Commuting">commuter</a> housing.<sup id="cite_ref-HeppleDoggett_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeppleDoggett-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2002 there were 100,000 people living within the AoNB area of the Chilterns.<sup id="cite_ref-didyouknow_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-didyouknow-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economic use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The hills have been exploited for their <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> for <a href="/wiki/Millennia" class="mw-redirect" title="Millennia">millennia</a>. The chalk has been <a href="/wiki/Quarried" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarried">quarried</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Manufacture" class="mw-redirect" title="Manufacture">manufacture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> for local building material.  <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">Beechwoods</a> supplied furniture makers with quality <a href="/wiki/Hardwood" title="Hardwood">hardwood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The area was once (and still is to a lesser degree) renowned for its <a href="/wiki/Chair" title="Chair">chair</a>-making industry,<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> centred on the towns of Chesham and High Wycombe (the nickname of <a href="/wiki/Wycombe_Wanderers" class="mw-redirect" title="Wycombe Wanderers">Wycombe Wanderers</a> Football Club is the Chairboys). <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a> was and remains a scarce resource in the Chilterns.  Historically it was drawn from the <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> via <a href="/wiki/Ponds" class="mw-redirect" title="Ponds">ponds</a>, deep <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">wells</a>, occasional springs or <a href="/wiki/Bourne_(stream)" title="Bourne (stream)">bournes</a> and chalk streams and rivers.  Today the chalk aquifer is exploited via a network of <a href="/wiki/Pumping_station" title="Pumping station">pumping stations</a> to provide a public supply for domestic consumption, agriculture and business uses, both within and well-beyond the Chilterns area. Rivers such as the <a href="/wiki/River_Chess" title="River Chess">River Chess</a> directly supply <a href="/wiki/Watercress" title="Watercress">watercress</a> beds. <a href="/wiki/Over-exploitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-exploitation">Over-exploitation</a> has possibly led to the disappearance of some streams over at least long periods.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nettle_bed_kiln_(small).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nettle_bed_kiln_%28small%29.JPG/180px-Nettle_bed_kiln_%28small%29.JPG" width="180" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nettle_bed_kiln_%28small%29.JPG/270px-Nettle_bed_kiln_%28small%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nettle_bed_kiln_%28small%29.JPG/360px-Nettle_bed_kiln_%28small%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nettle_bed_kiln_(small).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Nettlebed" title="Nettlebed">Nettlebed</a>\'s one remaining <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In a region short of building stone, local <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> deposits and <a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">timber</a> provided the raw materials for <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> manufacture. Where available, <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> was also used for <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>; it is still used in modern <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a>, although restricted to decoration to give a vernacular appearance.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mediaeval" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediaeval">Mediaeval</a> <a href="/wiki/Strip_parish" title="Strip parish">strip parishes</a> reflected the diversity of land from clay <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">farmland</a>, through wooded slopes to downland. Their boundaries were often drawn to include a section of each type of land, resulting in an irregular county boundary between, say, Bedfordshire and Hertfordshire. These have tended to be smoothed out by successive reorganisations.\n</p><p>In modern times, as people have come to appreciate open country, the area has become a visitor destination and the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a> has acquired land to preserve its character, for example at <a href="/wiki/Ashridge" title="Ashridge">Ashridge</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Tring" title="Tring">Tring</a>. In places, with the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a> <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a>, action has been taken to maintain open downland by suppressing the natural growth of <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrubland">scrub</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> woodland. In the 1920s and 1930s, the <a href="/wiki/Youth_Hostels_Association_(England_%26_Wales)" title="Youth Hostels Association (England &amp; Wales)">Youth Hostels Association</a> established several <a href="/wiki/Youth_hostel" class="mw-redirect" title="Youth hostel">youth hostels</a> for people visiting the hills.\n</p><p>The hills have been used as a location for telecommunication relay stations such as <a href="/wiki/Stokenchurch_BT_Tower" title="Stokenchurch BT Tower">Stokenchurch BT Tower</a> and that at <a href="/wiki/Zouches_Farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Zouches Farm">Zouches Farm</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Chilterns is an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) and so enjoys special protection from major developments, which should not take place in such areas except in exceptional circumstances. This protection applies to major development proposals that raise issues of national significance.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 2000 the government confirmed that the landscape qualities of AONBs are equivalent to those of National Parks, and that the protection given to both types of area by the land use planning system should also be equivalent.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chilterns_Conservation_Board">Chilterns Conservation Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Chilterns Conservation Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Chilterns Conservation Board was established by Parliamentary Order in July 2004. It is an independent body comprising 27 members drawn from the relevant local authorities and from those living in local communities within the Chiltern AONB area.\n</p><p>The Board\xe2\x80\x99s purposes are set out in <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000</a>: In summary these are:- First, to conserve and enhance the natural beauty of the AONB, and increase the understanding and enjoyment by the public of the special qualities of the AONB. Second, while taking account of the first purpose, to foster the economic and social wellbeing of local communities within the AONB. Third, to publish and promote the implementation of a management plan for the AONB.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In contrast to <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">National Parks</a>, the Chilterns - as other AONBs - do not possess their own <a href="/wiki/Planning_permission" title="Planning permission">planning authority</a>. The Board has an advisory role on planning and development matters and seeks to influence the actions of local government by commenting upon <a href="/wiki/Planning_permission" title="Planning permission">planning applications</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The local authorities (four County Councils, one Unitary Authority and ten District and Borough Councils) are expected to respect the area\'s status as a designated Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Speed_Rail_2">High Speed Rail 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: High Speed Rail 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In October 2010 the government confirmed proposals for the <a href="/wiki/High_Speed_2" title="High Speed 2">High Speed 2</a> rail link from London to Birmingham with a recommended route through the Chilterns <a href="/wiki/AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="AONB">AONB</a>. The Conservation Board has made clear it is opposed to the routing of HS2 through the Chilterns AONB.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chiltern_Hundreds">Chiltern Hundreds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chiltern_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Chiltern Hundreds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Chilterns includes the <a href="/wiki/Chiltern_Hundreds" title="Chiltern Hundreds">Chiltern Hundreds</a>.
