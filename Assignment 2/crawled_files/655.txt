<span class="geo">50.10; -5.27</span></span></span></a></span></span></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Helston</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Hellys</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a town and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It is situated at the northern end of the <a href="/wiki/The_Lizard" title="The Lizard">Lizard Peninsula</a> approximately 12 miles (19&#160;km) east of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> and 9 miles (14&#160;km) south-west of <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Helston is the most southerly town on the island of Great Britain and is around 1.5 miles (2.4&#160;km) farther south than <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>. The population in 2011 was 11,700.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The former <a href="/wiki/Stannary" title="Stannary">stannary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">cattle market town</a> is best known for the annual <a href="/wiki/Furry_Dance" title="Furry Dance">Furry Dance</a> (known locally as the Flora Dance), said to originate from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval period</a>. However, the <a href="/wiki/Furry_Dance#Pageant" title="Furry Dance">Hal-an-Tow</a> is reputed to be of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic origin</a>. The song, and music, associated with the <a href="/wiki/The_Floral_Dance" title="The Floral Dance">Furry Dance</a> is known to have been written in 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, the town celebrated the 800th anniversary of the granting of its Charter.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The name comes from the Cornish \'hen lis\' or \'old court\' and \'ton\' added later to denote a <a href="/wiki/Saxons#Saxons_in_Britain" title="Saxons">Saxon manor</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> refers to Henliston (which survives as the name of a road in the town). Only one edition refers to \'Henlistona\'.<sup id="cite_ref-Genuki_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Genuki-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It was granted its charter by <a href="/wiki/John,_King_of_England" title="John, King of England">King John</a> on 15 April 1201, for the price of forty marks of silver. It was here that tin ingots were weighed to determine the <a href="/wiki/Tin_coinage" title="Tin coinage">tin coinage</a> duty due to the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a> when a number of <a href="/wiki/Stannary" title="Stannary">stannary</a> towns were authorised by royal decree. A document of 1396 examined by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Henderson_(historian)" title="Charles Henderson (historian)">Charles Henderson</a> shows that the old form "Hellys" was still in use <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The manor of Helston in Kerrier was one of the seventeen <a href="/wiki/Antiqua_maneria" title="Antiqua maneria">Antiqua maneria</a> of the Duchy of Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The seal of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_borough" title="Ancient borough">borough</a> of Helston was St Michael his wings expanded and standing on a gateway. The two towers domed upon the up-turned dragon, impaling it with his spear and bearing upon his left arm an <a href="/wiki/Escutcheon_(heraldry)" title="Escutcheon (heraldry)">escutcheon</a> of the arms of England, viz Gu three lions passant guardant in pale Or, with the legend "<i>Sigillum comunitatis helleston burg</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is a matter of debate as to whether Helston was once a port, albeit no actual records exist.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A common belief is that in the 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Loe_Bar" class="mw-redirect" title="Loe Bar">Loe Bar</a> formed a barrier across the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Cober" title="River Cober">River Cober</a> cutting the town off from the sea. <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">Geomorphologists</a> believe the bar was most likely formed by rising sea levels, after the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>, blocking the river and creating a <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">barrier beach</a>. The beach is formed mostly of flint and the nearest source is found offshore under the drowned terraces of the former river that flowed between England and France, and now under the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a> describes Helston (1725) in <a href="/wiki/A_tour_thro%27_the_whole_island_of_Great_Britain" title="A tour thro&#39; the whole island of Great Britain">his tour around Great Britain</a> thus, \xe2\x80\xb3<i>This town is large and populous, and has four spacious streets, a handsome church, and a good trade: this town also sends members to Parliament.\'</i> He also mentions that the River Cober makes a tolerable good harbour and several ships are loaded with tin, although over one hundred years before Defoe, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Carew_(antiquary)" title="Richard Carew (antiquary)">Richard Carew</a> (1602) described Loe Bar as <i>"The shingle was relatively porous and fresh water could leave and seawater enter depending, on the relative heights of the pool and sea"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Defoe\'s description seems to be the first and possibly the origin of other sources claiming Helston to be a port in the historic period.<sup id="cite_ref-russell_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-russell-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Loe Pool is referred to in a document of 1302, implying the existence of Loe Bar at this date, if not much earlier, and thus precluding the passage of shipping up the Cober.  At the same time it was recorded that the burgesses of Helston exercised jurisdiction over the ships anchored at Gweek, but no mention was made of ships at Helston, and no customs records or other documentation of port traffic relating to Helston survives;<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> thus confirming the fact that Gweek has for centuries been the recognized port of Helston.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> There is no known archaeological evidence for the existence of a port at Helston* and there is no primary evidence to support Defoe\xe2\x80\x99s account.<sup id="cite_ref-russell_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-russell-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, contributing to the belief of a port at Helston was the discovery of what some people believe to be slipways and mooring rings, during excavations around 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> There was no known shipping from the sea after 1260, but before 1200, in \'the 1182 record of Godric of Helleston paying a fine of ten marks for exporting his corn out of England from Helston without a licence.\' This could be considered the most significant piece of documentary evidence signifying Helston\'s former port days, though it does not prove the case.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> At the time of Domesday Book, Gweek had no inhabitants whilst Helston was the largest settlement in the west of Cornwall, with 113 households.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1837 a plan was drawn up to open Loe Pool to shipping using a pier to counteract siltation, but it was never carried out.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The site of Helston\'s castle is now a bowling green near the Grylls Monument, which has been there since 1760. The castle was a simple pre-1086 <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey_castle" title="Motte-and-bailey castle">motte and bailey</a> structure, known as Henliston Castle,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> replaced in around 1280 by a stone structure of a similar design for <a href="/wiki/Edmund,_2nd_Earl_of_Cornwall" title="Edmund, 2nd Earl of Cornwall">Edmund, Earl of Cornwall</a>. By 1478 it had fallen into disuse and ruin.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Helston_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Helston (UK Parliament constituency)">Helston parliamentary constituency</a> was created in 1298 and elected two members to the <a href="/wiki/Unreformed_House_of_Commons" title="Unreformed House of Commons">Unreformed House of Commons</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a> reduced the number elected to one. Helston is now part of the <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="St Ives (UK Parliament constituency)">St Ives constituency</a>, which covers the western part of Cornwall and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>. The current member is the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Thomas_(politician)" title="Derek Thomas (politician)">Derek Thomas</a>. Helston is within the <a href="/wiki/South_West_England_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="South West England (European Parliament constituency)">South West England European Parliamentary Constituency</a>. At local government level, the town is administered by <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a> and Helston Town Council.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Helston is situated along the banks of the <a href="/wiki/River_Cober" title="River Cober">River Cober</a> in Cornwall. Downstream is Cornwall\'s largest natural lake <a href="/wiki/Loe_Pool" class="mw-redirect" title="Loe Pool">Loe Pool</a>, formed when a shingle bar blocked the mouth of the river by rising sea levels forming a barrier beach. To the south is the <a href="/wiki/Lizard_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Lizard Peninsula">Lizard Peninsula</a>, an area important for its complex geology and wildlife habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Helston is on the <a href="/wiki/A394_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A394 road">A394 road</a>. To the west, the A394 leads to Penzance; to the north-east it leads to <a href="/wiki/Penryn,_Cornwall" title="Penryn, Cornwall">Penryn</a> where it joins the <a href="/wiki/A39_road" title="A39 road">A39</a>, which leads south to Falmouth and north-east to <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a>. The B3297 runs north from Helston to <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">Redruth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The nearest railway station is <a href="/wiki/Redruth_railway_station" title="Redruth railway station">Redruth</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Main_Line" title="Cornish Main Line">Cornish main line</a>, although the <a href="/wiki/Helston_Railway" title="Helston Railway">Helston branch line railway</a> served the town until closure in the early 1960s. The branch left the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">ex-GWR</a> main line at <a href="/wiki/Gwinear_Road_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Gwinear Road railway station">Gwinear Road station</a> near <a href="/wiki/Hayle" title="Hayle">Hayle</a>, and ran 8.5 miles (13.7&#160;km) south to terminate at <a href="/wiki/Helston_railway_station" title="Helston railway station">Helston railway station</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Helston_Railway_Preservation_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Helston Railway Preservation Company">Helston Railway Preservation Company</a> is undertaking the restoration of part of the line. Bus services now link Helston to the rail network; <a href="/wiki/First_South_West" title="First South West">First South West</a> provides the (34) bus service from Redruth station to Helston as well as other services to nearby towns and the Lizard peninsula.\n</p><p>The nearest <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> is <a href="/wiki/Newquay_Cornwall_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Newquay Cornwall Airport">Newquay Cornwall Airport</a> which is approximately 35 miles (56&#160;km) north-east of Helston. This is the main commercial airport for Cornwall with regular scheduled services to many parts of the UK.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Helston has an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification">K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification</a> <i>Cfb</i>), similar to the rest of the British Isles. It is one of the mildest places in the country and frosts are rarely severe. The nearest <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather station</a> is <a href="/wiki/RNAS_Culdrose_(HMS_Seahawk)" title="RNAS Culdrose (HMS Seahawk)">RNAS Culdrose</a>, approximately 1 mile south-east of the town centre. Temperature extremes in the area since 1980 have ranged from \xe2\x88\x9210.9&#160;\xc2\xb0C (12.4&#160;\xc2\xb0F) during January 1987,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and up to 29.6&#160;\xc2\xb0C (85.3&#160;\xc2\xb0F) in August 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The coldest temperature in recent years was \xe2\x88\x926.2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (20.8&#160;\xc2\xb0F) in December 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Snow occurs in median every second year, almost in 2 \xe2\x80\x93 3 days in line or one alone, most often in January or February.<sup id="cite_ref-Wunderground_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wunderground-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable collapsible collapsed" style="width:90%; text-align:center; font-size:90%; line-height: 1.2em; class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_community">Culture and community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culture and community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_Day:_the_Furry_Dance_and_Hal-an-Tow_ceremonies">Flora Day: the Furry Dance and Hal-an-Tow ceremonies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Flora Day: the Furry Dance and Hal-an-Tow ceremonies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg/220px-Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg" width="220" height="116" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg/330px-Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg/440px-Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="529" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coinagehallstreet_helston.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coinagehall Street and the Blue Anchor Inn</div></div></div>\n<p>Flora Day occurs annually, on 8 May (except when the date falls on a Sunday or Monday&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Monday being Market Day&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 when it is the preceding Saturday) Helston hosts the <a href="/wiki/Furry_Dance" title="Furry Dance">Furry Dance</a>.\n</p><p>There are four dances throughout the day, the first starting at 7 a.m. (historically for domestic servants), the ladies in summer frocks and the gentlemen in white shirts and dark grey trousers with neckties bearing the town crest, loaned for the day. The second dance at 9.50 is when children from the town\'s schools dance dressed all in white, the individual schools denoted by the head dresses that the girls wear. The premier dance follows at Midday when the "gentry of the County" dance, the ladies in long ball-type gowns topped off with picture hats and the gentlemen wearing full <a href="/wiki/Morning_dress" title="Morning dress">morning dress</a>. The final dance of the day begins at 5 p.m., a dance historically for the tradespeople of the town.  Participants in this dance are the only dancers to dance around the town twice, having already danced in the 7 a.m. dance.\n</p><p>Only Helston-born people can dance in the lead set in each dance and the first male and female will only lead that dance once in their lifetime. Flora Day is administered by Stewards who elect stewards therefore continuing this wonderful occasion without outside interference. <a href="/wiki/Helston_Town_Band" title="Helston Town Band">Helston Town Band</a> play the famous tune and accompany all four dances on a long route around the town. The dwellings and shops of the town are festooned with <a href="/wiki/Common_Bluebell" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Bluebell">bluebells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silene_dioica" title="Silene dioica">campions</a> and whatever green foliage is available. Specific dances (not including the children\'s dance) go into and out of various private buildings, shops and grounds. The origins of the dance are not known but appear to represent a pre-Christian celebration of the passing of winter.\n</p><p>On the same day the "<a href="/wiki/Hal_an_Tow" class="mw-redirect" title="Hal an Tow">Hal an Tow</a>", another celebration of the coming of spring, is performed upon the streets of the town.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  This is a morning ritual that is traditionally more boisterous than the dance. It is a moving street theatre that appears to have its origins in the Middle Ages, and the themes tend to be more English than uniquely Cornish. The theatre consists of the Hal-an-tow song accompanied by dancing and acting out the content of the verses. The costumes and the song itself represent many different historical and mythical themes. It has evolved over time, the most recent verse (about <a href="/wiki/St_Piran" class="mw-redirect" title="St Piran">St Piran</a>) only appeared within the 21st century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellys_International_Guitar_Festival">Hellys International Guitar Festival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hellys International Guitar Festival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2017, a new festival was established by the lutenist <a href="/wiki/Ben_Salfield" title="Ben Salfield">Ben Salfield</a> and his promoters, Kernow Concerts,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> bringing international concert artists from the world of guitar music from as far away as <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> to perform concerts and give free lessons in Helston each August. The festival is initially based in The Old Cattle Market, next to Coronation Park and Boating Lake, and features some of the best artists in their field.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helston_Town_Band">Helston Town Band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Helston Town Band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Helston_Town_Band" title="Helston Town Band">Helston Town Band</a></div>\n<p>Helston Town Band has a rich history, which can be traced back to the turn of the 20th century. Indeed, there are members of the current band whose family connections extend back four generations. Inevitably, during the War the band reformed with new members and in 1946 numbers were consolidated when most of its pre-war members returned from active service. The band enjoyed steady progress at this time, which culminated in 1951 when it reached the National Third Section Finals at Belle Vue, Manchester.\n</p><p>In 1967, the band came under the direction of Edward Ashton, with whom the band gained much success and a reputation for consistently playing music to a high standard. Edward led the band to numerous successes in both local and regional contests, until his retirement in November 2002 after an incredible 35 years.\n</p><p>Following his retirement, the band appointed John Hitchens as their new Musical Director . The band has continued to flourish under John\xe2\x80\x99s direction: in 2003, they were crowned Cornish First Section Champions, and in 2004 they gained promotion to the National First Section.\n</p><p>In 2006, the band were crowned West of England First Section Champions, and received an invitation to compete in the National First Section Finals in Harrogate, where they achieved a commendable seventh place. The band went on to achieve third place at the West of England First Section Championships in 2007, and were delighted to become West of England First Section Champions once again in 2008.\n</p><p>These excellent results mean that Helston Town Band earned promotion and is competing in the <a href="/wiki/Brass_Band_Sections_in_Britain#Championship_section" class="mw-redirect" title="Brass Band Sections in Britain">Championship Section</a> in 2009 for this first time in its history.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches_and_places_of_interest">Churches and The house where he was born and lived in Helston is still standing and is indicated by a plaque above the door.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Helston_Museum" title="Helston Museum">Helston Museum</a>, founded in 1949, occupies the building originally designed as the town\'s Market House in 1837, with two separate buildings\xe2\x80\x94one for butter and eggs, the other the meat market. The exhibits are mostly concerned with Helston\'s agricultural and market town history. The museum also hosts art exhibitions and has a shop selling all things Cornish.\n</p><p>There are three Cornish crosses in Helston: one in Cross Street and two in Mr. Baddeley\'s garden (Cross Street). One of the latter crosses was removed from Tresprison, Wendron, and other from near Trelill Holy Well, Wendron. The cross from Trelill has ornament on the front and back of the shaft.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Grylls Monument, at the end of Coinagehall Street was built by public subscription in 1834 to thank Humphry Millet Grylls, a local banker, who stopped the closure of <a href="/wiki/Wheal_Vor" title="Wheal Vor">Wheal Vor</a>, a local mine that at the time employed over a thousand people.\n</p><p>Helston also hosts <a href="/wiki/The_Flambards_Experience" title="The Flambards Experience">The Flambards Experience</a>, formerly the Helston Aero Park, which is a theme park with a selection of rides together with a few remaining aviation exhibits. Nearby Wendron is home to the <a href="/wiki/Poldark_Mine" title="Poldark Mine">Poldark Mine</a> theme park, where visitors can go underground into the old workings.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools">Schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Helston_Community_College" title="Helston Community College">Helston Community College</a>, previously Gwealhellis Secondary Modern School, has the most pupils in Cornwall. Its South Site building has a long history as a grammar school and boasts <a href="/wiki/Derwent_Coleridge" title="Derwent Coleridge">Derwent Coleridge</a> as a headmaster, his pupils including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kingsley" title="Charles Kingsley">Charles Kingsley</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/John_Coleridge,_1st_Baron_Coleridge" title="John Coleridge, 1st Baron Coleridge">John Duke Coleridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Edmonds_(scientist)" title="Richard Edmonds (scientist)">Richard Edmonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rowe_Edmonds" title="Thomas Rowe Edmonds">Thomas Rowe Edmonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Rogers_(divine)" title="John Rogers (divine)">John Rogers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Trengrouse" title="Henry Trengrouse">Henry Trengrouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Trevenen" title="James Trevenen">James Trevenen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Another former headmaster was the botanist and author <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alexander_Johns" title="Charles Alexander Johns">Charles Alexander Johns</a> (1843\xe2\x80\x9347), who was also a former pupil.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The School Houses in grammar school days were Coleridge, Kingsley and Tennyson. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Tennyson" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfred Tennyson">Alfred Tennyson</a>\'s local connection was through his writing.\n</p><p>Helston has three primary schools. These are Parc Eglos, St. Michael\'s and Nansloe. The catchment area of Helston Community College includes these and many other schools from the surrounding villages.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_and_recreation">Sport and recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sport and recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bowlingclub_helston.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Bowlingclub_helston.jpg/220px-Bowlingclub_helston.jpg" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Bowlingclub_helston.jpg/330px-Bowlingclub_helston.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Bowlingclub_helston.jpg/440px-Bowlingclub_helston.jpg 2x" data-file-width="880" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bowlingclub_helston.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bowlingclub and the Grylls Monument</div></div></div>\n<p>The town has an active sporting scene, with <a href="/wiki/Helston_RFC" title="Helston RFC">Helston RFC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helston_Athletic_F.C." title="Helston Athletic F.C.">Helston Athletic FC</a> and Helston Cricket Club all having prominent roles within the community. The Swallows <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">Gymnastics</a> Club is also extremely popular within the area. Helston holds an annual road race An Resek Helys (The Race for Helston)<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and an annual triathlon.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The town has a <a href="/wiki/King_George%27s_Fields" title="King George&#39;s Fields">King George V Playing Field</a>, the home ground for the rugby club and finish line of An Resek Helys. Below the town is Coronation Park which has a man-made lake as its centrepiece where rowing boats can be hired in summer. A skate park is nearby in the same complex. The <a href="/wiki/Penrose,_Cornwall" title="Penrose, Cornwall">Penrose Amenity Area</a> lies across the road from Coronation Park. <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a>-owned, this area, once part of the Penrose Estate, offers walks alongside the River Cober which leads down to Loe Pool and the sea beyond Loe Bar.
