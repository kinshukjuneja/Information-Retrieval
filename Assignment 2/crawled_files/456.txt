(23) and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> (23)</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>National League of Professional Baseball Clubs</b>, known simply as the <b>National League</b> (<b>NL</b>), is the older of two leagues constituting <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and the world\'s oldest current professional team sports league. Founded on February 2, 1876, to replace the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Professional_Base_Ball_Players" title="National Association of Professional Base Ball Players">National Association of Professional Base Ball Players</a> (NAPBBP) of 1871&#8211;1875, (often called simply the "National Association"), the NL is sometimes called the <b>Senior Circuit</b>, in contrast to MLB\'s other  league, the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League of Professional Base Ball Clubs</a> ("<a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a>"), which was founded 25 years later. Both leagues currently have 15 teams. After two years of conflict in a "baseball war" of 1901-1902, the two leagues of 8 team franchises each, agreed in a "peace pact" to recognize each other as "major leagues", draft rules regarding player contracts, prohibiting "raiding", regulating relationships with  <a href="/wiki/Minor_League_Baseball" title="Minor League Baseball">minor leagues</a> and lower level clubs, and with each establishing a team in the  nation\'s largest metropolis of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, and the league champions of 1903 arranged to compete against each other in the new professional baseball championship tournament with the inaugural "<a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>" that Fall of 1903, succeeding earlier similar national series in previous decades since the 1880s. After the 1904 champions failed to reach a similar agreement, the two leagues also formalized the new World Series tournament beginning in 1905 as an arrangement between the leagues themselves. National League teams have won 48 of the 113 World Series championships contested from 1903 to 2017.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After only four seasons, by 1875, the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Professional_Base_Ball_Players" title="National Association of Professional Base Ball Players">National Association of Professional Base Ball Players</a> (NAPBBP - often referred to as the "National Association") was dangerously weak. The N.A. suffered from a lack of strong authority over clubs, unsupervised scheduling, unstable membership of cities, dominance by one team (the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Boston_Braves" title="History of the Boston Braves">Boston Red Stockings</a>), and an extremely low entry fee ($10 / ten dollars) that gave clubs no incentive to abide by league rules when it was not convenient to them.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_Hulbert" title="William Hulbert">William A. Hulbert</a> (1832-1882), a <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> businessman and an officer of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Stockings_(1870%E2%80%9389)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago White Stockings (1870\xe2\x80\x9389)">Chicago White Stockings</a> of 1870-1889, approached several N.A. clubs with the plans for a professional league for the sport of base ball with a stronger central authority and exclusive territories in larger cities only. Additionally, Hulbert had a problem\xe2\x80\x94five of his star players were threatened with expulsion from the NAPBBP because Hulbert had signed them to his club using what were considered questionable means. Hulbert had a great vested interest in creating his own league. After recruiting St. Louis privately, four western clubs met in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>, in January 1876. With Hulbert speaking for the four later in New York City on February 2, 1876, the National League of Professional Base Ball Clubs was established with eight charter members, as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Stockings_(1870%E2%80%9389)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago White Stockings (1870\xe2\x80\x9389)">Chicago White Stockings</a> from the N.A. (now the Chicago Cubs, not to be confused with the current <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics_(1860%E2%80%9376)" title="Philadelphia Athletics (1860\xe2\x80\x9376)">Philadelphia Athletics</a> from the N.A. (expelled after the 1876 season)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Boston_Braves" title="History of the Boston Braves">Boston Red Stockings</a>, the then dominant team in the N.A. (later the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Braves_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Braves (baseball)">Boston Braves</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves" title="History of the Atlanta Braves">Milwaukee Braves</a>, now the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a>, not to be confused with the present-day <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> of the later American League)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hartford_Dark_Blues" title="Hartford Dark Blues">Hartford Dark Blues</a> from the N.A. (folded after the 1877 season)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/New_York_Mutuals" title="New York Mutuals">Mutual of New York</a> from the N.A. (expelled after the 1876 season)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Brown_Stockings" title="St. Louis Brown Stockings">St. Louis Brown Stockings</a> from the N.A. (folded after the 1877 season, having committed to Louisville stars for 1878)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds_(1876%E2%80%931880)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati Reds (1876\xe2\x80\x931880)">Cincinnati Red Stockings</a>, a new franchise (disbanded after the 1879 season)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Louisville_Grays" title="Louisville Grays">Louisville Grays</a>, a new franchise (folded after the 1877 season when four players were banned for gambling)</li></ul>\n<p>The National League\'s formation meant the end of the old National Association after only five seasons, as its remaining clubs shut down or reverted to amateur or minor league status. The only strong club from 1875 excluded in 1876 was a second one in Philadelphia, often called the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_White_Stockings" title="Philadelphia White Stockings">White Stockings or later Phillies</a>.\n</p><p>The first game in National League history was played on April 22, 1876, at <a href="/wiki/History_of_Philadelphia" title="History of Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Street_Grounds" title="Jefferson Street Grounds">Jefferson Street Grounds</a>, at 25th &amp; Jefferson Streets, between the Philadelphia Athletics and the Boston baseball club. Boston won the game 6\xe2\x80\x935.\n</p><p>The new league\'s authority was soon tested after the first season. The Athletic and Mutual clubs fell behind in the standings and refused to make western road trips late in the season, preferring to play games against local non-league competition to recoup some of their financial losses rather than travel extensively incurring more costs. Hulbert reacted to the clubs\' defiance by expelling them, an act which not only shocked baseball followers (New York and Philadelphia were the two most populous cities in the league) and the then sports world, but made it clear to clubs that league schedule commitments, a cornerstone of competition integrity, were not to be ignored.\n</p><p>The National League operated with only six clubs during 1877 and 1878. Over the next several years, various teams joined and left the struggling league.  By 1880, six of the eight charter members had folded.  The two remaining original NL franchises, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> and Chicago, remain still in operation today as the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>. When all eight participants for 1881 returned for 1882\xe2\x80\x94the first off-season without turnover in membership\xe2\x80\x94the "circuit" consisted of a zig-zag line connecting the eight cities: <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Troy,_New_York" title="Troy, New York">Troy</a> (near the state capital of <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany, New York</a>), <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester</a> (<a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>), <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence</a>.\n</p><p>In 1883, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Gothams" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Gothams">New York Gothams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> began National League play. Both teams remain in the NL today, the Phillies with their original name and city and the Gothams (later renamed Giants) now in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> since 1957.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition_with_other_leagues">Competition with other leagues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Competition with other leagues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The NL encountered its first strong rival organization when the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a> began play in 1882. The A.A. played in cities where the NL did not have teams, offered Sunday games and alcoholic beverages in locales where permitted, and sold cheaper tickets everywhere (25 cents versus the NL\'s standard 50 cents, a hefty sum for many in 1882).\n</p><p>The National League and the American Association participated in a version of the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> seven times during their ten-year coexistence.  These contests were less organized than the modern Series, lasting as few as three games and as many as fifteen, with two Series (1885 and 1890) ending in disputed ties.  The NL won four times and the A.A. only once, in 1886.\n</p><p>Starting with the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> in 1887, the National League began to raid the American Association for franchises to replace NL teams that folded.  This undercut the stability of the A.A.\n</p><p>Other new leagues that rose to compete with the National League were the <a href="/wiki/Union_Association" title="Union Association">Union Association</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Players%27_League" title="Players&#39; League">Players\' League</a>. The Union Association was established in 1884 and folded after playing only one season, its league champion <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Maroons/Indianapolis_Hoosiers" title="St. Louis Maroons/Indianapolis Hoosiers">St. Louis Maroons</a> joining the NL. The Players\' League was established in 1890 by the Brotherhood of Professional Base-Ball Players, the sport\'s first players\' union, which had failed to persuade the NL to modify its labor practices, including a <a href="/wiki/Salary_cap" title="Salary cap">salary cap</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Reserve_clause" title="Reserve clause">reserve clause</a> that bound players to their teams indefinitely.  The NL suffered many defections of star players to the Players\' League, but the P.L. collapsed after one season.  The Brooklyn, Chicago, Pittsburgh, and New York franchises of the NL absorbed their Players\' League counterparts.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Expansion_.281887.E2.80.931899.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_(1887\xe2\x80\x931899)">Expansion (1887\xe2\x80\x931899)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion (1887\xe2\x80\x931899)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The labor strike of 1890 hastened the downfall of the American Association.  After the 1891 season, the A.A. disbanded and merged with the NL, which became known legally for the next decade as the "National League and American Association". The teams now known as the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> (originally Brooklyn) and <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> (as well as the now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Spiders" title="Cleveland Spiders">Cleveland Spiders</a>) had already switched from the A.A. to the NL prior to 1892. With the merger, the NL absorbed the St. Louis Browns (now known as the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>), along with three other teams that did not survive into the 20th century (for those three teams, see <a href="#Partnership_with_the_American_League">Partnership with the American League</a> below). While four teams that moved from the A.A. remain in the NL today (Pittsburgh [1887], Cincinnati [1890], Los Angeles [originally Brooklyn; 1890], and St. Louis [1892]), only two original NL franchises (1876) remain in the league: the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> (originally in Boston, and later Milwaukee). The Cubs are the only charter member to play continuously in the same city.  The other two pre-1892 teams still in the league are the Philadelphia Phillies and the San Francisco Giants (originally New York), both of which joined in 1883.\n</p><p>The National League became a 12-team circuit with monopoly status for the rest of the decade. The league became embroiled in numerous internal conflicts, not the least of which was a plan supported by some owners (and bitterly opposed by others) to form a "trust", wherein there would be one common ownership of all twelve teams. The NL used its <a href="/wiki/Monopsony" title="Monopsony">monopsony</a> power to force a $2,400 limit on annual player wages in 1894.\n</p><p>As the 20th century dawned, the NL was in trouble. Conduct among players was poor, and fistfights were a common sight at games. In addition to fighting each other, they fought with the umpires and often filled the air at games with foul language and obscenities. A game between the Orioles and Boston Beaneaters (a precursor to today\'s Atlanta Braves) in 1894 ended up having tragic consequences when players became engaged in a brawl and several boys in the stands started a fire. The blaze quickly got out of hand and swept through downtown Boston, destroying or damaging 100 buildings. Team owners  argued with each other and players hated the NL\'s $2,400 salary cap. Many teams also ran into trouble with city governments that forbade recreational activities on Sunday.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Billy_Sunday" title="Billy Sunday">Billy Sunday</a>, a prominent outfielder in the 1880s, became so disgusted with the behavior of teammates that he quit playing in 1891 to become one of America\'s most famous evangelical Christian preachers. Most fans appear to have felt the same way, because attendance at games was plummeting by 1900.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Partnership_with_the_American_League">Partnership with the American League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Partnership with the American League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After eight seasons as a 12-team league, the NL contracted back to eight teams for the 1900 season, eliminating its teams in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(19th_century)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Orioles (19th century)">Baltimore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Spiders" title="Cleveland Spiders">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Colonels" title="Louisville Colonels">Louisville</a> (which has never had another major league team since), and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Senators_(1891%E2%80%9399)" title="Washington Senators (1891\xe2\x80\x9399)">Washington</a>. This provided an opportunity for competition. Three of those cities received franchises in the new American League (AL) when the AL opened for business in 1900, with the approval of the NL, which regarded the AL as a lesser league. The AL declined to renew its National Agreement membership when it expired, and on January 28, 1901, the AL officially declared itself a second major league in competition with the NL. By 1903, the upstart AL had placed new teams in the National League cities of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, Chicago, New York, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>. Only the Cincinnati Reds and Pittsburgh Pirates had no AL team in their markets. The AL among other things enforced a strict conduct policy among its players.\n</p><p>The National League at first refused to recognize the new league, but reality set in as talent and money was split between the two leagues, diluting the league and decreasing financial success. After two years of bitter contention, a new version of the National Agreement was signed in 1903. This meant formal acceptance of each league by the other as an equal partner in major-league baseball, mutual respect of player contracts, and an agreement to play a postseason championship\xe2\x80\x94the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>.\n</p><p>Major League Baseball narrowly averted radical reorganization in November 1920. Dissatisfied with <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> President and National Commission head <a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson" title="Ban Johnson">Ban Johnson</a>, NL owners dissolved the league on November 8 during heated talks on MLB reorganization in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sox_Scandal" title="Black Sox Scandal">Black Sox Scandal</a>. Simultaneously, three AL teams also hostile to Johnson (<a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>) withdrew from the AL and joined the eight NL teams in forming a new National League; the 12th team would be whichever of the remaining five AL teams loyal to Johnson first chose to join; if none did so an expansion team would have been placed in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, by far the largest one-team city at that time. Four days later, on November 12, both sides met (without Johnson) and agreed to restore the two leagues and replace the ineffective National Commission with a one-man Commissioner in the person of federal Judge <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The National League circuit remained unchanged from 1900 through 1952.  In 1953 the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Braves_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Braves (baseball)">Braves</a> moved from Boston to <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>; in 1966 they moved again, to <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. In 1958 the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> moved to Los Angeles and San Francisco, respectively, bringing major league baseball to the West Coast of the U.S. for the first time.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisional_reorganization">Divisional reorganization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Divisional reorganization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The NL remained an eight-team league for over 60 years. (For the eight teams, see <a href="#Expansion_(1887\xe2\x80\x931899)">Expansion (1887\xe2\x80\x931899)</a> above, and <a href="#&quot;Classic_Eight&quot;">"Classic Eight"</a> below.) In 1962\xe2\x80\x94facing competition from the proposed <a href="/wiki/Continental_League" title="Continental League">Continental League</a> and confronted by the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a>\'s unilateral expansion in 1961\xe2\x80\x94the NL expanded by adding the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> and the Houston Colt .45s. The "Colts" were renamed the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> three years later. In 1969, the league added the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a> (now the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a>), becoming a 12-team league for the first time since 1899.\n</p><p>In 1969, as a result of its expansion to 12 teams, the National League\xe2\x80\x94which for its first 93 years had competed equally in a single grouping\xe2\x80\x94was reorganized into two <a href="/wiki/Division_(sport)" title="Division (sport)">divisions</a> of six teams (respectively named the <a href="/wiki/National_League_East" title="National League East">National League East</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_League_West" title="National League West">West</a>, although geographically it was more like North and South), with the division champions meeting in the <a href="/wiki/National_League_Championship_Series" title="National League Championship Series">National League Championship Series</a> (an additional round of postseason competition) for the right to advance to the World Series.\n</p><p>In 1993 the league expanded to 14 teams, adding the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Rockies" title="Colorado Rockies">Colorado Rockies</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Marlins" class="mw-redirect" title="Florida Marlins">Florida Marlins</a> (which became the Miami Marlins shortly after the end of the 2011 season). In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> became the league\'s fifteenth franchise, and the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a> moved from the AL to the NL, giving the NL 16 teams for the next 15 seasons.\n</p><p>In 1994, the league was again reorganized, into three geographical divisions (East, West and <a href="/wiki/National_League_Central" title="National League Central">Central</a>, all currently with five teams; from 1994 to 1997 the West had one fewer team, and from 1998 to 2012, the Central had one more team). A third postseason round was added at the same time: the three division champions plus a <a href="/wiki/Wild_card_(sports)" title="Wild card (sports)">wild card</a> team (the team with the best record among those finishing in second place) now advance to the preliminary <a href="/wiki/National_League_Division_Series" title="National League Division Series">National League Division Series</a>. Due to a players\' strike, however, the postseason was not actually held in 1994.\n</p><p>Before the 1998 season, the American League and the National League each added a fifteenth team. Because of the odd number of teams, only seven games could possibly be scheduled in each league on any given day. Thus, one team in each league would have to be idle on any given day. This would have made it difficult for scheduling, in terms of travel days and the need to end the season before October. In order for MLB officials to continue primarily intraleague play, both leagues would need to carry an even number of teams, so the decision was made to move one club from the AL Central to the NL Central. Eventually, Milwaukee agreed to change leagues.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Often characterized as being a more "traditional" or "pure" league, the National League never adopted the <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> rule that was adopted by the American League in 1973. In theory, this means the role of the <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> is greater in the National League than in the American, because the NL manager must take offense into account when making pitching substitutions and vice versa. However, this is disputed by some, such as former Detroit Tigers manager <a href="/wiki/Jim_Leyland" title="Jim Leyland">Jim Leyland</a>, who claims the American League is more difficult because AL managers are required to know exactly when to pull a pitcher, where an NL manager merely pulls his pitcher when that spot comes up in the batting order.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Overall, there are fewer <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a> and runs scored in the National League than in the American, due to the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> in the NL batting order.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  From the 1970s until the early 1990s, the use of artificial turf in place of natural grass was far more prevalent in National League ballparks than in the American League.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_interleague_play">Permanent interleague play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Permanent interleague play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For the first 96 years of its coexistence with the American League, National League teams faced their AL counterparts only in exhibition games or in the World Series. Beginning in 1997, however, <a href="/wiki/Interleague_play" title="Interleague play">interleague games</a> have been played during the regular season and count in the standings. As part of the agreement instituting interleague play, the American League\'s designated-hitter rule is used only in games where the American League team is the home team.\n</p><p>In 1999, the offices of American League and National League presidents were discontinued and all authority was vested in the Commissioner\'s office. The leagues subsequently appointed "honorary" presidents to carry out ceremonial roles such as the awarding of league championship trophies. Additionally, the distinction between AL and NL umpires was erased, and instead all umpires were unified under MLB control. With these actions, as well as the institution of interleague play, little remains to differentiate between the two leagues besides the American League\'s use of the designated hitter.\n</p><p>By 2011, MLB had changed its policy on interleague play, deciding to schedule interleague games throughout the season rather than only during specially designated periods. This policy would allow each league to have 15 teams, with one team in each league playing an interleague game on any given day. As a condition of the sale of the Astros to <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crane" title="Jim Crane">Jim Crane</a> in November 2011, the team agreed to move to the American League effective with the 2013 season.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Through the 2018 season, the Dodgers and Giants have won the most NL pennants, with 23 each. Representing the National League against the American, the Cardinals have won the most World Series (11) followed by the Giants (8), Dodgers (6), Pirates (5), and Reds (5). St. Louis also holds the distinction of being the only A.A. club to defeat an NL club in the 19th-century version of the World Series, having done so against their now-division rival Cubs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teams">Teams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a All franchises are still in the league, with five remaining in the same city.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Boston Beaneaters (later called the Boston Braves, then Milwaukee Braves, now the <a
