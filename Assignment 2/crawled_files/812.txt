href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/u\xcb\x90/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">u\xcb\x90</span><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a medium-sized and economically important <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Flowering plants">flowering plants</a> commonly known as the <b>mustards</b>, the <b>crucifers</b>, or the <b>cabbage family</b>. Most are <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbaceous plants">herbaceous plants</a>, some shrubs, with simple, although sometimes deeply incised, alternatingly set leaves without <a href="/wiki/Stipule" title="Stipule">stipules</a> or in leaf rosettes, with terminal inflorescences without bracts, containing flowers with four free sepals, four free alternating petals, two short and four longer free stamens, and a fruit with seeds in rows, divided by a thin wall (or septum).\n</p><p>The family contains 372 <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> and 4060 accepted <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The largest genera are <i><a href="/wiki/Draba" <i><a href="/wiki/Cardamine" title="Cardamine">Cardamine</a></i> (233 species), and <i><a href="/wiki/Alyssum" title="Alyssum">Alyssum</a></i> (207 species).\n</p><p>The family contains the <a oleracea</a></i> (e.g., broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, kale, collards), <i><a href="/wiki/Brassica_rapa" title="Brassica rapa">Brassica rapa</a></i> (turnip, Chinese cabbage, etc.), <i><a href="/wiki/Brassica_napus" class="mw-redirect" title="Brassica napus">Brassica napus</a></i> (rapeseed, etc.), <i><a href="/wiki/Raphanus_sativus" class="mw-redirect" title="Raphanus sativus">Raphanus sativus</a></i> (common radish), <i><a href="/wiki/Armoracia_rusticana" class="mw-redirect" title="Armoracia rusticana">Armoracia rusticana</a></i> (horseradish), but also a cut-flower <i><a href="/wiki/Matthiola" title="Matthiola">Matthiola</a></i> (stock) and the <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> (thale cress).\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Pieris_rapae" title="Pieris rapae">Pieris rapae</a></i> and other butterflies of the family <a href="/wiki/Pieridae" title="Pieridae">Pieridae</a> are some of the best-known pests of Brassicaceae species planted as commercial crops. <i>Trichoplusia ni</i> (<a href="/wiki/Cabbage_looper" title="Cabbage looper">cabbage looper</a>) moth is also becoming increasingly problematic for crucifers due to its resistance to commonly used pest control methods.<sup virginiensis</a></i>, depend upon native mustards for their survival, in their native habitats. class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1753 regarded the Brassicaceae as a natural group, naming them "Klass" Tetradynamia. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Barton_Rendle" title="Alfred Barton Rendle">Alfred Barton Rendle</a> placed the family in the order <a href="/wiki/Bessey_system#Oppositifoliae" title="Bessey system">Rhoedales</a>, while <a href="/wiki/George_Bentham" title="George Bentham">George Bentham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Dalton_Hooker" title="Joseph Dalton Hooker">Joseph Dalton Hooker</a> in their system published from 1862\xe2\x80\x931883, assigned it to their cohort Parietales (now the class <a href="/wiki/Violales" title="Violales">Violales</a>). Following Bentham and Hooker, <a href="/wiki/John_Hutchinson_(botanist)" title="John Hutchinson (botanist)">John Hutchinson</a> in 1948 and again in 1964 thought the Brassicaceae to stem from near the Papaveraceae. In 1994, a group of scientists including <a href="/wiki/Walter_Stephen_Judd" title="Walter Stephen Judd">Walter Stephen Judd</a> suggested to include the <a href="/wiki/Capparaceae" title="Capparaceae">Capparaceae</a> in the Brassicaceae. Early DNA-analysis showed that the Capparaceae - as defined at that moment - were <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>, and it was suggested to assign the genera closest to the Brassicaceae to the <a href="/wiki/Cleomaceae" title="Cleomaceae">Cleomaceae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Cleomaceae and Brassicaceae diverged approximately 41 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-SB_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SB-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> All three families have consistently been placed in one order (variably called Capparales or <a href="/wiki/Brassicales" title="Brassicales">Brassicales</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/APG_II" class="mw-redirect" title="APG II">APG II</a> system, merged Cleomaceae and Brassicaceae. Other classifications have continued to recognize the Capparaceae, but with a more restricted circumscription, either including <i>Cleome</i> and its relatives in the Brassicaceae or recognizing them in the segregate family <a href="/wiki/Cleomaceae" title="Cleomaceae">Cleomaceae</a>. The <a href="/wiki/APG_III_system" title="APG III system">APG III system</a> has recently adopted this last solution, but this may change as a consensus arises on this point. class="clade-slabel">&#8239;\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_pronunciation">Etymology and pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology and pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The name <i>Brassicaceae</i> comes to <a href="/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary" title="International scientific vocabulary">international scientific vocabulary</a> from <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a>, from <i><a href="/wiki/Brassica" title="Brassica">Brassica</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Type_genus" title="Type genus">type genus</a>, + <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-aceae#Suffix" class="extiw" title="wikt:-aceae">-aceae</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-MWU_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWU-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> a standardized <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> for plant family names in modern taxonomy. The genus name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Classical Latin</a> word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/brassica#Noun_2" class="extiw" title="wikt:brassica">brassica</a></i>, referring to <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cruciferous_vegetables" title="Cruciferous vegetables">cruciferous vegetables</a>. The alternative older name, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Cruciferae" class="extiw" title="wikt:Cruciferae">Cruciferae</a>, meaning "cross-bearing", describes the four <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petals</a> of mustard flowers, which resemble a <a href="/wiki/Cross" title="Cross">cross</a>. Cruciferae is one of eight plant family names, not derived from a genus name and without the suffix <i>-aceae</i> that are authorized alternative names.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genera">Genera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Genera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Brassicaceae_genera" title="List of Brassicaceae genera">List of Brassicaceae genera</a></div>\n<p>Version 1 of the Plantlist website lists 349 genera.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Species belonging to the Brassicaceae are mostly <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biennial_plant" title="Biennial plant">biennial</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous plants</a>, some are <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_shrub" class="mw-redirect" title="Dwarf shrub">dwarf shrubs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>, and very few <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a>. Although generally terrestrial, a few species such as <a href="/wiki/Water_awlwort" class="mw-redirect" title="Water awlwort">water awlwort</a> live submerged in fresh water. They may have a <a href="/wiki/Taproot" title="Taproot">taproot</a> or a sometimes woody <a href="/wiki/Caudex" title="Caudex">caudex</a> that may have few or many branches, some have thin or tuberous <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomes</a>, or rarely develop <a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">runners</a>. Few species have multi-cellular glands. <a href="/wiki/Trichome" title="Trichome">Hairs</a> consist of one cell and occur in many forms: from simple to forked, star-, tree- or T-shaped, rarely taking the form of a shield or scale. They are never topped by a gland. The <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stems</a> may be upright, rise up towards the tip, or lie flat, are mostly herbaceous but sometimes woody. Stems carry leaves or the stems may be leafless (in <i><a href="/wiki/Caulanthus" title="Caulanthus">Caulanthus</a></i>), and some species lack stems altogether. The leaves do not have <a href="/wiki/Stipule" title="Stipule">stipules</a>, but there may be a pair of glands at base of <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">leafstalks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedicel_(botany)" title="Pedicel (botany)">flowerstalks</a>. The leaf may be seated or have a leafstalk. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Morphology_(large-scale_features)" title="Leaf">leaf blade</a> is usually simple, entire or <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Divisions_of_the_blade" title="Leaf">dissected</a>, rarely <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Divisions_of_the_blade" title="Leaf">trifoliolate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Divisions_of_the_blade" title="Leaf">pinnately compound</a>. A leaf rosette at the base may be present or absent. The leaves along the stem are almost always <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Arrangement_on_the_stem" title="Leaf">alternately arranged</a>, rarely apparently opposite.<sup id="cite_ref-Neotropikey_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neotropikey-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The stomata are of the <a href="/wiki/Stoma#anisocytic" title="Stoma">anisocytic</a> type.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Genome_size" title="Genome size">genome size</a> of Brassicaceae compared to that of other Angiosperm families is very small to small (less than 3.425 million base pairs per cell), varying from 150 Mbp in <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sphaerocardamum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sphaerocardamum (page does not exist)">Sphaerocardamum</a></i> spp., to 2375 Mbp <i><a href="/wiki/Bunias_orientalis" title="Bunias orientalis">Bunias orientalis</a></i>. The number of <a href="/wiki/Homologous_chromosomes" class="mw-redirect" title="Homologous chromosomes">homologous chromosome sets</a> varies from four (n=4) in some <i><a href="/wiki/Physaria" title="Physaria">Physaria</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stenopetalum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stenopetalum (page does not exist)">Stenopetalum</a></i> species, five (n=5) in other <i>Physaria</i> and <i>Stenopetalum</i> species, <i>Arabidopsis thaliana</i> and a <i><a href="/wiki/Mathiola" class="mw-redirect" title="Mathiola">Mathiola</a></i> species, to seventeen (n=17). About 35% of the species in which chromosomes have been counted have eight sets (n=8). Due to <a href="/wiki/Polyploidy" title="Polyploidy">polyploidy</a>, some species may have up to 256 individual chromosomes, with some very high counts in the North-American species of <i>Cardamine</i>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cardamine_diphylla" title="Cardamine diphylla">C. diphylla</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">Hybridisation</a> is not unusual in Brassicaceae, especially in <i><a href="/wiki/Arabis" title="Arabis">Arabis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rorippa" title="Rorippa">Rorippa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cardamine" title="Cardamine">Cardamine</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Boechera" title="Boechera">Boechera</a></i>. Hybridisation between species originating in Africa and California, and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Speciation#Speciation_via_polyploidization" title="Speciation">polyploidisation</a> is surmised for <i><a href="/wiki/Lepidium" title="Lepidium">Lepidium</a></i> species native to Australia and New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-SB_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SB-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inflorescence_and_flower">Inflorescence and flower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inflorescence and flower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg/220px-Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg/330px-Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg/440px-Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3164" data-file-height="3292" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Floral_diagram_Erysimum_Bowles_Mauve_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Typical floral diagram of a Brassicaceae (<i>Erysimum</i> "Bowles\' Mauve")</div></div></div><p>Flowers may be arranged in <a href="/wiki/Raceme" title="Raceme">racemes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panicle" title="Panicle">panicles</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Corymb" title="Corymb">corymbs</a>, with pedicels sometimes in the axil of a bract, and few species have flowers that sit individually on flower stems that spring from the axils of rosette leaves. The orientation of the pedicels when fruits are ripe varies dependent on the species. The flowers are <a href="/wiki/Plant_reproductive_morphology#Terminology" title="Plant reproductive morphology">bisexual</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floral_symmetry#Actinomorphic" title="Floral symmetry">star symmetrical</a> (zygomorphic in <i><a href="/wiki/Iberis" title="Iberis">Iberis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Teesdalia" title="Teesdalia">Teesdalia</a></i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Ovary_(botany)#Superior_ovary" title="Ovary (botany)">ovary positioned above the other floral parts</a>. Each flower has four free or seldomly merged <a href="/wiki/Sepal" title="Sepal">sepals</a>, the lateral two sometimes with a shallow spur, which are mostly shed after flowering, rarely persistent, may be reflexed, spreading, ascending, or erect, together forming a tube-, bell- or urn-shaped calyx. Each flower has four <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petals</a>, set alternating with the sepals, although in some species these are rudimentary or absent. They may be differentiated into a <a href="/wiki/Petal#Shape_and_size" title="Petal">blade</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Petal#Shape_and_size" title="Petal">claw</a> or not, and consistently lack basal appendages. The blade is entire or has an indent at the tip, and may sometimes be much smaller than the claws. The mostly six <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a> are set in two whorls: usually the two lateral, outer ones are shorter than the four inner stamens, but very rarely the stamens can all have the same length, and very rarely species have different numbers of stamens such as sixteen to twenty four in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megacarpaea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Megacarpaea (page does not exist)">Megacarpaea</a></i>, four in <i><a href="/wiki/Cardamine_hirsuta" title="Cardamine hirsuta">Cardamine hirsuta</a></i>, and two in <i><a href="/wiki/Coronopus" title="Coronopus">Coronopus</a></i>. The filaments are slender and not fused, while the anthers consist of two pollen producing cavities, and open with longitudinal slits. The pollen grains are <a href="/wiki/Tricolpate" title="Tricolpate">tricolpate</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Receptacle_(botany)" title="Receptacle (botany)">receptacle</a> carries a variable number of <a href="/wiki/Nectar" title="Nectar">nectaries</a>, but these are always present opposite the base of the lateral stamens.<sup id="cite_ref-Neotropikey_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neotropikey-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BioDis_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BioDis-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><h3><span id="Ovary.2C_fruit_and_seed"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ovary,_fruit_and_seed">Ovary, fruit and seed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ovary, fruit and seed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There is one <a href="/wiki/Gynoecium#Gynoecium_position" title="Gynoecium">superior</a> <a href="/wiki/Gynoecium#Pistils" title="Gynoecium">pistil</a> that consists of two <a href="/wiki/Carpel" class="mw-redirect" title="Carpel">carpels</a> that may either sit directly above the base of the stamens or on a <a href="/wiki/Gynophore" title="Gynophore">stalk</a>. It initially consists of only one cavity but during its further development a thin wall grows that divides the cavity, both placentas and separates the two valves (a so-called false septum). Rarely, there is only one cavity without a septum. The 2\xe2\x80\x93600 <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">ovules</a> are usually along the side margin of the carpels, or rarely at the top. Fruits are capsules that open with two valves, usually towards the top. These are called <a href="/wiki/Silique" title="Silique">silique</a> if at least three times longer than wide, or silicle if the length is less than three times the width. The fruit is very variable in its other traits. There may be one persistent <a href="/wiki/Stigma_(botany)#Style" title="Stigma (botany)">style</a> that connects the ovary to the globular or conical <a href="/wiki/Stigma_(botany)" title="Stigma (botany)">stigma</a>, which is undivided or has two spreading or connivent lobes. The variously shaped seeds are usually yellow or brown in color, and arranged in one or two rows in each cavety. The <a href="/wiki/Cotyledon" title="Cotyledon">seed leaves</a> are entire or have a notch at the tip. The seed does not contain <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Neotropikey_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neotropikey-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_with_similar_families">Differences with similar families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Differences with similar families">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Brassicaceae have a bisymmetical corolla (left is mirrored by right, stem-side by out-side, but each quarter is not symmetrical), a septum dividing the fruit, lack <a href="/wiki/Stipule" title="Stipule">stipules</a> and have simple (although sometimes deeply incised) leaves. The <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister family</a> <a href="/wiki/Cleomaceae" title="Cleomaceae">Cleomaceae</a> has <a href="/wiki/Floral_symmetry#Zygomorphic" title="Floral symmetry">bilateral symmetrical</a> corollas (left is mirrored by right, but stem-side is different from out-side), stipules and mostly palmately divided leaves, and mostly no septum.<sup id="cite_ref-Neotropikey_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neotropikey-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Capparaceae generally have a <a href="/wiki/Gynophore" title="Gynophore">gynophore</a>, sometimes an <a href="/w/index.php?title=Androgynophore&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Androgynophore (page does not exist)">androgynophore</a>, and a variable number of stamens.<sup id="cite_ref-BioDis_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BioDis-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phytochemistry">Phytochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phytochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Almost all Brassicaceae have <a href="/wiki/C3_carbon_fixation" title="C3 carbon fixation">C3 carbon fixation</a>. The only exceptions are a few <i><a href="/wiki/Moricandia" title="Moricandia">Moricandia</a></i> species, which have a hybrid system between C3 and <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4 carbon fixation</a>, C4 fixation being more efficient in drought, high temperature and low nitrate availability.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Brassicaceae contain different cocktails of dozens of <a href="/wiki/Glucosinolate" title="Glucosinolate">glucosinolates</a>. They also contain enzymes called <a href="/wiki/Myrosinase" title="Myrosinase">myrosinases</a>, that convert the glucosinolates into <a href="/wiki/Isothiocyanate" title="Isothiocyanate">isothiocyanates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thiocyanate" title="Thiocyanate">thiocyanates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrile" title="Nitrile">nitriles</a>, which are toxic to many organisms, and so help guard against herbivory.<sup id="cite_ref-HAW_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAW-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Brassicaceae can be found almost on the entire land surface of the planet, but it is absent from Antarctica, and in some areas in the tropics i.e. north-eastern Brazil, the <a href="/wiki/Congo_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Congo basin">Congo basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Southeast_Asia" title="Maritime Southeast Asia">Maritime Southeast Asia</a> and tropical <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>. The area of origin of the family is possibly the <a href="/wiki/Irano-Turanian_Region" title="Irano-Turanian Region">Irano-Turanian Region</a>, where approximately 900 species occur in 150 different genera. About 530 of them are <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemics</a>. Next in abundance comes the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean Region">Mediterranean Region</a> with around 630 species (290 of which are endemic) in 113 genera. The family is less prominent in the <a href="/wiki/Saharo-Arabian_Region" title="Saharo-Arabian Region">Saharo-Arabian Region</a> - 65 genera, 180 species of which 62 endemic - and North-America (comprising the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Atlantic_Region" title="North American Atlantic Region">North American Atlantic Region</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Floristic_Region" title="Rocky Mountain Floristic Region">Rocky Mountain Floristic Region</a>) - 99 genera, 780 species of which 600 endemic -. South-America has 40 genera containing 340 native species, Southern Africa 15 genera with over 100 species, and Australia and New-Zealand have 19 genera with 114 species between them.<sup id="cite_ref-SB_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SB-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brassicaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Brassicaceae are almost exclusively <a href="/wiki/Entomophily" title="Entomophily">pollinated by insects</a>. A chemical mechanism in the pollen is active in many species to avoid <a href="/wiki/Selfing" title="Selfing">selfing</a>. Two notable exceptions are exclusive <a href="/wiki/Cleistogamy" title="Cleistogamy">self pollination in closed flowers</a> in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardamine_chenopodifolia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cardamine chenopodifolia (page does not exist)">Cardamine chenopodifolia</a></i>, and wind pollination in <i><a href="/wiki/Pringlea" title="Pringlea">Pringlea antiscorbutica</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BioDis_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BioDis-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Although it can be pollinated, <i><a href="/wiki/Alliaria_petiolata" title="Alliaria petiolata">Alliaria petiolata</a></i> is self-fertile. Most species reproduce sexually through seed, but <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardamine_bulbifera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cardamine bulbifera (page does not exist)">Cardamine bulbifera</a></i> produces <a href="/wiki/Gemma_(botany)" title="Gemma (botany)">gemmae</a> and in others, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cardamine_pentaphyllos" title="Cardamine pentaphyllos">Cardamine pentaphyllos</a></i>, the coral-like roots easily break into segments, that will grow into separate plants.<sup id="cite_ref-BioDis_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BioDis-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In some species, such as in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Cardamine" title="Cardamine">Cardamine</a></i>, seed pods open with force and so catapult the seeds quite far. Many of these have sticky seed coats, assisting long distance dispersal by animals, and this may also explain several intercontinental dispersal events in the genus, and its near global distribution. Brassicaceae are common on <a href="/wiki/Serpentine_soil" title="Serpentine soil">serpentine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> rich in <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>. Over a hundred species in the family accumulate <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, which is a record percentage.<sup id="cite_ref-MOBOT_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MOBOT-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Several <i>Alyssum</i> species can accumulate nickel up to 0.3% of their dry weight, and may be useful in soil remediation or even bio-mining.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Brassicaceae contain <a href="/wiki/Glucosinolate" title="Glucosinolate">glucosinolates</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Myrosinase" title="Myrosinase">myrosinases</a> inside their cells. When the cell is damaged, the myrosinases <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolise</a> the glucosinolates, leading to the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Isothiocyanate" title="Isothiocyanate">isothiocyanates</a>, which are <a href="/wiki/Plant_defense_against_herbivory" title="Plant defense against herbivory">compounds toxic to most animals</a>, fungi and bacteria. Some insect herbivores have developed counter adaptations such as rapid absorption of the glucosinates, quick alternative breakdown into non-toxic compounds and avoiding cell damage. In the whites family (Pieridae), one counter mechanisme involves glucosinolate sulphatase, which changes the glucosinolate, so that it cannot be converted to isothiocyanate. A second is that the glucosinates are quickly broken down, forming nitriles.<sup id="cite_ref-HAW_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAW-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Differences between the mixtures of glucosinolates between species and even within species is large, and individual plants may produce in excess of fifty individual substances. The energy penalty for synthesising all these glucosinolates may be as high as 15% of the total needed to produce a leaf. <i><a href="/wiki/Bittercress" title="Bittercress">Bittercress</a></i> (<i>Barbarea vulgaris</i>) also produces <a href="/wiki/Triterpenoid_saponin" title="Triterpenoid saponin">triterpenoid saponins</a>. These adaptations and counter adaptations probably have led to extensive diversification in both the Brassicaceae and one of its major pests, the butterfly family <a href="/wiki/Pieridae" title="Pieridae">Pieridae</a>. A particular cocktail of volatile glucosinates triggers egg-laying in many species. Thus a particular crop can sometimes be protected by planting bittercress as a deadly bait, for the saponins kill the caterpillars, but the butterfly is still lured by the bittercress to lay its egg on the leaves.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\nA moth that feeds on a range of Brassicaceae is the <a href="/wiki/Diamondback_moth" title="Diamondback moth">diamondback moth</a> (<i>Plutella xylostella</i>). Like the Pieridae, it is capable of converting isothiocyanates into less problematic <a href="/wiki/Nitrile" title="Nitrile">nitriles</a>. Managing this pest in crops became more complicated after resistance developed against a toxin produced by <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_thuringiensis" title="Bacillus thuringiensis">Bacillus thuringiensis</a></i>, which is used as a wide spectrum biological <a href="/wiki/Plant_protection" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant protection">plant protection</a> against caterpillars. <a href="/wiki/Parasitoid" title="Parasitoid">Parasitoid</a> wasps that feed on such insect herbivores are attracted to the chemical compounds released by the plants, and thus are able to locate their prey. The <a href="/wiki/Cabbage_aphid" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabbage aphid">cabbage aphid</a> (<i>Brevicoryne brassicae</i>) stores glucosinolates and synthesises its own myrosinases, which may deter its potential predators.<sup id="cite_ref-MOBOT_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MOBOT-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since its introduction in the 19th century, <i><a href="/wiki/Alliaria_petiolata" title="Alliaria petiolata">Alliaria petiolata</a></i> has been shown to be extremely successful as an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in temperate North America due, in part, to its secretion of allelopathic chemicals. These inhibit the <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a> of most competing plants and kill beneficial soil <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a> needed by many plants, such as many tree species, to successfully see their seedlings grow to maturity. The <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> formation of an herb layer carpet by this plant has been shown to dramatically alter forests, making them wetter, having fewer and fewer trees, and having more vines such as poison ivy, <i><a href="/wiki/Toxicodendron_radicans" title="Toxicodendron radicans">Toxicodendron radicans</a></i>. The overall herb layer <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> is also drastically reduced, particularly in terms of <a href="/wiki/Sedge" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedge">sedges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forb" title="Forb">forbs</a>. Research has found that removing 80 percent of the garlic mustard <a href="/wiki/Infestation" title="Infestation">infestation</a> plants did not lead to a particularly significant recovery of that <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">diversity</a>. Instead, it required around 100 percent removal. Given that not one of an estimated 76 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> that <a href="/wiki/Prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Prey">prey</a> on the plant has been approved for <a href="/wiki/Biological_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological control">biological control</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and the variety of mechanisms the plant has to ensure its dominance without them (e.g. high seed production, self-fertility, <a href="/wiki/Allelopathy" title="Allelopathy">allelopathy</a>, spring growth that occurs before nearly all native plants, roots that break easily when pulling attempts are made, a complete lack of palatability for herbivores at all life stages, et cetera) it is unlikely that such a high level of control can be established and maintained on the whole.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fs.fed.us_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fs.fed.us-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wiki.bugwood.org_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiki.bugwood.org-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It is estimated that adequate control can be achieved with the introduction of two European <a href="/wiki/Weevil" title="Weevil">weevils</a>, including one that is <a href="/wiki/Monophagous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophagous">monophagous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Landis_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landis-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reardon_2017_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reardon_2017-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/USDA" class="mw-redirect" title="USDA">USDA</a>\'s TAG group has blocked these introductions since 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-Becker_2017_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Becker_2017-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In addition to being invasive, garlic mustard also is a threat to native North American <i>Pieris</i> butterflies such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pieris_oleracea" title="Pieris oleracea">Pieris oleracea</a></i>, as they preferentially <a href="/wiki/Oviposit" class="mw-redirect" title="Oviposit">oviposit</a> on it, although it is toxic to their larvae.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Aethionema" title="Aethionema">Aethionema</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Alyssum" title="Alyssum">Alyssum</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Arabis" title="Arabis">Arabis</a></i>, <i><a title="Cheiranthus">Cheiranthus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Erysimum" title="Erysimum">Erysimum</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hesperis" title="Hesperis">Hesperis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iberis" title="Iberis">Iberis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lobularia_(plant)" title="Lobularia (plant)">Lobularia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lunaria" title="Lunaria">Lunaria</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Malcolmia" title="Malcolmia">Malcolmia</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Matthiola" title="Matthiola">Matthiola</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-SB_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SB-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Honesty_(plant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Honesty (plant)">Honesty</a> (<i>Lunaria annua</i>) is cultivated for the decorative value of the translucent remains of the fruits after drying.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> However, it can be a pest species in areas where it is not native.\n</p><p>The small Eurasian weed <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> is widely used as <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> in the study of the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a> of flowering plants (<a href="/wiki/Angiospermae" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiospermae">Angiospermae</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some species are useful as food plants for <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a>, such as certain wild mustard and cress species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Turritis_glabra" class="mw-redirect" title="Turritis id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Garlic mustard, <i><a href="/wiki/Alliaria_petiolata" title="Alliaria petiolata">Alliaria petiolata</a></i>, is one of the most aggressive and damaging <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Invasive aggressive mustard species are known for being self-fertile, seeding very heavily with small seeds that have a lengthy lifespan coupled with a very high rate of viability and germination, and for being completely unpalatable for both herbivores and insects in areas they are not native to. Garlic mustard is toxic to several rarer North American <i>Pieris</i> species.<sup id="cite_ref-wiki.bugwood.org_24-1" class="reference"><a
