Parliament">Act</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>, which established <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> councils in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>.  It came into effect on 1 April 1889, except for the <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a>, which came into existence on 21 March at the request of the <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London County Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1888&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Bill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1886" title="United Kingdom general election, 1886">1886 general election</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> administration headed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gascoyne-Cecil,_3rd_Marquess_of_Salisbury" title="Robert Gascoyne-Cecil, 3rd Marquess of Salisbury">Lord Salisbury</a> was formed. However the Conservatives did not have a majority of seats and had to rely on the support of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Unionist_Party" title="Liberal Unionist Party">Liberal Unionist Party</a>. As part of the price for this support the Liberal Unionists  demanded that a bill be introduced placing county government under the control of elected councils, modelled on the borough councils introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Corporations_Act_1835" title="Municipal Corporations Act 1835">Municipal Corporations Act 1835</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Accordingly, the <b>Local Government (England and Wales) Bill</b> was introduced to the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> on 19 March 1888, by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Local_Government_Board" title="President of the Local Government Board">President of the Local Government Board</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Thomson_Ritchie" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Thomson Ritchie">Charles Ritchie</a>. The Bill proposed the creation of elected county councils to take over the administrative functions of the magistrates of the <a href="/wiki/Quarter_Sessions" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter Sessions">Quarter Sessions</a> courts, that ten large cities should be "counties of themselves" for the purposes of local government and that each county was to be divided into urban and rural districts, based on existing <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_district" title="Sanitary district">sanitary districts</a>, governed by a district council. The county and district councils were to consist partly of directly elected "elective councillors" and partly of "selected councillors", chosen by the elective councillors in a similar manner to <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">aldermen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal boroughs</a>.\n</p><p>The counties to be used for local government were to be the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">historic counties of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_Wales" title="Historic counties of Wales">Wales</a>. A county council was to be formed for each of the <a href="/wiki/Riding_(division)" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding (division)">ridings</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and the three divisions of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> (<a href="/wiki/Holland,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Holland, Lincolnshire">Holland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kesteven" title="Kesteven">Kesteven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lindsey_(government_district)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lindsey (government district)">Lindsey</a>). In addition a new <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a> was to be formed from the area of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Board_of_Works" title="Metropolitan Board of Works">Metropolitan Board of Works</a>. This would have led to the creation of fifty-seven county councils. The boundaries of the counties were to be those used for parliamentary purposes, adjusted to include urban sanitary districts on county borders within a single county.\n</p><p>The ten cities to be "dealt with as separate counties" were <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Kingston-on-Hull</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>.\n</p><p>Existing urban and rural sanitary districts, created in 1872, were to be redesignated as urban and rural districts. Urban districts that lay across county boundaries were to be included in the county with the greater part of the population in the 1881 <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a>. Existing rural sanitary districts were to split on county lines to form rural districts.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Passage_through_Parliament">Passage through Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1888&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Passage through Parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There were a large number of changes to the Bill as it passed through parliament. The terms <a href="/wiki/Administrative_county" title="Administrative county">administrative county</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> were introduced to designate the new areas of local government, while the "selected councillors" became "county aldermen". The government withdrew the sections relating to the creation of district councils, which were eventually brought into existence by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1894" title="Local Government Act 1894">Local Government Act 1894</a>.\n</p><p>Members of both houses made representations on behalf of counties and boroughs, and this led to an increase in the number of local authorities.\n</p>\n<ul><li>The eastern and western divisions of <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> became administrative counties<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Isle of Ely</a> was separated from Cambridgeshire<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/East_Suffolk_(county)" title="East Suffolk (county)">eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Suffolk_(county)" title="West Suffolk (county)">western</a> divisions of <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> were divided for local government purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Soke_of_Peterborough" title="Soke of Peterborough">Soke of Peterborough</a> was separated from the remainder of Northamptonshire.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Attempts to create administrative counties for the <a href="/wiki/Cinque_Ports" title="Cinque Ports">Cinque Ports</a> and <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire_Potteries" title="Staffordshire Potteries">Staffordshire Potteries</a> were not successful.\n</p><p>The population limit for county boroughs was lowered twice, firstly to 100,000, then to 50,000. A number of smaller <a href="/wiki/Counties_corporate" class="mw-redirect" title="Counties corporate">counties corporate</a> were also given county borough status. Mr Ritchie conceded on 8 June:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"Now that they had gone down so far in population as 50,000 there arose a question as to the admission of boroughs which had not so large a population as 50,000, but which had very peculiar claims. He referred to the counties of cities. [...] Two or three of these cities had so small a population that he did not propose to deal with them in this way. The best course was to give the names of the cities which he proposed to include. They were Exeter, Lincoln, Chester, Gloucester, Worcester, and Canterbury."</p></blockquote>\n<p>The effect of these changes was to increase the number of county boroughs from ten to fifty-nine. With a population of around 50,000 at the 1881 census, the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> was initially proposed for county borough status.<sup id="cite_ref-davis_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davis-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="County_councils">County councils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1888&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: County councils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The councils were subject to <a href="/wiki/Triennial" title="Triennial">triennial</a> elections, the first taking place in January 1889. The county councils elected in 1889 were known as "provisional" councils until coming into their powers on 1 April. Every administrative county was divided into electoral divisions, each returning a single councillor. Following the election, the county councillors then elected county aldermen, there being one alderman for every three councillors. The London County Council had a different constitution, with two councillors elected for each parliamentary constituency in the county, and a ratio of one alderman to six councillors. The councillors appointed a chairman and vice chairman, who had a one-year term of office, although they could be reappointed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers">Powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1888&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The powers and responsibilities transferred from the quarter sessions to the councils were enumerated in the Act. These included:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Making and levying of rates</li>\n<li>Borrowing of money</li>\n<li>Passing of county accounts</li>\n<li>Maintenance and construction of county buildings such as shire halls, county halls, court houses and police stations</li>\n<li>Licensing of places of entertainment and of race courses</li>\n<li>Provisions of asylums for pauper lunatics</li>\n<li>Establishment and maintenance of reformatory and industrial schools</li>\n<li>Repair of county roads and bridges\xe2\x80\xa0</li>\n<li>Appointment, dismissal and setting of salaries for county officers</li>\n<li>Division of the county into polling places for parliamentary elections, and the provision of polling stations</li>\n<li>Control of contagious diseases in animals, and of destructive insects</li>\n<li>Fish conservancy and control of wild birds</li>\n<li>Weights and measures</li></ul>\n<p>\xe2\x80\xa0 The council could also declare a road a "main road" and take over its maintenance, and purchase existing bridges or build new ones.\n</p><p>County borough corporations also exercised these powers, in addition to those of a municipal borough.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standing_joint_committees">Standing joint committees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1888&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Standing joint committees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Control of the county police was to be exercised jointly by the quarter sessions and the county council through a standing joint committee. The committees were replaced by <a href="/wiki/Police_authority" title="Police authority">police authorities</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Police_Act_1964" title="Police Act 1964">Police Act 1964</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counties_for_other_purposes">Counties for other purposes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1888&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Counties for other purposes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Act also ensured that the boundaries used for what it terms "non-administrative purposes" would be synchronised with the borders between the administrative counties. The non-administrative purposes were stated to be <i>"<a href="/wiki/High_sheriff" title="High sheriff">sheriff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord lieutenant">lieutenant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Custos_rotulorum" title="Custos rotulorum">custos rotulorum</a>, justices, militia, coroner, or other"</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> thus approximating to the functions of modern <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceremonial county">ceremonial counties</a>.\n</p><p>The counties of Cambridgeshire, Lincolnshire, Northamptonshire, Suffolk, Sussex and Yorkshire were undivided so far as they were one county at the passing of the Act.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The three <a href="/wiki/Ridings_of_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Ridings of Yorkshire">ridings of Yorkshire</a> and the three <a href="/wiki/Parts_of_Lincolnshire" title="Parts of Lincolnshire">parts of Lincolnshire</a> therefore retained their status.\n</p><p>County boroughs were to be administrative counties of themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-S31_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  The Act provided that each county borough that had previously been part of a county (i.e., was not a county corporate) should continue to be part of that county for non-administrative purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-S31_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  If a county borough did not have a separate <a href="/wiki/Assizes_(England_and_Wales)" class="mw-redirect" title="Assizes (England and Wales)">commission of assize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oyer_and_terminer" title="Oyer and terminer">oyer and terminer</a> and jury service, or gaol delivery, it was deemed to be part of one or more adjoining counties for those purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-S31_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  The Act also provided for certain financial adjustments between county boroughs and adjoining counties.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Act did not in terms affect the status of cities and towns which were <a href="/wiki/Counties_corporate" class="mw-redirect" title="Counties corporate">counties corporate</a>.  Most of the counties corporate became county boroughs and therefore administrative counties of themselves, but while other county boroughs continued to be part of their existing counties for all other purposes, that did not apply to existing counties corporate.<sup id="cite_ref-S31_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S31-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  Those that did not become county boroughs became part of adjacent administrative counties but retained their existing lieutenancies and shrievalties.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_provisions">Other provisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_1888&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other provisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Under section 48 of the Act all <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(division)" title="Liberty (division)">liberties</a> and franchises, with the exception of those that became separate administrative counties, merged with the county they formed part of for parliamentary elections. The <a href="/wiki/Cinque_Ports" title="Cinque Ports">Cinque Ports</a>, together with "the two ancient towns and their members" (which for some purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Lieutenancy" class="mw-redirect" title="Lieutenancy">lieutenancy</a>, were considered a distinct county), were to become part of the county where they were situated. Section 49 allowed for the creation by provisional order of a council for the "<a href="/wiki/Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Scilly Isles">Scilly Islands</a>" to be established as a unitary authority outside the administrative county of Cornwall. This was duly formed in 1890 as the Isles of Scilly Rural District.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_administrative_counties_and_county_boroughs_created_in_1889">List of administrative
