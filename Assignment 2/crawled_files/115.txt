id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Ladies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A ballad by the name "Spanish Ladies" was registered in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Stationer%27s_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Stationer&#39;s Company">Stationer\'s Company</a> on December 14, 1624.<sup id="cite_ref-ox_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ox-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The oldest mention of the present song does not, however, appear until the 1796 logbook of <a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Nellie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="HMS Nellie (page does not exist)">HMS <i>Nellie</i></a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (May 2014)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> making it more likely an invention of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic era</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ox_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ox-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The timing of the mention in the <i>Nellie</i>\'s logbook suggests that the song was created during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition" title="War of the First Coalition">War of the First Coalition</a> (1793\xe2\x80\x9396), when the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> carried supplies to <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spain</a> to aid its resistance to <a href="/wiki/First_French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="First French Republic">revolutionary France</a>. It probably gained in popularity during the later <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a> when <a href="/wiki/British_Army_during_the_Napoleonic_Wars" title="British Army during the Napoleonic Wars">British soldiers</a> were transported throughout the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberian peninsula">Iberian peninsula</a> to assist rebels fighting against the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleonic Spain">French occupation</a>. After their victory over the <a href="/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="Grande Arm\xc3\xa9e">Grande Arm\xc3\xa9e</a>, these soldiers were returned to Britain but forbidden to bring their Spanish wives, lovers, and children with them.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The song predates the proper emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Sea_shanty" title="Sea shanty">sea shanty</a>. Shanties were the work songs of <a href="/wiki/Merchant_marine" class="mw-redirect" title="Merchant marine">merchant sailors</a>, rather than naval ones. However, in his 1840 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Poor_Jack" title="Poor Jack">Poor Jack</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Marryat" title="Frederick Marryat">Captain Frederick Marryat</a> reports that the song "Spanish Ladies"\xe2\x80\x94though once very popular\xe2\x80\x94was "now almost forgotten" and he included it in whole in order to "rescue it from oblivion".<sup id="cite_ref-captainjack_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-captainjack-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The emergence of shanties in the mid-19th century then revived its fortunes,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> to the point where it is now sometimes included as a "borrowed song" within the genre.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Ladies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>"Spanish Ladies" is the story of British navy men sailing north from Spain and along the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>. The crew are unable to determine their <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> by sighting as the distance between <a href="/wiki/Ushant" title="Ushant">Ushant</a> to the south and <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">the Scillies</a> to the north is wide. Instead, they locate themselves by the depth and the sandy bottom they have <a href="/wiki/Depth_sounding" title="Depth sounding">sounded</a>. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Ransome" title="Arthur Ransome">Arthur Ransome</a>, in his novel <a href="/wiki/Peter_Duck" title="Peter Duck">Peter Duck</a>, suggests that the succession of headlands on the English shore indicates the ship tacking up-channel away from the French coast, identifying a new landmark on each tack.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> However, one verse (quoted below) states that they had the wind at southwest and <a href="/wiki/Square_(sailing)" title="Square (sailing)">squared</a> their mainsails to run up the Channel, rather than beating against a northeasterly.\n</p><p>This is the version recorded in the 1840 <i>Poor Jack</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-captainjack_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-captainjack-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is one of many. Notable variations are shown in parentheses after each line.\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">\n<p>Farewell and adieu to you, Spanish ladies, (alt: "...to Spanish ladies")<br />\nFarewell and adieu to you, ladies of Spain; (alt: "...to ladies of Spain;")<br />\n&#160; &#160; &#160;For we have received orders (alt: "...\'re under orders")<br />\n&#160; &#160; &#160;For to sail to old England,<br />\nBut we hope in a short time to see you again. (alt: "And here\'s to the health of each true-hearted lass.")\n</p>\n</div>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Ladies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The song has been found in several different minor and major keys.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Cecil Sharp considers the version in minor keys to be the original.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several variants exist that utilize the same melody but substitute different lyrics. The one sung in <i><a href="/wiki/Jaws_(film)" title="Jaws (film)">Jaws</a></i> reset the destination from England to <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a>. "<a href="/wiki/Brisbane_Ladies" title="Brisbane Ladies">Brisbane Ladies</a>" is an Australian tune about <a href="/wiki/Drover_(Australian)" title="Drover (Australian)">drovers</a> instead of sailors. A significantly modified version called "<a href="/wiki/The_Ryans_and_the_Pittmans" title="The Ryans and the Pittmans">The Ryans and the Pittmans</a>", widely known as "We\'ll Rant and We\'ll Roar", is from Newfoundland; this version was recorded as "Rant and Roar" by <a href="/wiki/Great_Big_Sea" title="Great Big Sea">Great Big Sea</a> on their album <i><a href="/wiki/Up_(Great_Big_Sea_album)" title="Up (Great Big Sea album)">Up</a></i>. There is an American variant called "Yankee Whalermen". A version was created especially for the <i><a href="/wiki/Bluenose" title="Bluenose">Bluenose</a></i>, a famed Canadian ship based in <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>.\n</p><p>The melody is also used for the "<a href="/wiki/Streets_of_Laredo_(song)" title="Streets of Laredo (song)">Streets of Laredo</a>", a 19th-century American cowboy ballad, and the song forms part of Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_J._Wood" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry J. Wood">Henry J.
