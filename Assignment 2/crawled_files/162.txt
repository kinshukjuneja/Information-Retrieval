navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>\nThe <b>Major League Baseball relocations of 1950s\xe2\x80\x931960s</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Relocation_of_professional_sports_teams" title="Relocation of professional sports teams">move</a> of several <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> franchises to the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a>. This was in stark contrast to the early years of modern <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> intentionally put teams in <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> cities to compete directly with those teams. <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> were two-team towns, while New York City had three. This effectively had baseball confined to the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a>, with no teams located west of St. Louis or south of Washington, D.C.\n</p><p>The moves, though controversial in some circles, brought new prosperity to the game of baseball. As of 2018 <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> remains as the only market with two pre-expansion era teams, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>, though the White Sox have come close to relocating on several occasions.\n</p><p>The expansion of Major League Baseball out west mirrors the story of the expansion of the population in the United States. As the economy in the country grew, Americans headed out west. Baseball soon followed, likely because of the ease of <a href="/wiki/Air_travel" title="Air travel">air travel</a> by commercial jet. The American economy flourished in the 1960s, and baseball was able to expand and evolve at an unprecedented rate. Economic push and pull factors caused many teams to relocate, and the emergence of cities in the new frontier allowed baseball teams to pop up across the country. The moves of the Brooklyn Dodgers and New York Giants to California in 1958 broke the ice and opened the West Coast to the market of baseball. Starting in the 1960s, both the National and American Leagues expanded to add teams in new and existing cities, adding 14 teams as of 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MLBteams1903to1953.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/MLBteams1903to1953.png/400px-MLBteams1903to1953.png" width="400" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/MLBteams1903to1953.png/600px-MLBteams1903to1953.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/MLBteams1903to1953.png 2x" data-file-width="731" data-file-height="383" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MLBteams1903to1953.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cities that hosted MLB teams from 1903 to 1953; cities that hosted one team are in red, cities that hosted two teams are in black. New York/Brooklyn, with three teams, is in orange. Major league baseball did not experience relocation or expansion between 1903 and 1953.</div></div></div>\n<p>During the early years of the American League as a major league, the league placed franchises in cities that were either in direct competition with National League teams (New York City, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>) or in markets abandoned by the Senior Circuit after the 1899 contraction (<a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and briefly <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>). Only <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, home of the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a>, was a true "new" baseball market for the American League, although the National League had previously hosted the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Wolverines" title="Detroit Wolverines">Detroit Wolverines</a> between 1881 and 1888.\n</p><p>In these early years, only two National League markets did not have an American League counterpart: <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>. It is unknown why a second team was not placed in Cincinnati to compete with the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a>, though there was a reasoning behind Pittsburgh not getting an American League team. The <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> were one of baseball\'s dominant teams early in the 20th century, and as part of the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Constitution" title="Major League Baseball Constitution">National Agreement</a> in 1903, the Junior Circuit agreed not to place a team in Pittsburgh.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>After the relocation of the original Baltimore Orioles to New York in 1903 where they ultimately became the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>, no major league team would relocate for 50 years. The set-up was also reflective of the population at the time, as most of the major population areas were in the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a> in the aftermath of <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Reconstruction Era">Reconstruction</a> and later the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a>. Baseball was tied to the history and culture of New York City up until the 1950s. Three of the best teams in the league were located in New York City: the Yankees, Giants and Dodgers.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning_of_the_moves">Beginning of the moves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginning of the moves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Over the years, though, it became apparent that one team would be more popular than the other in a given market. In cities with multiple teams, owners would be competing with each over the same fan base.  The economics of the game began to change with the increase in live media coverage, especially television.  The owners had a plan, and that was to have one team in each city, so that owners would not need to fight each other over customers.  Boston would lose the Braves.  Philadelphia would lose the A\'s.  St. Louis would lose the Browns.  New York would lose the Giants and the Dodgers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Boston, despite the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Boston_Braves" title="History of the Boston Braves">Boston Braves</a> having been established much longer in the city and arguably being the oldest continuing <a href="/wiki/Professional_sports" title="Professional sports">professional sports</a> franchise in North America, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> were by far the more popular team at the gate and with fans. In addition, the Red Sox were largely successful on the field (except in the immediate years after selling <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> to the Yankees) while the Braves were an also-ran and often in the <a href="/wiki/Second_division_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Second division (baseball)">second division</a> of the National League. In stark contrast, the National League was able to hang on to St. Louis, where the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> were more successful than the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_St._Louis_Browns" title="History of the St. Louis Browns">St. Louis Browns</a>.\n</p><p>One oddity would be Philadelphia, where the American League\'s <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philadelphia_Athletics" title="History of the Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a> were by far the more popular team in the city, led by longtime manager <a href="/wiki/Connie_Mack" title="Connie Mack">Connie Mack</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> were mostly losing during this period. However, with the Phillies enjoying rare success in 1950 at the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Whiz_Kids_(baseball)" title="Whiz Kids (baseball)">Whiz Kids</a>, the tables instantly turned on the Athletics. Additionally, a "<a href="/wiki/Spite_fence" title="Spite fence">spite fence</a>" built at <a href="/wiki/Shibe_Park" title="Shibe Park">Shibe Park</a> in 1935 (to keep spectators outside of the ballpark from watching the game for free), had the unintended result of the Athletics alienating their fan base in Philadelphia.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Still, the Phillies would not win a <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> until 1980, the last of the "original 16" teams to win a series and 25 years after the A\'s left town, during which they would win three more World Series championships before the Phillies broke through.\n</p><p>In New York City, the Yankees had long passed the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> in popularity, though the Giants remained popular and the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="History of the Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> were gaining popularity as well.\n</p><p>Chicago would see the Cubs as roughly equal in popularity to the White Sox despite their constant losing seasons and the <a href="/wiki/Curse_of_the_Billy_Goat" title="Curse of the Billy Goat">Curse of the Billy Goat</a> allegedly affecting the Cubs from making the Fall Classic from 1945 to 2016. This can likely be attributed to the after effects of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sox_Scandal" title="Black Sox Scandal">Black Sox Scandal</a> of 1919 still apparent on the White Sox, who on several occasions nearly moved out of the Windy City. However the White Sox have not threatened, nor been considered a candidate for relocation, since the opening of new Comiskey Park (now <a href="/wiki/Guaranteed_Rate_Field" title="Guaranteed Rate Field">Guaranteed Rate Field</a>) in 1991, with their 2005 World Series victory further cementing their future in Chicago.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_moves">The moves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The moves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first moves were initially lateral moves. As they were tied down to slow railroad timetables, the first teams that moved stayed within Major League Baseball historical geographical core: the Northeast and upper Midwest, and to markets which did not have Major League Baseball.\n</p><p>The first move was the Boston Braves, who relocated (for 1953) to <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>, home of their top farm team, the Milwaukee Brewers. The City of Milwaukee fell in love with the Braves, with fan support of the team high, making the move highly profitable. The Milwaukee Braves would remain popular until the team relocated to Atlanta in 1966.\n</p><p>Other owners took notice and began their relocation threats as well. The St. Louis Browns moved to <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> for 1954, becoming the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> (this was the sole west-to-east move during the era, as all of the other relocating teams moved from east to west). The Philadelphia Athletics moved to <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a> for 1955, briefly displacing the Cardinals as the westernmost town in the majors. Save for some controversy with the Athletics, these moves were not controversial, as these were three of the least successful teams in the majors, although the Browns and Braves had both won league championships in the 1940s.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_exit_of_the_National_League_from_New_York_City">The exit of the National League from New York City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The exit of the National League from New York City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg/220px-Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg" width="220" height="106" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg/330px-Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg/440px-Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="579" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dodger-Stadium-Panorama-052707.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dodger Stadium in 2007</div></div></div>\n<p>Baseball experts consider <a href="/wiki/Walter_O%27Malley" title="Walter O&#39;Malley">Walter O\'Malley</a> to be "perhaps the most influential owner of baseball\'s early expansion era".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/1957_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1957 Major League Baseball season">1957 Major League Baseball season</a>, he moved the Dodgers to Los Angeles.<sup id="cite_ref-FYOL_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FYOL-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> For years O\'Malley had tried to secure a site for a new stadium for the Dodgers in Brooklyn to no avail. The Dodgers\' home at the time, <a href="/wiki/Ebbets_Field" title="Ebbets Field">Ebbets Field</a>, was old and obsolete. Despite the Dodgers dominating the league, attendance at Ebbets Field dwindled, likely because of its age and the difficulty of accessing the stadium by car. Another explanation for the decline in attendance is the migration to the suburbs. People living in the suburbs did not want to attend games at night in the inner city.<sup id="cite_ref-chu_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chu-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Frustrated, he began to look elsewhere.\n</p><p>With the post\xe2\x80\x93World War II population shifts south and west and the rise in transcontinental airplane service, many West Coast cities were actively pursuing Major League Baseball to relocate there; among them Los Angeles and San Francisco. <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Wyman" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosalind Wyman">Rosalind Wyman</a>, the then-head of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Chamber_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles Chamber of Commerce">Los Angeles Chamber of Commerce</a>, was one of those championing a major league team in Los Angeles. Beginning in 1955, with the city\'s blessing, she began to solicit several major league teams, including the Dodgers, with the idea that they move to Los Angeles. Wyman and city officials attended the <a href="/wiki/1956_World_Series" title="1956 World Series">1956 World Series</a> between the Dodgers and the Yankees, at Ebbets Field, initially to meet with <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="History of the Washington Senators (1901\xe2\x80\x9360)">Washington Senators</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Griffith" title="Calvin Griffith">Calvin Griffith</a> who was attending the game, to convince Griifith to move his ailing Senators to Los Angeles. O\'Malley, upon hearing of the planned meeting, immediately summoned Wyman for a meeting and earnestly began negotiations with her expressing his intent to have a new modern stadium built for the team, a request Wyman and city officials assured O\'Malley would be no problem. Initially O\'Malley planned to use his negotiations with Los Angeles as a ploy in his dealings with New York\'s all-powerful public building and development czar <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moses" title="Robert Moses">Robert Moses</a>, who was preventing O\'Malley from obtaining the site he was looking for in Brooklyn for his <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers_proposed_domed_stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers proposed domed stadium">new stadium</a>. However, as negotiations with Moses deteriorated \xe2\x80\x94 Moses wanted the Dodgers to move to a new stadium site in <a href="/wiki/Flushing_Meadows%E2%80%93Corona_Park" title="Flushing Meadows\xe2\x80\x93Corona Park">Flushing Meadows\xe2\x80\x93Corona Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>, which eventually became <a href="/wiki/Shea_Stadium" title="Shea Stadium">Shea Stadium</a>\xe2\x80\x94 O\'Malley realized that a deal with Los Angeles began to seem more of a reality. In addition, in Los Angeles, the Dodgers would be given land at <a href="/wiki/Chavez_Ravine" title="Chavez Ravine">Chavez Ravine</a> free of charge to build a stadium, which would eventually become <a href="/wiki/Dodger_Stadium" title="Dodger Stadium">Dodger Stadium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chu_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chu-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the same time <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Horace_Stoneham" title="Horace Stoneham">Horace Stoneham</a> was considering a move for the Giants. The Giants\' aging home, the <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a>, was becoming dilapidated by the mid-fifties, leading to a drop off in attendance for the team. Stoneham, like O\'Malley, was also unable to secure a new stadium site for the team and initially was looking to move the Giants to <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, home to the Giants\' top farm team, the <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis_Millers" title="Minneapolis Millers">Minneapolis Millers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> O\'Malley invited <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="San Francisco Mayor">San Francisco Mayor</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Christopher" title="George Christopher">George Christopher</a> to New York to meet with Stoneham and convinced him to join O\'Malley on the West Coast at the end of the 1957 season, thus bringing the two teams\' New York rivalry out to California. O\'Malley needed another team to go west with him, for had he moved out west alone, the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>\xe2\x80\x941,600&#160;mi (2,575&#160;km) away\xe2\x80\x94<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> would have been the closest National League team. The joint move would make West Coast road trips economical for visiting teams.<sup id="cite_ref-WiW_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WiW-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Since the meetings occurred during the 1957 season and against the wishes of <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_of_Baseball" title="Commissioner of Baseball">Commissioner of Baseball</a> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Frick" title="Ford Frick">Ford Frick</a>, there was media gamesmanship.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> When O\'Malley moved the Dodgers from Brooklyn, the story transcended the world of sport and he found himself on the cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cover_art" title="Cover art">cover art</a> for the issue was created by sports cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Willard_Mullin" title="Willard Mullin">Willard Mullin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-SC_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SC-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> long noted for his caricature of the "Brooklyn Bum" that personified the team.\n</p><p>The dual moves broke the hearts of New York\'s National League fans, but ultimately were successful for both franchises and for Major League Baseball.<sup id="cite_ref-FYOL_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FYOL-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The move was an immediate success as well, because the Dodgers set a major-league, single-game attendance record in their first home appearance, with 78,672&#160;fans at the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Memorial_Coliseum" title="Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum">Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum</a>, which would be their temporary home for several years until their new stadium was ready.<sup id="cite_ref-WiW_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WiW-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The moves received considerable controversy: the loss of the Dodgers was especially painful because the team was one of the last vestiges of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>\'s status as a <a href="/wiki/History_of_Brooklyn" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Brooklyn">city in its own right</a> prior to <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" class="mw-redirect" title="Borough (New York City)">1898</a> (professional baseball would not return to Brooklyn until 2001, in the form of the minor-league <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Cyclones" title="Brooklyn Cyclones">Brooklyn Cyclones</a> team playing at Coney Island, but Brooklyn would not receive another major league sports franchise until the relocation of the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Nets" title="Brooklyn Nets">New Jersey Nets</a> to the borough in 2012). Though New York still had the Yankees in the American League, the loss of a National League team in the nation\'s largest market drew the ire of National League fans.\n</p><p>New York City mayor <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Wagner" title="Robert F. Wagner">Robert F. Wagner</a> asked attorney William A. Shea to head up a committee to acquire a new National League team for New York.  After pitching to Philadelphia, Cincinnati, and Pittsburgh, Shea realized he was asking a team to do what he couldn\'t - that was move a team from a market that supported it.  Almost immediately the goal was switched from franchise relocation to league expansion.  Using contacts in government, he was able to force MLB (by lobbying for the end of MLB\'s Anti-Trust exemption) to add two American League teams and two National League teams.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> were eventually added as an <a href="/wiki/Expansion_team" title="Expansion team">expansion team</a>, beginning play in 1962. Ironically, the Yankees\' attendance actually declined slightly in the years immediately following the Dodgers\' and Giants\' departure, and the Mets consistently drew more fans than the Yankees through the early-to-mid 1970\'s. The Mets today are often considered "New York City\'s team" while the Yankees, due to their worldwide popularity, have a more global fanbase similar to that of teams in other sports like the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Cowboys" title="Dallas Cowboys">Dallas Cowboys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_moves">Subsequent moves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subsequent moves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Both the Braves and Athletics did not stay in their locations for very long. The Braves, despite success in Milwaukee, moved to <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> for 1966 while the Athletics set up shop in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a> for 1968. Both markets would eventually get replacement teams in the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a>, respectively. The Braves would not have consistent success in Atlanta until the 1990s, while the A\'s were successful during several periods thereafter.\n</p><p>Aside from the <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Pilots" title="Seattle Pilots">Pilots</a> moving to Milwaukee after one year (to become the Brewers), the other relocations since have involved <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D._C." class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, D. C.">Washington, D. C.</a> The original Washington Senators moved to the <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis%E2%80%93Saint_Paul" title="Minneapolis\xe2\x80\x93Saint Paul">Twin Cities</a> region to become the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins" title="Minnesota Twins">Minnesota Twins</a> (beginning play as such in 1961), and the expansion Senators that replaced them moved to <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Texas" title="Arlington, Texas">Arlington, Texas</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/Dallas-Fort_Worth_metroplex" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex">Dallas-Fort Worth</a> region) to become the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> (beginning play as such in 1972). Several relocation threats would be made by the Giants, White Sox, and Pirates (in the latter\'s case, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_drug_trials" title="Pittsburgh drug trials">Pittsburgh drug trials</a>); however, none would happen, and all three markets to which these teams threatened to move (<a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Area" title="Tampa Bay Area">Tampa Bay Area</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, respectively) would later receive <a href="/wiki/Expansion_team" title="Expansion team">expansion teams</a> of their own.\n</p><p>There were no more moves until 2005, when MLB moved the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a> to the American capital, where the Expos became the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a>.\n</p><p>Montreal is the only city which has lost a major league franchise since 1901 without eventually getting another team. This is not counting the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Federal_League" title="Federal League">Federal League</a> of 1914 and 1915; however, all Federal League markets save two\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>\xe2\x80\x94either had a franchise in one of the two established leagues at the time or got one later. As of 2018, MLB officials have expressed an interest in the return of baseball to Montreal, and the city is a leading candidate for a proposed expansion to 32 teams.<sup id="cite_ref-MG_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MG-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_factors">Economic factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economic factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most relocations during this time period did not involve a change in ownership. This means that the main economic incentive for the MLB to expand was not large offers from prospective buyers in western cities. Because the owners mainly retained control over their teams after relocation, there must be another economic benefit. The expansion occurred because franchise owners saw higher profit potential in the other location.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_factors">Internal factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Internal factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection_against_rival_leagues">Protection against rival leagues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Protection against rival leagues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Expansion of MLB to all cities capable of supporting a franchise in the sport is economically beneficial for the league as a whole. This reduces the possibility of the formation of a rival league, a problem that baseball dealt with in its early history. Monopoly control of the sport is essential to many aspects of player contracts, drafts and other parts of the game. Baseball\'s anti-trust exemption was important in the development and expansion of the league.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In MLB, all local TV revenue is assigned to the home team. The league has complex revenue sharing rules, but data shows that media revenue was an economic pull factor for the franchise relocations. The yearly gains in media revenue associated with the relocations are as follows: Boston to Milwaukee (\xe2\x88\x92$175,000),<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> St. Louis to Baltimore (+$257,000),<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Philadelphia to Kansas City (\xe2\x88\x92$90,000),<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Washington to Minnesota (+$400,000),<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Milwaukee to Atlanta (+$800,000),<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Kansas City to Oakland (+$800,000),<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Seattle to Milwaukee (\xe2\x88\x92$150,000),<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Washington to Texas (no change).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Data is unavailable for the relocation of the Dodgers and the Giants. Based on this data, TV revenues influenced the franchise move of Washington, Milwaukee, and Kansas City and perhaps St. Louis. TV revenues have played an important role in roughly half of the moves that have taken place. James Quirk argues that under a TV sharing arrangement similar to the National Football League, or even under a split with visiting teams, the TV lure would be much less of an incentive for moves.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1973_18-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1973-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External_factors">External factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: External factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The protection of the league from competition and economic benefits of media revenues are direct economic pull factors. Other advancements in the U.S. during this time also had an effect on the ability and desire of teams to relocate and the league to expand. The expansion of MLB coincided with an increase in the ease of travel by commercial jets, making it easier for players to fly across the continent. This is important given that when Walter O\'Malley moved his Dodgers to Los Angeles, the closest team, other than the San Francisco Giants, was in St. Louis.\n</p><p>The expansion of baseball is also accompanied by the increase in popularity of television. Baseball expanded, in large part, because interest in the sport grew leading up to and during the 1950s and 1960s. In home televisions allowed for this increase in popularity and helped make New York\'s pastime, America\'s pastime.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Major_League_Baseball_relocation_of_1950s%E2%80%9360s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From 1903 to 1952, a period of 50 years, no major league baseball team moved to a different city.  From 1953 to 1972, a period of 20 years, there were ten relocations.  In the <span class="currentage"></span>46 years since then, only one team has moved.  (The Montreal Expos moved to Washington and became the Nationals in 2005.)\n</p>\n<ul><li>1953: Boston Braves move to Milwaukee</li>\n<li>1954: St. Louis Browns move to Baltimore and become the Orioles</li>\n<li>1955: Philadelphia Athletics move to Kansas City</li>\n<li>1958: New York Giants move to San Francisco; Brooklyn Dodgers move to Los Angeles</li>\n<li>1961: Washington Senators move to Minneapolis\xe2\x80\x93Saint Paul and become the Minnesota Twins; new Washington Senators (replacing the original Senators) and Los Angeles Angels created as expansion teams</li>\n<li>1962: Houston Colt .45s (later renamed the Astros) and New York Mets (replacing the Dodgers and Giants) created as expansion teams</li>\n<li>1966: Milwaukee Braves move to Atlanta</li>\n<li>1968: Kansas City Athletics move to Oakland</li>\n<li>1969: San Diego Padres, Montreal Expos, Kansas City Royals (replacing the Athletics), and Seattle Pilots created as expansion teams</li>\n<li>1970: Seattle Pilots move to Milwaukee and become the Brewers (replacing the Braves)</li>\n<li>1972: Washington Senators move to Dallas\xe2\x80\x93Fort Worth and become the Texas Rangers</li>\n<li>1977: Seattle Mariners (replacing the Pilots) and Toronto Blue Jays created as expansion teams</li>\n<li>1993: Colorado Rockies and Florida Marlins (later renamed Miami Marlins) created as expansion teams</li>\n<li>1998: Arizona Diamondbacks and Tampa Bay Devil Rays (later renamed the Rays) created as expansion teams</li>\n<li>2005: Montreal Expos move to Washington D.C. and become the Washington Nationals (replacing
