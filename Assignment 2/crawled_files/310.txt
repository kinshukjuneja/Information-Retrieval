href="/wiki/Tombolo" title="Tombolo">tombolo</a>, a kind of <a href="/wiki/Sandbar" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandbar">sandbar</a>, called the Gugh Bar, which is exposed only at low tide. The Gugh is inhabited, with some three residents. The two islands of St Agnes and Gugh together have a population of 85 residents recorded in the 2011 census (73 were recorded in the 2001 census) and a landmass of 366 acres (148&#160;ha). Without the Gugh included, St Agnes is marginally smaller than <a href="/wiki/Bryher,_Isles_of_Scilly" class="mw-redirect" title="Bryher, Isles of Scilly">Bryher</a> in either population or area; however if Gugh is included with St Agnes, it is Bryher that is marginally smaller in area and population.\n</p><p>In earlier times many men from St Agnes earned a living as <a href="/wiki/Harbour_pilot" class="mw-redirect" title="Harbour pilot">pilots</a>, guiding transatlantic liners and other vessels through the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>. Now the mainstay of the economy is tourism, together with some <a href="/wiki/Bulb" title="Bulb">bulb</a> farming. Accommodation is limited, and St Agnes is the only populated island in the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> which has no hotel. However, it has a few <a href="/wiki/Bed_and_breakfast" title="Bed and breakfast">B&amp;Bs</a> and self-catering cottages, an ice cream shop, a campsite, a small post office and general store and a gift shop. It also has a pub (the Turk\'s Head) and a cafe, although these are closed in the winter.\n</p><p>The main population centre is in the north and middle of the island. The southern end of the island is covered by the <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a> <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wingletang_Down" class="mw-redirect" title="Wingletang Down">Wingletang Down</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighthouse">Lighthouse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lighthouse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The island\'s most notable landmark is its <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a>, which has been converted into living accommodation and the tower no longer contains a light. In 1680 <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a> began a survey of the coasts of England as it was known that the contemporary charts were inaccurate; the Isles of Scilly was plotted ten miles to the north. Trinity House was also given permission to erect and maintain one or more lighthouses on the islands. St Agnes was chosen as it is the most westerly of the inhabitable islands and close to the collection of rocks, tidal flows and currents, now known as the <a href="/wiki/Western_Rocks,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Western Rocks, Isles of Scilly">Western Rocks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vyvyan_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vyvyan-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It was built in 1680 and was coal fired until 1790, when it was converted to oil, with copper lamps and twenty-one revolving reflectors. A plaque records the original construction by Captains Hugh Hill and Simon Bayly, builders of the 1676 <a href="/wiki/Lowestoft#Lighthouse" title="Lowestoft">Lowestoft lighthouse</a>. There were two protests against the building of the St Agnes light. Officials from the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> complained that they would lose revenue from harbour dues and victualling as shipping would prefer to use the Isles of Scilly, and the Governor of Scilly on the grounds that he would lose money from wrecks.<sup id="cite_ref-vyvyan_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vyvyan-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It stands 74\' above the ground, and 138\' above mean high water. It was superseded in 1911 by the Peninnis Lighthouse and St Agnes lighthouse now serves as a <a href="/wiki/Daymark" title="Daymark">daymark</a> for shipping.\nPeninnis lighthouse is a 17-metre-tall black and white steel lattice tower situated on the southern extremity of St. Mary\'s island. The range was reduced from 17Nm to 9Nm under the Trinity House 2010 Aids to Navigation review. The light source employed is now LED.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_landmarks">Other landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Other landmarks include a <a href="/wiki/Standing_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing stone">standing stone</a> known as the Nag\'s Head (probably a natural formation). In 1707, many of the sailors who had drowned in the <a href="/wiki/Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707" title="Scilly naval disaster of 1707">great naval disaster off the Isles of Scilly</a> were reputedly buried on the St Agnes playing field.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Troytown maze</dt></dl>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Troy_Town" title="Troy Town">Troytown</a> Maze is said to have been laid out by the son of the lighthouse keeper in 1729, but may be much older. Although called a maze it is strictly a <a href="/wiki/Labyrinth" title="Labyrinth">labyrinth</a> with a convoluted path to the centre via seven rings. It is the only one outside Scandinavia made of beach pebbles, which may indicate it to be of Viking origin. References in the <a href="/wiki/Norse_sagas" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse sagas">Norse sagas</a> tell of raiders coming to Scilly as late as the mid-12th century. Any buried evidence of its origins may have been destroyed during an unofficial rebuild in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church">Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>See <a href="/wiki/St_Agnes%27_Church,_St_Agnes" title="St Agnes&#39; Church, St Agnes">St Agnes\' Church, St Agnes</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sporting_and_social_life">Sporting and social life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sporting and social life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Friday evenings in the summer (end of April until start of October) see men\'s domestic <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Pilot_Gig" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Pilot Gig">Cornish Pilot Gig</a> racing on Scilly, with the ladies\' race on Wednesday. After the race, supporters fill the Turk\'s Head to discuss the race and to socialise. The pub is open through the summer, but during the winter it only opens on Wednesdays for a quiz, and one other night.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_residents">Notable residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Periglis Cottage was the home of St Agnes\'s resident ornithologist Hilda M. In the south of the island <a href="/wiki/Wingletang_Down_(St._Agnes)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wingletang Down (St. Agnes)">Wingletang Down</a> is noted for its heath, dune grassland and rocky coast and is the only site in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">fern</a> <a href="/wiki/Ophioglossum_lusitanicum" title="Ophioglossum lusitanicum">least adder\'s\xe2\x80\x93tongue</a> (<i>Ophioglossum lusitanicum</i>) grows. Other rare plants include the <a href="/wiki/Orchid" class="mw-redirect" title="Orchid">orchid</a> autumn lady\xe2\x80\x99s-tresses (<i><a href="/wiki/Spiranthes_spiralis" title="Spiranthes spiralis">Spiranthes spiralis</a></i>) and early meadow-grass (<i><a href="/wiki/Poa_infirma" title="Poa infirma">Poa infirma</a></i>). The only freshwater pools on St Agnes are Big Pool and Little Pool in the north-west of the island which are part of the <b>Big Pool and Browarth Point</b> SSSI. Big Pool shows evidence for inundation<sup id="cite_ref-Banerjee_et_al._2001._6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Banerjee_et_al._2001.-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> by the 1775 Lisbon tsunami that was caused by the <a href="/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake" title="1755 Lisbon earthquake">1755 Lisbon earthquake</a>. The vegetation has some <a href="/wiki/Brackish" class="mw-redirect" title="Brackish">brackish</a> influence because of occasional influx of the sea during winter storms with rushes such as saltmarsh rush (<i><a href="/wiki/Juncus_gerardi" class="mw-redirect" title="Juncus gerardi">Juncus gerardi</a></i>) and sea club\xe2\x80\x93rush (<i><a href="/wiki/Scirpus_maritimus" class="mw-redirect" title="Scirpus maritimus">Scirpus maritimus</a></i>). The surrounding grassland, which is also a cricket pitch, is notable for the clovers amongst its flora including western clover (<i><a href="/wiki/Trifolium_occidentale" title="Trifolium occidentale">Trifolium occidentale</a></i>), suffocated clover (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trifolium_suffocatum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trifolium suffocatum (page does not exist)">T. suffocatum</a></i>) and subterranean clover (<i><a href="/wiki/Trifolium_subterraneum" title="Trifolium subterraneum">T. subterraneum</a></i>). Other plants include adder\xe2\x80\x99s-tongue (<i><a href="/wiki/Ophioglossum_vulgatum" title="Ophioglossum vulgatum">Ophioglossum vulgatum</a></i>) and small adder\xe2\x80\x99s-tongue (<i><a href="/wiki/Ophioglossum_azoricum" title="Ophioglossum azoricum">O. azoricum</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vagrant_birds">Vagrant birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vagrant birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>St Agnes is visited by <a href="/wiki/Birdwatcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Birdwatcher">birdwatchers</a>, particularly during the \xe2\x80\xb3Scilly season\xe2\x80\xb3 of September and October.
