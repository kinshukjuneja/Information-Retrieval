class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Orkneyinga saga</i></h1>\t\t\t<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">\n\n\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flatey_Book,_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Flatey_Book%2C_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg/220px-Flatey_Book%2C_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg" width="220" height="368" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Flatey_Book%2C_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg/330px-Flatey_Book%2C_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Flatey_Book%2C_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg/440px-Flatey_Book%2C_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1604" data-file-height="2685" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flatey_Book,_Orkneyinga_saga.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of a page from the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i>, as it appears in the 14th century <i><a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k">Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k</a></i>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i><b>Orkneyinga saga</b></i> (also called the <i><b>History of the Earls of Orkney</b></i> and <i><b>Jarls\' Saga</b></i>) is a historical narrative of the history of the <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> islands and their relationship with other local polities, particularly <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The saga has "no parallel in the social and literary record of Scotland"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford1987221_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford1987221-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and is "the only medieval chronicle to have <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> as the central place of action".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The main focus of the work is the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Orkney" title="Earl of Orkney">line of <i>jarls</i></a> who ruled the <a href="/wiki/Earldom_of_Orkney" title="Earldom of Orkney">Earldom of Orkney</a>, which constituted the <i>Nor\xc3\xb0reyjar</i> or <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">Northern Isles</a> of both Orkney and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> and there are frequent references to both archipelagoes throughout.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith198821_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith198821-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The narrative commences with a brief mythical ancestry tale and then proceeds to outline the Norse take-over of the <i>Nor\xc3\xb0reyjar</i> by <a href="/wiki/Harald_Fairhair" title="Harald Fairhair">Harald Fairhair</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the former event is not in doubt although the role of the latter King of Norway is no longer accepted by historians as a likelihood. The saga then outlines, with varying degrees of detail, the lives and times of the many <i>jarls</i> who ruled the islands between the 9th and 13th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The extent to which the earlier sections in particular can be considered genuine history rather than fiction have been much debated by scholars.\n</p><p>There are several recurring themes in the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i>, including strife between brothers, relationships between the jarls and the Norwegian crown, and raiding in the <i>Su\xc3\xb0reyjar</i> \xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198115\xe2\x80\x9316Introduction_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198115\xe2\x80\x9316Introduction-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In part, the saga\'s purpose was to provide a history of the islands and enable its readers to "understand themselves through a knowledge of their origins"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198119Introduction_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198119Introduction-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but even where its historical veracity is lacking it provides modern scholars with insights into the motives of the writers and the politics of 13th century Orkney.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_versions">Development and versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development and versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg/220px-Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg/330px-Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg/440px-Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2960" data-file-height="2020" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pg307_Mount_Hekla_from_Odde.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An early 19th century illustration of "<a href="/wiki/Hekla" title="Hekla">Mount Hekla</a> from <a href="/wiki/Oddi" title="Oddi">Odde</a>"</div></div></div>\n<p>This <a href="/wiki/Norse_saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse saga">Norse saga</a> was written around in the early thirteenth century (three centuries after some of the earliest events they record) by an unknown Icelandic author who was probably associated with the cultural centre at <a href="/wiki/Oddi" title="Oddi">Oddi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> belongs to the genre of "Kings\xe2\x80\x99 Sagas" within Icelandic saga literature, a group of histories of the kings of Norway, the best known of which is <i><a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Indeed Sturluson used <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> as one of his sources for <i>Heimskringla</i><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which was compiled around 1230 (and then <i>Heimskringla</i> became in turn a source for later versions of the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As was generally the case with <a href="/wiki/Old_Icelandic" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Icelandic">Icelandic language</a> writing of this period, the aims of the saga were to provide a sense of social continuity through the telling of history combined with an entertaining narrative drive.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The tales are thought to have been compiled from a number of sources, combining family pedigrees, praise poetry and oral <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a> with historical facts. In the case of the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> the document outlines the lives of the earls of Orkney and how they came about their earldom.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Woolf (2007) suggests that the task that the Icelandic compiler was faced with was not dissimilar to trying to write a "history of the Second World War on the basis of Hollywood movies".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He also notes that a problem with medieval Icelandic historiography in general is the difficulty of fixing of a clear chronology based on stories created in a largely illiterate society in which "AD dating was probably unknown" at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007277-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As the narrative approaches the period closer to the time it was written down, historians have greater confidence in its accuracy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> For example, there are significant family connections between Snorri Sturluson and Earl <a href="/wiki/Harald_Maddadsson" title="Harald Maddadsson">Harald Maddadsson</a> (d. 1206) and the original saga document was probably written down at about the time of Harald\'s death.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Vigassun (1887) identifies several different components to the saga, which may have had different authors and date from different periods.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVigassun1887ix\xe2\x80\x93xPreface_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVigassun1887ix\xe2\x80\x93xPreface-12">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> These are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Fundinn Noregr</i> chapters 1\xe2\x80\x933</li>\n<li><i>Iarla Sogur</i> chapters 4\xe2\x80\x9338 (of which the sections regarding Earl Einarr are the oldest texts in the saga as a whole<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVigassun1887xiiPreface_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVigassun1887xiiPreface-13">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>)</li>\n<li><i>St Magnus saga</i> chapters 39\xe2\x80\x9355</li>\n<li><i>Iarteina-bok</i> ("Miracle book") chapter 60</li>\n<li><i>The History of Earl Rognwald and Swain Asleifsson</i> chapters 56\xe2\x80\x9359 and 61\xe2\x80\x93118.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>A Danish translation dating to 1570 indicates that the original version of the saga ended with the death of <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Asleifsson" title="Sweyn Asleifsson">Sweyn Asleifsson</a>, who was killed fighting in Dublin in 1171. Various additions were then added circa 1234-5 when a grandson of Asleifsson and a lawmaker called Hrafn visited Iceland.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The oldest complete text is found in the late 14th century <i><a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k">Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJesch1992337_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJesch1992337-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but the first translation into <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> did not appear until 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrative">Narrative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Narrative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first three chapters of the saga are a brief folk legend that sets the scene for later events. It commences with characters associated with the <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">elements</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Snaer" class="mw-redirect" title="Snaer">Snaer</a> (snow), Logi (flame), Kari (storm) and Frosti (frost) and also gives a unique explanation for how Norway came to be named as such, involving Snaer\'s grandson <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B3r" title="N\xc3\xb3r">N\xc3\xb3r</a>. There is also a reference to claiming land by dragging a boat over a neck of land (reprised in chapter 41 when <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Barefoot" title="Magnus Barefoot">Magnus Barefoot</a> uses the same trick) and the division of the land between N\xc3\xb3r and his brother G\xc3\xb3r, which is a recurring theme in the saga.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198112Introduction_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198112Introduction-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This legend also gives the Orkney <i>jarls</i> an origin involving a <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunn" title="J\xc3\xb6tunn">giant</a> and king called <a href="/wiki/Fornj%C3%B3t" title="Fornj\xc3\xb3t">Fornj\xc3\xb3t</a> who lived in the far north. This clearly distinguishes them from the Norwegian kings as described in the <i><a href="/wiki/Ynglingatal" title="Ynglingatal">Ynglingatal</a></i> and may have been intended to give the <i>jarls</i> a more senior and more <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic</a> ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford201380\xe2\x80\x9383_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford201380\xe2\x80\x9383-19">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Having dealt with the mythical ancestry of the earls, the saga then moves on to topics that are apparently intended as genuine history.<sup id="cite_ref-OJsaga_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OJsaga-20">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harald_Fairhair_and_the_voyage_to_the_west">Harald Fairhair and the voyage to the west</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Harald Fairhair and the voyage to the west">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Black and white drawing of a snapshot showing shipmasts with flags and warriors marching below." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg/170px-Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg" width="170" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg/255px-Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg/340px-Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="760" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kong-magnus-berrfott-menn.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Magnus_Barefoot" title="Magnus Barefoot">Magnus Barefoot</a>\'s army in Ireland. Magnus\'s actions in the west clearly form the basis of the saga narrative about the submission of <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> to Harald Fairhair\'s fleet.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200827_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200827-21">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The next few chapters deal with the creation of the Earldom of Orkney; they are brief and contain much less detail than the later events the saga describes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198111Introduction-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The saga states that <a href="/wiki/Rognvald_Eysteinsson" title="Rognvald Eysteinsson">Rognvald Eysteinsson</a> was made the Earl of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B8re_og_Romsdal" title="M\xc3\xb8re og Romsdal">M\xc3\xb8re</a> by the King of Norway, Harald Fairhair. Rognvald accompanied the king on a great military expedition. First the islands of Shetland and Orkney were cleared of <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">vikings</a> who had been raiding Norway and then they continued on to Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>. During this campaign Rognvald\'s son Ivarr was killed and in compensation Harald granted Rognvald Orkney and Shetland. Rognvald thereafter returned to <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, giving these islands to his brother <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Eysteinsson" title="Sigurd Eysteinsson">Sigurd Eysteinsson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson1990332\xe2\x80\x93334&#39;&#39;Saga_of_Harald_Fairhair&#39;&#39;_c._22:_&quot;King_Harald&#39;s_Voyage_To_The_West&quot;_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson1990332\xe2\x80\x93334&#39;&#39;Saga_of_Harald_Fairhair&#39;&#39;_c._22:_&quot;King_Harald&#39;s_Voyage_To_The_West&quot;-22">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819c._4_&quot;To_Shetland_and_Orkney&quot;_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819c._4_&quot;To_Shetland_and_Orkney&quot;-23">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Sigurd had been the <a href="/wiki/Forecastle" title="Forecastle">forecastleman</a> on Harald\'s ship and after sailing back east the king "gave Sigurd the title of earl".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819c._4_&quot;To_Shetland_and_Orkney&quot;_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819c._4_&quot;To_Shetland_and_Orkney&quot;-23">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sigurd "the Mighty" then died in a curious fashion, following a battle with <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1el_Brigte_of_Moray" title="M\xc3\xa1el Brigte of Moray">M\xc3\xa1el Brigte of Moray</a>. Sigurd\'s son Gurthorm ruled for a single winter after this and died childless.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200828_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200828-24">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819c._5_&quot;A_poisoned_tooth&quot;_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819c._5_&quot;A_poisoned_tooth&quot;-25">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Rognvald\'s son Hallad then inherited the title. However, unable to constrain Danish raids on Orkney, he gave up the earldom and returned to Norway, which "everyone thought was a huge joke."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200830_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200830-26">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>First written down in the early 13th century, the saga is informed by the Norwegian politics of the day. Once, historians could write that no-one denied the reality of Harald Fairhair\'s expeditions to the west (recounted in detail in the <i><a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla</a></i>), but this is no longer the case. Thomson (2008) writes that Harald\'s "great voyage is so thoroughly ingrained in popular and scholarly history, both ancient and modern, that it comes as a bit of a shock to realise that it might not be true."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200825_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200825-27">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Norwegian contest with the <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Scots" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Scots">Kings of Scots</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> and the Isle of Man in the mid 13th century is the backdrop to the saga writer\'s intentions<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198752\xe2\x80\x9353_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198752\xe2\x80\x9353-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and in part at least the sagas aim to legitimise Norwegian claims to both the Northern Isles and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Isles" title="Kingdom of the Isles">Kingdom of the Isles</a> in the west.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200827_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200827-21">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It may be that the saga writers drew on a genuine tradition of a voyage by Harald to the west, or that they simply invented it wholesale for political purposes, but it is clear that there are elements included in the narrative that are drawn from the much later expeditions undertaken by Magnus Barefoot.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200827_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200827-21">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The situation faced by <a href="/wiki/Harald_Maddadsson" title="Harald Maddadsson">Earl Harald Maddadsson</a> of Orkney in 1195, shortly before the time that the sagas were first written down, when he was forced to submit himself to royal authority after an ill-judged intervention in Norwegian affairs would have made legendary material of this nature of considerable interest in Orkney at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200827\xe2\x80\x9328_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200827\xe2\x80\x9328-29">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  Nonetheless, the view that the Orkney earldom was created by "members of the M\xc3\xb8re family" continues to receive academic support.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelle200622_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelle200622-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Torf-Einarr">Torf-Einarr</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Torf-Einarr">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Hallad\'s failure led to Rognvald flying into a rage and summoning his sons Thorir and Hrollaug. He asked which of them wanted the islands but Thorir said the decision was up to the earl himself. Rognvald predicted that Thorir\'s path would keep him in Norway and that Hrollaug was destined seek his fortune in Iceland. <a href="/wiki/Torf-Einarr" title="Torf-Einarr">Einarr</a>, the youngest of his natural sons, then came forward and offered to go to the islands. Rognvald said: "Considering the kind of mother you have, slave-born on each side of her family, you are not likely to make much of a ruler. But I agree, the sooner you leave and the later you return the happier I\'ll be."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._6_&quot;Forecasts&quot;_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._6_&quot;Forecasts&quot;-31">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Despite his father\'s misgivings, on arrival in the Scottish islands, Einarr fought and defeated two Danish warlords who had taken residence there. Einarr then established himself as earl<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESturluson1992&#39;&#39;Harald_Harfager&#39;s_Saga&#39;&#39;:_c._27_&quot;Of_Earl_Torfeinar&#39;s_Obtaining_Orkney&quot;_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESturluson1992&#39;&#39;Harald_Harfager&#39;s_Saga&#39;&#39;:_c._27_&quot;Of_Earl_Torfeinar&#39;s_Obtaining_Orkney&quot;-32">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._6_&quot;Forecasts&quot;_and_c._7_&quot;Vikings_and_peat&quot;_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._6_&quot;Forecasts&quot;_and_c._7_&quot;Vikings_and_peat&quot;-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200873_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200873-34">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and founded a dynasty which retained control of the islands for centuries after his death.\n</p><p>The scene in which Einarr\'s father scorns him is a literary device which often figures in <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_literature" title="Old Norse literature">Old Norse literature</a>. The dialogue between the father and his sons has been interpreted as being about Rognvald\'s desire to cement his own position as Earl of M\xc3\xb8re and an allusion to the early history of Iceland, where the saga was written. Thorir is  a compliant son who Rognvald is happy to keep at home. Hrolluag is portrayed as a man of peace who will go to Iceland.  Einarr is aggressive and a threat to his father\'s position so can be spared for the dangers of Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198113Introduction_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198113Introduction-35">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> (In the <i><a href="/wiki/Landn%C3%A1mab%C3%B3k" title="Landn\xc3\xa1mab\xc3\xb3k">Landn\xc3\xa1mab\xc3\xb3k</a></i> version the equally aggressive brother <a href="/wiki/Rollo" title="Rollo">Hrolfr</a> is also present, and his destiny is anticipated to be in conveniently far-away <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>.)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198114Introduction_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198114Introduction-36">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg/220px-Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg/330px-Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg/440px-Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1734" data-file-height="1130" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Odin_rides_to_Hel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Odin Rides to Hel" (1908) by <a href="/wiki/W._G._Collingwood" title="W. G. Collingwood">W. G. Collingwood</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Einarr is also provided with various characteristics associated with <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>. Both have but one eye and the death of an opponent at Einarr\'s hands is offered to the god\xe2\x80\x94an act that contains a hint of Odin\'s own sacrifice to himself in the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A1vam%C3%A1l" title="H\xc3\xa1vam\xc3\xa1l">H\xc3\xa1vam\xc3\xa1l</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200836\xe2\x80\x9336_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200836\xe2\x80\x9336-39">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Einarr is a man of action who is self-made, and he is a successful warrior who (unlike his brothers) avenges his father\'s death. He leads a dramatic and memomorable life and emerges as "ancient, powerful and mysterious\xe2\x80\x94but as a literary figure rather than a real person".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200838_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200838-40">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> He is also a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/heathen" class="extiw" title="wikt:heathen">heathen</a> whose appearance at the commencement of the saga contrasts with the later martyrdom of his descendent <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Erlendsson,_Earl_of_Orkney" title="Magnus Erlendsson, Earl of Orkney">St Magnus</a> which event marks a "moral high-point" of the story.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200838_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200838-40">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sigurd_the_Stout">Sigurd the Stout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sigurd the Stout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The death of Earl <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Hlodvirsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sigurd Hlodvirsson">Sigurd Hlodvirsson</a> (980\xe2\x80\x931014) is the earliest known contemporary reference to the earldom of Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007300_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007300-41">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The 12th-century Irish source, the <i><a href="/wiki/Cogadh_Gaedhil_re_Gallaibh" class="mw-redirect" title="Cogadh Gaedhil re Gallaibh">Cogadh Gaedhil re Gallaibh</a></i>, records the events of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Clontarf" title="Battle of Clontarf">Battle of Clontarf</a> in 1014. The "foreigners and <a href="/wiki/Leinster" title="Leinster">Leinstermen</a>" were led by <a href="/wiki/Brodir_and_Ospak_of_Man" title="Brodir and Ospak of Man">Brodir</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and Sigurd, and the battle lasted all day, but the Irishmen ultimately drove back their enemies into the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._12_&quot;Death_in_Iceland&quot;_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._12_&quot;Death_in_Iceland&quot;-42">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198780_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198780-43">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> His death is corroborated by the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Ulster" title="Annals of Ulster">Annals of Ulster</a></i>, which record that amongst the dead was "Siuchraid son of Loduir, iarla Innsi Orcc" (i.e. of Sigurd, son of Hlodvir, Earl of Orkney).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007243_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007243-44">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_nicolai_arbo,_olaf_tryggvasson_king.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A group of warriors in medieval garb surround two men whose postures suggest they are about to embrace. The King summoned <i>jarl</i> Sigurd and said "I order you and all your subjects to be baptised. If you refuse, I\'ll have you killed on the spot and I swear I will ravage every island with fire and steel." Unsurprisingly, Sigurd agreed and the islands became Christian at a stroke.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson2008_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson2008-46">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> However, when the sagas were written down Orkney had been Christian for 200 years or more<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200866\xe2\x80\x9367_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200866\xe2\x80\x9367-47">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and the conversion tale itself has been described as "blatantly unhistorical".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011143\xe2\x80\x9344_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011143\xe2\x80\x9344-48">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> When the Norse arrived in the Northern Isles they would have found organised Christianity already thriving there, although there is no mention of this at all in the sagas.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011143\xe2\x80\x9344_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011143\xe2\x80\x9344-48">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earl_Thorfinn">Earl Thorfinn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Earl Thorfinn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Virtually nothing about <a href="/wiki/Thorfinn_Sigurdsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Thorfinn Sigurdsson">Thorfinn Sigurdsson</a>\'s life is corroborated from contemporary sources and the saga writer seems to have obtained most of his material from the poem <i>\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3rfinnsdr\xc3\xa1pa</i>, which was written by his court poet Arn\xc3\xb3r immediately following his death.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007243_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007243-44">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Orkneyinga Saga</i> says that a dispute between Thorfinn and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Hundason" title="Karl Hundason">Karl Hundason</a> began when the latter became "King of Scots" and claimed <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>, his forces successfully moving north and basing themselves in <a href="/wiki/Thurso" title="Thurso">Thurso</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198772_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198772-49">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In the war which followed, Thorfinn defeated Karl in a sea-battle off Deerness at the east end of the <a href="/wiki/Mainland,_Orkney" title="Mainland, Orkney">Orkney Mainland</a>. Then Karl\'s nephew Mutatan or Muddan, appointed to rule Caithness for him, was killed in Caithness by Thorkel Fosterer. Finally, a great battle at "Torfness" (probably <a href="/wiki/Tarbat_Ness" title="Tarbat Ness">Tarbat Ness</a> on the south side of the <a href="/wiki/Dornoch_Firth" title="Dornoch Firth">Dornoch Firth</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._20_&quot;Karl_Hundason&quot;_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._20_&quot;Karl_Hundason&quot;-50">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>) ended with Karl either being killed or forced to flee. Thorfinn, the saga says, then marched south through Scotland as far as <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a>, burning and plundering as he passed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._20_&quot;Karl_Hundason&quot;_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._20_&quot;Karl_Hundason&quot;-50">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> As a result of his military exploits Thorfinn became <a href="/wiki/Mormaer_of_Caithness" title="Mormaer of Caithness">Mormaer of Caithness</a> and during the first twenty years of his long earldom he was often based there.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith198827\xe2\x80\x9328_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith198827\xe2\x80\x9328-51">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> He was thus a vassal of both the King of Norway and the King of Scots, a status and title that was held by many of his successors.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;e&#93;</a></sup> Thorfinn also became <a href="/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_the_Kingdom_of_the_Isles" title="List of rulers of the Kingdom of the Isles">King of the Isles</a> from 1035, which title was only held amongst the earls of Orkney by Sigurd Eysteinsson before him, and after him possibly by <a href="/wiki/Einar_Sigurdsson" title="Einar Sigurdsson">Einar Sigurdsson</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200873_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200873-34">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and for a brief period by <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_the_Crusader" title="Sigurd the Crusader">Sigurd Magnusson</a> as the under-age son of Magnus Barefoot.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGregory18814\xe2\x80\x936_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory18814\xe2\x80\x936-54">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="St_Magnus">St Magnus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: St Magnus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Magnus_Erlendsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus Erlendsson">Magnus Erlendsson</a>, Thorfinn\'s grandson had a reputation for piety and gentleness, which the Norwegians viewed as cowardice. Having been taken hostage by King Magnus Barefoot, he refused to fight in a Viking raid in <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> because of his religious convictions, and instead stayed on board the ship during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anglesey_Sound" title="Battle of Anglesey Sound">Battle of Menai Straits</a>, singing psalms. His brother Erling died while campaigning with King Magnus, either at that same battle or in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200888_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200888-55">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Magnus_Kirk,_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/St_Magnus_Kirk%2C_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg/220px-St_Magnus_Kirk%2C_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/St_Magnus_Kirk%2C_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg/330px-St_Magnus_Kirk%2C_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/St_Magnus_Kirk%2C_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg/440px-St_Magnus_Kirk%2C_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="419" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Magnus_Kirk,_Egilsay_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1302639.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ruins of St Magnus Church, on the island of <a href="/wiki/Egilsay" title="Egilsay">Egilsay</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Magnus was obliged to take refuge in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, but returned to Orkney in 1105 and disputed the succession with his cousin <a href="/wiki/Haakon_Paulsson" title="Haakon Paulsson">Haakon Paulsson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Foghlam_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foghlam-56">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Having failed to reach an agreement, he sought help from King <a href="/wiki/Eystein_I_of_Norway" title="Eystein I of Norway">Eystein I of Norway</a>, who granted him the earldom of Orkney and he ruled jointly and amicably with Haakon until 1114.<sup id="cite_ref-Foghlam_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foghlam-56">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Eventually however, the followers of the two earls fell out, and the sides met at an assembly on the <a href="/wiki/Mainland_Orkney" class="mw-redirect" title="Mainland Orkney">Orkney mainland</a> in 1117. Peace was negotiated and the Earls arranged to meet each other to formalise this at Easter on the island of <a href="/wiki/Egilsay" title="Egilsay">Egilsay</a>, each bringing only two ships. Magnus arrived with his two, but then Haakon treacherously turned up with eight ships.<sup id="cite_ref-Towrie_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Towrie-57">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Magnus took refuge in the island\'s church overnight, but the following day he was captured and an angry Haakon made his cook Lifolf kill Magnus by striking him on the head with an axe. It was said that Magnus first prayed for the souls of his executioners.<sup id="cite_ref-Towrie_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Towrie-57">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Sometime later <a href="/wiki/William_the_Old" title="William the Old">William the Old</a>, Bishop of Orkney, was struck blind in his church, but subsequently had his sight restored after praying at the grave of Magnus. He also later sanctified the murdered Earl. <a href="/wiki/St_Magnus_Church,_Egilsay" title="St Magnus Church, Egilsay">St Magnus Church, Egilsay</a>, was constructed on the island shortly afterwards, at or near the supposed site of the murder.<sup id="cite_ref-HES_history_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HES_history-58">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Magnus\'s nephew, <a href="/wiki/Rognvald_Kali_Kolsson,_Earl_of_Orkney" class="mw-redirect" title="Rognvald Kali Kolsson, Earl of Orkney">R\xc3\xb6gnvald Kali Kolsson</a>, laid claim to the Earldom of Orkney, and was advised by his father Kol to promise the islanders to "build a stone minster at <a href="/wiki/Kirkwall" title="Kirkwall">Kirkwall</a>" in memory of his uncle the Holy Earl, and this became <a href="/wiki/St_Magnus_Cathedral" title="St Magnus Cathedral">St Magnus Cathedral</a>. When the cathedral, begun in 1137, was ready for consecration the relics of St Magnus were transferred there.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECallaghan2003167_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECallaghan2003167-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Sweyn_Asleifsson_and_Earl_R.C3.B6gnavald"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweyn_Asleifsson_and_Earl_R\xc3\xb6gnavald">Sweyn Asleifsson and Earl R\xc3\xb6gnavald</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sweyn Asleifsson and Earl R\xc3\xb6gnavald">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg/220px-St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg/330px-St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg/440px-St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1704" data-file-height="2272" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Magnus_Cathedral_Kirkwall_interior.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The interior of <a href="/wiki/St._Magnus_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Magnus Cathedral">St. Magnus Cathedral</a>, which was constructed in the 12th century under the supervision of Kol, father of Earl <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6gnvald_Kali_Kolsson" title="R\xc3\xb6gnvald Kali Kolsson">R\xc3\xb6gnvald Kali Kolsson</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECallaghan2003167_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECallaghan2003167-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>As this part of the saga was written only a few decades after Asleifsson\'s death it is reasonable to suppose these chapters more accurately reflect the events they describe than the histories of earlier times. Asleifsson is depicted as the quintessential Viking, a freebooter whose activities include drunkenness, murder and plundering and whose support is crucial to the politics of the Orkney earldom. Although he was not an earl his activities take up fully one quarter of the saga and the oldest version ends with his death.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198110Introduction-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011148\xe2\x80\x9349_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011148\xe2\x80\x9349-60">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His tale is closely bound up with that of Earl R\xc3\xb6gnvald, a more rounded character who is also a <a href="/wiki/Troubadour" title="Troubadour">troubadour</a> and, like his uncle Magnus, ultimately a saint, and it may be that the saga writers were seeking to portray them as exemplars of the Viking lifestyle. However, there may also be a more complex moral to the story. Although \xc3\x81sleifsson\'s dying words are "Be it known to all men... that I belong to the bodyguard of Saint R\xc3\xb6gnvald the Earl", he also blackmailed R\xc3\xb6gnvald and caused him a great deal of trouble. It is possible that the saga intends to cast R\xc3\xb6gnvald as a weak leader who was unable control his nobles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011148\xe2\x80\x9349_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011148\xe2\x80\x9349-60">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another interpretation of the narrative is that rather than seeing these two men as <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonist</a> that together they live in a  golden age where the  earl is a cultured ruler and <i>primus inter pares</i> but who owes his position in part to his band of  "worthy warriors" and is by no means a despot who rules by divine right. In this case the whole story may be seen as a reaction to the Norwegian royal propaganda of the age that sought to promote the central authority of the crown.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011150\xe2\x80\x9352_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011150\xe2\x80\x9352-61">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>If the height of the earls\' military strength was during the 11th century time of Thorfinn "the Mighty", the reign of Earl R\xc3\xb6gnvald Kali Kolson marks the 12th century cultural high point of the saga.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson2008101_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson2008101-62">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Earls">Later Earls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later Earls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Earl Harald "the Old" Maddadsson (c. 1134 \xe2\x80\x93 1206) ruled jointly with Earl Rognvald for part of his long tenure. When King <a href="/wiki/Eystein_II_of_Norway" title="Eystein II of Norway">Eystein Haraldsson</a> undertook a raiding expedition from Norway against Orkney he encountered Harald near <a href="/wiki/Thurso" title="Thurso">Thurso</a> and captured him. Harald was freed in return for a ransom in gold and by giving his oath to Eystein. Eystein then went on to raid the coasts of Scotland and England.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESturluson1992&#39;&#39;Saga_Of_Sigurd,_Inge,_And_Eystein,_The_Sons_Of_Harald&#39;&#39;:_c._20_&quot;War_Expedition_Of_King_Haraldson&quot;_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESturluson1992&#39;&#39;Saga_Of_Sigurd,_Inge,_And_Eystein,_The_Sons_Of_Harald&#39;&#39;:_c._20_&quot;War_Expedition_Of_King_Haraldson&quot;-63">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._91_&quot;Events_in_Orkney&quot;_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._91_&quot;Events_in_Orkney&quot;-64">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Possibly as a result of Eystein\'s activities, King <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">David I</a> granted half of Caithness to Harald\'s cousin, <a href="/wiki/Erlend_Haraldsson" title="Erlend Haraldsson">Erlend Haraldsson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOram2004100\xe2\x80\x93101_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOram2004100\xe2\x80\x93101-65">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The result was a political struggle which ended with Erlend\'s murder in 1154. Sweyn Asleifsson was again heavily involved in this dynastic conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981cc._92\xe2\x80\x93104_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981cc._92\xe2\x80\x93104-66">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> In 1153 King David died and was succeeded by his young grandson, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_IV_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm IV of Scotland">Malcolm IV</a>. King Eystein too died in a war with his brothers Ingi and <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_II_of_Norway" title="Sigurd II of Norway">Sigurd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESturluson1992&#39;&#39;Saga_Of_Sigurd,_Inge,_And_Eystein,_The_Sons_Of_Harald&#39;&#39;:_c._28_&quot;Of_King_Sigurd&#39;s_Fall&quot;_\xe2\x80\x93_c._32_&quot;King_Eystein&#39;s_Death&quot;_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESturluson1992&#39;&#39;Saga_Of_Sigurd,_Inge,_And_Eystein,_The_Sons_Of_Harald&#39;&#39;:_c._28_&quot;Of_King_Sigurd&#39;s_Fall&quot;_\xe2\x80\x93_c._32_&quot;King_Eystein&#39;s_Death&quot;-67">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> As a result, by 1158 Harald Maddadsson was undisputed Earl of Orkney, with neither the King of Scots nor the King of Norway in a position to contest his power.\n</p><p>The final, brief chapters of the later version of the saga depict the lives of Harald\'s four successors who ruled until the murder of <a href="/wiki/Jon_Haraldsson" title="Jon Haraldsson">Jon Haraldsson</a> in 1231, bringing the line of specifically Norse earls of Orkney to an end<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMuir2005127_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMuir2005127-68">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;f&#93;</a></sup> although Orkney and Shetland remained part of Norway until the 15th century.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_or_fable">History or fable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History or fable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png/220px-Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png/330px-Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png/440px-Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png 2x" data-file-width="972" data-file-height="773" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shetland_and_surrounding_lands.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> at centre, in relation to nearby territories</div></div></div>\n<p>There is clearly doubt about the <a class="external text" href="http://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/historicity">historicity</a> of many of the claims in the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> and the extent to which <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">euhemerism</a> may be an appropriate approach. Just as the backdrop to the supposed great expedition to the west undertaken by King Harald Fairhair that led to the founding of the Orkney earldom was the mid-13th century Norwegian contest with the <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Scots" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Scots">Kings of Scots</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198752\xe2\x80\x9353_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198752\xe2\x80\x9353-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> so also events that have been included in the saga as embellishments to the life of Earl Thorfinn have a number of parallels with the life of <a href="/wiki/Harald_Maddadsson" title="Harald Maddadsson">Harald Maddadsson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007243_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007243-44">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Woolf (2007) speculates that aspects of Thorfinn\'s story may have been included to legitimise the latter\'s adventures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007244_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007244-71">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Early in the narrative in particular, there are examples of obviously fictional elements such as Earl Sigurd\'s <a href="/wiki/Raven_banner" title="Raven banner">raven banner</a> and also in some later events such as the effects of the poisoned shirt that supposedly killed Earl <a href="/wiki/Harald_Haakonsson" title="Harald Haakonsson">Harald Haakonsson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OJsaga_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OJsaga-20">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore there is the regular use of standard Norse dramatic sitations.  For example the story of the killing of <a href="/wiki/Rognvald_Brusason" class="mw-redirect" title="Rognvald Brusason">Rognvald Brusason</a> by Earl Thorfinn "is saga-fiction, and it contains some stock-episodes which are repeated elsewhere in the saga".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200884_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200884-72">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Another example of the saga writer\'s fictional devices is found in the tale of Haakon Paulsson\'s travels through Scandinavia where he meets a fortune teller. The sooth-sayer\'s predictions are in effect a "table of contents" for his later travails with his cousin Magnus and an example of "the frequent need to disentangle the historical Magnus from the saga\'s story-telling techniques".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200890_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200890-73">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However there are also examples of events depicted in the saga that have been thought of as essentially fictional that have later been shown to have some basis in fact. For example, towards the end of his reign as earl, <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Eysteinsson" title="Sigurd Eysteinsson">Sigurd Eysteinsson</a> is said to have challenged a native ruler, M\xc3\xa1el Brigte the Bucktoothed, to a 40-man-a-side battle. Treacherously, Sigurd brought 80 men to the fight and M\xc3\xa1el Brigte was defeated and beheaded. Sigurd strapped the head to his saddle as a trophy, but as Sigurd rode home, M\xc3\xa1el Brigte\'s buck-tooth scratched his leg. The leg became inflamed and infected, and as a result Sigurd died. He was buried in a <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">tumulus</a> known as Sigurd\'s <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">Howe</a>, or  <i>Sigur\xc3\xb0ar-haugr</i>. The location of Sigurd\'s Howe is most probably modern-day Sidera or Cyderhall near <a href="/wiki/Dornoch" title="Dornoch">Dornoch</a>, which discovery was not made until the late 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198758\xe2\x80\x9359_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198758\xe2\x80\x9359-74">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;g&#93;</a></sup>  Another example concerns a King of Scots, apparently named <a href="/wiki/Karl_Hundason" title="Karl Hundason">Karl Hundason</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Who_was_Karl_Hundason">Who was Karl Hundason</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Who was Karl Hundason">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The identity of Karl Hundason, unknown to Scots and Irish sources, has long been a matter of dispute.  His existence rests solely on the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i>, and more particularly on those elements of <i>\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3rfinnsdr\xc3\xa1pa</i> which are preserved within it.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;h&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Eben_William_Robertson" title="Eben William Robertson">Robertson</a> (1862) proposed that Hundason should be identified with <a href="/wiki/Duncan_I_of_Scotland" title="Duncan I of Scotland">Duncan I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobertson1862477\xe2\x80\x93479&#39;&#39;&#39;II&#39;&#39;&#39;_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobertson1862477\xe2\x80\x93479&#39;&#39;&#39;II&#39;&#39;&#39;-79">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Forbes_Skene" title="William Forbes Skene">William Forbes Skene</a> suggested that Karl (or Kali) Hundason should be identified with "Malcolm MacKenneth", a son of <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_III_of_Scotland" title="Kenneth III of Scotland">Kenneth III</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkene1902&#39;&#39;&#39;1&#39;&#39;&#39;_c._5_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkene1902&#39;&#39;&#39;1&#39;&#39;&#39;_c._5-80">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Another candidate is <a href="/wiki/Macbeth,_King_of_Scotland" title="Macbeth, King of Scotland">MacBeth</a> whose father may be called "jarl Hundi" in <i><a href="/wiki/Nj%C3%A1l%27s_saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Nj\xc3\xa1l&#39;s saga">Nj\xc3\xa1l\'s saga</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198773_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198773-81">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Woolf (2007) proposes that Hundason, rather that being some hitherto unknown Scots king, was the son of Thorfinn\'s brother Hundi.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007309-10_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007309-10-82">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> However, Thomson (2008) notes that both the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> and <i>St Olaf\'s saga</i> suggest Hundi only lived "a short while" and was unlikely to have had a son himself.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200875\xe2\x80\x9377_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200875\xe2\x80\x9377-83">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Anderson (1990) suggested that this is "a fabulous story" and concluded that "[n]o solution to the riddle seems to be justified".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson1990576,_note_7_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson1990576,_note_7-84">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Muir (2005) points out that a literal translation of "Karl Hundisson" is "peasant son-of-a-dog", an insult that would have been obvious to Norse-speakers hearing the saga and that "we can assume this wasn\'t his real name".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMuir200547_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMuir200547-85">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The implication is that there is no purpose in seeking phonetic parallels with known Scots personages.  Thomson points out that both "Karl" and Hundi" are names used in other contexts without disparaging intentions although the combination is otherwise unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200875_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200875-86">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thomson also notes that the war with Hundasson seem to have taken place between 1029 and 1035 and that the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Ulster" title="Annals of Ulster">Annals of Ulster</a></i> record the violent death of <a href="/wiki/Gille_Coemg%C3%A1in_of_Moray" title="Gille Coemg\xc3\xa1in of Moray">Gillacomgain</a>, son of Mael Brigte and <a href="/wiki/Mormaer_of_Moray" title="Mormaer of Moray">Mormaer of Moray</a> in 1032. He too is thus a candidate for Thorfinn\'s Scots foe\xe2\x80\x94and the manner of his death by fire bears comparison with Arn\xc3\xb3r\'s poetic description of the aftermath of the battle at Torfness.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200879_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200879-87">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nWhoever Karl son of Hundi may have been, it appears that the saga is reporting a local conflict with a Scots ruler of Moray or Ross:</p><blockquote><p>[T]he whole narrative is consistent with the idea that the struggle of Thorfinn and Karl is a continuation of that which had been waged since the ninth century by the Orkney earls, notably Sigurd Rognvald\'s son, Ljot, and Sigurd the Stout, against the princes or <i>mormaers</i> of Moray, Sutherland, Ross, and Argyll, and that, in fine, Malcolm and Karl were <i>mormaers</i> of one of these four provinces.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETaylor1937338_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaylor1937338-88">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198771\xe2\x80\x9374_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198771\xe2\x80\x9374-89">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;i&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>It is therefore entirely possible that Thorfinn\'s campaign was not fought against the Scottish crown as such but that rather the Scots may have been his allies in a struggle they both had against the power of Moray.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200880_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200880-92">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">Gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gender">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>It is evident that the main characters of the saga are the exclusively male jarls and kings and their male supporters and antagonists. Nonetheless, it is believed that women enjoyed a relatively high status during the Viking Age, possibly due to the high degree of mobility in society<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford1987216_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford1987216-93">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> and they regularly appear in supporting roles. Amongst them are <a href="/wiki/Gunnhild,_Mother_of_Kings" title="Gunnhild, Mother of Kings">Gunnhild "Mother of Kings" Gormsd\xc3\xb3ttir</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ingibiorg_Finnsdottir" title="Ingibiorg Finnsdottir">Ingibiorg "the Earls\'-mother" Finnsdottir</a>, wife of Earl Thorfinn; Frakokk, whose poisoned shirt was involved in the death of her nephew Earl Harald Haakonsson; <a href="/wiki/Helga_Moddansdottir" class="mw-redirect" title="Helga Moddansdottir">Helga Moddansdottir</a>, sister of Frakkok and mother of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ingibj%C3%B6rg_Hakonsd%C3%B3ttir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ingibj\xc3\xb6rg Hakonsd\xc3\xb3ttir (page does not exist)">Ingibj\xc3\xb6rg Hakonsd\xc3\xb3ttir</a> who married  King <a href="/wiki/Olaf_I_Godredsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Olaf I Godredsson">Olaf Godredsson</a>; and  Gunnhild, sister of Magnus Erlendsson and mother of Earl Rognvald. On the other hand the idea of "courtly love" plays a much more prominent part in the literature of continental Europe for the same period than in the sagas in general.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTES\xc3\xa4vborg2010361_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTES\xc3\xa4vborg2010361-94">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orkney_and_Norway">Orkney and Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Orkney and Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Woolf (2007) argues that the "saga\xe2\x80\x99s main theme, in so far as it can be said to have one, seems to have been to tie in the history of the earldom with that of the kings of Norway and to establish clearly the history of the relationships between the two polities." Crawford (1987) observes several sub-themes: "of submission and of overlordship; the problem of dual allegiance and the threat of the earls looking to the kings of Scots as an alternative source of support; the Norwegian kings\' use of hostages; and their general aim of attempting to turn the Orkney earls into royal officials bound to them by oaths of homage, and returning tribute to them on a regular basis."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198776\xe2\x80\x9377_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198776\xe2\x80\x9377-95">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An example is the passing of the title of Earl of Orkney from Rognvald Eysteinsson, who received it from the Norwegian crown, to his brother Sigurd. The notion that Rognvald could hand over his title in this way has been interpreted in various ways. It may be that Rognvald considered the gift from the king as a mixed blessing,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMuir20056_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMuir20056-96">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> but this is an instance in which the writer of the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> attempts to reconcile the conflicting themes of independence from Norway (Rognvald gifts the islands to Sigurd) and dependence on royal authority (King Harald formalises the process by confirming Sigurd as earl).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200831_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200831-97">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> Beuermann (2011) speculates that Rognvald\'s transfer of power to his brother may have been an attempt by the saga writers to imply that the Orkney earldom had more independence from Norway than that of Rognvald\'s earldom of M\xc3\xb8re<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011120_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011120-98">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> and that the earl\'s holdings in <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a> may have allowed for an even greater degree of freedom of action. Such implications are more likely to be rooted in the writer\'s interest in emphasising Orcadian independence at the time of writing rather than the 9th/10th century events they purport to describe.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011121_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011121-99">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Similarly, the story of Torf-Einar in the next generation touches on this theme. The writer of the saga established Einarr\'s status in two contradictory ways. Although in the <i>Historia Norvegia</i> Rognvald of M\xc3\xb8re\'s family are described as "pirates" the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> provides them with a legally established earldom instated by the king. On the other hand Einarr success is largely down to his own efforts and he negotiates with King Harald rather than offers blind obedience. The author is thus able to emphasise both the legitimacy and independence of his <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">house</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200830\xe2\x80\x9331_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200830\xe2\x80\x9331-100">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinstrife">Kinstrife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Kinstrife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Joint earldoms were a frequent feature of the Norse earldom and Thomson (2008) identifies a different intention at the heart of the saga. This joint rulership was "inherently unstable and usually ended in violence".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200858\xe2\x80\x9359_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200858\xe2\x80\x9359-101">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> He identifies these family feuds as being the main theme, culminating in the martyrdom of St Magnus c.1115, and that the writer is emphasising the doom of "kin-slaying".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200858\xe2\x80\x9359_101-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200858\xe2\x80\x9359-101">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> Examples include the fratricidal strife that engulfed the sons of Earl Thorfinn in which they were abetted by <a href="/wiki/Ragnhild_Eriksdotter" title="Ragnhild Eriksdotter">Ragnhild Eriksdotter</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981cc._9-11_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981cc._9-11-102">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>  the deaths of Einar Sigurdsson and his nephew <a href="/wiki/Rognvald_Brusason" class="mw-redirect" title="Rognvald Brusason">Rognvald Brusason</a> at the hands of Thorkel Fosterer, the henchman of Thorfinn<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._16_&quot;Murder_of_an_earl&quot;_and_c._30_&quot;Earl_Thorfinn_and_King_Magnus&quot;_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._16_&quot;Murder_of_an_earl&quot;_and_c._30_&quot;Earl_Thorfinn_and_King_Magnus&quot;-103">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> as well as the homicidal dispute between the cousins Haakon Paulsson and Magnus Erlendsson itself that led to the latter\'s canonisation. Indeed, the rivalry between the two families that Paul and Erlend founded were still alive four generations later when the <i>Orkneyinga Saga</i> was written down.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200888_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200888-55">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the saga frequently mentions placenames it is largely silent on the subject of how the joint earldoms functioned on a geographical basis. It is possible that <a href="/wiki/Brusi_Sigurdsson" title="Brusi Sigurdsson">Brusi Sigurdsson</a>\'s share, described as the "northernmost part of the isles", was those islands lying north of the <a href="/wiki/Mainland,_Orkney" title="Mainland, Orkney">Orkney mainland</a>, that his brother <a href="/wiki/Einar_Sigurdsson" title="Einar Sigurdsson">Einar "Wry-mouth" Sigurdsson</a>\'s was originally the east Mainland and the south isles and that <a href="/wiki/Sumarlidi_Sigurdsson" title="Sumarlidi Sigurdsson">Sumarlidi Sigurdsson</a>\'s was the west Mainland.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;j&#93;</a></sup> However, it is also possible that Brusi\'s share was <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, which formed part of the earldom throughout the Norse period. This possibility is supported by a later reference to his son Rognvald as "Lord of the Shetlanders" and Thompson (2008) is in "no doubt " that Shetland was in Brusi\'s possession.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith198821_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith198821-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200870\xe2\x80\x9373_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200870\xe2\x80\x9373-106">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> It is likely that <a href="/wiki/Fair_Isle" title="Fair Isle">Fair Isle</a> marked the boundary between these shares both then and during later joint earldoms.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith198832_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith198832-107">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;k&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion_and_morality">Religion and morality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Religion and morality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Raven_banner_(Bayeux_Tapestry).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Raven_banner_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29.jpg/220px-Raven_banner_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29.jpg" width="220" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Raven_banner_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29.jpg/330px-Raven_banner_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Raven_banner_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29.jpg/440px-Raven_banner_%28Bayeux_Tapestry%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="695" data-file-height="755" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Raven_banner_(Bayeux_Tapestry).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail from the <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry" title="Bayeux Tapestry">Bayeux Tapestry</a>, showing a <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> knight carrying what appears to be a <a href="/wiki/Raven_banner" title="Raven banner">raven banner</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The accuracy with which the saga addresses the conversion of Orkney and Shetland to Christianity has been touched on above and the weight of archaeological evidence suggests that Christian burial was already widespread in Orkney by Sigurd Hlodvirsson\'s time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200864_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200864-110">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> The intention may have been to disown the influence of indigenous elements of Orcadian and Shetlandic culture (such as the existence of this religion prior to the arrival of the Norse) and emphasise that positive cultural developments came from Scandinavia, whilst at the same time critiquing the unduly blunt method of Norwegian interference in this case.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011143\xe2\x80\x9344_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeuermann2011143\xe2\x80\x9344-48">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The inclusion of the tale of the <a href="/wiki/Raven_banner" title="Raven banner">raven banner</a> in the saga material may convey the idea of a revival of heathenism in Orcadian society and a reaction to Norwegian attempts to control the islands. However, in the <i>Orkneyinga Saga</i> there is a vivid contrast between Sigurd\'s death clutching the raven banner and the later career of his son Thorfinn, who is credited with several achievements in bringing Orkney into mainstream Christendom. Taken as a whole the intention may be to draw attention to this transition.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200866\xe2\x80\x9367_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200866\xe2\x80\x9367-47">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>R\xc3\xb6gnvald Kali Kolsson\'s pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> has a different flavour from the politicking and violence that permeates much of the rest of the saga. There are romantic touches and the earl\'s poetry is emphasised. There is a case for regarding this episode as the "central quest"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198116Introduction_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198116Introduction-111">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> of the saga and the poetry emphasises some of the contradictions at the heart of the medieval Orcadian world. Society is both pagan and Christian, violence goes hand-in-hand with maganimity, kinstrife is frequent yet "the bonds of sworn friendship cannot be broken".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198117Introduction_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198117Introduction-112">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_elements">Other elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another recurring theme in the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> is raiding in the Hebrides,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198115\xe2\x80\x9316Introduction_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198115\xe2\x80\x9316Introduction-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> which is touched on during the saga\'s coverage of Rognvald Eysteinsson\'s life and times in the earliest part of the story and on several other occasions up to the last chapters and Sweyn Asleifsson\'s exploits. Remarkably, neither the <i>Orkneyinga Saga</i> nor any other Kings\' Sagas refers to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> campaign by <a href="/wiki/Magnus_II_of_Norway" title="Magnus II of Norway">Magnus Haraldsson</a> in 1058, which is the first such event attested in contemporary sources in the British Isles. The authors of the sagas may have believed these events were easily confused with Magnus Barefoot\'s later invasion and that as the earlier Magnus had no royal descendants at the time the stories were committed to writing no-one of importance would lose face by omitting them. Woolf (2007) suggests this shortcoming in the historical record "serves as a warning" about the saga\'s reliability in general.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007267_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoolf2007267-113">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nAt the time the <i>Orkneyinga saga</i> was first written down Haakon Paulsson\'s grandson, Harald Maddadsson, was Earl of Orkney and the writer clearly had some difficulty in portraying the kin-slaying of Magnus Erlendsson. Thomson (2008) concludes that the "assembly" that sentenced Magnus was either invented or heavily emphasised in order to "divert some of the blame from Hakon".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200896_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200896-114">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, in reporting on Earl Haakon\'s death the saga reports that this was "felt to be a great loss, his later years having been very peaceful".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._53_&quot;Death_of_Earl_Hakon&quot;_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards1981c._53_&quot;Death_of_Earl_Hakon&quot;-115">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> Commenting on the complex relationship between history and invention in the saga Joseph Anderson wrote:</p><blockquote><p>When great events and mighty deeds were preserved for posterity by oral recitation alone, it was necessary that the memory should be enabled to retain its hold of the elements of the story by some extraneous artistic aid, and therefore they were welded by the wordsmith\xe2\x80\x99s rhymes into a compact and homogenous \xe2\x80\x9clay\xe2\x80\x9d. Thus, worked into the poetical setting (as the jeweler mounts his gems to enhance their value and ensure their preservation), they passed as heirlooms from generation to generation, floating on the oral tradition of the people\xe2\x80\x9d.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson1873Introduction_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson1873Introduction-116">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>There are therefore many facets to this saga and, in part at least, its purpose was to "explore such social and psychological tensions as these in the history of the people of Orkney, and to help them understand themselves through a knowledge of their origins".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198119Introduction_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards198119Introduction-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_significance">Modern significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Modern significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The saga is not only of importance to scholars attempting to understand the Viking Age in the islands and their neighbouring lands, it is also plays a significant part in the modern culture of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">Northern Isles</a>. The vivid stories of life in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Scotland" title="Scandinavian Scotland">Scandinavian Scotland</a> invite the "people of Orkney and Shetland to recognise themselves as the inheritors and custodians of a dual culture, both Norse and Scottish".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEP\xc3\xa1lssonEdwards19819Introduction-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a However it has also been pointed out that Harald Maddadson was the son of a Scottish earl \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Matad,_Earl_of_Atholl" title="Matad, Earl of Atholl">Matad, Earl of Atholl</a> \xe2\x80\x93 and a female heiress of Orkney \xe2\x80\x93 Margaret, a daughter of Haakon Paulsson. It is the saga writer who adds the patronymic "Hundasson".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrawford198772_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawford198772-49">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson200876_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson200876-77">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-91"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-91">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There were only two Earls of Orkney named Sigurd. Sverre and his force confronted the \xc3\x98yskjeggs in the spring of 1194, when the two fleets met north of <a href="/wiki/Bergen,_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Bergen, Norway">Bergen</a>. In the ensuing battle the Norwegians claimed a decisive victory. King Sverre appears to have believed that Harald Maddadson was involved in the affair and he punished Harald by placing Shetland under the direct rule of the Norwegian Crown, which was not returned to the care of the Orkney earldom during his lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMuir2005_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMuir2005-108">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n</ol></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Citations">Citations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orkneyinga_saga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Citations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
