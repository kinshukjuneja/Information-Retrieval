The team is the forerunner of, but not directly connected with, the current <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> team.  After the conclusion of the 1877 season, a game-fixing scandal involving two players the Brown Stockings had acquired led the team to resign its membership in the NL.  The club then declared bankruptcy and folded.\n</p><p>The Brown Stockings did not meet a complete demise, however.  Organized by <a href="/wiki/Outfielder" title="Outfielder">outfielder</a> <a href="/wiki/Ned_Cuthbert" title="Ned Cuthbert">Ned Cuthbert</a>, a few members of the former club continued to play the following year, though not now bound to any league.  They played whomever they could, wherever they could, and still managed to draw crowds and make a profit, leading to play again the following two years.  The Brown Stockings regained some of their former success \xe2\x80\x93 enough of it that, despite the recent scandal, fans of the team seemed to exhibit a short memory and began to show interest in recreating another professional St. Louis baseball team.  In 1881, when <a href="/wiki/German_people" class="mw-redirect" title="German people">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Immigrant" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigrant">immigrant</a> <a href="/wiki/Chris_von_der_Ahe" title="Chris von der Ahe">Chris von der Ahe</a> \xe2\x80\x94 owner of a grocery store and saloon who was initially ignorant about baseball \xe2\x80\x94 saw the popularity of the club, he bought them out and soon became interested in having the team compete in a professional league.  Together with beer magnates in five other cities, the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a> was formed in late 1881, and professional baseball flourished in St. Louis \xe2\x80\x94 this time, with the resurrected <a href="/wiki/1882_St._Louis_Brown_Stockings_season" title="1882 St. Louis Brown Stockings season">Brown Stockings</a> the next year.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" or National Association (NA), in that league\'s final season, the Brown Stockings were the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_(NA)" title="St. Louis (NA)">first of two</a> teams to represent St. Louis in a professional baseball association in 1875 (Spink 1911).  Grand Avenue Grounds \xe2\x80\x93 the Brown Stockings\' home field \xe2\x80\x93 was later the site of <a href="/wiki/Sportsman%27s_Park" title="Sportsman&#39;s Park">Sportsman\'s Park</a>.  <a leading the league with a <a href="/wiki/On-base_plus_slugging#Adjusted_OPS_(OPS+)" title="On-base plus slugging">league-adjusted OPS</a> of 203.<sup id="cite_ref-Pike_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pike-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Eighteen-year-old <a href="/wiki/Pud_Galvin" title="Pud Galvin">Pud Galvin</a> is credited with leading the league in <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">ERA</a> (1.16) despite pitching just 62 <a href="/wiki/Inning" title="Inning">innings</a>, a very small total compared to the league leader in <a href="/wiki/Innings_pitched" title="Innings pitched">innings pitched</a> (<a href="/wiki/Al_Spalding" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Spalding">Al Spalding</a> with 570.2).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The Brown Stockings <a href="/wiki/1875_in_baseball" title="1875 in baseball">finished 39\xe2\x80\x9329</a> and in fourth place in their only season in the NA.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Like the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">White Stockings</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> (established <a href="/wiki/1871_Chicago_White_Stockings_season" title="1871 Chicago White Stockings season">1871</a>), the Brown Stockings adopted uniforms and acquired a nickname by descent with variation from the famous <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Red_Stockings" title="Cincinnati Red Stockings">Red Stockings</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a> (est. 1869), the self-proclaimed "original" professional baseball team, who garnered much public interest due to an undefeated streak during a <a href="/wiki/Barnstorm_(sports)" title="Barnstorm (sports)">barnstorming</a> tour in <a href="/wiki/1869_in_baseball" title="1869 in baseball">1869</a> and <a href="/wiki/1870_in_baseball" title="1870 in baseball">1870</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="National_League_entrant_.281876.E2.80.9377.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="National_League_entrant_(1876\xe2\x80\x9377)">National League entrant (1876\xe2\x80\x9377)</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The St. Louis Brown Stockings entered the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (NL) as a founding team the following <a href="/wiki/1876_in_baseball" title="1876 in baseball">season</a> along with five other former NA teams and two new professional league entrants.  <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Bradley" class="mw-redirect" title="George Washington Bradley">George Bradley</a> pitched the first <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitter</a> in <a href="/wiki/1876_St._Louis_Brown_Stockings_season" title="1876 St. Louis Brown Stockings season">Brown Stockings</a> defeated the <a href="/wiki/1876_Hartford_Dark_Blues_season" title="1876 Hartford Dark Blues season">Hartford Dark Blues</a> 2-0.   It was one of Bradley\'s 16 league-leading and record-setting <a href="/wiki/Shutouts_in_baseball" title="Shutouts in baseball">shutouts</a> that season and his 1.23 <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">ERA</a> also led the league.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradley_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Bradley and Pike (.323 <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a>) led the Brown Stockings to a 45-19 record and a third-place finish.<sup id="cite_ref-Pike_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pike-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  \n</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/1876_Chicago_White_Stockings_season" title="1876 Chicago White Stockings season">Chicago White Stockings</a> finished with the best regular-season record \xe2\x80\x93 making them the default NL champions as no playoff existed \xe2\x80\x93 due in part to their already feverish <a href="/wiki/Cardinals%E2%80%93Cubs_rivalry" title="Cardinals\xe2\x80\x93Cubs rivalry">rivalry</a>, the White Stockings and Brown Stockings faced off in an unofficial five-game playoff for the title "Champions of the West."  The Brown Stockings won the series.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expulsion">Expulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Louis_Brown_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>With Bradley losing his effectiveness due to an arm injury in <a href="/wiki/1877_St._Louis_Brown_Stockings_season" title="1877 St. Louis Brown Stockings season">1877</a> (his ERA increased to 3.31), the Brown Stockings slipped to 28-32 in 1877.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradley_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  The team signed stars <a href="/wiki/Jim_Devlin" title="Jim Devlin">Jim Devlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Hall_(baseball)" title="George Hall (baseball)">George Hall</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Grays" title="Louisville Grays">Louisville Grays</a>, only to become embroiled in a game-fixing scandal that resulted in the permanent expulsion of Devlin and Hall (and two other Grays players) from the league.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The Grays and Brown Stockings both filed for bankruptcy in the aftermath of the scandal.  However, the Brown Stockings continued to play as an independent barnstorming team on a semi-professional basis from 1878\xe2\x80\x931881.<sup id="cite_ref-Cash_55_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cash_55-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Aftermath_of_the_scandal_and_the_resurrection_of_the_Brown_Stockings_.281878.E2.80.9381.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_of_the_scandal_and_the_resurrection_of_the_Brown_Stockings_(1878\xe2\x80\x9381)">Aftermath of the scandal and the resurrection of the Brown Stockings (1878\xe2\x80\x9381)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Louis_Brown_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath of the scandal and the resurrection of the Brown Stockings (1878\xe2\x80\x9381)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Despite the team disbanding after the 1877 season, five members of that team \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Second_baseman" title="Second baseman">second baseman</a>/<a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> <a href="/wiki/Mike_McGeary" title="Mike McGeary">Mike McGeary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Outfielder" title="Outfielder">outfielder</a> <a href="/wiki/Ned_Cuthbert" title="Ned Cuthbert">Ned Cuthbert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shortstop" title="Shortstop">shortstop</a> <a href="/wiki/Dickey_Pearce" title="Dickey Pearce">Dickey Pearce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Third_baseman" title="Third baseman">third baseman</a> <a href="/wiki/Joe_Battin" title="Joe Battin">Joe Battin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Joe_Blong" title="Joe Blong">Joe Blong</a> all comprised a team, also called the Brown Stockings, to informally play in 1878.<sup id="cite_ref-Cash_55_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cash_55-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  However, at this time, popularity for baseball was low, and <i>St. Louis Republican</i> sportswriter <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Henry_Spink" title="Alfred Henry Spink">Al Spink</a> sought ways to bring back popularity for baseball in St. Louis. \n</p><p>The 1879 team started off by winning 24 of their first 25 games.<sup id="cite_ref-Cash_58_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cash_58-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  However, the team encountered a different kind of problem.  The Brown Stockings could not match their 1875 attendance average of around 2,300 per game due to their complete domination of the local amateur clubs; they averaged well under one thousand in that year after attendance fall offs between those two years.  To avert the issue, they played more competitive teams from out of town.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>   In spite of narrowing the competitive gap, St. Louis kept winning, and, as a result, more and more fans started showing up later in the year.<sup id="cite_ref-Cash_58_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cash_58-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1880 season was another polar season mixed with antipathy and surging fever.  August Solari, who leased Grand Avenue Park, was on the last year of the lease and the gate receipts did little to dissuade him from forgoing resigning the lease.  In fact, he threatened to dismantle the ballpark.  Cuthbert, who also worked for Von der Ahe at his Golden Lion Saloon, urged him to promote the team more.  At this time, Von der Ahe still had not made the full realization of baseball\'s popularity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  For months, it was Cuthbert talking about baseball with Von der Ahe, who understood very little about the actual game, that he began to realize its significance because of its profitability.<sup id="cite_ref-Cash_60_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cash_60-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="The_formation_of_the_Sportsman.27s_Park_and_Club_Association"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_formation_of_the_Sportsman\'s_Park_and_Club_Association">The formation of the Sportsman\'s Park and Club Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Louis_Brown_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The formation of the Sportsman&#039;s Park and Club Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Von der Ahe purchased the remainder of the lease on Grand Avenue Park, sold minority stock and raised enough money to renovate the dilapidated park.  John W. Peckington, another local saloon owner, became a minority owner, creating The Sportsman\'s Park and Club Association.<sup id="cite_ref-Cash_60_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cash_60-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Spink, who himself had not stopped lobbying for more interest in baseball during the sport\'s relative dormancy in St. Louis, became the secretary and business manager.  More fans began attend games and Spink arranged for out-of-town teams to play there, the first being a makeshift team from <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a> playing under the traditional name "Redlegs."  Profitability increased, and thus, more extensive renovations were completed, further increasing attendance, and again, profitability.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_formation_of_a_new_all-professional_league">The formation of a new all-professional league</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Louis_Brown_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The formation of a new all-professional league">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Again led by Cuthbert, the Brown Stockings continued to win in convincing fashion in 1881, finishing with a 35-15 record.  Notable opponents included the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Atlantics" title="Brooklyn Atlantics">Brooklyn Atlantics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a>, the Akrons, and the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Eclipse" class="mw-redirect" title="Louisville Eclipse">Louisville Eclipse</a>.  The club also continued to prosper at the gate.  An evolving baseball renaissance that flourished in St. Louis coincided (and possibly spilled over into) the rising enthusiasm of beer magnates over baseball in five other major cities\xe2\x80\x93\xe2\x80\x93Philadelphia, Baltimore, Cincinnati, Louisville, and Pittsburgh.  Proprietors who saw the Brown Stockings\' unprecedented success and profitability after disenfranchisement began spirited dialogue with Von der Ahe about constructing a new league that could rival, and compete, with the National League.  Further, the parties involved chiefly represented cities the NL had excluded.  NL-imposed restrictions upon Sunday play and alcohol consumption at their parks was prohibitive to the very means these owners made their fortune.  Ultimately, owners of the expansion teams announced the establishment of a new all-professional league called the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a> from the Hotel Gibson in Cincinnati on November 2, 1881.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  With that act, the St. Louis newspapers lauded Von der Ahe for resurrecting a "corpse" and transforming it "into the liveliest being imaginable."<sup id="cite_ref-Cash_55_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cash_55-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_alumni">Notable alumni</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
