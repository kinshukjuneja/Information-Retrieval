Charles\'s Castle</b> is a ruined artillery fort overlooking <a href="/wiki/New_Grimsby_harbour" class="mw-redirect" title="New Grimsby harbour">New Grimsby harbour</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Tresco,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Tresco, Isles of Scilly">Tresco</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>. Built between 1548 and 1551 to protect the islands from French attack, it would have held a <a href="/wiki/Artillery_battery" title="Artillery battery">battery of guns</a> and an accompanying garrison, designed to prevent enemy vessels from entering the harbour. The castle is polygonal in design, constructed from <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> stone, with the gun battery at the front, and a dining room, kitchen and living accommodation at the rear. An additional defensive earthwork was constructed around it during the 17th century. The design of the castle is unusual for the period, and is only seen elsewhere in <a href="/wiki/Blockhouse" title="Blockhouse">blockhouses</a> along the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>.\n</p><p>The castle\'s design was unsatisfactory, as its guns could not be angled so to fire down into the harbour, and its defences were considered vulnerable to attack. To mitigate this, an additional blockhouse was probably constructed below, closer to the water, but eventually a new fortification, the <a href="/wiki/Star_Castle,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Star Castle, Isles of Scilly">Star Castle</a>, was built instead on the neighbouring island of <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Mary&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Mary\'s</a>, which became the main fort in the Scilly Isles. In the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, the Scilly Isles were held by the Royalist sympathisers of King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>, who gave the castle its current name. The islands were then attacked by a Parliamentary force led by Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Blake_(admiral)" title="Robert Blake (admiral)">Robert Blake</a> in 1651, who landed on Tresco but bypassed the fort as he took the island. Its Royalist defenders blew up parts of the castle as they left, and some of its stone appears to have been used to build the newer <a href="/wiki/Cromwell%27s_Castle" title="Cromwell&#39;s Castle">Cromwell\'s Castle</a> by the harbour.\n</p><p>Although King Charles\'s Castle was being used to house soldiers in 1660, by the 18th century it was described as ruinous. After 1922, the castle passed into the guardianship of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Works_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Works (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Works</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Excavation_(archaeology)" title="Excavation (archaeology)">archaeological excavations</a> were carried out in 1954. In the 21st century the site is controlled by <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> and is open to visitors. It is protected under UK law as a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_monument" title="Scheduled monument">scheduled monument</a> and a Grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" href="/w/index.php?title=King_Charles%27s_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>King Charles\'s Castle was built between 1548 and 1551 to protect the <a href="/wiki/Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Scilly Isles">Scilly Isles</a> against French attack.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Tensions with France had grown during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> and spilled over into war in 1538.<sup id="cite_ref-ONeil1951P18_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeil1951P18-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Henry initially responded by fortifying the coasts of England, constructing new artillery forts designed to defend against the longer-range cannons that were becoming common in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-ONeil1951P18_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeil1951P18-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Henry\'s son, the nine-year-old <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">Edward VI</a>, inherited the throne in 1547, facing renewed war with France.<sup id="cite_ref-ONeil1951P18_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeil1951P18-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seymour,_1st_Duke_of_Somerset" title="Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset">Edward Seymour</a> was made the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Protector" title="Lord Protector">Lord Protector</a> to the King, and he appointed his brother, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Seymour,_1st_Baron_Seymour_of_Sudeley" title="Thomas Seymour, 1st Baron Seymour of Sudeley">Thomas</a>, as England\'s <a href="/wiki/Lord_Admiral" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Admiral">Lord Admiral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010P27_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010P27-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Thomas inspected the Scilly Isles personally and concluded that they were vulnerable to a French invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010P27_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010P27-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg/220px-Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg/330px-Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg/440px-Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Interior_of_King_Charles%27_Castle_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834051.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The interior of the hall</div></div></div>\n<p>As a result of the inspection, Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Fleming_(MP)" title="Francis Fleming (MP)">Francis Flemming</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-General_of_the_Ordnance" title="Lieutenant-General of the Ordnance">Lieutenant-General of the Ordnance</a>, was tasked in February 1558 with improving the defences on the islands.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010P27_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010P27-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Flemming was supported in this effort with a shipment of lead to aid in construction, and money raised from the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">dissolution of the monasteries</a> in England.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010P27_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010P27-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The building work initially focused on the island of <a href="/wiki/Tresco,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Tresco, Isles of Scilly">Tresco</a> and was carried out under the direction of John Killigrew, the captain of <a href="/wiki/Pendennis_Castle" title="Pendennis Castle">Pendennis Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010PP2627_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010PP2627-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Tresco was in need of modern defences, but Killigrew also wanted to use the work programme to increase his political influence on the island.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The castle was built as part of this programme of work.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010PP2627_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010PP2627-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was positioned on the high ground of Castle Down to protect <a href="/wiki/New_Grimsby_harbour" class="mw-redirect" title="New Grimsby harbour">New Grimsby harbour</a>, overlooking the narrow, northern entrance to the harbour.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It would have held a battery of guns and an accompanying garrison, designed to prevent enemy vessels from entering the harbour.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Edward Seymour fell from power in 1549, and after his removal a new survey was carried out by Captain William Tyrell, in part to confirm that the new fortifications were suitably located across the islands.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010P27_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010P27-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Building work across the Scilly Isles continued, expanding to include the neighbouring island of <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Mary&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Mary\'s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010P28_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010P28-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> At least 540 <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees from <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a> were dispatched to the islands in early 1550, as the islands lacked suitable sources of timber of their own.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodie2010P28_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodie2010P28-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Orders were given in 1551 to send <a href="/wiki/Bow_and_arrow" title="Bow and arrow">bows, arrows</a> and the ingredients required to make <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a> to the islands, where construction teams were kept at work throughout 1552.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Old Blockhouse appears to have been completed, but the Crown\'s resources had become badly stretched and it was decided at the end of 1552 to curtail further expenditure on the Scilly Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Between 1548 and 1552, a total of \xc2\xa33,123 had been spent on improving the fortifications on the islands; a 1579 survey suggested that, with the cost of the garrisons, the project had come to a total of \xc2\xa36,000.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;nb 1&#93;</a></sup> Edward\'s successor, Queen <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary I</a>, intended to establish a garrison of 150 soldiers on the islands, but it is uncertain if these numbers were ever achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> By 1558, Killigrew held the title of the "captain in the Castell of Tresco", referring to King Charles\'s Castle.<sup id="cite_ref-list.english-heritage.org.uk_resultsingle_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-list.english-heritage.org.uk_resultsingle-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Weaknesses">Weaknesses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Charles%27s_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Weaknesses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>It soon became evident that King Charles\'s Castle had been built in a poor location.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryandResearchListEntry_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryandResearchListEntry-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It was 40 metres (130&#160;ft) above sea level and its guns could only fire at enemy ships in the harbour by being angled downwards.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryandResearchListEntry_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryandResearchListEntry-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Artillery pieces in the 16th century could not fire in this position as the cannonballs would fall out of their muzzles, making the castle much less effective in defending Tresco.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryandResearchListEntry_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryandResearchListEntry-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> By 1554, a small blockhouse had been built beneath the castle on the future site of <a href="/wiki/Cromwell%27s_Castle" title="Cromwell&#39;s Castle">Cromwell\'s Castle</a>, overlooking the harbour just above the sea level to compensate for this weakness.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The concerns over the castle were significant.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Francis Godolphin, who at the time was renting Tresco from the Crown, commented at the end of the century that the castle could not defend the island as "it neither discovereth the whole harbour so through the iminent height thereof can make no good shot so steep downwards", and also complained about its fortification, "which is worst, is of so weak form as it cannot be defended".<sup id="cite_ref-ONeil1961P20_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeil1961P20-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Partially in response to these problems, the new <a href="/wiki/Star_Castle,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Star Castle, Isles of Scilly">Star Castle</a> was constructed on St Mary\'s island between 1593 and 1594; a more modern, well-positioned design, Star Castle rapidly became the key defensive site in the Scilly Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Charles%27s_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg/220px-Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg/330px-Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg/440px-Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cromwell_Castle_and_King_Charles%27s_Castle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The castle (top), overlooking <a href="/wiki/New_Grimsby_harbour" class="mw-redirect" title="New Grimsby harbour">New Grimsby harbour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cromwell%27s_Castle" title="Cromwell&#39;s Castle">Cromwell\'s Castle</a> (bottom)</div></div></div>\n<p>The Scilly Isles supported <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, and after a short period in Parliamentary control rebelled in favour of Charles in 1648.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cavaliers" class="mw-redirect" title="Cavaliers">Royalists</a> on Tresco appeared to have believed that King Charles\'s Castle was a critical defensive point on the island and probably built new earthwork defences around the castle to defend it against an attack from the land.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It is possible, however, that these defences instead date from around 1627, and were built by the King\'s engineer, Bernard Johnson.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> During this period the castle was given its current name.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryandResearch_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryandResearch-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tresco formed a base for Royalist <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a>, and Parliament became concerned that the Dutch, then hostile to England, might counter the piracy by occupying the islands, gaining a foothold they could then use against England.<sup id="cite_ref-EH_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EH-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 1651 Parliament sent Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Blake_(admiral)" title="Robert Blake (admiral)">Robert Blake</a> in charge of a naval task force to retake the islands.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Blake arrived at St Helen\'s Pool in April 1651, and set about invading the island of Tresco.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Taking the harbour of Old Grimsby in an amphibious assault on 18 April, he then simply bypassed King Charles\' Castle and marched south, using Tresco as a basis for then taking the neighbouring island of St Mary\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Royalist commander of the castle, William Edgecumbe, retreated from the castle on 19 April and the defenders blew up part of the site as they left, leaving the remains to the Parliamentary commander Colonel George Fleetwood.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Some of the castle\'s stonework appears to have been reused in the construction of the new Cromwell\'s Castle by the Parliamentarians, just beneath King Charles\'s Castle, the new fortification being sited in a much better position to defend the harbour.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1660, Charles II was restored to the throne and Edward Sherburne was sent to the Scilly Isles to inspect the defences.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011bP30_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011bP30-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He recommended that repairs be carried out to King Charles\'s Castle, which was then being used to house soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011bP30_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011bP30-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="18th.E2.80.9321st_centuries"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="18th\xe2\x80\x9321st_centuries">18th\xe2\x80\x9321st centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Charles%27s_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 18th\xe2\x80\x9321st centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Grimsby_Harbour,_1756.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/New_Grimsby_Harbour%2C_1756.png/300px-New_Grimsby_Harbour%2C_1756.png" width="300" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/New_Grimsby_Harbour%2C_1756.png/450px-New_Grimsby_Harbour%2C_1756.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/New_Grimsby_Harbour%2C_1756.png/600px-New_Grimsby_Harbour%2C_1756.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="568" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Grimsby_Harbour,_1756.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Grimsby harbour in 1756, showing King Charles\'s Castle (right), overlooking Cromwell\'s Castle (centre)</div></div></div>\n<p>The Crown, in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a>, leased the islands in 1687 to the Godolphin family, followed by Augustus Smith in 1834.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Antiquary" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiquary">antiquary</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Borlase" title="William Borlase">William Borlase</a> was critical of the castle\xe2\x80\x94by then in ruins\xe2\x80\x94during his 1752 visit, describing it as a "work of labour and expense, rather than of skill", and attributing its abandonment to its poor military utility.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The writer John Troutbeck, commenting at the end of the century, took a similar perspective, praising the thickness of the walls, but noting that it was ill-positioned to fire into the harbour, unlike its replacement below.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1922, the lease passed to <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Algernon_Dorrien-Smith" title="Arthur Algernon Dorrien-Smith">Arthur Dorrien-Smith</a>, who agreed to pass several properties on Tresco, including the castle, into the guardianship of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Works_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Works (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Works</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The castle was partially <a href="/wiki/Excavation_(archaeology)" title="Excavation (archaeology)">excavated</a> in 1954, uncovering coins, pottery and a buckle.<sup id="cite_ref-HeritageGateway_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeritageGateway-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Parts of the first floor of the battery were reconstructed from the fallen stonework uncovered during the dig.<sup id="cite_ref-list.english-heritage.org.uk_resultsingle_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-list.english-heritage.org.uk_resultsingle-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 21st century, the blockhouse is controlled by <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a>, the successor to the Ministry, and open to tourists.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryandResearch_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryandResearch-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It is protected as a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_monument" title="Scheduled monument">scheduled monument</a> and a Grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a> under UK law.<sup id="cite_ref-HeritageGateway_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeritageGateway-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Charles%27s_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Charles%27s_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:438px;max-width:438px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:232px;max-width:232px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:King_Charles%27s_Castle_earthworks.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" /></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:King_Charles%27s_Castle_plan.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/King_Charles%27s_Castle_plan.png/200px-King_Charles%27s_Castle_plan.png" width="200" height="174" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/King_Charles%27s_Castle_plan.png/300px-King_Charles%27s_Castle_plan.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/King_Charles%27s_Castle_plan.png/400px-King_Charles%27s_Castle_plan.png 2x" data-file-width="1969" data-file-height="1715" /></a></div></div><div style="clear:left"></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">The castle and its earthworks (left); detailed plan (right): A - bedroom; B - gun battery; C - dining room; D - kitchen; E - guardroom; F - bedroom</div></div></div>\n<p>King Charles\'s Castle is a polygonal stone building, composed of a gun battery on one side and living quarters on the other.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011bPP8ListEntry_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011bPP8ListEntry-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> On the west side, overlooking the sea, is a large room which originally contained the battery, with embrasures for five guns.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011bPP89_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011bPP89-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> At some point after its initial construction, the north-east gun <a href="/wiki/Embrasure" title="Embrasure">embrasure</a> was blocked by the construction of an internal chamber within the gun battery, 4.5 metres (15&#160;ft) by 2.3 metres (7&#160;ft 7&#160;in), with the embrasure being opened up to form a window.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011bPP89_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011bPP89-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Behind the battery is a large room, 8.8 metres (29&#160;ft) by 6 metres (20&#160;ft), forming a hall and kitchen, originally for the use of the garrison and containing a fireplace and oven.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The room has two bedrooms leading off it, each 2.9 metres (9&#160;ft 6&#160;in) square.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011bPP8ListEntry_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011bPP8ListEntry-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The north bedroom may have been heated by a small <a href="/wiki/Brazier" title="Brazier">brazier</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> A guardroom, 3.5 metres (11&#160;ft) square, forms the entrance to the building, leading into the hall.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011bPP8ListEntry_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011bPP8ListEntry-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The castle is made from <a href="/wiki/Ashlar" title="Ashlar">dressed</a> and rubble <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, incorporating an underling rocky outcrop into the design in the north-west and south-west corners.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In places the walls survive up to 3.4 metres (11&#160;ft) high and 1.65 metres (5&#160;ft 5&#160;in) thick, but are on average only 1.5 metres (4&#160;ft 11&#160;in) tall and 0.8 metres (2&#160;ft 7&#160;in) thick.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Opinions vary as to how tall the castle was when first built; it may have been two storeys high throughout, or may only have been that height in the gun battery, with the living accommodation being a single storey construction.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011BHistoryandResearch_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011BHistoryandResearch-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;nb 2&#93;</a></sup> The upper storey of the gun battery would have held additional guns.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Architecturally, the castle is unusual for this period.<sup id="cite_ref-ONeil1961P20_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeil1961P20-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Its design appears somewhat backward, particularly in comparison to the nearby fortification of <a href="/wiki/Harry%27s_Walls" title="Harry&#39;s Walls">Harry\'s Walls</a>, built at the same time as the castle, but which adopted a more contemporary design that employed <a href="/wiki/Bastions" class="mw-redirect" title="Bastions">bastions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryandResearch_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryandResearch-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Its closest equivalents are the blockhouses built by Henry VIII on the River Thames.<sup id="cite_ref-ONeil1961P20_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeil1961P20-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthworks">Earthworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Charles%27s_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Earthworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The castle is surrounded by a rectangular earthwork, probably built in 1627, and similar to that at <a href="/wiki/Sandsfoot_Castle" title="Sandsfoot Castle">Sandsfoot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pendennis_Castle" title="Pendennis Castle">Pendennis Castles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011BP10Pastscape_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011BP10Pastscape-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> It stretches away north-east from the castle, with an earth and rubble bank up to 2 metres (6&#160;ft 7&#160;in) high and 8 metres (26&#160;ft) wide, with a bastion and demi-bastion at its corners and a ditch, up to 0.4 metres (1&#160;ft 4&#160;in) deep and 3 metres (9.8&#160;ft) wide along the north and east sides.<sup id="cite_ref-BowdenBrodie2011BP10Pastscape_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowdenBrodie2011BP10Pastscape-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another line of earthworks survives around 40 metres (130&#160;ft) south-east of these earthworks; its bank is 0.3 metres (1&#160;ft 0&#160;in) high, with a ditch up to 0.2 metres (7.9&#160;in) deep.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowden2011P8_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowden2011P8-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> It lies just behind the crest of the hill, possibly for concealment, and is defended with bastions and orillons although it appears not to have been finished.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowden2011P8_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowden2011P8-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> It probably dates from the mid-16th century, possibly having been built at around the same time as the castle.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowden2011P8_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowden2011P8-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
