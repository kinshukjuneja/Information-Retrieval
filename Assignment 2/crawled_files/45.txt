Alphabet.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Irish Sign Language</b> (<b>ISL</b>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Teanga Chomhartha\xc3\xadochta na h\xc3\x89ireann</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">sign language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, used primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>. It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, alongside <a href="/wiki/British_Sign_Language" title="British Sign Language">British Sign Language</a> (BSL). Irish Sign Language is more closely related to <a href="/wiki/French_Sign_Language" title="French Sign Language">French Sign Language</a> (LSF) than to <a href="/wiki/British_Sign_Language" title="British Sign Language">BSL</a>, though it has influence from both languages. It has influenced sign languages in Australia and South Africa, and has little relation to either spoken <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> or <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Sign_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Deaf_Society" title="Irish Deaf Society">Irish Deaf Society</a> says that ISL "arose from within deaf communities", "was developed by deaf people themselves" and "has been in existence for hundreds of years". According to <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a>, the language has influence from both LSF and BSL, as well as from <a href="/wiki/Signed_French" title="Signed French">signed French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Signed_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Signed English">signed English</a>, BSL having been introduced in Dublin in 1816.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The first school for deaf children in Ireland was established in 1816 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Orpen" title="Charles Orpen">Dr. Charles Orpen</a>. The Claremont Institute was a <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> institution and given that Ireland was a part of the United Kingdom, it is no surprise that BSL (or some version of <a href="/wiki/Signed_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Signed English">signed English</a> based in BSL) was used for teaching and learning (Pollard 2006). McDonnell (1979) reports that the Irish institutions - <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Catholic</a> and Protestant - did not teach the children to speak and it was not until 1887 that Claremont report changing from a manual to an <a href="/wiki/Oralism" title="Oralism">oral approach</a>. For the Catholic schools, the shift to oralism came later: St. Mary\'s School for Deaf Girls moved to an oral approach in 1946 and St. Joseph\'s School for Deaf Boys shifted to oralism in 1956,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> though this did not become formal state policy until 1972. Sign language use was seriously suppressed and religion was used to further stigmatise the language (e.g. children were encouraged to give up signing for <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a> and sent to <a href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Reconciliation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacrament of Reconciliation">confession</a> if caught signing).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The fact that the Catholic schools are segregated on the basis of gender led to the development of a gendered-generational variant of Irish Sign Language that is still evident (albeit to a lesser degree) today.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>ISL was brought by Catholic missionaries to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>,and to Scotland and England, with remnants of ISL still visible in some variants of BSL, especially in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, and with some elderly <a href="/wiki/Auslan" title="Auslan">Auslan</a> Catholics still using ISL today. In South Africa, the Dominican nuns who established Catholic Schools saw a need for a school for the deaf, but due to resource constraints were not in a position to do this immediately. Instead, they wrote back to their Mother House in Cabra requesting an experienced teacher of the deaf. A deaf teacher, Ms. Bridget Lynne, responded. Remnants of gendered generational Irish Sign Language are thought to still be visible in some dialects of South African Sign Language<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>, which can probably be traced back to Ms. Lynne. \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oireachtas_bill">Oireachtas bill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Sign_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Oireachtas bill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The "Recognition of Irish Sign Language for the Deaf Community Bill 2016" passed all stages in the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> (Irish Parliament) on 14 December 2017, and was signed into law by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Higgins" title="Michael D. Higgins">Michael D Higgins</a> on 24 December 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Bill  ensures that public services are available through ISL and also outlines the need for greater access to education through sign language. Prior to the passage, there was no automatic right for deaf people to have an ISL <a href="/wiki/Language_interpretation" title="Language interpretation">interpreter</a> (except for criminal court proceedings). For the deaf community, recognition of ISL means more legal rights and better access to <a href="/wiki/Public_service" title="Public service">public services</a> - including <a href="/wiki/Education_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Education in the Republic of Ireland">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Healthcare in the Republic of Ireland">healthcare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Media_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Media of the Republic of Ireland">media</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banking" class="mw-redirect" title="Banking">banking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_code">Language code</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Sign_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language code">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-3" title="ISO 639-3">ISO 639-3</a> code for Irish Sign Language is \'isg\'; \'isl\' is the code for <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Sign_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Irish_manual_alphabet" title="Irish manual alphabet">Irish manual alphabet</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Auslan" title="Auslan">Australian Sign Language</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Sign_Language" title="Northern Ireland Sign Language">Northern Ireland Sign Language</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/South_African_Sign_Language" title="South African Sign Language">South African Sign Language</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Sign_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-e18-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-e18_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.ethnologue.com/18/language/isg/">Irish Sign Language</a> at <i><a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a></i> (18th ed., 2015)</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-Glottolog-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Glottolog_2-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Hammarstr\xc3\xb6m, Harald;
