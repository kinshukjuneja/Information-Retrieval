Luminais</a>, 1884 (Mus\xc3\xa9e des Beaux-Arts, <a href="/wiki/Quimper" title="Quimper">Quimper</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Ys</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="/i\xcb\x90/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">i\xcb\x90</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">EESS</span></i></a>), also spelled <b>Is</b> or <b>K\xc3\xaar-Is</b> in <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> (<i>k\xc3\xaar</i> is the Breton word for "city", see <a href="/wiki/Caer" title="Caer">caer</a>), and <b>Ville d\'Ys</b> in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_mythological_places" title="List of mythological places">mythical city</a> that was built on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and later swallowed by the ocean.  Most versions of the legend place the city in the <a href="/wiki/Baie_de_Douarnenez" title="Baie de Douarnenez">Baie de Douarnenez</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-breton_tradition_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-breton_tradition-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_legend">The legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Ys was built on land reclaimed from the sea<sup id="cite_ref-breton_tradition_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-breton_tradition-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Gradlon" title="Gradlon">Gradlon</a> (Gralon in <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>), King of <a href="/wiki/Cornouaille" title="Cornouaille">Cornouaille</a> (Kerne in Breton), upon the request of his daughter <a href="/wiki/Dahut" title="Dahut">Dahut</a> (also called Ahes),<sup id="cite_ref-women_celts_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-women_celts-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> who loved the sea. To protect Ys from inundation, a <a href="/wiki/Dike_(construction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dike (construction)">dike</a> was built with a gate that was opened for ships during low tide. The one key that opened the gate was held by the king.<sup id="cite_ref-romances_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romances-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ys is described as a city rich in commerce and the arts, with Gradlon\'s palace being made of marble, cedar and gold.<sup id="cite_ref-romances_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romances-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other versions of the legend tell that Ys was founded more than 2,000 years before Gradlon\'s reign in a then-dry location off the current coast of the Bay of Douarnenez, but the Breton coast had slowly given way to the sea so that Ys was under it at each high tide when Gradlon\'s reign began.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Most versions of the legend present Gradlon as a pious man with a wayward daughter, princess Dahut, who "had made a crown of her vices and taken for her pages the seven capital sins."<sup id="cite_ref-romances_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romances-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\nPrincess Dahut had a lover for whom she threw a secret banquet and, under the influence of wine, she stole the key to the gate from her father and opened the gate, and the water submerged the entire city.<sup id="cite_ref-romances_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romances-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Another version of the legend says that she stole the silver key to admit her lover, mistakenly opening the sluices in the dark.<sup id="cite_ref-romances_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romances-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Winwaloe" title="Winwaloe">St Gw\xc3\xa9nnol\xc3\xa9</a>, who, according to one version, had foretold the city\'s ruin due to its luxury, woke the king and commanded him to flee. He mounted his horse and took his daughter with him. As the water was about to overtake him, a voice called out: "Throw the demon thou carriest into the sea, if thou dost not desire to perish." Dahut fell from the horse\'s back, and Gradlon was saved.<sup id="cite_ref-romances_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romances-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Though this is the most common version, there is an ancient ballad that blames Gradlon himself for leading his people to extravagances of every kind and says that Dahut received the key from him.<sup id="cite_ref-romances_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romances-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="The_devil_as_Dahut.27s_lover"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_devil_as_Dahut\'s_lover">The devil as Dahut\'s lover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The devil as Dahut&#039;s lover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another version of the legend directly identifies Dahut\'s lover with the devil. Ys was the most beautiful and impressive city in Europe, but quickly became a city of sin under the influence of Dahut. She organized <a href="/wiki/Orgy" title="Orgy">orgies</a> and had the habit of killing her lovers when morning broke. <a href="/wiki/Winwaloe" title="Winwaloe">Saint Winwaloe</a> decried the corruption of Ys and warned of God\'s wrath and punishment, but was ignored by Dahut and the populace.\n</p><p>One day, a knight dressed in red came to Ys. Dahut asked him to come with her, and one night, he agreed. A storm broke out in the middle of the night and the waves could be heard smashing against the gate and the bronze walls. Dahut said to the knight: "Let the storm rage.  The gates of the city are strong, and it is King Gradlon, my father, who owns the only key, attached to his neck." The knight replied: "Your father the king sleeps. You can now easily take his key." Dahut stole the key from her father and gave it to the knight, who was none other than the <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">devil</a>. The devil then opened the gate.\n</p><p>Because the gate was open during storm and at high tide, a wave as high as a mountain collapsed on Ys. King Gradlon and his daughter climbed on Morvarc\'h, his magical horse. Saint Winwaloe approached them and told Gradlon: "Push back the demon sitting behind you!" Gradlon initially refused, but he finally gave in and pushed his daughter into the sea. The sea swallowed Dahut, who became a mermaid or <a href="/wiki/Morgen_(mythological_creature)" title="Morgen (mythological creature)">morgen</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Gradlon took refuge in <a href="/wiki/Quimper,_Finist%C3%A8re" class="mw-redirect" title="Quimper, Finist\xc3\xa8re">Quimper</a>, which became his new capital. An <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_statue" title="Equestrian statue">equestrian statue</a> of Gradlon still stands between the spires of the Cathedral of Saint Corentin in Quimper. Folklore asserts that the bells of the churches of Ys can still be heard in the calm sea. A legend says that when <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> is swallowed, the city of Ys will rise up from under the waves: <i><i lang="br" title="Breton language text">Pa vo beuzet Paris, Ec\'h adsavo Ker Is</i></i>  (<i>Par-Is</i> meaning "similar to Ys" in Breton).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">Interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This history is also sometimes viewed as the victory of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> over <a href="/wiki/Druidism" class="mw-redirect" title="Druidism">druidism</a>, as Gradlon was converted by Saint Winwaloe. Dahut and most inhabitants of Ys were worshippers of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic gods</a>. However, another Breton folktale asserts that Gradlon met, spoke with and consoled the last <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">Druid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, and oversaw his pagan burial, before building a chapel in his sacred grove.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_the_legend">Development of the legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development of the legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While legends and literature about Gradlon are much older, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Graelent" title="Graelent">Lai de Graelent</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Marie_de_France" title="Marie de France">Marie de France</a> probably written in the late 12th century, the story of Ys appears to have developed between the end of the fifteenth century and the seventeenth century. An early mention of Ys appears in <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Le_Baud" title="Pierre Le Baud">Pierre Le Baud</a>\'s <i>Cronicques et ystoires des Bretons</i> in which Gradlon is the king of the city, but Dahut is not mentioned. Bernard d\'Argentre\'s <i>La histoire de Bretagne</i> and mystery plays on the life of <a href="/wiki/St._Winwaloe" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Winwaloe">St. Winwaloe</a>, in the sixteenth century, also provide early references to the city.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The version of the story of Ys which appears in Albert Le Grand\'s <i>Vie des Saincts de la Bretagne Armorique</i> published in the seventeenth century already contains all the basic elements of the later story<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and has been said to be the first.<sup id="cite_ref-breton_tradition_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-breton_tradition-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The legend of Ys was confined to the folk of Brittany until 1839, when <a href="/wiki/T._Hersart_de_la_Villemarqu%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="T. Hersart de la Villemarqu\xc3\xa9">T. Hersart de la Villemarqu\xc3\xa9</a> published a collection of popular songs collected from <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral tradition</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Barzaz_Breizh" class="mw-redirect" title="Barzaz Breizh">Barzaz Breizh</a></i>. The collection achieved a wide distribution and brought Breton folk culture into European awareness. In the second edition, the poem "Livaden Geris" ("The Submersion of Ker-Is") appeared. The same basic story elements are present, but in this version the holy man is instead <a href="/wiki/Corentin_of_Quimper" title="Corentin of Quimper">St. Corentin</a>.\n</p><p>It appears that elements of the text of this version were adapted from the medieval Welsh poem "Seithennin" about the legend of <a href="/wiki/Cantre%27r_Gwaelod" title="Cantre&#39;r Gwaelod">Cantre\'r Gwaelod</a>, a very similar Welsh legend about a land that disappeared beneath the ocean as a result of human error. In this version, Dahut steals the key at the incitement of a lover. Also, here the element of Dahut as a mermaid or <a href="/wiki/Morgen_(mythological_creature)" title="Morgen (mythological creature)">morgen</a> has appeared as the last verses of the song refer to a fisherman seeing a mermaid combing her hair and singing a sad song.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Emile_Souvestre" class="mw-redirect" title="Emile Souvestre">Emile Souvestre</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Foyer_breton" title="Le Foyer breton">Le Foyer breton</a></i> also played a great part in making the legend widely known, and many 19th century English tellings of the story are closely derived from the <i>Foyer Breton\'</i>s tale "Keris".  In Souvestre\'s telling, the character of the Devil disguised as a man with a red beard has appeared.<sup id="cite_ref-breton_tradition_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-breton_tradition-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations_in_the_arts">Adaptations in the arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Adaptations in the arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Le_Roi_d%27Ys_Poster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Le_Roi_d%27Ys_Poster.jpg/200px-Le_Roi_d%27Ys_Poster.jpg" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/Le_Roi_d%27Ys_Poster.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="252" data-file-height="336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Le_Roi_d%27Ys_Poster.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Poster for <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Lalo" title="\xc3\x89douard Lalo">\xc3\x89douard Lalo</a>\'s 1888 opera, <i><a href="/wiki/Le_roi_d%27Ys" title="Le roi d&#39;Ys">Le roi d\'Ys</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>Several famous artistic adaptations of the Ys legend appeared in the late 19th and early 20th century.\n<a href="/wiki/E._V._Luminais" class="mw-redirect" title="E. V. Luminais">E. V. Luminais</a>\' painting <i>Flight of King Gradlon</i>, depicting Gradlon\'s escape from Ys, scored a success at the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Salon" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris Salon">Salon</a> of 1884.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Le_roi_d%27Ys" title="Le roi d&#39;Ys">Le roi d\'Ys</a></i>, an opera by the <a href="/wiki/Chronological_list_of_French_classical_composers" title="Chronological list of French classical composers">French composer</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Lalo" title="\xc3\x89douard Lalo">\xc3\x89douard Lalo</a> which premiered in 1888, transforms the story significantly, replacing the figure of Dahut with Margared, whose motive for opening the gates (with the aid of her own betrothed Karnac) is her jealousy at her sister Rozenn\'s marriage to Mylio (characters who are also inventions of Lalo).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Also inspired by the story of Ys is <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">Claude Debussy</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/La_cath%C3%A9drale_engloutie" title="La cath\xc3\xa9drale engloutie">La cath\xc3\xa9drale engloutie</a></i>, found in his first book of <i><a href="/wiki/Preludes_(Debussy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Preludes (Debussy)">Preludes</a></i> (published 1910). This is a <a href="/wiki/Prelude_(music)" title="Prelude (music)">prelude</a> intended to evoke the atmosphere of the legend by its sound.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The story is also an element in <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Blok" title="Alexander Blok">Alexander Blok</a>\'s 1912 verse drama <i>The Rose and the Cross</i>.\n</p><p>The city of Ys and the character Dahut feature prominently in the 1934 novel <i>Creep, Shadow!</i> by <a href="/wiki/A._Merritt" title="A. Merritt">A. Merritt</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Poul_Anderson" title="Poul Anderson">Poul Anderson</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Karen_Anderson_(writer)" title="Karen Anderson (writer)">Karen Anderson</a> retold the story in the tetralogy <i>The King of Ys</i> in the 1980s. They pictured Gradlon as a Roman soldier named Gratillonius.\n</p><p>The name Dahut and certain thematic elements of the story of Ys can be found in the survival/exploration noir game <i><a href="/wiki/Sunless_Sea" title="Sunless Sea">Sunless Sea</a></i>.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_King_in_Yellow" title="The King in Yellow">The King in Yellow</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_W._Chambers" title="Robert W. Chambers">Robert W. Chambers</a> contains the story "<a href="/wiki/The_Demoiselle_d%27Ys" title="The Demoiselle d&#39;Ys">The Demoiselle d\'Ys</a>".\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alan_Stivell" title="Alan Stivell">Alan Stivell</a>\'s renowned album "<a href="/wiki/Renaissance_of_the_Celtic_Harp" title="Renaissance of the Celtic Harp">Renaissance of the Celtic Harp</a>" opens with a track entitled "Ys".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
