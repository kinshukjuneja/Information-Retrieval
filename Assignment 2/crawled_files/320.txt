Isostatic rebound is still underway here.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Post-glacial rebound</b> (also called <b>isostatic rebound</b> or <b>crustal rebound</b>) is the rise of land masses after the lifting of the huge weight of <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, which had caused <a href="/wiki/Isostatic_depression" title="Isostatic depression">isostatic depression</a>. Post-glacial rebound and isostatic depression are phases of <b>glacial isostasy</b> (<b>glacial isostatic adjustment</b>, <b>glacioisostasy</b>), the  deformation of the Earth\'s crust in response to changes in ice mass distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-MilneOthers2013a_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MilneOthers2013a-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The direct raising effects of post-glacial rebound are readily apparent in parts of Northern <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_America" title="Northern America">Northern America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. However, through the processes of <i>ocean siphoning</i> and <i>continental levering</i>, the effects of post-glacial rebound on <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> are felt globally far from the locations of current and former ice sheets.<sup id="cite_ref-MilneOthers2008a_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MilneOthers2008a-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_showing_historical_levels_of_Lake_Superior.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e4/Diagram_showing_historical_levels_of_Lake_Superior.jpg/310px-Diagram_showing_historical_levels_of_Lake_Superior.jpg" width="310" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e4/Diagram_showing_historical_levels_of_Lake_Superior.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="334" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_showing_historical_levels_of_Lake_Superior.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Changes in the elevation of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> due to glaciation and post-glacial rebound</div></div></div>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, much of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> was covered by <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, which reached up to three kilometres thick during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial maximum">glacial maximum</a> about 20,000 years ago.  The enormous weight of this ice caused the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> to deform and warp downward, forcing the <a href="/wiki/Viscoelastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Viscoelastic">viscoelastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> material to flow away from the loaded region.  At the end of each <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial period</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> retreated, the removal of this weight led to slow (and still ongoing) uplift or rebound of the land and the return flow of mantle material back under the <a href="/wiki/Deglaciation" title="Deglaciation">deglaciated</a> area.  Due to the extreme <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the mantle, it will take many thousands of years for the land to reach an <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">equilibrium</a> level.\n</p><p>The uplift has taken place in two distinct stages.  The initial uplift following deglaciation was almost immediate due to the <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elastic</a> response of the crust as the ice load was removed.  After this elastic phase, uplift proceeded by slow viscous flow at an <a href="/wiki/Exponential_decay" title="Exponential decay">exponentially</a> decreasing rate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Today, typical uplift rates are of the order of 1&#160;cm/year or less. In northern Europe, this is clearly shown by the <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> data obtained by the BIFROST GPS network.<sup id="cite_ref-johansson-2002_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johansson-2002-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Studies suggest that rebound will continue for at least another 10,000 years.  The total uplift from the end of deglaciation depends on the local ice load and could be several hundred metres near the centre of rebound.\n</p><p>Recently, the term "post-glacial rebound" is gradually being replaced by the term "glacial isostatic adjustment". This is in recognition that the response of the Earth to glacial loading and unloading is not limited to the upward rebound movement, but also involves downward land movement, horizontal crustal motion,<sup id="cite_ref-johansson-2002_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johansson-2002-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sella-etal-2007_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sella-etal-2007-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> changes in global sea levels<sup id="cite_ref-peltier-1998_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peltier-1998-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the Earth\'s gravity field,<sup id="cite_ref-mitrovica-peltier-1993_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitrovica-peltier-1993-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> induced earthquakes,<sup id="cite_ref-wu-johnston-2000_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wu-johnston-2000-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and changes in the Earth\'s rotation.<sup id="cite_ref-wu-peltier-1984_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wu-peltier-1984-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Another alternate term is "glacial isostasy", because the uplift near the centre of rebound is due to the tendency towards the restoration of isostatic equilibrium (as in the case of isostasy of mountains). Unfortunately, that term gives the wrong impression that isostatic equilibrium is somehow reached, so by appending "adjustment" at the end, the motion of restoration is emphasized.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Post-glacial rebound produces measurable effects on vertical crustal motion, global sea levels, horizontal crustal motion, gravity field, Earth\'s rotation, crustal stress, and earthquakes. Studies of glacial rebound give us information about the flow law of mantle rocks, which is important to the study of mantle convection, <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> and the thermal evolution of the Earth.  It also gives insight into past ice sheet history, which is important to <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a>, and changes in global sea level. Understanding postglacial rebound is also important to our ability to monitor recent global change.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_crustal_motion">Vertical crustal motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vertical crustal motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png/200px-Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png" width="200" height="384" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png/300px-Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png/400px-Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png 2x" data-file-width="849" data-file-height="1632" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Suomi_jaakauden_jalkeen.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Much of modern <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> is former seabed or archipelago: illustrated are sea levels immediately after the last ice age.</div></div></div>\n<p>Erratic <a href="/wiki/Boulder" title="Boulder">boulders</a>, <a href="/wiki/U-shaped_valley" title="U-shaped valley">U-shaped valleys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drumlin" title="Drumlin">drumlins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esker" title="Esker">eskers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kettle_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Kettle lake">kettle lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a> <a href="/wiki/Glacial_striation" title="Glacial striation">striations</a> are among the common signatures of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>. In addition, post-glacial rebound has caused numerous significant changes to coastlines and landscapes over the last several thousand years, and the effects continue to be significant.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, Lake <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A4laren" title="M\xc3\xa4laren">M\xc3\xa4laren</a> was formerly an arm of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, but uplift eventually cut it off and led to its becoming a <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> in about the 12th century, at the time when <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> was founded at <a href="/wiki/Slussen" class="mw-redirect" title="Slussen">its outlet</a>. Marine seashells found in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a> sediments imply a similar event in prehistoric times. Other pronounced effects can be seen on the island of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96land" title="\xc3\x96land">\xc3\x96land</a>, Sweden, which has little topographic relief due to the presence of the very level <a href="/wiki/Stora_Alvaret" title="Stora Alvaret">Stora Alvaret</a>.  The rising land has caused the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> settlement area to recede from the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, making the present day villages on the west coast set back unexpectedly far from the shore.  These effects are quite dramatic at the village of <a href="/wiki/Alby,_%C3%96land" title="Alby, \xc3\x96land">Alby</a>, for example, where the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> inhabitants were known to subsist on substantial coastal fishing.\n</p><p>As a result of post-glacial rebound, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Bothnia" title="Gulf of Bothnia">Gulf of Bothnia</a> is predicted to eventually close up at <a href="/wiki/Kvarken" title="Kvarken">Kvarken</a> in more than 2,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kvarken" title="Kvarken">Kvarken</a> is a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Natural_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Natural Heritage Site">World Natural Heritage Site</a>, selected as a "type area" illustrating the effects of post-glacial rebound and the <a href="/wiki/Holocene_glacial_retreat" title="Holocene glacial retreat">holocene glacial retreat</a>.\n</p><p>In several other <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Nordic Countries">Nordic</a> ports, like <a href="/wiki/Tornio" title="Tornio">Tornio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pori" title="Pori">Pori</a> (formerly at <a href="/wiki/Ulvila" title="Ulvila">Ulvila</a>), the harbour has had to be relocated several times. Place names in the coastal regions also illustrate the rising land: there are inland places named \'island\', \'skerry\', \'rock\', \'point\' and \'sound\'. For example, <a href="/wiki/Oulunsalo" title="Oulunsalo">Oulunsalo</a> "island of <a href="/wiki/Oulujoki" title="Oulujoki">Oulujoki</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-ouloujoki_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ouloujoki-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> is a peninsula, with inland names such as <i>Koivukari</i> "Birch Rock", <i>Santaniemi</i> "Sandy Cape", and <i>Salmioja</i> "the brook of the Sound". (Compare  <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://kaino.kotus.fi/www/verkkojulkaisut/julk125/oulunsalo/oulunsalo_ennen.shtml">[1]</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://kaino.kotus.fi/www/verkkojulkaisut/julk125/oulunsalo/oulunsalo_nyt.shtml">[2]</a>.)\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG/200px-Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG" width="200" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG/300px-Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG/400px-Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1848" data-file-height="2145" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Post-glacial_rebound_in_British_Isles.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Post Glacial Rebound effects upon the land-level on the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, glaciation affected <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> but not southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and the post-glacial rebound of northern Great Britain (up to 10&#160;cm per century) is causing a corresponding downward movement of the southern half of the island (up to 5&#160;cm per century). This will eventually lead to an increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a> in southern England and south-western Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the glacial isostatic adjustment process causes the land to move relative to the sea, ancient shorelines are found to lie above present day sea level in areas that were once glaciated. On the other hand, places in the peripheral bulge area which was uplifted during glaciation now begins to subside. Therefore, ancient beaches are found below present day sea level in the bulge area. The "relative sea level data", which consists of height and age measurements of the ancient beaches around the world,  tells us that glacial isostatic adjustment proceeded at a higher rate near the end of deglaciation than today.\n</p><p>The present-day uplift motion in northern Europe is also monitored by a <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> network called BIFROST.<sup id="cite_ref-johansson-2002_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johansson-2002-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bifrost-harvard_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bifrost-harvard-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bifrost_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bifrost-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Results of GPS data show a peak rate of about 11&#160;mm/year in the north part of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Bothnia" title="Gulf of Bothnia">Gulf of Bothnia</a>, but this uplift rate decreases away and becomes negative outside the former ice margin.\n</p><p>In the near field outside the former ice margin, the land sinks relative to the sea. This is the case along the east coast of the United States, where ancient beaches are found submerged below present day sea level and Florida is expected to be submerged in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-peltier-1998_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peltier-1998-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> GPS data in North America also confirms that land uplift becomes subsidence outside the former ice margin.<sup id="cite_ref-sella-etal-2007_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sella-etal-2007-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_sea_levels">Global sea levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Global sea levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>To form the ice sheets of the last Ice Age, water from the oceans evaporated, condensed as snow and was deposited as ice in high latitudes. Thus global sea level fell during glaciation.\n</p><p>The ice sheets at the last <a href="/wiki/Glacial_maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial maximum">glacial maximum</a> were so massive that global sea level fell by about 120 metres. Thus continental shelves were exposed and many islands became connected with the continents through dry land. This was the case between the British Isles and Europe (<a href="/wiki/Doggerland" title="Doggerland">Doggerland</a>), or between Taiwan, the Indonesian islands and Asia (<a href="/wiki/Sundaland" title="Sundaland">Sundaland</a>). A <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">sub-continent also existed between Siberia and Alaska</a> that allowed the migration of people and animals during the last glacial maximum.<sup id="cite_ref-peltier-1998_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peltier-1998-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fall in sea level also affects the circulation of ocean currents and thus has important impact on climate during the glacial maximum.\n</p><p>During deglaciation, the melted ice water returns to the oceans, thus <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level in the ocean increases</a> again. However, geological records of sea level changes show that the redistribution of the melted ice water is not the same everywhere in the oceans. In other words, depending upon the location, the rise in sea level at a certain site may be more than that at another site. This is due to the gravitational attraction between the mass of the melted water and the other masses, such as remaining ice sheets, glaciers, water masses and mantle rocks<sup id="cite_ref-peltier-1998_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peltier-1998-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the changes in centrifugal potential due to Earth\'s variable rotation.<sup id="cite_ref-mitrovica-milne-davis-2001_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitrovica-milne-davis-2001-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horizontal_crustal_motion">Horizontal crustal motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Horizontal crustal motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Accompanying vertical motion is the horizontal motion of the crust. The BIFROST GPS network<sup id="cite_ref-bifrost_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bifrost-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> shows that the motion diverges from the centre of rebound.<sup id="cite_ref-johansson-2002_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johansson-2002-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, the largest horizontal velocity is found near the former ice margin.\n</p><p>The situation in North America is less certain; this is due to the sparse distribution of GPS stations in northern Canada, which is rather inaccessible.<sup id="cite_ref-sella-etal-2007_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sella-etal-2007-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tilt">Tilt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tilt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The combination of horizontal and vertical motion changes the tilt of the surface. That is, locations farther north rise faster, an effect that becomes apparent in lakes. The bottoms of the lakes gradually tilt away from the direction of the former ice maximum, such that lake shores on the side of the maximum (typically north) recede and the opposite (southern) shores sink.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This causes the formation of new rapids and rivers. For example, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pielinen" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Pielinen">Lake Pielinen</a>, which is large (90 x 30&#160;km) and oriented perpendicularly to the former ice margin, originally drained through an outlet in the middle of the lake near Nunnanlahti to Lake <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B6yti%C3%A4inen" title="H\xc3\xb6yti\xc3\xa4inen">H\xc3\xb6yti\xc3\xa4inen</a>. The change of tilt caused Pielinen to burst through the Uimaharju <a href="/wiki/Esker" title="Esker">esker</a> at the southwestern end of the lake, creating a new river (<a href="/wiki/Pielisjoki" title="Pielisjoki">Pielisjoki</a>) that runs to the sea via <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pyh%C3%A4selk%C3%A4" title="Lake Pyh\xc3\xa4selk\xc3\xa4">Lake Pyh\xc3\xa4selk\xc3\xa4</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Saimaa" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Saimaa">Lake Saimaa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The effects are similar to that concerning seashores, but occur above sea level. Tilting of land will also affect the flow of water in lakes and rivers in the future, and thus important for water resource management planning.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gravity_field">Gravity field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gravity field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Ice, water and mantle rocks have <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, and as they move around, they exert a gravitational pull on other masses towards them. Thus, the <a href="/wiki/Gravity_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravity field">gravity field</a>, which is sensitive to all mass on the surface and within the Earth, is affected by the redistribution of ice/melted water on the surface of the Earth and the flow of mantle rocks within.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Today, more than 6000 years after the last deglaciation terminated, the flow of <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> material back to the glaciated area causes the overall shape of the Earth to become less <a href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" class="mw-redirect" title="Oblate spheroid">oblate</a>. This change in the topography of Earth\'s surface affects the long-wavelength components of the gravity field.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The changing gravity field can be detected by repeated land measurements with absolute gravimeters and recently by the <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment">GRACE</a> satellite mission.<sup id="cite_ref-grace_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grace-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  The change in long-wavelength components of Earth\'s gravity field also perturbs the orbital motion of satellites and has been detected by <a href="/wiki/LAGEOS" title="LAGEOS">LAGEOS</a> satellite motion.<sup id="cite_ref-yoder-etal-1983_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yoder-etal-1983-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_datum">Vertical datum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vertical datum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The vertical <a href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Datum (geodesy)">datum</a> is a theoretical reference surface for altitude measurement and plays vital roles in many human activities, including land surveying and construction of buildings and bridges.  Since postglacial rebound continuously deforms the crustal surface and the gravitational field, the vertical datum needs to be redefined repeatedly through time.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="State_of_stress.2C_intraplate_earthquakes_and_volcanism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="State_of_stress,_intraplate_earthquakes_and_volcanism">State of stress, intraplate earthquakes and volcanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: State of stress, intraplate earthquakes and volcanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, plate-plate interaction results in earthquakes near plate boundaries. However, large earthquakes are found in intraplate environment like eastern Canada (up to M7) and northern Europe (up to M5) which are far away from present-day plate boundaries. An important intraplate earthquake was the magnitude 8 <a href="/wiki/1812_New_Madrid_earthquake" class="mw-redirect" title="1812 New Madrid earthquake">New Madrid earthquake</a> that occurred in mid-continental US in the year 1811.\n</p><p>Glacial loads provided more than 30 MPa of vertical stress in northern Canada and more than 20 MPa in northern Europe during glacial maximum. This vertical stress is supported by the mantle and the flexure of the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>. Since the mantle and the lithosphere continuously respond to the changing ice and water loads, the state of stress at any location continuously changes in time. The changes in the orientation of the state of stress is recorded in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Postglacial_faults&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Postglacial faults (page does not exist)">postglacial faults</a> in southeastern Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-wu-1996_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wu-1996-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> When the postglacial faults formed at the end of deglaciation 9000 years ago, the horizontal principal stress orientation was almost perpendicular to the former ice margin, but today the orientation is in the northeast-southwest, along the direction of <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>. This shows that the stress due to postglacial rebound had played an important role at deglacial time, but has gradually relaxed so that tectonic stress has become more dominant today.\n</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Mohr%E2%80%93Coulomb_theory" title="Mohr\xe2\x80\x93Coulomb theory">Mohr\xe2\x80\x93Coulomb theory</a> of  rock failure, large glacial loads generally suppress earthquakes, but  rapid deglaciation promotes earthquakes. According to Wu &amp; Hasagawa, the rebound stress that is available to trigger earthquakes today is of the order of 1 MPa.<sup id="cite_ref-wu-hasagawa-1996_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wu-hasagawa-1996-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This stress level is not large enough to rupture intact rocks but is large enough to reactivate pre-existing faults that are close to failure. Thus, both postglacial rebound and past tectonics play important roles in today\'s intraplate earthquakes in eastern Canada and southeast US.  Generally postglacial rebound stress could have triggered the intraplate earthquakes in eastern Canada and may have played some role in triggering earthquakes in the eastern US including the <a href="/wiki/1811%E2%80%9312_New_Madrid_earthquakes" title="1811\xe2\x80\x9312 New Madrid earthquakes">New Madrid earthquakes of 1811</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wu-johnston-2000_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wu-johnston-2000-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The situation in northern Europe today is complicated by the current tectonic activities nearby and by coastal loading and weakening.\n</p><p>Increasing pressure due to the weight of the ice during glaciation may have suppressed melt generation and volcanic activities below Iceland and Greenland. On the other hand, decreasing pressure due to deglaciation can increase the melt production and volcanic activities by 20-30 times.<sup id="cite_ref-Jull-McKenzie-1996_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jull-McKenzie-1996-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_global_warming">Recent global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Recent global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Recent <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> has caused mountain glaciers and the ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica to melt and global sea level to rise.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, monitoring <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> and the mass balance of ice sheets and glaciers allows people to understand more about global warming.\n</p><p>Recent rise in sea levels has been monitored by tide gauges and satellite altimetry (e.g. <a href="/wiki/TOPEX/Poseidon" title="TOPEX/Poseidon">TOPEX/Poseidon</a>).  As well as the addition of melted ice water from glaciers and ice sheets, recent sea level changes are affected by the thermal expansion of sea water due to global warming<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>, sea level change due to deglaciation of the last glacial maximum (postglacial sea level change), deformation of the land and ocean floor and other factors.  Thus, to understand global warming from sea level change, one must be able to separate all these factors, especially postglacial rebound, since it is one of the leading factors.\n</p><p>Mass changes of  ice sheets can be monitored by measuring changes in the ice surface height, the deformation of the ground below and the changes in the gravity field over the ice sheet. Thus <a href="/wiki/ICESat" title="ICESat">ICESat</a>, <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment">GRACE</a> satellite mission are useful for such purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-wahr-wingham-bentley-2000_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wahr-wingham-bentley-2000-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> However, glacial isostatic adjustment of the ice sheets affect ground deformation and the gravity field today. Thus understanding glacial isostatic adjustment is important in monitoring recent global warming.\n</p><p>One of the possible impacts of global warming-triggered rebound may be more volcanic activity in previously ice-capped areas such as Iceland and Greenland.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It may also trigger intraplate earthquakes near the ice margins of Greenland and Antarctica.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The speed and amount of postglacial rebound is determined by two factors: the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">rheology</a> (i.e., the flow) of the mantle, and the ice loading and unloading histories on the surface of Earth.\n</p><p>The viscosity of the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> is important in understanding <a href="/wiki/Mantle_convection" title="Mantle convection">mantle convection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, dynamical processes in Earth, the thermal state and thermal evolution of Earth. However viscosity is difficult to observe because creep experiments of mantle rocks take thousands of years to observe and the ambient temperature and pressure conditions are not easy to attain for a long enough time. Thus, the observations of postglacial rebound provide a natural experiment to measure mantle rheology. Modelling of glacial isostatic adjustment addresses the question of how viscosity changes in the radial<sup id="cite_ref-peltier-1998_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peltier-1998-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vermeersen-etal-1998_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vermeersen-etal-1998-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kaufmann-lambeck-2002_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaufmann-lambeck-2002-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and lateral directions<sup id="cite_ref-wang-wu-2006_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wang-wu-2006-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and whether the flow law is linear,  nonlinear,<sup id="cite_ref-karato-wu-1993_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-karato-wu-1993-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> or composite  rheology.<sup id="cite_ref-vanderwal-etal-2010_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanderwal-etal-2010-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ice thickness histories are useful in the study of <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciology</a> and paleo-oceanography. Ice thickness histories are traditionally deduced from the three types of information: First, the sea level data at stable sites far away from the centers of <a href="/wiki/Deglaciation" title="Deglaciation">deglaciation</a> give an estimate of how much water entered the oceans or equivalently how much ice was locked up at glacial maximum. Secondly, the location and dates of <a href="/wiki/Terminal_Moraines" class="mw-redirect" title="Terminal Moraines">terminal moraines</a> tell us the areal extent and retreat of past ice sheets. Physics of glaciers gives us the theoretical profile of ice sheets at equilibrium, it also says that the thickness and horizontal extent of <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/equilibrium" class="extiw" title="wikt:equilibrium">equilibrium</a> ice sheets are closely related to the basal condition of the ice sheets. Thus the volume of ice locked up is proportional to their instantaneous area. Finally, the heights of ancient beaches in the sea level data and observed land uplift rates (e.g. from <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> or <a href="/wiki/VLBI" class="mw-redirect" title="VLBI">VLBI</a>) can be used to <a href="/wiki/Constraint_(mathematics)" title="Constraint (mathematics)">constrain</a> local ice thickness. A popular ice model deduced this way is the ICE5G model.<sup id="cite_ref-peltier-2004_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peltier-2004-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Because the response of the Earth to changes in ice height is slow, it cannot record rapid fluctuation or surges of ice sheets, thus the ice sheet profiles deduced this way only gives the "average height" over a thousand years or so.<sup id="cite_ref-hughes-1998_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hughes-1998-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Glacial <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic</a> adjustment also plays an important role in understanding recent global warming and climate change.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before the eighteenth century, it was thought, in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, that sea levels were falling.  On the initiative of <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a> a number of marks were made in rock on different locations along the Swedish coast.  In 1765 it was possible to conclude that it was not a lowering of sea levels but an uneven rise of land.  In 1865 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jamieson" title="Thomas Jamieson">Thomas Jamieson</a> came up with a theory that the rise of land was connected with the ice age that had been first discovered in 1837.  The theory was accepted after investigations by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_De_Geer" title="Gerard De Geer">Gerard De Geer</a> of old shorelines in Scandinavia published in 1890<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_implications">Legal implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legal implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In areas where the rising of land is seen, it is necessary to define the exact limits of property. In Finland, the "new land" is legally the property of the owner of the water area, not any land owners on the shore. Therefore, if the owner of the land wishes to build a pier over the "new land", they need the permission of the owner of the (former) water area. The landowner of the shore may redeem the new land at market price.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Usually the owner of the water area is the <a href="/wiki/Partition_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Partition unit">partition unit</a> of the landowners of the shores, a collective holding corporation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Holocene_glacial_retreat" title="Holocene glacial retreat">Holocene glacial retreat</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Marine_terrace" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine terrace">Marine terrace</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Physical_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Physical impacts of climate change">Physical impacts of climate change</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">Stress (mechanics)</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-glacial_rebound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-MilneOthers2013a-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-MilneOthers2013a_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation encyclopaedia">Milne, G.; Shennan, I.
