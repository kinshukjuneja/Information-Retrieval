class="external text" href="https://www.worldcat.org/oclc/643483454">643483454</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><i><b>National Geographic</b></i> (formerly the <i><b>National Geographic Magazine</b></i> and branded also as <b>NAT GEO</b>) is the official magazine of the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a>. It has been published continuously since its first issue in 1888, nine months after the Society itself was founded. It primarily contains articles about science, geography, history, and world culture. The magazine is known for its thick square-bound glossy format with a yellow rectangular border and its extensive use of dramatic photographs. <a href="/wiki/Controlling_interest" title="Controlling interest">Controlling interest</a> in the magazine has been held by <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Fox" title="21st Century Fox">21st Century Fox</a> since 2015.\n</p><p>The magazine is published monthly, and additional map supplements are also included with subscriptions. It is available in a traditional printed edition and through an interactive online edition.  On occasion, special editions of the magazine are issued.\n</p><p>As of 2015, the magazine was circulated worldwide in nearly 40 local-language editions and had a global circulation of approximately 6.5 million per month according to data published by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> (down from about 12 million in the late 1980s) or 6.7 million according to National Geographic. This includes a US circulation of 3.5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The current <a href="/wiki/Editor-in-Chief" class="mw-redirect" title="Editor-in-Chief">Editor-in-Chief</a> of the <i>National Geographic Magazine</i> is <a href="/wiki/Susan_Goldberg" title="Susan Goldberg">Susan Goldberg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NatGeo-Masthead_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeo-Masthead-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Susan_Goldberg" title="Susan Goldberg">Goldberg</a> is also Editorial Director for National Geographic Partners, overseeing the print and digital expression of National Geographic\xe2\x80\x99s editorial content across its media platforms. She is responsible for News, Books (with the exception of National Geographic Kids books), <i>National Geographic Traveler</i> magazine, <i>National Geographic History</i> magazine, Maps, and all digital content with the exception of National Geographic Kids. <a href="/wiki/Susan_Goldberg" title="Susan Goldberg">Goldberg</a> reports to Gary Knell, CEO of National Geographic Partners.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Geographic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1915NatGeog.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/1915NatGeog.jpg/220px-1915NatGeog.jpg" width="220" height="305" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/1915NatGeog.jpg/330px-1915NatGeog.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/1915NatGeog.jpg 2x" data-file-width="344" data-file-height="477" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1915NatGeog.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>January 1915 cover of <i>The National Geographic Magazine</i></div></div></div>\n<p>The first issue of <i>National Geographic Magazine</i> was published on September 22, 1888, nine months after the Society was founded. It was initially a scholarly journal sent to 165 charter members and nowadays it reaches the hands of 40 million people each month.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Starting with its January 1905 publication of several full-page pictures of Tibet in 1900\xe2\x80\x931901, the magazine changed from being a text-oriented publication closer to a scientific journal to featuring extensive pictorial content, and became well known for this style. The June 1985 cover portrait of the presumed to be 12-year-old Afghan girl <a href="/wiki/Sharbat_Gula" class="mw-redirect" title="Sharbat Gula">Sharbat Gula</a>, shot by photographer <a href="/wiki/Steve_McCurry" title="Steve McCurry">Steve McCurry</a>, became one of the magazine\'s most recognizable images.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Kids" title="National Geographic Kids">National Geographic Kids</a></i>, the children\'s version of the magazine, was launched in 1975 under the name <i>National Geographic World</i>. From the 1970s through about 2010 the magazine was printed in <a href="/wiki/Corinth,_Mississippi" title="Corinth, Mississippi">Corinth, Mississippi</a>, by private printers until that plant was finally closed.\n</p><p>In the late 1990s, the magazine began publishing <i>The Complete National Geographic</i>, a digital compilation of all the past issues of the magazine. It was then sued over copyright of the magazine as a <a href="/wiki/Collective_work_(US)" title="Collective work (US)">collective work</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Greenberg_v._National_Geographic" title="Greenberg v. National Geographic">Greenberg v. National Geographic</a></i> and other cases, and temporarily withdrew the availability of the compilation. The magazine eventually prevailed in the dispute, and in July 2009 it resumed publishing a compilation containing all issues through December 2008. The compilation was later updated to make more recent issues available, and the archive and digital edition of the magazine are available online to the magazine\'s subscribers.\n</p><p>On September 9, 2015, the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> announced a deal with <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Fox" title="21st Century Fox">21st Century Fox</a> that would move the magazine to a new partnership, National Geographic Partners, in which 21st Century Fox would hold a 73 percent controlling interest.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In December 2017, <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney</a> announced that it would <a href="/wiki/Acquisition_of_21st_Century_Fox_by_Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Acquisition of 21st Century Fox by Disney">acquire 21st Century Fox</a>, including the latter\'s interest in National Geographic Partners.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Editors-in-chief">Editors-in-chief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Geographic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Editors-in-chief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The magazine had a single "editor" from 1888\xe2\x80\x931920. From 1920\xe2\x80\x931967, the chief editorship was held by the president of the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a>. Since 1967, the magazine has been overseen by its own "editor-in-chief".\n</p>\n<ul><li>John Hyde (October 1888&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 14 September 1900; Editor-in-Chief: 14 September 1900&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 February 1903)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Hovey_Grosvenor" title="Gilbert Hovey Grosvenor">Gilbert Hovey Grosvenor</a> (1875\xe2\x80\x931966)  (Editor-in-Chief: February 1903&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 20 January 1920; Managing Editor: 14 September 1900&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 February 1903; Assistant Editor: May 1899&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 14 September 1900)</li>\n<li>Gilbert Hovey Grosvenor (21 January 1920&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 5 May 1954)</li>\n<li>John Oliver LaGorce (1880\xe2\x80\x931959) (5 May 1954&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 8 January 1957)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Melville_Bell_Grosvenor" title="Melville Bell Grosvenor">Melville Bell Grosvenor</a> (1901\xe2\x80\x931982) (8 January 1957&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 1 August 1967)</li>\n<li>Frederick Vosburgh (1905\xe2\x80\x932005) (1 August 1967&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 October 1970)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Melville_Grosvenor" title="Gilbert Melville Grosvenor">Gilbert Melville Grosvenor</a> (1931\xe2\x80\x93 ) (October 1970&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 July 1980)</li>\n<li>Wilbur E. The magazine printed articles on Berlin, <a href="/wiki/Austrian_State_Treaty" title="Austrian State Treaty">de-occupied Austria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, and Communist China that deliberately downplayed politics to focus on culture.  In its coverage of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>, <i>National Geographic</i> focused on the scientific achievement while largely avoiding reference to the race\'s connection to nuclear arms buildup. There were also many articles in the 1930s, 40s and 50s about the individual states and their resources, along with supplement maps of each state. Many of these articles were written by longtime staff such as <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Simpich" title="Frederick Simpich">Frederick Simpich</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> There were also articles about biology and science topics.\n</p><p>In later years, articles became outspoken on issues such as <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental issues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical">chemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>. Series of articles were included focusing on the history and varied uses of specific products such as a single metal, gem, food crop, or agricultural product, or an archaeological discovery. Occasionally an entire month\'s issue would be devoted to a single country, past civilization, a natural resource whose future is endangered, or other theme. In recent decades, the National Geographic Society has unveiled <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society#Publications" title="National Geographic Society">other magazines</a> with different focuses. Whereas in the past, the magazine featured lengthy expositions, recent issues have shorter articles.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">Photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Geographic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG/220px-Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG/330px-Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG/440px-Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG 2x" data-file-width="752" data-file-height="515" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Taj_Mahal_1921.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Color photograph of the <a href="/wiki/Taj_Mahal" title="Taj Mahal">Taj Mahal</a>. Source: <i>The National Geographic Magazine</i>, March 1921</div></div></div>\n<p>In addition to being well known for articles about scenery, history, and the most distant corners of the world, the magazine has been recognized for its book-like quality and its standard of photography. It was during the tenure of Society President <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a> and editor Gilbert H. Grosvenor (GHG) that the significance of illustration was first emphasized, in spite of criticism from some of the Board of Managers \nwho considered the many illustrations an indicator of an \xe2\x80\x9cunscientific\xe2\x80\x9d conception of geography. By 1910, photographs had become the magazine\xe2\x80\x99s trademark and Grosvenor was constantly on the search for "dynamical pictures" as Graham Bell called them, particularly those that provided a sense of motion in a still image. In 1915, GHG began building the group of staff photographers and providing them with advanced tools including the latest darkroom.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The magazine began to feature some pages of <a href="/wiki/Color_photography" title="Color photography">color photography</a> in the early 1930s, when this technology was still in its early development. During the mid-1930s, <a href="/wiki/Luis_Marden" title="Luis Marden">Luis Marden</a> (1913\xe2\x80\x932003), a writer and photographer for <i>National Geographic</i>, convinced the magazine to allow its photographers to use the so-called "miniature" 35&#160;mm Leica cameras loaded with <a href="/wiki/Kodachrome" title="Kodachrome">Kodachrome</a> film over bulkier cameras with heavy <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">glass plates</a> that required the use of <a href="/wiki/Tripod_(photography)" title="Tripod (photography)">tripods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1959, the magazine started publishing small photographs on its covers, later becoming larger photographs.  National Geographic photography quickly shifted to digital photography for both its printed magazine and its website. In subsequent years, the cover, while keeping its yellow border, shed its oak leaf trim and bare table of contents, to allow for a full page photograph taken for one of the month\'s articles. Issues of <i>National Geographic</i> are often kept by subscribers for years and re-sold at thrift stores as collectibles. The standard for photography has remained high over the subsequent decades and the magazine is still illustrated with some of the highest-quality <a href="/wiki/Photojournalism" title="Photojournalism">photojournalism</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, <i>National Geographic</i> began an international photography competition , called the with over eighteen countries participating.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The January 2017 issue of <i>National Geographic</i> has Avery Jackson, a nine-year-old <a href="/wiki/Transgender_youth" title="Transgender youth">transgender girl</a>, on the cover. She is thought to be the first openly transgender person on <i>National Geographic</i>\xe2\x80\x99s cover.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In conservative Muslim countries like <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, photographs featuring topless or scantily clad members of primitive tribal societies are often <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">blacked out</a>; buyers and subscribers often complain that this practice decreases the artistic value of the photographs for which National Geographic is world-renowned.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallery">Gallery</span><span href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Maps" title="National Geographic Maps">National Geographic Maps</a> (originally the Cartographic Division) became a division of the National Geographic Society in 1915. The first supplement map, which appeared in the May 1918 issue of the magazine, titled <i>The Western Theatre of War</i>, served as a reference for overseas military personnel and soldiers\' families alike.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> On some occasions, the Society\'s map archives have been used by the United States government in instances where its own <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartographic</a> resources were limited.<sup id="cite_ref-GG_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n<a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> map room was filled with National Geographic maps. A National Geographic map of Europe is featured in the displays of the <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> <a href="/wiki/Churchill_Museum_and_Cabinet_War_Rooms" class="mw-redirect" title="Churchill Museum and Cabinet War Rooms">museum</a> in London showing Churchill\'s markings at the <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta Conference</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> leaders divided <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">post-war</a> Europe.\n</p><p>In 2001, <i>National Geographic</i> released an eight-<a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> set containing all its maps from 1888 to December 2000. In 2010, National Geographic Magazine received the top ASME awards for photojournalism and essay. In 2011, National Geographic Magazine received the top-award from ASME\xe2\x80\x94the Magazine of the Year Award.\n</p><p>In April 2014, <i>National Geographic</i> received the National Magazine Award ("Ellie") for best tablet edition for its multimedia presentation of Robert Draper\'s story "The Last Chase," about the final days of a tornado researcher who was killed in the line of duty.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2017, National Geographic received the National Magazine Award ("Ellie") for best website.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Geographic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On the magazine\'s February 1982 cover, the pyramids of Giza were altered, resulting in the first major scandal of the digital photography age and contributing to photography\'s "waning credibility".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The cover of the October 1988 issue featured a photo of a large ivory male portrait whose authenticity, particularly the alleged <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a> provenance, has been questioned.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1999, the magazine was embroiled in the <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeoraptor" title="Archaeoraptor">Archaeoraptor</a></i> scandal, in which it purported to have a fossil linking birds to dinosaurs. The fossil was a forgery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 2010, the magazine\'s Your Shot competition was awarded to William Lascelles for photography featuring a dog with fighter jets over its shoulder. The picture turned out to be a fraud.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2018, the editor of National Geographic, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Goldberg" title="Susan Goldberg">Susan Goldberg</a> said that historically the magazine\'s coverage of people around the world had been <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a>. Goldberg argued that the magazine  ignored non-white Americans and showed different groups as exotic, thereby promoting racial clich\xc3\xa9s.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a
