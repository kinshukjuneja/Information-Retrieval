Between 1,400 and 2,000 sailors lost their lives aboard the wrecked vessels, making the incident one of the worst maritime disasters in British naval history.<sup id="cite_ref-EarlySources_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EarlySources-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The disaster has been attributed to a combination of factors such as the <a href="/wiki/Navigator" title="Navigator">navigators</a>\' inability to accurately calculate their <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">positions</a>, errors in the available charts and pilot books, and inadequate id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From 29 July to 21 August, 1707, during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>, a combined British, <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Austrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch</a> force under the command of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Eugene_of_Savoy" title="Prince Eugene of Savoy">Prince Eugene of Savoy</a> <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Toulon_(1707)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Toulon (1707)">besieged</a> the French port of <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">Toulon</a>. Great Britain dispatched a fleet to provide naval support led by the <a href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Fleet_(Royal_Navy)" title="Admiral of the Fleet (Royal Navy)">Commander-in-Chief of the British Fleets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloudesley_Shovell" title="Cloudesley Shovell">Sir Cloudesley Shovell</a>. The ships sailed to the Mediterranean, attacked Toulon and managed to inflict damage on the French fleet caught in the siege. However, the overall campaign was unsuccessful, and the British fleet was ordered to return home, setting sail from <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> for <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> in late September. As the fleet sailed out on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>, passing the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> on their way to England, the weather worsened and storms gradually pushed the ships off their planned course. Finally, on the night of 22 October 1707 <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">Old Style</a>, (2 November 1707 by the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" href="/wiki/Master_(naval)" title="Master (naval)">sailing masters</a> believed they were on the last leg of their journey.<sup id="cite_ref-Lenox_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lenox-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The fleet was thought to be sailing safely west of <a href="/wiki/Ushant" title="Ushant">Ushant</a>, an island outpost off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Longitude_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Longitude-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, because of a combination of the bad weather and the mariners\' inability to accurately calculate their <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, the fleet was off course and closing in on the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> instead.<sup id="cite_ref-shipwrecks_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shipwrecks-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Before their mistake could be corrected, the fleet struck rocks and four ships were lost:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/HMS_Association_(1697)" class="mw-redirect" title="HMS Association Scilly">St Agnes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Annet, Isles of Scilly">Annet</a>, but she foundered in Smith Sound, sinking close to Menglow Rock and losing 28 of her crew of were killed in the sinking of the four ships is unknown. Statements vary between 1,400 and over 2,000,<sup id="cite_ref-submerged_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-submerged-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> making it one of the greatest maritime disasters in British history. For days afterwards, bodies continued to wash onto the shores of the isles along with the wreckage of the warships<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and personal effects. Many dead sailors from the wrecks were buried on the island of <a href="/wiki/St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Agnes, Isles of Scilly">St Agnes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shipwrecks.uk.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shipwrecks.uk.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\nAdmiral Shovell\'s body, along with those of his two <a href="/wiki/John_Narborough" title="John Narborough">Narborough</a> stepsons and his flag-captain, Edmund Loades, washed up on <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly#Porthellick_Cove" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Mary&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">Porthellick Cove</a> on <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Mary&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Mary\'s</a> the following day, almost seven miles (11&#160;km) from where the <i>Association</i> was wrecked. A small memorial was later erected at this site. The circumstances under which the admiral\'s remains were found gave rise to stories (see below). Shovell was temporarily buried on the beach on St Mary\'s (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://archive.is/20120913024516/http://www.submerged.co.uk/association%205%20big.jpg">see image</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> By order of <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> his body was later exhumed, embalmed and taken to London where he was interred in <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-submerged_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-submerged-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> His large marble monument in the south choir aisle was sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Grinling_Gibbons" title="Grinling Gibbons">Grinling Gibbons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-westminster_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-westminster-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> There is a memorial depicting the sinking of the <i>Association</i> in the church at the Narboroughs\' home of <a href="/wiki/Knowlton,_Kent" title="Knowlton, Kent">Knowlton</a> near Dover.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legends_of_the_disaster">Legends of the disaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legends of the disaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One myth associated with the disaster alleges that a common sailor on the flagship tried to warn Shovell that the fleet was off course but the Admiral had him hanged at the <a href="/wiki/Yardarm" class="mw-redirect" title="Yardarm">yardarm</a> for inciting mutiny. The story first appeared in the Scilly Isles in 1780, with the common sailor being a Scilly native who recognised the waters as being close to home but was punished for warning the Admiral. It was claimed that grass will never grow on the grave where Shovell was first buried at Porthellick Cove because of his tyrannical act against an islander. The myth was embellished in the 19th century when the punishment became instant execution and the sailor\'s knowledge of the fleet\'s position was attributed to superior navigational skills instead of local knowledge. While it is possible that a sailor may have debated the vessel\'s location and feared for its fate, such debates were common upon entering the English Channel, as noted by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a> in 1684. Naval historians have repeatedly discredited the story, noting the lack of any evidence in contemporary documents, its fanciful stock conventions and dubious origins.<sup id="cite_ref-cooke_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cooke-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-powell_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powell-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pickwell_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pickwell-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> However, the myth was revived in 1997 when author <a href="/wiki/Dava_Sobel" title="Dava Sobel">Dava Sobel</a> presented it as an unqualified truth in her book <i><a href="/wiki/Longitude_(book)" title="Longitude (book)">Longitude</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Longitude_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Longitude-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap">:<span>11-16</span></sup>\n</p><p>Another story that is often told is that Shovell was alive, at least barely, when he reached the shore of Scilly at Porthellick Cove, but was murdered by a woman for the sake of his priceless <a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">emerald</a> ring, which had been given to him by a close friend, Captain <a href="/wiki/James_Berkeley,_3rd_Earl_of_Berkeley" title="James Berkeley, 3rd Earl of Berkeley">James Lord Dursley</a>. At that time, the Scillies had a wild and lawless reputation.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> According to a letter written in 1709 by Edmund Herbert, who was sent to Scilly by Shovell\'s family to help locate and recover items belonging to the admiral, Sir Cloudesley\'s body was first found by two women "stript of his shirt" and "his ring was also lost off his hand, which however left ye impression on his finger."  Shovell\'s widow, Elizabeth, had offered a large reward for the recovery of any family property.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is claimed that the murder only came to light some thirty years later when the woman, on her deathbed, produced the stolen ring and confessed to a clergyman that she had killed the admiral.<sup id="cite_ref-Longitude_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Longitude-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The clergyman supposedly sent it back to the <a href="/wiki/James_Berkeley,_3rd_Earl_of_Berkeley" title="James Berkeley, 3rd Earl of Berkeley">3rd Earl of Berkeley</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-royalnavalmuseum_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royalnavalmuseum-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> although several historians doubt the murder legend as there is no record of the ring\'s return and the story stems from a romantic and unverifiable "deathbed confession".<sup id="cite_ref-powell_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powell-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pickwell_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pickwell-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Longitude_and_Latitude">Longitude and Latitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Longitude and Latitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ScillyUshant.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/ScillyUshant.png/220px-ScillyUshant.png" width="220" height="192" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/ScillyUshant.png/330px-ScillyUshant.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ad/ScillyUshant.png 2x" data-file-width="371" data-file-height="323" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ScillyUshant.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> with the positions of Scilly and Ushant, and Falmouth where Shovell sent some ships as the main fleet was heading for Portsmouth.<sup id="cite_ref-Lenox_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lenox-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div><p> The disastrous wrecking of a Royal Navy fleet in home waters brought great consternation to the nation. The main cause of the catastrophe has often been portrayed as the <a href="/wiki/Navigator" title="Navigator">navigators</a>\' inability to accurately calculate their <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Longitude_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Longitude-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Clearly, something better than <a href="/wiki/Dead_reckoning" title="Dead reckoning">dead reckoning</a> was needed to find a way through dangerous waters. As transoceanic travel grew in significance, so did the importance of reliable navigation. This eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/Longitude_Act_1714" class="mw-redirect" title="Longitude Act 1714">Longitude Act</a> in 1714, which established the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Longitude" title="Board of Longitude">Board of Longitude</a> and offered <a href="/wiki/Longitude_prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Longitude prize">large financial rewards</a> for anyone who could find a method of determining longitude accurately at sea.<sup id="cite_ref-Longitude_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Longitude-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> After many years, the consequence of the Act was that accurate <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">marine chronometers</a> were produced and the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_method" class="mw-redirect" title="Lunar distance method">lunar distance method</a> was developed, both of which became used throughout the world for navigation at sea.\n</p><p>However, it is not certain that the navigational error leading to the wrecking of Admiral Shovell\'s fleet was purely one of longitude, and no contemporary discussions are known that appear to have related the disaster to either navigation or longitude.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Royal Navy conducted a court-martial of the officers of the <i>Firebrand</i>, who were acquitted, but no officers survived from the other lost ships, so no other courts-martial took place. The Navy also conducted a survey of compasses from the surviving ships and of those at Chatham and Portsmouth dockyards, following comments from <a href="/wiki/William_Jumper" title="William Jumper">Sir William Jumper</a>, captain of the <i>Lenox</i>, that errors in the compasses had caused the navigational error. This showed what a poor state many were in; at Portsmouth, for example, only four of the 112 wooden cased compasses from nine of the returning vessels were found to be serviceable.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some have argued that the disaster was in fact caused more by an error in <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> than in longitude. According to contemporary reports, Shovell initially attempted to determine the fleet\'s position by astronomical observations and depth soundings before also consulting the <a href="/wiki/Master_(naval)" title="Master (naval)">sailing masters</a> of his other ships. Shovell\'s navigation officers believed that the fleet was at a position west of <a href="/wiki/Ushant" title="Ushant">Ushant</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707&amp;params=48_27_29_N_5_05_44_W_type:isle"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">48\xc2\xb027\xe2\x80\xb229\xe2\x80\xb3N</span> <span class="longitude">5\xc2\xb005\xe2\x80\xb244\xe2\x80\xb3W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">48.45806\xc2\xb0N 5.09556\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">48.45806; -5.09556</span></span></span></a></span>), except the sailing master of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Lenox_(1678)" title="HMS Lenox (1678)">HMS&#160;<i>Lenox</i></a> who judged that they were nearer to the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707&amp;params=49_56_10_N_6_19_22_W_dim:10000"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">49\xc2\xb056\xe2\x80\xb210\xe2\x80\xb3N</span> <span class="longitude">6\xc2\xb019\xe2\x80\xb222\xe2\x80\xb3W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">49.93611\xc2\xb0N 6.32278\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">49.93611; -6.32278</span></span></span></a></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-Lenox_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lenox-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>William May points out that the position of the Isles of Scilly themselves was not known accurately in either longitude or latitude. In addition, his analysis of the 40&#160;extant logbooks from the 21&#160;ships in the fleet do not show the error in longitude to be a significant factor compared to latitude.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_the_wrecks">Discovery of the wrecks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discovery of the wrecks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The ships of Sir Cloudesley Shovell\'s fleet lay undisturbed on the seabed for over 250 years,<sup id="cite_ref-wreck_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wreck-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> despite several salvage attempts in pursuit of the flagship\'s cargo of valuable coins, <a href="/wiki/Prize_of_war" title="Prize of war">spoils of war</a> from several battles, weapons, and personal effects.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In June 1967, the Royal Navy <a href="/wiki/Minesweeper_(ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="Minesweeper (ship)">minesweeper</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Puttenham_(M2784)" class="mw-redirect" title="HMS Puttenham (M2784)">HMS&#160;<i>Puttenham</i></a>, manned with twelve divers under the command of <a href="/wiki/Engineer_Officer_(Royal_Navy)" title="Engineer Officer (Royal Navy)">Engineer-Lieutenant</a> Roy Graham, sailed to the Isles of Scilly and dropped anchor off Gilstone Ledge, just to the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Rock,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Bishop Rock, Isles of Scilly">Bishop Rock</a><sup id="cite_ref-Farrell_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farrell-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and close to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Rocks,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Western Rocks, Isles of Scilly">Western Rocks</a>. The year before, Graham and other specialists from the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Command_Sub_Aqua_Club" title="Naval Air Command Sub Aqua Club">Naval Air Command Sub Aqua Club</a> had dived in this area on a first attempt to find the <i>Association</i>. He recalled some years later: "The weather was so bad, all we achieved was the sight of a blur of seaweed, seals and white water as we were swept through the Gilstone Reef and fortunately out the other side."<sup id="cite_ref-wreck_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wreck-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Graham_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> On their second attempt in summer 1967, using the minesweeper and supported by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy_Auxiliary_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Navy Auxiliary Service">Royal Navy Auxiliary Service</a>, Graham and his men finally managed to locate the remains of Admiral Shovell\'s flagship on the Gilstone Ledge.<sup id="cite_ref-Graham_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Parts of the wreck are in 10&#160;m (30&#160;ft) while others can be found at between 30&#160;m (90&#160;ft) and 40&#160;m (120&#160;ft) as the sea floor falls away from the reef.<sup id="cite_ref-larn_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-larn-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The divers first discovered a cannon, and on the third dive silver and gold coins were spotted underneath that cannon.<sup id="cite_ref-Graham_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> initially suppressed news of the discovery for fear of attracting <a href="/wiki/Treasure_hunting" title="Treasure hunting">treasure hunters</a>, but word was soon out and excited huge national interest.<sup id="cite_ref-Farrell_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farrell-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> As the Isles of Scilly are traditionally administered as part of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a> also has <a href="/wiki/Right_of_wreck" class="mw-redirect" title="Right of wreck">right of wreck</a> on all ships wrecked on the Scilly <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>. More than 2,000 coins and other <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">artefacts</a> were finally recovered from the wreck site and auctioned by <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby&#39;s">Sotheby\'s</a> in July 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> A further sale at Sotheby\'s in January 1970, by order of the <a href="/wiki/Receiver_of_Wreck" title="Receiver of Wreck">Isles of Scilly Wrecks Receiver</a>, made <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\xc2\xa3</a>10,175. Among the goods sold was Shovell\'s <a href="/wiki/Chamber_pot" title="Chamber pot">chamber pot</a> for \xc2\xa3270. A battered dining plate, which had been discovered during a dive in 1968, brought \xc2\xa32,100.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The rediscovery of the <i>Association</i> by naval divers and the finding of so many historical artefacts in her wreck also led to more government legislation, notably the <a href="/wiki/Protection_of_Wrecks_Act_1973" title="Protection of Wrecks Act 1973">Protection of Wrecks Act 1973</a>, passed in an attempt to preserve British historic wreck sites as part of the maritime heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-shipwrecks.uk.com_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shipwrecks.uk.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The wreck of <i>Firebrand</i> was discovered in 1982, and several items were recovered, including guns and anchors, a wooden <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal_(instrument)" title="Nocturnal (instrument)">nocturne</a> (for determining the time at night), a bell and carved cherubs.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Today photographs of the original diving expedition are on display at the Old Wesleyan Chapel in St. Mary\'s, of the team leader Lt Graham and a naval doctor examining human bones from the wreck of the <i>Association</i>, alongside the ship\'s bell of the <i>Firebrand</i> with "1692" engraved on it, and many more artefacts.<sup id="cite_ref-Farrell_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farrell-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, the three-hundredth anniversary of the disaster and its consequences were commemorated on the Isles of Scilly with a series of special events,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> organised by the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly#Local_government" title="Isles of Scilly">Council of the Isles of Scilly</a> in partnership with the local <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">AONB</a> office, <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isles_of_Scilly_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Isles of Scilly Museum (page does not exist)">Isles of Scilly Museum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depictions_in_popular_culture">Depictions in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
