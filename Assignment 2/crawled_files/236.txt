src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Capsella_bursa-pastoris_Sturm23.jpg/220px-Capsella_bursa-pastoris_Sturm23.jpg" width="220" height="339" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Capsella_bursa-pastoris_Sturm23.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>rosette (a), pointed leaves, flowers (c\xe2\x80\x93e), pods (i, k)</div></div></div>\n<p><i>C. bursa-pastoris</i> plants grow from a <a href="/wiki/Rosette_(botany)" title="Rosette (botany)">rosette</a> of lobed leaves at the base.  From the base emerges a stem about 0.2\xe2\x80\x930.5&#160;m (0.66\xe2\x80\x931.64&#160;ft) tall, which bears a few pointed leaves which partly grasp the stem.  The flowers, which appear in any month of the year in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-CTW_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTW-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>56</span></sup> are white and small, 2.5&#160;mm (0.098&#160;in) in diameter, with four petals and six stamens.<sup id="cite_ref-CTW_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTW-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> They are borne in loose <a href="/wiki/Raceme" title="Raceme">racemes</a>, and produce flattened, two-chambered seed pods known as <a href="/wiki/Siliques" class="mw-redirect" title="Siliques">siliques</a>, which are triangular to heart-shaped, each containing several seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-neltje_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neltje-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Like a number of other plants in several plant families, its seeds contain a substance known as <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilage</a>, a condition known as myxospermy.<sup id="cite_ref-western_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-western-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  The adaptive value of myxospermy is unknown,<sup id="cite_ref-western_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-western-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> although the fact that mucilage becomes sticky when wet has led some to propose that <i>C. bursa-pastoris</i> traps insects which then provide nutrients to the seedling, which would make it <a href="/wiki/Protocarnivorous_plant" title="Protocarnivorous plant">protocarnivorous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Capsella bursa-pastoris</i> is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> and is also used as a model organism, due to the variety of genes expressed throughout its life cycle that can be compared to genes that are well studied in <i>A. thaliana</i>. Unlike most <a href="/wiki/Angiosperm" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiosperm">flowering plants</a>, it <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">flowers</a> almost all year round.<sup id="cite_ref-neltje_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neltje-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-china_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-china-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Like other annual ruderals exploiting disturbed ground, <i>C. bursa-pastoris</i> reproduces entirely from seed, has a long <a href="/wiki/Soil_seed_bank" title="Soil seed bank">soil seed bank</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Preston2002_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Preston2002-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and short generation time,<sup id="cite_ref-Aksoy1998_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aksoy1998-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and is capable of producing several generations each year.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capsella_bursa-pastoris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It was formally described by the Swedish botanist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his seminal publication \'<a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a>\' in 1753, and then published by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Kasimir_Medikus" title="Friedrich Kasimir Medikus">Friedrich Kasimir Medikus</a> in <i>Pflanzen-Gattungen</i> (Pfl.-Gatt.) on page 85 in 1792.<sup id="cite_ref-Plantlist_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plantlist-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Capsella bursa-pastoris</i> subsp. <i>thracicus</i> (Velen.) Stoj. &amp; Stef. is the only known subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Plantlist_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plantlist-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_Coles_(botanist)" title="William Coles (botanist)">William Coles (botanist)</a> wrote in his book, \'Adam in Eden\' (published in 1657), "It is called Shepherd\'s purse or Scrip (wallet) from the likeness of the seed hath with that kind of leathearne bag, wherein Shepherds carry their Victualls [food and drink] into the field.""<sup id="cite_ref-Readers_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Readers-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\nIn England and Scotland, it was once commonly called \'mother\'s heart\', which is derived from a child\'s game/trick of picking the seed pod, which then would burst and the child would be accused of \'breaking his mother\'s heart\'.<sup id="cite_ref-Readers_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Readers-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capsella_bursa-pastoris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Common on cultivated ground, waysides and meadows, disturbed land and roadworks.<sup id="cite_ref-CTW_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTW-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capsella_bursa-pastoris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>C. bursa-pastoris</i> is gathered from the wild,<sup id="cite_ref-fao_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> or grown.<sup id="cite_ref-future_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It has many uses, including for food,<sup id="cite_ref-china_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-china-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-future_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> to supplement animal feed,<sup id="cite_ref-fao_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> for cosmetics,<sup id="cite_ref-fao_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and in traditional medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-china_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-china-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fao_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  It is cultivated as a commercial food crop in Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-naturesrestaurant_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturesrestaurant-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span
