id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> and town, on the shore of <a href="/wiki/Mount%27s_Bay" title="Mount&#39;s Bay">Mount\'s Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, United Kingdom. It is 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) east of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> and the tidal island of <a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Mount" title="St Michael&#39;s Mount">St Michael\'s Mount</a> is half-a-mile offshore. At <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">low water</a> a causeway links it to the town and at <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">high water</a> passenger boats carry visitors between Marazion and St Michael\'s Mount.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> \nMarazion is a thriving tourist resort with an active community of artists who produce and sell paintings and pottery in the town\'s art galleries.\n</p><p>Marazion lies within the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Cornwall Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Cornwall Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB). Almost a third of Cornwall has AONB designation, with the same status and protection as a National Park. On the western side of the town is <a href="/wiki/Marazion_Marsh" title="Marazion Marsh">Marazion Marsh</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">RSPB</a> reserve and a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI).\n</p>\n<div Marazion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marazion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of Marazion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Remains of an ancient bronze furnace, discovered near the town, tend to prove that <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> smelting was practised here at an early period.  Marazion was not recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1088.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its only charter was granted by Queen Elizabeth I.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_history">Medieval history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marazion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Medieval history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The charter attributed to <a href="/wiki/Robert,_Count_of_Mortain" title="Robert, Count of Mortain">Robert, Count of Mortain</a> granted lands and liberties to St Michael\'s Mount opposite Marazion and included a market on Thursdays. This appears to have been held from the first on the mainland. From it is probably derived the <i>Marghasbighan</i> (<i>Parvum Forum</i>, lit. "small marketplace") of the earlier and the <i>Marghasyewe</i> (Cornish: "Thursday Market"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>) or <i>Marketjew</i> (Forum Jovis) of the later charters. It may be added that a Jewish origin has been erroneously ascribed to the place from the name Marketjew.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is certain that <a href="/wiki/Richard,_Earl_of_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard, Earl of Cornwall">Richard, Earl of Cornwall</a> provided that the three fairs, on the two feasts of St Michael and at Mid-Lent, and the three markets which had hitherto been held by the priors of St Michael\'s Mount on land not their own at Marghasbighan, should in future be held on their own land at Marchadyou. He transferred in fact the fairs and markets from the <a href="/wiki/Demesne" title="Demesne">demesne</a> lands of the Bloyous in Marazion to those of the prior. Its earliest known charter was granted in 1257.\n</p><p>To remedy the loss incurred by this measure Ralph Bloyou in 1331 procured for himself and his heirs a market on Mondays and a fair on the vigil, feast and morrow of St Andrew at Marghasyon. In Leland\'s time the market was held at Marhasdeythyow (Forum Jovis), and both Norden (1582) and Carew (1602) tell us that Marcajewe signifies the Thursday\'s market, which, whether etymologically sound or not, shows that the prior\'s market had prevailed over its rival. In 1595 Queen Elizabeth granted to Marazion a charter of incorporation. This ratified the grant of St Andrew\'s fair, provided for another on the Feast of St Barnabas and established a market on Saturdays.\n</p><p>The corporation was to consist of a mayor, eight aldermen and twelve capital burgesses. This corporation continued to administer the affairs of the borough until it was dissolved under the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Corporations_Act_1835" title="Municipal Corporations Act 1835">Municipal Corporations Act 1835</a>, when the property belonging to it was vested in charity commissioners. The chairman of the commissioners retains possession of the regalia. Of the fairs, only the Michaelmas fair has survived and all the markets have gone. It is frequently stated that Marazion had formerly the right of returning two members to parliament, but that owing to its inability to pay the members\' expenses the right was lost. The seal of the borough of Marazion was On a shield the arms three castles triple turreted, with the legend "Semper Eadem".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marazion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth</a> an attempt was made to secure or recover the right, and two members are said to have been returned, but they were not allowed to take their seats. Marazion was once a flourishing town, owing its prosperity to the throng of pilgrims who came to visit St Michael\'s Mount (this ceased at the time of the Reformation). During the first half of the 16th century it was twice plundered; first by the French, and later by Cornish rebels. The rise and progress of the neighbouring borough of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> in the 17th century marginalised Marazion.\n</p><p>Penwith is believed to be the last part of Cornwall to speak Cornish as a community language. <a href="/wiki/Dolly_Pentreath" title="Dolly Pentreath">Dolly Pentreath</a>, the last recorded speaker came from <a href="/wiki/Paul,_Cornwall" title="Paul, Cornwall">Paul</a> in Penwith. A year following the death of Dolly Pentreath, <a href="/wiki/Daines_Barrington" title="Daines Barrington">Barrington</a> received a letter, written in Cornish and accompanied by an English translation, from a fisherman in <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a> named <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Bodinar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William Bodinar (page does not exist)">William Bodinar</a> stating that he knew of five people who could speak Cornish in that village alone. Barrington also speaks of a <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Nancarrow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Nancarrow (page does not exist)">John Nancarrow</a> from Marazion who was a native speaker and survived into the 1790s.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The graveyard of <a href="/wiki/Gulval" title="Gulval">Gulval</a> church is home to the remains of local pirate and smuggler <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_%27Eyebrows%27_Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John &#39;Eyebrows&#39; Thomas (page does not exist)">John \'Eyebrows\' Thomas</a> of Marazion.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/West_Cornwall_Railway" title="West Cornwall Railway">West Cornwall Railway</a> opened <a href="/wiki/Marazion_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Marazion railway station">Marazion railway station</a> on 11 March 1852 and its goods yard handled a large volume of perishable traffic &#8211; fish, fruit and vegetables &#8211; from the surrounding farms and harbours. Marazion station closed to passenger traffic in October 1964 and to freight in December 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> For many years the site of the closed station was home to <a href="/wiki/Pullman_(car_or_coach)" title="Pullman (car or coach)">Pullman</a> railway carriages which were used as <a href="/wiki/Camping_coach" title="Camping coach">camping coaches</a>. The site, though not conveniently located, is on Cornwall\'s still-operating passenger main-line, so there are aspirations to re-open it.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution" title="Royal National Lifeboat Institution">Royal National Lifeboat Institution</a> opened a \'Marazion Lifeboat Station\' in 1990, although the <a href="/wiki/D-class_lifeboat_(EA16)" title="D-class lifeboat (EA16)"><i>D</i>-class (EA16)</a> inshore <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboat</a> was actually kept in a shed on the quayside on St Michael\'s Mount. The station was closed on 31 October 2001 as it was proving difficult to find enough volunteer crew members. The boat was transferred to the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Penlee_Lifeboat_Station" title="Penlee Lifeboat Station">Penlee Lifeboat Station</a> at <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a> on the other side of Mounts Bay where there is a larger population to draw the crews from.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of the Second World War a number of naval vessels, the most famous of which was the battleship <i><a href="/wiki/HMS_Warspite_(03)" title="HMS Warspite (03)">HMS Warspite</a></i> were broken up on the beaches at Marazion. <i>HMS Warspite</i> was beached and broken up in 1947.\n</p><p>The local <a href="/wiki/Community_radio_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Community radio in the United Kingdom">community radio</a> station is <a href="/wiki/Coast_FM_(West_Cornwall)" title="Coast FM (West Cornwall)">Coast FM</a> (formerly Penwith Radio), which broadcasts on 96.5 and 97.2 <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marazion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The A394 road between Marazion, <a href="/wiki/Helston" title="Helston">Helston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penryn,_Cornwall" title="Penryn, Cornwall">Penryn</a> was once run by <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trusts" title="Turnpike trusts">Helston Turnpike Roads Trust</a>, which charged <a href="/wiki/Road_toll_(historical)" title="Road toll (historical)">tolls</a> for using the road. On 1 November 1880 the Trust put up for auction the <a href="/wiki/Toll_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Toll House">toll houses</a> at Marazion and <a href="/wiki/Wendron" title="Wendron">Wendron</a> as well as \xe2\x80\xb3materials\xe2\x80\xb3 such as granite posts, chains, gates, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The charging of tolls ended on 7 November and the road came under the management of the County (note:<a href="/wiki/Cornwall_County_Council#Council_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornwall County Council">Cornwall County Council</a> was not formed until 1889).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 1894 to 1974 Marazion was part of <a href="/wiki/West_Penwith_Rural_District" title="West Penwith Rural District">West Penwith Rural District</a> and in a reorganisation of local government in 1974 became part of <a href="/wiki/Penwith_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Penwith District Council">Penwith District Council</a>. Following the 1974 reorganisation Marazion parish regained its town status and right to elect a Mayor from the Marazion Town Council. As an historical oddity the civic regalia of the Mayor is still held by the Marazion Town Trust: for a Mayoral election in Marazion it is still a requirement for the Mayor-Elect to be appointed the Chairman of the Trust before the actual appointment can take place (this is reflected in the process of the Mayor choosing ceremony where two nominations take place for two separate offices). <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a> is now the principal local authority in the area (since April 2009). The coat of arms of the town of Marazion appears on the seal as three castles triple turreted and the motto is "Semper eadem" (Always the same).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The population in 2011 was 1,440<sup id="cite_ref-genuki_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-genuki-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and the electoral ward population which includes surrounding villages was 4,625 <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marazion_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107091.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a or historic buildings led <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Pevsner" title="Nikolaus Pevsner">Nikolaus Pevsner</a> to omit the town entirely from the first edition of his <i>Buildings of England</i> account of Cornwall. In the second edition Marazion is described as "attractive as a whole" and he says of the area near the ferry port: the "cobbled pavements and old houses .. give a look of Lyme Regis to the old centre of the town". Nevertheless, several notable architectural buildings lie in the eastern part of Marazion including <a href="/wiki/Chymorvah_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Chymorvah House">Chymorvah House</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Haven_Hotel" title="Mount Haven Hotel">Mount Haven Hotel</a> next to it, once one of the most haunted hotels in Cornwall, with views of the mount from its terrace. Also of note are <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marazion_Town_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marazion Town Hall (page does not exist)">Marazion Town Hall</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marazion_War_Memorial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marazion War Memorial (page does not exist)">Marazion War Memorial</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marazion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The original parish church is at <a href="/wiki/St_Hilary%27s_Church,_St_Hilary_(Cornwall)" title="St Hilary&#39;s Church, St Hilary (Cornwall)">St Hilary</a>. In Marazion there was a chapel of ease dedicated to St Hermes (recorded in 1308): by 1735 it had become ruinous and was rebuilt. In 1861 a new church (dedicated to All Saints) was built on the same site which became a parish church in 1893.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The architect was <a href="/wiki/J._P._St_Aubyn" class="mw-redirect" title="J. P. St Aubyn">J. P. St Aubyn</a>. The Methodist church is a classical revival building of 1862 with galleries and Pevsner mentions one grand Georgian house at the west end of the town.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Marazion Blues are an <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> team, founded in 1906, and disbanded in 2017 because of a lack of players.<sup id="cite_ref-cman05072018_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cman05072018-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> They were members of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Combination" title="Cornwall Combination">Cornwall Combination League</a> from 1961 to 2004, twice champions in 1978 and 1980 and cup winners in 1972 and 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Blues restarted in July 2018 and will play in the <a href="/wiki/Trelawny_League" title="Trelawny League">Trelawny League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cman05072018_19-1" class="reference"><a
