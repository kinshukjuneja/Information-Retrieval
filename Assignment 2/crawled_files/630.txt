hills along the <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>-<a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> border in south-western <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, which were designated an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-aonb_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aonb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The plateau is dominated by hard <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> bands of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Greensand" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Greensand">Upper Greensand</a> with some remnants of <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, and is cut through by river valleys. The hills support an extensive range of wildlife leading to the designation of 16&#160;<a href="/wiki/Sites_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" class="mw-redirect" title="Sites of Special Scientific Interest">Sites of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSIs).\n</p><p>There is evidence of human occupation since the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. Fortifications include the remains of ancient <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="Motte-and-bailey">motte-and-bailey</a> <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> airfields. There are also religious buildings such as <a href="/wiki/Dunkeswell_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunkeswell Abbey">Dunkeswell Abbey</a> and village churches. The hills are crossed by a network of minor roads with major transport routes including the <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5 motorway</a> running around the periphery.\n</p>\n<div href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">13</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_region">Natural region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Natural region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Blackdowns form a natural region that has been designated as a <a href="/wiki/National_character_area" class="mw-redirect" title="National character area">national character area</a> - No. 147 - by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>, the public body responsible for England\'s natural environment. Neighbouring natural regions are: the <a href="/wiki/Devon_Redlands" title="Devon Redlands">Devon Redlands</a> to the west, the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Taunton_and_Quantock_Fringes" title="Vale of Taunton and Quantock Fringes">Vale of Taunton and Quantock Fringes</a> to the north, the <a href="/wiki/Mid_Somerset_Hills" title="Mid Somerset Hills">Mid Somerset Hills</a> to the northeast, the <a href="/wiki/Yeovil_Scarplands" title="Yeovil Scarplands">Yeovil Scarplands</a> to the east and the <a href="/wiki/Marshwood_and_Powerstock_Vales" title="Marshwood and Powerstock Vales">Marshwood and Powerstock Vales</a> to the southeast.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Straddling the borders of Somerset and Devon, the Blackdown Hills AONB covers an area of 370 square kilometres (143&#160;sq&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Heavily cut with sharp valleys, the hills reach their highest point of 315 metres (1,033&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above sea level">above sea level</a> at <a href="/wiki/Staple_Hill,_Somerset" class="mw-redirect" title="Staple Hill, Somerset">Staple Hill</a> in Somerset. The hills in the southern part of the area, near <a href="/wiki/Honiton" title="Honiton">Honiton</a> in Devon, are more gentle. The Blackdown Hills are a sparsely populated area; much of the land is used for <a href="/wiki/Dairy_farming" title="Dairy farming">dairy farming</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Culmstock.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Culmstock.jpg/220px-Culmstock.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Culmstock.jpg/330px-Culmstock.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Culmstock.jpg/440px-Culmstock.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1299" data-file-height="866" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Culmstock.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old stone bridge with pedestrian refuges over <a href="/wiki/River_Culm" title="River Culm">River Culm</a> at <a href="/wiki/Culmstock" title="Culmstock">Culmstock</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/River_Culm" title="River Culm">River Culm</a> rises at a spring (<a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Blackdown_Hills&amp;params=ST2205016050_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">ST2205016050</a></span>) near <a href="/w/index.php?title=Culmhead&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Culmhead (page does not exist)">Culmhead</a> and flows west through <a href="/wiki/Hemyock" title="Hemyock">Hemyock</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Culmstock" title="Culmstock">Culmstock</a> to <a href="/wiki/Uffculme" title="Uffculme">Uffculme</a> before joining the <a href="/wiki/River_Exe" title="River Exe">River Exe</a> on the north-western outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>. The name of the river is thought to mean \'knot\' or \'tie\', in reference to the river\'s twists and loops;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or is derived from a <a href="/wiki/Celtic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic language">Celtic</a> river-name meaning <i>winding stream</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/River_Otter,_Devon" title="River Otter, Devon">River Otter</a> rises near <a href="/wiki/Otterford" title="Otterford">Otterford</a>, where a stream feeds the Otterhead lakes: (<span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Blackdown_Hills&amp;params=ST225152_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">ST225152</a></span>). It then flows south for 32 kilometres (20&#160;mi) through <a href="/wiki/East_Devon" title="East Devon">East Devon</a> to the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> at the western end of <a href="/wiki/Lyme_Bay" title="Lyme Bay">Lyme Bay</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> in the Otter Valley is one of Devon\'s largest groundwater sources, supplying drinking water to <a href="/wiki/Taunton" title="Taunton">Taunton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The other rivers are the <a href="/wiki/River_Yarty" title="River Yarty">River Yarty</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corry_Brook&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Corry Brook (page does not exist)">Corry Brook</a>.\n</p><p>Villages in the northern, Somerset part of the hills include <a href="/wiki/Staple_Fitzpaine" title="Staple Fitzpaine">Staple Fitzpaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckland_St_Mary" title="Buckland St Mary">Buckland St Mary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitestaunton" title="Whitestaunton">Whitestaunton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wambrook" title="Wambrook">Wambrook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Churchstanton" title="Churchstanton">Churchstanton</a>. The larger, more southerly area in Devon includes <a href="/wiki/Dunkeswell" title="Dunkeswell">Dunkeswell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upottery" title="Upottery">Upottery</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smeatharpe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Smeatharpe (page does not exist)">Smeatharpe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemyock" title="Hemyock">Hemyock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blackborough,_Devon" title="Blackborough, Devon">Blackborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yarcombe" title="Yarcombe">Yarcombe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Membury,_Devon" title="Membury, Devon">Membury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockland,_Devon" title="Stockland, Devon">Stockland</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sheldon,_Devon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sheldon, Devon (page does not exist)">Sheldon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotleigh" title="Cotleigh">Cotleigh</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The geology of the Blackdown Hills together with the adjoining <a href="/wiki/East_Devon_AONB" title="East Devon AONB">East Devon AONB</a> is unique in south-west England,<sup id="cite_ref-ryder_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ryder-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-plan_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plan-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> forming part of the only extensive outcrop of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Greensand" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Greensand">Upper Greensand</a> in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Blackdown Hills form a flat plateau dominated by hard <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> bands, made up of clay with flints, of Upper Greensand with some remnants of <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">cretaceous</a> rocks rest over eroded <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> beds, with an outcrop of <a href="/wiki/Rhaetian" title="Rhaetian">Rhaetian</a> beds.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the western areas the Upper Greensand is devoid of calcareous material but the sands yield fossils of marine <a href="/wiki/Bivalves" class="mw-redirect" title="Bivalves">bivalves</a> and gastropods (snails) preserved in <a href="/wiki/Silica" class="mw-redirect" title="Silica">silica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Along with the rest of south-west England, the Blackdown Hills have a <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate climate</a> that is generally wetter and milder than the rest of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The mean temperature is approximately 10&#160;\xc2\xb0C (50&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and shows a <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasonal</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_motion" title="Diurnal motion">diurnal</a> variation, but because of the modifying effect of the sea the range is less than in most other parts of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (UK).<sup id="cite_ref-temp_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temp-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> January is the coldest month with mean minimum temperatures between 1&#160;\xc2\xb0C (34&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and 2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (36&#160;\xc2\xb0F). July and August are the warmest months, with mean daily maxima around 21&#160;\xc2\xb0C (70&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-temp_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temp-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> December is normally the most cloudy month and June the sunniest.<sup id="cite_ref-sunchine_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sunchine-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> High pressure over the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> often brings clear skies to south-west England, particularly in summer.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">Cloud</a> often forms inland, especially near hills. The average annual sunshine totals around 1,600&#160;hours.<sup id="cite_ref-sunchine_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sunchine-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Rainfall tends to be associated with <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">depressions</a> or with convection. In summer, convection caused by solar surface heating sometimes forms shower clouds, and a large proportion of rain falls from showers and thunderstorms at this time of year. Average rainfall is around 35\xe2\x80\x9360&#160;inch (900\xe2\x80\x931500&#160;mm).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> About 10\xe2\x80\x9320&#160;days of snowfall is typical.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> From November to March, mean wind speeds are highest; winds are lightest from June to August. The predominant wind direction is from the south-west.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable collapsible" style="width:90%; Of these SSSIs 79% are deemed by <a href="/wiki/English_Nature" title="English Nature">English Nature</a> to be being positively managed.<sup id="cite_ref-plan_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plan-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> SSSI is a <a href="/wiki/Conservation_designation" title="Conservation designation">conservation designation</a> denoting a <a href="/wiki/Protected_area" title="Protected area">protected area</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, selected by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>, for areas with particular landscape and ecological characteristics. It provides some protection from development, from other damage, and (since 2000) from neglect, under the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000</a>.\n</p><p>The grasslands, heathland, meadows and mire support extensive populations of birds such as <a href="/wiki/Barn_owl" title="Barn owl">barn owls</a> (<i>Tyto alba</i>) and <a href="/wiki/European_nightjar" title="European nightjar">nightjar</a>, with butterflies including <a href="/wiki/Melanargia_galathea" title="Melanargia galathea">marbled white</a> (<i>Melanargia galathea</i>), <a href="/wiki/Green_hairstreak" title="Green hairstreak">green hairstreak</a> (<i>Callophrys rubi</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Gatekeeper_butterfly" class="mw-redirect" title="Gatekeeper butterfly">gatekeeper butterfly</a> (<i>Pyronia tithonus</i>). The flora includes the <a href="/wiki/Heath_spotted-orchid" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath spotted-orchid">heath spotted-orchid</a> (<i>Dactylorhiza maculata</i>), <a href="/wiki/Water_dropwort" title="Water dropwort">corky fruited water dropwort</a> (<i>pimpinelloides</i>), <a href="/wiki/Green-winged_orchid" class="mw-redirect" title="Green-winged orchid">green-winged orchid</a> (<i>Anacamptis morio</i>), <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">heather</a> (<i>Calluna vulgaris</i>), <a href="/wiki/Lousewort" class="mw-redirect" title="Lousewort">lousewort</a> (<i>Pedicularis</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Lotus_corniculatus" title="Lotus corniculatus">birds foot trefoil</a> (<i>Lotus corniculatus</i>). The hedgerows and woodlands are made up of <a href="/wiki/Ash_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Ash tree">ash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazel</a> (<i>Corylus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Salix_cinerea" title="Salix cinerea">grey willow</a> (<i>Salix cinerea</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Pedunculate_oak" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedunculate oak">pedunculate oak</a> (<i>Quercus robur</i>) which support populations of <a href="/wiki/Dormouse" title="Dormouse">dormouse</a> (<i>Gliridae</i>), <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">common lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_siskin" title="Eurasian siskin">siskin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iris_foetidissima" title="Iris foetidissima">stinking iris</a> (<i>Iris foetidissima</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Purple_hairstreak" title="Purple hairstreak">purple hairstreak</a> butterfly (<i>Neozephyrus quercus</i>). The rivers and streams are home to <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher" title="Kingfisher">kingfisher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otter" title="Otter">otter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Daubenton%27s_bat" title="Daubenton&#39;s bat">Daubenton\'s bat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Blackdown and Sampford Commons have extensive surviving examples of the <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath (habitat)">heathland</a>, carr woodland and marshy <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> habitats that have developed on the acidic soils overlying the Greensand and Keuper Marls of the Blackdown Hills. The heathland supports a typical invertebrate <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>, including a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterfly</a> species, and with <a href="/wiki/Spiders" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiders">spiders</a> notably abundant. The site is regionally important for birds which favour heathland habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Quants_Reserve" title="Quants Reserve">Quants</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> clearing in a <a href="/wiki/Forestry_plantation" class="mw-redirect" title="Forestry plantation">forestry plantation</a> well known for its <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> including <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">Duke of Burgundy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh_fritillary" title="Marsh fritillary">marsh fritillary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leptidea_sinapis" title="Leptidea sinapis">wood white</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> is a candidate for <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> (cSAC). These are designated under the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) as internationally important habitats.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Paleoenvironmental" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleoenvironmental">Paleoenvironmental</a> studies have shown that organic material began to accumulate on the Blackdown Hills in the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> periods with areas of open meadow, grass land with small woodland components being identified.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> There are several <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> burial sites including <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood%27s_Butts" title="Robin Hood&#39;s Butts">Robin Hood\'s Butts</a> near <a href="/wiki/Otterford" title="Otterford">Otterford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Notable archaeological sites include the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Hill_forts" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill forts">hill forts</a> at <a href="/wiki/Membury_Castle" title="Membury Castle">Membury Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hembury" title="Hembury">Hembury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Castle_Neroche" title="Castle Neroche">Castle Neroche</a>. Hembury is a <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Causewayed_enclosure" title="Causewayed enclosure">causewayed enclosure</a> near <a href="/wiki/Honiton" title="Honiton">Honiton</a>. It dates to the late fifth and early fourth millennia BC and is believed to have been the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Dumnonii" title="Dumnonii">Dumnonii</a> tribe. The fort is situated on a promontory to the north of and overlooking the <a href="/wiki/River_Dart" title="River Dart">River Dart</a> at approx 178&#160;m (584&#160;ft) above sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> It has given its name to some of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> pottery in southern <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">Britain</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill fort</a> was later built on the same site.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\nThere has been archaeological evidence found on the site of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> Military occupation, suggesting a <a href="/wiki/Fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort">Fort</a> within the existing Iron Age site. It is now a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_Ancient_Monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled Ancient Monument">Scheduled Ancient Monument</a>.\n</p><p>Fourteen hill slope enclosures, dating from the Iron Age have been identified on the Blackdown Hills,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and prehistoric remains, from about 100 BC, have been found in <a href="/wiki/Hemyock" title="Hemyock">Hemyock</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg/220px-Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg/330px-Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg/440px-Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1027" data-file-height="731" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Castle_Neroche_Somerset_Map.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earthworks at <a href="/wiki/Castle_Neroche" title="Castle Neroche">Castle Neroche</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Castle Neroche is a <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="Motte-and-bailey">motte-and-bailey</a> <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> on the site of an earlier <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill fort</a> near <a href="/wiki/Staple_Fitzpaine" title="Staple Fitzpaine">Staple Fitzpaine</a>. The hill rises to 260 metres (853&#160;ft) on the northern escarpment of the Blackdown Hills. The castle was probably built by Robert of Mortain in the 11th century and probably went out of use in the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Around the crossroads at Staple Fitzpaine there are several large sandstone boulders. They are called devilstones and are said to have been thrown by the Devil from <a href="/wiki/Castle_Neroche" title="Castle Neroche">Castle Neroche</a>. According to legend if you prick them with a pin they draw blood. English word \'Stapol\' means pillar or post and it is thought likely that this gave the village the first part of its name.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The second part of the name comes from the Fitzpaine family who owned the manor between 1233 and 1393.<sup id="cite_ref-completesomerset_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-completesomerset-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A Roman bath house and Edwardian folly in the village of <a href="/wiki/Whitestaunton" title="Whitestaunton">Whitestaunton</a> were excavated by the archaeological television programme <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Team" title="Time Team">Time Team</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> There is also evidence of iron workings in the Romano-British period, at Dunkeswell, which radiocarbon dating has placed in the 2nd century.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that these and other iron-based technologies gave the hills a fairly industrial landscape during the Romano-British period, providing a source of the name Blackdown Hills.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Local iron ores were smelted at Hemyock in small bloomeries (furnaces) to produce pure iron until the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At <a href="/w/index.php?title=Simonsburrow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Simonsburrow (page does not exist)">Simonsburrow</a> a battle between the native Britons and <a href="/wiki/Ine_of_Wessex" title="Ine of Wessex">King Ine</a>\'s Saxon army, put an end (temporarily) to the Kings expansion to the west. In 710, Ine and Nothhelm fought against <a href="/wiki/Geraint_of_Dumnonia" title="Geraint of Dumnonia">Geraint of Dumnonia</a>, according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>;<sup id="cite_ref-Swanton_42_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swanton_42-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_of_Worcester" title="John of Worcester">John of Worcester</a> states that Geraint was killed in this battle.<sup id="cite_ref-Forester_36_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forester_36-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  Ine\'s advance brought him control of what is now <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, the new border with Dumnonia being the <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">river Tamar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby_125_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby_125-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CulmstockBeacon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/CulmstockBeacon.jpg/220px-CulmstockBeacon.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/CulmstockBeacon.jpg/330px-CulmstockBeacon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/CulmstockBeacon.jpg/440px-CulmstockBeacon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2240" data-file-height="1488" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CulmstockBeacon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Stone structure built in 1588 to support a wooden pole and fire basket(s). Fire(s) would be lit as a signal that the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> had been sighted. Of the chain of beacon sites across Southern England, this is the only remaining stone structure.</div></div></div>\n<p>Just to the north of <a href="/wiki/Culmstock" title="Culmstock">Culmstock</a>, at Culmstock Beacon, is one of a chain of <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabethan">Elizabethan</a> beacons built to warn of possible invasion by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> On 5 November 1380, King <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II</a> granted Sir William and Lady Margaret Asthorpe a <a href="/wiki/Licence_to_crenellate" title="Licence to crenellate">licence to crenellate</a> the <a href="/wiki/Hemyock" title="Hemyock">Hemyock</a> manor house, meaning the permission to fortify it.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Over the centuries, Hemyock Castle had many notable owners including Lord Chief Justice Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Popham_(Lord_Chief_Justice)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Popham (Lord Chief Justice)">John Popham</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> it was held for <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a>, subjected to a brief but brutal <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a> and eventually <i>slighted </i>to destroy its military value. Parts of the castle walls, towers and moat still remain. They are a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_ancient_monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled ancient monument">scheduled ancient monument</a> and include displays of history and archaeology. The castle was also owned by General Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Graves_Simcoe" title="John Graves Simcoe">John Graves Simcoe</a> the first lieutenant-governor of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Canada" title="Upper Canada">Upper Canada</a> in 1792.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> He is buried at <a href="/wiki/Wolford_Chapel" title="Wolford Chapel">Wolford Chapel</a> near <a href="/wiki/Dunkeswell" title="Dunkeswell">Dunkeswell</a>. The chapel is now owned by the Province of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Early attempts were made by <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> to <a href="/wiki/Inclosure_Acts" title="Inclosure Acts">enclose</a> parts of the Blackdowns in the 1630s however this was opposed by the local lord and the commoners. He managed to enclose 1.634 acres (6,610&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) and soon sold these, but many of the hedges and fences were removed during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. This was followed  by further attempts at enclosure in 1658 but again only about a third was successfully enclosed, which remained the situation until 1833 when the rest of the hills were enclosed.<sup id="cite_ref-havinden_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-havinden-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wellington_Monument,_Somerset.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Wellington_Monument%2C_Somerset.jpg/170px-Wellington_Monument%2C_Somerset.jpg" width="170" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Wellington_Monument%2C_Somerset.jpg/255px-Wellington_Monument%2C_Somerset.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Wellington_Monument%2C_Somerset.jpg/340px-Wellington_Monument%2C_Somerset.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3398" data-file-height="5240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wellington_Monument,_Somerset.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Wellington_Monument,_Somerset" title="Wellington Monument, Somerset">Wellington Monument</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Coldharbour_Mill_Working_Wool_Museum" title="Coldharbour Mill Working Wool Museum">Coldharbour Mill</a> was built around 1800 to exploit the available water power of the <a href="/wiki/River_Culm" title="River Culm">River Culm</a> and was used for wool and yarn production until its commercial closure in 1981. It is now managed by an educational trust and plays a role in telling the industrial history of the area.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Wellington_Monument,_Somerset" title="Wellington Monument, Somerset">Wellington Monument</a> is located on Wellington Hill at <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid" title="Ordnance Survey National Grid">grid reference</a> <span style="white-space: nowrap" class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=Blackdown_Hills&amp;params=ST137171_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">ST137171</a></span>, 3&#160;km (1.9&#160;mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Wellington,_Somerset" title="Wellington, Somerset">Wellington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>. It was erected to celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Duke of Wellington\'s</a> victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a>. The foundation stone was laid in 1817, on land belonging to the Duke, but the monument was  not completed until 1854. Its design was inspired by an Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisk</a>, but in the shape of the type of <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonet</a> used by Wellington\'s armies.<sup id="cite_ref-curio_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-curio-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> It is now owned by the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a>, and is floodlit at night.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mare-and-Foal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Mare-and-Foal.jpg/220px-Mare-and-Foal.jpg" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Mare-and-Foal.jpg/330px-Mare-and-Foal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Mare-and-Foal.jpg/440px-Mare-and-Foal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1670" data-file-height="1391" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mare-and-Foal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Mare and Foal</i>, 1917, by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bevan" title="Robert Bevan">Robert Bevan</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The artist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Polhill_Bevan" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Polhill Bevan">Robert Polhill Bevan</a> worked in the Blackdown Hills from 1912\xe2\x80\x931925 as a guest of landowner and amateur artist Harold Harrison.  Until the end of his life Bevan continued to paint in the <a href="/wiki/Bolham,_Devon" title="Bolham, Devon">Bolham</a> valley and nearby <a href="/wiki/Luppitt" title="Luppitt">Luppitt</a> his angular style sitting well with the strong patterning of the landscape.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Many of the images that he produced in the area are now in national museums.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, airbases were built at <a href="/wiki/Dunkeswell" title="Dunkeswell">Dunkeswell</a>, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Upottery" title="RAF Upottery">Upottery</a> and <a href="/wiki/RAF_Culmhead" title="RAF Culmhead">Culmhead</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ashworth_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ashworth-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dunkeswell_Aerodrome" title="Dunkeswell Aerodrome">Dunkeswell Aerodrome</a> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>N/A</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EGTU</b>) was built in the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> by the <a href="/wiki/RAF" class="mw-redirect" title="RAF">RAF</a>, briefly used by the <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a>, and then the Fleet Airwing 7 of the <a href="/wiki/USN" class="mw-redirect" title="USN">USN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ashworth_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ashworth-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> It was the only American Navy air base commissioned on UK soil during the Second World War.\n</p><p>According to local legend, the Holman Clavel Inn is the home of a <a href="/wiki/Household_deity" title="Household deity">hearth spirit</a> called Chimbley Charlie.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Blackdown Hills have, since 1991, been designated as an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB).<sup id="cite_ref-aonb_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aonb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> As they have the same landscape quality, AONBs may be compared to the <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">national parks of England and Wales</a>. AONBs are created under the same legislation as the <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a>: the <a href="/wiki/National_Parks_and_Access_to_the_Countryside_Act_1949" title="National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949">National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949</a>. However, National Parks, unlike AONBs, have their own authorities and have special legal powers to prevent unsympathetic development. By contrast, there are very limited statutory duties imposed on local authorities within an AONB. Further regulation and protection of AONBs was added by the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000</a>.\n</p><p>The total population of the Blackdown Hills AONB in 2001 was 13,300; of which 10,500 live in Devon and 2,800 within Somerset.<sup id="cite_ref-plan_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plan-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Many of the villages have their own <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish councils</a> which have some responsibility for local issues. The Blackdown Hills AONB is managed by a partnership of public bodies, local organisations and voluntary groups with an active interest in the hills. Funding is provided by <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a> County Councils, <a href="/wiki/East_Devon" title="East Devon">East Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mid_Devon" title="Mid Devon">Mid Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Somerset" title="South Somerset">South Somerset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taunton_Deane" title="Taunton Deane">Taunton Deane</a> Councils and <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>.\n</p><p>The AONB straddles the borders of three parliamentary constituencies: <a href="/wiki/Tiverton_and_Honiton_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Tiverton and Honiton (UK Parliament constituency)">Tiverton and Honiton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taunton_Deane_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Taunton Deane (UK Parliament constituency)">Taunton Deane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yeovil_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Yeovil (UK Parliament constituency)">Yeovil</a>. The area is also part of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/South_West_England_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="South West England (European Parliament constituency)">South West England</a> constituency.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_sites">Religious sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religious sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg/170px-Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg/255px-Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg/340px-Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Staplefitzpainechurch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Church of <a href="/wiki/St_Peter" class="mw-redirect" title="St Peter">St Peter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Staple_Fitzpaine" title="Staple Fitzpaine">Staple Fitzpaine</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Dunkeswell_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunkeswell Abbey">Dunkeswell Abbey</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cistercian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> monastery and offshoot of <a href="/wiki/Forde_Abbey" title="Forde Abbey">Forde Abbey</a>, was founded in 1201 by <a href="/wiki/William_Brewer_(justice)" title="William Brewer (justice)">William Briwere</a>. The abbey was closed in 1539 and granted to Lord Russell. It was mostly demolished promptly, though a section remained in domestic use until the 19th century. In 1842, a parish church was built on a part of the site. Some surviving fragments of monastery include the partial end wall of the cellarers range and parts of a gatehouse. Some carved fragments survive within the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> church.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Church of <a href="/wiki/St_Peter" class="mw-redirect" title="St Peter">St Peter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Staple_Fitzpaine" title="Staple Fitzpaine">Staple Fitzpaine</a> was originally built in the <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> style, and has a Norman doorway reset in the south aisle. The chancel dates from the 14th century; the north aisle was added and the church <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">refenestrated</a> in the 15th century. The tower dates from about 1500. The south porch and the <a href="/wiki/Vestry" title="Vestry">vestry</a> are much more recent, dating from 1841. The <a href="/wiki/Crenellated" class="mw-redirect" title="Crenellated">crenellated</a> 3-stage tower, has <a href="/wiki/Merlon" title="Merlon">merlons</a> pierced with <a href="/wiki/Trefoil" title="Trefoil">trefoil</a> headed arches set on a <a href="/wiki/Quatrefoil" title="Quatrefoil">quatrefoil</a> pierced <a href="/wiki/Parapet" title="Parapet">parapet</a>. The church has been designated by <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> as a grade I <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Blackdown Hills are crossed by a network of minor roads. There are several major roads including the <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30</a>, <a href="/wiki/A303_road" title="A303 road">A303</a> and <a href="/wiki/A35_road" title="A35 road">A35</a>. The <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5 motorway</a> is at the northwestern boundary of the AONB. The <a href="/wiki/Bristol_to_Exeter_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Bristol to Exeter line">Bristol to Exeter line</a> and the remains of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Western_Canal" title="Grand Western Canal">Grand Western Canal</a> run, quite close in places, to the west of the M5 motorway but do not pass through the Blackdown Hills. The <a href="/wiki/West_of_England_Main_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="West of England Main Line">West of England Main Line</a> passes through the southern part of the hills between Axminster and Honiton, including a tunnel section east of Honiton.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Culm_Valley_Light_Railway" title="Culm Valley Light Railway">Culm Valley Light Railway</a> opened in 1876, having been built by local enterprise. The line was purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a>, which had operated it from the start, in 1880. The line closed to passengers in 1963 but served the milk depot at Hemyock until its closure in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dunkeswell_Aerodrome" title="Dunkeswell Aerodrome">Dunkeswell Aerodrome</a> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>N/A</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EGTU</b>) is now a busy civilian airfield with a mix of <a href="/wiki/Light_aircraft" title="Light aircraft">light aircraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microlight" class="mw-redirect" title="Microlight">microlights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parachuting" title="Parachuting">parachuting</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdown_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Blackdown Hills AONB is unique in that there are no towns or cities within its boundary. Employment opportunities are concentrated in the surrounding towns and in a number of small-scale industrial parks&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 notably around Dunkeswell airfield.  A resurgence of interest in local and sustainable food production has led to significant growth in the number and variety of small-scale food and drink businesses in the area in recent years.  Tourism is also a significant contributor to the local economy with visitors attracted by activities such as walking and riding.\n</p><p>The Blackdown Hills Business Association was established as a member organisation in 2002 to encourage and support all businesses that are based in or serve the Blackdown Hills. Membership stands at around 400 businesses.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
