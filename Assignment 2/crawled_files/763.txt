He was the son of <a href="/wiki/Tryggvi_Olafsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Tryggvi Olafsson">Tryggvi Olafsson</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Viken,_Norway" title="Viken, Norway">Viken</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vingulmark" title="Vingulmark">Vingulmark</a>, and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A5nrike" class="mw-redirect" title="R\xc3\xa5nrike">R\xc3\xa5nrike</a>), and, according to later sagas, the great-grandson of <a href="/wiki/Harald_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Harald I of Norway">Harald Fairhair</a>, first King of Norway.\n</p><p>Olaf had an important part in the conversion of the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> as he was the first Christian king of the nation. Many of these new converts were converted under threat of violence.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He is said to have built the first Christian church in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, in 995, and to have founded the city of <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a> in 997. A <a href="/wiki/Olav_Tryggvason_(statue)" title="Olav Tryggvason (statue)">statue of Olaf Tryggvason</a> is located in the city\'s central plaza.\n</p><p>Historical information on Olaf is sparse. He is mentioned in some contemporary English sources,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and some <a href="/wiki/Skald" title="Skald">skaldic</a> poems. The oldest narrative source mentioning him briefly is <a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Bremen" title="Adam of Bremen">Adam of Bremen</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gesta_Hammaburgensis_ecclesiae_pontificum" title="Gesta Hammaburgensis ecclesiae pontificum">Gesta Hammaburgensis ecclesiae pontificum</a></i> of <i>circa</i> 1070.\n</p><p>In the 1190s, two <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> versions of <i>"<a href="/wiki/%C3%93l%C3%A1fs_saga_Tryggvasonar" title="\xc3\x93l\xc3\xa1fs saga Tryggvasonar">\xc3\x93l\xc3\xa1fs saga Tryggvasonar</a>"</i> were written in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Oddr_Snorrason" title="Oddr Snorrason">Oddr Snorrason</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Gunnlaugr_Leifsson" title="Gunnlaugr Leifsson">Gunnlaugr Leifsson</a> - these are now lost, but are thought to form the basis of later Norse versions. <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a> gives an extensive account of Olaf in the <i><a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla</a></i> saga of <i>circa</i> 1230, using Oddr Snorrason\'s saga as his primary source. Modern historians do not assume that these late sources are accurate, and their credibility is debated.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The most detailed account is named <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%93l%C3%A1fs_saga_Tryggvasonar_en_mesta" title="\xc3\x93l\xc3\xa1fs saga Tryggvasonar en mesta">\xc3\x93l\xc3\xa1fs saga Tryggvasonar en mesta</a></i> [Greatest Saga of \xc3\x93l\xc3\xa1fr Tryggvason] and is recorded in the <i><a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k">Flateyjarb\xc3\xb3k</a></i>, and in the early 15th C.  <i><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The account in this article is primarily based on the late sagas.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_and_early_life">Birth and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Birth and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg/220px-Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg" width="220" height="407" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg/330px-Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg/440px-Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1734" data-file-height="3211" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trondheim_-_monument_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of Olaf in the city plaza of Trondheim. Between the king\'s legs lies the head of the slave Kark, Haakon jarl\'s murderer.</div></div></div>\n<p>There is uncertainty of both the date and the place of Olaf\'s birth. The earliest Norwegian written source, the <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Norwegi%C3%A6" title="Historia Norwegi\xc3\xa6">Historia Norwegi\xc3\xa6</a></i> of the late twelfth century, states that Olaf was born in the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a> after his mother fled there to escape the killers of Olaf\'s father. Another late 12th-century source, <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%81grip_af_N%C3%B3regskonungas%C3%B6gum" title="\xc3\x81grip af N\xc3\xb3regskonungas\xc3\xb6gum">\xc3\x81grip af N\xc3\xb3regskonungas\xc3\xb6gum</a></i>, states that Olaf\'s mother fled to Orkney with Olaf when he was three years old for the same reason. All the sagas agree that Olaf eventually came to <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus&#39;">Kievan Rus\'</a>, specifically the court of <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_I_of_Kiev" class="mw-redirect" title="Vladimir I of Kiev">Vladimir I of Kiev</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The version in <i>Heimskringla</i> is the most elaborate, but also the latest, and introduces elements to the story that are not found in earlier sources. It states that Olaf was born shortly after the murder of his father in 963, while other sources suggest a date between 964 and 969. The later dates cast doubt over Olaf\'s claim to be of Harald Fairhair\'s kin, and the legitimacy of his claim to the throne. Snorri Sturluson claims in <i>Olaf Tryggvson\'s saga</i> that Olaf was born on an islet in Fj\xc3\xa6rlandsvatnet, where his mother Astrid Eiriksdottir, daughter of Eirik Bjodaskalle, was hiding from her husband\'s killers, led by <a href="/wiki/Harald_Greycloak" title="Harald Greycloak">Harald Greycloak</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Eirik_Bloodaxe" class="mw-redirect" title="Eirik Bloodaxe">Eirik Bloodaxe</a>. Greycloak and his brothers had seized the throne from <a href="/wiki/Haakon_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Haakon I of Norway">Haakon the Good</a>. Astrid fled to her father\'s home in Oppland, then went on to Sweden where she thought she and Olaf would be safe. Harald sent emissaries to the king of Sweden, and asked for permission to take the boy back to Norway, where he would be raised by Greycloak\'s mother Gunhild. The Swedish king gave them men to help them claim the young boy, but to no avail. After a short scuffle Astrid (with her son) fled again. This time their destination was <a href="/wiki/Gar%C3%B0ar%C3%ADki" title="Gar\xc3\xb0ar\xc3\xadki">Gardarike</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a>), where Astrid\'s brother Sigurd was in the service of King Valdemar (<a href="/wiki/Vladimir_the_Great" title="Vladimir the Great">Vladimir I</a>). Olaf was three years old when they set sail on a merchant ship for <a href="/wiki/Novgorod" class="mw-redirect" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a>. The journey was not successful: in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> they were captured by <a href="/wiki/Estonian_vikings" class="mw-redirect" title="Estonian vikings">Estonian vikings</a>, and the people aboard were either killed or taken as slaves. Olaf became the possession of a man named Klerkon, together with his foster father Thorolf and his son Thorgils. Klerkon considered Thorolf too old to be useful as a slave and killed him, and then sold the two boys to a man named Klerk for a ram. Olaf was then sold to a man called Reas for a fine cloak.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Six years later, Sigurd Eirikson traveled to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Estonia" title="Ancient Estonia">Estonia</a> to collect taxes for King Valdemar. He saw a boy who did not appear to be a native. He asked the boy about his family, and the boy told him he was Olaf, son of Tryggve Olafson and Astrid Eiriksdattir. Sigurd then went to Reas and bought Olaf and Thorgils out from slavery, and took the boys with him to Novgorod to live under the protection of Valdemar.\n</p><p>Still according to <i>Heimskringla</i>, one day in the Novgorod marketplace Olaf encountered Klerkon, his enslaver and the murderer of his foster father. Olaf killed Klerkon with an axe blow to the head. A mob followed the young boy as he fled to his protector <a href="/wiki/Family_life_and_children_of_Vladimir_I" title="Family life and children of Vladimir I">Queen Allogia</a>, with the intent of killing him for his misdeed. Only after Allogia had paid <a href="/wiki/Weregild" title="Weregild">blood money</a> for Olaf did the mob calm down. As Olaf grew older, Vladimir made him chief over his men-at-arms, but after a couple years the king became wary of Olaf and his popularity with his soldiers. Fearing he might be a threat to the safety of his reign, Vladimir stopped treating Olaf as a friend. Olaf decided that it was better for him to seek his fortune elsewhere, and set out for the Baltic.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Raiding">Raiding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Raiding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Heimskringla</i> states that after leaving Novgorod, Olaf raided settlements and ports with success. In 982 he was caught in a storm and made port in <a href="/wiki/Wends" title="Wends">Wendland</a>, where he met <a href="/wiki/Queen_Geira" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Geira">Queen Geira</a>, a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Burislav" title="Burislav">King Burizleif</a>. She ruled the part of Wendland in which Olaf had landed, and Olaf and his men were given an offer to stay for the winter. Olaf accepted and after courting the Queen, they were married. Olaf began to reclaim the baronies which while under Geira\'s rule had refused to pay taxes. After these successful campaigns, he began raiding again both in <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A5ne" class="mw-redirect" title="Sk\xc3\xa5ne">Sk\xc3\xa5ne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alliance_with_Emperor_Otto_II">Alliance with Emperor Otto II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alliance with Emperor Otto II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Otto_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto II">Otto II</a> assembled a great army of <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frisia" title="Frisia">Frisians</a>, and Wends to fight the <a href="/wiki/Norse_paganism" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse paganism">Norse pagan</a> <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danes</a>. Olaf was part of this army because his father-in-law was king of Wendland. Otto\'s army met the armies of King <a href="/wiki/Harald_Bluetooth" title="Harald Bluetooth">Harald Bluetooth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haakon_Jarl" class="mw-redirect" title="Haakon Jarl">Haakon Jarl</a>, the ruler of Norway under the Danish king, at <a href="/wiki/Danevirke" title="Danevirke">Danevirke</a>, a great wall near <a href="/wiki/Schleswig" class="mw-redirect" title="Schleswig">Schleswig</a>. Otto\'s army was unable to break the fortification, so he changed tactics and sailed around it to <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a> with a large fleet. Otto won a large battle there, and forced Harald and Haakon with their armies to convert to Christianity. The constituents of Otto\'s army then returned to their homelands. Harald held to his new religion, but Haakon returned to worshipping the pagan gods when he came home.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_of_Geira_and_conversion">Death of Geira and wife Geira died. He felt so much sorrow from her death that he could no longer bear to stay in Wendland, and set out to plunder in 984. He raided from <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>. After four years he landed on one of the <a href="/wiki/Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Scilly Isles">Scilly Isles</a>. He heard of a <a href="/wiki/Fortune_teller" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortune teller">seer</a> who lived there. Desiring to test the seer, he sent one of his men to pose as Olaf. But the seer was not fooled. So Olaf went to see the hermit, now convinced he was a real fortune teller. And the seer told him:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Thou wilt become a renowned king, and do celebrated deeds. Many men wilt thou bring to faith and baptism, and both to thy own and others\' good; and that thou mayst have no doubt of the truth of this answer, listen to these tokens. When thou comest to thy ships many of thy people will conspire against thee, and then a battle will follow in which many of thy men will fall, and thou wilt be wounded almost to death, and carried upon a shield to thy ship; yet after seven days thou shalt be well of thy wounds, and immediately thou shalt let thyself be baptized.</p></blockquote>\n<p>After the meeting mutineers attacked Olaf, and he was wounded but survived, and as a result he converted to Christianity.\n</p><p>David Hugh Farmer, in the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Dictionary_of_Saints" title="Oxford Dictionary of Saints">Oxford href="/wiki/St_Helen%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Helen&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Helen\'s</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Farmer2011_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farmer2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By another account, <a href="/wiki/%C3%86lfheah_of_Canterbury" title="\xc3\x86lfheah of Canterbury">Saint \xc3\x86lfheah of Canterbury</a> baptised him near <a href="/wiki/Andover,_Hampshire" title="Andover, Hampshire">Andover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 994.<sup id="cite_ref-ASE378_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASE378-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, Henrietta Leyser, the author of \xc3\x86lfheah\'s entry in the <i><a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_National_Biography" title="Dictionary of National Biography">Oxford National Dictionary of Biography</a></i>, states that Olaf was already baptised and that the 994 event at Andover was a confirmation of his faith, part of a <a href="/wiki/Danegeld" title="Danegeld">Danegeld</a> treaty in which he agreed to no longer raid in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_to_Gyda">Marriage to Gyda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marriage to Gyda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 988, Olaf sailed to England, because a <i><a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">thing</a></i> had been called by Queen Gyda, sister of <a href="/wiki/Olaf_Cuaran" class="mw-redirect" title="Olaf Cuaran">Olaf Cuaran</a>, King of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. Gyda was the widow of an <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earl</a>, and was searching for a new husband. A great many men had come, but Gyda singled out Olaf, though he was wearing his bad weather clothes, and the other men wore their finest clothing. They were to be married, but another man, Alfvine, took objection, and challenged Olaf and his men to the Scandinavian duel or <a href="/wiki/Holmgang" title="Holmgang">holmgang</a>. Olaf and his men fought Alfvine\'s crew and won every battle, but did not kill any of them, instead they bound them. Alfvine was told to leave the country and never come back again. Gyda and Olaf married, and spent half their time in England and the other half in Ireland.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ascent to the throne">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb 2x" data-file-width="467" data-file-height="274" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sejdmen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>According to <a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla\'s</a> account, <a href="/wiki/Olav_V_of_Norway" title="Olav V of Norway">King Olaf</a> had practitioners of <a href="/wiki/Sei%C3%B0r" title="Sei\xc3\xb0r">sei\xc3\xb0r</a> tied and left on a <a href="/wiki/Skerry" title="Skerry">skerry</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tide</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 995, rumours began to surface in Norway of a king in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> of Norwegian blood. This caught the ear of Jarl Haakon, who sent Thorer Klakka to Ireland, posing as a merchant, to see if he was the son of Tryggve Olafson. Haakon told Thorer that if it were him, to lure him to Norway, so Haakon could have him under his power. Thorer befriended Olaf and told him of the situation in Norway, that Jarl Haakon  had become unpopular with the populace, because he often took daughters of the elite as <a href="/wiki/Concubine" class="mw-redirect" title="Concubine">concubines</a>, which was his right as ruler. He quickly grew tired of them and sent them home after a week or two. He had also been weakened by his fighting with the Danish king, due to his rejection of the Christian faith.\n</p><p>Olaf seized this opportunity, and sailed for Norway. When he arrived many men had already revolted against Haakon, who was forced to hide in a <a href="/wiki/Spider_hole" title="Spider hole">hole</a> dug in a <a href="/wiki/Pigsty" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigsty">pigsty</a>, together with his slave, Kark. When Olaf met the rebels they accepted him as their king, and together they started to search for Haakon. They eventually came to the farm where Haakon and Kark were hiding, but did not find them. Olaf held a meeting just outside the swine-sty and promised a great reward for the man who killed Haakon. The two men in the hole heard this speech, and Haakon became distrustful of Kark, fearing he would kill him for the reward. He could not leave the pigsty, nor could he stay awake indefinitely, and when he fell asleep Kark decapitated Haakon with a knife. The next day Kark went to Olaf and presented him the head of Haakon. King Olaf did not reward him, and instead decapitated him.\n</p><p>After his confirmation as King of Norway, Olaf traveled to the parts of Norway that had not been under the rule of Haakon, but that of the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Denmark" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Denmark">King of Denmark</a>; they also swore allegiance to him. He then demanded that they all be baptised, and most reluctantly they agreed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg/270px-Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg" width="270" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg/405px-Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg/540px-Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg 2x" data-file-width="785" data-file-height="502" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Olav_Tryggvasons_saga_-_Eriks_menn_entrer_Ormen_lange_-_Halfdan_Egedius.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Olaf\'s ship, the "Long Serpent", is attacked during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Svolder" title="Battle of Svolder">Battle of Svolder</a> (Illustration by Halfan Egedius)</div></div></div>\n<p>In 997 Olaf founded his seat of government in <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a>, where he had first held a <i>thing</i> with the revolters against Haakon. It was a suitable site because the <a href="/wiki/Nidelva" title="Nidelva">River Nid</a> twisted itself before going into the fjord, creating a peninsula that could be easily defended against terrestrial attacks by only one short wall.\n</p><p>Olaf continued to promote Christianity throughout his reign. He baptized the explorer <a href="/wiki/Leif_Ericson" class="mw-redirect" title="Leif Ericson">Leif Ericson</a>, who took a priest with him back to <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> to convert the rest of his kin.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Olaf also converted the people and Earl of the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a> to Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  At that time, the Orkney Islands were part of Norway.\n</p><p>It has been suggested that Olaf\'s ambition was to rule a united Christian <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, and it is known that he made overtures of marriage to <a href="/wiki/Sigrid_the_Haughty" title="Sigrid the Haughty">Sigrid the Haughty</a>, queen of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, but negotiations failed because of her steadfast pagan faith. Instead he made an enemy of her, and did not hesitate to involve himself in a quarrel with King <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_I_of_Denmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Sweyn I of Denmark">Sweyn I of Denmark</a> by marrying Sweyn\'s sister Thyre, who had fled from her heathen husband <a href="/wiki/Burislav" title="Burislav">Burislav</a>, the semi-legendary "King of <a href="/wiki/Wends" title="Wends">Wends</a>", in defiance of her brother\'s authority.\n</p><p>Both his Wendish and his Irish wife had brought Olaf wealth and good fortune, but, according to the Sagas, they were also his undoing, for it was on an expedition undertaken in 1000 to wrest her lands from Burislav that he was waylaid off the island Svolder by the combined Swedish, Danish, and Wendish fleets, together with the ships of Earl Haakon\'s sons. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Svolder" title="Battle of Svolder">Battle of Svolder</a> ended in the death of the Norwegian king. Olaf fought to the last on his great vessel <i><a href="/wiki/Ormen_Lange_(longship)" title="Ormen Lange (longship)">Ormrinn Langi</a></i> (Long Serpent), the mightiest ship in the North, and finally leapt overboard and was seen no more.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg/300px-Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg" width="300" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg/450px-Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg/600px-Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1254" data-file-height="970" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter-nicolai-arbo-svolder.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Slaget ved Svolder</i><br />Peter Nicolai Arbo</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacies">Legacies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_successor">Possible successor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Possible successor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the early 11th century a Viking chieftain named <a href="/wiki/Tryggvi_the_Pretender" title="Tryggvi the Pretender">Tryggvi</a> invaded Norway, claiming to be the son of Olaf and Gyda. His invasion was defeated by forces loyal to <a href="/wiki/Cnut_the_Great" title="Cnut the Great">Cnut the Great</a>\'s son <a href="/wiki/Svein_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Svein of Norway">Svein of Norway</a>.\n</p><p>An account preserved in <i>Morkinskinna</i> relates that Tryggvi was actually killed by a farmer after the battle. Many years later, when <a href="/wiki/Harald_Hardrada" title="Harald Hardrada">Harald Hardrada</a> was king of Norway, he passed by the site of the battle. The king met an old friend of his who pointed out the alleged assassin. After questioning the purported killer and hearing him confess, King Harald had the man <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanged</a>, citing the familial bond between him and Tryggvi and his duty to avenge the latter\'s death.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rumours_of_survival">Rumours of survival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rumours of survival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For some time after the Battle of Svolder, there were rumors that Olaf had survived his leap into the sea and had made his way to safety. Accounts reported by Oddr Snorrason included sightings of Olaf in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, and elsewhere in Europe and the Mediterranean. Both King <a href="/wiki/Ethelred_the_Unready" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethelred the Unready">Ethelred the Unready</a> and Olaf\'s sister Astrid allegedly received gifts from Olaf long after he was presumed dead. The latest sighting reported by Oddr took place in 1046.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forcible_conversions">Forcible conversions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Forcible conversions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Olaf routinely used force to compel conversion to Christianity, including execution and torture of those who refused.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Several instances of Olaf\'s attempts led to days of remembrance among modern heathens similar to the feast days of Christian <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyrs</a>. <a href="/wiki/Raud_the_Strong" title="Raud the Strong">Raud the Strong</a>, remembered 9 January, refused to convert and, after a failed attempt using a wooden pin to pry open his mouth to insert a snake, was killed by a snake goaded by a hot poker through a drinking horn into Raud\'s mouth and down his throat. Eyvind Kinnrifi, remembered 9 February, likewise refused and was killed by a brazier of hot coals resting on his belly. The possibly apocryphal figure, <a href="/wiki/Sigrid_the_Haughty" title="Sigrid the Haughty">Sigrid the Haughty</a>, remembered 9 November, was said to have refused to marry Olaf if it meant forgoing her forefathers\' religion, upon which Olaf slapped her with his glove, an act that prompted her to unite his enemies against him years later.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancestors_from_the_sagas">Ancestors from the sagas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_Tryggvason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ancestors from the sagas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="noresize"><table class="collapsible collapsed" style="margin:0.3em auto;clear:none;min-width:60em;width:auto;font-size:88%;border:1px solid #aaa"><tbody><tr><th style="padding:0.2em 0.3em 0.2em 4.3em;background:none;width:auto">Ancestry of Olaf Tryggvason given in the sagas - many connections are dubious</th></tr><tr><td style="text-align:center"><table style="border-collapse:separate;border-spacing:0;line-height:130%;margin-left:auto;
