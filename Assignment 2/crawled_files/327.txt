1666</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Snorri Sturluson</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (<small>Icelandic:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Icelandic" title="Help:IPA/Icelandic">[\xcb\x88sn\xc9\x94r\xc9\xaa \xcb\x88st\xca\x8frtl\xca\x8fs\xc9\x94n]</a></span>; 1179 \xe2\x80\x93 23 September 1241) was an <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Icelandic</a> historian, poet, and politician. He was elected twice as <a href="/wiki/Lawspeaker" title="Lawspeaker">lawspeaker</a> to the Icelandic parliament, the <a href="/wiki/Althing" title="Althing">Althing</a>. He was the author of the <i><a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">Prose Edda</a></i> or <i>Younger Edda</i>, which consists of <i><a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">Gylfaginning</a></i> ("the fooling of Gylfi"), a narrative of <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1ldskaparm%C3%A1l" title="Sk\xc3\xa1ldskaparm\xc3\xa1l">Sk\xc3\xa1ldskaparm\xc3\xa1l</a></i>, a book of poetic language, and the <i><a href="/wiki/H%C3%A1ttatal" title="H\xc3\xa1ttatal">H\xc3\xa1ttatal</a></i>, a list of verse forms. He was also the author of the <i><a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla</a></i>, a history of the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> kings that begins with legendary material in <i><a href="/wiki/Ynglinga_saga" title="Ynglinga saga">Ynglinga saga</a></i> and moves through to early medieval <a href="/wiki/History_of_Scandinavia" title="History of Scandinavia">Scandinavian history</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> For stylistic and methodological reasons, Snorri is often taken to be the author of <i><a href="/wiki/Egil%27s_saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Egil&#39;s saga">Egil\'s saga</a></i>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Hvammur_%C3%AD_D%C3%B6lum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hvammur \xc3\xad D\xc3\xb6lum (page does not exist)">Hvammur \xc3\xad D\xc3\xb6lum</a><span His parents were Sturla \xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb0arson the elder<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> of Hvammur and his second wife, Gu\xc3\xb0n\xc3\xbd B\xc3\xb6\xc3\xb0varsd\xc3\xb3ttir.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He had two older brothers, \xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb0r Sturluson (b. 1165) and <a href="/wiki/Sighvatr_Sturluson" title="Sighvatr Sturluson">Sighvatr Sturluson</a> (b. 1170), two sisters (Helga and Vigd\xc3\xads) and nine <a href="/wiki/Half-siblings" class="mw-redirect" title="Half-siblings">half-siblings</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Snorri was raised from the age of three (or four) by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3n_Loftsson" title="J\xc3\xb3n Loftsson">J\xc3\xb3n Loftsson</a>, a relative of the Norwegian royal family, in <a href="/wiki/Oddi" title="Oddi">Oddi</a>, Iceland. As Sturla was trying to settle a lawsuit with the priest and chieftain (<i>go\xc3\xb0or\xc3\xb0sma\xc3\xb0r</i>) P\xc3\xa1ll S\xc3\xb6lvason, P\xc3\xa1ll\'s wife (\xc3\x9eorbj\xc3\xb6rg Bjarnard\xc3\xb3ttir) lunged suddenly at him with a knife \xe2\x80\x94 intending, she said, to make him like his one-eyed hero <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> \xe2\x80\x94 but bystanders deflected the blow to his cheek instead.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The resulting settlement would have beggared P\xc3\xa1ll, but J\xc3\xb3n Loftsson intervened in the Althing to mitigate the judgment and, to compensate Sturla, offered to raise and educate Snorri.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Snorri therefore received an excellent education and made connections that he might not otherwise have made.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He attended the school of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A6mundr_fr%C3%B3%C3%B0i" title="S\xc3\xa6mundr fr\xc3\xb3\xc3\xb0i">S\xc3\xa6mundr fr\xc3\xb3\xc3\xb0i</a>, grandfather of J\xc3\xb3n Loftsson, at Oddi, and never returned to his parents\' home. His father died in 1183 and his mother as guardian soon wasted Snorri\'s share of the inheritance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Source needed for second part of sentence (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> J\xc3\xb3n Loftsson died in 1197. The two families then arranged a marriage in 1199 between Snorri and Herd\xc3\xads, the daughter of Bersi Vermundarson. From her father, Snorri inherited an estate at Borg and a chieftainship. He soon acquired more property and chieftainships.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Snorri and Herd\xc3\xads were together for four years at Borg. They had at least two children, Hallbera and J\xc3\xb3n. The marriage succumbed to Snorri\'s philandering, and in 1206, he settled in <a href="/wiki/Reykholt,_Western_Iceland" title="Reykholt, Western Iceland">Reykholt</a> as manager of an estate there, but without Herd\xc3\xads. He made significant improvements to the estate, including a hot outdoor bath (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Snorralaug&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Snorralaug (page does not exist)">Snorralaug</a>). The bath and the buildings have been preserved to some extent.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> During the initial years at Reykholt he fathered five children by three different women: Gu\xc3\xb0r\xc3\xban Hreinsd\xc3\xb3ttir, Oddn\xc3\xbd, and \xc3\x9eur\xc3\xad\xc3\xb0ur Hallsd\xc3\xb3ttir.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_life">National life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snorri_Sturluson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Snorri quickly became known as a poet, but was also a lawyer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1215, he became <a href="/wiki/Lawspeaker" title="Lawspeaker">lawspeaker</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Althing" title="Althing">Althing</a>, the only public office of the Icelandic commonwealth and a position of high respect. In the summer of 1218, he left the lawspeaker position and sailed to <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, by royal invitation. There he became well acquainted with the teen-aged King <a href="/wiki/Haakon_IV_of_Norway" title="Haakon IV of Norway">H\xc3\xa1kon H\xc3\xa1konarson</a> and his co-regent, <a href="/wiki/Skule_B%C3%A5rdsson" title="Skule B\xc3\xa5rdsson">Jarl Sk\xc3\xbali</a>. He spent the winter as house-guest of the jarl. They showered gifts upon him, including the ship in which he sailed, and he in return wrote poetry about them. In the summer of 1219 he met his Swedish colleague, the <a href="/wiki/Lawspeaker" title="Lawspeaker">lawspeaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Eskil_Magnusson" title="Eskil Magnusson">Eskil Magnusson</a>, and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Kristina_Nilsdotter" title="Kristina Nilsdotter">Kristina Nilsdotter Blake</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Skara" title="Skara">Skara</a>. They were both related to royalty and probably gave Snorri an insight into the history of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Snorri was mainly interested in history and culture. The Norwegian regents, however, cultivated Snorri, made him a <i><a href="/wiki/Norwegian_noble_titles#Titles_of_the_old_nobility_(1st_system)" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwegian noble titles">skutilsvein</a></i>, a senior title roughly equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a>, and received an oath of loyalty. The king hoped to extend his realm to Iceland, which he could do by a resolution of the <a href="/wiki/Althing" title="Althing">Althing</a>, of which Snorri had been a key member.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 1220, Snorri returned to Iceland and by 1222 was back as lawspeaker of the Althing, which he held this time until 1232. The basis of his election was entirely his fame as a poet. Politically he was the king\'s spokesman, supporting union with Norway, a platform that acquired him enemies among the chiefs. In 1224, Snorri married Hallveig Ormsdottir (c. 1199\xe2\x80\x931241), a granddaughter of J\xc3\xb3n Loftsson, now a widow of great means with two young sons, and made a contract of joint property ownership (or <i>helmingaf\xc3\xa9lag</i>) with her.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Their children did not survive to adulthood, but Hallveig\'s sons and seven of Snorri\'s children did live to adulthood.\n</p><p>Snorri was the most powerful chieftain in Iceland during the years 1224\xe2\x80\x931230.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Failure_in_Iceland">Failure in Iceland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snorri_Sturluson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Failure in Iceland">edit</a><span Snorri\'s strategy was to consolidate power over them, at which point he could offer Iceland to the king. His first moves were civic. On the death in 1222 of S\xc3\xa6mundur, son of J\xc3\xb3n Loftsson, he became a suitor for the hand of his daughter, S\xc3\xb3lveig. Herd\xc3\xads\' silent vote did nothing for his suit. His nephew, Sturla Sighvatsson, Snorri\'s political opponent, stepped in to marry her in 1223, the year before Snorri met Hallveig.\n</p><p>A period of clan feuding followed. Snorri perceived that only resolute, saga-like actions could achieve his objective, but he proved unwilling or incapable of carrying them out. He raised an armed party under another nephew, B\xc3\xb6\xc3\xb0var \xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb0arson, and another under his son, \xc3\x93r\xc3\xa6kja, with the intent of executing a first strike against his brother Sighvatur and Sturla Sighvatsson. On the eve of battle he dismissed those forces and offered terms to his brother.\n</p><p>Sighvatur and Sturla with a force of 1000 men drove Snorri into the countryside, where he sought refuge among the other chiefs. \xc3\x93r\xc3\xa6kja undertook <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Guerrilla">guerrilla</a> operations in the fjords of western Iceland and the war was on.\n</p><p>Haakon IV made an effort to intervene from afar, inviting all the chiefs of Iceland to a peace conference in Norway. This maneuver was transparent to Sighvatur, who understood, as apparently Snorri did not, what could happen to the chiefs in Norway. Instead of killing his opponents he began to insist that they take the king up on his offer.\n</p><p>This was \xc3\x93r\xc3\xa6kja\'s fate, who was captured by Sturla during an ostensible peace negotiation at Reykjaholt, and also of \xc3\x9eorleifur \xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb0arson, a cousin of Snorri\'s, who came to his assistance with 800 men and was deserted by Snorri on the battlefield in a flare-up over the chain of command. In 1237, Snorri thought it best to join the king.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_end_of_Snorri_and_the_Commonwealth">The end of Snorri and the Commonwealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snorri_Sturluson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The end of Snorri and the Commonwealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Sturlungs" title="Age of the Sturlungs">Age of the Sturlungs</a></div>\n<p>The reign of <a href="/wiki/Haakon_IV_of_Norway" title="Haakon IV of Norway">Haakon IV</a> (H\xc3\xa1kon H\xc3\xa1konarson),  <a href="/wiki/List_of_Norwegian_monarchs" title="List of Norwegian monarchs">King of Norway</a>, was troubled by civil war relating to questions of succession and was at various times divided into quasi-independent regions under contenders. There were always plots against the king and questions of loyalty but nevertheless managed to build up the Norwegian state from what it had been.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>When Snorri arrived in Norway for the second time, it was clear to the king that he was no longer a reliable agent. The conflict between Haakon and Sk\xc3\xbali was beginning to escalate into civil war. Snorri stayed with the jarl, or chief, and his son and the jarl made him a jarl hoping to command his allegiance. In August 1238, Sigvat and four of his sons (Sturla, Mark\xc3\xbas, Kolbeinn, and \xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb0ur Kr\xc3\xb3kur, the latter two being executed after the battle), were killed at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_%C3%96rlygssta%C3%B0ir" title="Battle of \xc3\x96rlygssta\xc3\xb0ir">Battle of \xc3\x96rlygssta\xc3\xb0ir</a> in Iceland against <a href="/wiki/Gissur_%C3%9Eorvaldsson" title="Gissur \xc3\x9eorvaldsson">Gissur \xc3\x9eorvaldsson</a> and Kolbein the Young, chiefs whom they had provoked. Snorri, \xc3\x93r\xc3\xa6kja, and \xc3\x9eorleifur requested permission to return home. As the king now could not predict Snorri\'s behavior, permission was denied. He was explicitly ordered to remain in Norway on the basis of his honorary rank. Sk\xc3\xbali on the other hand gave permission and helped them book passage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Snorri must have had his own ideas about the king\'s position and the validity of his orders, but at any rate he chose to disobey them; his words according to <i>Sturlunga saga</i>, \'\xc3\xbat vil ek\' (literally \'I want out\', but idiomatically \'I will go home\'), have become proverbial in Icelandic.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He returned to Iceland in 1239.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The king was distracted by the necessity to confront Sk\xc3\xbali, who declared himself king in 1239. He was defeated militarily and killed in 1240. Meanwhile, Snorri resumed his chieftainship and made a bid to crush Gissur by prosecuting him in court for the deaths of Sigvat and Sturla. A meeting of the Althing was arranged for the summer of 1241 but Gissur and Kolbein arrived with several hundred men. Snorri and 120 men formed around a church. Gissur chose to pay fines rather than to attack.\n</p><p>Meanwhile, in 1240, after the jarl\'s defeat, but before his removal from the scene, Haakon sent two agents to Gissur bearing a secret letter with orders to kill or capture Snorri. Gissur was being invited now to join the unionist movement, which he could accept or refuse, just as he pleased. His initial bid to take Snorri at the Althing failed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Hallveig died of natural causes. When the family bickered over the inheritance, Hallveig\'s sons, Klaeing and Orm, asked assistance from their uncle Gissur. Holding a meeting with them and Kolbein the Younger, Gissur brought out the letter. Orm refused. Shortly after, Snorri received a letter in <a href="/wiki/Cipher_runes" title="Cipher runes">cipher runes</a> warning him of the plot, but he could not understand them.<sup id="cite_ref-enoksen3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enoksen3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gissur led seventy men on a daring raid to his house, achieving complete surprise. Snorri Sturluson was assassinated in his house at <a href="/wiki/Reykholt,_Western_Iceland" title="Reykholt, Western Iceland">Reykholt</a> in autumn of 1241. It is not clear that he was given the option of surrender. He fled to the cellar. There, <a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%ADmon_kn%C3%BAtur&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="S\xc3\xadmon kn\xc3\xbatur (page does not exist)">S\xc3\xadmon kn\xc3\xbatur</a> asked <a href="/wiki/%C3%81rni_beiskur" title="\xc3\x81rni beiskur">Arni the Bitter</a> to strike him. Then Snorri said: <i>Eigi skal h\xc3\xb6ggva!</i>\xe2\x80\x94"Do not strike!" S\xc3\xadmon answered: "H\xc3\xb6gg \xc3\xbe\xc3\xba!" \xe2\x80\x94 "You strike now!" Snorri replied: <i>Eigi skal h\xc3\xb6ggva!</i>\xe2\x80\x94"Do not strike!" and these were his last words.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This act was not popular in either Iceland or Norway. To diminish the odium, the king insisted that if Snorri had submitted he would have been spared. The fact that he could make such an argument reveals how far his influence in Iceland had come. Haakon went on suborning the chiefs of Iceland. In 1262, the Althing ratified union with Norway and royal authority was instituted in Iceland. Each member swore an oath of personal loyalty to the king, a practice which continued as each new king came to the throne, until absolute and hereditary monarchy was formally accepted by the Icelanders in 1662.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snorri_Sturluson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Snorri Sturluson\'s writings provide information and indications concerning persons and events influencing the peoples inhabiting North Europe during periods for which relevant information is scarce: thus, for example, he can be used to illuminate relations between England and Scandinavia during the 10th and 11th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Snorri is considered a figure of enduring importance<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> in this regard,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Halvdan_Koht" title="Halvdan Koht">Halvdan Koht</a> describing his work as "surpassing anything else that the Middle Ages have left us of historical literature".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  He also provided an early account of the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To an extent, the legacy of Snorri Sturluson also played a role in politics long after his death. His writings could be used in support of the claims of later Norwegian kings concerning the venerability and extent of their rule. Later, <i>Heimskringla</i> factored in establishing a national identity during the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_union_between_Norway_and_Sweden_in_1905" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissolution of the union between Norway and Sweden in 1905">Norwegian national independence movement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Icelanders" title="Icelanders">Icelandic</a> perception of Snorri in the 20th century and to date has been colored by the historical views adopted when they sought to <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Independence_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Icelandic Independence Movement">sever their ties with Denmark</a>, any revision of which still has strong nationalistic sentiments to contend with. To serve such views, Snorri and other leading Icelanders of his time are sometimes judged with some <a href="/wiki/Presentism_(literary_and_historical_analysis)" title="Presentism (literary and historical analysis)">presentism</a>, on the basis of concepts that came into vogue only centuries later, such as <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snorri_Sturluson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Snorres gate, a street in the district of  <a href="/wiki/St._Hanshaugen" title="St. Hanshaugen">St. Hanshaugen</a> in Oslo, was named in his honor during 1896.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> There\'s also  <i>Snorrabraut</i>, a thoroughfare in Eastern Central Reykjavik, Iceland, dating from the 1940s.</li>\n<li>A statue of Snorri Sturluson, by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Vigeland" title="Gustav Vigeland">Gustav Vigeland</a>, is located at Reykholt. The Norwegian Government donated the statue to the Icelandic nation in 1947.<sup id="cite_ref-kjelda_2007_3_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kjelda_2007_3-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>. The original intention of donating it on the 700th anniversary of Snorri\'s death, was precluded by <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. A copy of the Reykholt statue was unveiled in  <a href="/wiki/Bergen,_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Bergen, Norway">Bergen, Norway</a> during  1948.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>A model of the Reykholt statue appeared on an Icelandic commemorative postage stamp in 1941.<sup id="cite_ref-kjelda_2007_3_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kjelda_2007_3-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The 700th anniversary of his death was also recognized by the issue of a set of six Norwegian commemorative postage stamps during 1941. Each stamp featured   illustrations from <i>Heimskringla</i> by Norwegian artist <a href="/wiki/Harald_Damsleth" title="Harald Damsleth">Harald Damsleth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a
