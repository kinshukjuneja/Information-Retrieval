href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> January 13, 1732)<sup id="cite_ref-dates_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dates-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> \xe2\x80\x93 May 9, 1810) was an American army officer. He served as a <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">major general</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. Lincoln is notable for being involved in three major surrenders during the war: his participation in the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Saratoga" title="Battles of Saratoga">Battles of Saratoga</a> (sustaining a wound shortly afterward) contributed to <a href="/wiki/John_Burgoyne" title="John Burgoyne">John Burgoyne</a>\'s surrender of a British army, he oversaw the largest American surrender of the war at the 1780 <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Charleston" title="Siege of Charleston">Siege of Charleston</a>, and, as <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>\'s second in command, he formally accepted the British surrender at <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown" title="Siege of Yorktown">Yorktown</a>.\n</p><p>After the war Lincoln was active in politics in his native <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, running several times for lieutenant governor but only winning one term in that office. He served from 1781 to 1783 as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">United States Secretary of War</a>. In 1787, Lincoln led a militia army (privately funded by Massachusetts merchants) in the suppression of <a href="/wiki/Shays%27_Rebellion" title="Shays&#39; Rebellion">Shays\' Rebellion</a>, and was a strong supporter of the new <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>. He was for many of his later years the politically influential customs collector of the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Boston" title="Port of Boston">Port of Boston</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Benjamin Lincoln was born on January 24, 1733,<sup id="cite_ref-dates_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dates-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Hingham,_Massachusetts" title="Hingham, Massachusetts">Hingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay" title="Province of Massachusetts Bay">Province of Massachusetts Bay</a>, the sixth child and first son of Colonel Benjamin Lincoln and his second wife Elizabeth Thaxter Lincoln.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Lincoln\'s ancestors were among those who first settled in Hingham, beginning with Thomas Lincoln \'the cooper,\' who was among several Lincolns who settled in Hingham when it was part of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony" title="Massachusetts Bay Colony">Massachusetts Bay Colony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Lincoln\'s father, one of the wealthiest men in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_Massachusetts" title="Suffolk County, Massachusetts">Suffolk County</a>, served as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Governor%27s_Council" title="Massachusetts Governor&#39;s Council">governor\'s council</a> from 1753 until 1770, and occupied many other civic posts before his death in 1771.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Lincoln\'s maternal grandfather, Col. <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Thaxter" title="Samuel Thaxter">Samuel Thaxter</a>, one of the most prominent and influential citizens in Hingham, became Colonel in a regiment and one of those commissioned to settle the boundary between Massachusetts and Rhode Island in 1719.\n</p><p>In his early life, Lincoln worked on the family farm, and attended the local school.  He followed his father into public life, becoming town constable at 21, and in 1755 he joined the 3rd Regiment of the Suffolk County militia (where his father was colonel) as an adjutant.<sup id="cite_ref-M14_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M14-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  In 1756, at the age of 23, Lincoln married Mary Cushing, daughter of Elijah Cushing of <a href="/wiki/Pembroke,_Massachusetts" title="Pembroke, Massachusetts">Pembroke, Massachusetts</a>, whose ancestors were also among the founders of Hingham.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They had eleven children, seven of which survived to adulthood.<sup id="cite_ref-M14_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M14-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  In 1757, he was elected the town clerk of Hingham, a post he held for twenty years.  He continued to be active in the militia during the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>, but saw no action, and was promoted to major by the end of the conflict in 1763.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lincoln was elected a Hingham town selectman in 1765, a post to which he held for six years.  During this tenure political opposition rose in the province to Parliamentary tax measures, polarizing the political landscape of the colony.  Lincoln sided with the opposition, eventually becoming a leading force among Hingham\'s <a href="/wiki/Patriot_(American_Revolution)" title="Patriot (American Revolution)">Patriots</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  In 1770, in a list of resolutions passed by the inhabitants of Hingham, Lincoln outlined the measures urged by residents towards the non-importation of British goods, and he condemned the <a href="/wiki/Boston_massacre" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston massacre">Boston massacre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1772, Lincoln was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant colonel (United States)">lieutenant colonel</a> of the 3rd Regiment of the Suffolk militia.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  That same year he won election as a representative of the town to the provincial assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Revolution">American Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: American Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With the arrival of General <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gage" title="Thomas Gage">Thomas Gage</a> as governor of the colony in 1774, the provincial assembly was dissolved, but reformed itself into the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Provincial_Congress" title="Massachusetts Provincial Congress">Massachusetts Provincial Congress</a>.  Lincoln continued to win election to this body, and was placed on committees overseeing militia organization and supply, a position that came to be of utmost importance when the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> broke out with the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a> in April 1775.  He was then appointed to the congress\' committee of safety, and also was elected to its executive council, which exercised executive authority over the province outside <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">besieged Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  He was deeply involved in ensuring that supplies of all sorts reached the nascent <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> outside Boston, procuring supplies from blankets to gunpowder.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_of_New_York">Defense of New York</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Defense of New York">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In January 1776, Lincoln was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">major general</a> of the Massachusetts militia, overseeing the coastal defenses of the state.  After the British <a href="/wiki/Fortification_of_Dorchester_Heights" title="Fortification of Dorchester Heights">evacuated Boston</a>, he and Continental Army General <a href="/wiki/Artemas_Ward" title="Artemas Ward">Artemas Ward</a> oversaw attempts to improve the state\'s coastal fortifications, and he was ordered to hold the state\'s militia brigades in readiness in case the British returned.  In May 1776 he directed the state forces that successfully drove the last <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> ships from <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harbor" title="Boston Harbor">Boston Harbor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite his lack of combat experience, Lincoln began lobbying state representatives to the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> officers commission, anticipating that the aging and ill General Ward might soon step down.  The idea was generally well received, with one representative writing that Lincoln was "a good man for a Brigadier General" and "a man of abilities", even though he had not "had much experience".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  While a Continental commission was not immediately forthcoming, Lincoln was placed in command of a brigade of militia the state sent to join General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York Town</a> in September 1776.<sup id="cite_ref-M27_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M27-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When Lincoln reached southwestern <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, Washington first ordered him to prepare an expedition across <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Sound" title="Long Island Sound">Long Island Sound</a> to raid British positions on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>.  The expedition was aborted when Washington began to retreat from New York after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Long_Island" title="Battle of Long Island">Battle of Long Island</a> (<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brooklyn" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>) in August 1776, and Lincoln was ordered to bring two regiments to join Washington\'s army as it later retreated northward from <a href="/wiki/New_York_and_New_Jersey_campaign" title="New York and New Jersey campaign">New York Town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M27_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M27-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  Lincoln\'s troops secured the Continental retreat to <a href="/wiki/White_Plains,_New_York" title="White Plains, New York">White Plains, New York</a>, and were in the main Continental formation during the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Plains" title="Battle of White Plains">Battle of White Plains</a> in October 1776; this portion of the troops saw no action in the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  The enlistment terms of his men expiring, Lincoln returned briefly to Massachusetts to take command of new recruits for the coming year\'s campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  Based on a recommendation from General Washington as "a gentleman well worthy of notice in the Military Line", Congress commissioned Lincoln a major general in the Continental Army on February 14, 1777.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lincoln\'s first command was that of a forward outpost at <a href="/wiki/Bound_Brook,_New_Jersey" title="Bound Brook, New Jersey">Bound Brook, New Jersey</a>, only 3 miles (4.8&#160;km) from British sentries outside <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick,_New_Jersey" title="New Brunswick, New Jersey">New Brunswick</a>. He established his headquarters at the nearby <a href="/wiki/Van_Horne_House" title="Van Horne House">Van Horne House</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Forage_War" title="Forage War">months of skirmishing</a>, his post was the target of a surprise attack on April 13, 1777.  In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bound_Brook" title="Battle of Bound Brook">Battle of Bound Brook</a> he was defeated by a much larger force under the command of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Lord Cornwallis</a>, barely escaping capture.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saratoga">Saratoga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Saratoga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In July Washington dispatched three of his best officers, Colonel Morgan, General Arnold and General Lincoln north to assist <a href="/wiki/Philip_Schuyler" title="Philip Schuyler">Philip Schuyler</a>, and afterward Schuyler\'s replacement <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Gates" title="Horatio Gates">Horatio Gates</a>, in the defense of upstate <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> against the advance of General <a href="/wiki/John_Burgoyne" title="John Burgoyne">John Burgoyne</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Saratoga_campaign" title="Saratoga campaign">expedition from Quebec</a>.  Lincoln was ordered to coordinate the activities of the New England militia being recruited for the effort.  General Schuyler asked Lincoln, from a base in southern <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>, to harass the British supply line as the British moved south from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Ticonderoga" title="Fort Ticonderoga">Fort Ticonderoga</a> toward <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany</a>.  Lincoln\'s job was complicated by New Hampshire\'s militia leader <a href="/wiki/John_Stark" title="John Stark">John Stark</a>, who categorically refused to put his forces under Lincoln\'s command.  Stark had previously held a Continental commission and resigned over a promotion snub, and had only agreed to serve in his native New Hampshire if he was not under Continental authority.  Stark led his forces to victory in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bennington" title="Battle of Bennington">Battle of Bennington</a> in mid-August, while Lincoln\'s force was still growing.  By early September Lincoln had 2,000 men under his command, and launched several detachments at the British supply line.  One of those detachments, led by Colonel <a href="/wiki/John_Brown_of_Pittsfield" title="John Brown of Pittsfield">John Brown</a>, successfully harassed British positions outside Fort Ticonderoga, freeing American prisoners and even making an unsuccessful attempt to capture the fort in late September.  By this time General Gates, who had taken command from Schuyler in August, had ordered Lincoln\'s force to join him near <a href="/wiki/Stillwater,_New_York" title="Stillwater, New York">Stillwater, New York</a>.  Lincoln arrived on September 22, three days after the strategically conclusive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Freeman%27s_Farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Freeman&#39;s Farm">Battle of Freeman\'s Farm</a> where  Colonel Morgan\'s sharpshooters killed most of the officers, including British General Fraser, and three quarters of the artillerymen, resulting in the capture of 6 of the 10 British cannons.  There Lincoln\'s militia were to hold the eastern shore of the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>.\n</p><p>Lincoln\'s militia played no role in the American victory at the October 7 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bemis_Heights" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Bemis Heights">Battle of Bemis Heights</a>, since the action took place on the western side of the river, but Lincoln was in command of the American right in the battle.  These forces saw little action, which was concentrated on the American left.  American forces, led by Generals <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enoch_Poor" title="Enoch Poor">Enoch Poor</a>, successfully breached the British defenses, undoing the small gains the British had made at Freeman\'s Farm and compelling Burgoyne to retreat a short distance.  That evening Gates ordered Lincoln to perform reconnaissance; the British left fell back as Lincoln and his 1,500 man force probed them, and Lincoln was able to occupy the former British lines.  In council that evening Lincoln recommended that the ford at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Edward_(village),_New_York" title="Fort Edward (village), New York">Fort Edward</a> be fortified against the possibility that Burgoyne would attempt to reach it and cross the river in an attempt to return to Ticonderoga.  Gates agreed with the plan, and ordered Lincoln\'s militia to do so.  During these movements Lincoln encountered a British company; in the ensuing skirmish Lincoln\'s right ankle was shattered by a musket ball, much like General Arnold was hit in the leg during his charge during the Battle of Bemis Heights, even though General Gates had already relieved Arnold of command due to insubordination in Arnold\'s manner of disagreement with Gates\' battle plans.\n</p><p>Lincoln was transported to Albany, where he was treated, and where he learned of Burgoyne\'s October 17 surrender.  His son helped him return him to Hingham in February 1778, where he convalesced for several months.  The injury left his right leg two inches shorter than the left, and for many years the ankle wound was prone to reopening and the danger of infection.  During his recovery Lincoln learned that General Arnold\'s seniority had been restored, reducing Lincoln to the lowest-ranked major general.  Although he considered resignation over the slight, Washington and supporters in the Continental Congress assured him of his value to the army.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg/175px-Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg" width="175" height="276" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg/263px-Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg/350px-Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2481" data-file-height="3906" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Continental_Congress_John_Hancock_General_Benjamin_Lincoln.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Broadside from <a href="/wiki/John_Hancock" title="John Hancock">John Hancock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a>, with mention of letter from Benjamin Lincoln, 1777. Printed by <a href="/wiki/John_Dunlap" title="John Dunlap">John Dunlap</a></div></div></div>\n<h3><span id="Southern_theater_.281778.E2.80.9381.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_theater_(1778\xe2\x80\x9381)">Southern theater (1778\xe2\x80\x9381)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Southern theater (1778\xe2\x80\x9381)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War">Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Charleston" title="Siege of Charleston">Siege of Charleston</a></div>\n<p>Lincoln rejoined Washington outside New York in August 1778, and was appointed commander of the Southern department in September.  Washington sent Lincoln, Lafayette and Lee (Henry "Light Horse Harry") to the Southern Department to oppose the British army under Clinton and Cornwallis. Lincoln participated in the unsuccessful French-led <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Savannah" title="Siege of Savannah">siege</a> of <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a> in October 1779, after which he retreated to <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>. He took command of the garrison of Charleston. In March 1780 the city was surrounded by a sizable British force dispatched from New York. After a <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Charleston" title="Siege of Charleston">relatively brief siege</a> Lincoln was forced to surrender more than 5,000 men to Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Sir Henry Clinton</a> on May 12, 1780, but did so in a way that allowed the South Carolina militia to escape as well as some Continental forces, that annoyed the British toward Lincoln.\n</p><p>Lincoln, desperate for more troops, had pleaded with the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_legislature" class="mw-redirect" title="South Carolina legislature">South Carolina legislature</a> to arm 1,000 enslaved African Americans to ward off the approaching British. Rather than see armed slaves, the legislature began negotiations with the British commanders to allow the British forces to pass through South Carolina.\n</p><p>The loss of the southern army at Charleston was one of the worst Continental defeats of the war. He was denied the honors of war in surrendering, due to the British annoyance at his facilitating the escape of SC militia units and some Continental forces, such as those with Lafayette and Lee, which deeply rankled Lincoln. Lincoln was <a href="/wiki/Parole#Prisoners_of_war" title="Parole">paroled</a>, and in the court of inquiry no charges were ever brought against him.  The British subsequently sought to enlist large numbers of black soldiers.\n</p><p>After being exchanged for the British Major General <a href="/wiki/William_Phillips_(British_Army_officer)" title="William Phillips (British Army officer)">William Phillips</a> in November 1780,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Lincoln returned to Washington\'s main army.  He led a large portion of the Army south from Head of Elk Maryland to Hampton, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, to march to the west to Yorktown where the British were encamped.   Lincoln played a major role in the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown" title="Siege of Yorktown">Siege of Yorktown</a> and the surrender of Lord Cornwallis on October 19, 1781. Cornwallis plead illness, and so did not attend the surrender ceremony, choosing instead to send his second-in-command, the Irish General <a href="/wiki/Charles_O%27Hara" title="Charles O&#39;Hara">Charles O\'Hara</a>. General Washington refused to accept Cornwallis\' sword from O\'Hara, directing O\'Hara to present it instead to Lincoln, Washington\'s own second-in-command.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Benjamin_lincoln.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Benjamin_lincoln.jpg/175px-Benjamin_lincoln.jpg" width="175" height="273" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Benjamin_lincoln.jpg/263px-Benjamin_lincoln.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Benjamin_lincoln.jpg/350px-Benjamin_lincoln.jpg 2x" data-file-width="920" data-file-height="1437" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Benjamin_lincoln.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Hon. B. Lincoln, Esq., major general in the American Army. Etching from 1782</div></div></div>\n<h3><span id="Secretary_at_War_.281781.E2.80.9383.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Secretary_at_War_(1781\xe2\x80\x9383)">Secretary at War (1781\xe2\x80\x9383)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Secretary at War (1781\xe2\x80\x9383)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From 1781 to late 1783, Lincoln served as the first <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">United States Secretary of War</a>. He was appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="Confederation Congress">Confederation Congress</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation and Perpetual Union</a>, adopted 1781.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> He was succeeded in the post by <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> artillery chief, Gen. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a>, (who later continued in the position under the new <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Federal Constitution</a> of 1787, with first President <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, during his first term, 1789-1795). He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1781.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_politics">Post-war politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-war politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_(1733-1810),_Hingham_Cemetery,_MA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_%281733-1810%29%2C_Hingham_Cemetery%2C_MA.jpg/275px-Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_%281733-1810%29%2C_Hingham_Cemetery%2C_MA.jpg" width="275" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_%281733-1810%29%2C_Hingham_Cemetery%2C_MA.jpg/413px-Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_%281733-1810%29%2C_Hingham_Cemetery%2C_MA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_%281733-1810%29%2C_Hingham_Cemetery%2C_MA.jpg/550px-Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_%281733-1810%29%2C_Hingham_Cemetery%2C_MA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="669" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grave_of_Benjamin_Lincoln_(1733-1810),_Hingham_Cemetery,_MA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tomb of Gen. Benjamin Lincoln, Hingham Cemetery, Hingham, Massachusetts</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1787, Lincoln was a member of the Massachusetts state convention that ratified the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>. Earlier in 1787, Lincoln helped put an end to an uprising of farmers known as <a href="/wiki/Shays%27_Rebellion" title="Shays&#39; Rebellion">Shays\' Rebellion</a>. The uprising, one of whose consequences was increased calls for a stronger central U.S. government, ended when Lincoln\'s militia attacked the rebels and forced them to surrender in February.\n</p><p>Lincoln was one of only 10 men who received electoral votes during the first election for President and Vice President of the  United States,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> receiving the vote of an unrecorded elector from the State of Georgia.\n</p><p>Lincoln also served as one of the first trustees of <a href="/wiki/Derby_Academy_(Hingham)" title="Derby Academy (Hingham)">Derby Academy</a>, founded in <a href="/wiki/Hingham,_Massachusetts" title="Hingham, Massachusetts">Hingham</a> by Sarah Hersey Derby, widow of a wealthy <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Massachusetts" title="Salem, Massachusetts">Salem</a> shipping magnate.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>He stayed active in public life in various capacities, including a term as <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Massachusetts" title="Lieutenant Governor of Massachusetts">Lieutenant Governor of Massachusetts</a> and many years as the Collector of the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Boston" title="Port of Boston">Port of Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He retired from public life in 1809 and died in Hingham on May 9, 1810.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GeneralBenjaminLincolnHouse.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/GeneralBenjaminLincolnHouse.jpg/220px-GeneralBenjaminLincolnHouse.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GeneralBenjaminLincolnHouse.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/General_Benjamin_Lincoln_House" title="General Benjamin Lincoln House">General Benjamin Lincoln House</a>, where Lincoln was born</div></div></div>\n<p>Lincoln is buried in the Old Ship Burying Ground behind the <a href="/wiki/Old_Ship_Church" title="Old Ship Church">Old Ship Church</a> in Hingham. Among the pallbearers at Lincoln\'s funeral were <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton_Tufts" title="Cotton Tufts">Cotton Tufts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Treat_Paine" title="Robert Treat Paine">Robert Treat Paine</a>, Richard Cranch and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Melvill_(American_patriot)" title="Thomas Melvill (American patriot)">Thomas Melvill</a>. In Lincoln\'s honor, the bells at Boston and other places were tolled for an hour; the flags of vessels, and those at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Independence_(Massachusetts)" title="Fort Independence (Massachusetts)">Fort Independence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Warren_(Massachusetts)" title="Fort Warren (Massachusetts)">Fort Warren</a>, as well as those at the <a href="/wiki/Charlestown_Navy_Yard" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlestown Navy Yard">Charlestown Navy Yard</a> were lowered to half-mast.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>He was one of the few men to have been involved in the three major surrenders of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>: twice as a victor (at Yorktown and Saratoga), and once as the defeated party (at Charleston). In spite of the major role he played during the war, he tends to be less well-remembered than many of his contemporaries in the Continental Army.\n</p><p>Places named "Lincoln" in the <a href="/wiki/American_South" class="mw-redirect" title="American South">American South</a> tend to be named after Benjamin Lincoln rather than <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>, the 16th President (the two were not related).  Counties and/or towns in <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Alabama" title="Lincoln, Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_County,_Georgia" title="Lincoln County, Georgia">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_County,_Kentucky" title="Lincoln County, Kentucky">Kentucky</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_County,_Missouri" title="Lincoln County, Missouri">Missouri</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_County,_North_Carolina" title="Lincoln County, North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_County,_Tennessee" title="Lincoln County, Tennessee">Tennessee</a> are named in his honor, as are communities in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lincolnton,_North_Carolina" title="Lincolnton, North Carolina">Lincolnton</a>), <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Vermont" title="Lincoln, Vermont">Lincoln</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lincolnville,_Maine" title="Lincolnville, Maine">Lincolnville</a>).  Streets in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_South_Carolina" title="Columbia, South Carolina">Columbia, South Carolina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>, bear his name, as does Lincoln Hall at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> Training Center in <a href="/wiki/Yorktown,_Virginia" title="Yorktown, Virginia">Yorktown, Virginia</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/General_Benjamin_Lincoln_House" title="General Benjamin Lincoln House">Lincoln\'s lifelong home</a> still stands. It was declared a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1972, and is listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-dates-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-dates_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-dates_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">In the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>, then in use in England, the year began on March 25.
