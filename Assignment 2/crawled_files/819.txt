language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">An Mhuir Cheilteach</i>; <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Y M\xc3\xb4r Celtaidd</i>; <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">An Mor Keltek</i>; <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>: <i lang="br">Ar Mor Keltiek</i>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">La mer Celtique</i>) is the area of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> off the south coast of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> bounded to the east by <a href="/wiki/Saint_George%27s_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint George&#39;s Channel">Saint George\'s Channel</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> other limits include the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel" title="Bristol Channel">Bristol Channel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a>, as well as adjacent portions of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. The southern and western boundaries are delimited by the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>, which drops away sharply. The <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> are an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> of small islands in the sea.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celtic_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Celtic Sea takes its name from the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> heritage of the bounding lands to the north and east.<sup id="cite_ref-Haslam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haslam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The name was first proposed by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_William_Lyons_Holt" title="Ernest William Lyons Holt">E.W.L. Holt</a> at a 1921 meeting in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> of fisheries experts from England, Wales, Ireland, Scotland and France.<sup id="cite_ref-Haslam_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haslam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The northern portion of this sea had previously been considered as part of <a href="/wiki/Saint_George%27s_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint George&#39;s Channel">Saint George\'s Channel</a> and the southern portion as an undifferentiated part of the "<a href="/wiki/Southwest_Approaches" title="Southwest Approaches">Southwest Approaches</a>" to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. The need for a common name came to be felt because of the common <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine biology</a>, geology and <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Haslam_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haslam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was adopted in France before being common in the English-speaking countries;<sup id="cite_ref-Haslam_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haslam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> in 1957 <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Le_Danois" title="\xc3\x89douard Le Danois">\xc3\x89douard Le Danois</a> wrote, "the name <i>Celtic Sea</i> is hardly known even to oceanographers."<sup id="cite_ref-Danois1957_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danois1957-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It was adopted by marine biologists and oceanographers, and later by petroleum exploration firms.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is named in a 1963 British atlas,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> but a 1972 article states "what British maps call the <a href="/wiki/Western_Approaches" title="Western Approaches">Western Approaches</a>, and what the oil industry calls the Celtic Sea [...] certainly the residents on the western coast [of Great Britain] don\'t refer to it as such."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limits">Limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celtic_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are no land features to divide the Celtic Sea from the open Atlantic Ocean to the south and west. For these limits, Holt suggested the 200-<a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathom</a> (370&#160;m; 1,200&#160;ft) marine <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">contour</a> and the island of <a href="/wiki/Ushant" title="Ushant">Ushant</a> off the tip of Brittany.\n</p><p>The definition approved by 1974 by the UK <a href="/wiki/Hydrographer_of_the_Navy" title="Hydrographer of the Navy">Hydrographer of the Navy</a> for use in <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> Charts was "bounded roughly by lines joining Ushant, <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land&#39;s End">Land\'s End</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hartland_Point" title="Hartland Point">Hartland Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lundy_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Lundy Island">Lundy Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Govan%27s_Head" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Govan&#39;s Head">St. Govan\'s Head</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosslare_Harbour" title="Rosslare Harbour">Rosslare</a>, thence following the Irish coast south to <a href="/wiki/Mizen_Head" title="Mizen Head">Mizen Head</a> and then along the 200-metre <a href="/wiki/Isobath" class="mw-redirect" title="Isobath">isobath</a> to approximately the latitude of Ushant."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" Head].\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seabed">Seabed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celtic_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seabed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The seabed under the Celtic Sea is called the <b>Celtic Shelf</b>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> of Europe.  The northeast portion has a depth of between 90 and 100&#160;m (300\xe2\x80\x93330&#160;ft), increasing towards Saint George\'s Channel. In the opposite direction, sand ridges pointing southwest have a similar height, separated by troughs approximately 50&#160;m (160&#160;ft) deeper. These ridges were formed by tidal effects when the sea level was lower. South of <a href="/wiki/50th_parallel_north" title="50th parallel north">50\xc2\xb0N</a> the topography is more irregular.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Oil and gas exploration in the Celtic Sea has had limited commercial success. The <a href="/wiki/Kinsale_Head_gas_field" title="Kinsale Head gas field">Kinsale Head gas field</a> supplied much of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> in the 1980s and 1990s.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_of_the_Celtic_Sea">Ecology of the Celtic Sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celtic_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology of the Celtic Sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Celtic Sea has a rich fishery with total annual catches of 1.8 million tonnes as of 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Four <a href="/wiki/Cetacean" class="mw-redirect" title="Cetacean">cetacean</a> species occur frequently in the area: <a href="/wiki/Minke_whale" title="Minke whale">minke whale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin" title="Bottlenose dolphin">bottlenose dolphin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short-beaked_common_dolphin" title="Short-beaked common dolphin">short-beaked common dolphin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harbor_porpoise" class="mw-redirect" title="Harbor porpoise">harbor porpoise</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Formerly it held an abundance of <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammals" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine mammals">marine mammals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12"
