id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the Bishop Rock in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Bank" title="Cortes Bank">Cortes Bank</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n\n</p><p>The <b>Bishop Rock</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Men Epskop</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a very small islet in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> known for its <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a>. \nIt is in the westernmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> 45&#160;km (28&#160;mi) off the southwestern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornish peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinness Book of Records">Guinness Book of Records</a></i> lists it as the world\'s smallest <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">island</a> with a building on it.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The original iron lighthouse was begun in 1847 but was washed away before it could be completed. The present building was completed in 1858 and was first lit on 1 September that year. Prior to the installation of the helipad, visitors to the lighthouse would rappel from the top (with winches installed at the lamp level and at the base below) to boats waiting away from the lighthouse.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bishop Rock is also at the eastern end of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> shipping route used by ocean liners in the first half of the 20th century; the western end being the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Lower_New_York_Bay" title="Lower New York Bay">Lower New York Bay</a>. This was the route that ocean liners took when competing for the Transatlantic speed record, known as the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Riband" title="Blue Riband">Blue Riband</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bishop_Rock,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the late 13th century, when the Isles of Scilly were under the jurisdiction of John de Allet and his wife Isabella, anyone convicted of felony \xe2\x80\xb3<i>ought to be taken to a certain rock in the sea, with two barley loaves and a pitcher of water and left until the sea swallowed him up</i>\xe2\x80\xb3.<sup id="cite_ref-larn92_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-larn92-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The rock was recorded as <i>Maen Escop</i> in 1284 and <i>Maenenescop</i> in 1302. In Welsh, Maen Esgob means Bishop Rock. The outer rocks to the west of <a href="/wiki/St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Agnes, Isles of Scilly">St Agnes</a> used to be known as the Bishop and Clerk, but exactly how they acquired their names is not known for certain. One explanation is that when a fleet of <a href="/wiki/Cargo_ship" title="Cargo ship">merchantmen</a> out of Spain were wrecked 200 years ago, only Miles Bishop and John and Henry Clerk survived.<sup id="cite_ref-larn92_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-larn92-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Another possible explanation is that the shape of the rock is similar to a bishop\'s <a href="/wiki/Mitre" title="Mitre">mitre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Maginnis_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maginnis-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>East of Bishop Rock are the <a href="/wiki/Western_Rocks,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Western Rocks, Isles of Scilly">Western Rocks</a> and the Gilstone Reef,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> where <a href="/wiki/Cloudesley_Shovell" title="Cloudesley Shovell">Admiral Shovell</a>\'s flagship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Association_(1697)" class="mw-redirect" title="HMS Association (1697)">HMS <i>Association</i></a> was wrecked in the <a href="/wiki/Scilly_naval_disaster_of_1707" title="Scilly naval disaster of 1707">great naval disaster of 1707</a>. Shovell\'s remains were repatriated to England by order of Queen Anne shortly after their initial burial in the Isles of Scilly.\n</p><p>The earliest recorded wreck on the rock itself was in 1839, when the brig <i>Theodorick</i> struck in rough misty weather on 4 September. She was out of <a href="/wiki/Essaouira" title="Essaouira">Mogodore</a> for London carrying a general cargo. In the early hours of 12 October 1842, the 600-tonne <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer" title="Paddle steamer">paddle steamer</a> <i>Brigand</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Packet_boat" title="Packet boat">packet boat</a>, which was en route from <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> to <a href="/wiki/St_Petersburg" class="mw-redirect" title="St Petersburg">St Petersburg</a>, struck the rock with such force that it stove in two large bow plates. The rocks then acted as a pivot, and she swung round and heeled into the rock port side, crushing the paddle-wheel and box to such an extent that it penetrated the engine room. She drifted over seven miles in two hours, before sinking in 90 m. All the crew were saved.<sup id="cite_ref-Maginnis_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maginnis-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1901 a <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">barque</a> named <i>Falkland</i> struck the rock, her main yard hitting the lighthouse itself.<sup id="cite_ref-nty1901_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nty1901-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span drawing by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Eugenio_Ribera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Eugenio Ribera (page does not exist)">Jos\xc3\xa9 Eugenio Ribera</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PHE_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PHE-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>An 1818 Report by the Surveyor\xe2\x80\x93General of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> on the dangers to shipping in Cornwall proposed that a lighthouse be built, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a>, upon Bishop Rock, given its location as the westernmost rock of the Isles of Scilly. The plan was considered by the Government and building was expected soon, as the engineer <a href="/wiki/John_Rennie_the_Elder" title="John Rennie the Elder">John Rennie the Elder</a> made an offer to build it.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Government did not take up the offer, but <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a> surveyed Bishop Rock in 1843, with a view to building a lighthouse, and work began in 1847.<sup id="cite_ref-CN114_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN114-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The engineer in chief, <a href="/wiki/James_Walker_(engineer)" title="James Walker (engineer)">James Walker</a>, decided on a 120-foot-tall (37&#160;m) design consisting of accommodation and a light on top of iron legs.<sup id="cite_ref-CN114_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN114-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The light was never lit, since on 5 February 1850 a storm washed the tower away.<sup id="cite_ref-CN114_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN114-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the second attempt, James Walker began building a stone structure in 1851.<sup id="cite_ref-CN114_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN114-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The site presented a number of difficulties: the paucity of available land area and the slope of the rock meant that the lowest stone had to be laid below the low water level of the lowest spring tides.<sup id="cite_ref-CN116_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN116-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The resident engineer was Nicholas Douglass assisted by first <a href="/wiki/James_Nicholas_Douglass" title="James Nicholas Douglass">James</a> and then <a href="/wiki/William_Douglass_(engineer,_born_1831)" title="William Douglass (engineer, born 1831)">WIlliam</a> his sons. Despite multiple problems, the tower was completed without loss of life, and the lighthouse shone its first light on 1 September 1858.<sup id="cite_ref-CN116_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN116-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The total cost of the lighthouse was \xc2\xa334,559.<sup id="cite_ref-CN116_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN116-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovation">Renovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bishop_Rock,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1881, Sir <a href="/wiki/James_Nicholas_Douglass" title="James Nicholas Douglass">James Nicholas Douglass</a> inspected the tower and designed a renovation to reinforce the structure and increase its height by 40 feet. The work was begun in 1882 and completed in 1887, under the supervision of Douglass\'s eldest surviving son, <a href="/wiki/William_Douglass_(engineer)" title="William Douglass (engineer)">William Tregarthen Douglass</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bishop_Rock,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Bishop Lighthouse is often referred to as <b>"King of the lighthouses"</b> and it is indeed a very impressive structure. With a height of 49 metres (161&#160;ft) the lighthouse is the equal tallest in England, together with <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-trinityhouse_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trinityhouse-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Expenditure on the lighthouses at Bishop Rock, include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The first iron lighthouse: \xc2\xa312,500<sup id="cite_ref-trinityhouse_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trinityhouse-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The second granite lighthouse: \xc2\xa334,559 <sup id="cite_ref-trinityhouse_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trinityhouse-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> (equivalent to \xc2\xa33,192,000 in 2016) <sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The third improved lighthouse: \xc2\xa364,889<sup id="cite_ref-trinityhouse_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trinityhouse-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> (equivalent to \xc2\xa36,622,000 in 2016) <sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Total cost: \xc2\xa3111,948 <sup id="cite_ref-trinityhouse_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trinityhouse-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The interior of the light house consists of the following:<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Below and inside the lighthouse are 10 floors<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> with spiral staircase to the 2nd floor with a door (made from gun metal (likely bronze) and installed in 1887<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>) that leads down an external metal (likely bronze) ladder to climb down to the large exterior base. From the base another metal ladder provides access to a stone staircase to the water line.\n</p><p>Here is what is in the lighthouse:\n</p>\n<ul><li>1st floor \xe2\x80\x93 Water tank (providing fresh water for lighthouse keeper)</li>\n<li>2nd floor \xe2\x80\x93 Entrance room with metal door leading to exterior ladder to base below</li>\n<li>3rd floor \xe2\x80\x93 Store room, with window</li>\n<li>4th floor \xe2\x80\x93 First oil room, with oil tanks formerly used to light the lamp</li>\n<li>5th floor \xe2\x80\x93 Second oil room, with window</li>\n<li>6th floor \xe2\x80\x93 Living room for lighthouse keeper, with window</li>\n<li>7th floor \xe2\x80\x93 Bedroom for lighthouse keeper, with window</li>\n<li>8th floor \xe2\x80\x93 Store room</li>\n<li>9th floor \xe2\x80\x93 Service room</li>\n<li>10th floor \xe2\x80\x93 Lamp</li></ul>\n<p>Difficulty reaching the lighthouse by boat led Trinity House to build a helipad on top of the lighthouse in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-CN126_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN126-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The tower has been fully automated since 15 December 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-CN127_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CN127-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bishop_Rock,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The rock is the subject of a short orchestral descriptive work by the late <a href="/wiki/Doreen_Carwithen" title="Doreen Carwithen">Doreen Carwithen</a> (Mary Alwyn) which has been recorded by the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a> under the baton of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hickox" title="Richard Hickox">Richard Hickox</a>.\n</p><p>The lighthouse was used as a filming location for one of the current <a href="/wiki/BBC_One_%27Circle%27_idents" title="BBC One &#39;Circle&#39; idents">BBC One Idents</a> and was also featured in the last segment of the documentary series <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Men_in_a_Boat_(TV_Series)#Three_Men_in_Another_Boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Three Men in a Boat (TV Series)">Three Men in More Than One Boat</a></i>. The lighthouse was also featured in the 2010 <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> documentary <a href="/wiki/Islands_of_Britain_(tv_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Islands of Britain (tv series)">Islands of Britain</a>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Clunes" title="Martin Clunes">Martin Clunes</a>.\n</p><p>The lighthouse featured in <a href="/wiki/BBC_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC TV">BBC TV</a> children\'s programme <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Peter" title="Blue Peter">Blue Peter</a></i> in 1975, when presenter <a href="/wiki/Lesley_Judd" title="Lesley Judd">Lesley Judd</a> visited but "looked like she was about to plunge into the murky depths"<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> "disaster nearly struck as she travelled by rope to the lighthouse from a boat. Her harness snapped, leaving Lesley with no support should she lose her grip on the rope."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The lighthouse
