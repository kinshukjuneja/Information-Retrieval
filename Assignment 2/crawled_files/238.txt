(2,016)</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Common Travel Area</b> (<b>CTA</b>; <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Comhlimist\xc3\xa9ar Taistil</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Open_border" title="Open border">open borders</a> area comprising the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>. The <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a> are not included. Based on agreements that are not legally binding, the internal borders of the Common Travel Area (CTA) are subject to minimal controls, if any, and can normally be crossed by British and Irish citizens with minimal <a href="/wiki/Identity_document" title="Identity document">identity documents</a> with certain exceptions.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NoID_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoID-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The maintenance of the CTA involves considerable co-operation on immigration matters between the British and Irish authorities.\n</p><p>In 2014, the British and Irish governments began a trial system of mutual recognition of each other\'s visas for onward travel within the Common Travel Area. As of June 2016 it applies to Chinese and Indian nationals and is limited to certain visa types. Other nationalities and those holding non-qualifying visas still require separate visas to visit both countries and may not avail of a transit visa exception if wishing to transit though the UK to Ireland.\n</p><p>Since 1997, the Irish government has imposed systematic identity checks on air passengers coming from the United Kingdom and selective checks on sea passengers, and occasional checks on land crossings.<sup id="cite_ref-irishcontrols_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irishcontrols-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1923_agreement">1923 agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1923 agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> seceded from the United Kingdom in 1922 at a time when systematic passport and immigration controls were becoming standard at international frontiers. Although the British had imposed entry controls in the past&#160;&#8211;&#32;notably during the French Revolution<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>&#160;&#8211;&#32;the imposition of such controls in the 20th century dated from the <a href="/wiki/Aliens_Act_1905" title="Aliens Act 1905">Aliens Act 1905</a>, before which there was a system of registration for arriving foreigners.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Before the creation of the Irish Free State, British immigration law applied in Ireland as part of the United Kingdom. With the imminent prospect of Irish independence in 1922, the <a href="/wiki/British_Home_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="British Home Office">British Home Office</a> was disinclined to impose passport and immigration controls between the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, which would have meant patrolling a porous and meandering 499&#160;km (310&#160;mi) long<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_%E2%80%93_United_Kingdom_border" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Ireland \xe2\x80\x93 United Kingdom border">land border</a>. If, however, the pre-1922 situation were to be continued, the Irish immigration authorities would have to continue to enforce British immigration policy after independence. The Irish Department for Home Affairs was found to be receptive to continuing with the <i>status quo</i> and an informal agreement to this effect was reached in February 1923: each side would enforce the other\'s immigration decisions and the Irish authorities would be provided with a copy of Britain\'s suspect-codex (or \'Black Book\') of any <i><a href="/wiki/Persona_non_grata" title="Persona non grata">personae non gratae</a></i> in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The agreement was provided for in UK law by deeming the Irish Free State to be part of the United Kingdom for the purposes of immigration law.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It was fully implemented in 1925 when legislation passed in both countries provided for the recognition of the other\'s landing conditions for foreigners.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This may be considered to have been the high point of the CTA&#160;&#8211;&#32;although it was not called that at the time&#160;&#8211;&#32;as it almost amounted to a common immigration area. A foreigner who had been admitted to one state could, unless his or her admission had been conditional upon not entering the other state, travel to the other with only minimal bureaucratic requirements.\n</p><p>The CTA was suspended on the outbreak of war in 1939, and travel restrictions were introduced between the islands of Great Britain and Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This meant that travel restrictions even applied to people travelling within the UK if they were travelling from Northern Ireland to elsewhere in the UK.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1952_agreement">1952 agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1952 agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the war, the Irish re-instated their previous provisions allowing free movement<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> but the British declined to do so pending the agreement of a "similar immigration policy"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> in both countries. Consequently, the British maintained immigration controls between the islands of Ireland and Great Britain until 1952, to the consternation of Northern Ireland\'s <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionist</a> population.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>No agreement on a similar immigration policy was publicised at the time, but a year after the Irish Minister for Justice referred to the lifting of immigration controls between the two islands as "a matter for the British themselves", the British began referring to the CTA in legislation for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The content of the agreement appears to be that a foreigner would be refused entry to the United Kingdom if they wished to travel onward to Ireland (and vice versa) and is provided for in relevant immigration law.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The CTA has meant that Ireland has been required to follow changes in British immigration policy.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This was notable in 1962 when Irish law was changed in response to the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Immigrants_Act_1962" title="Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962">Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962</a>, which imposed immigration controls between the United Kingdom and Commonwealth countries, while in Ireland the <i>Aliens Order 1962</i> replaced the state\'s previous provision exempting all <a href="/wiki/British_subject" title="British subject">British subjects</a> from immigration control,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> with one exempting only those born in the United Kingdom. The scope of the Irish provision was much more restrictive than the British legislation as it excluded from immigration control only those British citizens born in the United Kingdom, and imposed immigration controls on those born outside the UK. The latter group would have included individuals who were British citizens by descent or by birth in a British colony. This discrepancy between Britain\'s and Ireland\'s definition of a British citizen was not resolved until 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="2008_proposal_to_introduce_immigration_controls_and.2For_identity_checks_between_Great_Britain_and_the_island_of_Ireland"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_proposal_to_introduce_immigration_controls_and/or_identity_checks_between_Great_Britain_and_the_island_of_Ireland">2008 proposal to introduce immigration controls and/or identity checks between Great Britain and the island of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2008 proposal to introduce immigration controls and/or identity checks between Great Britain and the island of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In July 2008, the <a href="/wiki/UK_Border_Agency" title="UK Border Agency">UK Border Agency</a> (the predecessor of <a href="/wiki/UK_Visas_and_Immigration" title="UK Visas and Immigration">UK Visas and Immigration</a>) published a consultation paper on the CTA that envisaged the imposition of immigration controls for non-CTA nationals, and new measures for identity checks of CTA nationals, as well as an advance passenger information system, on all air and sea crossings between the islands of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.\n</p><p>While passport controls were proposed to be applied to travellers between Great Britain and the Republic of Ireland, the nature of possible identity controls between Great Britain and Northern Ireland was not clear. This led to controversy because  Northern Ireland is part of the United Kingdom, with a prominent <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionist</a> describing the proposed arrangements as "intolerable and preposterous".<sup id="cite_ref-london-times_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-london-times-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The nature of identity checks between Northern Ireland and Great Britain was characterised by the British government as follows:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Section 14 of the Police and Justice Act 2006 introduced a new power that will allow the police to capture passenger, crew and service information on air and sea journeys within the United Kingdom. ... It is expected that this police power will only apply to air and sea routes between Great Britain and Northern Ireland. Passengers will not be required to use passports, but may be required to produce one of several types of documentation, including passports, when travelling, to enable the carrier to the meet the requirements of a police request.</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Liam Byrne, Minister of State for Immigration, Citizenship and Nationality, House of Commons Debate, 14 January 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>As far as <a href="/wiki/Irish_border" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish border">the land border</a> is concerned, the proposal indicated that the border would be "lightly controlled"<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and a joint statement in 2008 by both governments confirmed that there are no plans for fixed controls on either side of the border.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 1 April 2009, an amendment moved by <a href="/wiki/Robin_Dixon,_3rd_Baron_Glentoran" title="Robin Dixon, 3rd Baron Glentoran">Lord Glentoran</a> in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> defeated the British Government\'s proposal and preserved the CTA.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The relevant clause was re-introduced by Home Office minister <a href="/wiki/Phil_Woolas" title="Phil Woolas">Phil Woolas</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Public_Bill_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Bill Committee">Public Bill Committee</a> in June,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> but again removed in July after opposition pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_agreement">2011 agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2011 agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>2011 marked the first public agreement between the British and Irish governments concerning the maintenance of the CTA. Officially entitled the "Joint Statement Regarding Co-Operation on Measures to Secure the External Common Travel Area Border"<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> it was signed in Dublin on 20 December 2011 by the UK\'s immigration minister, <a href="/wiki/Damian_Green" title="Damian Green">Damian Green</a> and Ireland\'s Minister for Justice, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shatter" title="Alan Shatter">Alan Shatter</a>. The two ministers also signed an unpublished memorandum of understanding at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In common with its unpublished predecessors the 2011 agreement is nonbinding, with its eighth clause stating that the agreement "is not intended to create legally binding obligations, nor to create or confer any right, privilege or benefit on any person or party, private or public".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The agreement commits the two governments to continue their co-operation through the CTA, to align their lists of visa-free countries, to develop "electronic border management system/s",<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> to engage in data sharing to combat the "abuse" of the CTA,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and to work toward a "fully-common short stay visit visa".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="2016.E2.80.932017:_Brexit"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2016\xe2\x80\x932017:_Brexit">2016\xe2\x80\x932017: Brexit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2016\xe2\x80\x932017: Brexit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The UK voted to leave the European Union in a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">referendum on 23 June 2016</a>. Their <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">withdrawal</a> would make the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border" title="Republic of Ireland\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom border">Republic of Ireland\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom border</a>  on the island of Ireland an <a href="/wiki/External_border_of_the_European_Union" title="External border of the European Union">external border of the European Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> However, the Irish and UK governments and the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_European_Council" title="President of the European Council">President of the European Council</a> have stated that they do not wish for a hard border in Ireland, taking into account the historical and social "sensitivities" that permeate the island.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In September 2016 the British <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Exiting_the_European_Union" title="Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union">Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Davis_(British_politician)" title="David Davis (British politician)">David Davis</a>, stated that the UK government would not seek a return to a "hard border" between the UK and Republic of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2016, the UK and Irish governments considered in outline a plan entailing British immigration controls being applied at Irish ports and airports after Brexit, so that Britain might control migration by EU citizens (other than Irish nationals) across the open border into the United Kingdom. This would avoid passport checks being required between the Republic of Ireland and Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> However, this agreement was never finalised and was met by opposition from political parties in the Republic of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> On 23 March 2017, it was confirmed that British immigration officials would not be allowed to use Irish ports and airports.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2017, the UK government\'s policy paper on the position of EU citizens in the UK stated a desire to "protect the Common Travel Area arrangements", stating that "Irish citizens residing in the UK will not need to apply for "settled status" to protect their entitlements".<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_immigration_checks">Identity and immigration checks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Identity and immigration checks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Channel_Islands">Channel Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Jersey_Customs_and_Immigration_Service" title="States of Jersey Customs and Immigration Service">Jersey Customs and Immigration Service</a> on passengers arriving in the Channel Islands only from outside the CTA.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span immigration officers to examine (i.e. request identity documents from) travellers arriving in the state from elsewhere in the CTA and to refuse them permission to land if they are not entitled to enter.<sup id="cite_ref-irishcontrols_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irishcontrols-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Although this is stated to apply only to people other than Irish and British citizens, both of the latter groups are effectively covered as they may be required to produce identity documents to prove that they are entitled to the CTA arrangements.\n</p><p>Although it is difficult to be exact about the nature of current border checks when entering Ireland from another part of the CTA, fixed controls are maintained only at ports and airports<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> while targeted controls are conducted along the land border in what are referred to as "intelligence driven operations".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Air passengers arriving in Ireland from elsewhere in the CTA are no longer segregated from those arriving from outside the CTA;<sup id="cite_ref-Pachero_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pachero-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> consequently all air passengers must pass through Irish immigration checks, administered by the <a href="/wiki/Garda_National_Immigration_Bureau" title="Garda National Immigration Bureau">Garda National Immigration Bureau</a> (GNIB). While British citizens are not required to be in possession of a valid travel document as a condition of entry, they may be required to satisfy immigration officials as to their nationality.\n</p><p>The nature of the Irish controls was described by an Irish <a href="/wiki/High_Court_(Ireland)" title="High Court (Ireland)">High Court</a> judge, Mr Justice Gerard Hogan, in the following terms:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The practical result of this is that all persons arriving by air from the United Kingdom face Irish immigration controls. While in theory both Irish and British citizens are entitled to arrive here free from immigration control by virtue of the common travel area, increasingly in practice such passengers who arrive by air from the United Kingdom are required to produce their passports (or, at least, some other form of acceptable identity document) in order to prove to immigration officers that they are either Irish or British citizens who can avail of the common travel area. Whatever about anyone else, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Heller" title="Joseph Heller">Joseph Heller</a> certainly would have approved.<sup id="cite_ref-Pachero_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pachero-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In 2012, a pilot project was set up to use civilian staff from the Immigration section of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Naturalisation_and_Immigration_Service" title="Irish Naturalisation and Immigration Service">Irish Naturalisation and Immigration Service</a> (INIS) to work with GNIB staff at immigration control at <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a>. INIS staff will be responsible for performing all "in-booth" duties (including examining arriving passengers), but will not take part in any matters related to restraint, detention or arrest.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="UK_Transit_Visas_requirement">UK Transit Visas requirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: UK Transit Visas href="/wiki/Visa_policy_of_the_United_Kingdom#Transit" title="Visa policy of the United Kingdom">Visa nationals</a> wishing to transit through the UK to Ireland must have a valid UK visa (in addition to an Irish visa if necessary) and pass through UK immigration before continuing their journey.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isle_of_Man">Isle of Man</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Isle of Man">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Isle of Man government reports that, "[o]n-entry Immigration Control is rarely undertaken in the Isle of Man as there are very few transport services entering the Isle of Man from outside the CTA."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> As of April 2018 the only regular commercial air service between the Isle of Man and an airport outside the Common Travel Area is <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> in Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Isle of Man is considered a part of the UK for customs purposes, and so there are no routine customs checks on travellers arriving from the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-ukba.homeoffice.gov.uk_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ukba.homeoffice.gov.uk-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/UK_Border_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Border Force">UK Border Force</a> does not carry out routine immigration checks on travellers (regardless of nationality) arriving in the UK from another part of the CTA.<sup id="cite_ref-ukba.homeoffice.gov.uk_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ukba.homeoffice.gov.uk-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> However, because the Channel Islands have VAT free status, the UK carries out selective customs checks on travellers arriving from there.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Travel_within_the_UK">Travel within the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Travel within the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>There are no border controls between the four constituent countries of the UK, and consequently the land frontiers between England, Scotland and Wales are completely open. However, section 8<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Prevention_of_Terrorism_(Temporary_Provisions)_Act_1974" class="mw-redirect" title="Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1974">Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1974</a> provided for temporary powers to examine persons travelling between Northern Ireland and Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Schedule 7 of the <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_Act_2000" title="Terrorism Act 2000">Terrorism Act 2000</a> provides for similar powers and remains in force.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the Home Office, with regard to the legal basis for identity screening and immigration checks at airports and sea ports, as carried out on passengers travelling between Northern Ireland and Great Britain,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> "Immigration Enforcement officers may arrest without warrant anyone they have reasonable grounds to suspect has committed an immigration offence and/or may be liable for removal directions." Section 31.19.3 of the Enforcement Instructions and Guidance (part of the visas and immigration operational guidance),<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> relating to the case law <i>Baljinder Singh v. Hammond</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> said "Any questioning must be consensual. The paragraph 2 power to examine does not include a power to compel someone to stop or to require someone to comply with that examination. Should a person seek to exercise their right not to answer questions and leave, there is no power to arrest that person purely on suspicion of committing an immigration offence."\n</p><p>As in most countries, airlines may require photo identification (e.g. a passport or a driving licence) for internal flights between destinations within the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_visa_system">Common visa system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Common visa system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Visa_policy_of_Ireland" title="Visa policy of Ireland">Visa policy of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visa_policy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Visa policy of the United Kingdom">Visa policy of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>In October 2014, the British and Irish governments signed a memorandum of understanding paving the way for mutually recognised visas allowing visitors to travel to Britain and Ireland on a single visa. Chinese and Indian nationals were the first and, as of 2018, the only nationalities to get the benefit of the scheme. It was proposed to conduct a review of the scheme in 2015 with a view to expand it to all other countries by the end of that year,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> but no such review took place, and the scheme has not been extended to other nationalities. The scheme exists in parallel to the Irish visa waiver programme which waives the visa requirement for the nationals of 17 countries if they hold valid UK short-stay visas and enter Ireland directly from the UK.\n</p><p>While the CTA has, for most of its history, involved an open or relatively open border, since the Second World War this has not meant that someone who legally entered one part of the CTA was automatically entitled to enter another part. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Schengen_Agreement" title="Schengen Agreement">Schengen Agreement</a>, the CTA currently provides no mechanism for the mutual recognition of leave to enter and remain, and the UK and Ireland operate  separate visa systems with distinct entry requirements. In general, a UK visa will not allow entry to Ireland nor vice versa.\n</p><p>The Channel Islands and the Isle of Man allow entry to holders of UK visas (with some exceptions). Bailiwick of Guernsey and Jersey immigration authorities routinely check non-EEA nationals seeking to enter the UK to ensure they have valid UK permissions.\n</p><p>In July 2011 Ireland introduced a limited pilot visa waiver programme under which the normal requirement for certain nationalities to hold an Irish visa is waived for visitors to the UK who hold valid UK visas.\n</p><p>Nationalities class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While British and Irish citizens enjoy the right to live in each other\'s countries under European Union law, the provisions that apply to them are generally more far reaching than those that apply to other <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a> nationals. There now are identity checks at least for air travel, and British and Irish citizens may be requested to produce a valid identity document when crossing the border.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_citizens_in_Ireland">British citizens in Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: British citizens in Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Under Irish law, all British citizens&#160;&#8211;&#32;including <a href="/wiki/Manx_people" title="Manx people">Manx people</a> and Channel Islanders, who are not entitled to take advantage of the European Union\'s <a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_(European_Union)" class="mw-redirect" title="Four Freedoms (European Union)">freedom of movement</a> provisions&#160;&#8211;&#32;are exempt from immigration control and immune from deportation.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> They are entitled to live in Ireland without any restrictions or conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Residence_rights_of_UK_citizens_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Residence_rights_of_UK_citizens-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> They have, with limited exceptions,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> never been treated as foreigners under Irish law, having never been subject to the Aliens Act 1935 or to any orders made under that Act.<sup id="cite_ref-Residence_rights_of_UK_citizens_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Residence_rights_of_UK_citizens-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> British citizens can thus move to Ireland to live, work or retire and unlike other EU citizens, they are not required to demonstrate having sufficient resources or have private health insurance in order to retire. This is due to the fact that British citizens are also entitled to use Irish public services on the same basis as Irish citizens in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Residence_rights_of_UK_citizens_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Residence_rights_of_UK_citizens-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_citizens_in_the_United_Kingdom">Irish citizens in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Irish citizens in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law_and_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="British nationality law and the Republic of Ireland">British nationality law and the Republic of Ireland</a></div>\n<p>Before 1949, all Irish citizens were considered under British law to be <a href="/wiki/British_subject" title="British subject">British subjects</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> After Ireland <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act_1948" title="Republic of Ireland Act 1948">declared itself a republic</a> in that year, a consequent <a href="/wiki/Ireland_Act_1949" title="Ireland Act 1949">British law</a> gave Irish citizens a similar status to <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_citizen" title="Commonwealth citizen">Commonwealth citizens</a> in the United Kingdom, notwithstanding that they had ceased to be such. Thus, much like British citizens in Ireland, Irish citizens in the United Kingdom have never been treated as foreigners. Irish citizens have, however, like Commonwealth citizens, been subject to immigration control in Britain since the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Immigrants_Act_1962" title="Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962">Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962</a>. Unlike Commonwealth citizens, Irish citizens have generally not been subject to entry control in the United Kingdom and, if they move to the UK, are considered to have \'settled status\' (a status that goes beyond <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_leave_to_remain" title="Indefinite leave to remain">indefinite leave to remain</a>). They may be subject to deportation from the UK upon the same basis as other <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a> nationals.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> In February 2007 the British government announced that a specially lenient procedure would apply to the deportation of Irish citizens compared to the procedure for other European Economic Area nationals.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>  As a result, Irish nationals are not routinely considered for deportation from the UK when they are released from prison.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_European_Economic_Area_nationals">Other European Economic Area nationals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other European Economic Area nationals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Nationals of member states of the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a> other than British and Irish nationals have the right to freely enter and reside in the UK and Ireland under <a href="/wiki/European_Union_law" title="European Union law">European Union law</a>. They are required to carry a valid <a href="/wiki/Travel_document" title="Travel document">travel document</a>, a passport or a <a href="/wiki/National_identity_card" class="mw-redirect" title="National identity card">national identity card</a>, for entering the CTA and for travelling between Ireland and the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schengen_Area">Schengen Area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Schengen Area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1985, five member states of the then <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> signed the <a href="/wiki/Schengen_Agreement" title="Schengen Agreement">Schengen Agreement</a> on the gradual dropping of border controls between them. This treaty and the implementation convention of 1990 paved the way for the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Schengen_Area" title="Schengen Area">Schengen Area</a>. Implemented in 1995, by 1997 all <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">European Union member states</a> except the United Kingdom and Ireland had signed the Agreement. The <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam Treaty">Amsterdam Treaty</a>, which was drafted that year, incorporated Schengen into EU law, while giving Ireland and the UK an opt-out permitting them to maintain systematic passport and immigration controls at their frontiers. The wording of the treaty makes Ireland\'s opt-out from eliminating border controls conditional on the Common Travel Area being maintained.\n</p><p>The British government has always refused to lower its border controls as it believes that the island status of the CTA puts the UK in a better position to enforce immigration controls than mainland European countries with "extensive and permeable land borders".<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> While not signing the Schengen Treaty, Ireland has always looked more favourably on joining but has not done so to maintain the CTA and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border" title="Republic of Ireland\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom border">its open border</a> with <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> though in 1997 Ireland amended its Aliens Order to permit identity and immigration controls on arrivals from the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-irishcontrols_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irishcontrols-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification_requirements">Identification requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Travel_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Identification requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most transport operators permit passengers to travel within the Common Travel Area without a passport, although <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> require all passengers to carry a passport or a national identity card. In 2014 a <a href="/wiki/Private_member%27s_bill" title="Private member&#39;s bill">private member\'s bill</a> was put before the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Irish parliament</a> which proposed to prohibit transport operators from requiring the production of a passport for travel within the Common Travel Area, but it was not passed.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Photo ID is required for Irish or British citizens travelling by air at the minimum. The Irish government in October 2015, started issuing <a href="/wiki/Irish_passport#Passport_Card" title="Irish passport">passport cards</a> and are the same size as national identity cards from other EU countries, which are accepted by all transport operators but the issuing of a passport card requires the holder to already have a conventional passport book.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
