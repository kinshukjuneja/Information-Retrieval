Wright</a>, third baseman for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> (2004-Present).</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>third baseman</b>, abbreviated <i>3B</i>, is the player in <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> whose responsibility is to defend the area nearest to <b>third base</b> \xe2\x80\x94  the third of four bases a baserunner must touch in succession to score a run. In the scoring system used to record defensive plays, the third baseman is assigned the number \'5\'.\n</p><p>The third baseman requires good reflexes in reacting to batted balls, as he is often the closest <a href="/wiki/Infielder" title="Infielder">infielder</a> (roughly 90\xe2\x80\x93120 feet) to the batter. The third base position requires a strong and accurate arm, as the third baseman often makes long throws to first base. The third baseman sometimes must throw quickly to <a href="/wiki/Second_baseman" title="Second baseman">second base</a> in time to start a <a href="/wiki/Double_play" title="Double play">double play</a>. The third baseman must also field <a href="/wiki/Batted_ball" title="Batted ball">fly balls</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baseball#The_playing_field" title="Baseball">fair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baseball#The_playing_field" title="Baseball">foul</a> territory.\n</p><p>Third base is known as the "<b>hot corner</b>", because the third baseman is relatively close to the batter and most right-handed hitters tend to hit the ball hard in this direction. A third baseman must possess good hand-eye coordination and quick reactions in order to catch hard <a href="/wiki/Line_drive" class="mw-redirect" title="Line drive">line drives</a> sometimes in excess of 125 miles per hour (201&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Third basemen often must begin in a position even closer to the batter if a <a href="/wiki/Bunt_(baseball)" title="Bunt (baseball)">bunt</a> is expected, creating a hazard if the ball is instead hit sharply. As with middle <a href="/wiki/Infielder" title="Infielder">infielders</a>, right-handed throwing players are standard at the position because they do not need to turn their body before throwing across the <a href="/wiki/Infielder" title="Infielder">infield</a> to first base. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Squires" title="Mike Squires">Mike Squires</a>, who played fourteen games at third base in 1982 and 1983, is a very rare example of a third baseman who threw lefty.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Some third basemen have been converted from middle infielders or <a href="/wiki/Outfielder" title="Outfielder">outfielders</a> because the position does not require them to run as fast.\n</p><p>Expectations of how well a third baseman should be able to hit have varied a great deal over time; in the early years of the sport, these expectations were similar to those for <a href="/wiki/Shortstop" title="Shortstop">shortstops</a>, the third baseman being merely the less skilled defensive player. Players who could hit with more ability often were not suited for third base, either because they were left-handed or because they were not mobile enough for the position. However, the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Live-ball_era" title="Live-ball era">live-ball era</a> in the 1920s created a greater demand for more offense, and third basemen have since been expected to hit either for a high <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">average</a> (.290 or better) or with moderate to substantial power.  Since the 1950s the position has become more of a power position with sluggers such as <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Mathews" title="Eddie Mathews">Eddie Mathews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schmidt" title="Mike Schmidt">Mike Schmidt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ron_Santo" title="Ron Santo">Ron Santo</a> becoming stars.\n</p><p>There are fewer third basemen in the <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> than there are Hall of Famers of any other position. Furthermore, with the notable exception of <a href="/wiki/John_McGraw_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="John McGraw (baseball)">John McGraw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Cox" title="Bobby Cox">Bobby Cox</a>, few third basemen have gone on to have successful <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">managing</a> careers, with <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Dykes" title="Jimmy Dykes">Jimmy Dykes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Negro_League_baseball" class="mw-redirect" title="Negro League baseball">Negro Leaguer</a> <a href="/wiki/Dave_Malarcher" title="Dave Malarcher">Dave Malarcher</a> being perhaps the next most prominent managers who began their careers at third base.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox"
