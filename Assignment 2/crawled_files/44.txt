class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parmigianino_003b.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In <a href="/wiki/Parmigianino" title="Parmigianino">Parmigianino</a>\'s <i><a stylized poses, and lack of clear perspective.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Mannerism</b>, also known as <b>Late Renaissance</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a style in <a href="/wiki/European_art" class="mw-redirect" title="European art">European art</a> that emerged in the later years of the Italian <a href="/wiki/High_Renaissance" title="High Renaissance">High Renaissance</a> around 1520, spreading by about 1530 and lasting until about the end of the 16th century in Italy, when the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> style largely replaced it. <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mannerism" title="Northern Mannerism">Northern Mannerism</a> continued into the early 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Painting_style" class="mw-redirect" title="Painting style">Stylistically</a>, Mannerism encompasses a variety of approaches influenced by, and reacting to, the harmonious ideals associated with artists such as <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, and early <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>. Where High Renaissance art emphasizes proportion, balance, and ideal beauty, Mannerism exaggerates such qualities, often resulting in compositions that are asymmetrical or unnaturally elegant.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The style is notable for its intellectual sophistication as well as its artificial (as opposed to naturalistic) qualities.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It favors compositional tension and instability rather than the balance and clarity of earlier Renaissance painting. Mannerism in literature and music is notable for its highly florid style and intellectual sophistication.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The definition of Mannerism and the phases within it continue to be a subject of debate among art historians. For example, some scholars have applied the label to certain early modern forms of literature (especially poetry) and music of the 16th and 17th centuries. The term is also used to refer to some late <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a> painters working in northern Europe from about 1500 to 1530, especially the <a href="/wiki/Antwerp_Mannerism" title="Antwerp Mannerism">Antwerp Mannerists</a>\xe2\x80\x94a group unrelated to the Italian movement. Mannerism also has been applied by analogy to the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Silver Age of Latin literature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Laocoon_Pio-Clementino_Inv1059-1064-1067.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Laocoon_Pio-Clementino_Inv1059-1064-1067.jpg/230px-Laocoon_Pio-Clementino_Inv1059-1064-1067.jpg" width="230" height="221" class="thumbimage" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Laocoon_Pio-Clementino_Inv1059-1064-1067.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mannerism role-model: <i><a href="/wiki/Laoco%C3%B6n_and_His_Sons" title="Laoco\xc3\xb6n and His Sons">Laoco\xc3\xb6n and His Sons</a></i>, an ancient sculpture, rediscovered in 1506; now in the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican</a>. The artists of Mannerism greatly admired this piece of sculpture.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The word <i>mannerism</i> derives from the Italian <i>maniera</i>, meaning "style" or "manner". Like the English word "style", <i>maniera</i> can either indicate a specific type of style (a beautiful style, an abrasive style) or indicate an absolute that needs no qualification (someone "has style").<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the second edition of his <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Most_Excellent_Painters,_Sculptors,_and_Architects" title="Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects">Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects</a></i> (1568), <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> used <i>maniera</i> in three different contexts:  to discuss an artist\'s manner or method of working; to describe a personal or group style, such as the term <i>maniera greca</i> to refer to the Byzantine style or simply to the <i>maniera</i> of Michelangelo; and to affirm a positive judgment of artistic quality.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Vasari was also a Mannerist artist, and he described the period in which he worked as "la maniera moderna", or the "modern style".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> James V. Mirollo describes how "bella maniera" poets attempted to surpass in virtuosity the sonnets of Petrarch.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This notion of "bella maniera" suggests that artists who were thus inspired looked to copying and bettering their predecessors, rather than confronting nature directly. In essence, "bella maniera" utilized the best from a number of source materials, synthesizing it into something new.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a stylistic label, "Mannerism" is not easily defined. It was used by Swiss historian <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Burckhardt" title="Jacob Burckhardt">Jacob Burckhardt</a> and popularized by German <a href="/wiki/Art_history" title="Art history">art historians</a> in the early 20th century to categorize the seemingly uncategorizable art of the Italian 16th century \xe2\x80\x94 art that was no longer found to exhibit the harmonious and rational approaches associated with the High Renaissance. \xe2\x80\x9cHigh Renaissance\xe2\x80\x9d connoted a period distinguished by harmony, grandeur and the revival of classical antiquity. The term Mannerist was redefined in 1967 by <a href="/wiki/John_Shearman" title="John Shearman">John Shearman</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> following the exhibition of Mannerist paintings organised by <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Grossmann" title="Fritz Grossmann">Fritz Grossmann</a> at <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Art_Gallery" title="Manchester Art Gallery">Manchester City Art Gallery</a> in 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The label \xe2\x80\x9cMannerism\xe2\x80\x9d was used during the 16th century to comment on social behaviour and to convey a refined virtuoso quality or to signify a certain technique.\nHowever, for later writers, such as the 17th-century Gian Pietro Bellori, "la maniera" was a derogatory term for the perceived decline of art after Raphael, especially in the 1530s and 1540s.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> From the late 19th century on, art historians have commonly used the term to describe art that follows Renaissance classicism and precedes the Baroque.\n</p><p>Yet historians differ as to whether Mannerism is a style, a movement, or a period; and while the term remains controversial it is still commonly used to identify European art and culture of the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_development">Origin and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg/230px-Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg" width="230" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg/345px-Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg/460px-Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1085" data-file-height="820" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dividing_water_from_Heaven.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Collected figures, ignudi, from Michelangelo\'s Sistine Chapel ceiling</div></div></div>\n<p>By the end of the High Renaissance, young artists experienced a crisis:<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> it seemed that everything that could be achieved was already achieved. No more difficulties, technical or otherwise, remained to be solved. The detailed knowledge of anatomy, light, physiognomy and the way in which humans register emotion in expression and gesture, the innovative use of the human form in figurative composition, the use of the subtle gradation of tone, all had reached near perfection. The young artists needed to find a new goal, and they sought new approaches.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> At this point Mannerism started to emerge.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The new style developed between 1510 and 1520 either in Florence,<sup id="cite_ref-friedlaender_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-friedlaender-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> or in Rome, or in both cities simultaneously.<sup id="cite_ref-freedberg175_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freedberg175-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_role_models">Origins and role models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins and role models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This period has been described as a "natural extension"<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> of the art of <a href="/wiki/Andrea_del_Sarto" title="Andrea del Sarto">Andrea del Sarto</a>, Michelangelo, and Raphael. Michelangelo developed his own style at an early age, a deeply original one which was greatly admired at first, then often copied and imitated by other artists of the era.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> One of the qualities most admired by his contemporaries was his <i>terribilit\xc3\xa0</i>, a sense of awe-inspiring grandeur, and subsequent artists attempted to imitate it.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Other artists learned Michelangelo\'s impassioned and highly personal style by copying the works of the master, a standard way that students learned to paint and sculpt. His <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel_ceiling" title="Sistine Chapel ceiling">Sistine Chapel ceiling</a> provided examples for them to follow, in particular his representation of collected figures often called <i><a href="/wiki/Ignudi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ignudi">ignudi</a></i> and of the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Sibyl" title="Libyan Sibyl">Libyan Sibyl</a>, his <a href="/wiki/Vestibule_(Architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vestibule (Architecture)">vestibule</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Laurentian_Library" title="Laurentian Library">Laurentian Library</a>, the figures on his <a href="/wiki/Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici">Medici</a> tombs, and above all his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Judgment_(Michelangelo)" title="The Last Judgment (Michelangelo)">Last Judgment</a></i>. The later Michelangelo was one of the great role models of Mannerism.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Young artists broke in to his house and stole drawings from him.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In his book <i>Lives of the Most Eminent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects</i>, Giorgio Vasari noted that Michelangelo stated once: "Those who are followers can never pass by whom they follow".<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_competitive_spirit">The competitive spirit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The competitive spirit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The competitive spirit was cultivated by patrons who encouraged sponsored artists to emphasize virtuosic technique and to compete with one another for commissions. It drove artists to look for new approaches and dramatically illuminated scenes, elaborate clothes and compositions, elongated proportions, highly stylized poses, and a lack of clear perspective. Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo were each given a commission by Gonfaloniere <a href="/wiki/Piero_Soderini" title="Piero Soderini">Piero Soderini</a> to decorate a wall in the Hall of Five Hundred in Florence. These two artists were set to paint side by side and compete against each other, fueling the incentive to be as innovative as possible.\n</p>\n<table class="mw-headline" id="Early_mannerism">Early mannerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early mannerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg/230px-Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg" width="230" height="383" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg/345px-Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg/460px-Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1576" data-file-height="2622" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jacopo_Pontormo_004.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Pontormo" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacopo Pontormo">Jacopo Pontormo</a>, <i>Entombment</i>, 1528; Santa Felicit\xc3\xa0, Florence</div></div></div>\n<p>The early Mannerists in Florence\xe2\x80\x94especially the students of <a href="/wiki/Andrea_del_Sarto" title="Andrea del Sarto">Andrea del Sarto</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_da_Pontormo" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacopo da Pontormo">Jacopo da Pontormo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosso_Fiorentino" title="Rosso Fiorentino">Rosso Fiorentino</a> who are notable for elongated forms, precariously balanced poses, a collapsed perspective, irrational settings, and theatrical lighting. <a href="/wiki/Parmigianino" title="Parmigianino">Parmigianino</a> (a student of <a href="/wiki/Antonio_da_Correggio" title="Antonio da Correggio">Correggio</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Romano" title="Giulio Romano">Giulio Romano</a> (Raphael\xe2\x80\x99s head assistant) were moving in similarly stylized aesthetic directions in Rome.  These artists had matured under the influence of the High Renaissance, and their style has been characterized as a reaction to or exaggerated extension of it. Instead of studying nature directly, younger artists began studying Hellenistic sculpture and paintings of masters past. Therefore, this style is often identified as "anti-classical\xe2\x80\x9d,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> yet at the time it was considered a natural progression from the High Renaissance. The earliest experimental phase of Mannerism, known for its "anti-classical" forms, lasted until about 1540 or 1550.<sup id="cite_ref-freedberg175_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freedberg175-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Marcia B. Hall, professor of art history at Temple University, notes in her book <i>After Raphael</i> that Raphael\'s premature death marked the beginning of Mannerism in Rome.\n</p><p>In past analyses, it has been noted that mannerism arose in the early 16th century contemporaneously with a number of other social, scientific, religious and political movements such as the <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">Copernican model</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">Sack of Rome</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>\'s increasing challenge to the power of the Catholic Church. Because of this, the style\'s elongated forms and distorted forms were once interpreted as a reaction to the idealized compositions prevalent in High Renaissance art.<sup id="cite_ref-grove_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grove-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This explanation for the radical stylistic shift c. 1520 has fallen out of scholarly favor, though early Mannerist art is still sharply contrasted with High Renaissance conventions; the accessibility and balance achieved by Raphael\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_of_Athens" title="The School of Athens">School of Athens</a></i> no longer seemed to interest young artists.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_maniera">High maniera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: High maniera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The second period of Mannerism is commonly differentiated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> from the earlier, so-called "anti-classical" phase.\nSubsequent mannerists stressed intellectual conceits and artistic virtuosity, features that have led later critics to accuse them of working in an unnatural and affected "manner" (<i>maniera</i>). Maniera artists looked to their older contemporary Michelangelo as their principal model; theirs was an art imitating art, rather than an art imitating nature. Art historian <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Joseph_Freedberg" title="Sydney Joseph Freedberg">Sydney Joseph Freedberg</a> argues that the intellectualizing aspect of maniera art involves expecting its audience to notice and appreciate this visual reference\xe2\x80\x94a familiar figure in an unfamiliar setting enclosed between "unseen, but felt, quotation marks".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The height of artifice is the Maniera painter\'s penchant for deliberately misappropriating a quotation. <a href="/wiki/Agnolo_Bronzino" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnolo Bronzino">Agnolo Bronzino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> exemplify this strain of Maniera that lasted from about 1530 to 1580. Based largely at courts and in intellectual circles around Europe, Maniera art couples exaggerated elegance with exquisite attention to surface and detail: porcelain-skinned figures recline in an even, tempered light, acknowledging the viewer with a cool glance, if they make eye contact at all. The Maniera subject rarely displays much emotion, and for this reason works exemplifying this trend are often called \'cold\' or \'aloof.\' This is typical of the so-called "stylish style" or <i>Maniera</i> in its maturity.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread_of_mannerism">Spread of mannerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spread of mannerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg/230px-Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg" width="230" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg/345px-Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg/460px-Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1142" data-file-height="1185" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Howard_Earl_of_Surrey_1546.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>English Mannerism: <a href="/wiki/Henry_Howard,_Earl_of_Surrey" title="Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey">Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey</a>, 1546, a rare English Mannerist portrait by a Flemish immigrant</div></div></div>\n<p>The cities Rome, Florence, and Mantua were Mannerist centers in Italy. Venetian painting pursued a different course, represented by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> in his long career.  A number of the earliest Mannerist artists who had been working in Rome during the 1520s fled the city after the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">Sack of Rome</a> in 1527. As they spread out across the continent in search of employment, their style was disseminated throughout Italy and Northern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The result was the first international artistic style since the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Other parts of Northern Europe did not have the advantage of such direct contact with Italian artists, but the Mannerist style made its presence felt through prints and illustrated books. European rulers, among others, purchased Italian works, while northern European artists continued to travel to Italy, helping to spread the Mannerist style. Individual Italian artists working in the North gave birth to a movement known as the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mannerism" title="Northern Mannerism">Northern Mannerism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a>, for example, was presented with <a href="/wiki/Bronzino" title="Bronzino">Bronzino</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Venus,_Cupid,_Folly_and_Time" title="Venus, Cupid, Folly and Time">Venus, Cupid, Folly and Time</a></i>. The style waned in Italy after 1580, as a new generation of artists, including the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_degli_Incamminati" title="Accademia degli Incamminati">Carracci</a> brothers, <a href="/wiki/Caravaggio" title="Caravaggio">Caravaggio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cigoli" title="Cigoli">Cigoli</a>, revived naturalism. <a href="/wiki/Walter_Friedlaender" class="mw-redirect" title="Walter Friedlaender">Walter Friedlaender</a> identified this period as "anti-mannerism", just as the early mannerists were "anti-classical" in their reaction away from the aesthetic values of the High Renaissance<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and today the Carracci brothers and Caravaggio are agreed to have begun the transition to Baroque-style painting which was dominant by 1600.\n</p><p>Outside of Italy, however, Mannerism continued into the 17th century. In France, where Rosso traveled to work for the court at <a href="/wiki/School_of_Fontainebleau" title="School of Fontainebleau">Fontainebleau</a>, it is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Henry_II_style" title="Henry II style">Henry II style</a>" and had a particular impact on architecture. Other important continental centers of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mannerism" title="Northern Mannerism">Northern Mannerism</a> include the court of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Rudolf II</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>. Mannerism as a stylistic category is less frequently applied to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> visual and decorative arts, where native labels such as "<a href="/wiki/Elizabethan" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabethan">Elizabethan</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean</a>" are more commonly applied. Seventeenth-century Artisan Mannerism is one exception, applied to architecture that relies on pattern books rather than on existing precedents in Continental Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Of particular note is the Flemish influence at Fontainebleau that combined the eroticism of the French style with an early version of the vanitas tradition that would dominate seventeenth-century Dutch and Flemish painting. Prevalent at this time was the "pittore vago," a description of painters from the north who entered the workshops in France and Italy to create a truly international style.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture">Sculpture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sculpture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As in painting, early Italian Mannerist sculpture was very largely an attempt to find an original style that would top the achievement of the <a href="/wiki/High_Renaissance" title="High Renaissance">High Renaissance</a>, which in sculpture essentially meant Michelangelo, and much of the struggle to achieve this was played out in commissions to fill other places in the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_della_Signoria" title="Piazza della Signoria">Piazza della Signoria</a> in Florence, next to <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/David_(Michelangelo)" title="David (Michelangelo)">David</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Baccio_Bandinelli" class="mw-redirect" title="Baccio Bandinelli">Baccio Bandinelli</a> took over the project of <i><a href="/wiki/Hercules_and_Cacus" title="Hercules and Cacus">Hercules and Cacus</a></i> from the master himself, but it was little more popular then than it is now, and maliciously compared by <a href="/wiki/Benvenuto_Cellini" title="Benvenuto Cellini">Benvenuto Cellini</a> to "a sack of melons", though it had a long-lasting effect in apparently introducing relief panels on the <a href="/wiki/Pedestal" title="Pedestal">pedestal</a> of statues.  Like other works of his and other Mannerists it removes far more of the original block than Michelangelo would have done.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  Cellini\'s bronze <i><a href="/wiki/Perseus_with_the_head_of_Medusa" class="mw-redirect" title="Perseus with the head of Medusa">Perseus with the head of Medusa</a></i> is certainly a masterpiece, designed with eight angles of view, another Mannerist characteristic, and artificially stylized in comparison with the <i>David</i>s of Michelangelo and Donatello.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Originally a goldsmith, his famous gold and enamel <a href="/wiki/Cellini_Salt_Cellar" title="Cellini Salt Cellar">Salt Cellar</a> (1543) was his first sculpture, and shows his talent at its best.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Small bronze figures for collector\'s <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(room)" title="Cabinet (room)">cabinets</a>, often mythological subjects with nudes, were a popular Renaissance form at which <a href="/wiki/Giambologna" title="Giambologna">Giambologna</a>, originally <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a> but based in Florence, excelled in the later part of the century. He also created life-size sculptures, of which two entered the collection in the Piazza della Signoria. He and his followers devised elegant elongated examples of the <i><a href="/wiki/Figura_serpentinata" title="Figura serpentinata">figura serpentinata</a></i>, often of two intertwined figures, that href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Giorgio Vasari">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a>\'s opinions about the art of painting emerge in the praise he bestows on fellow artists in his multi-volume <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Artists" class="mw-redirect" title="Lives of the Artists">Lives of the Artists</a></i>: he believed that excellence in painting demanded refinement, richness of invention (<i>invenzione</i>), expressed through virtuoso technique (<i>maniera</i>), and wit and study that appeared in the finished work, all criteria that emphasized the artist\'s intellect and the patron\'s sensibility. The artist was now no longer just a trained member of a local Guild of St Luke. Now he took his place at court alongside scholars, poets, and humanists, in a climate that fostered an appreciation for elegance and complexity. The coat-of-arms of Vasari\'s <a href="/wiki/Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici">Medici</a> patrons appears at the top of his portrait, quite as if it were the artist\'s own. The framing of the woodcut image of <a href="/wiki/Vasari" class="mw-redirect" title="Vasari">Vasari</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Artists" class="mw-redirect" title="Lives of the Artists">Lives of the Artists</a></i> would be called "<a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean</a>" in an English-speaking milieu. In it, Michelangelo\'s Medici tombs inspire the anti-architectural "architectural" features at the top, the papery pierced frame, the satyr nudes at the base. As a mere frame it is extravagant: Mannerist, in short.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gian_Paolo_Lomazzo">Gian Paolo Lomazzo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gian Paolo Lomazzo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another literary figure from the period is <a href="/wiki/Gian_Paolo_Lomazzo" title="Gian Paolo Lomazzo">Gian Paolo Lomazzo</a>, who produced two works\xe2\x80\x94one practical and one metaphysical\xe2\x80\x94that helped define the Mannerist artist\'s self-conscious relation to his art. His <i>Trattato dell\'arte della pittura, scoltura et architettura</i> (Milan, 1584) is in part a guide to contemporary concepts of <a href="/wiki/Decorum" title="Decorum">decorum</a>, which the Renaissance inherited in part from Antiquity but Mannerism elaborated upon. Lomazzo\'s systematic codification of aesthetics, which typifies the more formalized and academic approaches typical of the later 16th century, emphasized a consonance between the functions of interiors and the kinds of painted and sculpted decors that would be suitable. Iconography, often convoluted and abstruse, is a more prominent element in the Mannerist styles. His less practical and more metaphysical <i>Idea del tempio della pittura</i> (<i>The ideal temple of painting</i>, Milan, 1590) offers a description along the lines of the "four temperaments" theory of human nature and personality, defining the role of individuality in judgment and artistic invention.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_Mannerist_artists">Some Mannerist Mannerist design was extended to luxury goods like silver and carved furniture. A sense of tense, controlled emotion expressed in elaborate symbolism and <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegory</a>, and an ideal of female beauty characterized by elongated proportions are features of this style.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agnolo_Bronzino">Agnolo Bronzino</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Agnolo Bronzino">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Mannerist portraits by <a href="/wiki/Bronzino" title="Bronzino">Agnolo Bronzino</a> are distinguished by a serene elegance and meticulous attention to detail. As a result, Bronzino\'s sitters have been said to project an aloofness and marked emotional distance from the viewer. There is also a virtuosic concentration on capturing the precise pattern and sheen of rich textiles.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alessandro_Allori">Alessandro Allori</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Alessandro Allori">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Allori" title="Alessandro Allori">Alessandro Allori</a>\'s (1535\xe2\x80\x931607) <i>Susanna and the Elders</i> (<i>below</i>) is distinguished by latent eroticism and consciously brilliant still life detail, in a crowded, contorted composition.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tintoretto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a>\'s <i>Last Supper</i> (below) focuses on light and motion, bringing the image to dramatic life.  Unlike more traditional views of the Last Supper, Tintoretto depicts Heaven opening up into the room, and the <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angels</a> looking on in awe, in line with the old Catholic maxim that "If the angels were capable of envy, they would envy the Eucharist."\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="El_Greco">El Greco</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: El Greco">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/El_Greco" title="El Greco">El Greco</a> attempted to express <a href="/wiki/Religious" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious">religious</a> emotion with exaggerated traits. After the realistic depiction of the human form and the mastery of perspective achieved in high Renaissance Classicism, some artists started to deliberately distort proportions in disjointed, irrational space for emotional and artistic effect. El Greco still is a deeply original artist. El Greco has been characterized by modern scholars as an artist so individual that he belongs to no conventional school.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Key aspects of Mannerism in El Greco include the jarring "acid" palette, elongated and tortured anatomy, irrational perspective and light, and obscure and troubling iconography.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benvenuto_Cellini">Benvenuto Cellini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Benvenuto Cellini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Benvenuto_Cellini" title="Benvenuto Cellini">Benvenuto Cellini</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Cellini_Salt_Cellar" title="Cellini Salt Cellar">Cellini Salt Cellar</a> of gold and enamel in 1540 featuring <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphitrite" title="Amphitrite">Amphitrite</a> (water and earth) placed in uncomfortable positions and with elongated proportions. It is considered a masterpiece of Mannerist sculpture.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joachim_Wtewael">Joachim Wtewael</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Joachim Wtewael">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Joachim_Wtewael" title="Joachim Wtewael">Joachim Wtewael</a> (1566\xe2\x80\x931638) continued to paint in a Northern Mannerist style until the end of his life, ignoring the arrival of the Baroque, and making him perhaps the last significant Mannerist artist still to be working.  His subjects included large scenes with still life in the manner of <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Aertsen" title="Pieter Aertsen">Pieter Aertsen</a>, and mythological scenes, many small <a id="Mannerist_architecture">Mannerist architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Mannerist architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Mannerist architecture was characterized by visual trickery and unexpected elements that challenged the renaissance norms.<sup id="cite_ref-vanda_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanda-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Flemish artists, many of whom had traveled to Italy and were influenced by Mannerist developments there, were responsible for the spread of Mannerist trends into Europe north of the Alps, including into the realm of architecture. <sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> During the period, architects experimented with using architectural forms to emphasize solid and spatial relationships. The Renaissance ideal of harmony gave way to freer and more imaginative rhythms. The best known architect associated with the Mannerist style, and a pioneer at the <a href="/wiki/Laurentian_Library" title="Laurentian Library">Laurentian Library</a>, was <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> (1475\xe2\x80\x931564).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  He is credited with inventing the <a href="/wiki/Giant_order" title="Giant order">giant order</a>, a large pilaster that stretches from the bottom to the top of a fa\xc3\xa7ade.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> He used this in his design for the <a href="/wiki/Campidoglio" class="mw-redirect" title="Campidoglio">Campidoglio</a> in Rome.\n</p><p>Prior to the 20th century, the term <i>Mannerism</i> had negative connotations, but it is now used to describe the historical period in more general non-judgmental terms.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  Mannerist architecture has also been used to describe a trend in the 1960s and 1970s that involved breaking the norms of modernist architecture while at the same time recognizing their existence.<sup id="cite_ref-venturi_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venturi-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Defining mannerist in this context, architect and author <a href="/wiki/Robert_Venturi" title="Robert Venturi">Robert Venturi</a> wrote "Mannerism for architecture of our time that acknowledges conventional order rather than original expression but breaks the conventional order to accommodate complexity and contradiction and thereby engages ambiguity unambiguously." <sup id="cite_ref-venturi_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venturi-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_examples">Renaissance examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Renaissance examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An example of mannerist architecture is the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Farnese" title="Villa Farnese">Villa Farnese</a> at <a href="/wiki/Caprarola" title="Caprarola">Caprarola</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> in the rugged country side outside of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. The proliferation of engravers during the 16th century spread Mannerist styles more quickly than any previous styles.\n</p><p>Dense with ornament of "Roman" detailing, the display doorway at Colditz Castle exemplifies this northern style, characteristically applied as an isolated "set piece" against unpretentious vernacular walling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>From the late 1560s onwards, many buildings in <a href="/wiki/Valletta" title="Valletta">Valletta</a>, the new capital city of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, were designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Cassar" title="Girolamo Cassar">Girolamo Cassar</a> in the Mannerist style. Such buildings include <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Co-Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="St. John&#39;s Co-Cathedral">St. John\'s Co-Cathedral</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster%27s_Palace_(Valletta)" title="Grandmaster&#39;s Palace (Valletta)">Grandmaster\'s Palace</a> and the seven original <a href="/wiki/Langue_(Knights_Hospitaller)#Valletta" title="Langue (Knights Hospitaller)">auberges</a>. Many of Cassar\'s buildings were modified over the years, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> period. However, a few buildings, such as <a href="/wiki/Auberge_d%27Aragon" title="Auberge d&#39;Aragon">Auberge d\'Aragon</a> and the exterior of St. John\'s Co-Cathedral, still retain most of Cassar\'s original Mannerist design.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mannerism_in_literature_and_music">Mannerism in literature and music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Mannerism in literature and music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Metaphysical_poets" title="Metaphysical poets">Metaphysical poets</a></div>\n<p>In English literature, Mannerism is commonly identified with the qualities of the "Metaphysical" poets of whom the most famous is <a href="/wiki/John_Donne" title="John Donne">John Donne</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The witty sally of a Baroque writer, <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a>, against the verse of Donne in the previous generation, affords a concise contrast between Baroque and Mannerist aims in the arts:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>He affects the metaphysics, not only in his satires but in his amorous verses, <i>where nature only</i> should reign; and <i>perplexes the minds</i> of the fair sex with <i>nice</i><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <i>speculations</i> of philosophy when he should <i>engage their hearts</i> and entertain them with the softnesses of love.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1957_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1957-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> (italics added)\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>The rich musical possibilities in the poetry of the late 16th and early 17th centuries provided an attractive basis for the <a href="/wiki/Madrigal_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrigal (music)">madrigal</a>, which quickly rose to prominence as the pre-eminent musical form in Italian musical culture, as discussed by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Carter_(musicologist)" title="Tim Carter (musicologist)">Tim Carter</a>:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The madrigal, particularly in its aristocratic guise, was obviously a vehicle for the \xe2\x80\x98stylish style\xe2\x80\x99 of Mannerism, with poets and musicians revelling in witty conceits and other visual, verbal and musical tricks to delight the connoisseur.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>The word Mannerism has also been used to describe the style of highly florid and <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">contrapuntally</a> complex <a href="/wiki/Polyphonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyphonic">polyphonic</a> music made in France in the late 14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> This period is now usually referred to as the <i><a href="/wiki/Ars_subtilior" title="Ars subtilior">ars subtilior</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mannerism_and_theatre">Mannerism and theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Mannerism and theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>The Early Commedia dell\'Arte (1550\xe2\x80\x931621): The Mannerist Context</i> by Paul Castagno discusses Mannerism\'s effect on the contemporary professional theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Castagno\'s was the first study to define a theatrical form as Mannerist, employing the vocabulary of Mannerism and maniera to discuss the typification, exaggerated, and effetto meraviglioso of the comici dell\'arte. See Part II of the above book for a full discussion of Mannerist characteristics in the commedia dell\'arte. The study is largely iconographic, presenting a pictorial evidence that many of the artists who painted or printed commedia images were in fact, coming from the workshops of the day, heavily ensconced in the maniera tradition.\n</p><p>The preciosity in <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Callot" title="Jacques Callot">Jacques Callot</a>\'s minute engravings seem to belie a much larger scale of action. Callot\'s <i>Balli di Sfessania</i> (literally, dance of the buttocks) celebrates the commedia\'s blatant eroticism, with protruding phalli, spears posed with the anticipation of a comic ream, and grossly exaggerated masks that mix the bestial with human. The eroticism of the innamorate (lovers) including the baring of breasts, or excessive veiling, was quite in vogue in the paintings and engravings from the second school at Fontainebleau, particularly those that detect a Franco-Flemish influence. Castagno demonstrates iconographic linkages between genre painting and the figures of the commedia dell\'arte that demonstrate how this theatrical form was embedded within the cultural traditions of the late cinquecento.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Commedia_dell.27arte.2C_disegno_interno.2C_and_the_discordia_concors"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Commedia_dell\'arte,_disegno_interno,_and_the_discordia_concors">Commedia dell\'arte, <i>disegno interno</i>, and the <i>discordia concors</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Commedia dell&#039;arte, disegno interno, and the discordia concors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Important corollaries exist between the <i>disegno interno</i>, which substituted for the <i>disegno esterno</i> (external design) in mannerist painting. This notion of projecting a deeply subjective view as superseding nature or established principles (perspective, for example), in essence, the emphasis away from the object to its subject, now emphasizing execution, displays of virtuosity, or unique techniques. This inner vision is at the heart of commedia performance. For example, in the moment of improvisation the actor expresses his virtuosity without heed to formal boundaries, decorum, unity, or text. Arlecchino became emblematic of the mannerist <i>discordia concors</i> (the union of opposites), at one moment he would be gentle and kind, then, on a dime, become a thief violently acting out with his batte. Arlecchino could be graceful in movement, only in the next beat, to clumsily trip over his feet. Freed from the external rules, the actor celebrated the evanescence of the moment; much the way Cellini would dazzle his patrons by draping his sculptures, unveiling them with lighting effects and a sense of the marvelous. The presentation of the object became as important as the object itself.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neo-Mannerism">Neo-Mannerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Neo-Mannerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to art critic <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Saltz" title="Jerry Saltz">Jerry Saltz</a>, "Neo-Mannerism" (new Mannerism) is among several clich\xc3\xa9s that are "squeezing the life out of the art world".<sup id="cite_ref-saltz_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saltz-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  Neo-Mannerism describes art of the 21st century that is turned out by students whose academic teachers "have scared [them] into being pleasingly meek, imitative, and ordinary".<sup id="cite_ref-saltz_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saltz-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
