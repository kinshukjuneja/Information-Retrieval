id="England_during_the_Tudor_period_.281486.E2.80.931603.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="England_during_the_Tudor_period_(1486\xe2\x80\x931603)">England during the Tudor period (1486\xe2\x80\x931603)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: England during the Tudor period (1486\xe2\x80\x931603)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor period</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Renaissance">English Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: English Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance" title="English Renaissance">English Renaissance</a></div>\n<p>The term, "<a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance" title="English Renaissance">English Renaissance</a>" is used by many historians to refer to a cultural movement in England in the 16th and 17th centuries that was heavily influenced by the Italian Renaissance. This movement is characterised by the flowering of English music (particularly the English adoption and development of the madrigal), notable achievements in drama (by William Shakespeare, Christopher Marlowe, and Ben Jonson), and the development of English epic poetry (most famously Edmund Spenser\'s <i>The Faerie Queene</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The idea of the Renaissance has come under increased criticism by many cultural historians, and some have contended that the "English Renaissance" has no real tie with the artistic achievements and aims of the northern Italian artists (<a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>) who are closely identified with the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>.\n</p><p>Other cultural historians have countered that, regardless of whether the name "renaissance" is apt, there was undeniably an artistic flowering in England under the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor monarchs</a>, culminating in Shakespeare and his contemporaries.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_rise_of_the_Tudors">The rise of the Tudors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The rise of the Tudors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some scholars date the beginning of Early Modern Britain to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Roses" title="Wars of the Roses">Wars of the Roses</a> and the crowning of  <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry Tudor</a> in 1485 after his victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bosworth_Field" title="Battle of Bosworth Field">battle of Bosworth Field</a>.  Henry VII\'s largely peaceful reign ended decades of civil war and brought the peace and stability to England that art and commerce need to thrive.  A major war on English soil would not occur again until the English Civil War of the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During this period Henry VII and his son <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> greatly increased the power of the English monarchy.  A similar pattern was unfolding on the continent as new technologies, such as <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a>, and social and ideological changes undermined the power of the feudal nobility and enhanced that of the sovereign.  Henry VIII also <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">made use</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> to seize the power of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">confiscating the property of the monasteries</a> and declaring himself the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Supremacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Supremacy">head</a> of the new <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican Church">Anglican Church</a>.  Under the Tudors the English state was centralized and rationalized as a bureaucracy built up and the government became run and managed by educated functionaries.  The most notable new institution was the <a href="/wiki/Star_Chamber" title="Star Chamber">Star Chamber</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right</a> of kings to rule over their subjects.  <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a> was a major proponent of this idea and wrote extensively on it.\n</p><p>The same forces that had reduced the power of the traditional aristocracy also served to increase the power of the commercial classes.  The rise of trade and the central importance of money to the operation of the government gave this new class great power, but power that was not reflected in the government structure.  This would lead to a long contest during the 17th century between the forces of the monarch and parliament.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Elizabethan_era_.281558.E2.80.931603.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Elizabethan_era_(1558\xe2\x80\x931603)">Elizabethan era (1558\xe2\x80\x931603)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Elizabethan era (1558\xe2\x80\x931603)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_era" title="Elizabethan era">Elizabethan era</a></div>\n<p>The <b>Elizabethan Era</b> is the reign of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth I</a> (1558\xe2\x80\x931603) and is known to be a <a href="/wiki/Golden_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden age">golden age</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_England" title="History of England">English history</a>. It was the height of the <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance" title="English Renaissance">English Renaissance</a> and saw the flowering of <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_poetry" title="English poetry">poetry</a>. This was also the time during which <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabethan theatre">Elizabethan theatre</a> was famous and <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, among others, composed plays that broke away from England\'s past style of plays and theatre. It was an age of expansion and exploration abroad, while at home the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> became entrenched in the national mindset.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Elizabethan Age is viewed so highly because of the contrasts with the periods before and after. It was a brief period of largely internal peace between the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a> and the battles between <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholicism">Catholics</a> and the battles between <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">parliament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">monarchy</a> that engulfed the 17th century. The Protestant/Catholic divide was settled, for a time, by the <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_Religious_Settlement" title="Elizabethan Religious Settlement">Elizabethan Religious Settlement</a>, and parliament was not yet strong enough to challenge royal absolutism. \nEngland was also well-off compared to the other nations of Europe. The <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> had come to an end under the weight of foreign domination of the peninsula. France was embroiled in its own religious battles that would only be settled in 1598 with the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a>. In part because of this, but also because the English had been expelled from their last outposts on the continent, the centuries long conflict between France and England was largely suspended for most of Elizabeth\'s reign.\n</p><p>The one great rival was Spain, with which England conflicted both in Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> in skirmishes that exploded into the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585)">Anglo-Spanish War</a> of 1585\xe2\x80\x931604. An attempt by <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a> to invade England with the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> in 1588 was famously defeated, but the tide of war turned against England with a disastrously unsuccessful attack upon Spain, the <a href="/wiki/English_Armada" title="English Armada">Drake-Norris Expedition of 1589</a>. Thereafter Spain provided some support for Irish Catholics in a draining <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War_(Ireland)" title="Nine Years&#39; War (Ireland)">guerrilla war</a> against England, and Spanish naval and land forces inflicted a series of defeats upon English forces. This badly damaged both the English Exchequer and economy that had been so carefully restored under Elizabeth\'s prudent guidance. English colonisation and trade would be frustrated until the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_London,_1604" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of London, 1604">Treaty of London</a> the year following Elizabeth\'s death.\n</p><p>England during this period had a centralised, well-organised, and effective government, largely a result of the reforms of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>. Economically, the country began to benefit greatly from the new era of trans-Atlantic trade.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland_from_15th_century_to_1603">Scotland from 15th century to 1603</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scotland from 15th century to 1603">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Scotland advanced markedly in educational terms during the 15th century with the founding of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">University of St Andrews</a> in 1413, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a> in 1450 and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen" title="University of Aberdeen">University of Aberdeen</a> in 1495, and with the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Education_Act_1496" title="Education Act 1496">Education Act 1496</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1468 the last great acquisition of Scottish territory occurred when <a href="/wiki/James_III_of_Scotland" title="James III of Scotland">James III</a> married <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Denmark,_Queen_of_Scotland" title="Margaret of Denmark, Queen of Scotland">Margaret of Denmark</a>, receiving the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Shetland Islands">Shetland Islands</a> in payment of her dowry.\n</p><p>After the death of James III in 1488, during or after the Battle of Sauchieburn, his successor <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James IV</a> successfully ended the quasi-independent rule of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Isles" title="Lord of the Isles">Lord of the Isles</a>, bringing the Western Isles under effective Royal control for the first time. In 1503, he married <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII\'s</a> daughter, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Tudor" title="Margaret Tudor">Margaret Tudor</a>, thus laying the foundation for the 17th century <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Crowns" title="Union of the Crowns">Union of the Crowns</a>. James IV\'s reign is often considered to be a period of cultural flourishing, and it was around this period that the European <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> began to infiltrate Scotland. James IV was the last known Scottish king known to speak <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Gaelic</a>, although some suggest his son could also.\n</p><p>In 1512, under a treaty extending the Auld Alliance, all nationals of Scotland and France also became nationals of each other\'s countries, a status not repealed in France until 1903 and which may never have been repealed in Scotland.  However a year later, the Auld Alliance had more disastrous effects when James IV was required to launch an invasion of England to support the French when they were attacked by the English under <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>. The invasion was stopped decisively at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Flodden_Field" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Flodden Field">battle of Flodden Field</a> during which the King, many of his nobles, and over 10,000 troops\xe2\x80\x94<i>The Flowers of the Forest</i>\xe2\x80\x94were killed. The extent of the disaster impacted throughout Scotland because of the large numbers killed, and once again Scotland\'s government lay in the hands of regents. The song <i><a href="/wiki/Flowers_of_the_Forest" title="Flowers of the Forest">The Flooers o\' the Forest</a></i> commemorated this, an echo of the poem <i><a href="/wiki/Gododdin" title="Gododdin">Y Gododdin</a></i> on a similar tragedy in about 600.\n</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/James_V_of_Scotland" title="James V of Scotland">James V</a> finally managed to escape from the custody of the regents with the aid of his redoubtable mother in 1528, he once again set about subduing the rebellious Highlands, Western and Northern isles, as his father had had to do. He married the French noblewoman <a href="/wiki/Marie_de_Guise" class="mw-redirect" title="Marie de Guise">Marie de Guise</a>. His reign was fairly successful, until another disastrous campaign against England led to defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Solway_Moss" title="Battle of Solway Moss">battle of Solway Moss</a> (1542). James died a short time later. The day before his death, he was brought news of the birth of an heir: a daughter, who became <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a>. James is supposed to have remarked in <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> that <i>"it cam wi a lass, it will gang wi a lass"</i>\xe2\x80\x94referring to the House of Stewart which began with Walter Stewart\'s marriage to the daughter of Robert the Bruce. Once again, Scotland was in the hands of a regent, James Hamilton, Earl of Arran.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Mary.2C_Queen_of_Scots"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mary,_Queen_of_Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mary, Queen of Scots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Within two years, the <a href="/wiki/The_Rough_Wooing" class="mw-redirect" title="The Rough Wooing">Rough Wooing</a>, Henry VIII\'s military attempt to force a marriage between Mary and his son, Edward, had begun. This took the form of border skirmishing. To avoid the "rough wooing", Mary was sent to France at the age of five, as the intended bride of the heir to the French throne. Her mother stayed in Scotland to look after the interests of Mary\xe2\x80\x94and of France\xe2\x80\x94although the <a href="/wiki/James_Hamilton,_2nd_Earl_of_Arran" class="mw-redirect" title="James Hamilton, 2nd Earl of Arran">Earl of Arran</a> acted officially as regent.\n</p><p>In 1547, after the death of Henry VIII, forces under the English regent <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seymour,_1st_Duke_of_Somerset" title="Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset">Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset</a> were victorious at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pinkie_Cleugh" title="Battle of Pinkie Cleugh">Battle of Pinkie Cleugh</a>, the climax of the <i>Rough Wooing</i> and followed up by occupying Edinburgh. However it was to no avail since much of Scotland was still an unstable environment. She did not do well and after only seven turbulent years, at the end of which Protestants had gained complete control of Scotland, she had  perforce to abdicate. Imprisoned for a time in Loch Leven Castle, she eventually escaped and attempted to regain the throne by force. After her defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Langside" title="Battle of Langside">Battle of Langside</a> in 1568 she took refuge in England, leaving her young son, <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI</a>, in the hands of regents. In England she became a focal point for Catholic conspirators and was eventually executed on the orders of her kinswoman <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_Reformation">Protestant Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Protestant Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Scottish Reformation</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:108px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Knox.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/33/John_Knox.jpg" width="106" height="150" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="106" data-file-height="150" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Knox.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In 1559 <a href="/wiki/John_Knox" title="John Knox">John Knox</a> returned from ministering in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> to lead the <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinist</a> reformation in Scotland</div></div></div>\n<p>During the 16th century, Scotland underwent a <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>. In the earlier part of the century, the teachings of first <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> and then <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a> began to influence Scotland. The execution of a number of Protestant preachers, most notably the Lutheran influenced <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Hamilton_(martyr)" title="Patrick Hamilton (martyr)">Patrick Hamilton</a> in 1528 and later the proto-Calvinist <a href="/wiki/George_Wishart" title="George Wishart">George Wishart</a> in 1546 who was burnt at the stake in St. Andrews by <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Beaton" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Beaton">Cardinal Beaton</a> for heresy, did nothing to stem the growth of these ideas. Beaton was assassinated shortly after the execution of George Wishart.\n</p><p>The eventual Reformation of the Scottish Church followed a brief civil war in 1559\xe2\x80\x9360, in which English intervention on the Protestant side was decisive. A Reformed confession of faith was adopted by Parliament in 1560, while the young <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a>, was still in France. The most influential figure was <a href="/wiki/John_Knox" title="John Knox">John Knox</a>, who had been a disciple of both John Calvin and George Wishart. <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> was not totally eliminated, and remained strong particularly in parts of the highlands.\n</p><p>The Reformation remained somewhat precarious through the reign of Queen Mary, who remained Roman Catholic but tolerated Protestantism. Following her deposition in 1567, her infant son <a href="/wiki/James_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI">James VI</a> was raised as a Protestant. In 1603, following the death of the childless Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a>, the crown of England passed to James. He took the title <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I of England</a> and James VI of Scotland, thus unifying these two countries under his personal rule. For a time, this remained the only political connection between two independent nations, but it foreshadowed the eventual 1707 union of Scotland and England under the banner of the  Great Britain.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Early_Stuart_era:_1603.E2.80.931660"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Stuart_era:_1603\xe2\x80\x931660">Early Stuart era: 1603\xe2\x80\x931660</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early Stuart era: 1603\xe2\x80\x931660">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Crowns" title="Union of the Crowns">Union of the Crowns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean era</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caroline_era" title="Caroline era">Caroline era</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_Interregnum" class="mw-redirect" title="English Interregnum">English Interregnum</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">English Restoration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">House of Stuart</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Union_of_the_Crowns">Union of the Crowns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Union of the Crowns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg/170px-James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg" width="170" height="246" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg/255px-James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg/340px-James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2661" data-file-height="3849" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_I_of_England_by_Daniel_Mytens.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I of England</a> by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Mytens" class="mw-redirect" title="Daniel Mytens">Daniel Mytens</a>  (1621)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Crowns" title="Union of the Crowns">Union of the Crowns</a> refers to the accession of <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_Scots" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Scots">King of Scots</a>, to the throne as King <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I of England</a> while remaining King James VI of Scotland. One man ruled two separate kingdoms with separate governments and cabinets.  The two countries remained distinct and separate until the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union</a> in 1707.  Within eight hours of the death of Elizabeth, James was proclaimed king in London, the news received without protest or disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean era</a></b> refers to the years of the reign of James I in England, 1603\xe2\x80\x931625. The Jacobean era succeeds the <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_era" title="Elizabethan era">Elizabethan era</a> and precedes the <a href="/wiki/Caroline_era" title="Caroline era">Caroline era</a>, and specifically denotes a style of <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_architecture" title="Jacobean architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decorative_arts" title="Decorative arts">decorative arts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/English_literature#Jacobean_literature" title="English literature">literature</a> that is predominant of that period.\n</p><p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Caroline_era" title="Caroline era">Caroline era</a></b> refers to the years of the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> over both countries, 1625\xe2\x80\x941642. It was followed by the  <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> (1642\xe2\x80\x931651) and the <a href="/wiki/English_Interregnum" class="mw-redirect" title="English Interregnum">English Interregnum</a> (1651\xe2\x80\x931660), when there was no king.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Civil_War">English Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: English Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a></b> consisted of a series of armed conflicts and political machinations that took place between Parliamentarians (known as <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Roundheads</a>) and Royalists (known as <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Cavaliers</a>) between 1642 and 1651. The <a href="#The_First_English_Civil_War">first</a> (1642\xe2\x80\x931646) and <a href="#The_Second_English_Civil_War">second</a> (1648\xe2\x80\x931649) <a href="/wiki/Civil_war" title="Civil war">civil wars</a> pitted the supporters of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a> against the supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Long_Parliament" title="Long Parliament">Long Parliament</a>, while the <a href="#The_Third_English_Civil_War">third war</a> (1649\xe2\x80\x931651) saw fighting between supporters of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">King Charles II</a> and supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Rump_Parliament" title="Rump Parliament">Rump Parliament</a>. The Civil War ended with the Parliamentary victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Worcester" title="Battle of Worcester">Battle of Worcester</a> on 3 September 1651. \nThe <i><a href="/wiki/Diggers" title="Diggers">Diggers</a></i> were a group begun by <a href="/wiki/Gerrard_Winstanley" title="Gerrard Winstanley">Gerrard Winstanley</a>  in 1649 who  attempted to reform the existing <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a> with an agrarian lifestyle based upon their ideas for the creation of small <a href="/wiki/Egalitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Egalitarian">egalitarian</a> rural communities. They were one of a number of <a href="/wiki/Nonconformists" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonconformists">nonconformist</a> <a href="/wiki/English_Dissenters" title="English Dissenters">dissenting groups</a> that emerged around this time.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg/220px-The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg" width="220" height="202" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg/330px-The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg/440px-The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg 2x" data-file-width="472" data-file-height="433" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Arrival_of_William_III.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Arrival of William III</i> by Sir <a href="/wiki/James_Thornhill" title="James Thornhill">James Thornhill</a>. <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> landed in England on 5 November (<a href="/wiki/Guy_Fawkes_night" class="mw-redirect" title="Guy Fawkes night">Guy Fawkes</a> day) 1688.</div></div></div> \n<p>The <b>English <a href="/wiki/Interregnum" title="Interregnum">Interregnum</a></b> was the period of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary">parliamentary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> rule in the land occupied by modern-day <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> after the English Civil War. It began with the <a href="/wiki/Regicide" title="Regicide">regicide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> in 1649 and ended with the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">restoration</a> of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> in 1660.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Protectorate_1653.E2.80.931660"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protectorate_1653\xe2\x80\x931660">Protectorate 1653\xe2\x80\x931660</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protectorate 1653\xe2\x80\x931660">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Civil War led to the trial and <a href="/wiki/Execution_of_Charles_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Execution of Charles I">execution of Charles I</a>, the exile of his son Charles II, and the replacement of the English monarchy with first the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a> (1649\xe2\x80\x931653) and then with a <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">Protectorate</a> (1653\xe2\x80\x931659), under the personal rule of <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>. Upon his death, his son took over, but proved a weak ruler with very little support.  The military and religious elements that supported Cromwell began disputing with each other.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Later_Stuart_era_1660.E2.80.931714"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Stuart_era_1660\xe2\x80\x931714">Later Stuart era 1660\xe2\x80\x931714</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Later Stuart era 1660\xe2\x80\x931714">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Restoration_1660.E2.80.931688"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration_1660\xe2\x80\x931688">Restoration 1660\xe2\x80\x931688</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Restoration 1660\xe2\x80\x931688">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration (England)</a></div>\n<p>In 1660, the remaining members of the <a href="/wiki/Long_Parliament" title="Long Parliament">Long Parliament</a> (1640\xe2\x80\x931660) rejected the anarchy and confusion since Cromwell\'s death. Elite and popular opinion called for a restoration of the monarchy under the Stuarts.  There was widespread revulsion against the intense moralism and high taxes of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_the_Major_Generals" class="mw-redirect" title="Rule of the Major Generals">Rule of the Major Generals</a> in 1657. Nostalgia for Royal traditionalism was strong. Gen. <a href="/wiki/George_Monck" class="mw-redirect" title="George Monck">George Monck</a>, a former supporter of Cromwell, Let the movement for a royal restoration.  Charles II in exile paid close attention to the developments and readied himself to take the throne. From Breda in Holland he issued the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Breda" title="Declaration of Breda">Declaration of Breda</a>, promising generosity and indicating his willingness to leave the settlement to Parliament. The Convention Parliament, which had been elected to negotiate with the King, invited Charles to return, He landed at Dover amid great enthusiasm on May 26, 1660.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg/220px-Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg" width="220" height="306" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg/330px-Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1e/Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg 2x" data-file-width="380" data-file-height="529" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Collecting the dead for burial during the Great Plague</div></div></div>\n<p>The new parliament, known as the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier_Parliament" title="Cavalier Parliament">Cavalier Parliament</a> enacted the <a href="/wiki/Clarendon_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Clarendon Code">Clarendon Code</a>, designed to shore up the position of the re-established <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. Strict rules were set up such that only genuine members of the established Church could hold office. The major foreign policy issue was The trade rivalry with  the Dutch, leading to the inconclusive <a href="/wiki/Second_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Second Anglo-Dutch War">Second Anglo-Dutch War</a> of 1665-67. The only positive result was the acquisition of new Netherland, which became New York.  Coincidental with the war with the Dutch, was the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_London" title="Great Plague of London">Great Plague of London</a> of 1665-66, which at its worst cost 1000 deaths a day in London.  On top of that, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a> burned out the main commercial districts of London; it destroyed 13,000 buildings, but few lives were lost.  In 1670, King Charles entered into the <a href="/wiki/Secret_treaty_of_Dover" class="mw-redirect" title="Secret treaty of Dover">secret treaty of Dover</a>, an alliance with his first cousin King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a>. Louis agreed to aid him in the <a href="/wiki/Third_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Third Anglo-Dutch War">Third Anglo-Dutch War</a> and pay him a pension, and Charles secretly promised to convert to Catholicism at an unspecified future date; he did so on his deathbed. Charles attempted to introduce religious equality for Catholics and non-Anglican Protestant dissenters with his 1672 <a href="/wiki/Royal_Declaration_of_Indulgence" title="Royal Declaration of Indulgence">Royal Declaration of Indulgence</a>. Elite opinion rejected it and Parliament forced him to withdraw it. In 1679, <a href="/wiki/Titus_Oates" title="Titus Oates">Titus Oates</a>\'s highly exaggerated revelations of a supposed "<a href="/wiki/Popish_Plot" title="Popish Plot">Popish Plot</a>" sparked the <a href="/wiki/Exclusion_Crisis" title="Exclusion Crisis">Exclusion Crisis</a> when it was revealed that Charles\'s brother and heir (<a href="/wiki/James,_Duke_of_York" class="mw-redirect" title="James, Duke of York">James, Duke of York</a>) was a Catholic. The issue was whether or not to exclude James from succession to the throne. The crisis sparked the creation of the first political parties: the pro-exclusion <a href="/wiki/British_Whig_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="British Whig Party">Whig Party</a> demanded that James never reach the throne. The anti-exclusion <a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory party</a> believed it was against God\'s will to interfere with the legitimate succession, and supported  both the King and James. After the discovery of the failed 1683 <a href="/wiki/Rye_House_Plot" title="Rye House Plot">Rye House Plot</a> to murder Charles and James, some Whig leaders were executed or forced into exile. Charles dissolved Parliament in 1681, and ruled alone until his death on 6 February 1685.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glorious_Revolution_1688-89">Glorious Revolution 1688-89</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Glorious Revolution 1688-89">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a></div>\n<p>When Charles II died in 1685 his brother became <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">King James II</a>; He ruled with the support of the Tory party. He forced a series of highly unpopular proposals that would restore Catholicism to England. The <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_Rebellion" title="Monmouth Rebellion">Monmouth Rebellion</a> broke out in western areas that was brutally suppressed.   Elite opinion strongly turned against the king, and in late 1688  the elites invited <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mary_II_of_England" title="Mary II of England">Mary II</a> to govern.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  James went into exile in France, where his claims to the English throne were promoted by King Louis XIV. In England the claims were upheld by the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> faction of Tories, who in alliance with France were a military threat to the throne for the next half-century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>   William III ruled 1689\xe2\x80\x931702, while his wife <a href="/wiki/Mary_II_of_England" title="Mary II of England">Queen Mary II</a> was the nominal co-ruler until her death in 1694. Constitutionally, the Glorious Revolution established a precedent that British monarchs could not govern without the consent of Parliament, as enacted through the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> of 1688, the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689" title="Bill of Rights 1689">English Bill of Rights</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1701" title="Act of Settlement 1701">Hanoverian succession</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Dutch_Wars">Anglo-Dutch Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Anglo-Dutch Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Dutch_Wars" title="Anglo-Dutch Wars">Anglo-Dutch Wars</a> were a series of three wars which took place between the English and the Dutch from 1652 to 1674. The causes included political disputes and increasing competition from merchant shipping.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  Religion was not a factor, since both sides were Protestant. The British in the first war (1652\xe2\x80\x9354) had the naval advantage with larger numbers of more powerful "<a href="/wiki/Ship_of_the_line" title="Ship of the line">ships of the line</a>" which were well suited to the naval tactics of the era.. The British also captured numerous Dutch merchant ships. In the second war (1665\xe2\x80\x9367) Dutch naval victories followed. This second war cost London ten times more than it had planned on, and the king sued for peace in 1667 with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Breda_(1667)" title="Treaty of Breda (1667)">Treaty of Breda</a>. It ended the fights over "mercantilism" (that is, the use of force to protect and expand national trade, industry, and shipping.) Meanwhile, the French were building up fleets that threatened both the Netherlands and Great Britain.  In third war (1672\xe2\x80\x9374), The British counted on a new alliance with France but the outnumbered Dutch outsailed both of them, and King Charles II ran short of money and political support. The Dutch gained domination of sea trading routes until 1713. The British gained the thriving colony of <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a>, and renamed it New York.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century">18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Georgian_era" title="Georgian era">Georgian era</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of the United Kingdom">History of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The 18th century was characterised by numerous major wars,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> especially with France, with the growth and collapse of the First British Empire, with the origins of the Second British Empire, and with steady economic and social growth at home.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Peace between England and the Netherlands in 1688 meant that the two countries entered the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War" title="Nine Years&#39; War">Nine Years\' War</a> as allies, but the conflict \xe2\x80\x93 waged in Europe and overseas between France, Spain and the Anglo-Dutch alliance \xe2\x80\x93 left the English a stronger colonial power than the Dutch, who were forced to devote a larger proportion of their military budget on the costly land war in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The 18th century would see England (after 1707, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a>) rise to be the world\'s dominant colonial power, and France becoming its main rival on the imperial stage.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1701, England, Portugal and the Netherlands sided with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> against Spain and France in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>.  The conflict, which France and Spain were to lose, lasted until 1714. The British Empire was territorially enlarged: from France, gaining <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a>, and from Spain, <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Menorca" title="Menorca">Menorca</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, which is still a <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territory" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territory">British overseas territory</a> to this day, became a critical naval base and allowed Britain to control the Atlantic entry and exit point to the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaty_of_Union">Treaty of Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Treaty of Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union" title="Treaty of Union">Treaty of Union</a></div>\n<p>The united <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> was born on May 1, 1707, shortly after the parliaments of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">England</a> had ratified the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union" title="Treaty of Union">Treaty of Union</a> of 1706 by each approving <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union</a> combining the two parliaments and the two royal titles. Deeper political integration had been a key policy of <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> (reigned 1702\xe2\x80\x9314). Under the aegis of the Queen and her advisors a <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union" title="Treaty of Union">Treaty of Union</a> was drawn up, and negotiations between England and Scotland began in earnest in 1706.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scottish proponents of union believed that failure to accede to the Bill would result in the imposition of union under less favourable terms, and months of fierce debate in both capital cities and throughout both kingdoms followed. In Scotland, the debate on occasion dissolved into civil disorder, most notably by the notorious \'Edinburgh Mob\'. The prospect of a union of the kingdoms was deeply unpopular among the Scottish population at large, and talk of an uprising was widespread.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> However Scotland could not long continue. Following the financially disastrous <a href="/wiki/Darien_Scheme" class="mw-redirect" title="Darien Scheme">Darien Scheme</a>, the near-bankrupt <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a> reluctantly accepted the proposals. Supposed financial payoffs to Scottish parliamentarians were later referred to by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> when he wrote "We\'re bought and sold for English gold, <a href="/wiki/Such_a_Parcel_of_Rogues_in_a_Nation" title="Such a Parcel of Rogues in a Nation">Such a Parcel of Rogues in a Nation</a>!<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Recent historians, however, have emphasised the legitimacy of the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Acts of Union took effect in 1707, uniting the separate Parliaments and crowns of England and Scotland and forming the single <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a>.  Queen Anne (already Queen of both England and Scotland) became formally the first occupant of the unified British throne, with Scotland sending forty-five Members to join all existing Members from the parliament of England in the new <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Great_Britain" title="House of Commons of Great Britain">House of Commons of Great Britain</a>, as well as 16 <a href="/wiki/Representative_peers" class="mw-redirect" title="Representative peers">representative peers</a> to join all existing peers from the parliament of England in the new <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jacobite_risings">Jacobite risings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Jacobite risings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_risings" title="Jacobite risings">Jacobite risings</a></div>\n<p>Dynastic security was a factor in Britain, as, indeed, it was in other countries.  The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">House of Stuart</a> had abdicated the throne when King James II (1633\xe2\x80\x931701) fled to France in 1688. However he and his son <a href="/wiki/James_Francis_Edward_Stuart" title="James Francis Edward Stuart">the "Old Pretender" (1688\xe2\x80\x931766)</a> claimed to be the legitimate kings, and had the support of important elements in England, as well as <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">King Louis XIV in France.</a> The main issue was religion; the Stuarts had the support of Catholic Europe, while the Whigs in Britain were staunch opponents of Catholicism.   The great majority of Tories refused to support the Jacobites publicly, although there were numerous quiet supporters.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> After the death of King William III (1702) and Queen Anne (1714), the succession went to the Protestant <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hanover" title="House of Hanover">House of Hanover</a>, starting with King George I in 1714. They were Germans who were not especially popular in Britain. The island nation was vulnerable only to a seaborne invasion, which the Jacobites plotted and attempted.  The major attempts were the <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_rising_of_1715" title="Jacobite rising of 1715">Jacobite rising of 1715</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_rising_of_1745" title="Jacobite rising of 1745">Jacobite rising of 1745</a>. Both failed to rally significant popular support, and the Jacobite defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Culloden" title="Battle of Culloden">Battle of Culloden</a> in 1746 ending any realistic hope of a Stuart restoration.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Historian Basil Williams says, "there was never any serious danger to the dynasty."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_trading_nation">The trading nation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The trading nation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The era was prosperous as entrepreneurs extended the range of their business around the globe. By the 1720s Britain was one of the most prosperous countries in the world, and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a> boasted:  \n</p>\n<dl><dd>we are the most "diligent nation in the world. Vast trade, rich manufactures, mighty wealth, universal correspondence, and happy success have been constant companions of England, and given us the title of an industrious people."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>While the other major powers were primarily motivated toward territorial gains, and protection of their dynasties (such as the Habsburg and Bourbon dynasties, and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">House of Hohenzollern</a>), Britain had a different set of primary interests.  Its main diplomatic goal (besides protecting the homeland from invasion)  was building a worldwide trading network for its merchants, manufacturers, shippers and financiers.  This required a hegemonic <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> so powerful that no rival could sweep its ships from the world\'s trading routes, or invade the British Isles.  The London government enhanced the private sector by incorporating numerous privately financed London-based companies for establishing trading posts and opening import-export businesses across the world. Each was given a monopoly of trade to the specified geographical region. The first enterprise was the <a href="/wiki/Muscovy_Company" title="Muscovy Company">Muscovy Company</a> set up in 1555 to trade with Russia.  Other prominent enterprises included the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>, and the  <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson&#39;s Bay Company">Hudson\'s Bay Company</a> in Canada.\n</p><p>The Company of Royal Adventurers Trading to Africa  had been set up in 1662 to trade in gold, ivory and slaves in Africa; it was reestablished as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_African_Company" title="Royal African Company">Royal African Company</a> in 1672 and focused on the slave trade. British involvement in the each of the four major wars, 1740 to 1783, paid off handsomely in terms of trade.  Even the loss of the 13 colonies was made up by a very favorable trading relationship with the new United States of America.  British gained dominance in the trade with India, and largely dominated the highly lucrative slave, sugar, and commercial trades originating in West Africa and the West Indies. China would be next on the agenda. Other powers set up similar monopolies on a much smaller scale; only the Netherlands emphasized trade as much as England.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  British exports soared from \xe2\x82\xa46.5 million in 1700, to \xe2\x82\xa414.7 million in 1760 and \xe2\x82\xa443.2 million in 1800.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was one major fiasco that caused heavy losses. The <a href="/wiki/South_Sea_Bubble" class="mw-redirect" title="South Sea Bubble">South Sea Bubble</a> was a business enterprise that exploded in scandal. The <a href="/wiki/South_Sea_Company" title="South Sea Company">South Sea Company</a> was a private business corporation supposedly set up much like the other trading companies, with a focus on South America. Its actual purpose was to renegotiate previous high-interest government loans amounting to \xe2\x82\xa431 million through market manipulation and speculation. It issued stock four times in 1720 that reached about 8,000 investors. Prices kept soaring every day, from \xe2\x82\xa4130 a share to \xe2\x82\xa41,000, with insiders making huge paper profits. The Bubble collapsed overnight, ruining many speculators. Investigations showed bribes had reached into high places\xe2\x80\x94even to the king.  His chief minister <a href="/wiki/Robert_Walpole" title="Robert Walpole">Robert Walpole</a> managed to wind it down with minimal political and economic damage, although some losers fled to exile or committed suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slave_trade">Slave trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Slave trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An important result of the  War of the Spanish Succession was enlarging England\'s role in the slave trade.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  Of special importance was the successful secret negotiation with France to obtain thirty-year monopoly on the Spanish slave trade, called the <a href="/wiki/Asiento" title="Asiento">Asiento</a>. Anne also allowed colonies like Virginia to make laws that promoted black slavery. Anne had secretly negotiated with France to get its approval regarding  the <i>Asiento.</i><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> She boasted to Parliament of her success in taking the <i>Asiento</i> away from France and London celebrated her economic coup.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Most of the slave trade involved sales to Spanish colonies in the Caribbean, and to Mexico, as well as sales to British colonies in the Caribbean and in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>   Historian Vinita Ricks says the agreement allotted Queen Anne "22.5% (and King Philip V, of Spain 28%) of all profits collected for her personal fortune." Ricks concludes that the Queen\'s "connection to slave trade revenue meant that she was no longer a neutral observer.  She had a vested interest in what happened on slave ships."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  In addition to  sales to the Spanish colonies, England had its own sugar islands in the Caribbean, especially Jamaica,  Barbados, Nevis, and Antigua, which provided a steady flow of profits from the slave labor that produced the sugar.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warfare_and_finance">Warfare and finance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Warfare and finance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From 1700 to 1850, Britain was involved in 137 wars or rebellions. Apart from losing the <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a>, it was generally successful in warfare, and was especially successful in financing its military commitments.  France and Spain, by contrast, went bankrupt.  Britain maintained a relatively large and expensive <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, along with a small standing army. When the need arose for soldiers it hired mercenaries or financed allies who fielded armies. The rising costs of warfare forced a shift in government financing from the income from royal agricultural estates and special imposts and taxes to reliance on customs and excise taxes and, after 1790, an income tax. Working with bankers in the City, the government raised large loans during wartime and paid them off in peacetime. The rise in taxes amounted to 20% of national income, but the private sector benefited from the increase in economic growth. The demand for war supplies stimulated the industrial sector, particularly naval supplies, munitions and textiles, which gave Britain an advantage in international trade during the postwar years.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Empire">British Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: British Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historiography_of_the_British_Empire" title="Historiography of the British Empire">Historiography of the British Empire</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clive.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Clive.jpg/220px-Clive.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Clive.jpg/330px-Clive.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Clive.jpg/440px-Clive.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1870" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clive.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lord_Clive" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Clive">Lord Clive</a> meeting with <a href="/wiki/Mir_Jafar" title="Mir Jafar">Mir Jafar</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plassey" title="Battle of Plassey">Battle of Plassey</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Hayman" title="Francis Hayman">Francis Hayman</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1762</span>)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years&#39; War">Seven Years\' War</a>, which began in 1756, was the first war waged on a global scale, fought in Europe, India, North America, the Caribbean, the Philippines and coastal Africa.  The signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1763)" title="Treaty of Paris (1763)">Treaty of Paris (1763)</a> had important consequences for Britain and its empire.  In North America, France\'s future as a colonial power there was effectively ended with the ceding of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> to Britain (leaving a sizeable French-speaking population under British control) and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_(New_France)" title="Louisiana (New France)">Louisiana</a> to Spain.  Spain ceded <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> to Britain.  In India, the <a href="/wiki/Carnatic_Wars#Third_Carnatic_War_(1757\xe2\x80\x931763)" title="Carnatic Wars">Carnatic War</a> had left France still in control of its <a href="/wiki/French_India" title="French India">enclaves</a> but with military restrictions and an obligation to support British client states, effectively leaving the future of India to Britain.  The <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain_in_the_Seven_Years%27_War" title="Great Britain in the Seven Years&#39; War">British victory over France</a> in the Seven Years\' War therefore left Britain as the world\'s dominant colonial power.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1760s and 1770s, relations between the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> and Britain became increasingly strained, primarily because of resentment of the British Parliament\'s ability to tax American colonists without their consent.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>  Disagreement turned to violence and in 1775 the <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a> began.  The following year, the colonists <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">declared the independence of the United States</a> and with economic and naval assistance from France, would go on to win the war in 1783. The <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Paris_(1783)" title="Peace of Paris (1783)">Treaties of Versailles</a> were signed, also ending war with the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-French_War_(1778%E2%80%931783)" title="Anglo-French War (1778\xe2\x80\x931783)">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1779-1783)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1779-1783)">Spanish</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Fourth Anglo-Dutch War">Fourth Anglo-Dutch War</a> ended the following year.\n</p><p>The loss of the United States, at the time Britain\'s most populous colony, is seen by historians as the event defining the transition between the "first" and "second" empires,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> in which Britain shifted its attention away from the Americas to Asia, the Pacific and later Africa.  <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Wealth_of_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Wealth of Nations">Wealth of Nations</a></i>, published in 1776, had argued that colonies were redundant, and that <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> should replace the old <a href="/wiki/Mercantilist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercantilist">mercantilist</a> policies that had characterised the first period of colonial expansion, dating back to the protectionism of Spain and Portugal.  The growth of trade between the newly independent United States and Britain after 1783<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> confirmed Smith\'s view that political control was not necessary for economic success.\n</p><p>During its 1st century of operation, the focus of the <a href="/wiki/British_East_India_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="British East India Company">British East India Company</a> had been trade, not the building of an empire in India.   Company interests turned from trade to territory during the 18th century as the Mughal Empire declined in power and the British East India Company struggled with its French counterpart, <i><a href="/wiki/French_East_India_Company" title="French East India Company">La Compagnie fran\xc3\xa7aise des Indes orientales</a></i>, during the <a href="/wiki/Carnatic_Wars" title="Carnatic Wars">Carnatic Wars</a> of the 1740s and 1750s.  The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plassey" title="Battle of Plassey">Battle of Plassey</a>, which saw the British, led by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Clive" title="Robert Clive">Robert Clive</a>, defeat the French and their Indian allies, left the Company in control of <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> and a major military and political power in India.  In the following decades it gradually increased the size of the territories under its control, either ruling directly or indirectly via local puppet rulers under the threat of force of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army" title="Indian Army">Indian Army</a>, 80% of which was composed of native Indian <a href="/wiki/Sepoys" class="mw-redirect" title="Sepoys">sepoys</a>.\n</p><p>In 1770, <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> became the first European to visit the eastern coast of Australia whilst on a scientific <a href="/wiki/First_voyage_of_James_Cook" title="First voyage of James Cook">voyage</a> to the South Pacific.  In 1778, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Joseph Banks</a>, Cook\'s botanist on the voyage, presented evidence to the government on the suitability of <a href="/wiki/Botany_Bay" title="Botany Bay">Botany Bay</a> for the establishment of a penal settlement, and in 1787 the first shipment of <a href="/wiki/Convictism_in_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Convictism in Australia">convicts</a> set sail, arriving in 1788.\n</p><p>At the threshold to the 19th century, Britain was challenged again by France under <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>, in a struggle that, unlike previous wars, represented a contest of ideologies between the two nations.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> \nIt was not only Britain\'s position on the world stage that was threatened: Napoleon threatened invasion of Britain itself, and with it, a fate similar to the countries of continental Europe that his armies had overrun. The <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> were therefore ones that Britain invested large amounts of capital and resources to win. French ports were blockaded by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, which won a decisive victory over the French fleet at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Trafalgar</a> in 1805.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_of_state_power">Growth of state power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_modern_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Growth of state power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Recently historians have undertaken a deeper exploration of the growth of state power. The especially look at the <i>long 18th century</i>, from about 1660 to 1837 from four fresh perspectives.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  The first approach, developed by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_MacDonagh" title="Oliver MacDonagh">Oliver MacDonagh</a>, presented an expansive and centralized administrative state while deemphasizing the influence of Benthamite utilitarianism.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>   The second approach, as developed by  Edward Higgs, conceptualizes the state as an information-gathering entity, paying special attention to local registrars and the census.  He brings in such topics as spies, surveillance of  Catholics, the 1605 Gunpowder Plot led by Guy Fawkes to overthrow the government, and the Poor Laws, and demonstrates similarities to the surveillance society of the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  John Brewer introduced the third approach with his depiction of the  unexpectedly powerful, centralized \'fiscal-military\' state during the eighteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>  finally there have been numerous recent studies that explore the state as an abstract entity capable of commanding the loyalties of those people over whom it rules.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
