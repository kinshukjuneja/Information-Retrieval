Loggetta of <a href="/wiki/Campanile_di_San_Marco" class="mw-redirect" title="Campanile di San Marco">Campanile di San Marco</a>, Venice (reconstructed).</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Jacopo d\'Antonio Sansovino</b> (2 July 1486 \xe2\x80\x93 27 November 1570) was an <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> sculptor and architect, known best for his works around the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_San_Marco" title="Piazza San Marco">Piazza San Marco</a> in Venice. <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a>, in the Preface to his <i>Quattro Libri</i> was of the opinion that Sansovino\'s <a href="/wiki/Biblioteca_Marciana" title="Biblioteca Marciana">Biblioteca Marciana</a> was the best building erected since Antiquity. <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> uniquely printed his <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Most_Excellent_Painters,_Sculptors,_and_Architects" title="Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects">Vita</a></i> of Sansovino separately.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" tocsection-6"><a href="#Further_reading"><span class="tocnumber">5.1</span> <span class="toctext">Further reading</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacopo_Sansovino&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>He was born in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> and apprenticed with <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Sansovino" title="Andrea Sansovino">Andrea Sansovino</a> whose name he subsequently adopted, changing his name from Jacopo Tatti.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> he attracted the notice of <a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Bramante</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raffaello_Santi" class="mw-redirect" title="Raffaello Santi">Raphael</a> and made a <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a> model of the <i><a href="/wiki/Deposition_from_the_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Deposition from the Cross">Deposition of Christ</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Perugino" class="mw-redirect" title="Perugino">Perugino</a> to use.\n</p><p>He returned to <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> in 1511 where he received commissions for <a href="/wiki/Marble_sculpture" title="Marble sculpture">marble sculptures</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_James_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint James the Great">St. James</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_del_Fiore" class="mw-redirect" title="Santa Maria del Fiore">Duomo</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Bacchus</a>, now in the <a href="/wiki/Bargello" title="Bargello">Bargello</a>. His proposals for sculpture to adorn the <a href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade" class="mw-redirect" title="Fa\xc3\xa7ade">fa\xc3\xa7ade</a> of the Church of <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_San_Lorenzo_di_Firenze" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica di San Lorenzo di Firenze">San Lorenzo</a>, however, were rejected by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, who was in charge of the scheme, to whom he wrote a bitter letter of protest in 1518.\n</p><p>In the period of 1510-17 he shared a studio with the painter <a href="/wiki/Andrea_del_Sarto" title="Andrea del Sarto">Andrea del Sarto</a>, with whom he shared models. Like all sixteenth-century Italian architects, Sansovino devoted considerable energy to elaborate but temporary structures related to courtly ritual. The triumphant entry of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a> into Florence in 1515 was a highpoint of this genre. He subsequently returned to Rome where he stayed for nine years, leaving for <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in the year of the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(1527)" title="Sack of Rome (1527)">Sack of Rome</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacopo_Sansovino&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1529, Sansovino became chief architect and superintendent of properties (Protomaestro or <i>Proto</i>) to the <a href="/wiki/Procurators_of_San_Marco" class="mw-redirect" title="Procurators of San Marco">Procurators of San Marco</a>, making him one of the most influential artists in Venice. The appointment came with a salary of 80 ducats and an apartment near the clocktower in San Marco. Within a year his salary was raised to 180 ducats per year.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> His masterworks embody prominent structures and buildings in central Venice found near <a href="/wiki/Piazza_San_Marco" title="Piazza San Marco">Piazza San Marco</a>, specifically the rusticated <a href="/wiki/Zecca_of_Venice" title="Zecca of Venice">Zecca</a> (public mint), the highly decorated Loggetta and its sculptures adjoining the <a href="/wiki/St_Mark%27s_Campanile" title="St Mark&#39;s Campanile">Campanile</a>, and various statues and <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">reliefs</a> for the <a href="/wiki/San_Marco_di_Venezia" class="mw-redirect" title="San Marco di Venezia">Basilica of San Marco</a>. He also helped rebuild a number of buildings, churches, palaces, and institutional buildings including the churches of <a href="/wiki/San_Zulian" title="San Zulian">San Zulian</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francesco_della_Vigna" title="San Francesco della Vigna">San Francesco della Vigna</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Martino,_Venice" title="San Martino, Venice">San Martino</a>, San Geminiano (now destroyed), Santo Spirito in Isola, and the church of the Incurabili. Among palaces and buildings are the Scuola Grande della Misericordia (early plans), Ca\' de Dio, <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Dolfin_Manin" title="Palazzo Dolfin Manin">Palazzo Dolfin Manin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Corner" class="mw-redirect" title="Palazzo Corner">Palazzo Corner</a>, Palazzo Moro, and the Fabbriche Nuove di <a href="/wiki/Rialto" title="Rialto">Rialto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His masterpiece is the Library of Saint Mark\'s, the <i><a href="/wiki/Biblioteca_Marciana" title="Biblioteca Marciana">Biblioteca Marciana</a></i>, one of Venice\'s most richly decorated Renaissance structures, which stands in front of the Doge\'s palace, across the piazzetta. Construction spanned fifty years and cost over 30,000 ducats.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In it he successfully made the architectural language of <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">classicism</a>, traditionally associated with severity and restraint, palatable to the Venetians with their love of surface decoration. This paved the way for the graceful architecture of <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a>.\n</p><p>He died in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and his sepulchre is in the Baptistery of <a href="/wiki/St._Mark%27s_Basilica" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Mark&#39;s Basilica">St. Mark\'s Basilica</a>. His most important follower in the medium of sculpture was <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Vittoria" title="Alessandro Vittoria">Alessandro Vittoria</a>; another disciple was the architect and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Danese_Cataneo" class="mw-redirect" title="Danese Cataneo">Danese Cataneo</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallery">Gallery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
