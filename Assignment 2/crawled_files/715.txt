The rank is generally thought to have originated in Sicily from a conflation of <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">\xd8\xa3\xd9\x85\xd9\x8a\xd8\xb1 \xd8\xa7\xd9\x84\xd8\xa8\xd8\xad\xd8\xb1</span>&#8206;, <i><a href="/wiki/Am%C4%ABr" class="mw-redirect" title="Am\xc4\xabr">am\xc4\xabr</a> al-ba\xe1\xb8\xa5r</i>, "commander of the sea", with Latin <i>admirabilis</i><sup id="cite_ref-etymonline.com_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> ("admirable") or <i>admiratus</i> ("admired"), although alternative etymologies derive the word directly from Latin, or from the Turkish military and naval rank <a href="/wiki/Miralay" title="Miralay">miralay</a>. The French version - "Amiral" without the additional "d" - tends to add evidence for the Arab origin.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> and the U.S., a "full" admiral is equivalent to a "full" <a href="/wiki/General_officer" title="General officer">general</a> in the army, and is above <a href="/wiki/Vice_admiral" title="Vice admiral">vice admiral</a> and below <a href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_fleet" title="Admiral of the fleet">admiral of the fleet</a> (or fleet admiral). In NATO, admirals have a rank code of <a href="/wiki/OF-9" class="mw-redirect" title="OF-9">OF-9</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Four-star_rank" title="Four-star rank">four-star rank</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Admiral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word <i lang="enm" title="Middle English language text">admiral</i> in Middle English comes from Anglo-French <i lang="xno" title="Anglo-Norman language text">amiral</i>, "commander", from <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">admiralis</i>, <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">admirallus</i>. These themselves come from <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> <i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text"><a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">am\xc4\xabr</a></i>, or <i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">am\xc4\xabr al-</i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">\xd8\xa3\xd9\x85\xd9\x8a\xd8\xb1&#160;\xd8\xa7\xd9\x84\xd9\x80</span>), "commander of", as in <i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">am\xc4\xabr al-ba\xe1\xb8\xa5r</i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">\xd8\xa3\xd9\x85\xd9\x8a\xd8\xb1&#160;\xd8\xa7\xd9\x84\xd8\xa8\xd8\xad\xd8\xb1</span>), "commander of the sea".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The term was in use for the <a href="/wiki/Christodulus" title="Christodulus">Greco-Arab naval leaders</a> of <a href="/wiki/Norman-Arab-Byzantine_culture" title="Norman-Arab-Byzantine culture">Norman Sicily</a>, which had formerly been ruled by Arabs, at least by the early 11th century.\n</p><p>The Norman <a href="/wiki/Roger_II_of_Sicily" title="Roger II of Sicily">Roger II of Sicily</a> (1095\xe2\x80\x931154), employed a Greek Christian known as <a href="/wiki/George_of_Antioch" title="George of Antioch">George of Antioch</a>, who previously had served as a naval commander for several North African Muslim rulers. Roger styled George in <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid</a> fashion as <i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text"><a href="/wiki/Amir_al-umara" title="Amir al-umara">Amir of Amirs</a></i>, i.e. "Commander of Commanders", with the title becoming Latinized in the 13th century as <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">ammiratus ammiratorum</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sicily#Demographics" title="Sicily">Sicilians</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoese</a> took the first two parts of the term and used them as one word, <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">amiral</i>, from their <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Aragon</a> opponents. The <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> gave their sea commanders similar titles while in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> the word changed to <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">almirante</i>. As the word was used by people speaking <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> or Latin-based languages it gained the "d" and endured a series of different endings and spellings leading to the English spelling <i lang="enm" title="Middle English language text">admyrall</i> in the 14th century and to <i>admiral</i> by the 16th century.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_history">Further history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Admiral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Further history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word "admiral" has today come to be almost exclusively associated with the highest <a href="/wiki/Naval_officer_ranks" title="Naval officer ranks">naval rank</a> in most of the world\'s <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">navies</a>, equivalent to the army rank of (full) <a href="/wiki/General" class="mw-redirect" title="General">general</a>. However, this wasn\'t always the case; for example, in some European countries prior to the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, admiral was the third highest naval rank after <a href="/wiki/General_admiral" title="General admiral">general admiral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_admiral" title="Grand admiral">grand admiral</a>.\n</p><p>The rank of admiral has also been subdivided into various grades, several of which are historically extinct while others remain in use in most present day navies. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> used colours (red, white, and blue, in descending order) to indicate <a href="/wiki/Admiral_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Admiral (United Kingdom)">seniority of its admirals</a> until 1864; for example, <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson" class="mw-redirect" title="Horatio Nelson">Horatio Nelson</a>\'s highest rank was vice admiral of the white. The generic term for these naval equivalents of army generals is <a href="/wiki/Flag_officer" title="Flag officer">flag officer</a>. Some navies have also used army-type titles for them, such as the <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Cromwellian</a> "general at sea".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
