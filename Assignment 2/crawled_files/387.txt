The club, established by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lucas_(baseball)" title="Henry Lucas (baseball)">Henry Lucas</a>, were the one near-major league quality entry in the <a href="/wiki/Union_Association" title="Union Association">Union Association</a>, a league that lasted only one season, due in large part to the dominance of the Maroons.  When the UA folded after playing just one season, the Maroons joined the National League.  In 1887 the Maroons relocated to Indianapolis and became the <b>Indianapolis Hoosiers</b>, playing three more seasons before folding.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#St._Louis_Maroons"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">St. Louis Maroons</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Indianapolis_Hoosiers"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Indianapolis Hoosiers</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="St._Louis_Maroons">St. Louis Maroons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Louis_Maroons/Indianapolis_Hoosiers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: St. Louis Maroons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The St. Louis Maroons debuted on April 20, 1884, at the <a href="/wiki/Union_Grounds_(St._Louis)" title="Union Grounds (St. Louis)">Union Base Ball Park</a>, defeating <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Browns/Pittsburgh_Stogies" title="Chicago Browns/Pittsburgh Stogies">the UA Chicago club</a>, 7-2. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Van_Noye_Lucas" title="Henry Van Noye Lucas">Henry Van Noye Lucas</a>, the founder of the Union Association and owner of the Maroons, stocked his team with most of the league\'s best talent.  They started the season 20-0, a mark that would not be topped in major American professional sports until the <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Warriors" title="Golden State Warriors">Golden State Warriors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/NBA" class="mw-redirect" title="NBA">NBA</a> surpassed it 131 years later <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_Golden_State_Warriors_season" title="2015\xe2\x80\x9316 Golden State Warriors season">in the 2015\xe2\x80\x9316 season</a>. The Maroons went 94-19 in that season; for comparison, the Maroons\' record would project to 135-27 under the modern schedule of 162 games, while Pythagorean expectation based on the Maroons\' results (887 runs scored, 452 runs allowed) and a 162-game schedule would translate to a record of 129-33, but these results are of questionable merit, and their closest rivals, the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Outlaw_Reds" title="Cincinnati Outlaw Reds">Cincinnati Outlaw Reds</a>, finished 21 games behind. Those figures indicate something of the quality of the remainder of the organization, which many derided as the "Onion League".\n</p><p>One of the Maroons\' major stars was pitcher <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Sweeney" title="Charlie Sweeney">Charlie Sweeney</a>, best known today as the pitcher who left <a href="/wiki/Old_Hoss_Radbourn" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Hoss Radbourn">Old Hoss Radbourn</a> to shoulder the pitching burden alone with the <a href="/wiki/Providence_Grays" title="Providence Grays">Providence Grays</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>. Radbourn went on to pitch most of the rest of the Providence club\'s games, winning a total of 60. Sweeney won 24 with the Maroons after having already won 17 with the Grays, so he had a fair year as well.\n</p><p>After the Union Association collapsed, the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> was persuaded to bring the St. Louis Union entry into the established league, to try to provide some competition for the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Browns_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis Browns (NL)">St. Louis Browns</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a>. Unfortunately for the Maroons, the Browns were at the peak of their game, winning pennants four straight years (1885\xe2\x80\x931888). Meanwhile, the Maroons, facing much better competition in the National League, finished well off the National League pace in 1885 and 1886.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fred_Dunlap" title="Fred Dunlap">Fred Dunlap</a> hit for the cycle for the Maroons on May 24, 1886.\n</p><p>Following the 1886 season, the team was sold to the league, which in turn sold it to <a href="/wiki/John_T._Brush" title="John T. Brush">John T. Brush</a>. He moved the team to <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>, where they were renamed the <b>Hoosiers</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Brush owned the stadium in Indianapolis, which had been previously used by the previous Hoosier team.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indianapolis_Hoosiers">Indianapolis Hoosiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Louis_Maroons/Indianapolis_Hoosiers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Indianapolis Hoosiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This was the second major league team to bear the name <b>Indianapolis Hoosiers</b>, though they bore no relationship to the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Hoosiers_(American_Association)" title="Indianapolis Hoosiers (American Association)">earlier team</a> that played in <a href="/wiki/1884_in_baseball" title="1884 in baseball">1884</a>. The Hoosiers three seasons in the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> from <a href="/wiki/1887_in_baseball" title="1887 in baseball">1887</a> to <a href="/wiki/1889_in_baseball" title="1889 in baseball">1889</a> and posted records of 37-89 (8th), 50-85 (7th) and 59-75 (7th), respectively.\n</p><p>The team played its weekday home games at <a href="/wiki/Athletic_Park_(Indianapolis)" class="mw-redirect" title="Athletic Park (Indianapolis)">Athletic Park</a>. Due to <a href="/wiki/Blue_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue laws">blue laws</a>, the club staged Sunday games outside the city limits, at <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Grounds" title="Bruce Grounds">Bruce Grounds</a> in 1887 and at Indianapolis Park during 1888-89. When the team folded, Brush became part-owner of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> member <a href="/wiki/Amos_Rusie" title="Amos Rusie">Amos Rusie</a> made his big league debut with the 1889 Hoosiers.  <a href="/wiki/Jack_Glasscock" title="Jack Glasscock">Jack Glasscock</a> <a href="/wiki/Hit_for_the_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hit for the cycle">hit for the cycle</a> for the Hoosiers on August 8, 1889.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
