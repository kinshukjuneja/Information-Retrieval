href="/w/index.php?title=Plantlife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It was founded in 1989. Its first president was Professor <a href="/wiki/David_Bellamy" title="David Bellamy">David Bellamy</a>. \n</p><p>Its patron is <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">HRH the Prince of Wales</a>, its president is <a href="/wiki/Philip_Mould" title="Philip Mould">Philip Mould</a> OBE and its chairman is Professor David Hill CBE. English gardener and television presenter <a href="/wiki/Rachel_De_Thame" title="Rachel De Thame">Rachel De Thame</a> is their vice-president. The chief executive is Marian Spain, who took over from Victoria Chester in 2014.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantlife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Plantlife\'s principal activities in Britain include the management of 4,500 acres (18&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of rare and important plant habitats as <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a>, lobbying and campaigning in support of wild plant conservation, and organising surveys aimed at generating public interest in wild plants. Plantlife helps run an annual National Plant Monitoring Survey, and a rare species conservation programme, "Back from the Brink". It was a lead partner of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">HRH the Prince of Wales</a>\' Coronation Meadows project.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although much of Plantlife\'s work is centred on plants, it is also involved in the conservation of fungi. Its work in this area includes surveying <a href="/wiki/Waxcap_grassland" title="Waxcap grassland">waxcap grasslands</a> and publishing a strategy for conserving fungi in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The group also has an international programme which includes projects on medicinal plant conservation and sustainable use in the id="County_Flowers_competition">County Flowers competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantlife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: County Flowers competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_county_flowers_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="List of county flowers of the United Kingdom">List of county flowers of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>In 2002 Plantlife ran a competition to select <a href="/wiki/County_flower" class="mw-redirect" title="County flower">county flowers</a> for all counties of the UK. The general public was invited to vote for the bloom they felt most represented their county. The list was declared in 2004.\n</p><p>Although sometimes contested, all have, to date, stuck. The one exception was the <a href="/wiki/County_flower" class="mw-redirect" title="County flower">county flower</a> of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>: originally <a href="/wiki/Alexanders" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexanders">Alexanders</a> won the vote. However, a campaign led by the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Daily_Press" title="Eastern Daily Press">Eastern Daily Press</a> was successful in requesting a change to the <a href="/wiki/Poppy_(flower)" class="mw-redirect" title="Poppy (flower)">poppy</a>, which was felt to be more representative.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Back_from_the_Brink_programme">The Back from the Brink programme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantlife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Back from the Brink programme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Plantlife\'s "Back from the Brink" programme was initiated in 1991. Its intention was to focus conservation efforts on some of the rarest plant species in Britain. It initially concentrated on <a href="/wiki/Vascular_plant" title="Vascular plant">vascular plants</a> but was extended to cover <a href="/wiki/Lower_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower plant">lower plants</a> and fungi. As of 2006, 101 species are covered by the programme. The programme included survey work to establish information about populations of these species, monitoring of populations to identify change over time and the factors relating to this, research into ecological requirements of the species, and site management work aimed at maintaining or restoring habitat conditions suitable for these species. Since 2008 the programme has gradually expanded to include a much larger list of species, this is in response to the publication of both the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Red_List" title="Regional Red List">UK Red List</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="United Kingdom Biodiversity Action Plan">UK Biodiversity Action Plan</a>. To effectively deliver conservation of an ever expanding list of rare species the work will be directed at habitats, where it is hoped that suites of species will respond.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Important_Plant_Areas">Important Plant Areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantlife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Important Plant Areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2007, Plantlife announced the establishment of 150 <a href="/wiki/Important_Plant_Areas" title="Important Plant Areas">Important Plant Areas</a> (or IPAs) across the UK. These areas were nominated for their internationally important wild plant populations. Since then they have been actively raising awareness of these ecologically important habitats and encouraging their long-term protection and improvement through the adoption of an \'ecosystem-based\' conservation approach.\n</p><p>The IPA programme is endorsed by national conservation organisations including the <a href="/wiki/RSPB" class="mw-redirect" title="RSPB">RSPB</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Wildlife_Trusts" title="The Wildlife Trusts">the Wildlife Trusts</a>, and also by UK government bodies including <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Natural_Heritage" title="Scottish Natural Heritage">Scottish Natural Heritage</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_Council_for_Wales" title="Countryside Council for Wales">Countryside Council for Wales</a>.\n</p><p>Plantlife\'s international team has had some success in spreading the concept abroad.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a
