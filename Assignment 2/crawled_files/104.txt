Orkney</b> refers to a period in the human occupation of the <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> archipelago of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> that was the latter part of these islands\' <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a>. The period of prehistory prior to occupation by the genus <i>Homo</i> is part of the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Scotland" title="Geology of Scotland">geology of Scotland</a>. Although some <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">written records</a> refer to Orkney during the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire" title="Scotland during the Roman Empire">Roman invasions of Scotland</a>, prehistory in northern Scotland does not end until the commencement of the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Early_Middle_Ages" title="Scotland in the Early Middle Ages">Early Historic Period</a> around AD 600.\n</p><p>There are numerous important prehistoric remains in Orkney, especially from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period, four of which form a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. There are diverse reasons for the abundance of the archaeological record. The <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> bedrock provides easily workable stone materials and the wind-blown sands have helped preserve several sites.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The relative lack of industrialisation and low incidence of <a href="/wiki/Plough" title="Plough">ploughing</a> also have helped to preserve these ancient monuments. Local tradition hints at both a fear and veneration of these ancient structures (perhaps inherited from the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> period of occupation) that may have helped to retain their structural integrity.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Prehistory is conventionally divided into a number of shorter periods but differentiating these various <a href="/wiki/List_of_archaeological_periods" title="List of archaeological periods">eras of human history</a> is a complex task &#8211; their boundaries are uncertain and the changes between them are gradual. The <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> lasted until the retreat of the ice, the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> until the adoption of farming and the Neolithic until <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a> commenced. A number of the sites span long periods of time and in particular the distinctions between the Neolithic and the later periods are not clear cut.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The extraordinary wealth of structures from the Neolithic is not matched either by the early periods, for which the evidence of human occupation is sparse or non-existent, or the later <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> which provides a relative dearth of evidence. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> supported a return to monumental building, especially of <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">brochs</a>.\n</p><p>Formal excavations were first recorded in the late 18th century and as they proceeded an understanding of the structures involved progressed from little more than folklore to modern archaeological science. The sites discussed are found on the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Mainland" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Mainland">Orkney Mainland</a> unless otherwise stated.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Paleolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>No traces have yet been found in Scotland of either a <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a> presence or of <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i> during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacials</a>. The first indications of humans occur only after the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">ice retreated</a> in the <a href="/wiki/11th_millennium_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="11th millennium BC">11th millennium BC</a> and the current <a href="/wiki/Flandrian_interglacial" title="Flandrian interglacial">Flandrian interglacial</a> began. Since that time the landscape of Orkney has been altered by both human and natural forces.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Initially, <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea levels</a> were lower than at present due to the large volume of ice that remained. This meant that the Orkney islands may have been attached to the mainland, as was the present-day island of Great Britain to <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental Europe</a>. Much of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> basin was also dry land until after 4000 <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">BC</a>. This would have made travel to northern Scotland relatively easy for early human settlers. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic</a> rise of land makes estimating post-glacial coastlines a complex task.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mesolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The very limited <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> record provides scant evidence of Mesolithic life in Orkney in particular and Scotland north of <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> in general. "Lithic scatter" sites at Seatter, South Ettit, Wideford Hill, Valdigar and <a href="/wiki/Loch_of_Stenness" title="Loch of Stenness">Loch of Stenness</a> have produced small polished stone tools and chippings. A charred hazelnut shell, recovered during the excavations at Longhowe in <a href="/wiki/Tankerness" title="Tankerness">Tankerness</a> in 2007, has been dated to 6820-6660 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, there is no evidence to suggest whether or not these sites were in year-round occupation and no Mesolithic burial sites have been uncovered anywhere in Scotland to date.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A recently excavated site on <a href="/wiki/Stronsay" title="Stronsay">Stronsay</a> has produced a thousand pieces of <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> and what may be evidence of a temporary camp. With a tentative dating of 7000 BC or older it may prove to be the oldest settlement site found so far on Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>About 6000 BC the <a href="/wiki/Storegga_Slides" class="mw-redirect" title="Storegga Slides">Storegga Slides</a> of the coast of Norway created a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> that reached 25 metres (82&#160;ft) above normal high tides in places. Evidence of widespread coastal inundations from a wave 8 metres (26&#160;ft) high has been found as far south as <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a> and the impact on shore-dwelling mesolithic societies in Orkney would have been considerable.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic">Neolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Knapofhowarinsun.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Knapofhowarinsun.jpg/220px-Knapofhowarinsun.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Knapofhowarinsun.jpg/330px-Knapofhowarinsun.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Knapofhowarinsun.jpg/440px-Knapofhowarinsun.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Knapofhowarinsun.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The seaward walls of Knap of Howar</div></div></div>\n<p>The assemblage of monumental Neolithic structures in Orkney is without parallel in the United Kingdom and on the Orkney Mainland provides an entire landscape of features from this period. During this time, complex new societies came to the fore that were a radical departure from the earlier hunter-gatherers and which were capable of creating substantial structures. The Neolithic in Scotland lasted from approximately 4000 to 2200 BC and Orkney as a whole has nearly 3,000 identified Neolithic sites all told. British archaeologists have often interpreted this era as having two distinct phases; the Earlier Neolithic dominated by regional styles of pottery and architecture followed by a relatively abrupt change into the Later Neolithic characterised by new traditions found throughout the British Isles that incorporate structures on a grander scale. In the Orcadian context, there are definite developments during the Neolithic, but the changes are gradual and tend to build on earlier ideas rather than appearing to form two distinct periods.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The great Orcadian Neolithic monuments were constructed almost a millennium before the <a href="/wiki/Sarsen" title="Sarsen">sarsen</a> stones of <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> were erected.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> At one time it was believed that this flowering of culture was essentially peripheral and that its origins were to be found to the south on mainland Great Britain. However, recently discovered evidence shows that Orkney was the starting place for much of the megalithic culture, including styles of architecture and pottery, that developed much later in the southern British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_dwellings_and_chambered_cairns">Early dwellings and chambered cairns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early dwellings and chambered cairns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg/220px-Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg/330px-Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg/440px-Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Knapp_of_Howar_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The interior of Knap of Howar</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Knap_of_Howar" title="Knap of Howar">Knap of Howar</a> Neolithic farmstead is probably the oldest preserved house in northern Europe. Situated on the island of <a href="/wiki/Papa_Westray" title="Papa Westray">Papa Westray</a> (which may have been combined with nearby <a href="/wiki/Westray" title="Westray">Westray</a> in the early Neolithic), the farmstead consists of two adjacent rounded rectangular thick-walled buildings with low doorways linked by a passageway. This structure was inhabited for 900 years from 3700 BC but was evidently built on the site of an even older settlement. <a href="/wiki/Unstan_ware" title="Unstan ware">Unstan ware</a> pottery pieces were found on the site, which was only discovered in the 1920s when this part of the coastline was exposed by gales and tides.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Barnhouse_Settlement" title="Barnhouse Settlement">Barnhouse Settlement</a> is a cluster of at least fifteen buildings, including one that may have been used for communal gatherings, occupied between 3200-2950 BC. The design of the houses, which were built above ground level, includes a central hearth, recessed box beds and stone dressers. There is a network of stone drains leading to a common ditch.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Pottery of the <a href="/wiki/Grooved_ware_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Grooved ware people">grooved ware</a> type, flints and stone tools have been found, as well as three flakes of <a href="/wiki/Pitchstone" title="Pitchstone">pitchstone</a> thought to have come from the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Isle of Arran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Barn_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barn-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barnhouse04.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Barnhouse04.jpg/220px-Barnhouse04.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Barnhouse04.jpg/330px-Barnhouse04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Barnhouse04.jpg/440px-Barnhouse04.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="721" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Barnhouse04.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>House 3 in the Barnhouse Settlement, with <a href="/wiki/Loch_of_Harray" title="Loch of Harray">Loch of Harray</a> beyond</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">Skara Brae</a> consists of ten clustered houses and is northern Europe\'s most complete Neolithic village. Occupied between 3100&#8211;2500 BC the houses are similar to those at Barnhouse, but they are linked by common passages and were built into a large <a href="/wiki/Midden" title="Midden">midden</a> containing ash, bones, shells, stone and organic waste. Only the roofs, which were probably supported by timber or whalebone, would have been visible from the outside. In each case the stone dressers were erected so that they dominated the view on entering the house through the low doors and there are elaborate carvings of unknown meaning on some of the stones in the houses and passages. A variety of bone beads, pins and pendants and four <a href="/wiki/Carved_Stone_Balls" class="mw-redirect" title="Carved Stone Balls">carved stone balls</a> were also discovered at the site, which was only revealed after a storm in the winter of 1850 ripped away the grass from a covering sand dune. The existing ruins mostly belong to a secondary phase of building with the foundations of the first phase largely hidden from view.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are two main types of <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a> on these islands: the Orkney/<a href="/wiki/Cromarty" title="Cromarty">Cromarty</a> type with a burial chamber approached through a low passage and usually divided into "stalls" by upright stone slabs, and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maeshowe_type&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maeshowe type (page does not exist)">Maeshowe type</a> (see below), which is a later development with a cruciform layout and an elongated passage.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The island of <a href="/wiki/Rousay" title="Rousay">Rousay</a> has a substantial number of prehistoric sites (see also below), including fifteen of such tombs, resulting it becoming known as the "Egypt of the north".<sup id="cite_ref-Rous_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rous-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Midhowe_Chambered_Cairn" title="Midhowe Chambered Cairn">Midhowe Chambered Cairn</a> on the western shore of the island is the finest example. The exterior walls of this large stone burial mound survive to well over head-height and the constituent stones are arranged in a herring bone pattern. The original interior chambers were simple in style and dived into two or three stalls, but were later enlarged to include twelve separate compartments set along a 23 metres (75&#160;ft) passageway.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> There are other substantial tombs at Blackhammer, Taversoe Tuick, and Yarso.<sup id="cite_ref-Rous_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rous-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Enlargement and elaboration of burial cairns as the Neolithic progressed is a theme found throughout Scotland, and the move from simple and private tombs to larger structures, some with entrances apparently designed for public gatherings may also be linked to the emergence of landscape-scale ceremonial complexes.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Other chambered tombs of significance include those at <a href="/wiki/Unstan_Chambered_Cairn" title="Unstan Chambered Cairn">Unstan</a> and Bookan on the <a href="/wiki/Mainland,_Orkney" title="Mainland, Orkney">Mainland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holm_of_Papa" title="Holm of Papa">Holm of Papa Westray</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-H84_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H84-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Links_of_Noltland" title="Links of Noltland">Links of Noltland</a>, a site on the north coast of the island of <a href="/wiki/Westray" title="Westray">Westray</a> has been excavated since the 1980s. In 2009 a lozenge-shaped figurine was discovered, which may have been carved 2500-3000 BC and is believed to be the earliest representation of a human face ever found in Scotland. The face has two dots for eyes, heavy brows and an oblong nose and a pattern of hatches on the body could represent clothing. Archaeologist Richard Strachan described it as a find of "astonishing rarity".<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Heart_of_Neolithic_Orkney">The Heart of Neolithic Orkney</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Heart of Neolithic Orkney">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg/220px-Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg/330px-Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg/440px-Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Skara_Brae_house_1_5.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A stone dresser at Skara Brae</div></div></div>\n<p>Skara Brae, <a href="/wiki/Maeshowe" title="Maeshowe">Maeshowe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Brodgar" title="Ring of Brodgar">Ring of Brodgar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Stones_of_Stenness" class="mw-redirect" title="Stones of Stenness">Stones of Stenness</a> together form the <a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Neolithic_Orkney" title="Heart of Neolithic Orkney">Heart of Neolithic Orkney</a> <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in the western part of the Orkney Mainland and which was inscribed in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> This small area has provided a rich archaeological heritage in a location that is relatively remote from both the main centres of population in Scotland and from more densely populated parts of Europe. However, it would be a mistake to imagine that because Orkney is so placed today that this was always so. There is a substantial amount of evidence that suggests that a variety of the smaller islands in the British Isles developed an advanced society in the Neolithic that took several centuries longer to develop on the mainland of Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> It is also clear that whilst the flow of ideas and technologies in Britain has often been from the south to the north, that at this time, it is evident that Orkney played a significant role in the development of British Neolithic culture.\n</p><p>There is also the possibility that tribal differences were part of the Neolithic cultural landscape. Unstan Ware pottery is associated with small settlements like Knap of Howar, and stalled tombs such as Midhowe. <a href="/wiki/Grooved_Ware" class="mw-redirect" title="Grooved Ware">Grooved Ware</a> pottery on the other hand tends to be associated with larger \'village\' settlements like Skara Brae and Barnhouse, and with Maes Howe style tombs.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Maeshowe">Maeshowe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Maeshowe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MaesHowe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/MaesHowe.jpg/220px-MaesHowe.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/MaesHowe.jpg/330px-MaesHowe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/MaesHowe.jpg/440px-MaesHowe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MaesHowe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Maeshowe" title="Maeshowe">Maeshowe</a> <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Dating from about 3000 BC, Maeshowe is a large chambered cairn and <a href="/wiki/Passage_grave" title="Passage grave">passage grave</a>. "<a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Norse language">Howe</a>" as an element in a name, from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> word <i>haugr</i> meaning mound or barrow, is common throughout Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The grass mound hides a complex of passages and chambers built of carefully crafted slabs of sandstone that in scale and accomplishment has few equals in prehistoric Europe. It is aligned so that the rear wall of its central chamber, a rough cube of 4.5 cubic metres (5.9&#160;cu&#160;yd) is illuminated on the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a>. It gives its name to the Maeshowe type of chambered cairns, (see above) that include other significant sites such as <a href="/wiki/Cuween_Hill_Chambered_Cairn" title="Cuween Hill Chambered Cairn">Cuween Hill</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quanterness&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Quanterness (page does not exist)">Quanterness</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wideford_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wideford Hill (page does not exist)">Wideford Hill</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/Quoyness" class="mw-redirect" title="Quoyness">Quoyness</a> on <a href="/wiki/Sanday,_Orkney" title="Sanday, Orkney">Sanday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H84_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H84-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-maes_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maes-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After it fell into disuse during the Bronze Age, Maeshowe was re-opened and used centuries later by <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> from about the 9th to the 12th centuries AD. The Norsemen left a series of <a href="/wiki/Runic_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Runic alphabet">runic</a> inscriptions on the stone walls of the chamber, some of which were left by a group of crusaders in the winter of 1153&#8211;54. Over thirty individual inscriptions remain, one of the largest such collections in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ring_of_Brodgar">Ring of Brodgar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ring of Brodgar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Brodgar" title="Ring of Brodgar">Ring of Brodgar</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Henge" title="Henge">henge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stone_circle" title="Stone circle">stone circle</a> 104 metres (341&#160;ft) in diameter, originally made of 60 stones (of which only 27 remain standing) set within a circular ditch up to 3 metres (9.8&#160;ft) deep and 10 metres (33&#160;ft) wide. Some of the remaining stones are 4.5 metres (15&#160;ft) high and it has been estimated that the ditch alone took 80,000 man-hours to construct. The ring stands on a small <a href="/wiki/Isthmus" title="Isthmus">isthmus</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Loch_of_Stenness" title="Loch of Stenness">Lochs of Stenness</a> and Harray and it is generally thought to have been erected between 2500 BC and 2000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orkney_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Orkney_1.jpg/220px-Orkney_1.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Orkney_1.jpg/330px-Orkney_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Orkney_1.jpg/440px-Orkney_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="531" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orkney_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Ring of Brodgar</div></div></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ness_of_Brodgar">Ness of Brodgar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ness of Brodgar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Excavations by <a href="/wiki/Orkney_College" title="Orkney College">Orkney College</a> at the nearby <a href="/wiki/Ness_of_Brodgar" title="Ness of Brodgar">Ness of Brodgar</a> site between the Ring and the Stones of Stenness revealed several buildings, both ritual and domestic and the works suggest there were likely to be more in the vicinity. One structure appears to be 20 metres (66&#160;ft) long by 11 metres (36&#160;ft) wide. Pottery, bones, stone tools and polished stone mace heads were discovered.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>  Perhaps the most important find was the remains of a large stone wall which may have been 100 metres (330&#160;ft) long and 4 metres (13&#160;ft) or more wide. It appears to traverse the entire peninsula the site is on and may have been a symbolic barrier between the <a href="/wiki/Ritual_landscape" title="Ritual landscape">ritual landscape</a> of the Ring and the mundane world around it.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2010 a rock coloured red, orange and yellow was unearthed. Although containers of pigments have been found previously at sites such as Skara Brae, this was the first discovery in Britain, and possibly in Northern Europe, of evidence that Neolithic peoples used paint to decorate their buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> It is thought that the primitive paint could have been made from iron ore, mixed with animal fat, milk or eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Only a week later a stone with a zigzag <a href="/wiki/Chevron_(insignia)" title="Chevron (insignia)">chevron</a> pattern painted with a red pigment was discovered nearby.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>More recent excavations have revealed a large temple complex on the site without parallel in western Europe, with more than a dozen large temples that were linked to outhouses and kitchens by carefully constructed stone pavements.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_2012_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2012-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\nThe site is now considered to be of such significance that Nick Card, director of excavations, was prompted to say in 2012: "We need to turn the map of Britain upside down when we consider the Neolithic and shrug off our south-centric attitudes...London may be the cultural hub of Britain today, but 5,000 years ago, Orkney was the centre for innovation for the British isles. Ideas spread from this place. The first grooved pottery, which is so distinctive of the era, was made here, for example, and the first henges \xe2\x80\x93 stone rings with ditches round them \xe2\x80\x93 were erected on Orkney. Then the ideas spread to the rest of the Neolithic Britain. This was the font for new thinking at the time."<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_2012_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2012-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stones_of_Stenness">Stones of Stenness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stones of Stenness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stenness_1983.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Stenness_1983.jpg/220px-Stenness_1983.jpg" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Stenness_1983.jpg/330px-Stenness_1983.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Stenness_1983.jpg/440px-Stenness_1983.jpg 2x" data-file-width="749" data-file-height="517" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stenness_1983.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Stenness Watch Stone stands outside the circle</div></div></div>\n<p>The Stones of Stenness are five remaining <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megaliths</a> of a henge, the largest of which is 6 metres (20&#160;ft) high. The site is thought to date from 3100 BC, one of the earliest dates for a henge anywhere in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-Sten_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sten-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-A68_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A68-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The Stones are part of a landscape that evidently had considerable ritual significance for the "<a href="/wiki/Grooved_ware_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Grooved ware people">Grooved ware people</a>". The Ring of Brodgar lies about 1.2 kilometres (0.75&#160;mi)  to the north-west, and Maeshowe is a similar distance to the east. Barnhouse is only 150 metres (490&#160;ft) to the north.<sup id="cite_ref-Barn_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barn-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The existing <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megaliths</a> were originally part of an elliptical shaped stone circle of 12 stones, about 32 metres (105&#160;ft) in diameter surrounded by a ditch that was 2 metres (6.6&#160;ft) wide and 7 metres (23&#160;ft) deep and with a single entrance causeway on the north side that faces towards the Barnhouse Settlement. The Watch Stone stands outside the circle to the north-west and is 5.6 metres (18&#160;ft) high. Other smaller stones include a square stone like a huge hearth setting in the centre of the circle and this along with the bones of cattle, sheep, wolves and dogs found in the ditch suggest ritual sacrifice and feasting.<sup id="cite_ref-A68_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A68-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Even in the 18th century the site was still associated with traditions and rituals, by then relating to <a href="/wiki/List_of_Norse_gods_and_goddesses" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Norse gods and goddesses">Norse gods</a>. The "<a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> Stone" was pierced with a circular hole, and was used by local couples for plighting engagements by holding hands through the gap.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> In the early 19th century a local landowner and recent immigrant to Orkney decided to remove the Stones on the grounds that local people were trespassing and disturbing his land in using the stones. He started in December 1814 by smashing the Odin Stone. This caused outrage and he was stopped after destroying one other stone and toppling another. The toppled stone was re-erected in 1906 along with some inaccurate reconstruction inside the circle.<sup id="cite_ref-Sten_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sten-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Late_Neolithic_sites">Other Late Neolithic sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other Late Neolithic sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dwarfie_Stane,_Island_of_Hoy,_Orkney.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Dwarfie_Stane%2C_Island_of_Hoy%2C_Orkney.jpg/220px-Dwarfie_Stane%2C_Island_of_Hoy%2C_Orkney.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Dwarfie_Stane%2C_Island_of_Hoy%2C_Orkney.jpg/330px-Dwarfie_Stane%2C_Island_of_Hoy%2C_Orkney.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Dwarfie_Stane%2C_Island_of_Hoy%2C_Orkney.jpg/440px-Dwarfie_Stane%2C_Island_of_Hoy%2C_Orkney.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1296" data-file-height="972" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dwarfie_Stane,_Island_of_Hoy,_Orkney.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Dwarfie Stane</div></div></div>\n<p>The Isbister Chambered Cairn, popularly known as the "<a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Eagles" title="Tomb of the Eagles">Tomb of the Eagles</a>" is located on the cliffs of <a href="/wiki/South_Ronaldsay" title="South Ronaldsay">South Ronaldsay</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Chambered_tomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Chambered tomb">chambered tomb</a> was in use for 800 years or more from 3150 BC, and has five separate stalls and three side-chambers. 16,000 human bones were found during the excavations, as well as 725 bird bones, predominantly <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_sea_eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="White-tailed sea eagle">white-tailed sea eagle</a> and over 25 kilograms (55&#160;lb) of pottery shards.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Dwarfie_Stane" title="Dwarfie Stane">Dwarfie Stane</a> tomb on the island of <a href="/wiki/Hoy" title="Hoy">Hoy</a> is made from a single huge block of red <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> with a hollowed-out central chamber. This style is quite unlike any other Neolithic Orkney site and probably dates from about 2500 BC. It was the first Orcadian ancient monument to be described in writing, appearing in the 16th century <i>Descriptio Insularum Orchadiarum</i> by Joannem Ben who provided the explanation for its existence as having been built and used by <a href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Giant (mythology)">giants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age">Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Bronze Age in Scotland lasted from approximately 2200 BC to 800 BC and northern Scotland has produced a relative dearth of remains from this period in comparison to the Neolithic and later Iron Age. This may in part be due to deteriorating weather conditions in the second millennium BC.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> In Orkney, fewer large stone structures were built during this period, burials were now being made in small cists well away from the great megalithic sites and a new <a href="/wiki/Beaker_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Beaker people">Beaker</a> culture began to dominate. Nonetheless the great ceremonial circles continued in use<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> metalworking was slowly introduced to Scotland from Europe over a lengthy period.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> There is agreement amongst historians that from about 1000 BC it is legitimate to talk of a <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> culture in Scotland,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> although the nature of the Orcadian Celtic civilisation and their relationships to their neighbours remains largely unknown.\n</p><p>In addition to various Mainland sites such as Knowes of Trotty, Kirbuster Hill and the impressive Plumcake Mound near the Ring of Brodgar there are various Bronze Age structures on other islands such as Tofts Ness on Sanday, Warness on Eday, the remains of two houses on <a href="/wiki/Holm_of_Faray" title="Holm of Faray">Holm of Faray</a>, and a burnt mound and farmstead on <a href="/wiki/Auskerry" title="Auskerry">Auskerry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age">Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Iron_Age">Early Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Early Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>\nThe Iron Age provides numerous substantial building remains. In the 1970s excavations at Quanterness, near the site of the Neolithic chambered tomb, revealed an <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_roundhouse" title="Atlantic roundhouse">Atlantic roundhouse</a>. This was built about 700 BC using stone stripped from the older building that had fallen into disuse some two millennia previously. </p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Midhowe_Broch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Midhowe_Broch.jpg/220px-Midhowe_Broch.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Midhowe_Broch.jpg/330px-Midhowe_Broch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Midhowe_Broch.jpg/440px-Midhowe_Broch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Midhowe_Broch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Midhowe Broch on the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Rousay" title="Rousay">Rousay</a></div></div></div><p>Numerous similar finds have been made at for example, Bu on the Mainland and Pierowall Quarry on Westray. These are also many impressive <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">broch</a> sites. These are substantial stone towers that developed out of the roundhouse tradition in north and west Scotland, whose <a href="/wiki/Drystone" class="mw-redirect" title="Drystone">dry-stone</a> walls may have reached 13 metres (43&#160;ft) in height. Although Orkney has no broch towers where the surviving walls are more than a few metres high, several important sites have been excavated which have numerous associated buildings forming a "broch village".<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Midhowe_Broch" title="Midhowe Broch">Midhowe Broch</a> lies close to the chambered cairn of the same name on Rousay. There appear to have been at least two separate periods of occupation and at some point buttresses were added to the exterior of the wall, suggesting the structure was in need of support. It is one of 11 broch sites on either side of the <a href="/wiki/Eynhallow" title="Eynhallow">Eynhallow</a> Sound.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> Burroughston Broch on the island of <a href="/wiki/Shapinsay" title="Shapinsay">Shapinsay</a> was built in the second half of the first millennium BC and excavated in the mid 19th century. Its earth cladding is intact, allowing visitors to peer down into the <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">broch</a> from above. The walls are up to 4 metres (13&#160;ft) thick in places and there is a complete chamber off the entrance passage. The remains of stone furniture are evident in the interior.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Burroughston_Broch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Burroughston_Broch.jpg/220px-Burroughston_Broch.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Burroughston_Broch.jpg/330px-Burroughston_Broch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Burroughston_Broch.jpg/440px-Burroughston_Broch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Burroughston_Broch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Burroughston_Broch" title="Burroughston Broch">Burroughston Broch</a>, Shapinsay</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Mine_Howe" title="Mine Howe">Mine Howe</a>, located near Tankerness in the parish of <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews,_Orkney" title="St Andrews, Orkney">St Andrews</a>, is a prehistoric <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subterranean" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:subterranean">subterranean</a> man-made chamber dug 7 metres (23&#160;ft) deep inside a large mound. Its purpose is not obvious. The walls are lined with stones fitted to form an arch over the cavity and steep steps lead to a rock floor. The entrance is at the top of the small hill and there is a surrounding ditch and evidence of sophisticated metal working around the site.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Rennibister_Earth_House" title="Rennibister Earth House">Rennibister Earth House</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Souterrain" title="Souterrain">souterrain</a>  consisting of an oval chamber with a corbelled roof supported by pillars. Although these structures are usually associated with the storage of food this site is reminiscent of the Neolithic chambered tombs and excavations revealed 18 human skeletons.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wheelhouse_(archaeology)" title="Wheelhouse (archaeology)">Wheelhouses</a> are stone buildings from the later Iron Age whose characteristic features include an outer wall within which a circle of stone piers (bearing a resemblance to the spokes of a wheel) form the basis for <a href="/wiki/Lintel" title="Lintel">lintel</a> arches supporting <a href="/wiki/Corbel" title="Corbel">corbelled</a> roofing with a hearth at the hub.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Eight presumed sites have been identified in Orkney although the style is different from those of <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>. The Orkney sites are four on Sanday, one on Calf of Eday, one at Hillock of Burroughston on Shapinsay and two on the Mainland at Burrian Broch and Broch of Gurness.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_influence_of_Rome">The influence of Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The influence of Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For a brief period Orkney emerged from prehistory and into <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">protohistory</a>. The Greek explorer <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> visited Britain sometime between 322 and 285 BC and may have circumnavigated the mainland. In his <i>On the Ocean</i> he refers to the most northerly point as <i>Orcas</i>, conceivably a reference to Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg/220px-Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg/330px-Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg/440px-Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="638" data-file-height="477" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Broch_of_Gurness2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Gurness" title="Broch of Gurness">Broch of Gurness</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Remarkably, the earliest written record of a formal connection between <a href="/wiki/Roman_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman empire">Rome</a> and Scotland is the attendance of the "King of Orkney" who was one of eleven British kings who submitted to the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> at <a href="/wiki/Camulodunum" title="Camulodunum">Colchester</a> in AD 43 following the <a href="/wiki/Roman_invasion_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman invasion of Britain">invasion of southern Britain</a> three months earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> The long distances and short period of time involved strongly suggest a prior connection between Rome and Orkney, although no evidence of this has been found and the contrast with later <a href="/wiki/Caledonia" title="Caledonia">Caledonian</a> resistance to Rome is striking.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a>, the Roman geographer, recorded in his <i>De Chorographia</i>, written c. 43 AD, that there were thirty <a href="/wiki/List_of_Orkney_islands" title="List of Orkney islands">Orkney islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-B12_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B12-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> There is certainly evidence of an Orcadian connection with Rome prior to AD 60 from pottery found at the <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Gurness" title="Broch of Gurness">Broch of Gurness</a> and 1st and 2nd century Roman coins have been found at Lingro broch.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire" title="Scotland during the Roman Empire">Roman presence in Scotland</a> was however little more than a series of relatively brief interludes of partial military occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> As Roman influence waned in Scotland from 211 onwards,<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> Orkney faded from history again and the Celtic Iron Age way of life continued, largely unchanged.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pictish_rule">Pictish rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pictish rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brough_of_Birsay.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Brough_of_Birsay.jpg/220px-Brough_of_Birsay.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Brough_of_Birsay.jpg/330px-Brough_of_Birsay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Brough_of_Birsay.jpg/440px-Brough_of_Birsay.jpg 2x" data-file-width="638" data-file-height="424" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brough_of_Birsay.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The tidal islet of Brough of Birsay</div></div></div>\n<p>In the centuries following Rome\'s excursions into Scottish territory Orkney was, at least for a time, part of the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a> kingdom. Very little is known about the Pictish Orcadians, the main archaeological relics being <a href="/wiki/Pictish_symbol_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Pictish symbol stones">symbol stones</a>. One of the best examples is located on the <a href="/wiki/Brough_of_Birsay" title="Brough of Birsay">Brough of Birsay</a>; it shows 3 warriors with spears and sword scabbards combined with traditional Pictish symbols.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-R39_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R39-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> This small tidal island has a long history of settlement that continued into the Norse period.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Adomnan" class="mw-redirect" title="Adomnan">Adomnan</a>, the biographer of St <a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">Columba</a>, states that there were Orcadians at the court of the Pictish High King, <a href="/wiki/Bridei_I_of_the_Picts" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridei I of the Picts">Bridei</a>, in AD 565.<sup id="cite_ref-R39_102-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R39-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> These Orcadians were described as "hostages" which could imply difficult relations between Orkney and the king, although they may have simply been guests at the court.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> A Pictish cemetery was found in the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Skaill_House" title="Skaill House">Skaill House</a> (adjacent to Skara Brae) in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> probably arrived in Orkney in the 6th century and organised church authority emerged in the 8th century. The <a href="/wiki/Buckquoy_spindle-whorl" title="Buckquoy spindle-whorl">Buckquoy spindle-whorl</a> found at a Pictish site on <a href="/wiki/Birsay" title="Birsay">Birsay</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Ogham" title="Ogham">Ogham</a>&#8211;inscribed artefact whose interpretation has caused controversy although it is now generally considered to be of both Irish and Christian origin. Evidence associated with the St Boniface Church on Papa Westray suggests this island had been the seat of the Christian bishopric of Orkney in Pictish times.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> The 8th century was also the time the <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> invasions of the Scottish seaboard commenced and with them came the arrival of a new culture and language for the Orkney islands. The Norse era has provided a variety of written records, the substantial <a href="/wiki/Orkneyinga_Saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkneyinga Saga">Orkneyinga Saga</a> amongst them and at this point the archipelago fully emerges into the historic era.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_history">Archaeological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prehistoric_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Archaeological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Formal excavations are first recorded at Earl\'s Knoll on <a href="/wiki/Papa_Stronsay" title="Papa Stronsay">Papa Stronsay</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_Account_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Statistical Account of Scotland">Statistical Account of Scotland</a> in 1795.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> As with the Dwarfie Stane, the mound was assumed to be a giant\'s grave at the time. Following soon after this, work on the "Picts-house" (i.e. chambered tomb) at Quanterness commenced, but little else of note was achieved until the mid 19th century. F. W. L. Thomas, whose day job was as a <a href="/wiki/Captain_(naval)" title="Captain (naval)">Captain</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> published <i>The Celtic Antiquities of Orkney</i> in 1852,<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> which listed various sites and aimed to interest "antiquarians" in the subject. His hopes were met and about a dozen chambered tombs were worked on between 1849 and 1867 by James Farrer, R.J. Hebden and <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Petrie_(archaeologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="George Petrie (archaeologist) (page does not exist)">George Petrie</a>. However, other than work at Unstan near <a href="/wiki/Stromness" title="Stromness">Stromness</a> there was then a lull for about six decades. Then, from the late 1920s, work recommenced with the assistance of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Ancient_and_Historical_Monuments_of_Scotland" title="Royal Commission on the Ancient and Historical Monuments of Scotland">Royal Commission on the Ancient and Historical Monuments of Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Works_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Works (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Works</a>. The most eminent archaeologist to work here at this time was <a href="/wiki/Vere_Gordon_Childe" class="mw-redirect" title="Vere Gordon Childe">Vere Gordon Childe</a>. He was involved in excavations at Skara Brae and Rinyo, but it was only when a shard of pottery was discovered at the latter site that it became understood that these settlements dated to the Neolithic rather than the Iron Age.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> A further 18 tombs were excavated before 1950, including five on <a href="/wiki/Eday" title="Eday">Eday</a> and one on the <a href="/wiki/Calf_of_Eday" title="Calf of Eday">Calf of Eday</a> and by the 1960s the outlines of a modern understanding of Orcadian prehistory had emerged. The advent of <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> enabled even more detailed dates to be established and refuted earlier theories that the chambered tombs of Orkney had developed from similar structures found in the Eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, such as those built by the <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization#Burial_and_mortuary_practice" title="Minoan civilization">Minoans</a>, when it became clear that the former pre-dated the latter by a considerable margin.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic_Timeline">Neolithic Timeline</span><span
