He was believed to be one of the wealthiest men of the state, owning tens of thousands of acres of land, many slaves, several distilleries, a tavern, and ferries.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Clay was born in <a href="/wiki/Powhatan_County,_Virginia" title="Powhatan County, Virginia">Powhatan County, Virginia</a> in 1757 to Charles and Martha Clay. After serving in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> during the American Revolution, he joined the westward migration to <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, where he became a <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_(surveying)" class="mw-redirect" title="Surveyor (surveying)">surveyor</a>. This gave him a chance to assess lands for cultivation and development. He was a cousin of US Congressman and statesman <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> and Alabama governor <a href="/wiki/Clement_Comer_Clay" title="Clement Comer Clay">Clement Comer Clay</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kentucky">Kentucky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kentucky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Clay developed and owned several distilleries and a tavern in central Kentucky, where development occurred near Lexington. He also started a business providing ferry service at several stops across the winding <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_River" title="Kentucky River">Kentucky River</a>.\n</p><p>In 1789 he was elected Kentucky\'s representative to the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="Virginia House of Delegates">Virginia House of Delegates</a> (when the western territory was still part of that state). Later he was elected and served in both the house and senate of the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_General_Assembly" title="Kentucky General Assembly">Kentucky General Assembly</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_family">Marriage and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1795, Green Clay at 38 married the much younger Sally Lewis (d. 1867) in Kentucky. She was the daughter of Thomas and Elizabeth (Payne) Lewis.<sup id="cite_ref-polk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They had seven children, of whom six survived to adulthood.<sup id="cite_ref-whitehall_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whitehall-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Their first child was Elizabeth Lewis Clay (1798-1887)<sup id="cite_ref-eliza_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eliza-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and other daughters were Pauline, Rodes and Sallie.<sup id="cite_ref-polk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> \nTheir sons were Sidney,<sup id="cite_ref-polk_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brutus_J._Clay" title="Brutus J. Clay">Brutus J. Clay</a> (b.1808) and <a href="/wiki/Cassius_Marcellus_Clay_(politician)" title="Cassius Marcellus Clay (politician)">Cassius Marcellus Clay</a> (b.1810).\n</p><p>At age 17, Elizabeth married <a href="/wiki/John_Speed_Smith" title="John Speed Smith">John Speed Smith</a>, who became a politician in Kentucky and U.S. Congressman.<sup id="cite_ref-eliza_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eliza-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Their son <a href="/wiki/Green_Clay_Smith" title="Green Clay Smith">Green Clay Smith</a> was named for his maternal grandfather; like his father, maternal uncles Brutus and Cassius, and cousins, he also became a politician in Kentucky and the U.S. Congress.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_of_1812">War of 1812</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: War of 1812">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, Clay was commissioned as a general in the Kentucky <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a>.  In the spring of 1813, he was ordered to the aid of General <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Harrison" title="William Henry Harrison">William Henry Harrison</a>, who was besieged by British forces at <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Fort_Meigs" title="Siege of Fort Meigs">Fort Meigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>. Clay fought his way into the fort; however, many of his men were taken prisoner by <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh" title="Tecumseh">Tecumseh</a> after they had captured a British <a href="/wiki/Artillery_battery" title="Artillery battery">artillery battery</a>.\n</p><p>When the British abandoned the siege, Clay was left in command of the fort. He was still commanding when the British returned in July 1813. In an attempt by Tecumseh to lure Clay and the garrison out of the fort, the native warriors staged a mock battle, appearing to ambush a column of American reinforcements.  Clay was not fooled, since he knew no reinforcements were coming. He was able to hold out until the British again retreated.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After the war, Clay returned to his <a href="/wiki/Plantations_in_the_American_South" title="Plantations in the American South">plantation</a>, and spent the rest of his life directing the labor of his many slaves in cultivating commodity crops of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>. He is thought to have been the wealthiest man in Kentucky of his time, as his many slaves were valuable as property. He died at his home in 1828 at the age of 73, and was buried with <a href="/wiki/Masonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Masonic">Masonic</a> rites in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Kentucky" title="Richmond, Kentucky">Richmond, Kentucky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His widow Sally Lewis Clay married again, to Jeptha Dudley, a <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> minister. She moved with him to <a href="/wiki/Frankfort,_Kentucky" title="Frankfort, Kentucky">Frankfort, Kentucky</a>, living there for nearly 40 years before her death in 1867.<sup id="cite_ref-whitehall_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whitehall-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_honors">Legacy and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Clay_County,_Kentucky" title="Clay County, Kentucky">Clay County, Kentucky</a>, was named in his honor.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-polk-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-polk_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-polk_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-polk_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=FXQUAAAAYAAJ&amp;q=Green+Clay#v=onepage&amp;q=%22Green%20Clay%22&amp;f=false">E.
