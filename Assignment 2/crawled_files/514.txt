Islands">Orkney</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prehistoric Orkney">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Orkney" title="Prehistoric Orkney">Prehistoric Orkney</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg/280px-Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg" width="280" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg/420px-Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg/560px-Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orkney_Skara_Brae.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> dwellings at <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">Skara Brae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a></div></div></div>\n<p>As with <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Scotland" title="Prehistoric Scotland">Prehistoric Scotland</a> generally, <a href="/wiki/Hunter_gatherer" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter gatherer">hunter gatherers</a> followed the slow retreat of <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciation</a>. The rapid spread of <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> culture up the western seaways soon brought early farming settlements and <a href="/wiki/Megalithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Megalithic">Megalithic</a> culture. The prevalent use of the local sandstone, found ready split into convenient building slabs on the shore, preserved numerous structures from this period, including prehistoric villages, <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">brochs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Souterrain" title="Souterrain">souterrain</a> structures, <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standing_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing stone">standing stones</a>.\n</p><p>The oldest stone house still standing in northern Europe (occupied from <a href="/wiki/3500_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="3500 BC">3500 BC</a> to <a href="/wiki/3100_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="3100 BC">3100 BC</a>) is at <a href="/wiki/Knap_of_Howar" title="Knap of Howar">Knap of Howar</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Papa_Westray" title="Papa Westray">Papa Westray</a>, with walls intact to a low eaves height, and stone furniture looking very usable. Finely made and decorated <a href="/wiki/Unstan_ware" title="Unstan ware">Unstan ware</a> pottery links the inhabitants to <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a> tombs nearby. At <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">Skara Brae</a> on the <i>Mainland</i>, passageways connect similar houses into a village, dating from about <a href="/wiki/3000_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="3000 BC">3000 BC</a> to <a href="/wiki/2500_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="2500 BC">2500 BC</a>. Pottery found here is of the <a href="/wiki/Grooved_ware_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Grooved ware people">grooved ware</a> style which was found at the <a href="/wiki/Standing_Stones_of_Stenness" title="Standing Stones of Stenness">Standing Stones of Stenness</a>, close to the exceptional <a href="/wiki/Maeshowe" title="Maeshowe">Maeshowe</a> <a href="/wiki/Passage_grave" title="Passage grave">passage grave</a> type <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn" title="Chambered cairn">chambered cairn</a> of about the same period.\n</p><p>The nearby <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Brodgar" title="Ring of Brodgar">Ring of Brodgar</a> circle of standing stones was one of the first to be analysed by Professor <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Thom" title="Alexander Thom">Alexander Thom</a> to establish the likely use of standing stones as astronomical observatories. Another <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> village has been found in the vicinity at <a href="/wiki/Barnhouse_Settlement" title="Barnhouse Settlement">Barnhouse Settlement</a>.\n</p><p>The brochs of Orkney occur on several islands. Many of these structures, such as <a href="/wiki/Burroughston_Broch" title="Burroughston Broch">Burroughston Broch</a> on <a href="/wiki/Shapinsay" title="Shapinsay">Shapinsay</a>, are isolated fortified houses. Others, such as the <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Gurness" title="Broch of Gurness">Broch of Gurness</a>, are surrounded by numerous other dwellings and ancillary structures.  In many cases the brochs also are surrounded by elaborate ditch and rampart defences.  Both Burroughston Broch<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the Broch of Gurness have interesting guard chambers within their thick <a href="/wiki/Drystone" class="mw-redirect" title="Drystone">drystone</a> walls to monitor the single entrance passages.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age">Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Iron Age brought impressive "<a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">Brochs</a>" or round towers, and "weems" or underground houses. Such implements as have survived are primitive, and include <a href="/wiki/Quern-stone" title="Quern-stone">quern-stones</a> (for grinding <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grain</a>), stone whorls and bone combs used in primitive forms of weaving, and specimens of simple pottery ware. Little is known of the culture and language of the early inhabitants. Only two languages are found in pre-Norse Orkney, <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Irish language">Old Gaelic (Old Irish)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> were aware of (and probably circumnavigated) the Orkney Islands, which they called "Orcades", thought to be a <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> name. A "king of the Orcades" was one of the 11 rulers said to have paid tribute to <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">Claudius</a> following <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">his invasion of Britain</a> in <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;43. 4th and 5th century sources include the Islands in a <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a>. Archaeological evidence suggests that the Romans only traded with the inhabitants, perhaps through intermediaries, no signs of occupation have been found.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_medieval_period">Early medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> regained power until dispossessed by the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> in the 9th century, with the possible interruption of a short period towards the beginning of the 6th century, when <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Riata" title="D\xc3\xa1l Riata">D\xc3\xa1l Riata</a> <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaels</a> may have established a footing in the islands, followed by <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic</a> <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionaries</a> in about 565. They were companions of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Columba" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Columba">Saint Columba</a> and their efforts to convert the folk to Christianity seem to have impressed the popular imagination, for the names of several islands include the epithet "Papa" in commemoration of the preachers old.<a href="/w/index.php?title=Attribution%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Attribution? (page does not exist)">Attribution?</a>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norwegian_rule">Norwegian rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earldom_of_Orkney" title="Earldom of Orkney">Earldom of Orkney</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Viking_grave_from_Orkney.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" having made the islands the headquarters of their aggressive expeditions (carried out indifferently against their own <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and the coasts and isles of Scotland), <a href="/wiki/Harald_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Harald I of Norway">Harold H\xc3\xa5rfagre</a> ("Fair Hair") subdued the rovers in 875 and annexed both Orkney and <a They remained under the rule of Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earls</a> until 1231, when the line of the <i>jarls</i> became extinct. In that year the earldom of Caithness was granted to Magnus, second son of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Angus" title="Earl of Angus">Earl of Angus</a>, whom the king of Norway apparently confirmed in the title. Recent studies from the field of population genetics reveal a significant percentage of Norse ethnic heritage\xe2\x80\x94up to one third of the Y chromosomes on the islands are from western Norwegian ancestry, as opposed to Shetland, where over half the male lineage is of Norwegian stock.\n</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Jarl_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jarl (title)">jarls</a> of href="/wiki/Henry_Sinclair,_1st_Earl_of_Orkney" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Sinclair, 1st Earl of Orkney">Henry Sinclair, 1st Earl of Orkney</a> may have sailed to <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> in 1398, returning in 1400.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_rule">Scottish rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scottish rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1468 Orkney and Shetland were pledged by <a href="/wiki/Christian_I_of_Denmark" title="Christian I of Denmark">Christian I</a>, in his capacity as king of Norway, for the payment of the <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a> of his daughter Margaret, betrothed to <a href="/wiki/James_III_of_Scotland" title="James III of Scotland">James III of Scotland</a>, and as the money was never paid, their connection with the crown of Scotland has been perpetual.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1471 James bestowed the castle and lands of <a href="/wiki/Ravenscraig_Castle" title="Ravenscraig Castle">Ravenscraig</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a> on William, earl of Orkney, in exchange for all his rights to the earldom of Orkney, which, by act of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">parliament</a>, passed on 20 February 1472, was annexed to the Scottish crown.<sup id="cite_ref-Macdougall1982_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macdougall1982-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The last full-scale battle to take place on Orkney soil\xe2\x80\x94the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Summerdale" title="Battle of Summerdale">Battle of Summerdale</a>\xe2\x80\x94was fought in 1529, between the Sinclairs of Orkney and the Sinclairs of <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1564 Lord <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stewart,_1st_Earl_of_Orkney" title="Robert Stewart, 1st Earl of Orkney">Robert Stewart</a>, natural son of <a href="/wiki/James_V_of_Scotland" title="James V of Scotland">James V of Scotland</a>, who had visited Kirkwall twenty-four years before, was made <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriff</a> of Orkney and Shetland, and received possession of the estates of the udallers; in 1581 he was created earl of Orkney by <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI</a>, the charter being ratified ten years later to his son Patrick, but after Patrick\'s execution in 1614 the earldom was again annexed to the crown.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The islands were the rendezvous of the <a href="/wiki/James_Graham,_5th_Earl_and_1st_Marquess_of_Montrose" class="mw-redirect" title="James Graham, 5th Earl and 1st Marquess of Montrose">Marquess of Montrose</a>\'s expedition in 1650 which culminated in his imprisonment and death. Cromwell was last attacked by a foreign conquering force prior to the union of Scotland and England in 1650. During <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">the Protectorate</a> they were visited by a detachment of <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>\'s troops, who attacked various locations with his English Navy and the Roundhead Army. It is claimed by sympathetic writers that he initiated the inhabitants into various industrial arts and new methods of agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Re: St Magnus Cathedral..... "During Oliver Cromwell\'s siege of the cathedral in 1651, the building was damaged and then suffered for a time when it was used by Cromwell\'s Roundheads as a barracks and stable for their horses."\n<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.orkneyjar.com/history/stmagnus/magcath.htm">http://www.orkneyjar.com/history/stmagnus/magcath.htm</a>\n</p><p>In 1707 the islands were granted to the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Morton" title="Earl of Morton">earl of Morton</a> in mortgage, redeemable by the Crown on payment of 30,000 pounds, and subject to an annual feu-duty of 500 pounds; but in 1766 his estates were sold to Sir Lawrence Dundas, ancestor of the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Zetland" title="Marquess of Zetland">Earls of Zetland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In early times, both the archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> and the archbishop of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> disputed with the Norwegians ecclesiastical jurisdiction over Orkney and the right of consecrating bishops; but ultimately the Norwegian bishops, the first of whom was <a href="/wiki/William_the_Old" title="William the Old">William the Old</a> (consecrated in 1102), continued the canonical succession. The see remained vacant from 1580 to 1606, and from 1638 till the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a>, and, after the accession of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a>, the episcopacy was finally abolished (1697), although many of the clergy refused to conform.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> of Orkney is wholly Norse, and the Norse tongue which evolved into the local <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn</a>, at last extinguished by the constant influx of settlers from Scotland, lingered until the end of the 18th century. Readers of <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Scott</a>\'s <i>Pirate</i> will remember the frank contempt which Magnus Troil expressed for the Scots, and his opinions probably accurately reflected the general Norse feeling on the subject. When the islands were given as security for the princess\'s dowry, there seems reason to believe that it was intended to redeem the pledge, because it was then stipulated that the Norse system of government and the law of <a href="/wiki/Olav_II_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Olav II of Norway">Saint Olaf</a> should continue to be observed in Orkney and Shetland. Thus the <a href="/wiki/Udal" class="mw-redirect" title="Udal">udal</a> succession and mode of land tenure (that is, absolute <a href="/wiki/Freehold_(real_property)" class="mw-redirect" title="Freehold (real property)">freehold</a> as distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> tenure) lingered to some extent, and the remaining udallers held their lands and passed them on without written title.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_century">Twentieth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Twentieth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The islands cluster round the huge deep-water anchorage of <a href="/wiki/Scapa_Flow" title="Scapa Flow">Scapa Flow</a> like a protecting hand, and in both <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> had a major base there, enabling them to challenge any attempt by German warships to emerge into the ocean through the Norwegian Sea. After the Armistice in 1918, the German High Seas Fleet was transferred in its entirety to Scapa Flow while a decision was to be made on its future; however, the German sailors opened their sea-cocks and <a href="/wiki/Scuttling" title="Scuttling">scuttled</a> all the ships. Most ships were salvaged, but the remaining wrecks are now a favoured haunt of recreational divers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One month into World War II, the Royal Navy battleship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_Oak_(08)" title="HMS Royal Oak (08)">HMS Royal Oak</a> was sunk by a German <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> in Scapa Flow.  As a result, barriers were built to close most of the access channels; these had the additional advantage of creating causeways whereby travellers can go from island to island by road instead of being obliged to rely on boats.  In the course of the Second World War Italian prisoners of war were kept on Orkney Mainland; they improvised a chapel with elaborate architecture out of corrugated iron and other base materials, which is now a tourist attraction.  The Scapa Flow base was closed in 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_years">Recent years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recent years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the 1960s and 1970s there were reports<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> about the potential for <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> mining between <a href="/wiki/Stromness" title="Stromness">Stromness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yesnaby" title="Yesnaby">Yesnaby</a>. <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>\'s plans to open such a mine were halted in 1980 after local campaigning, which included production of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Yellow_Cake_Revue" title="The Yellow Cake Revue">The Yellow Cake Revue</a></i> by composer and conductor <a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Peter Maxwell Davies</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> who lived on the neighbouring island of <a href="/wiki/Hoy" title="Hoy">Hoy</a>. The title refers to <a href="/wiki/Yellowcake" title="Yellowcake">yellowcake</a>, the powder produced in an early stage of the processing of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> ore.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum</a> prompted some Orcadians to look for ways to recast the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_status_of_Orkney" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitutional status of Orkney">Constitutional status of Orkney</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legend">Legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the Arthurian legend, Orkney is the home to King Lot, Sir <a href="/wiki/Gareth" title="Gareth">Gareth</a>, Sir Gaheris, Sir Gawaine, and Sir Agravain.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Orkney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.megalithic.co.uk/article.php?sid=7891">C.
