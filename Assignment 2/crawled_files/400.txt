Profesional de Puerto Rico</i> and Puerto Rico Baseball League, is the main <a href="/wiki/Professional_baseball" title="Professional baseball">professional baseball</a> <a href="/wiki/Sports_league" title="Sports league">league</a> in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. In 2007, the LBPRC suspended operations for the first time since its creation. In 2008, the organization resumed operations, after restructuring. These included renaming the league to Puerto Rico Baseball League and establishing a website.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In May 2012, the league debuted its current name, choosing to honor <a href="/wiki/List_of_halls_and_walks_of_fame" title="List of halls and walks of fame">hall of famer</a> <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Clemente" title="Roberto Clemente">Roberto Clemente</a> by naming it after him and adopting his philosophy of athletic development.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Currently consisting of four teams, the league\'s champion participates in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Series" id="Inception_and_B\xc3\xa9isbol_Rom\xc3\xa1ntico_(1938\xe2\x80\x931970)">Inception and <i>B\xc3\xa9isbol Rom\xc3\xa1ntico </i> (1938\xe2\x80\x931970)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liga_de_B%C3%A9isbol_Profesional_Roberto_Clemente&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Inception and B\xc3\xa9isbol Rom\xc3\xa1ntico (1938\xe2\x80\x931970)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Baseball" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican Baseball">Puerto Rican Baseball</a> began at the end of the 19th century. The first two clubs were founded in 1897: the <i>Almendares</i> Baseball Club owned by Francisco Alamo Armas, and the <i>Borinquen</i> Baseball Club owned by Santos Filippi.<sup id="cite_ref-BPR_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BPR-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/El_Pais" class="mw-redirect" title="El Pais">El Pais</a></i> newspaper, the first game was played on January 11, 1898 at the old <a href="/wiki/Velodrome" title="Velodrome">velodrome</a> in <a href="/wiki/Santurce,_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="Santurce, San Juan, Puerto Rico">Santurce</a>, San Juan. The <i>Borinquen</i> beat the <i>Almendares</i> 3 to 0. The first game to complete nine innings was played on January 30, 1898, when the <i>Borinquen</i> beat the <i>Almendares</i> again, 9 to 3.<sup id="cite_ref-BPR_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BPR-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Enrique Huyke, a professor from Mayag\xc3\xbcez, originally suggested the establishment of an organized baseball league in Puerto Rico. The idea was adopted by Teofilo Maldonado, president of the Commission on Recreation and Sports. A third party, Gabriel Castro, helped Huyke with the scouting of organizations which had to pay a thousand dollars prior to being admitted, while independent entities like the Guayama Rotary Club collaborated in raising funds for the teams.<sup id="cite_ref-p.9_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Liga de B\xc3\xa9isbol Semiprofesional de Puerto Rico</i> (LBSPR) was founded in 1938, as a semi-professional league.<sup id="cite_ref-p.7_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.7-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Six team formed the new organization, these were the Senadores de San Juan, <a href="/wiki/Leones_de_Ponce_(baseball)" title="Leones de Ponce (baseball)">Leones de Ponce</a>, Criollos de Caguas, Brujos de Guayama and Grises Orientales de Humacao.<sup id="cite_ref-p.8_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.8-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The first Puerto Rican players to play in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Bithorn" title="Hiram Bithorn">Hiram Bithorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luis_Olmo" title="Luis Olmo">Luis Olmo</a>, were active in the rosters of San Juan and Caguas. Bithorn became the younger manager in the league\'s history, receiving the office when he was 22 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-p.83_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.83-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Huyke served as manager for Mayag\xc3\xbcez for less than a month, because the team\'s owner threatened to remove its entry if he did not take the office.<sup id="cite_ref-p.9_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Maldonado was named the league\'s commissioner.\n</p><p>Changes in the rosters were allowed only during the first half of the season, meeting a deadline before the beginning of the second half. While the number of games has historically varied from 40 to 80 games per season.<sup id="cite_ref-p.10_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.10-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The All-Star game was scheduled to coincide with the traditional <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Three Kings Day</a>, an idea suggested by Angel Colon so that the proceeds could be used to buy toys.<sup id="cite_ref-p.10_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.10-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This date was used for decades, until the emergence of television forced the league to comply with the dates requested by the networks.<sup id="cite_ref-p.222_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.222-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> One of the main figures of the league in its early stage was veteran Negro leagues\' veteran, <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Navarro" class="mw-redirect" title="Emilio Navarro">Emilio Navarro</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-p.78_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.78-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Cangrejeros de Santurce and Tiburones de Aguadilla joined the original teams in the 1939\xe2\x80\x9340 season. On January 8, 1939, Humacao and San Juan played an 18-inning game that lasted four hours, establishing a record.<sup id="cite_ref-p.220_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.220-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Between 1938 and 1941, the LBSPR was part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Congress" title="National Baseball Congress">National Semi-Professional Baseball Congress</a>, with titleholder Guayama facing the champions of the Congress\' United States branch in best-of-seven series in 1939, where they defeated the <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Cementers" class="mw-redirect" title="Duncan Cementers">Duncan Cementers</a>, and again in 1940, with a roster that included the league\'s Most Valuable Player, Satchel Paige.<sup id="cite_ref-p.9_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1939\xe2\x80\x9340, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Cepeda" title="Pedro Cepeda">Perucho Cepeda</a>, the defending batting champion, won the league\'s batting title over <a href="/wiki/Josh_Gibson" title="Josh Gibson">Josh Gibson</a>, who managed to win it the following season.<sup id="cite_ref-p.74_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.74-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Criollos de Caguas won their first championship in 1940\xe2\x80\x9341, defeating the Cangrejeros in the final series. Santurce\'s Luis Cabrera was the last player to win the LBSPR\' Most Valuable Player award.<sup id="cite_ref-p.81_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.81-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> On September 14, 1941, Rafael Delgado M\xc3\xa1rquez proposed that the league was renamed to <i>Liga de B\xc3\xa9isbol Profesional de Puerto Rico</i> (LBPPR), effectively moving the league towards professionalism.<sup id="cite_ref-p.9_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A format of <i>vueltas</i> (halves) was adopted<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> since the league\'s creation, where the winners of each vuelta facing each other for the championship. However, Ponce managed to win three consecutive championships (1941\xe2\x80\x931945) without having to participate in a final by winning both <i>vueltas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-p.9_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, the fans of teams that were mathematically eliminated by this format lost interest in the tournament. Some players also concluded their participation in the season after the teams were eliminated. To deal with this issue, the league established a new playoff format, where the top four teams where included.<sup id="cite_ref-p.9_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Francisco_Coimbre" class="mw-redirect" title="Francisco Coimbre">Francisco Coimbre</a> was a key member of Ponce\'s success with his offensive, winning two batting championships, establishing a league record for most consecutive games with hits and not recording a single strikeout in three consecutive seasons (1939\xe2\x80\x9342).<sup id="cite_ref-p.10_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.10-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The consistency of his performances earned him a Most Valuable Player award. Other players that participated for Ponce included Juan Guilbe and Griffin Tirado.<sup id="cite_ref-p.80_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.80-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> World War II affected the league directly, reducing the 1942\xe2\x80\x9343 season\'s length with only four active teams. This number of teams continued until 1946, while the rule that allowed the participation of three imported players per team, was suspended from 1942 to 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-p.9_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the 1944\xe2\x80\x9345 season, Canena Marquez debuted in the league establishing a record of highest batting average for a rookie
