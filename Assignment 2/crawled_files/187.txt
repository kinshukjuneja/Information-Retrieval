In some events, wild cards are chosen freely by the organizers. Other events have fixed rules. Some North American professional sports leagues compare the records of teams which did not qualify directly by winning a <a href="/wiki/Division_(sport)" title="Division (sport)">division</a> or <a sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: International sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In international sports, the term is perhaps best known in reference to big international sporting events such as the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Championships,_Wimbledon" title="The Championships, Wimbledon">Wimbledon</a>. Countries which fail to produce athletes who meet qualification standards are granted "wild cards", which allow them to enter competitors whose proven abilities are below the standard otherwise required. In some instances, wild cards are given to the host nation in order to boost its chances.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In Olympic and World Championship competitions in track and field and swimming, however, nations are automatically allowed to enter two competitors, so these instances are technically not wild cards. In some other Olympic sports, such as judo, archery, and badminton, wild cards are in use, and they are granted by the respective sport federations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On rare occasions, a competitor who had gained entry by wild card succeeds in winning a medal or the championship.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Kye_Sun-Hui" class="mw-redirect" title="Kye Sun-Hui">Kye Sun-Hui</a> won gold in <a href="/wiki/Judo_at_the_1996_Summer_Olympics" title="Judo at the 1996 Summer Olympics">judo at the 1996 Summer Olympics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ding_Junhui" title="Ding Junhui">Ding Junhui</a> won the <a href="/wiki/2005_China_Open_(snooker)" title="2005 China Open (snooker)">2005 China Open</a> snooker championship, <a href="/wiki/Goran_Ivani%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Goran Ivani\xc5\xa1evi\xc4\x87">Goran Ivani\xc5\xa1evi\xc4\x87</a> won the <a href="/wiki/2001_Wimbledon_Championships_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_Singles" title="2001 Wimbledon Championships \xe2\x80\x93 Men&#39;s Singles">2001 Wimbledon Championships</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Clijsters" title="Kim Clijsters">Kim Clijsters</a> won the <a href="/wiki/2009_US_Open_%E2%80%93_Women%27s_Singles" title="2009 US Open \xe2\x80\x93 Women&#39;s Singles">2009 US Open</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lin_Dan" title="Lin Dan">Lin Dan</a> won the <a href="/wiki/2013_BWF_World_Championships" title="2013 BWF World Championships">2013 BWF World Championships</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In North American professional sports leagues, "wild card" refers to a team that qualifies for the championship playoffs without winning their specific conference or division outright. The number of wild card teams varies. In most cases, the rules of the league call for the wild card team to survive an extra round or to play the majority of their postseason games away from home.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The term "wild card" does not apply to postseason formats where a set number of teams per division qualify. Former examples include: the <a href="/wiki/American_Football_League" title="American Football League">American Football League</a>\'s 1969 playoffs (qualifying the top two finishers from each division), the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a>\'s 1967-through-1970 playoffs (top four finishers from each division) and 1971\xe2\x80\x931972 playoffs (top two finishers in each division), and the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a>\'s 1968\xe2\x80\x931974 and 1982\xe2\x80\x931993 playoffs (top four finishers from each division) are not true wild-card formats. When a wild-card playoff format is used, the number of teams in a division that qualify is not fixed; the divisional champion automatically qualifies, but non-division winners qualify, based either on league record or conference record.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_League_Baseball">Major League Baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major League Baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><i>See also: <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_wild_card" title="Major League Baseball wild card">Major League Baseball wild card</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_AL_Wildcard_winners" class="mw-redirect" title="List of AL Wildcard winners">List of AL Wildcard winners</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_NL_Wildcard_winners" class="mw-redirect" title="List of NL Wildcard winners">List of NL Wildcard winners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_division_winners" title="Major League Baseball division winners">Major League Baseball division winners</a> (and wild-card winners) and <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_wild-card_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Major League Baseball wild-card game">Major League Baseball wild-card game</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB), wild-card <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_postseason" title="Major League Baseball postseason">playoff</a> spots are given to the two teams in each league (four teams overall) with the best records among the non-division winners. The initial wild-card format was implemented after MLB expanded to 28 teams and realigned its two leagues to each have three divisions. Since a three-team playoff would require one team to receive a bye, the wild card was created to allow a fourth team. The wild card has been in effect since <a href="/wiki/1995_in_baseball" title="1995 in baseball">1995</a>, although it was supposed to be used in <a href="/wiki/1994_in_baseball" title="1994 in baseball">1994</a>; the postseason was canceled due to the <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1994\xe2\x80\x9395 Major League Baseball strike">players\' strike</a>. In <a href="/wiki/2012_in_baseball" title="2012 in baseball">2012</a>, a second wild card was added to each league. The two wild card teams in each league face each other in a <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_wild-card_playoff" class="mw-redirect" title="Major League Baseball wild-card playoff">one-game playoff</a>, with the winner facing the number 1 seed in the <a href="/wiki/Division_Series" title="Division Series">Division Series</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The advantages of the wild card format are that it allows a second- (or third-) place team a chance to win the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>, even if there is a dominant division winner. As the wild cards are not awarded by division, the additional teams are part of league-wide races for the fourth and fifth spots. Critics of the wild card, such as broadcaster <a href="/wiki/Bob_Costas" title="Bob Costas">Bob Costas</a> in his book <i>Fair Ball: A Fan\'s Case for Baseball</i>, have argued that, of the four major North American sports, baseball, having the most regular-season games (now 162), places the largest importance on the regular season, and the wild cards diminish the importance of the regular season by permitting a "second banana" team to make the playoffs, and that while it creates a league-wide race, it is for second place (and maybe third place) in a division, and takes away what would otherwise be a pennant race between first- and second-place teams, and can lead to teams playing for the wild card rather than to win the division. The second wild card was added in 2012 to address the issue of teams being content to rest players and win the wild card instead of trying to win the division. Also, because of the "sudden-death" round, these teams often use their best <a href="/wiki/Starting_pitcher" title="Starting pitcher">starting pitcher</a>, leaving them unavailable for much of the Division Series.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A wild-card team must surrender <a href="/wiki/Home-field_advantage" class="mw-redirect" title="Home-field advantage">home-field advantage</a> the first two rounds of the playoffs. For the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>, however, home-field advantage is determined without reference to wild-card status. Prior to <a href="/wiki/2003_in_baseball" title="2003 in baseball">2003</a>, it was decided by alternating each year between the American and National Leagues. From <a href="/wiki/2003_World_Series" title="2003 World Series">2003</a> to <a href="/wiki/2016_World_Series" title="2016 World Series">2016</a>, it was granted to the winner of the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a>. Since <a href="/wiki/2017_World_Series" title="2017 World Series">2017</a>, it has been granted to the team with the better record. In the <a href="/wiki/2002_World_Series" title="2002 World Series">2002 World Series</a>, both the <a href="/wiki/Anaheim_Angels" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaheim Angels">Anaheim Angels</a> and the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> were wild-card teams, as were the Giants and the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2014_World_Series" title="2014 World Series">2014 World Series</a>. The World Series champions in <a href="/wiki/1997_World_Series" title="1997 World Series">1997</a>, <a href="/wiki/2003_World_Series" title="2003 World Series">2003</a>, <a href="/wiki/2004_World_Series" title="2004 World Series">2004</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2011_World_Series" title="2011 World Series">2011</a> were also wild-card teams.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. href="/wiki/2014_World_Series" title="2014 World Series">2014</a> <a href="/wiki/2014_Kansas_City_Royals_season" title="2014 Kansas City Royals season">Kansas City Royals</a></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Football_League">National Football League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National Football League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NFL_playoffs" title="NFL playoffs">NFL playoffs</a></div>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL), each of the two conferences sends two wild-card teams along with four division champions to its postseason. The first round of the playoffs is called the "Wild Card Round". In this round, each conference\'s two best (by regular-season record) division champions are exempted from play and granted automatic berths in the "Divisional Round". The four division champions are seeded from #1 through #4, while the two wild card teams are seeded #5 and #6; within these separations, seeding is by regular-season record. In the "Wild Card Round", the #6 team (a wild card team) plays against the #3 team (a division champion), and the #5 team (a wild card team) plays against the #4 team (a division champion). The division champions have automatic home-field advantage in these games. In the "Divisional Round", the worst seeded remaining team plays the #1 seeded team, while the best seeded remaining team that played in the wildcard round plays the #2 seed. Both the #1 seed and #2 seed have home-field advantage in the divisional round.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The NFL was the first league ever to use the wild-card format. The decision to implement a Wild Card coincided with the completion of the <a href="/wiki/AFL-NFL_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="AFL-NFL merger">AFL-NFL merger</a> in 1970. Prior to the merger, the right to compete in the postseason for the NFL title was restricted to division/conference champions. Until 1967, a tiebreaker game was played to resolve a deadlock for first place in either of the two conferences. When the league expanded to 16 teams, it realigned into four divisions and expanded the playoffs to two rounds. Tiebreaker games were eliminated in favor of the use of performance-based criteria to determine division champions. The rival <a href="/wiki/American_Football_League" title="American Football League">American Football League</a>, which reached a final size of ten teams in two divisions, also restricted its postseason to division winners until the 1969 season (the AFL\'s last as a separate league) when it expanded the playoffs to include division runners-up. The runners-up played the winners of the opposite divisions for the right to contest the AFL Championship Game.\n</p><p>Following AFL upsets in the last two Super Bowls prior to the merger, the merged league realigned into two conferences of thirteen teams each, with three "old-line" NFL teams joining the AFL teams in the newly-formed <a href="/wiki/American_Football_Conference" title="American Football Conference">American Football Conference</a>. The decision to make the conferences equal in size meant they could not feasibly align into anything except three divisions of four and five teams each. This led to a debate as to how the postseason of the merged league should be structured. Both the NFL and AFL playoff formats of 1969 had attracted fierce critics. The NFL format was criticized for its ability to cause a team tied for first overall in the league to miss the playoffs (this happened once, in 1967, when the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Colts" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Colts">Baltimore Colts</a> missed the postseason despite a .917 winning percentage after losing a tiebreaker to the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Rams" title="Los Angeles Rams">Los Angeles Rams</a>). The AFL\'s 1969 playoffs were criticized by NFL purists for breaking with longstanding tradition, also, due to the fact that they allowed runners-up to qualify no matter how much disparity existed between the divisions, the AFL playoff structure could allow a mediocre team to qualify - this did occur when the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Oilers" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston Oilers">Houston Oilers</a>, a .500 team, finished second in the Eastern Division - the Oilers were throttled in the playoffs 56-7 by the Western champion <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Raiders" title="Oakland Raiders">Oakland Raiders</a>. The Raiders went on to lose the AFL title game to the Western runner-up (and eventual <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_IV" title="Super Bowl IV">Super Bowl IV</a> champion) <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Chiefs" title="Kansas City Chiefs">Kansas City Chiefs</a>.\n</p><p>Despite Kansas City\'s win, some purists argued for the tradition of having only division champions contest the playoffs to continue. Had they prevailed, the post-merger NFL playoffs would have consisted of six teams and might have closely resembled the playoffs of the modern <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League" title="Canadian Football League">Canadian Football League</a>, with the regular season champion of each conference earning the right to host the championship game against the winner of a game between the champions of the other two divisions. However, the old-line NFL owners, who still expected their teams to dominate the merged league for at least the first half of the 1970\'s, thought a repeat of the 1967 Colts-Rams fiasco would be very likely under the new alignment combined with a six team format. In any event, most owners in both conferences wanted to keep the even four-team playoff field in each conference. This was established by having the three division champions in each conference joined by the best second-place finisher in the conference.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>As with much of the NFL\'s nomenclature, the "wild card" was not initially referred to as such and was instead referred as the "Best Second-Place Team" (or sometimes simply as the "Fourth Qualifier").  The media, however, began referring to the qualifying teams as "wild cards". Eventually, the NFL officially adopted the term. During the 1975, 1976, and 1977 seasons, the divisional playoffs featured the #1 seed hosting the wild card team and the #2 seed hosting the #3 seed unless the #1 seed and wild card team were divisional rivals. In that case, the #1 seed hosted the #3 seed and the #2 seed hosted the wild card team. This was also the format used in Major League Baseball from 1995 through 2011.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 1978, the playoffs were expanded to 10 teams; however, the restriction against teams in the same division playing each other in the divisional round continued until the playoffs expanded to 12 teams in 1990. During this time, the #1 seed hosted the winner of the #4 vs #5 wild card game, while the #2 seed played the #3 seed. If the #1 seed and the winner of the #4 vs #5 wild card game were in the same division, then the #1 seed played the #3 seed, while the #2 seed played the #4 vs #5 winner. When Major League Baseball expanded its playoffs to 10 teams in 2012, it also used this format, although teams in the same division could play each other in the Division Series. From 1970 through 1974, the NFL used a rotation to determine which teams would host conference semifinal and final games, and which teams would play which other teams (coincidentally, baseball also used a rotation when it began to have this number of teams, for both of the aforementioned purposes, from 1995\xe2\x80\x931997 before switching to the seeding method).\n</p><p>The number of wild-card qualifiers was expanded to two per conference in 1978 \xe2\x80\x94  the divisional winners were granted a <a href="/wiki/Bye_week" class="mw-redirect" title="Bye week">bye week</a> while the wild card teams played (hence the origin of the phrase "Wild Card Round"). Like wild card teams before, the wild card game winner played the #1 seed, or the #2 seed if they and the #1 seed were divisional rivals.  The playoffs were expanded again to three wild cards per conference in 1990 (or 12 teams total, which remains now) with the lowest ranked divisional winner losing its bye.  Following the addition of the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Texans" title="Houston Texans">Houston Texans</a> in 2002, the league added a fourth division to each conference.  The league decided not to change the number of playoff teams, and thus the number of wild card qualifiers was reduced to two per conference, with the two lowest ranked divisional winners not receiving byes.  The term "Wild Card Round" continues to be used for the opening weekend of NFL playoffs, even though that weekend has involved both wild card and non-wild card teams since 1990.\n</p><p>As of the start of the <a href="/wiki/2018_NFL_season" title="2018 NFL season">2018 season</a>, there has never been a meeting of two wild card teams in either conference\'s championship game or in the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a>. By extension, this means that two wild card teams have not met in the playoffs (and, by further extension, that a wild card team has not hosted a playoff game) since the league expanded to 32 teams in 2002.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wild_Card_Super_Bowl_champions">Wild Card Super Bowl champions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Thus the Chiefs, who finished second in the West, defeated the East Division champion <a href="/wiki/1969_New_York_Jets_season" title="1969 New York Jets season">New York Jets</a> in the AFL semifinals and then defeated the West Division champion <a href="/wiki/1969_Oakland_Raiders_season" title="1969 Oakland Raiders season">Oakland Raiders</a> to advance to <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_IV" title="Super Bowl IV">Super Bowl IV</a>, where they beat the <a href="/wiki/1969_Minnesota_Vikings_season" title="1969 Minnesota Vikings season">Minnesota Vikings</a>. Because the term "wild card" was not instituted until the following year, the Chiefs are not included in the above list, but are recognized as the first team to win the Super Bowl without winning a division title.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Basketball_Association">National Basketball Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: National Basketball Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NBA_playoffs" title="NBA playoffs">NBA playoffs</a></div>\n<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a> (NBA) include wild-card teams in their playoff structures, the term "wild card" is seldom used; instead, each playoff team is most commonly denoted by its seeding position within the conference.\n</p><p>In the NBA, division champions within each conference were given the #1-3 seeds based on their regular-season records. The two fourth-placed wild-card teams are awarded the #7 and #8 seeds, respectively, also based on their regular-season records. In the NBA playoffs, home court advantage is determined strictly by regular-season record, without regard to seeding.\n</p><p>Before the <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_NBA_season" title="2006\xe2\x80\x9307 NBA season">2006\xe2\x80\x9307 NBA season</a>, the NBA seeded its teams in the same manner as the NHL. Until 2015, the NBA seeds the three division winners and the wild-card team with the best record by regular-season record. This means that the wild-card with the best record got a seed as high as #2 (if that team is in the same division as the team with the best record in the conference); however, the next four wild-card teams will still be limited to the #5 through #8 seeds. This change was made to ensure that the two best teams in each conference could not meet until the conference final, and also (allegedly) to try and eliminate incentives for a playoff-bound team to <a href="/wiki/Match_fixing" title="Match fixing">deliberately lose</a> games at the end of the regular season in order to "choose" a higher-seeded team that has won fewer games (and, due to the unique home-court rules of the NBA, possibly gain home-court advantage for that series).\n</p><p>The notion of "wild cards" was essentially abolished the <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_NBA_season" title="2015\xe2\x80\x9316 NBA season">2015\xe2\x80\x9316 NBA season</a>, as changes made prior to the season mean the top eight teams in each conference qualify regardless of divisional rank, with the seeded teams ranked by percentage. The only particular advantage to winning a division now is that a divisional title serves as the first tiebreaker for qualification seeding purposes. Unlike some other leagues, there is no tie-breaker advantage at all to finishing in any divisional rank lower than first - meaning (for example) that while a division winner <i>will</i> automatically win a tie-breaker over another division\'s runner-up, a division runner-up <i>will not</i> automatically win a tie-breaker over teams finishing third, fourth or fifth in other divisions. The new format means it is possible for an especially weak division to send <i>no</i> teams (not even its champion) to the NBA playoffs, although as of the start of the <a href="/wiki/2018%E2%80%9319_NBA_season" title="2018\xe2\x80\x9319 NBA season">2018\xe2\x80\x9319 season</a> this has not yet occurred.\n</p><p>In the NBA, the winner of the #1 vs. #8 series goes on to face the winner of the #5 vs. #4 series, while the winner of the #2 vs. #7 series faces the winner of the #6 vs. #3 series. Notice that the winner of the #1 vs. #8 series will usually play against a wild-card team in the Conference Semifinals; this is arranged deliberately to "reward" the #1 seeded team by giving it the most winnable matchups in the first and second rounds.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Hockey_League">National Hockey League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: National Hockey League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="Stanley Cup playoffs">Stanley Cup playoffs</a></div>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> (NHL), the first, second, and third place teams in each division qualify for the playoffs automatically, and two additional teams, regardless of divisional alignment, also qualify by having the best records among the remaining teams in the conference. These teams are referred to as the Wild Cards. The division champions play the Wild Cards, while the second and third placed teams in each division play each other; therefore the bracket is fixed, like the NBA. Home ice advantage is given to the higher seed in the divisional rounds, with the better record being used in the <a href="/wiki/NHL_Conference_Finals" title="NHL Conference Finals">Conference Finals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cup_Finals" title="Stanley Cup Finals">Stanley Cup Finals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Home-ice_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Home-ice-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The NHL\'s current format is similar in some respects to the "cross-over rule" used by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League" title="Canadian Football League">Canadian Football League</a> since 1997 in that it the format emphasizes intra-divisional ranking and brackets in the playoff structure and yet allows two teams from one division to qualify for the playoffs at the expense of the two teams finishing with worse records and in the same divisional ranks in another division. The main difference is that the CFL only allows the lowest ranked playoff qualifying team from a division to cross over into the other division\'s playoffs, whereas in the NHL it is possible for either Wild Card team to "cross over" to the other division, or even (in cases where four teams qualify from each division) for the Wild Card teams to swap divisional playoff brackets. Also, unlike the CFL the NHL does not require the second Wild Card qualifier to have an outright better record than a superior-ranked team in the other division - in the event of such a tie at the end of the NHL season standard tie-breaking procedures are used to determine playoff qualification.\n</p><p>From <a href="/wiki/1999_Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="1999 Stanley Cup playoffs">1999</a> until <a href="/wiki/2013_Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="2013 Stanley Cup playoffs">2013</a>, division champions within each conference were given the #1-3 seeds based on their regular-season records. Among the remaining teams within each conference, five additional teams with the best records are awarded seeds #4 through #8. The division champions (first-third seeds) and the team with the best record that isn\'t a division champion (fourth seed) were given home ice advantage in the opening playoff series, in which they face the eighth-seeded through fifth-seeded teams, respectively. However, the playoff format differed slightly from that of the NBA. In the NHL, the highest winning seed of the first round played the lowest winning seed of the first round in the next round of the playoffs. For example, if the #1, #4, #6, and #7 seeds win their respective first round series then the second round of the playoffs matched the #1 seed (highest) versus the #7 seed (lowest) and the #4 seed (2nd highest) versus the #6 seed (second lowest). Home ice advantage in each NHL playoff series prior to the Stanley Cup Finals was granted by superior seed, even if the "wild card" team had a better regular season record.  For the Finals, the team with the better record will receive home ice advantage.<sup id="cite_ref-Home-ice_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Home-ice-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_League_Soccer">Major League Soccer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Major League Soccer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MLS_Cup_Playoffs" title="MLS Cup Playoffs">MLS Cup Playoffs</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> (MLS), the top level of <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">soccer (football)</a> in both countries, used a wild card format starting in its <a href="/wiki/2011_MLS_Cup_Playoffs" title="2011 MLS Cup Playoffs">2011 playoffs</a>. The top three teams from each of its two conferences automatically qualified for the conference semifinals, while the four remaining teams with the highest point totals in league play, without regard to conference, earned "wild cards" into the playoffs. The wild card matches were single games, with the #7 seed hosting the #10 seed and the #8 seed hosting the #9 seed. The lowest surviving seed then played the <a href="/wiki/Supporters%27_Shield" title="Supporters&#39; Shield">Supporters\' Shield</a> winner (i.e., the team with the highest point total), while the other surviving wild card played the top seed in the other conference.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The "wild card" format was revamped for the 2012 season. Since 2015, the top six teams from each conference qualify for the playoffs, with the #1 &amp; #2 seeds in each conference automatically qualifying for the conference semifinals, and seeds #3-6 in each conference being wild cards. The lowest-seeded winner (played between the #4 &amp; #5, and #3 &amp; #6 seeds) in each conference plays the #1 seed, and the next-lowest playing the #2 seed in the conference semifinals.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_championship_curling">Canadian championship curling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canadian championship curling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Curling_Canada" title="Curling Canada">Curling Canada</a> introduced Wild Card teams starting with the <a href="/wiki/2018_Scotties_Tournament_of_Hearts" title="2018 Scotties Tournament of Hearts">2018 Scotties Tournament of Hearts</a> and <a href="/wiki/2018_Tim_Hortons_Brier" title="2018 Tim Hortons Brier">2018 Tim Hortons Brier</a>. The change was made as part of a wider set of changes which expanded the tournaments to 17 teams and eliminated the unpopular Pre-Qualifying Tournaments. From 2018, the round robin stage of the Tournament of Hearts and Brier will consist of two <a href="/wiki/Seed_(sports)" title="Seed (sports)">seeded</a> "pools" of eight teams as opposed to the old format consisting a single group of twelve teams. This allows the main tournament to include "Team Canada" (either the defending champions or, when the champions decline to or are unable to defend their title, the runners-up) and teams representing all fourteen constituent associations representing the ten provinces and three territories plus <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ontario" title="Northern Ontario">Northern Ontario</a>. The remaining two participants in the tournament are the Wild Cards, which compete in an MLB-style play-in game prior to the main tournament to determine the sixteenth team in the main tournament. Just as is the case with MLB division titles, the format is designed to give teams an incentive to win their provincial championships. The Wild Cards are the top two teams in the <a href="/wiki/CTRS_standings" title="CTRS standings">Canadian Team Ranking System (CTRS) standings</a> that did not win either the previous year\'s tournament or their respective provincial or territorial championship. The top ranked of these two teams receives the <a href="/wiki/Hammer_(curling)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hammer (curling)">hammer</a> (last rock) to start the game. \n</p><p>The CTRS standings are also used to determine the seeding of all teams in the main tournament, with one important caveat - for the purposes of seeding the round robin pools and so as to allow the main round robin schedule to be drawn up prior to the Wild Card game, the ranking of the top Wild Card team is the ranking that is used for seeding purposes regardless of who wins the game. Whereas teams in the Tournament of Hearts and Brier are traditionally referred to by their respective province or territory (other than Northern Ontario and Team Canada), the team that wins the Wild Card game is referred to as the "Wild Card" for the duration of the tournament. As is the case with Team Canada, the Wild Card retains that designation even if the team that is representing the same province or territory as the Wild Card team is eliminated prior to the Wild Card team.\n</p><p>With the introduction of pools, the round robin portion of the Tournament of Hearts and Brier now consists of two stages. The top four teams in each pool qualify for the second stage, formally known as the "Championship Pool." Unlike most tournaments which use a similar format, teams carry over their entire round robin records from the preliminary stage as opposed to only those results against teams that also qualify. This ensures that each Championship Pool team still plays eleven games that count for the purposes of determining playoff qualification.\n</p><p>The format is designed to ensure that a competitive team fills the Wild Card slot - due to the significant disparity in playing caliber between the top teams of Canada\'s fourteen member associations, it is widely expected that the Wild Card will consistently come from one of the provinces with the toughest fields in the playdowns, and that it will consistently be a championship contending team.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_Football_League">Canadian Football League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Canadian Football League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League" title="Canadian Football League">Canadian Football League</a> does not officially use the term "wild card" to denote any playoff qualifier, its <b>crossover rule</b> acts similar to a wild card in many respects.\n</p><p>Calls to change the CFL\'s playoff format came about soon after the CFL finished its evolution from two regional conferences, which were originally the <a href="/wiki/Interprovincial_Rugby_Football_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Interprovincial Rugby Football Union">Interprovincial Rugby Football Union</a> in the East and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Interprovincial_Football_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Interprovincial Football Union">Western Interprovincial Football Union</a> in the West. Although the CFL was officially founded in 1958, its constituent sections did not fully merge until 1981. At the time, it was agreed that three teams from each division would qualify, regardless of overall league standings. This quickly proved controversial, as a wide disparity in playing caliber had emerged between the East and West divisions. Since the league had also implemented a fully balanced schedule (each of the nine teams played each opponent once at home and once on the road), this disparity (made even worse by the fact the West had one more team than the East) was fully exposed in the standings - in each of the first three seasons of the new format, the fourth place team missed the playoffs with records as good as 9-7 in the West, while in the East records as bad as 3-13 were good enough for third place and a playoff berth. In 1981, even the fifth place Western team\'s record of 6-10 was good enough for outright possession of sixth place overall.\n</p><p>In 1986, the playoff format was changed. The new format the fourth place team in one division to qualify if it finished with an outright better record than the third place team in the other division. The Eastern owners agreed in exchange for expanding the schedule to 18 games, and also with a stipulation that the qualifying fourth-place team would stay in its own division for the playoffs. As a result, the change introduced the possibility of a four team bracket in one division and a two game total point series in the other (the two game total point format was nothing new in Canadian football - it was commonly used until the early 1970\'s). It also introduced the possibility of the first place teams losing their traditional byes based on results elsewhere in the league. This occurred in 1986, when the 11-7 <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Stampeders" title="Calgary Stampeders">Calgary Stampeders</a> qualified in place of the 4-14 <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Alouettes" title="Montreal Alouettes">Montreal Alouettes</a>. The Alouettes folded before the start of the following season. Although it is highly questionable whether a 1986 playoff appearance would have saved the floundering Montreal franchise, the CFL quickly re-instated the traditional playoff format for the 1987 season. It also moved the <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Blue_Bombers" title="Winnipeg Blue Bombers">Winnipeg Blue Bombers</a> (the West\'s easternmost team) to the Eastern Division. This balanced the divisions both in numbers as well as, to a considerable extent, in playing caliber, and the reduction in teams also caused the schedule to be changed to emphasize more divisional games. As a result, the three top finishers of the two divisions finishers always had the six best league records from 1987 up until the start of the league\'s <a href="/wiki/CFL_USA" class="mw-redirect" title="CFL USA">U.S. expansion experiment</a>, which started in the 1993.\n</p><p>The current rule was adopted after the league re-activated the Alouettes and reverted to an all-Canadian alignment in 1996. It allows the fourth place team of one division to "cross over" and take the place of the third place team in the other divisional bracket, provided the fourth place team has more points (i.e. an outright better record) than the third place team. Since the cross over team enters as the third place team, it never receives home field advantage in the playoffs, even if its record is better than that of one or both of the qualifying teams in the other division.\n</p><p>As of 2018, all teams to qualify under this rule have crossed over from the Western Division to the Eastern bracket, although there have been a handful of occasions where a fourth placed Eastern team was in mathematical contention for a Western cross-over berth late in the season. Cross-over teams have advanced as far as the Eastern Final, but as of 2018 have never advanced to the <a href="/wiki/Grey_Cup" title="Grey Cup">Grey Cup</a> game. There is no provision for a fifth placed team in one division to cross over in place of the other division\'s runner-up, even if it has a better record - a somewhat academic point as of 2018, as since 1981 this scenario has yet to recur although on occasion it has been a possibility up to the final week of the regular season.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_tennis">Professional tennis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Professional tennis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In professional <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a> tournaments, a <b>wild card</b> refers to a tournament entry awarded to a player at the discretion of the organizers. High ranked players can also ask for a wild card if they want to enter a non-mandatory tournament after the normal entry deadline, for example because they lost early in another tournament. This means a wild card player sometimes becomes the top seed.\n</p><p>Three of the Grand Slam tournaments swap some wild cards: the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Open" title="Australian Open">Australian Open</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Open" title="French Open">French Open</a>, and <a href="/wiki/US_Open_(tennis)" title="US Open (tennis)">US Open</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> A New York Times journalist has described this practice as "nothing more than mutual back scratching" and "an outdated symbol of elitism".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_wildcards">Notable wildcards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notable wildcards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Goran_Ivani%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Goran Ivani\xc5\xa1evi\xc4\x87">Goran Ivani\xc5\xa1evi\xc4\x87</a> won the <a href="/wiki/2001_Wimbledon_Championships_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_Singles" title="2001 Wimbledon Championships \xe2\x80\x93 Men&#39;s Singles">Wimbledon</a> Men\'s Singles Championships having been handed a wild-card entry by the organising <a href="/wiki/All_England_Lawn_Tennis_and_Croquet_Club" title="All England Lawn Tennis and Croquet Club">All England Lawn Tennis and Croquet Club</a>. This allowed track owners to advertise and guarantee to fans that the most popular drivers would participate in the race (pleasing fans in attendance, and preventing no-shows) even if the driver had an unfortunate mishap (e.g., blown engine) or crash during time trials. In 2010, FIBA selected Germany, Lithuania, Lebanon, and Russia as the wild cards, with Lithuania finishing third, and Russia making it to the quarterfinals. The Champions Cup maintains a system originally created for the Heineken Cup in which some "wild card" teams advance to the competition\'s knockout stages. Also starting in 2014\xe2\x80\x9315, the organiser of the Champions Cup, <a href="/wiki/European_Professional_Club_Rugby" title="European Professional Club Rugby">European Professional Club Rugby</a>, introduced this type of "wild card" to the second-level <a href="/wiki/European_Rugby_Challenge_Cup" title="European Rugby Challenge Cup">European Rugby Challenge Cup</a>.\n</p><p>During the last five seasons of the Heineken Cup (2009\xe2\x80\x9310 to 2013\xe2\x80\x9314), another "wild card" system allowed teams to parachute into the original <a href="/wiki/European_Challenge_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="European Challenge Cup">European Challenge Cup</a>, which has now been replaced by the current Challenge Cup. This was scrapped with the creation of the current Challenge Cup.\n</p><p>Both the Champions Cup and current Challenge Cup involve 20 clubs (compared to 24 in the Heineken Cup and 20 in the original Challenge Cup), divided into pools of four clubs with each club playing a double round-robin within its pool. In both competitions, eight clubs advance to the knockout stages. The top club in each pool advances; the three "wild card" places are filled by the second-place clubs with the best overall records. For the Champions Cup, the number of wild cards increased by one from the Heineken Cup era; both versions of the Challenge Cup had three wild cards, but the original version filled them in an entirely different manner.\n</p><p>In the final years of the Heineken Cup, starting in 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 and ending with the 2014 reorganisation of European club rugby, the three second-place teams with the next-best records after those that advanced to the Heineken Cup knockout stage parachuted into the Challenge Cup.\n</p><p>The tiebreaking procedure used to determine overall seeding, which was devised in the Heineken Cup era and carried over intact into the current era, is almost as elaborate as that of the NFL, with a total of seven steps (a coin flip is the last).\n</p><p>Prior to 2009\xe2\x80\x9310, the original Challenge Cup also had "wild card" teams entering its knockout stages. The top club in each pool advanced to the knockout stage, along with the three second-place teams with the best records, using the same tiebreaking procedure as the Heineken Cup. Starting in 2009\xe2\x80\x9310, only the winner of each pool entered the knockout stage, to be joined by the teams parachuting in from the Heineken Cup. As noted above, with the creation of the new Challenge Cup, that competition abandoned this system in favour of the system used in the Champions Cup.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Super_Rugby">Super Rugby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Super Rugby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Super_Rugby" title="Super Rugby">Super Rugby</a> competition, involving regional franchises from Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa, adopted a new playoff system with "wild cards" when it expanded to 15 teams in <a href="/wiki/2011_Super_Rugby_season" title="2011 Super Rugby season">2011</a>.\n</p><p>In its previous incarnations as Super 12 and Super 14 (each number reflecting the number of teams in the competition), it used a <a href="/wiki/Shaughnessy_playoff_system" title="Shaughnessy playoff system">Shaughnessy playoff system</a> in which the top four teams advanced to a knockout stage. The expansion to 15 teams led to major changes in the competition format.\n</p><p>Through the <a href="/wiki/2015_Super_Rugby_season" title="2015 Super Rugby season">2015 season</a>, the competition was divided into three conferences of five teams each, with every conference consisting solely of teams from one of the participating countries. At the end of the regular season, the winners of each conference received playoff berths. These teams were joined by three "wild cards", specifically the three non-winners with the most competition points without regard to conference. (Tiebreakers were employed as necessary.)\n</p><p>With the expansion of Super Rugby to 18 teams in <a href="/wiki/2016_Super_Rugby_season" title="2016 Super Rugby season">2016</a>, featuring a <a href="/wiki/Southern_Kings" title="Southern Kings">permanent sixth franchise</a> for South Africa and new teams based in <a href="/wiki/Jaguares_(Super_Rugby)" title="Jaguares (Super Rugby)">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunwolves" title="Sunwolves">Japan</a>, the competition format was changed again.\n</p><p>The competition is now divided into the Australasian Group, including all Australian and New Zealand teams, and the African Group, consisting of the South African teams plus the Argentine and Japanese franchises. In turn, the Australasian Group is divided into Australia and New Zealand Conferences, and the African Group is split into Africa 1 and Africa 2 Conferences. As in the 2011\xe2\x80\x9315 system, the conference winners will receive playoff berths. The number of wild cards will increase to four, with the three top non-winners from the Australasian Group and the top non-winner from the African Group, again based on competition points, earning those spots.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippine_Basketball_Association">Philippine Basketball Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Philippine Basketball Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Basketball_Association" title="Philippine Basketball Association">Philippine Basketball Association</a>, the playoffs are done after an elimination (in 2005-06, a classification) round where the top two teams with the best records are given semi-final byes, the next 3 are given quarterfinal byes, the next 4 are given entry to the wildcard phase, and the tenth team is eliminated.\n</p><p>The winner of the wild card playoffs, varying in format from a round-robin, a single-elimination or sudden death, usually meets the strongest quarterfinalist (the 3rd seed). The wild card winner\'s next opponent for the quarterfinals rested while the wild card phase was ongoing so the chance of advancing to the semi-finals (in which a team rested longer) is slim.\n</p><p>The <b>only wild card champion</b> are the 7th-seeded <a href="/wiki/Barangay_Ginebra_Kings" class="mw-redirect" title="Barangay Ginebra Kings">Barangay Ginebra Kings</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2004_PBA_Fiesta_Conference" title="2004 PBA Fiesta Conference">2004 PBA Fiesta Conference</a> after 7 years of championship drought they made an epic run all the way to the throne, in which the top 2 teams were given semifinal byes while the bottom eight went through a knock-out wild card tournament. Since the addition of the quarterfinal bye, no wild card has entered the Finals, although the <a href="/wiki/Barako_Bull_Energy" title="Barako Bull Energy">Air21 Express</a> won the third-place trophy at the <a href="/wiki/2005-06_PBA_Fiesta_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="2005-06 PBA Fiesta Conference">2005-06 PBA Fiesta Conference</a>.\n</p><p>The wild card set up was no longer used when the league reverted to the 3-conference format starting from the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_PBA_season" title="2010\xe2\x80\x9311 PBA season">2010\xe2\x80\x9311 PBA season</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ISU_Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating_Final">ISU Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: ISU Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For both the junior and senior <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating_Final" title="Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final">Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final</a> (which starting in the 2008\xe2\x80\x932009 <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating" title="Figure skating">figure skating</a> season will be merged into a single two-division event), the hosting federation may issue a wild card invitation to one of their own skaters should no skater from the host country qualify for the event through the <a href="/wiki/ISU_Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating" title="ISU Grand Prix of Figure Skating">Grand Prix</a> circuit.  Use of the wild card has not been common; however, it was used at the <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932008_ISU_Junior_Grand_Prix" title="2007\xe2\x80\x932008 ISU Junior Grand Prix">2007\xe2\x80\x932008 Junior Grand Prix Final</a> by the Polish federation.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="International_association_football_.28soccer.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="International_association_football_(soccer)">International association football (soccer)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: International association football (soccer)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The same rationale exists in international association football, specifically FIFA World Cup qualifying stages. In FIFA World Cup qualifying stages in Europe, for example, 2nd placed teams are all ranked by number of points. The team with the most points is guaranteed automatic qualification to the FIFA World Cup, while the team with the least points is eliminated (points from victories against the last-placed team in each country\'s respective group being deducted), while teams in between generally play a 2-game playoff to determine qualification.\n</p><p>Other qualifying regions which use a wild card format include CONMEBOL, CONCACAF, and the AFC.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="All-Ireland_Senior_Football_Championship">All-Ireland Senior Football Championship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: All-Ireland Senior Football Championship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/All-Ireland_Senior_Football_Championship" title="All-Ireland Senior Football Championship">All-Ireland Senior Football Championship</a>, the premier competition in <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_football" title="Gaelic football">Gaelic football</a>, each of the <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_Ireland" title="Counties of Ireland">thirty-two counties</a> in Ireland as well as <a href="/wiki/London_GAA" title="London GAA">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_GAA" title="New York GAA">New York</a> play in their respective Provincial Championships through a <a href="/wiki/Knockout_tournament" class="mw-redirect" title="Knockout tournament">knock-out cup competition</a> format without <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament#Seeding" title="Single-elimination tournament">seeds</a>. The winners of each of the four Provincial Championships earn one of eight places in the All-Ireland Quarter Finals.\n</p><p>The thirty teams that fail to win their respective Provincial Championships receive a second opportunity to reach the All-Ireland Series via the <a href="/wiki/All-Ireland_Senior_Football_Championship#Current_format" title="All-Ireland Senior Football Championship">All Ireland Qualifiers</a> (also known as the \'back door\', similar to a Wild Card).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_cycling">Road cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Road cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In road cycling (teams), a wild card refers to an invitation to a race which a particular team would not normally be able to enter. Usually used for top division (currently <a href="/wiki/UCI_World_Tour" title="UCI World Tour">UCI World Tour</a>) races where the organization want more teams, lower league teams will be invited. It is very common to offer a wild card for teams from the same country to help local sport and to boost national pride. For example, for the 100th "<a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a>" in <a href="/wiki/2013_Tour_de_France" title="2013 Tour de France">2013</a>, the organisers (the <a href="/wiki/Amaury_Sport_Organisation" title="Amaury Sport Organisation">Amaury Sport Organisation</a>) awarded three wild cards to French teams: <a href="/wiki/Cofidis_(cycling_team)" title="Cofidis (cycling team)">Cofidis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sojasun_(cycling_team)" title="Sojasun (cycling team)">Sojasun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Team_Europcar" class="mw-redirect" title="Team Europcar">Team Europcar</a>, all of which were <a href="/wiki/UCI_Professional_Continental" class="mw-redirect" title="UCI Professional Continental">UCI Professional Continental</a> teams at the time and therefore not automatically invited, unlike the <a href="/wiki/UCI_ProTeam" class="mw-redirect" title="UCI ProTeam">UCI ProTeams</a> which made up the vast majority of the entry list.\nTeams can apply for a wild card.\n</p><p>In January 2015, Team <a href="/wiki/MTN-Qhubeka" class="mw-redirect" title="MTN-Qhubeka">MTN-Qhubeka</a> from South Africa accepted an invitation to participate in the 2015 edition of "<a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a>". MTN-Qhubeka was the first African team to receive a wild card entry into the event that was held from 4 to 26 July 2015.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby_World_Cup">Rugby World Cup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Rugby World Cup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the current format of the Rugby World Cup, a team that finishes 3rd in their group automatically gains a berth in the next Rugby World Cup (although they do not advance to the next round).\n</p><p>Additionally, the Rugby World Cup qualifying format uses a <a href="/wiki/Repechage" title="Repechage">repechage</a>, in which teams from other regions that did not gain the automatic spot play each other for the final spots in the World Cup.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_card_(sports)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-Home-ice-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-Home-ice_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-Home-ice_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nhl.com/standings">"NHL Hockey Standings"</a>. <i>NHL.com</i><span class="reference-accessdate">.
