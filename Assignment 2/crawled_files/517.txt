the Tamar catchment</div>\n</td></tr>\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n</tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Tamar</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/e\xc9\xaa/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">e\xc9\xaa</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/\xc9\x91\xcb\x90r/: &#39;ar&#39; in &#39;far&#39;">\xc9\x91\xcb\x90r</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Dowr Tamar</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> in south west <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, that forms most of the border between <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> (to the east) and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> (to the west). The area is a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> due to its historic mining activities.\n</p><p>The Tamar\'s <a href="/wiki/Source_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Source (river)">source</a> is less than 6&#160;km (3.7&#160;mi) from the north Cornish coast, but it flows southward and its course runs across the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Peninsula" title="South West Peninsula">peninsula</a> to the south coast. The total length of the river is 61 miles (98&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> At its mouth, the Tamar flows into the <a href="/wiki/Hamoaze" title="Hamoaze">Hamoaze</a> before entering <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Sound" title="Plymouth Sound">Plymouth Sound</a>, a bay of the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>. Tributaries of the river include the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Inny,_Cornwall" title="River Inny, Cornwall">Inny</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Ottery" title="River Ottery">Ottery</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Kensey" title="River Kensey">Kensey</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Lynher" title="River Lynher">Lynher</a> (or <i>St Germans River</i>) on the Cornish side, and the <a href="/wiki/River_Deer" title="River Deer">Deer</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Tavy" title="River Tavy">Tavy</a> on the Devon side.\n</p><p>The name Tamar (or Tamare) was mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> in the second century in his <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)"><i>Geography</i></a>. The name is said to mean "Great Water."<sup id="cite_ref-Furneaux_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furneaux-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Foot_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Neale_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neale-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Tamar is one of several British rivers whose ancient name is assumed by some to be derived from a prehistoric river word apparently meaning "dark flowing" and which it shares with the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames#Etymology" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The seventh century <i><a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Cosmography" title="Ravenna Cosmography">Ravenna Cosmography</a></i> mentions a Roman settlement named Tamaris, but it is unclear which of the towns along the Tamar this refers to. <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Cornwall" title="Launceston, Cornwall">Launceston</a> and the Roman fort at <a href="/wiki/Calstock" title="Calstock">Calstock</a> have been variously suggested.\n</p>\n<div href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The river (and/or land on its banks) is designated as a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI), a European Special Area of Conservation, and an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It is also designated as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_and_West_Devon_Mining_Landscape" title="Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape">Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2013, <a href="/wiki/South_West_Water" title="South West Water">South West Water</a> was fined \xc2\xa350,000 after it admitted permitting the discharge of sewage from its Camels Head treatment plant into a tributary of the River Tamar for eight years.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tamar_Valley_AONB">Tamar Valley AONB</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tamar Valley AONB">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tamar_Valley_AONB" title="Tamar Valley AONB">Tamar Valley AONB</a></div>\n<p>Together, the Tamar, Tavy and Lynher form the Tamar Valley, a designated <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>. The Tamar Valley Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty covers around 195&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (75&#160;sq&#160;mi) around the lower Tamar (below <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Cornwall" title="Launceston, Cornwall">Launceston</a>) and its tributaries the <a href="/wiki/River_Tavy" title="River Tavy">Tavy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Lynher" title="River Lynher">Lynher</a>. It was first proposed in 1963, but was not designated until 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-ref2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The highest point in the AONB is <a href="/wiki/Kit_Hill" title="Kit Hill">Kit Hill</a>, 334 metres above sea level.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Special_Area_of_Conservation">European Special Area of Rocky reefs in low salinity estuarine conditions far inland on the Tamar are very unusual and support species such as the hydroid Cordylophora caspia. The Tamar is one of a few estuaries where zonation of rocky habitats (intertidal and subtidal) can be observed along an estuarine gradient.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Site of Special Scientific Interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tamar%E2%80%93Tavy_Estuary" title="Tamar\xe2\x80\x93Tavy Estuary">Tamar\xe2\x80\x93Tavy Estuary</a> is a Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI) covering the tidal <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a> of the River Tamar and the <a href="/wiki/River_Tavy" title="River Tavy">River Tavy</a>. Part of the Tamar estuary also forms the Tamar Estuary Nature Reserve, owned by the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Wildlife_Trust" title="Cornwall Wildlife Trust">Cornwall Wildlife Trust</a>. The site was designated in 1991 for its biodiversity and varying habitats that support a large number of <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildfowl">wildfowl</a> species, as well as the special interest of its marine biology. The site supports a nationally important wintering population of <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocet</a> and supports species such as <a href="/wiki/Black-tailed_godwit" title="Black-tailed godwit">black-tailed godwit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whimbrel" title="Whimbrel">whimbrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenshank" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenshank">greenshank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spotted_redshank" title="Spotted redshank">spotted redshank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_sandpiper" title="Green sandpiper">green sandpiper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_plover" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden plover">golden plover</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: UNESCO World Heritage Site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_and_West_Devon_Mining_Landscape" title="Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape">Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape</a></div>\n<p>Throughout human history the valley has been almost continuously exploited for its rich mineral and metal deposits including silver, tin, lead and arsenic leaving a unique archaeological landscape which forms a significant part of the Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape. Remains include wheal or engine houses, deep and open cast mines dating from the Bronze Age through to the medieval and modern era, the export docks at <a href="/wiki/Morwellham_Quay" title="Morwellham Quay">Morwellham Quay</a> were once an international centre of trade in copper, lead and arsenic. The valley, with the <a href="/wiki/Stannary" title="Stannary">stannary</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Tavistock,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Tavistock, Devon">Tavistock</a> was added to the World Heritage List during the 30th Session of the UNESCO World Heritage Committee in Vilnius, July 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a The Upper Lake was constructed in the 1970s and supplies fresh water to the <a href="/wiki/Bude" title="Bude">Bude</a> area, as well as having some recreational use.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Border">Border</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Border">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The east <a href="/wiki/Bank_(geography)" title="Bank (geography)">bank</a> of the Tamar was fixed as the border of Cornwall by <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelstan" title="\xc3\x86thelstan">King Athelstan</a> in the year 936.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Several villages north of <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Cornwall" title="Launceston, Cornwall">Launceston</a>, to the west of the Tamar, were transferred to Devon somewhen in the eleventh century; the border was changed to follow the <a href="/wiki/River_Ottery" title="River Ottery">River Ottery</a> westward, rather than the Tamar. The county boundary was restored to the Tamar in 1966, when the civil parishes of <a href="/wiki/North_Petherwin" title="North Petherwin">North Petherwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Werrington,_Cornwall" title="Werrington, Cornwall">Werrington</a> were transferred from Devon to Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Counties_(Detached_Parts)_Act_1844" title="Counties (Detached Parts) Act 1844">Counties (Detached Parts) Act 1844</a> ensured parishes were entirely within one county. The Act also transferred part of the parish of <a href="/wiki/Bridgerule" title="Bridgerule">Bridgerule</a> to Devon and part of the parish of <a href="/wiki/North_Tamerton" title="North Tamerton">North Tamerton</a> to Cornwall \xe2\x80\x94 these latter transfers created two of the present-day \'exceptions\' to the river boundary.\n</p>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" The oldest bridge still extant is at Horsebridge (1437), and the next oldest is Greystone Bridge near Lawhitton \xe2\x80\x94 this arched stone bridge was built in 1439. Gunnislake New Bridge was built in 1520 by Sir Piers Edgcumbe, the owner of Cotehele and Mount Edgcumbe. The <a href="/wiki/Gunnislake" title="Gunnislake">Gunnislake</a> bridge was a main route into south east Cornwall and the lowest bridge over the Tamar until the <a href="/wiki/Tamar_Bridge" title="Tamar Bridge">Tamar Bridge</a> at Saltash was opened in 1962.\n</p><p>The lower Tamar is spanned also by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Bridge" title="Royal Albert Bridge">Royal Albert Bridge</a> (built 1859), a rail bridge now adjacent to the newer Tamar Bridge. Both of these bridges are between <a href="/wiki/Saltash" title="Saltash">Saltash</a> (known as the \'Gateway to Cornwall\') and Plymouth. Further downstream, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Hamoaze" title="Hamoaze">Hamoaze</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/Torpoint_Ferry" title="Torpoint Ferry">Torpoint Ferry</a> \xe2\x80\x94 a <a href="/wiki/Chain_ferry" class="mw-redirect" title="Chain ferry">chain ferry</a> connecting Torpoint to Devonport \xe2\x80\x94 and at the most downstream part of the Hamoaze there is also the <a href="/wiki/Cremyll_Ferry" title="Cremyll Ferry">Cremyll Ferry</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Calstock_Viaduct" class="mw-redirect" title="Calstock Viaduct">Calstock Viaduct</a> is another notable structure on the Tamar, being 120 feet (37&#160;m) high with twelve 60-foot (18&#160;m) wide arches, of which three of the piers stand in the river, and was built between 1904 and 1907.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Table_of_crossings">Table of crossings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Table of crossings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Listed below are all crossings of the River Tamar, in order heading downstream. once an important industrial port, it featured an <a href="/wiki/Canal_inclined_plane" title="Canal inclined plane">inclined plane</a> which led up to the <a href="/wiki/Tavistock_Canal" title="Tavistock Canal">Tavistock Canal</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The total length of the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal river</a> (which, like other tidal waters in England, has public right of navigation) is 19.2 miles (30.9&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-iwa_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iwa-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Head_of_tide" title="Head of tide">normal tidal limit</a> (noted on <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> maps as \'NTL\') is a <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weir</a> just downstream of the village of <a href="/wiki/Gunnislake" title="Gunnislake">Gunnislake</a>. Waterborne traffic through the <a href="/wiki/Hamoaze" title="Hamoaze">Hamoaze</a> is controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Harbour_Master" title="Queen&#39;s Harbour Master">Queen\'s Harbour Master</a> for Plymouth,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> who is responsible for managing navigation along the entire tidal Tamar.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> has one of its three main naval bases at <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Devonport" title="HMNB Devonport">Devonport</a>, situated on the Hamoaze, upstream of which the river is now used largely by recreational craft. Excursions operate (April to October only) on the river between Plymouth and <a href="/wiki/Calstock" title="Calstock">Calstock</a>; excursions used to operate as far as <a href="/wiki/Morwellham_Quay" title="Morwellham Quay">Morwellham Quay</a>, but were suspended indefinitely in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> A passenger ferry also operates April to October between <a href="/wiki/Cotehele" title="Cotehele">Cotehele</a> Quay and Calstock.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A typical Tamar vessel was  a sailing <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a>, built on the open river bank, of up to 60 tons, with a peaked, <a href="/wiki/Gaff_rig" title="Gaff rig">gaff-rigged mainsail</a> and a fore <a href="/wiki/Staysail" title="Staysail">staysail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The Tamar was navigable by seagoing ships of up to 400 <a href="/wiki/Tonnage" title="Tonnage">register tons</a> as far inland as <a href="/wiki/Weir_Quay" title="Weir Quay">Weir Quay</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-booker62_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker62-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> near <a href="/wiki/Bere_Alston" title="Bere Alston">Bere Alston</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Tamar%E2%80%93Tavy_Estuary" title="Tamar\xe2\x80\x93Tavy Estuary">estuary</a> narrows into the tidal river, some 8.4 miles (13.5&#160;km) upstream from Plymouth Sound. Vessels of 300 tons sailed as far inland as Morwellham,<sup id="cite_ref-otter32_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-otter32-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DBB75_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DBB75-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> 17.2 miles (27.7&#160;km) along the river from the sea. A further stretch of 1.8 miles (2.9&#160;km) upstream to Weir Head, near Gunnislake, was accessible to smaller boats. Weir Head is just downstream of the <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weir</a> at Gunnislake (the tidal limit) and is the final place to turn boats;<sup id="cite_ref-iwa_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iwa-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> it was from here that smaller craft could begin their journey on the <b>Tamar Manure Canal</b>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" excursion steamer <i>Alexandra</i>, 127 <a href="/wiki/Gross_tonnage" title="Gross tonnage">gross tons</a>, 126 feet in length, reversing at the entrance to the Tamar Manure Canal, in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1794 the Tamar Manure Navigation Company was formed to extend <a href="/wiki/Navigability" title="Navigability">navigation</a> inland for a further 30 miles, to Tamerton Bridge in <a href="/wiki/North_Tamerton" title="North Tamerton">North Tamerton</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> of 1796 authorised the works, with the company empowered to improve and manage navigation on the Tamar from Morwellham Quay upstream to Boat Pool, at Blanchdown (just upstream of Gunnislake), and to construct a canal from there.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The project however advanced no further than <a href="/wiki/Gunnislake" title="Gunnislake">Gunnislake</a>, with work ceasing in 1808. Barges of up to 30 tons could then proceed as far as Gunnislake New Bridge,<sup id="cite_ref-booker_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> bypassing the weir (above Weir Head) via a 500-yard canal to the west of the river. As the only completed work of the original project, this short canal became known as the Tamar Manure (Navigation) Canal. It had one <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">lock</a> located at the Weir Head end of the canal, allowing vessels to rise from the tidal Tamar to the water level above the weir.<sup id="cite_ref-canalr_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canalr-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The navigable route along the Tamar and the Manure Canal that was managed by the company (from Morwellham Quay upstream to Blanchdown) was known as the Tamar Manure Navigation and was 3.0 miles (4.8&#160;km) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-iwa_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iwa-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The import of fertilizer (at the time of the construction of the canal all types of which were typically referred to as "manure") and coal and the export of bricks and granite along this short navigation proved profitable for many years.<sup id="cite_ref-booker_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The navigation from Launceston to Tamerton was completed in 1826 as part of a separate project, the <a href="/wiki/Bude_Canal" title="Bude Canal">Bude Canal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The Tamar Manure Navigation ceased functioning in 1929 and the company was wound up in 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-canalr_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canalr-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 997 according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>, raiding Vikings travelled up the Tamar and then the Tavy river as far as <a href="/wiki/Lydford" title="Lydford">Lydford</a>, and burned <a href="/wiki/Ordwulf" title="Ordwulf">Ordwulf</a>\'s monastery at <a href="/wiki/Tavistock,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Tavistock, Devon">Tavistock</a>.\n</p><p>The old ferry crossings were later to develop into the busy river quays of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.  In mediaeval times the transport of goods to supply the Benedictine abbey at <a href="/wiki/Tavistock_Abbey" title="Tavistock Abbey">Tavistock</a>, four miles by <a href="/wiki/Trail" title="Trail">track</a> from the river port of Morwellham, was significant.<sup id="cite_ref-booker28_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker28-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Sea sand from the coast was imported to spread on farmland, until in the eighteenth century a dressing of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">lime</a> was found to be more beneficial. Large quantities of limestone and coal were then imported to burn in the numerous <a href="/wiki/Lime_kiln" title="Lime kiln">limekilns</a> on the river quays; the lime had to be made locally as it was not <a href="/wiki/Slaking_(geology)" title="Slaking (geology)">slaked</a> before application and was too reactive for transport by water after burning.  Later, street sweepings and other refuse from <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devonport,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Devonport, Devon">Devonport</a>, together with bones for the newly discovered <a href="/wiki/Bone_meal" title="Bone meal">bone fertiliser</a>, were carried inland to manure the fields. Other regular imports were timber from <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_region" title="Baltic region">Baltic</a>, in large <a href="/wiki/Beam_(structure)" title="Beam (structure)">baulks</a> for use as <a href="/wiki/Pit_prop" title="Pit prop">supports</a> in the mines, and coal from Wales to supply the mine pumping engines.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Horsebridge_-_geograph.org.uk_-_22243.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Horsebridge, built in 1437, is still used by motor traffic.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tavistock,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Tavistock, Devon">Tavistock</a> was one of the three <a href="/wiki/Stannary" title="Stannary">stannary towns</a> of Devon and large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Black_tin" title="Black tin">refined tin ore</a> were exported through Morwellham from  twelfth century until 1838, when the requirement to pay duty on the metal at one of the specified towns was relaxed.<sup id="cite_ref-booker28_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker28-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The opening of the <a href="/wiki/Tavistock_Canal" title="Tavistock Canal">Tavistock Canal</a>, between Tavistock and Morwellham, in 1817 facilitated traffic.  Later, the <a href="/wiki/East_Cornwall_Mineral_Railway" title="East Cornwall Mineral Railway">East Cornwall Mineral Railway</a> provided an outlet through the quays of <a href="/wiki/Calstock" title="Calstock">Calstock</a> from the Cornish side of the valley.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  Other significant cargoes exported were quarried granite and, later, copper, lead and manganese ores, with their important by-product of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>. Arsenic was extracted from <a href="/wiki/Arsenopyrite" title="Arsenopyrite">mispickel</a>, once regarded as a waste product but later offering an important source of revenue as  copper and tin extraction declined in profitability.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The refined product was exported worldwide, in particular to the southern United States, where it was used as an insecticide in the cotton fields.\n</p><p>In the thirteenth century lead and silver output from the royal mines on the <a href="/wiki/Bere_Ferrers" title="Bere Ferrers">Bere peninsula</a> (between the Tamar and the <a href="/wiki/River_Tavy" title="River Tavy">Tavy</a>) was significant, and production continued intermittently until the nineteenth century. The <a href="/wiki/Percival_Norton_Johnson" title="Percival Norton Johnson">Johnson Matthey</a> smelting works at Weir Quay extracted silver and lead not only from local ore, but from ore imported by sea from Europe and as far away as <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-booker62_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker62-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fluorspar" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluorspar">Fluorspar</a> from the lead mine <a href="/wiki/Tailings" title="Tailings">tailings</a> was exported to France for use in the manufacture of glassware.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div href="/wiki/Stonehouse,_Plymouth" title="Stonehouse, Plymouth">Stonehouse</a>) at the mouth of the river offered an important market for the valley\'s agricultural produce, needed in particular to serve the victualling requirements of the <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Devonport" title="HMNB Devonport">royal dockyard</a>, and this was always carried by boat.  In 1820 or 1821 the first paddle steamer on the Tamar inaugurated a service between Calstock and Devonport to deliver foodstuffs.<sup id="cite_ref-Booker83_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Booker83-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In 1859 a rail connection from Plymouth to London was opened, and fresh produce could be landed at the Devonport steamer quays in the evening and be on sale in London by the next morning.<sup id="cite_ref-booker233_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker233-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The growing city population created a large demand for sightseeing cruises on the river and this was a significant source of traffic from 1823, with the launch of the <a href="/wiki/Post_Office_Packet_Service#Falmouth" title="Post Office Packet Service">Cornish steam packet</a> <i>Sir Francis Drake</i>, until the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">second world war</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Booker83_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Booker83-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kitteridge75_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kitteridge75-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mineral traffic on the river diminished towards the latter end of the nineteenth century, after the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Devonport_and_South_Western_Junction_Railway" title="Plymouth, Devonport and South Western Junction Railway">Plymouth, Devonport and South Western Junction Railway</a> reached Tavistock in 1859 (so making the Tavistock Canal to Morwellham redundant for transport, although it remains in use as a source of <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>) and as the copper and tin mines became exhausted.<sup id="cite_ref-booker30_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-booker30-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The decline accelerated  from 1894 when the East Cornwall  Mineral Railway, until then linked to the outside world only through the port of Calstock, was extended to the Plymouth, Devonport and South Western Junction Railway at <a href="/wiki/Bere_Alston" title="Bere Alston">Bere Alston</a>. Tourist and market traffic on the river, using purpose-built or converted steamers, remained substantial until the Devonport piers were closed and the ships requisitioned on the outbreak of war in 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-Kitteridge75_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kitteridge75-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span Bridge">Royal Albert Bridge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tamar_Bridge" title="Tamar Bridge">Tamar Bridge</a> at <a href="/wiki/Saltash" title="Saltash">Saltash</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Rocks around the edge of <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a> were mineralised by fluids driven by the heat of the earth\'s core, which gave rise to ores containing <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and other minerals in the Valley.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The medieval estate of <a href="/wiki/Cotehele" title="Cotehele">Cotehele</a>, owned by the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Mount_Edgcumbe" title="Earl of Mount Edgcumbe">Edgcumbe</a> family, was a significant producer of <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mint" title="Royal Mint">Royal Mint</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> During the industrial revolution, there was significant mining activity near the river, between Gunnislake and Weir Quay. During this period, the Tamar was an important river for shipping copper from ports such as <a href="/wiki/Morwellham_Quay" title="Morwellham Quay">Morwellham Quay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calstock" title="Calstock">Calstock</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Quay_(Devon)" title="New Quay (Devon)">New Quay (Devon)</a> to south Wales where it would be smelted. The valley forms district A10i of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_and_West_Devon_Mining_Landscape" title="Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape">Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape</a>. The river has long been famous for the quality of its <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> whilst the valley was known nationally for the high quality, and early, soft fruit and market gardens sheltered by its steeply winding slopes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Alliterative_Morte_Arthure" title="Alliterative Morte Arthure">Alliterative Morte Arthure</a> states that the mortal combat of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mordred" title="Mordred">Mordred</a> took place close to the banks of the river.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A traditional Cornish tale claims that the devil would never dare to cross the River Tamar into Cornwall for fear of ending up as a <a href="/wiki/Pasty" title="Pasty">pasty</a> filling.<sup id="cite_ref-pastie_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pastie-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Though unusual landscape features are often named after the devil (e.g. devil\'s frying pan) it used to be said that the devil never came to Cornwall: he once reached Torpoint and immediately noticed that various kinds of pie were customary; he feared that devilly pie might be the next kind so returned to Devon.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> This legend is set to music in the traditional Cornish folk song <a href="/wiki/Fish_and_Tin_and_Copper" title="Fish and Tin and Copper">Fish and Tin and Copper</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legend_of_Tamara">Legend of Tamara</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Legend of Tamara">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The legend behind the name involves a nymph by the name of Tamara, who lived in the underworld. Tamara wanted to wander freely in the mortal world, against the advice of her parents. One day, wandering in <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a>, she happened to meet two giants called Tavy and Torridge (or Tawradge). Both giants became smitten with Tamara and vied for her affections. Tamara led the giants on a dance, but never let them touch her, instead darting out of reach whenever they came too close. Tamara\'s father, who had been out looking for her, located her just as the giants finally caught up with her near <a href="/wiki/Morwenstow" title="Morwenstow">Morwenstow</a>. He flew into a rage and used a spell to put Tavy and Tawradge into a deep sleep. This infuriated Tamara, who subsequently refused to return to the underworld with her father. He became even more enraged and cast a spell on Tamara, turning her into a bubbling spring, which produced the Tamar river and flowed all the way to the sea. Tawradge awoke to find his beloved had become a river; in despair, he sought the advice of a magician, who turned him into a river (the <a href="/wiki/River_Torridge" title="River Torridge">River Torridge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Taw" title="River Taw">River Taw</a>) so that he could hope to reunite with Tamara. Tawradge was never able to find and merge with his beloved Tamara, instead turning north toward <a href="/wiki/Bideford" title="Bideford">Bideford</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel" title="Bristol Channel">Bristol Channel</a> and is still said to mourn his love, the Tamar. This legend explains why the River Torridge, which rises only 500 meters from the Tamar, veers away from the Tamar and forms a huge arc, eventually flowing to the North Devon coast. The other giant, Tavy, also awoke in despair and sought the aid of his own father, also a powerful magician. His father turned Tavy into a river as well, and the <a href="/wiki/River_Tavy" title="River Tavy">Tavy</a> set off in search of the Tamar, eventually finding her and merging with her into a wide and beautiful estuary.<sup id="cite_ref-Furneaux_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furneaux-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Foot_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Neale_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neale-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_of_England" title="List of rivers of England">List of rivers of England</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/HMS_Tamar" title="HMS Tamar">HMS Tamar</a>, a vessel named after the river</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tamar_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="Tamar Site">Tamar Site</a>, an area in Hong Kong named after the ship, which was named after the river</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Tamar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
