End</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Penn an Wlas</i> or <i>Pedn an Wlas</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">headland</a> and holiday It is the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_points_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Extreme points of the United Kingdom">most westerly</a> point of mainland Cornwall and England, situated within the <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a> peninsula about eight miles (13&#160;km) west-south-west of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> at the western end of the <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30 road</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land%27s_End&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The actual Land\xe2\x80\x99s End or Peal Point, is a modest headland compared with nearby headlands such as Pedn-men-dhu overlooking <a href="/wiki/Sennen_Cove" title="Sennen Cove">Sennen Cove</a> and Pordenack, to the south. The present hotel and tourist complex is at Carn Kez, 200 m south of the actual Land\xe2\x80\x99s End.<sup id="cite_ref-neave_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neave-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Land\'s End has a particular resonance because it is often used to suggest distance. Land\'s End to <a href="/wiki/John_o%27_Groats" title="John o&#39; Groats">John o\' Groats</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> is a distance of 838 miles (1,349&#160;km) by road and this <i><a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End_to_John_o%27_Groats" title="Land&#39;s End to John o&#39; Groats">Land\'s End to John o\' Groats</a></i> distance is often used to define charitable events such as end-to-end walks and races in the UK. Land\'s End to the <a href="/wiki/Northernmost_point_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Northernmost point of England">northernmost point of England</a> is a distance of 556 miles (895&#160;km) by road.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The cliffs are made of <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, an igneous rock, which means that the cliffs will be more resistant to weathering, and will have steeper cliff faces. There are two varieties of granite represented at Land\'s End. Adjacent to the hotel the granite is coarse-grained with large <a href="/wiki/Phenocrysts" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenocrysts">phenocrysts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Orthoclase" title="Orthoclase">orthoclase</a>, sometimes more than 5&#160;in (13&#160;cm) in length. To the north, at the First and Last House, there is a finer grained granite with fewer and smaller phenocrysts, and the different granites can be seen from a distance by the smoother weathering of the finer variety. The granite dates to 268\xe2\x80\x93275 million years ago of the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian period</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-geology_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geology-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The contact zone between the Land\'s End <a href="/wiki/Pluton" title="Pluton">granite pluton</a> and the altered \xe2\x80\xb3country rocks\xe2\x80\xb3 is nearby and the <a href="/wiki/Longships_Lighthouse" title="Longships Lighthouse">Longships Lighthouse</a>, offshore, is built on the country rock.<sup id="cite_ref-geology_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geology-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Land\'s End is a popular venue for rock climbers.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Longships" title="Longships">Longships</a>, a group of rocky <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islets</a> are just over 1 mile (1.6&#160;km) offshore, and together with the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Stones_Reef" title="Seven Stones Reef">Seven Stones Reef</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> which lie about 28 miles (45&#160;km) southwest \xe2\x80\x94 are part of the mythical lost land of <a href="/wiki/Lyonesse" title="Lyonesse">Lyonesse</a>, referred to in <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">Arthurian</a> literature.\n</p><p>The area around Land\'s End has been designated part of an <a href="/wiki/Important_Plant_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Important Plant Area">Important Plant Area</a>, by the organisation <a href="/wiki/Plantlife" title="Plantlife">Plantlife</a>, for rare species of <a href="/wiki/Flora_and_fauna_of_Cornwall" title="Flora and fauna of Cornwall">flora</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land%27s_End&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1769, the antiquarian <a href="/wiki/William_Borlase" title="William Borlase">William Borlase</a> wrote:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Of this time we are to understand what Edward I. says (Sheringham. p. 129.) that Britain, Wales, and Cornwall, were the portion of <a href="/wiki/Belinus" title="Belinus">Belinus</a>, elder son of <a href="/wiki/Dunwallo" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunwallo">Dunwallo</a>, and that that part of the Island, afterwards called England, was divided in three shares, viz. Britain, which reached from the Tweed, Westward, as far as the <a href="/wiki/River_Exe" title="River Exe">river Ex</a>; Wales inclosed by the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">Severn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Wales" title="River Dee, Wales">Dee</a>; and Cornwall from the river Ex to the Land\'s-End.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Tourists have been visiting Land\xe2\x80\x99s End for over three hundred years. In 1649, an early visitor was the poet <a href="/wiki/John_Taylor_(poet)" title="John Taylor (poet)">John Taylor</a>, who was hoping to find subscribers for his new book <i>Wanderings to see the Wonders of the West</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1878 people left Penzance by horse-drawn vehicles from outside the Queens and Union hotels and travelled via <a href="/wiki/St_Buryan" title="St Buryan">St Buryan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treen_(St_Levan)" title="Treen (St Levan)">Treen</a>, to see the <a href="/wiki/Logan_Rock" title="Logan Rock">Logan Rock</a>. There was a short stop to look at <a href="/wiki/Porthcurno" title="Porthcurno">Porthcurno</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cable_%26_Wireless_Worldwide" title="Cable &amp; Wireless Worldwide">Eastern Telegraph Company</a> followed by refreshments at the First and Last Inn in <a href="/wiki/Sennen" title="Sennen">Sennen</a>. They then headed for Land\xe2\x80\x99s End, often on foot or horse, because of the uneven and muddy lanes. Over one hundred people could be at Land\'s End at any one time.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At Carn Kez, the First and Last Inn owned a small house which looked after the horses while visitors roamed the cliffs. The house at Carn Kez developed into the present hotel.<sup id="cite_ref-neave_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neave-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The earliest part of the house was damaged by the <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> when a plane returning from a raid on <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> jettisoned its remaining bombs. 53 fisherman were injured or killed. In the build-up to <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">D-Day</a> American troops were billeted in the hotel leaving the building in a bad state.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lands_end_map1946.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lands_end_map1946.png/220px-Lands_end_map1946.png" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lands_end_map1946.png/330px-Lands_end_map1946.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lands_end_map1946.png/440px-Lands_end_map1946.png 2x" data-file-width="1533" data-file-height="1206" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lands_end_map1946.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1946 map of Land\'s End</div></div></div>\n<p>Land\'s End was owned by a Cornish family until 1982, when it was sold to David Goldstone. In 1987, <a href="/wiki/Peter_de_Savary" title="Peter de Savary">Peter de Savary</a> outbid the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a> to purchase Land\xe2\x80\x99s End for almost \xc2\xa37 million from David Goldstone.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He had two new buildings erected and much of the present <a href="/wiki/Theme_park" class="mw-redirect" title="Theme park">theme park</a> development was instigated by him<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. He sold both Land\'s End and John o\' Groats to businessman Graham Ferguson Lacey in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The current owners purchased Land\'s End in 1996 and formed a company named <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Great_Britain_PLC" title="Heritage Great Britain PLC">Heritage Great Britain PLC</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Attractions at the theme park include children\'s playgrounds and recorded music. Twice a week in August, Land\'s End hosts \'Magic in the Skies\', a night-time firework spectacular with music by British composer Christopher Bond and narrated by actress <a href="/wiki/Miriam_Margolyes" title="Miriam Margolyes">Miriam Margolyes</a>. Within the complex is the Land\'s End Hotel.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 2012, Land\'s End received worldwide publicity as the starting point of the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics" title="2012 Summer Olympics">2012 Summer Olympics</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Olympic_torch_relays" title="List of Olympic torch relays">torch relay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_to_end">End to end</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land%27s_End&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: End to end">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Land\'s End is either the start or finishing point, of end to end journeys with <a href="/wiki/John_o%27Groats" class="mw-redirect" title="John o&#39;Groats">John o\'Groats</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. One of the earliest was by Carlisle who left Land\'s End on 23 September 1879, went to John O\'Groats House and arrived back at Land\'s End on 15 December; taking 72 days (exclusive of Sundays); covering 3,899 miles (6,275&#160;km). To prove his journey, he kept a log book which was stamped at any Post Office he passed.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> An early end to end on bicycle was completed by Messrs Blackwell and Harman of <a href="/wiki/Canonbury" title="Canonbury">Canonbury</a> Bicycle Club. Starting at Land\'s End they covered 900 miles (1,400&#160;km) in thirteen days in July/August 1880.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greeb">Greeb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land%27s_End&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Greeb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On the south side of Carn Kez the land slopes away to a shallow valley containing a small stream and the former Greeb Farm. In 1879 a derrick was used for hauling seaweed from the beach 40 feet below; for use as a soil improver.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a
