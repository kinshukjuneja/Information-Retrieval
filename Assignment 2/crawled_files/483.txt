England</b>, formerly known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Government_Office" title="Government Office">government office</a> regions</b>, are the highest tier of sub-national division in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Between 1994 and 2011, nine regions had officially <a href="/wiki/Devolved" class="mw-redirect" title="Devolved">devolved</a> functions within government. While they no longer fulfill this role, they continue to be used for statistical and some administrative purposes. They define areas (constituencies) for the purposes of elections to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>. <a href="/wiki/Eurostat" title="Eurostat">Eurostat</a> also uses them to demarcate <a href="/wiki/First_level_NUTS_of_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="First level NUTS of the European Union">first level</a> <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics</a> (NUTS) regions ("NUTS 1 regions") within the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. The regions generally follow the boundaries of the former <a href="/wiki/Historical_and_alternative_regions_of_England#Standard_statistical_regions" title="Historical and alternative regions of England">standard regions</a>, established in the 1940s for statistical purposes.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/London_region" class="mw-redirect" title="London region">London region</a> has a directly elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">Assembly</a>. Six regions have <a href="/wiki/Local_authority_leaders%27_board" title="Local authority leaders&#39; board">local authority leaders\' boards</a> to assist with correlating the headline policies of local authorities. The remaining two regions no longer have any administrative functions, having abolished their regional local authority leaders\' boards.\n</p><p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Regional_chamber" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional chamber">regional chambers</a> were established in the eight regions outside London, which produced strategic plans and recommendations to local authorities. The regions also had an associated (central) <a href="/wiki/Government_Office" title="Government Office">Government Office</a> with some responsibility for coordinating policy, and, from 2007, a part-time <a href="/wiki/Regional_minister" title="Regional minister">regional minister</a> within the Government. <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_committees_of_the_United_Kingdom_Parliament#Regional" class="mw-redirect" title="List of committees of the United Kingdom Parliament">regional Select Committees</a> were established in 2009. However, the chambers and select committees were abolished in May 2010, restoring these functions to the main tier of local government,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> with limited functions transferred to the regional local authority leaders\' boards created in 2009. Regional ministers were not reappointed by the incoming <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron\xe2\x80\x93Clegg coalition">Coalition Government</a>, and the Government Offices were abolished in 2011.\n</p><p>From 2011, <a href="/wiki/Combined_authorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined authorities">combined authorities</a> have been introduced in some <a href="/wiki/City_region_(United_Kingdom)" title="City region (United Kingdom)">city regions</a>, with similar responsibilities to the former regional chambers (and in some cases, replacing a regional local authority leaders\' board on a smaller scale), but which also receive additional delegated functions from central government relating to transport and economic policy.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Regional_development_agencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional development agencies">Regional development agencies</a> were public bodies established in all nine regions in 1998 to promote economic development. They had certain delegated functions, including administering European Union regional development funds, and received funding from the central government as well. These were abolished in 2012, with statutory functions returning to local authorities and central government, however smaller scale <a href="/wiki/Local_enterprise_partnership" title="Local enterprise partnership">local enterprise partnerships</a> were voluntarily established to take on some functions relating to coordinating economic priorities and development.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Historical_and_alternative_regions_of_England" title="Historical and alternative regions of England">Historical and alternative regions of England</a></div>\n<p>After about 500 AD, England comprised seven <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> <a href="/wiki/Heptarchy" title="Heptarchy">territories</a>&#160;&#8211;&#32; <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_East_Angles" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of the East Angles">East Anglia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Essex" title="Kingdom of Essex">Essex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kent" title="Kingdom of Kent">Kent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sussex" title="Kingdom of Sussex">Sussex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>&#160;&#8211;&#32; often referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Heptarchy" title="Heptarchy">heptarchy</a>. The boundaries of some of these, which later unified as the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>, roughly coincide with those of modern regions. During <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>\'s Protectorate in the 1650s, the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_the_Major-Generals" title="Rule of the Major-Generals">rule of the Major-Generals</a> created 10 regions in England and Wales of similar size to the modern regions.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Proposals for administrative regions within England were mooted by the British government prior to the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. In 1912 the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_Act_1914" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Rule Act 1914">Third Home Rule Bill</a> was passing through parliament. The Bill was expected to introduce a devolved parliament for Ireland, and as a consequence calls were made for similar structures to be introduced in Great Britain or "Home Rule All Round". On 12 September the <a href="/wiki/First_Lord_of_the_Admiralty" title="First Lord of the Admiralty">First Lord of the Admiralty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, gave a speech in which he proposed 10 or 12 regional parliaments for the United Kingdom. Within England, he suggested that London, Lancashire, Yorkshire, and the Midlands would make <a href="/wiki/Natural_region" title="Natural region">natural regions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> While the creation of regional parliaments never became official policy, it was for a while widely anticipated and various schemes for dividing England devised.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> By the 1930s, several competing systems of regions were adopted by central government for such purposes as census of population, agriculture, electricity supply, civil defence and the regulation of road traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1946 nine "standard regions" were set up, in which central government bodies, statutory undertakings and regional bodies were expected to cooperate.<sup id="cite_ref-balchin_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balchin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, these had declined in importance by the late 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-wannop_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wannop-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Creation of some form of provinces or regions for England was an intermittent theme of post-<a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> British governments. The <a href="/wiki/Redcliffe-Maud_Report" title="Redcliffe-Maud Report">Redcliffe-Maud Report</a> proposed the creation of eight provinces in England, which would see power devolved from central government. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a>\'s administration in the 1970s did not create a regional structure in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, waiting for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Royal Commission on the Constitution</a>, after which government efforts were concentrated on a constitutional settlement in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> for the rest of the decade. In England, the majority of the Commission "suggested regional coordinating and advisory councils for England, consisting largely of indirectly elected representatives of local authorities and operating along the lines of the Welsh advisory council". One-fifth of the advisory councils would be nominees from central government. The boundaries suggested were the "eight now [in 1973] existing for economic planning purposes, modified to make boundaries to conform with the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">new county structure</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A minority report by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Crowther-Hunt" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Crowther-Hunt">Lord Crowther-Hunt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alan_T._Peacock" title="Alan T. Peacock">Alan T. Peacock</a> suggested instead seven regional assemblies and governments within Great Britain (five within England), which would take over substantial amounts of the central government.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some elements of <a href="/wiki/Regional_planning_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional planning in England">regional development and economic planning</a> began to be established in England from the mid-1960s onwards. In most of the standard regions, Economic Planning Councils and Boards were set up, comprising appointed members from local authorities, business, trade unions and universities, and in the early 1970s these produced a number of regional and sub-regional planning studies.<sup id="cite_ref-balchin_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balchin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> These institutions continued to operate until they were abolished by the incoming <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Government_1979-1983" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative Government 1979-1983">Conservative government</a> in 1979. However, by the mid-1980s local authorities in most regions had jointly established standing conferences to consider regional planning issues. Regional initiatives were bolstered by the 1986 Government <a href="/wiki/Green_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Paper">Green Paper</a> and 1989 <a href="/wiki/White_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="White Paper">White Paper</a> on <i>The Future of Development Plans</i>, which proposed the introduction of strong regional guidance within the planning system,<sup id="cite_ref-balchin_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balchin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and by the Government\'s issuing of Strategic Guidance at a regional level, from 1986 onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-wannop_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wannop-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions_as_areas_of_administration">Regions as areas of administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regions as areas of administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In April 1994, the <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a> ministry created a set of ten <a href="/wiki/Government_Office" title="Government Office">Government Office Regions</a> for England. Prior to 1994, although various central government departments had different regional offices, the regions they used tended to be different and <i>ad hoc</i>. The stated purpose was as a way of co-ordinating the various regional offices more effectively: they initially involved the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Trade_and_Industry_(United_Kingdom)" title="Department of Trade and Industry (United Kingdom)">Department of Trade and Industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Employment" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Employment">Department of Employment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Department of Transport</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" title="Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Department for the Environment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>\'s victory in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997 general election</a>, the government created <a href="/wiki/Regional_development_agency" title="Regional development agency">regional development agencies</a>. Around a decade later the Labour administration also founded the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Improvement_and_Efficiency_Partnership" title="Regional Improvement and Efficiency Partnership">Regional Improvement and Efficiency Partnerships</a> (RIEPs) with \xc2\xa3185m of devolved funding to enhance councils\' capacity to improve and take the lead in their own improvement.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Maastricht_Treaty" title="Maastricht Treaty">Maastricht Treaty</a> encouraged the creation of regional boundaries for selection of members for the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_the_Regions" class="mw-redirect" title="Committee of the Regions">Committee of the Regions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>: <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> had each constituted a region, but England represents such a large proportion of the population of the United Kingdom that further division was thought necessary. The English regions, which initially numbered ten, also replaced the <a href="/wiki/Historical_and_alternative_regions_of_England" title="Historical and alternative regions of England">Standard Statistical Regions</a>. <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a> originally constituted a region in itself, but in 1998 it was merged into the <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a> region, creating the nine present-day regions.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Since 1999, the nine regions have also been used as England\'s <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> constituencies<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and as statistical <a href="/wiki/NUTS:UK" class="mw-redirect" title="NUTS:UK">NUTS</a> level 1 regions. Since 1 July 2006, there have also been ten <a href="/wiki/NHS_Strategic_Health_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="NHS Strategic Health Authority">NHS Strategic Health Authorities</a>, each of which corresponds to a region, except for <a href="/wiki/South_East_England" title="South East England">South East England</a>, which is divided into western and eastern parts.\n</p>\n<div role="img" class="noresize" style="width: 370px; float: right; clear: right; font-size: 85%; line-height: 1; text-align: center; background-color: #ffffff; position: relative; max-width: 100%; overflow-x: auto;"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/English_regions_2009.svg/370px-English_regions_2009.svg.png" width="370" height="449" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/English_regions_2009.svg/555px-English_regions_2009.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/English_regions_2009.svg/740px-English_regions_2009.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1108" data-file-height="1345" />\n<div style="position:absolute; left:282.6px; top:269.5px"><a href="/wiki/East_of_England" title="East of England">East of<br />England</a></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:264.1px; top:317.6px"><a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">London</a></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:227.1px; top:214px"><a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East<br />Midlands</a></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:171.6px; top:239.9px"><a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(region)" title="West Midlands (region)">West<br />Midlands</a></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:201.2px; top:132.6px"><a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_and_the_Humber" title="Yorkshire and the Humber">Yorkshire and<br />the Humber</a></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:186.4px; top:58.6px"><a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North<br />East</a></div>\n<div style="position:absolute; left:138.3px; top:101.15px"><a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West</a></div>\n</div>\n<p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Regional_assembly_(England)" title="Regional assembly (England)">regional chambers</a> were created in the eight English regions outside London under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Development_Agencies_Act_1998" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Development Agencies Act 1998">Regional Development Agencies Act 1998</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The powers of the assemblies were limited, and members were appointed, largely by local authorities, rather than being directly elected. The functions of the English regions were essentially devolved to them from Government departments or were taken over from pre-existing regional bodies, such as regional planning conferences and regional employers\' organisations. Each assembly also made proposals for the UK members of the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_the_Regions" class="mw-redirect" title="Committee of the Regions">Committee of the Regions</a>, with members drawn from the elected councillors of the local authorities in the region. The final nominations were made by central government.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Although they were publicly funded, one of the Regional Assemblies claimed not to be a public authority and therefore not subject to the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Information_Act_2000" title="Freedom of Information Act 2000">Freedom of Information Act 2000</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As power was to be devolved to Scotland, Northern Ireland and Wales without a counterweight in England, a series of <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendums</a> were planned to establish elected regional assemblies in some of the regions. The first was held in <a href="/wiki/London_referendum,_1998" class="mw-redirect" title="London referendum, 1998">London in 1998</a> and was passed. The <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a> were created in 2000. A <a href="/wiki/Northern_England_referendums,_2004" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern England referendums, 2004">referendum</a> was held in North East England on 4 November 2004, but the proposal for an elected assembly was rejected. Plans to hold further referendums in other regions were first postponed and then cancelled. A campaign for the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish assembly">Cornish assembly</a>, including a petition to the UK government in 2001, was largely ignored and no referendum was held.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2007, a Treasury Review for new Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> recommended that greater powers should be given to local authorities and that the Regional Chambers should be phased out of existence by 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The same year, nine <a href="/wiki/Regional_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Minister">Regional Ministers</a> were appointed by the incoming <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> government. Their primary goal was stated as being to improve communication between central government and the regions of England.<sup id="cite_ref-gos_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gos-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The assemblies were effectively replaced by smaller <a href="/wiki/Local_authority_leaders%27_board" title="Local authority leaders&#39; board">local authority leaders\' boards</a> between 2008 and 2010, and formally abolished on 31 March 2010, as part of a "Sub-National Review of Economic Development and Regeneration". Most of their functions transferred to the relevant <a href="/wiki/Regional_development_agency" title="Regional development agency">regional development agency</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Local_authority_leaders%27_board" title="Local authority leaders&#39; board">local authority leaders\' boards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2010, the incoming <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron\xe2\x80\x93Clegg coalition">Coalition Government</a> announced its intentions to abolish regional strategies and return spatial planning powers to local government. These plans include the withdrawal of funding to the existing eight Local Authority Leaders\' Boards, with their statutory functions also being assumed by local councils. The boards in most cases continue to exist as voluntary associations of council leaders, funded by the local authorities themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> No appointments as Regional Ministers were made by the incoming UK government in 2010.\n</p><p>These changes did not affect the directly elected <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a>, which was established by separate legislation as part of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a>. In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> remains administered by the Greater London Authority, which consists of an elected <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a> and a separately elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a>.\n</p><p>Following the abolition of the Government Offices in 2011, it was announced that the former Government Office Regions (GOR) would henceforth be known, for the purposes of statistical analysis, simply as Regions.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_regions">List of regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: NUTS 1 statistical regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/NUTS_1_statistical_regions_of_England" title="NUTS 1 statistical regions of England">NUTS 1 statistical regions of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/NUTS_statistical_regions_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="NUTS statistical regions of the United Kingdom">NUTS statistical regions of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics</a> (NUTS) is a <a href="/wiki/Geocode" class="mw-redirect" title="Geocode">geocode</a> <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standard</a> for referencing the <a href="/wiki/Country_subdivision" class="mw-redirect" title="Country subdivision">subdivisions</a> of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland for statistical purposes. The NUTS code for the UK is <i>UK</i> and there are 12 <a href="/wiki/First_level_NUTS_of_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="First level NUTS of the European Union">first level</a> regions within the state. Within the UK, there are 9 such regions in England, together with <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. The standard is developed and regulated by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU). The NUTS standard is instrumental in delivering the EU\'s <a href="/wiki/Structural_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Structural Fund">Structural Funds</a>. A hierarchy of three levels is established by <a href="/wiki/Eurostat" title="Eurostat">Eurostat</a>. The sub-structure corresponds to <a href="/wiki/Administrative_division" title="Administrative division">administrative divisions</a> within the country. Formerly, the further NUTS divisions (IV and V) existed; these have now been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Local_administrative_unit" title="Local administrative unit">Local administrative unit</a> (LAU-1 and LAU-2 respectively).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="City_regions">City regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: City regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/City_region_(United_Kingdom)" title="City region (United Kingdom)">City region (United Kingdom)</a></div>\n<p>In its later years the Labour government adopted the concept of city regions, regions consisting of a metropolitan area and its hinterland or <a href="/wiki/Travel_to_Work_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Travel to Work Area">Travel to Work Areas</a>. Two such areas were considered for giving statutory powers: <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester_City_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Greater Manchester City Region">Greater Manchester City Region</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_Region" title="Leeds City Region">Leeds City Region</a>. However, this was later discontinued as a result of the May 2010 general election, although the <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron\xe2\x80\x93Clegg coalition">Conservative\xe2\x80\x93Liberal Democrat coalition government</a> did agree to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester_Combined_Authority" title="Greater Manchester Combined Authority">Greater Manchester Combined Authority</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Combined_Authority" title="West Yorkshire Combined Authority">West Yorkshire Combined Authority</a> in 2011, with all other proposals and the <a href="/wiki/Regional_development_agency" title="Regional development agency">regional development agencies</a> being subsumed into the <a href="/wiki/Local_enterprise_partnership" title="Local enterprise partnership">local enterprise partnerships</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions_of_England">Subdivisions of England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subdivisions of England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_England" title="Subdivisions of England">Subdivisions of England</a></div>\n<p>Local government in England does not follow a uniform structure. Therefore, each region is divided into a range of <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England" title="Metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties of England">further subdivisions</a>. London is divided into <a href="/wiki/London_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="London borough">London boroughs</a> while the other regions are divided into <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan counties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shire_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Shire county">shire counties</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authorities</a>. Counties are further divided into <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">districts</a> and some areas are also <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">parished</a>. Regions are also divided into sub-regions, which usually group socio-economically linked local authorities together. However, the sub-regions have no official status and are little used other than for strategic planning purposes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/7448/1622442.pdf">Local government geography and history</a>, Department for Communities and Local Government.
