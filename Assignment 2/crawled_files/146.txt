They were known as the <b>Louisville Eclipse</b> from 1882 to 1884, and as the Louisville Colonels from 1885 to 1891. The latter name derived from the historic <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_colonel" class="mw-redirect" title="Kentucky colonel">Kentucky colonels</a>. After the AA folded in 1891, the Colonels joined the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> and played through the 1899 season.  Until the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a> relocated to Washington in 2004, Louisville was the last city to lose a major league baseball franchise and not have another franchise eventually replace it.\n</p><p>"Colonels" was also the name of <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Colonels_(minor_league_baseball)" title="Louisville Colonels (minor league baseball)">several minor league baseball teams</a> that played in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>, in the 20th century.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louisville_Colonels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: American Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After spending several years as a well-known semi-pro team, the Eclipse joined the newly made American Association in 1882. The Eclipse\'s backer, local distiller <a href="/w/index.php?title=J._H._Pank&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="J. H. Pank (page does not exist)">J. H. Pank</a>, was named vice-president of the AA, and the team was to be run by a consortium led by <a href="/w/index.php?title=W._L._Lyons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="W. L. Lyons (page does not exist)">W. L. Lyons</a>. Accompanying them to the major leagues was their star player, infielder <a href="/wiki/Pete_Browning" title="Pete Browning">Pete Browning</a>, who had already achieved some measure of local fame. The team got off to a good start, finishing in second place, their best finish for several seasons.\n</p><p>Managing partner Lyons resigned in mid-1888, with team secretary <a href="/wiki/Mordecai_Davidson" title="Mordecai Davidson">Mordecai Davidson</a> taking over. The following season, the team sank to a 27\xe2\x80\x93111 record and a last place finish. As a result, Davidson surrendered control of the team to the AA. In the 1896 season the Colonels lost five straight games in two days, including a tripleheader on September 7 and a doubleheader the next day, all against the Baltimore Orioles. With the prohibition of triple headers in the early 1920s, this record still stands. Even worse, the 1889 Colonels were the first team in major-league history to lose 100 games in a single season.\n</p><p>In 1890 the team, which had been purchased by <a href="/wiki/Barney_Dreyfuss" title="Barney Dreyfuss">Barney Dreyfuss</a>, would bounce back with a vengeance. The Colonels won the 1890 pennant in the AA and became the one and only team to rise from the cellar to the pennant in one season. That year the AA was considered only the third-best behind the NL and the <a href="/wiki/Players%27_League" title="Players&#39; League">Players\' League</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_League">National League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louisville_Colonels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: National League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1892 the American Association dissolved, and the Eclipse moved to the <a href="/wiki/19th_century_National_League_teams" class="mw-redirect" title="19th century National League teams">National League</a> and played there until 1899. In 1900 Dreyfuss acquired controlling interest of the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pirates</a> and brought 14 Colonels players with him, including future Hall of Famers <a href="/wiki/Honus_Wagner" title="Honus Wagner">Honus Wagner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Clarke" title="Fred Clarke">Fred Clarke</a>, marking the end of the original Colonels organization and Louisville as a Major League Baseball host city.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_achievements">Notable achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louisville_Colonels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In September 1882, Louisville pitchers threw two <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitters</a> in the span of nine days; <a href="/wiki/Tony_Mullane" title="Tony Mullane">Tony Mullane</a> on September 11, followed by Guy Hecker on September 19.  Other Louisville pitchers who threw no-hitters were Ben Sanders on August 22, 1892, and <a href="/wiki/Deacon_Phillippe" title="Deacon Phillippe">Deacon Phillippe</a>, a rookie, on May 25, 1899.  <a href="/wiki/Pete_Browning" title="Pete Browning">Pete Browning</a> <a href="/wiki/Hit_for_the_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hit for the cycle">hit for the cycle</a> twice for Louisville, on August 8, 1886 and June 7, 1889.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_Colonels_players">Notable Colonels players</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
