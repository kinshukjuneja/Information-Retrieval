the Atlanta Braves">Boston Red Stockings</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>National Association of Professional Base Ball Players</b> (<b>NAPBBP</b>), or known simply as the <b>National Association</b> (<b>NA</b>), was founded in 1871 and continued through the 1875 season. It succeeded and incorporated several professional clubs from the previous <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Base_Ball_Players" title="National Association of Base Ball Players">National Association of Base Ball Players</a> (NABBP) of 1857-1870, sometimes called "the amateur association"; in turn several of its clubs created the succeeding <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League of Professional Baseball Clubs</a>. Later shortened simply to be called the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>, it was founded 1876, the earliest one half of modern <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) in America, with the later competing <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League of Professional Base Ball Clubs</a> in 1901, known too as the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input href="/w/index.php?title=National_Association_of_Professional_Base_Ball_Players&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1869, the previously amateur National Association of Base Ball Players, in response to concerns that some teams were paying players, established a professional category. The <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Red_Stockings" title="Cincinnati Red Stockings">Cincinnati Red Stockings</a> were the first team to declare their intention to become fully professional. Other teams quickly followed suit. By 1871, several clubs, wanting to separate fully from the amateur association, broke away to found the National Association of Professional Base Ball Players. (The remaining amateur clubs founded the National Association of Amateur Base Ball Players, which only lasted two years). In 1876, wanting an even stronger central organization, six clubs from the NA and two independents established the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves#Boston_years" title="History of the Atlanta Braves">Boston Red Stockings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hartford_Dark_Blues" title="Hartford Dark Blues">Hartford</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mutuals" title="New York Mutuals">Mutual</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athletic_of_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="Athletic of Philadelphia">Athletic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Brown_Stockings" title="St. Louis Brown Stockings">St. Louis Brown Stockings</a> from the NA plus independent clubs <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Grays" title="Louisville Grays">Louisville</a> and Cincinnati.\n</p><p>The NA was the first professional baseball league. Its status as a major league is <a href="/wiki/National_Association_as_a_major_league" title="National Association as a major league">in dispute</a>. <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> do not recognize it as a major league, but the NA comprised most of the professional clubs and the highest caliber of play then in existence. Its players, managers, and umpires are included among the "major leaguers" who define the scope of many encyclopedias and many databases developed by SABR or Retrosheet.\n</p><p>Several factors limited the lifespan of the National Association including dominance by a single team (Boston) for most of the league\'s existence, instability of franchises as several were placed in cities too small to financially support professional baseball, lack of central authority, and suspicions of the influence of gamblers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Member_clubs">Member clubs</span><span baseball clubs in the 19th century were often known by what is now regarded as a "nickname", although it was actually the club\'s name. This was a practice carried over from the amateur days .The singular form of a "nickname" was often the team name itself, with its base city "understood" and was so listed in the standings. Rather than "Brooklyn Atlantics", the team was simply called "Atlantic", or "Atlantic of Brooklyn" if deemed necessary by the writer. Another common practice was to refer to the team in the plural, hence the "Bostons" the "Chicagos" or the "Mutuals". Sometimes the team would have a nickname, usually something to do with the team colors, such as the Boston Red Stockings, Chicago White Stockings, and Mutual Green Stockings.\n</p><p>This practice of using the singular form of the "nickname" as the team name faded with time, although as recently as the early 1900s, the team generally known as "<a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a>" was shown in the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> standings as "Athletic", the traditional way. That team sported an old-English "A" on its jerseys, as had its nominative predecessors.\n</p><p>Later, the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encyclopedia_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Encyclopedia of Baseball (page does not exist)">Encyclopedia of Baseball</a></i> attempted to retrofit the names into a modern context.
