title="Island">island</a>, usually too small for human habitation. It may simply be a rocky <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reef</a>. A skerry can also be called a low <a href="/wiki/Stack_(geology)" title="Stack (geology)">sea stack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A skerry may have vegetative life such as moss and small, hardy grasses. They also, in some areas of the world, are rested upon by animals such as <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a> or <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">birds</a>, though usually not inhabited.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Etymology"><span class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skerry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term <i>skerry</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>sker</i>, which means a rock in the sea (which in turn derives from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Indo-European">Proto-Indo-European</a> root *<i>sker</i>-, "cut", in the sense of a rock cut off from the land).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Old Norse term <i>sker</i> was brought into the English language via the Scots language word spelled skerrie or skerry. It is a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scandinavian language">Scandinavian languages</a>\' words for <i>skerry</i> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a>: <i lang="fo">sker</i>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>: <i lang="da">sk\xc3\xa6r</i>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">sk\xc3\xa4r</i>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <i lang="no">skj\xc3\xa6r / skjer</i>, found also in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Sch\xc3\xa4re</i>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <i lang="fi">kari</i>, <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>: <i lang="et">sk\xc3\xa4\xc3\xa4r</i>, <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>: <i lang="lv">\xc5\xa1\xc4\x93ra</i>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian</a>: <i lang="lt">\xc5\xa0cheras</i> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">\xd1\x88\xd1\x85\xd0\xb5\xd1\x80\xd1\x8b</span> (shkhery). In <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>, it appears as <i>sgeir</i>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sula_Sgeir" title="Sula Sgeir">Sula Sgeir</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> as <i>sceir,</i> in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> as <i>sgeri,</i> and in <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a> as <i>skeyr.</i>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skerry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Skerries are most commonly formed at the outlet of <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a> where submerged glacially formed valleys at right angles to the coast join with other cross valleys in a complex array. In some places near the seaward margins of fjorded areas, the ice-scoured channels are so numerous and varied in direction that the rocky coast is divided into thousands of island blocks, some large and mountainous while others are merely rocky points or rock <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a> that menace navigation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skerry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island fringe of Norway is such a group of glacially formed skerries (called a <i>skj\xc3\xa6rg\xc3\xa5rd</i>); many of the cross fjords are so arranged that they parallel the coast and provide a protected channel behind an almost unbroken succession of mountainous islands and skerries. By this channel one can travel through a protected passage almost the entire 1,600&#160;km route from <a href="/wiki/Stavanger" title="Stavanger">Stavanger</a> to <a href="/wiki/North_Cape,_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="North Cape, Norway">North Cape</a>, Norway. The <a href="/wiki/Blindleia" title="Blindleia">Blindleia</a> is a skerry-protected waterway that starts near <a href="/wiki/Kristiansand" title="Kristiansand">Kristiansand</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, and continues past <a href="/wiki/Lillesand" title="Lillesand">Lillesand</a>.\n</p><p>The "<a href="/wiki/Inside_Passage" title="Inside Passage">inside passage</a>" provides a similar route from <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> to <a href="/wiki/Skagway,_Alaska" title="Skagway, Alaska">Skagway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. Yet another such skerry-protected passage extends from the <a href="/wiki/Straits_of_Magellan" class="mw-redirect" title="Straits of Magellan">Straits of Magellan</a> north for 800&#160;km (500&#160;mi) along the west coast of the South American continent.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> coast along <a href="/wiki/Bohusl%C3%A4n" title="Bohusl\xc3\xa4n">Bohusl\xc3\xa4n</a> is likewise guarded by skerries. Even the east coast of Sweden, in the Baltic Sea, has many big sk\xc3\xa4rg\xc3\xa5rdar (archipelagos), notably <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_archipelago" title="Stockholm archipelago">Stockholm archipelago</a> - <i>Stockholms sk\xc3\xa4rg\xc3\xa5rd</i>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg/270px-Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg" width="270" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg/405px-Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg/540px-Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loch_Uisgebhagh.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Islets and skerries in Loch Uisgebhagh, east of <a href="/wiki/Benbecula" title="Benbecula">Benbecula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The southwestern coast of <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> also has a great many skerries; so many, in fact, that they form an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago_Sea" title="Archipelago Sea">archipelago</a>. This area is experiencing <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a> that connects the rising islands as they break sea level, revealing till deposits and eventually clay bottoms. The skerries exist as small rocky islands before uplift of adjacent terrain changes the classification of this landform into a <a href="/wiki/Tombolo" title="Tombolo">tombolo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>, the best examples are the <a href="/wiki/Minina_Skerries" title="Minina Skerries">Minina Skerries</a>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Kara_Sea" title="Kara Sea">Kara Sea</a>, in the western shores of the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sumsky_Skerries" title="Sumsky Skerries">Sumsky Skerries</a> (Sumskiye Shkhery) <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Skerry&amp;params=64_24_N_35_30_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">64\xc2\xb024\xe2\x80\xb2N</span> <span class="longitude">35\xc2\xb030\xe2\x80\xb2E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">64.400\xc2\xb0N 35.500\xc2\xb0E</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">64.400; 35.500</span></span></span></a></span>, located in the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a>.\n</p><p>The United Kingdom has a large number of skerries including <a href="/wiki/Staple_Island" title="Staple Island">Staple Island</a> (an Outer <a href="/wiki/Farne_Islands" title="Farne Islands">Farne Island</a>) in England; a small rocky outcrop near the <a href="/wiki/Fowlsheugh" title="Fowlsheugh">Fowlsheugh</a> in northeast Scotland; numerous reefs in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Dubh_Artach" title="Dubh Artach">Dubh Artach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skerryvore" title="Skerryvore">Skerryvore</a>; and <a href="/wiki/The_Skerries,_Northern_Ireland" title="The Skerries, Northern Ireland">The Skerries</a>, located off the Antrim coast of Northern Ireland.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Skerries,_Dublin" title="Skerries, Dublin">Skerries</a> is the name of a coastal area of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland, with many skerries offshore, including <a href="/wiki/Rockabill" title="Rockabill">Rockabill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shenick_Island" title="Shenick Island">Shenick Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colt_Island" title="Colt Island">Colt Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Patrick%27s_Island" title="St Patrick&#39;s Island">St Patrick\'s Island</a>.\n</p><p>The most southerly skerries are perhaps the <a href="/wiki/Skrap_Skerries" title="Skrap Skerries">Skrap Skerries</a> off <a href="/wiki/South_Georgia_Island" title="South Georgia Island">South Georgia</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
