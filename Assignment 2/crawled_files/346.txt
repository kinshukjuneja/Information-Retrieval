style="text-align:center;background-color: #efefef">Demographics</th></tr><tr><th scope="row">Population</th><td>\n0</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Annet</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw"><b>Anet</b></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kittiwake" title="Kittiwake">kittiwake</a></i>) is the second largest of the fifty or so uninhabited <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, 1 kilometre (0.62&#160;mi) west of <a href="/wiki/St_Agnes,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Agnes, Isles of Scilly">St Agnes</a> with a length of 1 kilometre (0.62&#160;mi) <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and approximately 22 hectares (54 acres) in area. The low-lying island is almost divided in two by a narrow neck of land at West Porth which can, at times, be covered by waves. At the northern end of the island are the two granite carns of Annet Head and Carn Irish and three smaller carns known as the Haycocks. The rocky outcrops on the southern side of the island, such as South Carn, are smaller. Annet is a bird sanctuary and the main seabird breeding site in Scilly.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The island is closed to the public from 15 April to 20 August every year to limit the disturbance to the breeding seabirds for which it has been designated a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI). It is also within part of the Isles of Scilly <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB), <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Coast_(England_and_Wales)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heritage Coast (England and Wales)">Isles of Scilly Heritage Coast</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Plantlife" title="Plantlife">Plantlife\xe2\x80\x99s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Important_Plant_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Important Plant Area">Isles of Scilly Important Plant Area</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The island is managed by the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly_Wildlife_Trust" title="Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust">Isles of Scilly Wildlife Trust</a><sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> who lease it from the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-9"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Little has been found on Annet in the way of human remains apart from a prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Hut_circle" title="Hut circle">hut circle</a>, a fragmentary field system and several limpet <a href="/wiki/Middens" class="mw-redirect" title="Middens">middens</a>. Bones of cattle and sheep were found indicating that they were eaten here and probably grazed the island.<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is proposed to designate the whole of Annet as a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_Ancient_Monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled Ancient Monument">Scheduled Ancient Monument</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The name of the island is first recorded in 1302 as <i>Anet</i>. Also recorded as <i>Anete</i> in 1305, <i>Anet</i> in 1339, <i>Agnet</i> in 1570 and <i>Agnet iland</i> alias <i>Annett</i> in 1650.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 19th century Annet was \xe2\x80\xb3<i>used for pasturage by the inhabitants of other islands</i>\xe2\x80\xb3<sup id="cite_ref-Hitchens_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hitchens-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> although with only one freshwater seepage there could not have been many animals grazing on the island.\n</p><p>The SS <i>Castleford</i> struck the <a href="/wiki/Western_Rocks,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Western Rocks, Isles of Scilly">Crebawethans</a> in June 1877 and led to some of her cargo of 250 to 450 cattle being landed on the island and staying there for up to 10 days. Gurney (1889) reported that "... the animals trampled everything and would have caused an immense amount of damage at the peak of the shearwater and storm petrel nesting season".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It seems unlikely that many stayed for 10 days because of the need for fresh water. Cattle were washed up on the Cornish coast as far as <a href="/wiki/Mount%27s_Bay" title="Mount&#39;s Bay">Mount\'s Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cornwall" title="St Ives, Cornwall">St Ives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Another ship wrecked nearby, the world\'s only seven-masted schooner, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_W._Lawson_(ship)" title="Thomas W. Lawson (ship)"><i>Thomas W Lawson</i></a> spilled her cargo of oil on 14 December 1907 causing the loss of many birds. In 1971 Rex Cowan found the wreck of <a href="/wiki/Hollandia_(1742_ship)" title="Hollandia (1742 ship)">VOC <i>Hollandia</i></a> together with a large quantity of coins, bronze cannons and mortars. The ship hit Gunner Rock on 13 July 1743 with the loss of 276 lives.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_history">Natural history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The geology of Annet is of <a href="/wiki/Hercynian" class="mw-redirect" title="Hercynian">Hercynian</a> granite overlain with <a href="/wiki/Raised_beach" title="Raised beach">raised beach</a> deposits. On the southern end there is a "thick bed of soil, part sand and part soil".<sup id="cite_ref-Gurney_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gurney-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The island is low-lying with a top height of 18 m and the coast consists of boulder storm beaches. The effects of wind exposure, salt spray and lack of topography, restricts diversity and only 53 species of vascular plants have been recorded. The north of the island is dominated by a well developed, thick, thrift (<i><a href="/wiki/Armeria_maritima" title="Armeria maritima">Armeria maritima</a></i>) turf whilst the southern part is dominated by dense stands of bracken (<i><a href="/wiki/Pteridium_aquilinum" title="Pteridium aquilinum">Pteridium aquilinum</a></i>), bramble (<i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_fruticosus" title="Rubus fruticosus">Rubus fruticosus</a></i>) and bluebells (<i><a href="/wiki/Hyacinthoides_non-scripta" title="Hyacinthoides non-scripta">Hyacinthoides non-scripta</a></i>), with some sand sedge (<i><a href="/wiki/Carex_arenaria" title="Carex arenaria">Carex arenaria</a></i>) and Yorkshire fog (<i><a href="/wiki/Holcus_lanatus" title="Holcus lanatus">Holcus lanatus</a></i>). Thickets of tree mallow (<i><a href="/wiki/Lavatera_arborea" class="mw-redirect" title="Lavatera arborea">Lavatera arborea</a></i>) have developed at the back of some of the boulder beaches. There were scattered colonies of shore dock (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rumex_rupestris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rumex rupestris (page does not exist)">Rumex rupestris</a></i>) until a storm in 1982 swept away some of the boulder beaches. One colony remains in the corner of a relatively sheltered beach in the south of the island at a freshwater seepage. Shore dock is one of the primary reasons for the selection of the Isles of Scilly as a <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> (SAC).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Annet is the \xe2\x80\xb3British stronghold\xe2\x80\xb3 for the lichen <i><a href="/wiki/Roccella_fuciformis" class="mw-redirect" title="Roccella fuciformis">Roccella fuciformis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\nA description of the island by Hilda Quick, a former resident of St Agnes published in 1964 is still relevant today:<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Many people are disappointed at being unable to visit the famous bird sanctuary, but in fact, there is very little to see there by day. There will be shearwater corpses lying about, (victims of the gulls) several large colonies of gulls, some oystercatchers, rock pipits, and wrens. The walking on the island is horrid, much of it over loose stones hidden in long grass, some bramble and bracken, and awkward great clumps of thrift. What Annet is famous for is its colonies of Manx shearwaters and storm petrels; but since they nest underground, and only come and go in the dark, there is nothing to be seen of them by day. One has to camp out for the night, which is often a chilly process, and requires special permission. The puffins that use&#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a>?</i>&#93; to breed there in such numbers that their bodies were used for paying rent are now few, and can best be seen from a boat as they swim on the water.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Annet is considered to be of outstanding importance as a seabird colony. Twelve species nest here, of which two, European storm\xe2\x80\x93petrel (<i><a href="/wiki/Hydrobates_pelagicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrobates pelagicus">Hydrobates pelagicus</a></i>) and lesser black-backed gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_fuscus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus fuscus">Larus fuscus</a></i>) have nationally important breeding populations. The storm\xe2\x80\x93petrel breeds amongst the boulders of the more stable storm beaches. The largest population of Manx shearwater (<i><a href="/wiki/Puffinus_puffinus" class="mw-redirect" title="Puffinus puffinus">Puffinus puffinus</a></i>) in the islands breed here and the other annual breeding species are puffin (<i><a href="/wiki/Fratercula_arctica" class="mw-redirect" title="Fratercula arctica">Fratercula arctica</a></i>), greater black-backed gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_marinus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus marinus">Larus marinus</a></i>), razorbill (<i><a href="/wiki/Alca_torda" class="mw-redirect" title="Alca torda">Alca torda</a></i>), kittiwake (<i><a href="/wiki/Rissa_tridactyla" class="mw-redirect" title="Rissa tridactyla">Rissa tridactyla</a></i>), fulmar (<i><a href="/wiki/Fulmarus_glacialis" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulmarus glacialis">Fulmarus glacialis</a></i>), herring gull (<i><a href="/wiki/Larus_argentatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus argentatus">L. argentatus</a></i>) and shag (<i><a href="/wiki/Phalacrocorax_aristotelis" class="mw-redirect" title="Phalacrocorax aristotelis">P. aristotelis</a></i>). Common tern (<i><a href="/wiki/Sterna_hirundo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sterna hirundo">Sterna hirundo</a></i>) breed on the island most years as do cormorant (<i><a href="/wiki/Phalacrocorax_carbo" class="mw-redirect" title="Phalacrocorax carbo">Phalacrocorax carbo</a></i>) and very rarely Arctic tern (<i><a href="/wiki/Sterna_paradisaea" class="mw-redirect" title="Sterna paradisaea">S. paradisaea</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Parslow_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parslow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_birds">Breeding birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Annet has long had a reputation for being the best island for breeding birds and Jessie Mothersole visiting in 1910 described the island thus:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Annet is known by the name of " Bird Island," from the immense numbers that breed there. In the early summer the sea all round is black with puffins and razor-bills, their white breasts being hardly noticeable as they sit on the surface of the water&#160;; and the air above is dark with clouds of gulls, and full of their ceaseless cry. Puffins (also called sea-parrots) have bred on the islands from time immemorial.<sup id="cite_ref-Mothersole_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mothersole-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Numbers of breeding birds have fallen over the years  and in the last 150 years some of the threats have been recorded.\n</p><p>\nJ H Gurney visited the island in May 1887 and estimated the population to be 200 pairs. He reported: </p><blockquote><p>There had been a terrible robbery on Annet, a few days before our visit, of Shearwaters or their eggs, or both, and the south end of the island was dug over in all directions. Notwithstanding this, we had no difficulty in finding the objects of our search; indeed the ground was so honeycombed, that it was impossible in some places to avoid stepping on, and breaking into, their domiciles.</p></blockquote><p> The raid on the colony had been carried out by men from <a href="/wiki/Tresco,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Tresco, Isles of Scilly">Tresco</a> and they were brought before the Lord Proprietor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Algernon_Smith-Dorrien-Smith" title="Thomas Algernon Smith-Dorrien-Smith">Mr Dorrien\xe2\x80\x93Smith</a>; Gurney did not report on their punishment if any. He also reported that Annet was the only island without rats.<sup id="cite_ref-Gurney_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gurney-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A few weeks later cattle from the SS <i>Castleford</i> were landed on Annet after she hit the nearby <a href="/wiki/Western_Rocks,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Western Rocks, Isles of Scilly">Crebawethans</a> and was said to have "trampled everything to pieces, broke in all the Shearwaters\' holes, probably destroying many birds, and made a ruin of everything". Another wreck, the <i>Thomas W Lawson</i> on 14 December 1907, spilled her cargo of oil and many of the rabbits and birds on Annet "were seen to lie upon the shore". The smell of oil could still be smelt on nearby St Agnes 18 months later.\n</p><p>At the time of Jessie Mothersole\'s visit in 1910, visitors were only allowed one hour on the island and shooting and egg collecting was forbidden. Despite this, Annet figured highly on the list of places to visit for egg collecting. An examination of the egg collection at the <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum,_London" title="Natural History Museum, London">Natural History Museum</a> (NHM) shows 45 eggs taken between 1880 and 1936 by 14 individuals and one group from <a href="/wiki/Royal_Holloway,_University_of_London" title="Royal Holloway, University of London">Holloway College</a>; doubtless there are many more eggs in private collections. Names on the data cards include well known wildlife experts such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rothschild" title="Charles Rothschild">Charles Rothschild</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_Frohawk" title="Frederick William Frohawk">F W Frohawk</a>. An indication of how common and acceptable egg collecting was at the time, is the issue of day permits to visit uninhabited islands by the warden A A Dorrien\xe2\x80\x93Smith of Tresco. A NHM data card for three eggs in the collection has a permit for landing on Annet on 24 May 1931 attached and allowed the Souter brothers to land on any island, except tern islands, for up to one hour.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>European storm\xe2\x80\x93petrel</b>\nThe only breeding sites for European storm-petrel in England are on the Isles of Scilly with 11 colonies and an estimated 1,475 occupied sites (i.e.  breeding pairs). Annet had the majority of breeding pairs with 938 occupied sites during the Seabird 2000 survey declining to 788 occupied sites in a repeat survey in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-Seabird2000_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seabird2000-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>Other species</b>\nThe most recent count of seabirds on the Isles of Scilly was the Seabird 2000 count and on Annet 209 nests were found to be occupied by shag out of a total of 1,109 for the islands. On Scilly, they breed almost exclusively beneath boulder beaches or holes in low cliffs. Manx shearwater was estimated to occupy 123 burrows out of a total of 201 for the islands\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x93 a 74% decrease from a previous survey in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 22 September 2002 the first <a href="/wiki/Lanceolated_warbler" title="Lanceolated warbler">lanceolated warbler</a> (<i>Locustella lanceolata</i>) recorded on the Isles of Scilly was found during a search for migrant birds. Also seen was two <a href="/wiki/Sedge_warbler" title="Sedge warbler">sedge warbler</a> (<i>Acrocephalus schoenobaenus</i>) and four <a href="/wiki/Willow_warbler" title="Willow warbler">willow warbler</a> (<i>Phylloscopus trochilus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mammals">Mammals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mammals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1996 the Isles of Scilly was designated a European Marine Site and the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Management_Organisation" title="Marine Management Organisation">Marine Management Organisation</a> is required to monitor the site at least every six years. The  grey seal (<i><a href="/wiki/Halichoerus_grypus" class="mw-redirect" title="Halichoerus grypus">Halichoerus grypus</a></i>) is a named species and surveys were carried out in 2005 and 2010. Annet is one of the top four sites within the islands for numbers of seals and, during August to December 2010, over half of the observed pups seen were on Annet (46 out of 85).<sup id="cite_ref-Sayer_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sayer-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Records of some of the rarer <a href="/wiki/Cetaceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Cetaceans">cetaceans</a> are from strandings and the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> was the first organisation to publish such reports for Britain. Strandings are often the only indication that rare species live, or pass through, an area and in 1917 an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>, a 13.6&#160;m (45&#160;ft) male <a href="/wiki/Fin_whale" title="Fin whale">fin whale</a> (<i>Balaenoptera physalus</i>) was washed up.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> (previously English Nature) began a rat extermination programme in the 1990s with systematic baiting now being carried out by the Wildlife Trust. Brown rats (<i><a href="/wiki/Rattus_norvegicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Rattus norvegicus">Rattus norvegicus</a></i>) have been eradicated from Annet and a feasibility study has found that the eradication of rats from St Agnes and <a href="/wiki/Gugh,_Isles_of_Scilly" class="mw-redirect" title="Gugh, Isles of Scilly">Gugh</a> would benefit the breeding birds on Annet by removing the most likely source of invasion. The study also found that small mammals also benefit from the eradication programme which should help the Annet population of Scilly shrew (<i><a href="/wiki/Crocidura_suaveolens" class="mw-redirect" title="Crocidura suaveolens">Crocidura suaveolens</a></i>). A rat eradication programme started on St Agnes and Gugh on 8 November 2013 and seems to be successful as none have been recorded in December. The eradication team will visit Annet in January 2014 to check for signs of rats.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Rabbit (<i><a href="/wiki/Oryctolagus_cuniculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Oryctolagus cuniculus">Oryctolagus cuniculus</a></i>) has also been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Bell_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A gull pellet found in 2002 contained the skull of a small mammal and when examined was found to be a water vole (<i><a href="/wiki/Arvicola_terrestris" class="mw-redirect" title="Arvicola terrestris">Arvicola terrestris</a></i>) which has never been found on the Isles of Scilly. Water vole is extinct at the nearest possible mainland source, Cornwall, and the original location of the skull is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Invertebrates">Invertebrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annet,_Isles_of_Scilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Invertebrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lepidopterist" class="mw-redirect" title="Lepidopterist">lepidopterist</a> Austin Richardson made annual visits to the Isles of Scilly from 1956 to 1962 recording the moths and butterflies. href="#cite_note-ukmoths-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> K Smith visited Annet in June 1961 to record the <a href="/wiki/Diptera" class="mw-redirect" title="Diptera">diptera</a> and also found a moth, the
