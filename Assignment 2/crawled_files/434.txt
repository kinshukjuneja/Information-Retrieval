class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word "Romanichal" is derived from <i>Romani chal</i>, where <i>chal</i> is <a href="/wiki/Angloromani_language" title="Angloromani language">Angloromani</a> for "fellow".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Romanichal are found across the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lowland_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowland Scotland">Lowland Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. The Romanichal diaspora emigrated from the British Isles to other parts of the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a>. Based on some estimates, there are now more people of Romanichal descent in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> than in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They are also found in smaller numbers in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.\nThere also exists a small community in Malta who have colonial Romanichal ancestry.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Angloromani_language" title="Angloromani language">Angloromani language</a></div>\n<p>The Romani people in England are thought to have spoken the <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">Romani language</a> until the 19th century, when it was replaced by English and Angloromani, a <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole language</a> that combines the <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> of English with the Romani lexicon.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> All Romanichals also speak English.\n</p><p>Many Angloromani words have been incorporated into English, particularly in the form of <a href="/wiki/British_slang" title="British slang">British slang</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Romani_people" title="History of the Romani people">History of the Romani people</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Movimiento_gitano.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Movimiento_gitano.jpg/300px-Movimiento_gitano.jpg" width="300" height="249" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Movimiento_gitano.jpg/450px-Movimiento_gitano.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Movimiento_gitano.jpg/600px-Movimiento_gitano.jpg 2x" data-file-width="647" data-file-height="536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Movimiento_gitano.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The migration of the Romanies through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Africa">Northern Africa</a> to Europe.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Romani people have origins in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and began migrating westwards from the 11th century. The first groups of Romani people arrived in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> by the end of the 15th century, escaping conflicts in <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Southeastern Europe">Southeastern Europe</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>).\n</p><p>In 1506 there are recorded Romani persons in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-English_Gypsies_1875_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_Gypsies_1875-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> arrived from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 1512.<sup id="cite_ref-English_Gypsies_1875_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_Gypsies_1875-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Soon the leadership passed laws aimed at stopping the Romani immigration and at the assimilation of those already present.\n</p><p>During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Egyptians_Act_1530" title="Egyptians Act 1530">Egyptians Act (1530)</a> banned Romanies from entering the country and required those living in the country to leave within 16 days. Failure to do so could result in confiscation of property, imprisonment and deportation. During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary I</a> the act was amended with the <a href="/wiki/Egyptians_Act_1530#Egyptians_Act_1554" title="Egyptians Act 1530">Egyptians Act (1554)</a>, which removed the threat of punishment to Romanies if they abandoned their "naughty, idle and ungodly life and company" and adopted a settled lifestyle, but on the other hand increased the penalty for noncompliance to death.\n</p><p>In 1562 a new law offered Romanies born in England and Wales the possibility of becoming English subjects, if they assimilated into the local population. Despite this legislation, the Romani population managed to survive, but it was forced into a marginal lifestyle and subjected to continuous discrimination from the state authorities and many of the local non-Romanies. In 1596, 106 men and women were condemned to death at <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> just for being Romani, and nine were executed. The others were able to prove that they were born in England.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Rid" title="Samuel Rid">Samuel Rid</a> authored two early works about them in the early 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the 1780s, gradually, the anti-Romani laws were repealed, although not all. The identity of the Romanichals was formed between the years 1660 and 1800, as a Romani group living in Britain.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persecution">Persecution</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RumneyDavey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/RumneyDavey.jpg/220px-RumneyDavey.jpg" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/RumneyDavey.jpg/330px-RumneyDavey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/RumneyDavey.jpg/440px-RumneyDavey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="854" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RumneyDavey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A British Romanichal</div></div></div>\n<p>England began to deport Romanichals as early as 1544, principally to <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> a process that was continued and encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MacRitchie,_David_1894_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacRitchie,_David_1894-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Kale" title="Finnish Kale">Finnish Kale</a>, a Romani group in Finland, maintain that their ancestors had originally been a Romani group who travelled from <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> thereby supporting the idea that they and the Scandinavian Travellers/Romani are distantly related to present-day Scottish Romani and English Romanichals.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1603 an <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a> was made for the transportation of Romanichal to <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>. Other European countries forced the further transport of the Romani of Britain to the Americas. Many times those deported in this manner did not survive as an <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a>, because of the separations after the round up, the sea passage and the subsequent settlement as slaves, all destroying their social fabric. At the same time, voluntary emigration began to the <a href="/wiki/English_overseas_possessions" title="English overseas possessions">English overseas possessions</a>. Romani groups which survived continued the expression of the Romani culture there.\n</p><p>In the years following the <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a>, Australia was the preferred destination for Romanichal transportation, due to its use as a penal colony. The exact number of British Romani deported to Australia is unknown. It has been suggested that three Romanichal were present on the <a href="/wiki/First_Fleet" title="First Fleet">First Fleet</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-identity_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> one of whom was thought to be <a href="/wiki/James_Squire" title="James Squire">James Squire</a><sup id="cite_ref-identity_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> who founded Australia\'s first commercial brewery in 1798, and whose grandson <a href="/wiki/James_Farnell" title="James Farnell">James Farnell</a> who became the first native-born Premier of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> in 1877. The total Romani population seems to be an extremely low number when we consider that British Romani people made up just 0.01% of the original 162,000 convict population.<sup id="cite_ref-identity_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, it has been suggested that Romanichal were one of the main target groups and were discriminated against due to the transportation laws of England in the mid-18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Donohoe_1988_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donohoe_1988-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It is often difficult to distinguish British Romani people of Wales and England from the majority of non-Romani convicts at the time, therefore the precise number of British Romanies transported is not known, although there are occurrences of Romani names and possible families within the convict population; however it is unclear if such people were members of the established Romani community.<sup id="cite_ref-Donohoe_1988_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donohoe_1988-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Fragmentary records do exist and it is thought with confidence at least fifty or more British Romanies may have been transported to Australia, although the actual figure could be higher.<sup id="cite_ref-identity_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> What is clear is that such deportation (as for all convicts) was particularly harsh:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"For Romani convicts transportation meant social and psychological death; exiled they had little hope of returning to England to re-establish family ties, cultural roots, continuous expression and validation that would have revived their Romani identity in the convict era."<sup id="cite_ref-identity_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>One, however, is known to have returned. Henry Lavello (or Lovell) was repatriated with a full pardon with a son born to an Aboriginal woman who accompanied him back to England.<sup id="cite_ref-identity_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Donohoe_1988_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donohoe_1988-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Racism against Romanichal and other travelling peoples is still endemic within Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 2008 the media reported that Romani experience a higher degree of racism than any other group in the UK, including asylum-seekers, and a Mori poll indicated that a third of UK residents admitted to being prejudiced against Romani.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery">Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_Britain_and_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavery in Britain and Ireland">Slavery in Britain and Ireland</a></div>\n<p>In the 17th century <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> shipped Romanichals as <a href="/wiki/Slaves" class="mw-redirect" title="Slaves">slaves</a> to the American southern plantations<sup id="cite_ref-The_Pariah_Syndrome_1987_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Pariah_Syndrome_1987-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and there is documentation of English Romanies being owned by freed black slaves in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MacRitchie,_David_1894_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacRitchie,_David_1894-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Pariah_Syndrome_1987_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Pariah_Syndrome_1987-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Gypsies, according to the legal definition, were anyone identifying themselves to be Egyptians or Gypsies.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The works of <a href="/wiki/George_Borrow" title="George Borrow">George Borrow</a> reflect the influences this had on the Romani Language of England and others contain references to Romanies being <i>bitcheno pawdel</i> or <i>Bitchade pardel</i>, to be "sent across" to America or Australia, a period of Romani history not forgotten by Romanies in Britain today. One term reflects this in the contemporary Angloromani for "magistrate" is <i>bitcherin\' mush</i>, the "transporter."\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(April 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Historically, Romanichals earned a living doing agricultural work and would move to the edges of towns for the winter months. There was casual work available on farms throughout the spring, summer and autumn months; spring would start with seed sowing, planting potatoes and fruit trees, early summer with weeding, and there would be a succession of harvests of crops from summer to late autumn. Of particular significance was the <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hop</a> industry, which employed thousands of Romanichals both in spring for <a href="/wiki/Vine_training" title="Vine training">vine training</a> and for the harvest in early autumn. Winter months were often spent doing casual labour in towns or selling goods or services door to door.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mass industrialisation of agriculture in the 1960s led to the disappearance of many of the casual farm jobs Romanichals had traditionally carried out.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 20th century onwards Romanichals became, and remain, the mainstay of travelling funfairs, scrap metal dealing, horse dealing, tree surgery, tarmacking, asphalt paving, hawking, fortune telling and wooden rose making. They have also produced notable boxers such as <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wharton_(boxer)" title="Henry Wharton (boxer)">Henry Wharton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Billy_Joe_Saunders" title="Billy Joe Saunders">Billy Joe Saunders</a> as well as some notable footballers like <a href="/wiki/Freddy_Eastwood" title="Freddy Eastwood">Freddy Eastwood</a>, and journalists, psychotherapists, nurses and all manner of professions.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Travel">Travel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Travel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Romanichal_wagon.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Romanichal_wagon.JPG/220px-Romanichal_wagon.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Romanichal_wagon.JPG/330px-Romanichal_wagon.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Romanichal_wagon.JPG/440px-Romanichal_wagon.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Romanichal_wagon.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Romanichal style Reading Vardo late 19th century.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vardo_(Romani_wagon)" title="Vardo (Romani wagon)">Vardo (Romani wagon)</a></div>\n<p>Originally, Romanichals would travel on foot, or with light, horse-drawn carts, and typical of other Romani groups would build <a href="/wiki/Bender_tent" title="Bender tent">Bender tents</a> where they settled for a time. A Bender is a type of tent constructed from a frame of bent hazel branches (hazel is chosen for its straightness and flexibility), covered with canvas or tarpaulin. These tents are still favoured by <a href="/wiki/New_Age_Travellers" class="mw-redirect" title="New Age Travellers">New Traveller</a> groups.\n</p><p>Around the mid- to late 19th century, Romanichals started using wagons that incorporated living spaces on the inside. These they called <i>Vardos</i> and were often brightly and colorfully decorated on the inside and outside. In the present day, Romanichals are more likely to live in <a href="/wiki/Travel_trailer" class="mw-redirect" title="Travel trailer">caravans</a> or houses.\n</p><p>Over 60% of 21st-century Romanichal families live in houses of bricks and mortar whilst the remaining 40% still live in various forms of traditional Traveller modes of transport, such as caravans, trailers or static caravans (a small minority still live in <a href="/wiki/Vardo_(Romani_wagon)" title="Vardo (Romani wagon)">Vardos</a>).\n</p><p>According to the Regional Spatial Strategy caravan count for 2008, there were 13,386 caravans owned by Romani in the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(region)" title="West Midlands (region)">West Midlands</a> region of England, whilst a further 16,000 lived in bricks and mortar. Of the 13,386 caravans, 1,300 were parked on unauthorised sites (that is, on land where Romani were not given permission to park). \nOver 90% of Britain\'s travelling Romanichals live on authorised sites where they pay full rates (<a href="/wiki/Council_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Council tax">council tax</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On most traveller Romanichal sites there are usually no toilets or showers inside caravans because in Romanichal culture this is considered unclean, or \'mochadi\'. Most sites have separate utility blocks with toilets, sinks and electric showers. Many Romanichals will not do their laundry inside, especially not underwear, and subsequently many utility blocks also have washing machines. In the days of horse-drawn wagons and Vardos, Romanichal women would do their laundry in a river, being careful to wash upper body garments further upstream from underwear and lower body garments, and personal bathing would take place much further downstream. In some modern trailers, a double wall separates the living areas from the toilet and shower.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to the (British) <a href="/wiki/Caravan_Sites_Act_1968" title="Caravan Sites Act 1968">Caravan Sites Act 1968</a> which greatly reduced the number of caravans allowed to be pitched on authorised sites, many Romanichals cannot find legal places on sites with the rest of their families.\n</p><p>Like most nomadic groups, Romanichals travel around for work, usually following set routes and set stopping places (called \xe2\x80\x98atching tans\xe2\x80\x99) which have been established for hundreds of years. May traditional stopping places were established before land ownership changed and any land laws were in place. Many atching tans were established by feudal land owners in the Middle Ages, when Romani would provide agricultural or manual labour services in return for lodgings and food.\n</p><p>Today, most Romani travel within the same areas that were established generations ago. Most people can trace their presence in an area back over a hundred or two hundred years. Many traditional stopping places were taken over by local government or by settled individuals decades ago and have subsequently changed hands numerous times; however Romani have long historical connections to such places and do not always willingly give them up. \nMost families are identifiable by their traditional wintering base, where they will stop travelling for the winter, and this place will be technically where a family is \xe2\x80\x98from\xe2\x80\x99.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_acts_of_legislation">British acts of legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: British acts of legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Romanichal&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Romani Horse Fair</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Enclosure_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclosure Act">Enclosure Act</a> of 1857 created the offence of injury or damage to village greens and interruption to its use or enjoyment as a place of exercise and recreation. The <a href="/wiki/Commons_Act" title="Commons Act">Commons Act</a> 1876 makes encroachment or inclosure of a village green, and interference with or occupation of the soil unlawful unless it is with the aim of improving enjoyment of the green.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Caravan_Sites_and_Control_of_Development_Act_1960" title="Caravan Sites and Control of Development Act 1960">Caravan Sites and Control of Development Act 1960</a> states that no occupier of land shall cause or permit the land to be used as a caravan site unless he is the holder of a site licence. It also enables a district council to make an order prohibiting the stationing of caravans on common land, or a town or village green. These acts had the overall effect of preventing travellers using the vast majority of their traditional stopping places, much to the relief of local residents.\n</p><p>The Caravan Sites Act 1968 required local authorities to provide caravan sites for travellers if there was a demonstrated need. This was resisted by many councils who would claim that there were no Romanies living in their areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The result was that insufficient pitches were provided for travellers, leading to a situation whereby holders of a pitch could no longer travel, for fear of losing it.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Justice_and_Public_Order_Act_1994" title="Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994">Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994</a> removed the duty of local councils to provide authorised pitches and gave the Council and Police powers to move travellers on, subject to certain welfare issues. The official response of the government was that travellers should buy land and apply for <a href="/wiki/Planning_permission" title="Planning permission">planning permission</a> to occupy it. However, those that did so found it extremely difficult to get planning permission, with more than 90% of applications by travellers refused.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In the first phase of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nazis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazis">Nazis</a> drew up lists of Romani individuals (many of them Romanichals) and persons with Romani ancestry from the United Kingdom to be interned and subjected to <a href="/wiki/Porajmos" class="mw-redirect" title="Porajmos">Porajmos</a> in the event of the country\'s occupation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The crisis of the 1960s decade, caused by the <a href="/wiki/Caravan_Sites_Act_1968" title="Caravan Sites Act 1968">Caravan Sites Act 1968</a> (stopping new private sites being built until 1972), led to the appearance of the "British Gypsy Council" to fight for the rights of the Romanichals.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, the issue of "travellers" (referring to <a href="/wiki/Irish_Travellers" title="Irish Travellers">Irish Travellers</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Age_Travellers" class="mw-redirect" title="New Age Travellers">New Age Travellers</a> as well as Romanichal and other groups of Romani people) became a 2005 <a href="/wiki/General_election" title="General election">general election</a> issue, with the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> promising to review the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a>. This law, which absorbs the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> into UK <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>, is seen by some to permit the granting of retrospective planning permission. Severe population pressures and the paucity of <a href="/wiki/Greenfield_land" title="Greenfield land">greenfield</a> sites have led to <i>travellers</i> purchasing land and setting up residential settlements very quickly, thus subverting the planning restrictions.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Romanichal including other ethnic groups of travellers, Irish Travellers and New Age Travellers, argued in response that thousands of retrospective planning permissions are granted in Britain in cases involving non-Romani applicants each year and that statistics showed that 90% of planning applications by Romanies and travellers were initially refused by <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local councils</a>, compared with a national average of 20% for other applicants, disproving claims of preferential treatment favouring Romanies.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>They also argued that the root of the problem was that many traditional stopping-places had been barricaded off and that the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Justice_and_Public_Order_Act_1994" title="Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994">Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994</a> passed by the previous Conservative government had effectively criminalized their community, for example by removing local authorities\xe2\x80\x99 responsibility to provide sites, thus leaving the travellers with no option but to purchase unregistered new sites themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
