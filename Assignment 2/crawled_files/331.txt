href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Franchise history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Professional baseball in the Pittsburgh area began in 1876 with the organization of an independent (non-league) club based in a then-separate city called <a href="/wiki/Allegheny,_Pennsylvania" title="Allegheny, Pennsylvania">Allegheny City</a>, across the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_River" title="Allegheny River">Allegheny River</a> from Pittsburgh. The team joined the minor league <a href="/wiki/International_Association_for_Professional_Base_Ball_Players" title="International Association for Professional Base Ball Players">International Association</a> in 1877, only to fold the following season.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1882, a new Allegheny team began play as a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a>. Chartered as the Allegheny Base Ball Club of Pittsburgh,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the team was listed as "Allegheny" in the standings, and was sometimes called the "Alleghenys" (rarely the "Alleghenies") in that era\'s custom of referring to a team by its pluralized city or club name. After five mediocre seasons in the A.A., Pittsburgh became the first <a href="/wiki/Major_North_American_professional_sports_teams" class="mw-redirect" title="Major North American professional sports teams">A.A. team</a> to switch to the older <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> in 1887. At the time, <a href="/wiki/William_A._Nimick" title="William A. Nimick">William A. Nimick</a> was club president and <a href="/wiki/Horace_Phillips_(baseball)" title="Horace Phillips (baseball)">Horace Phillips</a> manager.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In those early days, the club benefited three times from mergers with defunct clubs. The A.A. club picked up a number of players from a defunct <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Buckeyes_(AA)" title="Columbus Buckeyes (AA)">team</a> in 1885.\n</p><p>The Alleghenys were severely crippled during the 1890 season, when nearly all of their stars jumped to the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Burghers" title="Pittsburgh Burghers">Pittsburgh Burghers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Players%27_League" title="Players&#39; League">Players\' League</a>. With a decimated roster, the team experienced what is still the worst season in franchise history, going 23\xe2\x80\x93113.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The battle nearly ruined McKnight, and he was forced to return his franchise to the league. However, almost immediately after this, McKnight joined the backers of the Burghers as a minority owner, which then repurchased the Pittsburgh National League franchise and rechartered it under a different corporate name. They were thus able to legally recover the services of most of the players who had jumped to the upstart league a year earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-TBT_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TBT-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The new owners also signed highly regarded second baseman <a href="/wiki/Lou_Bierbauer" title="Lou Bierbauer">Lou Bierbauer</a>, who had previously played with the AA\'s <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics_(American_Association)" title="Philadelphia Athletics (American Association)">Philadelphia Athletics</a>. The Athletics failed to include him on their reserve list, and the Alleghenys picked him up. Nonetheless, this led to loud protests by the Athletics, and in an official complaint, an AA official claimed the Alleghenys\' actions were "piratical".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This incident (which is discussed at some length in <i>The Beer and Whisky League</i>, by David Nemec, 1994) quickly accelerated into a schism between the leagues that contributed to the demise of the A.A. Although the Alleghenys were never found guilty of wrongdoing, they made sport of being denounced for being "piratical" by renaming themselves "the Pirates" for the 1891 season.<sup id="cite_ref-Piratehistory1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Piratehistory1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The nickname was first acknowledged on the team\'s uniforms in 1912.\n</p><p>After the 1899 season, the Pirates made what is arguably the best player transaction in franchise history when they picked up nearly all of the star players from the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Colonels" title="Louisville Colonels">Louisville Colonels</a>. Louisville owner <a href="/wiki/Barney_Dreyfuss" title="Barney Dreyfuss">Barney Dreyfuss</a> had been told that the Colonels were slated for elimination when the N.L. contracted from 12 to 8 teams. He secretly purchased a half-interest in the Pirates, then after the season sent nearly all of the Colonels\' stars up the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> to Pittsburgh. Since the transaction occurred before the Colonels officially folded, it was structured as a trade; the Pirates sent four relatively unknown players to Louisville.<sup id="cite_ref-TBT_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TBT-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Despite their nickname, the Pirates at least waited until after the season to pull off this blockbuster trade. This is unlike what happened in 1899 to the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Spiders" title="Cleveland Spiders">Cleveland Spiders</a> and, to a lesser extent, the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(19th_century)" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Orioles (19th century)">Baltimore Orioles</a>, who were also part of two-team ownerships. Dreyfuss later bought full control of the team and kept it until his death in 1932.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1901.E2.80.931945"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1901\xe2\x80\x931945">1901\xe2\x80\x931945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1901\xe2\x80\x931945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Bolstered by former Colonels shortstop <a href="/wiki/Honus_Wagner" title="Honus Wagner">Honus Wagner</a> (who was born and raised in the Pittsburgh area) and player/manager <a href="/wiki/Fred_Clarke" title="Fred Clarke">Fred Clarke</a>, the Pirates completely dominated the National League, in part because they lost few star players to the rival <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a>. However, owing to injuries to their starting pitchers, they lost <a href="/wiki/1903_World_Series" title="1903 World Series">the first modern World Series ever played</a>, in 1903, to <a href="/wiki/1903_Boston_Americans_season" title="1903 Boston Americans season">Boston</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deacon_Phillippe" title="Deacon Phillippe">Deacon Phillippe</a> pitched five complete games, winning three of them, but it was not enough. With largely the same star players, the Pirates would continue to be a strong team over the next few years, and won their <a href="/wiki/1909_World_Series" title="1909 World Series">first World Series title</a> in 1909, defeating the <a href="/wiki/1909_Detroit_Tigers_season" title="1909 Detroit Tigers season">Detroit Tigers</a> in seven games. The same year, the club opened <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Field" title="Forbes Field">Forbes Field</a>, which would be its home stadium for the next 61 years.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg/350px-1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg" width="350" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg/525px-1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg/700px-1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5581" data-file-height="3412" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1909_Pittsburgh_Pirates_on_a_boat_FINAL.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 1909 Pirates in a poster celebrating their National League pennant. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Chance" title="Frank Chance">Frank Chance</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_McGraw" title="John McGraw">John McGraw</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a>, two teams the Pirates beat for the pennant, are being made to walk the plank.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Pirates originally played in <a href="/wiki/Recreation_Park_(Pittsburgh)" title="Recreation Park (Pittsburgh)">Recreation</a> (Union) and <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Park_(Pittsburgh)" title="Exposition Park (Pittsburgh)">Exposition</a> Parks, all in what was then <a href="/wiki/Allegheny,_Pennsylvania" title="Allegheny, Pennsylvania">Allegheny City</a>. Allegheny City was annexed by Pittsburgh in December 1907. Accordingly, the Pirates did not play their first major league game in Pittsburgh until 1908 \xe2\x80\x93 over 25 years after their founding.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The decline of Honus Wagner, considered by many to be the greatest shortstop ever, led to a number of losing seasons, culminating in a disastrous 51\xe2\x80\x93103 record in 1917; however, veteran outfielder <a href="/wiki/Max_Carey" title="Max Carey">Max Carey</a> and young players <a href="/wiki/Pie_Traynor" title="Pie Traynor">Pie Traynor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kiki_Cuyler" title="Kiki Cuyler">Kiki Cuyler</a>, along with a remarkably deep pitching staff, brought the Pirates back into the spotlight. The Pirates recovered from a 3\xe2\x80\x931 deficit to win the <a href="/wiki/1925_World_Series" title="1925 World Series">1925 World Series</a> over the <a href="/wiki/1925_Washington_Senators_season" title="1925 Washington Senators season">Washington Senators</a>, and reached the <a href="/wiki/1927_World_Series" title="1927 World Series">1927 World Series</a> before being swept by the <a href="/wiki/1927_New_York_Yankees_season" title="1927 New York Yankees season">New York Yankees</a>, who at that time had built the most dominant team in baseball. The 1927 season was the first for the sharp-hitting combination of brothers <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Waner" title="Lloyd Waner">Lloyd Waner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Waner" title="Paul Waner">Paul Waner</a>, who along with shortstop <a href="/wiki/Arky_Vaughan" title="Arky Vaughan">Arky Vaughan</a> ensured that the Pirates had plenty of Hall of Fame-caliber position players through 1941. However, the Pirates\' crushing defeats in 1927 and 1938 (when they lost the pennant to the <a href="/wiki/1938_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1938 Chicago Cubs season">Chicago Cubs</a> in the final days of the season) were tremendous setbacks.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1946.E2.80.931957"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1946\xe2\x80\x931957">1946\xe2\x80\x931957</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1946\xe2\x80\x931957">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The post-World War II years were not kind to the Pirates, despite the presence of a genuine star in <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Kiner" title="Ralph Kiner">Ralph Kiner</a>, who led or co-led the National League in home runs for seven consecutive seasons (1946 through 1952). Kiner would be elected to the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in 1975.\n</p><p>In 1946, the long era of ownership by the <a href="/wiki/Barney_Dreyfuss" title="Barney Dreyfuss">Barney Dreyfuss</a> family came to an end when it sold the team to a syndicate that was led by <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a> businessman <a href="/wiki/Frank_E._McKinney" title="Frank E. McKinney">Frank McKinney</a>; the group also included entertainer <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a>, Pittsburgh attorney <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Johnson" title="Thomas P. Johnson">Thomas P. Johnson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>-based real estate tycoon <a href="/wiki/John_W._Galbreath" title="John W. Galbreath">John W. Galbreath</a>. The new owners seemingly scored a coup when they purchased veteran slugger <a href="/wiki/Hank_Greenberg" title="Hank Greenberg">Hank Greenberg</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> for the <a href="/wiki/1947_in_baseball" title="1947 in baseball">1947</a> season. The <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Hall-of-Fame-bound</a> Greenberg, then 36, had led the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> in home runs (44) and <a href="/wiki/Runs_batted_in" class="mw-redirect" title="Runs batted in">runs batted in</a> (127) in <a href="/wiki/1946_in_baseball" title="1946 in baseball">1946</a>, his first full season after almost five years in military service. But a contentious relationship with the Detroit front office ended his 12-year career with the Tigers after a .319 <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a>, 306 homers and 1,528 <a href="/wiki/Hit_(baseball)" title="Hit (baseball)">hits</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RalphKiner1953bowman.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/RalphKiner1953bowman.jpg/170px-RalphKiner1953bowman.jpg" width="170" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/RalphKiner1953bowman.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="393" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RalphKiner1953bowman.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1953 Bowman Color baseball card of <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Kiner" title="Ralph Kiner">Ralph Kiner</a>. Kiner led the NL in <a href="/wiki/Home_runs" class="mw-redirect" title="Home runs">home runs</a> for seven straight seasons (1946\xe2\x80\x931952), although he shared the HR title on three occasions.</div></div></div>\n<p>With Greenberg a fearsome, right-handed power hitter, the Pirates\' new ownership decided to make <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Field" title="Forbes Field">Forbes Field</a> a more inviting home run destination.  They reduced the previously cavernous dimensions of the <a href="/wiki/Left_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Left field">left field</a> and left-center-field section of the park by 30 feet (9.1&#160;m) by moving the <a href="/wiki/Bullpen" title="Bullpen">bullpens</a> to the base of the old <a href="/wiki/Left_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Left field">left field</a> wall, behind a new fence that was 335 feet (102&#160;m) from home plate down the left-field line.\n</p><p>The shorter porch was immediately dubbed "Greenberg Gardens" in honor of the former Tiger.  As events turned out, however, Greenberg\'s playing career was winding down rapidly.  He played only 125 games for the <a href="/wiki/1947_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1947 Pittsburgh Pirates season">1947 Pirates</a>, hit 25 home runs (18 at Forbes Field),<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> batted only .249, and then retired from the game.  Kiner\xe2\x80\x94who, as rookie, had led the National League in homers with 23 playing in the old Forbes Field\xe2\x80\x94became the beneficiary of the new left-field configuration, which was quickly renamed "<a href="/wiki/Kiner%27s_Korner" title="Kiner&#39;s Korner">Kiner\'s Korner</a>." He slugged 51 home runs in 1947, then followed with seasons of 40, 54, 47, 42 and 37 blasts to lead the NL, sharing the home run title with <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Mize" title="Johnny Mize">Johnny Mize</a> (1947\xe2\x80\x931948) and <a href="/wiki/Hank_Sauer" title="Hank Sauer">Hank Sauer</a> (1952).\n</p><p>While attendance figures at Forbes Field rose to among the top in the NL, the team built around Kiner placed in the <a href="/wiki/First_division_(baseball)" title="First division (baseball)">first division</a> only once \xe2\x80\x93 in 1948 \xe2\x80\x93 and in 1952 compiled one of the worst records in major league history, winning 42 and losing 112 games (.273) and finishing 54\xc2\xbd games out of first place.  The new ownership group reorganized during <a href="/wiki/1950_in_baseball" title="1950 in baseball">1950</a>, when Galbreath, Crosby and Johnson bought out McKinney\'s investment, and Galbreath became majority owner.  His family would control the destiny of the Pirates for the next 35 years.\n</p><p>Galbreath\'s first major move, the hiring of <a href="/wiki/Branch_Rickey" title="Branch Rickey">Branch Rickey</a> as <a href="/wiki/General_manager_(baseball)" title="General manager (baseball)">general manager</a> after the 1950 campaign, was initially a great disappointment to Pittsburgh fans. Rickey had invented the <a href="/wiki/Farm_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Farm system">farm system</a> with the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">Cardinals</a> and broken the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_color_line" title="Baseball color line">baseball color line</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Dodgers</a>, building dynasties with each club. In Pittsburgh, though, he purged the roster of its higher-salaried veterans (including Kiner in 1953) and flooded the team with young players. Many of those youngsters faltered, but those who fulfilled Rickey\'s faith in them \xe2\x80\x93 pitchers <a href="/wiki/Vern_Law" title="Vern Law">Vern Law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Friend" title="Bob Friend">Bob Friend</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roy_Face" title="Roy Face">Elroy Face</a>, shortstop <a href="/wiki/Dick_Groat" title="Dick Groat">Dick Groat</a>, second baseman <a href="/wiki/Bill_Mazeroski" title="Bill Mazeroski">Bill Mazeroski</a>, and especially outfielder <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Clemente" title="Roberto Clemente">Roberto Clemente</a>, drafted from Brooklyn after his only minor league season (1954) \xe2\x80\x93 would form the nucleus of the Pirates\' <a href="/wiki/1960_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1960 Pittsburgh Pirates season">1960 championship club</a>. Moreover, as in St. Louis and Brooklyn, Rickey put into place one of baseball\'s most successful farm and scouting systems, keeping the Pirates competitive into the late 1970s. However, all this was not evident when Rickey (then 73) retired due to ill health in 1955, with the Pirates still struggling to escape the NL basement.\n</p><p>"Kiner\'s Korner", meanwhile, was demolished in 1954, restoring Forbes Field to its previous, pitching-friendly contour.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1958.E2.80.931969"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1958\xe2\x80\x931969">1958\xe2\x80\x931969</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1958\xe2\x80\x931969">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 1948 team was the only postwar Pirates squad with a winning record until 1958, <a href="/wiki/Danny_Murtaugh" title="Danny Murtaugh">Danny Murtaugh</a>\'s first full season as manager. Murtaugh is widely credited with inventing the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Closer_(baseball)" title="Closer (baseball)">closer</a> by frequently playing Face late in close games. The 1960 team featured eight All-Stars, but was widely predicted to lose the World Series to a powerful <a href="/wiki/1960_New_York_Yankees_season" title="1960 New York Yankees season">New York Yankees</a> team. In one of the most memorable World Series in history, the Pirates were defeated by ten or more runs in three games, won three close games, then recovered from a 7\xe2\x80\x934 deficit late in Game 7 eventually to win on a <a href="/wiki/Walk-off_home_run" title="Walk-off home run">walk-off home run</a> by Mazeroski, a second baseman better known for defensive wizardry. The 1960 Pirates were the only team between 1945 and 2001 to have not succumbed to the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Ex-Cubs_Factor" title="Ex-Cubs Factor">Ex-Cubs Factor</a>" in the postseason. They also became the first team to win a World Series on a home run, a feat later achieved by the <a href="/wiki/1993_Toronto_Blue_Jays_season" title="1993 Toronto Blue Jays season">Toronto Blue Jays</a> in <a href="/wiki/1993_World_Series" title="1993 World Series">1993</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Joe_Carter" title="Joe Carter">Joe Carter</a>\'s home run came in Game 6 of the 1993 Series. Mazeroski\'s homer remains the only walk-off home run in Game 7 of a World Series.\n</p><p>The 1960s would continue with extremely solid defensive play by Bill Mazeroski and the great offensive and defensive abilities of Clemente, baseball\'s first <a href="/wiki/List_of_players_from_Puerto_Rico_in_Major_League_Baseball" class="mw-redirect" title="List of players from Puerto Rico in Major League Baseball">Puerto Rican</a> superstar. Clemente was regarded as one of the game\'s best all-time hitters, and possessed a tremendous arm in right field. Although not the first black-Hispanic baseball player (an honor belonging to <a href="/wiki/Minnie_Mi%C3%B1oso" title="Minnie Mi\xc3\xb1oso">Minnie Mi\xc3\xb1oso</a>), Clemente\'s charisma and leadership in humanitarian causes made him an icon across the continent. During his playing career, Clemente was often overlooked, but today many consider him to have been one of the greatest right fielders in baseball history.\n</p><p>Even with Roberto Clemente, however, the Pirates struggled to post winning marks from 1961 to 1964, and Murtaugh was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Walker" title="Harry Walker">Harry Walker</a> in 1965. With Walker, a renowned batting coach, at the helm, and the hitting of Clemente, <a href="/wiki/Matty_Alou" title="Matty Alou">Matty Alou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manny_Mota" title="Manny Mota">Manny Mota</a> and others, the Bucs fielded contending, 90+ win teams in both 1965 and 1966, with Clemente claiming the National League MVP Award in the latter year. However, Pittsburgh had no answer for the pitching of the Dodgers and the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a>, and finished third both seasons. In 1967, they fell back to .500, and did not contend through the rest of the 1960s.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1970.E2.80.931979_and_.22The_Family.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1970\xe2\x80\x931979_and_&quot;The_Family&quot;">1970\xe2\x80\x931979 and "The Family"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1970\xe2\x80\x931979 and &quot;The Family&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ThreeRiversStadium.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/ThreeRiversStadium.jpg/250px-ThreeRiversStadium.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/ThreeRiversStadium.jpg/375px-ThreeRiversStadium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/ThreeRiversStadium.jpg/500px-ThreeRiversStadium.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ThreeRiversStadium.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view of Three Rivers Stadium.</div></div></div>\n<h4><span id="1970.E2.80.931973"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1970\xe2\x80\x931973">1970\xe2\x80\x931973</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1970\xe2\x80\x931973">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Slugger <a href="/wiki/Willie_Stargell" title="Willie Stargell">Willie Stargell</a> became a fixture in the Pittsburgh lineup in the late 1960s, and the Pirates returned to prominence in 1970. Murtaugh returned as manager and the Pirates\' home field, <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Field" title="Forbes Field">Forbes Field</a>, was demolished in favor of the multi-purpose <a href="/wiki/Three_Rivers_Stadium" title="Three Rivers Stadium">Three Rivers Stadium</a>. In 1970, the Pirates won their first of five <a href="/wiki/National_League_East" title="National League East">National League East</a> division titles over the next seven years, and won their fourth <a href="/wiki/1971_World_Series" title="1971 World Series">World Series</a> in 1971 behind a .414 Series <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a> by Clemente. They also thought they had a genuine superstar pitcher (historically rare for the Pirates) in <a href="/wiki/Steve_Blass" title="Steve Blass">Steve Blass</a>, who pitched two masterful games in the World Series against <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore</a> and had excellent seasons in 1968 and 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On September 1, 1971, the Pirates made Major League Baseball history by fielding the first all-black/minority starting lineup: second baseman <a href="/wiki/Rennie_Stennett" title="Rennie Stennett">Rennie Stennett</a>, center fielder <a href="/wiki/Gene_Clines" title="Gene Clines">Gene Clines</a>, right fielder <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Clemente" title="Roberto Clemente">Roberto Clemente</a>, left fielder <a href="/wiki/Willie_Stargell" title="Willie Stargell">Willie Stargell</a>, catcher <a href="/wiki/Manny_Sanguill%C3%A9n" title="Manny Sanguill\xc3\xa9n">Manny Sanguill\xc3\xa9n</a>, third baseman <a href="/wiki/Dave_Cash_(baseball)" title="Dave Cash (baseball)">Dave Cash</a>, first baseman <a href="/wiki/Al_Oliver" title="Al Oliver">Al Oliver</a>, shortstop <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Jackie Hern\xc3\xa1ndez">Jackie Hern\xc3\xa1ndez</a>, and pitcher <a href="/wiki/Dock_Ellis" title="Dock Ellis">Dock Ellis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Manager Danny Murtaugh had never hesitated using minority players in the lineup, but even the players took notice when he posted the lineup that day.  Blass later recalled, "The thing I remember about it, when he was interviewed afterwards, Murtaugh said, \xe2\x80\x98I put the nine best athletes out there. The best nine I put out there tonight happened to be black. No big deal. Next question.\xe2\x80\x99"<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The sports world was stunned and saddened when Clemente died in a plane crash on December 31, 1972, while accompanying a shipment of relief supplies to the victims of an <a href="/wiki/1972_Nicaragua_earthquake" title="1972 Nicaragua earthquake">earthquake in Nicaragua</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tom_Walker_(1970s_pitcher)" title="Tom Walker (1970s pitcher)">Tom Walker</a>, then-<a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a> pitcher and future father of Pirates second baseman <a href="/wiki/Neil_Walker_(baseball)" title="Neil Walker (baseball)">Neil Walker</a>, offered to join Clemente that day but Clemente insisted that he stay home. Thus, Roberto Clemente had an influence on the 2013 playoff run.  He had reached a milestone by rapping his <a href="/wiki/3,000_hit_club" title="3,000 hit club">3,000th career hit</a>, a stand-up <a href="/wiki/Double_(baseball)" title="Double (baseball)">double</a> off <a href="/wiki/Jon_Matlack" title="Jon Matlack">Jon Matlack</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> on September 30, 1972, in what would prove to be his last regular-season at-bat. The <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> waived its usual waiting requirement and inducted Clemente immediately. Pittsburgh would erect a statue and name a <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Clemente_Bridge" title="Roberto Clemente Bridge">bridge</a> and park near the stadium after him, as well as a street in the Oakland neighborhood near the former site of Forbes Field.\n</p><p>In 1973, Blass suffered a mysterious decline in his pitching abilities, posting a 9.85 <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">ERA</a>. To this day, pitchers who suddenly lose the ability to throw strikes are said to have "Steve Blass disease."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Blass retired soon after; he has since, for almost two decades, been one of the Pirates\' radio and TV announcers.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="1974.E2.80.931978"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1974\xe2\x80\x931978">1974\xe2\x80\x931978</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1974\xe2\x80\x931978">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Pirates made the playoffs in 1974 and 1975, but lost the <a href="/wiki/National_League_Championship_Series" title="National League Championship Series">National League Championship Series</a> to the <a href="/wiki/1974_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1974 Los Angeles Dodgers season">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1975_Cincinnati_Reds_season" title="1975 Cincinnati Reds season">Cincinnati Reds</a>, respectively. The speedy <a href="/wiki/Omar_Moreno" title="Omar Moreno">Omar Moreno</a> and the power-hitting <a href="/wiki/Dave_Parker" title="Dave Parker">Dave Parker</a> joined Stargell in the lineup during this period. After the 1976 season, in which the Bucs finished in second place behind the cross-state <a href="/wiki/1976_Philadelphia_Phillies_season" title="1976 Philadelphia Phillies season">Philadelphia Phillies</a>, Danny Murtaugh died. The Pirates struck a trade with the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> in which catcher <a href="/wiki/Manny_Sanguill%C3%A9n" title="Manny Sanguill\xc3\xa9n">Manny Sanguill\xc3\xa9n</a> was sent to Oakland for manager <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Tanner" title="Chuck Tanner">Chuck Tanner</a>. The <a href="/wiki/1977_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1977 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pirates</a> would finish second to the Phillies once again in 1977, with Parker winning a batting title. It was also in 1977 that the Pirates began wearing yellow and black uniforms with pillbox caps. Stargell would award teammates with "Stargell Stars" on their caps for excellent plays on the field. The following year, the <a href="/wiki/1978_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1978 Pittsburgh Pirates season">Pirates</a> turned the end of the 1978 season into an impromptu race for the NL East, as they tried to chase down the collapsing <a href="/wiki/1978_Philadelphia_Phillies_season" title="1978 Philadelphia Phillies season">Phillies</a>, who ultimately won the division, only to fall short during the final home stand of the season (ironically against the Phillies). Despite this, Parker won another batting title and was named <a href="/wiki/National_League_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="National League Most Valuable Player Award">National League MVP</a> to go with it.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1979">1979</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1979">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1979_Pittsburgh_Pirates_season" title="1979 Pittsburgh Pirates season">1979 Pittsburgh Pirates season</a></div>\n<p>Adopting the popular song "<a href="/wiki/We_Are_Family_(song)" title="We Are Family (song)">We Are Family</a>" by the Philadelphia <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a> group <a href="/wiki/Sister_Sledge" title="Sister Sledge">Sister Sledge</a> as their theme song, the 1979 Pirates held off the <a href="/wiki/1979_Montreal_Expos_season" title="1979 Montreal Expos season">Montreal Expos</a> to claim the pennant. "We Are Family" was elevated from theme song to anthem status (and is still nearly synonymous with the \'79 Pirates), with fans chanting "Fam-a-lee!" from the stands. The Pirates faced the Baltimore Orioles again in the <a href="/wiki/1979_World_Series" title="1979 World Series">World Series</a>, which (like 1971) they won in seven games, on October 17, <a href="/wiki/1979_World_Series" title="1979 World Series">1979</a>. During the 1979 championship season, a Pirate player was designated as Most Valuable Player in every available category: All-Star Game MVP (Dave Parker), NL Championship Series MVP (Willie Stargell), World Series MVP (Willie Stargell), and National League MVP (Willie Stargell, shared with <a href="/wiki/Keith_Hernandez" title="Keith Hernandez">Keith Hernandez</a> of the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">Cardinals</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1980.E2.80.931985_Galbreaths_looking_to_sell"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1980\xe2\x80\x931985_Galbreaths_looking_to_sell">1980\xe2\x80\x931985 Galbreaths looking to sell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1980\xe2\x80\x931985 Galbreaths looking to sell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After three world titles and six division titles since 1948 the Galbreaths began listening to offers to sell the franchise.  <a href="/wiki/Edward_J._Lewis" title="Edward J. Lewis">Edward J. Lewis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Development" title="Oxford Development">Oxford Development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_J._DeBartolo,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Edward J. DeBartolo, Sr.">Edward J. DeBartolo, Sr.</a> made a serious offer to purchase the team in early 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Both the city of New Orleans, Louisiana and Tampa, Florida put packages together to attempt to lure the team out of Pittsburgh.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1986.E2.80.931996:_The_Leyland_era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1986\xe2\x80\x931996:_The_Leyland_era">1986\xe2\x80\x931996: The Leyland era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1986\xe2\x80\x931996: The Leyland era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the 1979 World Series, the Pirates entered a period of decline, steadily declining until they were regarded as the worst team in baseball during the mid-1980s. After the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_drug_trials" title="Pittsburgh drug trials">Pittsburgh Drug Trials</a> and a league worst finish in 1985, the Galbreath family sold the franchise to the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Associates" title="Pittsburgh Associates">Pittsburgh Associates</a>, a consortium of area businesses determined to keep the team from relocating.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jim_Leyland" title="Jim Leyland">Jim Leyland</a> took over as manager in 1986, and under his guidance the Pirates gradually climbed out of the cellar. They featured young and exciting players such as the "outfield of dreams" <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Bonilla" title="Bobby Bonilla">Bobby Bonilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andy_Van_Slyke" title="Andy Van Slyke">Andy Van Slyke</a>; infielders <a href="/wiki/Jay_Bell" title="Jay Bell">Jay Bell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Buechele" title="Steve Buechele">Steve Buechele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sid_Bream" title="Sid Bream">Sid Bream</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Lind" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Lind">Jos\xc3\xa9 Lind</a>; catcher <a href="/wiki/Mike_LaValliere" title="Mike LaValliere">Mike LaValliere</a>, and pitchers <a href="/wiki/Doug_Drabek" title="Doug Drabek">Doug Drabek</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Smiley_(baseball)" title="John Smiley (baseball)">John Smiley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stan_Belinda" title="Stan Belinda">Stan Belinda</a>.\n</p><p>As a rookie in 1982, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Ray_(baseball)" title="Johnny Ray (baseball)">Johnny Ray</a> played in every game and was named the Rookie of the Year by the Sporting News.\n</p><p>In 1988, the young team finished 85\xe2\x80\x9375 and seemed ready to compete for a pennant. However, the 1989 season was a major setback, with injuries depleting the squad and leading to a fifth-place finish. Among the low points of the season was a game against <a href="/wiki/Phillies%E2%80%93Pirates_rivalry" title="Phillies\xe2\x80\x93Pirates rivalry">rival</a> <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> in <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Stadium" title="Veterans Stadium">Philadelphia</a> on <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/B06080PHI1989.htm">June 8, 1989</a>, where the Pirates became the first team in major-league history to score 10 runs in the first inning and nevertheless lose the game.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Pirates broadcaster (and former pitcher) <a href="/wiki/Jim_Rooker" title="Jim Rooker">Jim Rooker</a> famously vowed that if the team blew the lead, he would walk home from Philadelphia\xe2\x80\x94a vow he fulfilled after the season while raising money for charity.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pirates1989.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f6/Pirates1989.jpg/220px-Pirates1989.jpg" width="220" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f6/Pirates1989.jpg/330px-Pirates1989.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f6/Pirates1989.jpg/440px-Pirates1989.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2828" data-file-height="1560" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pirates1989.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pirates clinch the Division Title in St. Louis, 1990.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Pirates would win the first three NL East titles of the 1990s, but failed to advance to the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> each time. In both <a href="/wiki/1991_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1991 National League Championship Series">1991</a> and <a href="/wiki/1992_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1992 National League Championship Series">1992</a>, they lost closely contested League Championship Series to the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a>.\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/1990_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1990 National League Championship Series">1990 National League Championship Series</a>, <a href="/wiki/1991_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1991 National League Championship Series">1991 National League Championship Series</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1992_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1992 National League Championship Series">1992 National League Championship Series</a></div>\n<h3><span id="1996.E2.80.932010:_The_Dark_Ages"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1996\xe2\x80\x932010:_The_Dark_Ages">1996\xe2\x80\x932010: The Dark Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1996\xe2\x80\x932010: The Dark Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the 1992 season, the front office set out to rebuild the team, giving up several high-payroll players in favor of a younger crew. The Pirates were unable to produce a winning season until 2013, accumulating a 20-year losing streak \xe2\x80\x93 the longest in any of the four major professional North American sports leagues.<sup id="cite_ref-17lost_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17lost-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The closest the Pirates had come to fielding a winning team during that period was the 1997 team, which finished second in the NL Central despite having a losing record and a payroll of $9 million. The 1997 team was eliminated from playoff contention during the season\'s final week. In 2012, they finished in 4th place with a 79\xe2\x80\x9383 record and were not eliminated until late September.  The 2013 team became the first Pirates team since 1992 to secure a winning season.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="2001.E2.80.932007:_A_new_ballpark"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2001\xe2\x80\x932007:_A_new_ballpark">2001\xe2\x80\x932007:  A new ballpark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2001\xe2\x80\x932007: A new ballpark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PNC_Park,_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/PNC_Park%2C_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg/220px-PNC_Park%2C_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/PNC_Park%2C_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg/330px-PNC_Park%2C_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/PNC_Park%2C_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg/440px-PNC_Park%2C_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PNC_Park,_Home_of_Pittsburgh_Pirates.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/PNC_Park" title="PNC Park">PNC Park</a> opened in 2001.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 2001, the Pirates opened a new stadium, <a href="/wiki/PNC_Park" title="PNC Park">PNC Park</a>, featuring a simple concept and strategic usage of the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> skyline.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>General manager <a href="/wiki/Dave_Littlefield" title="Dave Littlefield">Dave Littlefield</a> was installed July 13, 2001, midway through the 2001 season, and began overhauling the team to comply with owner <a href="/wiki/Kevin_McClatchy" title="Kevin McClatchy">Kevin McClatchy</a>\'s dictum to drastically reduce the payroll. Enigmatic but talented third baseman <a href="/wiki/Aramis_Ram%C3%ADrez" title="Aramis Ram\xc3\xadrez">Aramis Ram\xc3\xadrez</a> was traded to the <a href="/wiki/2003_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="2003 Chicago Cubs season">Chicago Cubs</a> in 2003 for a fairly minimal return under pressure to dump his $6 million salary for 2004, and he proceeded to become a star for the Cubs. <a href="/wiki/Brian_Giles" title="Brian Giles">Brian Giles</a> was one of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>\'s best hitters for several years, but he and his $9 million salary were also traded in 2003 to the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> for youngsters <a href="/wiki/%C3%93liver_P%C3%A9rez" title="\xc3\x93liver P\xc3\xa9rez">\xc3\x93liver P\xc3\xa9rez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Bay" title="Jason Bay">Jason Bay</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cory_Stewart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cory Stewart (page does not exist)">Cory Stewart</a>. Pirate fans found this trade much more palatable in the short run, as P\xc3\xa9rez led the majors in <a href="/wiki/Strikeouts" class="mw-redirect" title="Strikeouts">strikeouts</a> per inning and Bay won the <a href="/wiki/MLB_Rookie_of_the_Year_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="MLB Rookie of the Year Award">Rookie of the Year Award</a> in 2004, while Giles put up a subpar season by his standards. After the 2004 season, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Kendall" title="Jason Kendall">Jason Kendall</a> went to the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> in a cross-exchange of high-salary players.\n</p><p>Illustrating the Pirates\' rebuilding efforts, at the close of the 2005 season, the team fielded the youngest roster in baseball, with an average age of 26.6 years. During the course of the season, 14 players were called up from its Triple-A affiliate. On September 6, manager <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_McClendon" title="Lloyd McClendon">Lloyd McClendon</a> was fired after 5 losing seasons. On October 11, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Tracy_(baseball)" title="Jim Tracy (baseball)">Jim Tracy</a> was hired as the new manager.  The 2006 season got off to a slow start with the Pirates losing their first six games.  The Bucs stood at an abysmal 30\xe2\x80\x9360 mark by the time they hosted the <a href="/wiki/2006_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="2006 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All Star Game</a> at PNC Park. During the second half of the season, however, the Pirates made a successful turnaround and finished the second half with a 37\xe2\x80\x9335 record. This is the first time the Pirates have finished the second half of the season with a winning record since 1992. Third baseman <a href="/wiki/Freddy_Sanchez" title="Freddy Sanchez">Freddy Sanchez</a> won the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Major_League_Baseball_batting_champions" title="List of Major League Baseball batting champions">National League batting title</a> for the 2006 season with an average of .344.\n</p><p>The 2007 season was a year of transition for the Pirates. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nutting" title="Robert Nutting">Robert Nutting</a> replaced McClatchy as majority owner, becoming the sixth majority owner in Pirates history. On July 6, 2007, Kevin McClatchy announced he was stepping down as the Pirates CEO at the end of the 2007 season.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  On September 7, 2007, Nutting fired general manager <a href="/wiki/Dave_Littlefield" title="Dave Littlefield">Dave Littlefield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="2008.E2.80.932010:_Nutting_takes_over"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2008\xe2\x80\x932010:_Nutting_takes_over">2008\xe2\x80\x932010: Nutting takes over</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2008\xe2\x80\x932010: Nutting takes over">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Pittsburgh Pirates began to shape their organizational management team late in the 2007 season. On September 13, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Coonelly" title="Frank Coonelly">Frank Coonelly</a>, chief labor counsel for Major League Baseball, was introduced as the team\'s new president.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> On September 25, 2007, the Pirates announced the hiring of <a href="/wiki/Neal_Huntington" title="Neal Huntington">Neal Huntington</a>, formerly a scout in the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> organization, as the team\'s new general manager.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> On October 5, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Tracy_(baseball)" title="Jim Tracy (baseball)">Jim Tracy</a> was fired by the Pirates, leaving them with another search for a manager. <a href="/wiki/Torey_Lovullo" title="Torey Lovullo">Torey Lovullo</a> had originally been named as a leading candidate for the position,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> but his name was gradually replaced by others in the minor league ranks, one being <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Lynx" title="Ottawa Lynx">Ottawa Lynx</a> manager <a href="/wiki/John_Russell_(catcher)" title="John Russell (catcher)">John Russell</a>, who eventually was named the new manager November 5, 2007. He had originally been the third base coach under previous manager <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_McClendon" title="Lloyd McClendon">Lloyd McClendon</a> from 2003 to 2005 until he was fired by the previous General Manager <a href="/wiki/Dave_Littlefield" title="Dave Littlefield">Dave Littlefield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Karstens_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karstens_2.jpg/220px-Karstens_2.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karstens_2.jpg/330px-Karstens_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karstens_2.jpg/440px-Karstens_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2787" data-file-height="1837" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Karstens_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Pirates-Nationals game in 2010</div></div></div>\n<p>As the Pirates once again failed to produce a winning record, the team began another round of rebuilding. Prior to the trade deadline, the Pirates made several deals that sent several accomplished veterans to other franchises. On July 26,  the Pirates traded <a href="/wiki/Left_fielder" title="Left fielder">left fielder</a> <a href="/wiki/Xavier_Nady" title="Xavier Nady">Xavier Nady</a> and pitcher <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1maso_Marte" title="D\xc3\xa1maso Marte">D\xc3\xa1maso Marte</a> to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> in return for <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_T%C3%A1bata" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 T\xc3\xa1bata">Jos\xc3\xa9 T\xc3\xa1bata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ohlendorf" title="Ross Ohlendorf">Ross Ohlendorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dan_McCutchen" class="mw-redirect" title="Dan McCutchen">Dan McCutchen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Karstens" title="Jeff Karstens">Jeff Karstens</a>. Karstens began his career with the Pirates at 2\xe2\x80\x930 and came within 4 outs of pitching the first perfect game in franchise history on August 6, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> On July 31, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Bay" title="Jason Bay">Jason Bay</a> was traded to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> in a three-team deal that sent <a href="/wiki/Manny_Ram%C3%ADrez" class="mw-redirect" title="Manny Ram\xc3\xadrez">Manny Ram\xc3\xadrez</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andy_LaRoche" title="Andy LaRoche">Andy LaRoche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Morris" title="Bryan Morris">Bryan Morris</a> to the Pirates from the Dodgers and <a href="/wiki/Brandon_Moss" title="Brandon Moss">Brandon Moss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Craig_Hansen" title="Craig Hansen">Craig Hansen</a> to the Pirates from the Red Sox.  On November 24, the Pirates signed <a href="/wiki/Rinku_Singh" title="Rinku Singh">Rinku Singh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinesh_Patel" title="Dinesh Patel">Dinesh Patel</a> as undrafted free agents, making them the first Indian citizens to sign a contract with any American professional sports team.<sup id="cite_ref-pittsburgh.pirates.mlb.com_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pittsburgh.pirates.mlb.com-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Both men are pitchers, who were first spotted in the "Million Dollar Arm" contest organized in India by J.B. Bernstein earlier in 2008.  Trading away players for prospects continued in 2009, as the team\'s only 2008 All-Star <a href="/wiki/Nate_McLouth" title="Nate McLouth">Nate McLouth</a> was traded to the Atlanta Braves for prospects <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Locke_(baseball)" title="Jeff Locke (baseball)">Jeff Locke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Morton_(pitcher)" title="Charlie Morton (pitcher)">Charlie Morton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gorkys_Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Gorkys Hern\xc3\xa1ndez">Gorkys Hern\xc3\xa1ndez</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> On June 30, the team dealt <a href="/wiki/Nyjer_Morgan" title="Nyjer Morgan">Nyjer Morgan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sean_Burnett" title="Sean Burnett">Sean Burnett</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a>, as well as sending utility player <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hinske" title="Eric Hinske">Eric Hinske</a> to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>. This upset some Pirates players, including <a href="/wiki/Adam_LaRoche" title="Adam LaRoche">Adam LaRoche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Wilson_(shortstop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jack Wilson (shortstop)">Jack Wilson</a>, who questioned the direction of the team.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  LaRoche was later traded to the Red Sox in exchange for minor leaguers <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Strickland" title="Hunter Strickland">Hunter Strickland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argenis_D%C3%ADaz" title="Argenis D\xc3\xadaz">Argenis D\xc3\xadaz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> On July 29, Wilson was traded to the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> in exchange for shortstop <a href="/wiki/Ronny_Cede%C3%B1o" title="Ronny Cede\xc3\xb1o">Ronny Cede\xc3\xb1o</a> and Minor League players <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Clement" title="Jeff Clement">Jeff Clement</a>, Aaron Pribanic, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brett_Lorin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brett Lorin (page does not exist)">Brett Lorin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Adcock_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nathan Adcock (baseball)">Nathan Adcock</a>. The same day, the Pirates traded Sanchez to the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> in exchange for <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tim_Alderson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tim Alderson (page does not exist)">Tim Alderson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> On September 7, the Pittsburgh Pirates were defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> 4\xe2\x80\x932. The loss was the Pirates\' 82nd of the year, and it clinched for them the longest streak of consecutive losing seasons in any North American professional sport.<sup id="cite_ref-17lost_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17lost-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="2011.E2.80.93present:_Clint_Hurdle_era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2011\xe2\x80\x93present:_Clint_Hurdle_era">2011\xe2\x80\x93present: Clint Hurdle era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 2011\xe2\x80\x93present: Clint Hurdle era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On November 14, 2010, after a disastrous 2010 season, which saw 105 losses, the Pirates hired a new manager in <a href="/wiki/Clint_Hurdle" title="Clint Hurdle">Clint Hurdle</a>. Hurdle was a part of the Colorado Rockies 2007 NL Pennant and was the hitting coach for the American League Champion Texas Rangers during the 2010 season. Although the losing streak would continue for two more seasons, the Pirates began to show signs of success. By 2012 it seemed the Pirates were on pace to break the streak, but unfortunately a second late season collapse resulted in the 20th consecutive losing season. The team had added free agent pitcher <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Liriano" title="Francisco Liriano">Francisco Liriano</a> but his arrival to the team was delayed by a broken non-throwing arm. The Pirates entered the <a href="/wiki/2013_MLB_All_Star_Game" class="mw-redirect" title="2013 MLB All Star Game">2013 MLB All Star Game</a> with a record of 56-37 and had sent four all stars (<a href="/wiki/Andrew_McCutchen" title="Andrew McCutchen">Andrew McCutchen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Grilli" title="Jason Grilli">Jason Grilli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvarez_(baseball)" title="Pedro \xc3\x81lvarez (baseball)">Pedro \xc3\x81lvarez</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Locke_(baseball)" title="Jeff Locke (baseball)">Jeff Locke</a>). The Pirates finished the season 94-68 and clinched a wild card berth. They eventually were eliminated by the NL champion <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>. McCutchen received the <a href="/wiki/NL_MVP" class="mw-redirect" title="NL MVP">NL MVP</a> and Pedro Alvarez was tied for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=NL_Leader_in_home_runs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="NL Leader in home runs (page does not exist)">NL Leader in home runs</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="2011.E2.80.932012:_Late_season_collapses"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2011\xe2\x80\x932012:_Late_season_collapses">2011\xe2\x80\x932012: Late season collapses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2011\xe2\x80\x932012: Late season collapses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kevin_Correia_on_June_13,_2012.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kevin_Correia_on_June_13%2C_2012.jpg/220px-Kevin_Correia_on_June_13%2C_2012.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kevin_Correia_on_June_13%2C_2012.jpg/330px-Kevin_Correia_on_June_13%2C_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kevin_Correia_on_June_13%2C_2012.jpg/440px-Kevin_Correia_on_June_13%2C_2012.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="1100" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kevin_Correia_on_June_13,_2012.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kevin_Correia" title="Kevin Correia">Kevin Correia</a>, June 2012</div></div></div>\n<p>The 2011 season had a promising start, as the Pirates were above .500 at the All-Star Break for the first time since 1992. The Pirates also sent three players to the <a href="/wiki/2011_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="2011 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">2011 Major League Baseball All-Star Game</a> with the selections of starting pitcher <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Correia" title="Kevin Correia">Kevin Correia</a>, closer <a href="/wiki/Joel_Hanrahan" title="Joel Hanrahan">Joel Hanrahan</a> and center fielder <a href="/wiki/Andrew_McCutchen" title="Andrew McCutchen">Andrew McCutchen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\nOn July 15, and again on July 18, the Pirates moved into first place of the NL Central. This marked the first two times that the Pirates were in first place this late in the season since 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  The last day they were in first was July 25. The Bucs split four games with the Braves on July 25 to July 28. After this they lost 10 games in a row and never recovered. Starting in late July rumblings of "<a href="/wiki/America%27s_Team" title="America&#39;s Team">America\'s Team</a>" began to surface, with <a href="/wiki/ESPN_Sunday_Night_Baseball" class="mw-redirect" title="ESPN Sunday Night Baseball">ESPN</a> television picking up two of their games for their nightly baseball telecast. The Pirates acquired two former all-stars through the trade market during the Trade Deadline on July 31: First baseman Derrek Lee from the Orioles and outfielder Ryan Ludwick from the Padres.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  On July 26, a questionable call at home plate led to a 19-inning loss against the Atlanta Braves. The Pirates then won only one game from that point to August 8, including a season-high 10-game losing streak in that span. Despite a promising season, with a loss to the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> on September 14, 2011, the team lost its 82nd game of the season, ensuring a 19-year losing season streak.\n</p><p>In 2012, the Pirates aggressively pursued and acquired Yankees pitcher <a href="/wiki/A._J._Burnett" title="A. J. Burnett">A. J. Burnett</a> to bolster their starting pitching staff.  During the first few months of the season, team pitching held up very well, but the offense was sluggish.  However, the bats began to pick up in June as many of the players began to dramatically improve offensively. By the time July hit, the Pirates had soared to first place in the NL Central at the All Star break \xe2\x80\x93 the first time the Bucs entered the break at first place since the "Freak Show" team of 1997. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_McCutchen" title="Andrew McCutchen">Andrew McCutchen</a> would lead the majors in batting average and the "<a href="/wiki/Zoltan_(hand_gesture)" title="Zoltan (hand gesture)">Power of Zoltan</a>" (reference to a character scene in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Dude,_Where%27s_My_Car%3F" title="Dude, Where&#39;s My Car?">Dude, Where\'s My Car?</a></i>) began to sweep PNC Park and the city of Pittsburgh.\n</p><p>The Pirates were 63\xe2\x80\x9347 on August 8, but a stunning disintegration left the team scuffling down the stretch, effectively eliminating them from the NL Central race and the Wild card race. On September 28, the Pirates were no-hit by Cincinnati Reds pitcher <a href="/wiki/Homer_Bailey" title="Homer Bailey">Homer Bailey</a>; the loss was their 81st of the season, ensuring their 20th consecutive non-winning season. They lost their 82nd game on September 30, clinching their 20th consecutive losing season, extending the longest such streak in North American sports history.  They are also the only Major League Baseball team to be 16 games over .500 after \xe2\x85\x94 of the season complete and finish with a losing record. The 82nd loss came 4\xe2\x80\x933 to the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> at PNC Park on September 30, with the Reds scoring twice in the ninth inning off of All-Star closer <a href="/wiki/Joel_Hanrahan" title="Joel Hanrahan">Joel Hanrahan</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="2013.E2.80.932015:_Return_to_the_Playoffs"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2013\xe2\x80\x932015:_Return_to_the_Playoffs">2013\xe2\x80\x932015: Return to the Playoffs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Pittsburgh_Pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2013\xe2\x80\x932015: Return to the Playoffs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>After two consecutive seasons of late season collapses, Huntington and Hurdle were determined to be aggressive in free agency, adding players and veteran leadership that would stave off another Pittsburgh Pirates collapse in 2013. In the offseason, the team agreed with veteran catcher <a href="/wiki/Russell_Martin" title="Russell Martin">Russell Martin</a> on a two-year, $17 million deal. Martin, while providing decent offensive numbers, was considered to be a major defensive upgrade over the previous year\'s platoon of <a href="/wiki/Rod_Barajas" title="Rod Barajas">Rod Barajas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_McKenry" title="Michael McKenry">Michael McKenry</a>. In 2012, Barajas and McKenry had combined to throw out 19 of 173 baserunners, for a caught stealing percentage of 11%, while Russell Martin held a career caught stealing percentage of 30.25% entering the 2013 season. On December 26, 2012, the Pirates traded established closer <a href="/wiki/Joel_Hanrahan" title="Joel Hanrahan">Joel Hanrahan</a> and infielder <a href="/wiki/Brock_Holt" title="Brock Holt">Brock Holt</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> for reliever <a href="/wiki/Mark_Melancon" title="Mark Melancon">Mark Melancon</a>, and prospects Jerry Sands, Iv\xc3\xa1n DeJes\xc3\xbas Jr., and Stolmy Pimentel. With this move, the Pirates re-signed setup man <a href="/wiki/Jason_Grilli" title="Jason Grilli">Jason Grilli</a> to a two-year deal worth $4.5 million, to become the new Pirates closer. Melancon, who was under-valued after a miserable, injury-riddled 2012 season, replaced Grilli as the Pirates setup man. The trade wound up leaning strongly in favor of the Pirates, as Grilli and Melancon would anchor the so-called "Shark Tank" bullpen as one of the best bullpens in MLB as well as both being selected to the <a href="/wiki/2013_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="2013 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a>, while Hanrahan wound up only playing in nine games for the Red Sox before suffering an injury to his pitching arm and requiring <a href="/wiki/Tommy_John_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tommy John surgery">Tommy John surgery</a> just as his contract was getting ready to expire at the end of the season. Ultimately, Hanrahan would never pitch in the Majors again.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On February 8, 2013, the Pirates finalized a two-year, $12.75 million deal with starting pitcher <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Liriano" title="Francisco Liriano">Francisco Liriano</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The deal had been delayed two months because Liriano had broken his non-throwing arm in December 2012, and had refused to take a physical.\n</p><p>Though the Pirates made no trades before the July 31 deadline, they would make some moves to shore up their lineup for a potential playoff run, acquiring <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Byrd" title="Marlon Byrd">Marlon Byrd</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Buck_(baseball)" title="John Buck (baseball)">John Buck</a> from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> on August 27, 2013<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  and trading with the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins" title="Minnesota Twins">Minnesota Twins</a> for <a href="/wiki/Justin_Morneau" title="Justin Morneau">Justin Morneau</a> on August 31, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On September 3, 2013, the Pirates defeated the Milwaukee Brewers 4-3 in Milwaukee to win their 81st game of the season, clinching their first non-losing season since 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Then, following a four-game losing streak, the Pirates defeated the Texas Rangers 1-0 on September 9 for their 82nd win, clinching their first winning season in 21 years.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> 14 days later, due to a combination of a Pirates win and a <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Nationals</a>\' loss to the St. Louis Cardinals, the Pirates headed to postseason play for the first time in twenty-one seasons. By finishing the season with a three-game sweep of the Cincinnati Reds, their chief competition for the NL Wild Card, the Pirates hosted and won their first home playoff game since 1992 in the National League Wild Card Game on October 1, 2013 with a score of 6-2.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>That victory put the Pirates into the National League Division Series against the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a>.  On October 3, 2013, the Cardinals rode a 7-run inning to a 9-1 win over the Pirates.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The Pirates evened the series with a 7-1 victory on in Game 2 on October 4,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> tying the series heading into Game 3. The Pirates then took a 2-1 Series lead by defeating the Cardinals in dramatic fashion at PNC Park 5-3 before dropping the remaining two games to the Cardinals by scores of 2-1 and 6-1, thereby being eliminated from further post-season play.\n</p><p>In 2014, the Pirates made the National League Wild Card game but lost at home to the eventual World Series champions, the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> by a score of 8-0.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Brandon_Crawford" title="Brandon Crawford">Brandon Crawford</a> hit a grand slam while the Pirates could not recover and were once again denied a playoff series win.\n</p><p>In 2015, the Pirates finished with the second best record in baseball at 98-64, however, despite many injuries to key players, the division rival St. Louis Cardinals had the best record in baseball at 100-62, winning the NL Central division. The 98-win season was good enough to ensure another home Wild Card game, this time hosting the 97-win Chicago Cubs. The eventual 2015 NL Cy Young winner, <a href="/wiki/Jake_Arrieta" title="Jake Arrieta">Jake Arrieta</a>, pitched dominantly for the Cubs, hurling a complete game shutout, handing the Pirates a 4-0 loss.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
