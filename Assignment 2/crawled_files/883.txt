It has become an influential romance and tragedy, retold in numerous sources with many variations. The tragic story is of the adulterous love between the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornish</a> knight <a href="/wiki/Tristan" title="Tristan">Tristan</a> (Tristram) and the Irish princess <a href="/wiki/Iseult" title="Iseult">Iseult</a> (Isolde, Yseult, etc.). \n</p><p>The narrative predates and most likely influenced the romance of <a href="/wiki/Lancelot" title="Lancelot">Lancelot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guinevere" title="Guinevere">Guinevere</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_Britain" title="Matter of Britain">Matter of Britain</a> and has had a substantial effect on <a href="/wiki/Art_of_Europe" title="Art of Europe">Western art</a>, the idea of <a href="/wiki/Romance_(love)" title="Romance (love)">romantic love</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Western_literature" title="Western literature">Western literature</a> since it first appeared in the <a href="/wiki/12th_century" title="12th century">12th century</a>. While the details of the story differ from one author to another, the overall plot structure remains much the same.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are two main traditions of the <a href="/wiki/Tristan" title="Tristan">Tristan</a> legend. The early tradition comprised the French romances of two poets from the second half of the 12th century, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_of_Britain" title="Thomas of Britain">Thomas of Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9roul" title="B\xc3\xa9roul">B\xc3\xa9roul</a>. Later traditions come from the <a href="/wiki/Prose_Tristan" title="Prose Tristan">Prose <i>Tristan</i></a> (c. 1240), which was markedly different from the earlier tales written by Thomas and B\xc3\xa9roul. The Prose <i>Tristan</i> became the common medieval tale of Tristan and <a href="/wiki/Iseult" title="Iseult">Iseult</a> that would provide the background for the writings of Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malory" title="Thomas Malory">Thomas Malory</a>, the English author who wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Morte_d%27Arthur" title="Le Morte d&#39;Arthur">Le Morte d\'Arthur</a></i> (c. 1469).\n</p><p>The story and character of Tristan vary from poet to poet. Even the spelling of his name varies a great deal, although "Tristan" is the most popular spelling. Most versions of the Tristan story follow the same general outline.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg/220px-Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg/330px-Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg/440px-Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg 2x" data-file-width="542" data-file-height="360" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rogelio_de_Egusquiza_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Rogelio_de_Egusquiza" title="Rogelio de Egusquiza">Rogelio de Egusquiza</a>\'s <i>Tristan und Isolde</i> (1915)</div></div></div>\n<p>After defeating the Irish knight <a href="/wiki/Morholt" class="mw-redirect" title="Morholt">Morholt</a>, Tristan travels to <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Ireland</a> to bring back the fair Iseult for his uncle, King <a href="/wiki/Mark_of_Cornwall" title="Mark of Cornwall">Mark of Cornwall</a>, to marry. Along the way, they ingest a love potion which causes the pair to fall madly in love. In the courtly version, the potion\'s effects last a lifetime, but, in the common versions, the potion\'s effects wane after three years. In some versions, they ingest the potion accidentally; in others, the potion\'s maker instructs Iseult to share it with Mark, but she deliberately gives it to Tristan instead. Although Iseult marries Mark, she and Tristan are forced by the potion to seek one another, as lovers. While the typical noble Arthurian character would be shamed by such an act, the love potion that controls them frees Tristan and Iseult from responsibility. The king\'s advisors repeatedly endeavour to have the pair tried for adultery, but the couple continually use trickery to preserve their fa\xc3\xa7ade of innocence. In B\xc3\xa9roul\'s version, the love potion eventually wears off, and the two lovers are free to make their own choice as to whether to cease their adulterous relationship or to continue.\n</p><p>As with the Arthur-Lancelot-Guinevere love triangle, Tristan, King Mark, and Iseult of Ireland all love each other. Tristan honours, respects, and loves King Mark as his mentor and adopted father; Iseult is grateful that Mark is kind to her; and Mark loves Tristan as his son and Iseult as a wife. But every night, each has horrible dreams about the future. Tristan\'s uncle eventually learns of the affair and seeks to entrap his nephew and his bride. Also present is the endangerment of a fragile kingdom, the cessation of war between Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>). Mark acquires what seems proof of their guilt and resolves to punish them: Tristan by hanging and Iseult by burning at the stake, later lodging her in a <a href="/wiki/Leper_colony" title="Leper colony">leper colony</a>. Tristan escapes on his way to the gallows. He makes a miraculous leap from a chapel and rescues Iseult. The lovers escape into the forest of Morrois and take shelter there until discovered by Mark. They make peace with Mark after Tristan\'s agreement to return Iseult of Ireland to Mark and leave the country. Tristan then travels to <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, where he marries (for her name and her beauty) Iseult of the White Hands, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Hoel" title="Hoel">Hoel</a> of Brittany and sister of <a href="/wiki/Kahedin" title="Kahedin">Kahedin</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg/220px-Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg" width="220" height="276" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg/330px-Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg/440px-Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1587" data-file-height="1994" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boys_King_Arthur_-_N._C._Wyeth_-_p190.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1922 illustration by <a href="/wiki/N._C._Wyeth" title="N. C. Wyeth">N. C. Wyeth</a> <br /><small> "King Mark slew the noble knight Sir Tristram as he sat harping before his lady la Belle Isolde."</small></div></div></div>\n<p>In the Prose <i>Tristan</i> and works derived from it, Tristan is mortally wounded by Mark, who treacherously strikes Tristan with a poisoned lance while the latter is playing a harp for Iseult. The poetic versions of the Tristan legend offer a very different account of the hero\'s death. According to Thomas\' version, Tristan was wounded by a poison lance while attempting to rescue a young woman from six knights. Tristan sends his friend Kahedin to find Iseult of Ireland, the only person who can heal him. Tristan tells Kahedin to sail back with white sails if he is bringing Iseult, and black sails if he is not. Iseult agrees to return to Tristan with Kahedin, but Tristan\'s jealous wife, Iseult of the White Hands, lies to Tristan about the colour of the sails. Tristan dies of grief, thinking that Iseult has betrayed him, and Iseult dies swooning over his corpse. Several versions of the Prose <i>Tristan</i> include the traditional account of Tristan\'s death found in the poetic versions. \n</p><p>In French sources, such as those carefully picked over and then given in English by the well-sourced and best-selling Belloc translation of 1903, it is stated that a thick bramble briar grows out of Tristan\'s grave, growing so much that it forms a bower and roots itself into Iseult\'s grave. It goes on that King Mark tries to have the branches cut three separate times, and each time the branches grow back and intertwine. This behaviour of briars would have been very familiar to medieval people who worked on the land. Later tellings sweeten this aspect of the story, by having Tristan\'s grave grow a briar, but Iseult\'s grave grow a rose tree, which then intertwine with each other. Further tellings refine this aspect even more, with the two plants being said to have been <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honeysuckle" title="Honeysuckle">honeysuckle</a>.\n</p><p>A few later stories even record that the lovers had a number of children. In some stories they produced a son and a daughter they named after themselves; these children survived their parents and had adventures of their own. In the romance <i>Ysaie the Sad</i>, the eponymous hero is the son of Tristan and Iseult; he becomes involved with the <a href="/wiki/Fairy" title="Fairy">fairy</a> king <a href="/wiki/Oberon" title="Oberon">Oberon</a> and marries a girl named Martha, who bears him a son named Mark.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_the_legend">Origins of the legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins of the legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_and_Western">Persian and Western</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Persian and Western">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are many theories present about the origins of Tristanian legend, but historians disagree over which is the most accurate. Some scholars suggest that the 11th-century <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian</a> story <i><a href="/wiki/Vis_and_R%C4%81min" title="Vis and R\xc4\x81min">Vis and R\xc4\x81min</a></i> must have been the model for the Tristan legend because the similarities are too great to be coincidental.<sup id="cite_ref-Roman_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roman-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-D\xc4\xabn_Gurg\xc4\x81n\xc4\xab_2008_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D\xc4\xabn_Gurg\xc4\x81n\xc4\xab_2008-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The evidence for the Persian origin of Tristan and Iseult is very circumstantial<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and different theories have been suggested how this Persian story might have reached the West, some suggesting story-telling exchanges during the crusades in Syrian court<sup id="cite_ref-D\xc4\xabn_Gurg\xc4\x81n\xc4\xab_2008_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D\xc4\xabn_Gurg\xc4\x81n\xc4\xab_2008-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and through minstrels who had free access to both <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusader</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saracen" title="Saracen">Saracen</a> camps in the Holy Land.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is a "<a href="/wiki/Mark_of_Cornwall#The_Drustanus_Stone" title="Mark of Cornwall">Drustanus Stone</a>" in Cornwall with an inscription referring to <i>Drustan</i>, but not all historians agree that the Drustan referred to is the archetype of Tristan. There are references to <i>March ap Meichion</i> ("Mark") and <i>Trystan</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Triads" title="Welsh Triads">Welsh Triads</a>, in some of the <a href="/wiki/Gnomic_poetry" title="Gnomic poetry">gnomic poetry</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mabinogion" title="Mabinogion">Mabinogion</a></i> stories and in the 11th-century <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiography</a> of <a href="/wiki/Illtud" title="Illtud">Illtud</a>.\n</p><p>A character called Drystan appears as one of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>\'s advisers at the end of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dream_of_Rhonabwy" title="The Dream of Rhonabwy">The Dream of Rhonabwy</a></i>, an early 13th-century tale in the Welsh prose collection known as the <i>Mabinogion</i>, and Iseult is listed along with other great men and women of Arthur\'s court in another, much earlier <i>Mabinogion</i> tale, <i><a href="/wiki/Culhwch_and_Olwen" title="Culhwch and Olwen">Culhwch and Olwen</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogues">Analogues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analogues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Possible Irish antecedents to the Tristan legend have received much scholarly attention. An ill-fated <i>triant\xc3\xa1n an ghr\xc3\xa1</i> or love triangle features into a number of Irish works, most notably in the text called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pursuit_of_Diarmuid_and_Gr%C3%A1inne" title="The Pursuit of Diarmuid and Gr\xc3\xa1inne">T\xc3\xb3raigheacht Dhiarmada agus Ghr\xc3\xa1inne</a></i> or <i>The Pursuit of <a href="/wiki/Diarmuid_Ua_Duibhne" title="Diarmuid Ua Duibhne">Diarmuid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A1inne" title="Gr\xc3\xa1inne">Gr\xc3\xa1inne</a></i>. In the story, the aging <a href="/wiki/Fionn_mac_Cumhaill" title="Fionn mac Cumhaill">Fionn mac Cumhaill</a> takes the young princess, Gr\xc3\xa1inne, to be his wife. At the betrothal ceremony, however, she falls in love with Diarmuid, one of Fionn\'s most trusted warriors. Gr\xc3\xa1inne gives a <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_potion" class="mw-redirect" title="Sleeping potion">sleeping potion</a> to all present but him, eventually convincing him to elope with her. The fugitive lovers are then pursued all over Ireland by the <a href="/wiki/Fianna" title="Fianna">Fianna</a>. Another Irish analogue is <i>Sc\xc3\xa9la Cano meic Gartn\xc3\xa1in</i>, preserved in the 14th-century <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Book_of_Lecan" title="Yellow Book of Lecan">Yellow Book of Lecan</a>. In this tale, Cano is an exiled Scottish king who accepts the hospitality of King Marcan of Ui Maile. His young wife, Credd, drugs all present, and then convinces Cano to be her lover. They try to keep a tryst while at Marcan\'s court, but are frustrated by courtiers. Eventually Credd kills herself and Cano dies of grief. In the Ulster Cycle there is the text <i>Clann Uisnigh</i> or <i>Deirdre of the Sorrows</i> in which <a href="/wiki/Naoise" title="Naoise">Naoise mac Usnech</a> falls for Deirdre, who was imprisoned by King <a href="/wiki/Conchobar_mac_Nessa" title="Conchobar mac Nessa">Conchobar mac Nessa</a> due to a prophecy that <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> would plunge into civil war due to men fighting for her beauty. Conchobar had pledged to marry Deirdre himself in time to avert war, and takes his revenge on Clann Uisnigh. The death of Naoise and his kin leads many Ulstermen to defect to <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a>, including Conchobar\'s stepfather and trusted ally <a href="/wiki/Fergus_mac_R%C3%B3ich" title="Fergus mac R\xc3\xb3ich">Fergus mac R\xc3\xb3ich</a>, eventually precipitating the <i><a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1in_B%C3%B3_C%C3%BAailnge" title="T\xc3\xa1in B\xc3\xb3 C\xc3\xbaailnge">T\xc3\xa1in B\xc3\xb3 C\xc3\xbaailnge</a></i>.\n</p><p>Some scholars believe <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pyramus_and_Thisbe" title="Pyramus and Thisbe">Pyramus and Thisbe</a></i>, as well as the story of <a href="/wiki/Ariadne" title="Ariadne">Ariadne at Naxos</a> might have also contributed to the development of the Tristan legend.<sup id="cite_ref-Roman_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roman-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The sequence in which Tristan and Iseult die and become interwoven trees also parallels Ovid\'s love story of <a href="/wiki/Baucis_and_Philemon" title="Baucis and Philemon">Baucis and Philemon</a> in which two lovers are transformed in death into two different trees sprouting from the same trunk. However this also occurs in the saga of Deidre of the Sorrows making the link more tenuous.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_with_King_Arthur">Association with King Arthur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Association with King Arthur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In its early stages, the tale was probably unrelated to contemporary Arthurian literature,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but the earliest surviving versions already incorporated references to Arthur and his court. The connection between Tristan and Iseult and the Arthurian legend was expanded over time, and sometime shortly after the completion of the <a href="/wiki/Lancelot-Grail" title="Lancelot-Grail">Vulgate Cycle</a> (or the Lancelot-Grail) in the first quarter of the 13th century, two authors created the vast Prose <i>Tristan</i>, which fully establishes Tristan as a <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_the_Round_Table" title="Knights of the Round Table">Knight of the Round Table</a> who even participates in the Quest for the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_medieval_Tristan_literature">Early medieval Tristan literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early medieval Tristan literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Courtly_branch">Courtly branch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Courtly branch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The earliest representation of what scholars name the "courtly" version of the Tristan legend is in the work of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_of_Britain" title="Thomas of Britain">Thomas of Britain</a>, dating from 1173. Only ten fragments of his <i>Tristan</i> poem, representing six manuscripts, have ever been located: the manuscripts in Turin and Strassburg are now lost, leaving two in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, one in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> and one in <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Roman_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roman-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In his text, Thomas names another <i><a href="/wiki/Trouv%C3%A8re" title="Trouv\xc3\xa8re">trouv\xc3\xa8re</a></i> who also sang of Tristan, though no manuscripts of this earlier version have been discovered. There is also a passage telling how Iseult wrote a short <a href="/wiki/Lai_(poetic_form)" title="Lai (poetic form)">lai</a> out of grief that sheds light on the development of an unrelated legend concerning the death of a prominent <a href="/wiki/Troubadour" title="Troubadour">troubadour</a>, as well as the composition of lais by noblewomen of the 12th century.\n</p><p>The next essential text for knowledge of the courtly branch of the Tristan legend is the abridged translation of Thomas made by <a href="/wiki/Brother_Robert" title="Brother Robert">Brother Robert</a> at the request of King <a href="/wiki/Haakon_IV_of_Norway" title="Haakon IV of Norway">Haakon Haakonson</a> of Norway in 1227. King Haakon had wanted to promote <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin</a>-Norman culture at his court, and so commissioned the translation of several French Arthurian works. The Nordic version presents a complete, direct narrative of the events in Thomas\' Tristan, with the telling omission of his numerous interpretive diversions. It is the only complete representative of the courtly branch in its formative period.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Preceding the work of Brother Robert chronologically is the <i>Tristan and Isolt</i> of <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_von_Strassburg" title="Gottfried von Strassburg">Gottfried von Strassburg</a>, written circa 1211\xe2\x80\x931215. The poem was Gottfried\'s only known work, and was left incomplete due to his death with the retelling reaching half-way through the main plot. The poem was later completed by authors such as <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_von_Freiberg" title="Heinrich von Freiberg">Heinrich von Freiberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_von_T%C3%BCrheim" title="Ulrich von T\xc3\xbcrheim">Ulrich von T\xc3\xbcrheim</a>, but with the "common" branch of the legend as the ideal source.<sup id="cite_ref-Gottfried_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gottfried-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_branch">Common branch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Common branch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The earliest representation of the "common branch" is <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9roul" title="B\xc3\xa9roul">B\xc3\xa9roul</a>\'s <i>Le Roman de Tristan</i>, the first part of which is generally dated between 1150 and 1170, and the latter part between 1181 and 1190. The branch is so named due to its representation of an earlier non-chivalric, non-courtly, tradition of story-telling, making it more reflective of the Dark Ages than of the refined High Middle Ages. In this respect, they are similar to <a href="/wiki/Layamon%27s_Brut" title="Layamon&#39;s Brut">Layamon\'s <i>Brut</i></a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Perlesvaus" title="Perlesvaus">Perlesvaus</a></i>. As with Thomas\' works, knowledge of B\xc3\xa9roul\'s is limited. There were a few substantial fragments of his works discovered in the 19th century, and the rest was reconstructed from later versions.<sup id="cite_ref-EFTP_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EFTP-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The more substantial illustration of the common branch is the German version by <a href="/wiki/Eilhart_von_Oberge" title="Eilhart von Oberge">Eilhart von Oberge</a>. Eilhart\'s version was popular, but pales in comparison with the later Gottfried.<sup id="cite_ref-Gottfried_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gottfried-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Questions_regarding_a_common_source">Questions regarding a common source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Questions regarding a common source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The French medievalist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_B%C3%A9dier" title="Joseph B\xc3\xa9dier">Joseph B\xc3\xa9dier</a> thought all the Tristan legends could be traced to a single original poem, adapted by Thomas of Brittany into French from an original Cornish or Breton source. He dubbed this hypothetical original the "Ur-Tristan", and wrote his still-popular <i>Romance of Tristan and Iseult</i> as an attempt to reconstruct what this might have been like. In all likelihood, Common Branch versions reflect an earlier form of the story; accordingly, B\xc3\xa9dier relied heavily on Eilhart, B\xc3\xa9roul and Gottfried von Strassburg, and incorporated material from other versions to make a cohesive whole. Some scholars still consider B\xc3\xa9dier\'s argument convincing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A new English translation of B\xc3\xa9dier\'s <i>Roman de Tristan et Iseut</i> (1900) by Edward J. Gallagher was published in 2013 by Hackett Publishing Company. A translation by <a href="/wiki/Hilaire_Belloc" title="Hilaire Belloc">Hilaire Belloc</a>, first published in 1913, was republished in 2005.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_medieval_versions">Later medieval versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Later medieval versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French">French</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: French">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Contemporary with B\xc3\xa9roul and Thomas, the famous <a href="/wiki/Marie_de_France" title="Marie de France">Marie de France</a> presents a Tristan episode in one of her <a href="/wiki/The_Lais_of_Marie_de_France" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lais of Marie de France">lais</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Chevrefoil" title="Chevrefoil">Chevrefoil</a>". It concerns another of Tristan\'s clandestine returns to Cornwall in which the banished hero signals his presence to Iseult by means of an inscription on a branch of a <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnut</a> tree placed on the road she will travel. The title refers to the symbiosis of the <a href="/wiki/Honeysuckle" title="Honeysuckle">honeysuckle</a> and hazelnut tree which die when separated, as do Tristan and Iseult: "Ni vous sans moi, ni moi sans vous." ("Neither you without me, nor me without you.") This episode is reminiscent of one in the courtly branch when Tristan uses wood shavings put in a stream as signals to meet in the garden of Mark\'s palace.\n</p><p>There are also two 12th-century <i>Folies Tristan</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> poems identified as the Berne and the <a href="/wiki/Folie_Tristan_d%27Oxford" title="Folie Tristan d&#39;Oxford">Oxford versions</a>, which relate Tristan\'s return to Marc\'s court under the guise of a madman. Besides their own importance as episodic additions to the Tristan story and masterpieces of narrative structure, these relatively short poems significantly contributed to restoring the missing parts of B\xc3\xa9roul\'s and Thomas\' incomplete texts.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The great <i><a href="/wiki/Trouv%C3%A8re" title="Trouv\xc3\xa8re">trouv\xc3\xa8re</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Chr%C3%A9tien_de_Troyes" title="Chr\xc3\xa9tien de Troyes">Chr\xc3\xa9tien de Troyes</a> claims to have written a Tristan story, though no part of it has ever been found. He mentions this in the introduction to <a href="/wiki/Clig%C3%A8s" title="Clig\xc3\xa8s">Clig\xc3\xa8s</a>, a romance that many see as a kind of anti-Tristan with a happy ending. Some scholars speculate his Tristan was ill-received, prompting Chretien to write <i>Clig\xc3\xa8s</i> \xe2\x80\x93 a story with no Celtic antecedent \xe2\x80\x93 to make amends.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After B\xc3\xa9roul and Thomas, the most important development in French Tristaniana is a complex grouping of texts known broadly as the <a href="/wiki/Prose_Tristan" title="Prose Tristan">Prose <i>Tristan</i></a>. Extremely popular in the 13th and 14th century, the narratives of these lengthy versions vary in detail from manuscript to manuscript. Modern editions run twelve volumes for the long version, which includes Tristan\'s participation in the Quest for the Holy Grail, or five volumes for a shorter version without the Grail Quest.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It had a great influence on later medieval literature, and inspired parts of the <a href="/wiki/Post-Vulgate_Cycle" title="Post-Vulgate Cycle">Post-Vulgate Cycle</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Palamedes_(Arthurian_legend)" title="Palamedes (Arthurian legend)">Roman de Palamedes</a></i>, and Thomas Malory\'s <i>Le Morte d\'Arthur</i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The earliest complete source of the Tristan material in English was <i><a href="/wiki/Sir_Tristrem" title="Sir Tristrem">Sir Tristrem</a></i>, a romance of some 3344 lines written circa 1300. It is preserved in the famous <a href="/wiki/Auchinleck_manuscript" title="Auchinleck manuscript">Auchinleck manuscript</a> at the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Scotland" title="National Library of Scotland">National Library of Scotland</a>. The narrative largely follows the courtly tradition. As is true with many medieval English adaptations of French Arthuriana, the poem\'s artistic achievement can only be described as average, though some critics have tried to rehabilitate it, claiming it is a parody. Its first editor, Sir <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>, provided a sixty line ending to the story, which has been printed with the romance in every subsequent edition.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The only other medieval handling of the Tristan legend in English is Sir Thomas Malory\'s <i>The Book of Sir Tristram de Lyones</i>, a shortened "translation" of the French <i>Prose Tristan</i> in <i>Le Morte d\'Arthur</i>. Since the <a href="/wiki/Winchester_Manuscript" class="mw-redirect" title="Winchester Manuscript">Winchester Manuscript</a> surfaced in 1934, there has been much scholarly debate whether the Tristan narrative, like all the episodes in <i>Le Morte d\'Arthur</i>, was originally intended to be an independent piece or part of a larger work.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nordic">Nordic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nordic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The popularity of Brother Robert\'s version spawned a unique parody, <i>Saga Af Tristram ok \xc3\x8dsodd</i>, as well as the poem <i>Tristrams kv\xc3\xa6\xc3\xb0i</i>. In the collection of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> prose-translations of Marie de France\'s lais \xe2\x80\x93 called <i><a href="/wiki/Strengleikar" title="Strengleikar">Strengleikar</a></i> (Stringed Instruments) \xe2\x80\x93 two lais with Arthurian content have been preserved, one of them being the "Chevrefoil", translated as "Geitarlauf".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the 19th century, scholars had found Tristan legends spread across the Nordic world, from Denmark to the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>. These stories, however, diverged greatly from their medieval precursors. In one Danish ballad, for instance, Tristan and Iseult are made brother and sister. Other unlikely innovations occur in two popular Danish <a href="/wiki/Chapbook" title="Chapbook">chapbooks</a> of the late 18th-century <i>Tristans saga ok Inionu</i> and <i>En tragoedisk Historie om den \xc3\xa6dle og tappre Tistrand</i>, in which Iseult is made the princess of India. The popularity of these chapbooks inspired Icelandic poets <a href="/wiki/Sigur%C3%B0ur_Brei%C3%B0fj%C3%B6r%C3%B0" title="Sigur\xc3\xb0ur Brei\xc3\xb0fj\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0">Sigur\xc3\xb0ur Brei\xc3\xb0fj\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0</a> and N\xc3\xadels J\xc3\xb3nsson to write <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADmur" title="R\xc3\xadmur">r\xc3\xadmur</a>, long verse narratives, inspired by the Tristan legend.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch">Dutch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dutch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A 158 line fragment of a Dutch version (ca. 1250) of Thomas of Britain\'s <i>Tristan</i> exists. It is being kept in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%96sterreichische_Nationalbibliothek" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x96sterreichische Nationalbibliothek">\xc3\x96sterreichische Nationalbibliothek</a>, Series nova 3968.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh">Welsh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Welsh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A short Tristan narrative, perhaps related to the B\xc3\xa9roul text, exists in six Welsh manuscripts dating from the late 16th to the mid 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">Spanish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Spanish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the first third of the 14th century, the famous <a href="/wiki/Arcipreste_de_Hita" class="mw-redirect" title="Arcipreste de Hita">Arcipreste de Hita</a> wrote a version of the Tristan story. <i>Carta enviada por Hiseo la Brunda a Trist\xc3\xa1n</i>. <i>Respuesta de Trist\xc3\xa1n</i> was a unique 15th-century romance written in the form of imaginary letters between the two lovers. Then there was a famous <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> reworking of the French Prose Tristan, <i>Libro del muy esforzado caballero Don Trist\xc3\xa1n de Leon\xc3\xads y de sus grandes hechos en armas</i> first published in <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">Valladolid</a> in 1501, then republished in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> in 1511, 1520, 1525, 1528, 1533 and 1534; additionally a second part, <i>Tristan el Joven</i>, was created which dealt with Tristan\'s son, Tristan of Leonis.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech">Czech</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Czech">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A 13th century verse romance exists in <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, based on the German Tristan poems by <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_von_Strassburg" title="Gottfried von Strassburg">Gottfried von Strassburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_von_Freiberg" title="Heinrich von Freiberg">Heinrich von Freiberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eilhart_von_Oberge" title="Eilhart von Oberge">Eilhart von Oberge</a>. It is the only known <i>verse</i> representative of the Tristan story in a <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic language</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian">Italian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Italian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ponte_Two_couples.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Ponte_Two_couples.jpg/250px-Ponte_Two_couples.jpg" width="250" height="101" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Ponte_Two_couples.jpg/375px-Ponte_Two_couples.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Ponte_Two_couples.jpg/500px-Ponte_Two_couples.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="323" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ponte_Two_couples.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_dal_Ponte" title="Giovanni dal Ponte">Giovanni dal Ponte</a>\'s <i>Two couples - <a href="/wiki/Paris_(mythology)" title="Paris (mythology)">Paris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">Helen</a>, Tristan and Iseult</i> (1410s)</div></div></div>\n<p>The Tristan legend proved very popular in Italy; there were many <i>cantari</i>, or oral poems performed in the public square, either about him, or frequently referencing him:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Cantari di Tristano</i></li>\n<li><i>Due Tristani</i></li>\n<li><i>Quando Tristano e Lancielotto combattiero al petrone di Merlino</i></li>\n<li><i>Ultime imprese e morte Tristano</i></li>\n<li><i>Vendetta che fe Messer Lanzelloto de la Morte di Messer Tristano</i></li></ul>\n<p>There are also four differing versions of the Prose Tristan in medieval Italy, most named after their place of composition or library in which they are currently to be found:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Tavola Ritonda</i></li>\n<li><i>Tristano Panciaticchiano</i></li>\n<li><i>Tristano Riccardiano</i></li>\n<li><i>Tristano Veneto</i></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belarusian">Belarusian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Belarusian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Belarusian prose <i>Povest o Tryshchane</i> represents the furthest eastern advance of the legend, and, composed in the 1560s, is considered by some critics to be the last "medieval" Tristan or Arthurian text period.\n</p><p>Its lineage goes back to the <i>Tristano Veneto</i>. <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, at that time, controlled large parts of the <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbo</a>-<a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatian</a> language area, engendering a more active literary and cultural life there than in most of the Balkans during this period. The manuscript of the Povest states that it was translated from a (lost) Serbian intermediary. Scholars assume that the legend must have journeyed from Venice, through its Balkan colonies, finally reaching a last outpost in this Slavic language.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Tristan story was very popular in several art media, from <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> mirror-cases to the 13th-century Sicilian <a href="/wiki/Tristan_Quilt" title="Tristan Quilt">Tristan Quilt</a>. Many of the manuscripts with literary versions are <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated</a> with miniatures.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_works">Modern works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Modern works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In English, the Tristan story suffered the same fate as the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_Britain" title="Matter of Britain">Matter of Britain</a> generally. After being mostly ignored for about three centuries, there was a <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a> of original Arthurian literature, mostly narrative verse, in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. \n</p><p>Tristan material in this revival included <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Tennyson" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfred Tennyson">Alfred Tennyson</a>\'s <i>The Last Tournament</i>, one of his <i><a href="/wiki/Idylls_of_the_King" title="Idylls of the King">Idylls of the King</a></i>; <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Arnold" title="Matthew Arnold">Matthew Arnold</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tristram_and_Iseult" title="Tristram and Iseult">Tristram and Iseult</a></i>; and <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a>\'s epic poem <i><a href="/wiki/Tristram_of_Lyonesse" title="Tristram of Lyonesse">Tristram of Lyonesse</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a>\'s <i>The Famous Tragedy of the Queen of Cornwall at Tintagel in Lyonnesse</i> is a one-act play which was published in 1923 (the book includes an imaginary drawing of the castle at the period).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rutland_Boughton" title="Rutland Boughton">Rutland Boughton</a>\'s opera <i>The Queen of Cornwall</i> (1924) was based on Thomas Hardy\'s play.\n</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, most Tristan texts were in the form of prose novels or short stories:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bernard_Cornwell" title="Bernard Cornwell">Bernard Cornwell</a> includes a "historical" interpretation of the legend as a side story in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Warlord_Chronicles" title="The Warlord Chronicles">The Warlord Chronicles</a></i>.</li>\n<li>Novelist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Berger_(US_novelist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Berger (US novelist)">Thomas Berger</a> retold the story of Tristan and Isolde in his 1978 interpretation of Arthurian legend, <i><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rex:_A_Legendary_Novel" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Rex: A Legendary Novel">Arthur Rex: A Legendary Novel</a></i>.</li>\n<li>The story is also referenced in <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Thomas Quiller-Couch">Arthur Thomas Quiller-Couch</a> ("Q") started <i>Castle Dor</i>, a retelling of the Tristan and Iseult myth in modern circumstances with an innkeeper in the role of King Mark, his wife as Iseult and a <a href="/wiki/Breton_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Breton people">Breton</a> onion-seller as Tristan, the plot set in "Troy", his name for his home town of <a href="/wiki/Fowey" title="Fowey">Fowey</a>. The book was left unfinished at Quiller-Couch\'s death and was completed many years later, in 1962, by <a href="/wiki/Daphne_du_Maurier" title="Daphne du Maurier">Daphne du Maurier</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Miles" title="Rosalind Miles">Rosalind Miles</a> wrote a trilogy about Tristan and Isolde. The first, <i>Tristan and Iseult</i>, is a retelling of the story for young adults and was first published in 1971. It received the Boston-Globe Horn Book Award in 1972, and was runner-up for the 1972 Carnegie Medal. It is set primarily in Cornwall in the southern peninsula of Britain. The story appears again as a chapter of her later Arthurian novel, <i>The Sword and the Circle</i> (1981).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Diana_L._Paxson" title="Diana L. Paxson">Diana L. Paxson</a>\'s 1988 novel <i>The White Raven</i> tells the tale of Tristan and Iseult, called in her book "Drustan" and "Esseilte," from the perspective of Iseult\'s handmaiden Brangien ("Branwen"), who was mentioned in various of the medieval stories.</li>\n<li>In <a href="/wiki/Bengali_literature" title="Bengali literature">Bengali literature</a> the story has been depicted by author <a href="/wiki/Sunil_Gangopadhyay" title="Sunil Gangopadhyay">Sunil Gangopadhyay</a> in the novel <i>Sonali Dukkho</i>.</li>\n<li>Joseph B\xc3\xa9dier\'s <i>Romance of Tristan and Iseult</i> is quoted as a source by <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a> in the afterword to his novel <a href="/wiki/Brazil_(novel)" title="Brazil (novel)"><i>Brazil</i></a> about the lovers Trist\xc3\xa3o and Isabel.</li>\n<li>In <a href="/wiki/Harry_Turtledove" title="Harry Turtledove">Harry Turtledove</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternate history</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ruled_Britannia" title="Ruled Britannia">Ruled Britannia</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Christopher Marlowe</a> (who lives longer in the novel\'s timeline than he did in our history) writes a play called <i>Yseult and Tristan</i> to compete with his friend <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>\'s immensely popular <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i>. No details of the play are given.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg/220px-DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg/330px-DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg/440px-DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg 2x" data-file-width="780" data-file-height="620" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DR_1933_505_Nothilfe_Wagner_Tristan_und_Isolde.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wagner\'s opera <i><a href="/wiki/Tristan_und_Isolde" title="Tristan und Isolde">Tristan und Isolde</a></i> celebrated in a 1933 German stamp</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1832, <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Donizetti" title="Gaetano Donizetti">Gaetano Donizetti</a> references this story in <i><a href="/wiki/L%27elisir_d%27amore" title="L&#39;elisir d&#39;amore">L\'elisir d\'amore</a></i> as the character of Adina sings the story to the ensemble, inspiring Nemorino to ask the charlatan Dulcamara for the magic elixir. Composed in 1859, <i><a href="/wiki/Tristan_und_Isolde" title="Tristan und Isolde">Tristan und Isolde</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> is now considered one of the most influential pieces of music of all time. In his work, Tristan is portrayed as a doomed romantic figure, while <a href="/wiki/Isolde" class="mw-redirect" title="Isolde">Isolde</a> fulfils Wagner\'s quintessential feminine r\xc3\xb4le as the redeeming woman. \n</p><p>Twentieth-century composers also used the legend (often with Wagnerian overtones) in their compositions. <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Olivier the Wagnerian version as well as retellings of the legend.\n</p>\n<ul><li>The Swiss composer <a href="/wiki/Frank_Martin_(composer)" title="Frank Martin (composer)">Frank Martin</a> wrote a chamber opera <i>Le vin herb\xc3\xa9</i> between 1938\xe2\x80\x931940 after being influenced by Wagner.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Blind_Guardian" title="Blind Guardian">Blind Guardian</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Power_metal" title="Power metal">power metal</a> band from Germany, also has a song inspired by Tristan and Iseult\'s story, "The Maiden and the Minstrel Knight", from their <a href="/wiki/A_Night_at_the_Opera_(Blind_Guardian_album)" title="A Night at the Opera (Blind Guardian album)"><i>A Night at the Opera</i></a> album.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Colin_Meloy" title="Colin ep.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Patrick_Wolf" title="Patrick Wolf">Patrick Wolf</a>, English singer and songwriter, has a song about the Tristan and Iseult legend: "Tristan" from his second album <i><a href="/wiki/Wind_in_the_Wires" title="Wind in the Wires">Wind in the Wires</a></i>.</li>\n<li>Inspired by Thomas Hardy\'s play <i>The Famous Tragedy of The Queen of Cornwall</i> the English composer The first performance took place at the Glastonbury Festival in 1924. Already famous for "The Immortal Hour" and "Bethlehem", Boughton\'s growth as a unique and powerful operatic composer is evident in this treatment of the Tristram and Isolde legend. Feeling that Hardy\'s play offered too much unrelieved grimness he received the playwright\'s permission to import a handful of lyrics from his earlier published poetical works. The result is an altogether impressive and effective work, thought by many to be Boughton\'s masterpiece in this genre. In 2010 it was recorded on the Dutton Epoch label, in which <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Corp" title="Ronald Corp">Ronald Corp</a> conducts the <a href="/wiki/New_London_Orchestra" title="New London Orchestra">New London Orchestra</a>, members of the <a href="/wiki/The_London_Chorus" title="The London Chorus">London Chorus</a> and with soloists <a href="/wiki/Neol_Davies" title="Neol Davies">Neal Davies</a> (King Mark), Heather Shipp (Queen Iseult), <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Imbrailo" title="Jacques Imbrailo">Jacques Imbrailo</a> (Sir Tristam) and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Rodgers" title="Joan Rodgers">Joan Rodgers</a> (Iseult of Brittany).</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tristan_and_Iseult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The story has also been adapted into film many times.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<ul><li>The earliest is probably the 1909 French film <i>Tristan et Yseult</i>, an early, silent version of the story.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This was followed by another French film of the same name two years later, which offered a unique addition to the story. Here, it is Tristan\'s jealous slave Rosen who tricks the lovers into drinking the love potion, then denounces them to Mark. Mark has pity on the two lovers, but they commit double suicide anyway.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A third silent French version appeared in 1920, and follows the legend fairly closely.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<ul><li>One of the most celebrated and controversial Tristan films was 1943\'s <i><a href="/wiki/L%27%C3%89ternel_Retour" class="mw-redirect" title="L&#39;\xc3\x89ternel Retour">L\'\xc3\x89ternel Retour</a></i> (<i>The Eternal Return</i>), directed by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Delannoy" title="Jean Delannoy">Jean Delannoy</a> (screenplay by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">Jean Cocteau</a>). It is a contemporary retelling of the story with a man named Patrice in the Tristan role fetching a wife for his friend Marke. However, an evil dwarf tricks them into drinking a love potion, and the familiar plot ensues.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The film was made in France during the <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy regime</a>, and elements in the movie reflect <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazi</a> ideology, with the beautiful, blonde hero and heroine off-set by the <a href="/wiki/Untermensch" title="Untermensch">Untermensch</a> dwarf. The dwarf is given a larger role than in most interpretations of the legend; its conniving rains havoc on the lovers, much like the Jews of Nazi stereotypes.</li>\n<li>This was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> French film <i>Tristan et Iseult</i> in 1972 and the Irish <i>Lovespell</i>, featuring <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Clay" title="Nicholas Clay">Nicholas Clay</a> as Tristan and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Mulgrew" title="Kate Mulgrew">Kate Mulgrew</a> as Iseult; coincidentally, Clay went on to play Lancelot in <a href="/wiki/John_Boorman" title="John Boorman">John Boorman</a>\'s epic <i><a href="/wiki/Excalibur_(film)" title="Excalibur (film)">Excalibur</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The 1970 Spanish film <i>Tristana</i> is only tangentially related to the Tristan story. The Tristan role is assumed by the female character Tristana, who is forced to care for her aging uncle, Don Lope, though she wishes to marry Horacio.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The popular German film <i><a href="/wiki/Fire_and_Sword" title="Fire and Sword">Fire and Sword</a></i> premiered in 1981. It was very accurate to the story, though it cut the Iseult of Brittany subplot.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>French director <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois Truffaut">Fran\xc3\xa7ois Truffaut</a> adapted the subject to modern times for his 1981 film <i><a href="/wiki/La_Femme_d%27%C3%A0_c%C3%B4t%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="La Femme d&#39;\xc3\xa0 c\xc3\xb4t\xc3\xa9">La Femme d\'\xc3\xa0 c\xc3\xb4t\xc3\xa9</a></i> (<i>The Woman Next Door</i>), while 1988\'s <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Shadow_of_the_Raven" title="In the Shadow of the Raven">In the Shadow of the Raven</a></i> transported the characters to medieval Iceland. Here, Trausti and Isolde are warriors from rival tribes who come into conflict when Trausti kills the leader of Isolde\'s tribe, but a local bishop makes peace and arranges their marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-Harty_22-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harty-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">Bollywood</a> director <a href="/wiki/Subhash_Ghai" title="Subhash Ghai">Subhash Ghai</a> transfers the story to modern India and the United States in his 1997 musical <i><a href="/wiki/Pardes_(film)" title="Pardes (film)">Pardes</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Indian_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian American">Indian American</a> Kishorilal (<a href="/wiki/Amrish_Puri" title="Amrish Puri">Amrish Puri</a>) raises his orphaned nephew Arjun (<a href="/wiki/Shahrukh_Khan" class="mw-redirect" title="Shahrukh Khan">Shahrukh Khan</a>). Eventually, Pardes sends Arjun back to India to lure the beautiful Ganga (<a href="/wiki/Mahima_Chaudhary" class="mw-redirect" title="Mahima Chaudhary">Mahima Chaudhary</a>) as a bride for his selfish, shallow son Rajiv (Apoorva Agnihotri). Arjun falls for Ganga, and struggles to remain loyal to his cousin and beloved uncle. The film features the Bollywood hit "I Love My India".</li>\n<li>The 2002 French animated film <i>Tristan et Iseut</i> is a bowdlerized version of the traditional tale aimed at a family audience.</li>\n<li>The most recent Tristan film is 2006\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tristan_%26_Isolde_(film)" title="Tristan &amp; Isolde (film)">Tristan &amp; Isolde</a></i>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Tony_Scott" title="Tony Scott">Tony Scott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>, written by Dean Georgaris, directed by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Reynolds_(director)" title="Kevin Reynolds (director)">Kevin Reynolds</a>, and starring <a href="/wiki/James_Franco" title="James Franco">James Franco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sophia_Myles" title="Sophia Myles">Sophia Myles</a>. In this version, Tristan is a Cornish warrior who was raised by Lord Marke after his parents were killed at a young age. In a fight with the Irish, Tristan defeats Morholt, the Irish King\'s second, but is poisoned in the process. The poison dulls all his senses and his companions believe him dead. He is sent off in a boat meant to cremate a dead body. Isolde, dismayed over her unwilling betrothal to Morholt, leaves her home and finds Tristan on the Irish coast. She tells Tristan that she is called Bragnae, which is the name of her maidservant. Isolde takes care of him and hides him from her father. They spend long days together and come to care for each other. Eventually they confess their feelings for one another and consummate their love. Tristan\'s boat is discovered and Isolde\'s father begins a search for a Cornish warrior in Ireland. Isolde helps Tristan escape but cannot leave with him. Tristan returns to England and learns of a tournament between the Cornish tribes for the hand of the Irish princess named Isolde. He agrees to participate to win the princess as Marke\'s wife. After winning the tournament and discovering that the princess is the woman who had rescued him, Tristan is devastated but decides to bury his feelings, because her marriage to Marke would end decades of bloodshed. Eventually Tristan cannot stand to be apart from Isolde any longer and they start their adulterous relationship. Later, they are found out but Marke frees them after hearing their story. Tristan, however, returns to defend Marke against a rebellion. He dies a hero, with Isolde at his side.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
