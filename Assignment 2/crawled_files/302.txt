id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a></div>\n<p>The Cornish language is a <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic language</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>. All of these are directly descended from the <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">British language</a> formerly spoken throughout most of Britain. The language went into decline following the introduction of the English <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a></i> (in 1549) and by the turn of the 19th century had ceased to be used as a community language, (see main article for further discussion.)\n</p><p>During the 19th century researchers began to study the language from any remaining isolated speakers and in 1904 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jenner" title="Henry Jenner">Henry Jenner</a> published <i>A Handbook in the Cornish Language</i> which started the revival proper. Although less than 1% of Cornwall\'s population speak the language and \'mother tongue\' speakers are in their hundreds rather than thousands, the language continues to play a significant part in the culture of Cornwall.\n</p><p>Some events will use Cornish, in short phrases, openings, greetings or names. There is a healthy tradition of <a href="/wiki/Cornish_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish music">music</a> in the language, which can also be enjoyed by non-speakers. The vast majority of place names in Cornwall are derived from the language, and many people who live in Cornwall know a few words or phrases, e.g. \'Kernow bys vyken!\' (\'Cornwall forever!). Many Cornish houses, businesses, children, pets and boats<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> are named in the language, thus it has use as an "official community language" and any Cornish speaker will often be asked to provide translations. A sign of this role is that two<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of Cornwall\'s five MPs (now six) once swore their oaths to the Queen in Cornish.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2011)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_literature_and_folklore">Cornish literature and folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cornish literature and folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_literature" title="Cornish literature">Cornish literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_folklore" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish folklore">Cornish folklore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Cornish_writers" title="List of Cornish writers">List of Cornish writers</a></div>\n<p>The ancient <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic</a> country shares much of its cultural history with neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a> in England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> further afield. Historic records of authentic Cornish mythology or history are hard to verify but the earliest <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> language (such as the <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_manumissions" title="Bodmin manumissions">Bodmin manumissions</a>) marks the separation of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primitive_Cornish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Primitive Cornish (page does not exist)">Primitive Cornish</a> from <a href="/wiki/Old_Welsh" title="Old Welsh">Old Welsh</a> often dated to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Deorham" title="Battle of Deorham">Battle of Deorham</a> in 577.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Due to language erosion and possible suppression caused by the dominant English language<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and culture in the later medieval period, many works of Cornish language are thought to have been lost, particularly at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">dissolution of the religious houses</a> of (<a href="/wiki/Glasney_College" title="Glasney College">Glasney College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crantock_Parish_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Crantock Parish Church">Crantock College</a> for example)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, which were regarded as repertories<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of \'Welsh\' (i.e., foreign) conservatism by the English. Cornish grievances against the policies of the English government led to the unsuccessful uprisings of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Rebellion_of_1497" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Rebellion of 1497">Cornish Rebellion of 1497</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a> of 1549.\n</p><p>However, significant portions of the \'<a href="/wiki/Matter_of_Britain" title="Matter of Britain">Matter of Britain</a>\' relate to the people of Cornwall and Brittany as they do to the modern \'Welsh\'--this extends from <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Mabinogion" title="Mabinogion">Mabinogion</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>-derived tales of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> which make frequent and explicit reference to the geography of the early Brythonic nation, such as his capital at \'<a href="/wiki/Kelliwic" class="mw-redirect" title="Kelliwic">Kelliwic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cerniw" class="mw-redirect" title="Cerniw">Cerniw</a>\' and the legendary sea fortress of King <a href="/wiki/Mark_of_Cornwall" title="Mark of Cornwall">Mergh</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tintagel_Castle" title="Tintagel Castle">Tintagel</a>.\n</p><p>By the Shakespearean period, these ancient texts still maintained a currency demonstrated by <i><a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a></i> based on the ancient tale of <a href="/wiki/Leir_of_Britain" title="Leir of Britain">Leir of Britain</a> which names <a href="/wiki/Corineus" title="Corineus">Corineus</a> the eponymous founder of the Cornish nation; he traditionally wrestled the giant <a href="/wiki/Goemagot" class="mw-redirect" title="Goemagot">Goemagot</a> into the sea at <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Hoe" title="Plymouth Hoe">Plymouth Hoe</a> and claimed the land beyond for his people; the probable origin of the tale of <a href="/wiki/Jack_the_Giant_Killer" title="Jack the Giant Killer">Jack the Giant Killer</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The earliest Cornish literature is in the Cornish language and Cornwall produced a substantial number of <a href="/wiki/Passion_play" class="mw-redirect" title="Passion play">passion plays</a> during the Middle Ages. Many are still extant, and provide valuable information about the language: they were performed in round \'<a href="/wiki/Plain-an-Gwarry" title="Plain-an-Gwarry">plen a gwary</a>\' (place for playing) open-air theatres.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>There is much traditional <a href="/wiki/Cornish_folklore" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish folklore">folklore in Cornwall</a>, often tales of giants, mermaids, <a href="/wiki/Piskies" class="mw-redirect" title="Piskies">piskies</a> or the \'pobel vean\' (little people.) These are still surprisingly popular today, with many events hosting a \'droll teller\' to tell the stories: such myths and stories have found much publishing success, particularly in children\'s books.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Writing in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Cornish" title="Anglo-Cornish">Cornish dialect</a> has generally been overshadowed by the Cornish language<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. However, from the 19th century onwards poems and short stories have been published, often with a typically Cornish humour<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Some Cornish newspapers have featured a column written in Cornish dialect. e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Cornish_%26_Devon_Post" title="Cornish &amp; Devon Post">The Cornish &amp; Devon Post</a></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Then there are literary works in standard English including conversations between dialect speakers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><i>Cornish World</i>, a colour magazine produced in Cornwall and covering all aspects of Cornish life has proved popular with the descendants of Cornish emigrants as well as Cornish residents. It includes a column in the Cornish language.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_writers_and_poets">Cornish writers and poets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cornish writers and poets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Causley" title="Charles Causley">Charles Causley</a> was born in <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Cornwall" title="Launceston, Cornwall">Launceston</a> and is perhaps the best known of Cornish poets.\n</p><p>The Nobel-prizewinning novelist <a href="/wiki/William_Golding" title="William Golding">William Golding</a> who was born in <a href="/wiki/St_Columb_Minor" title="St Columb Minor">St Columb Minor</a> in 1911, and returned to live near <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a> from 1985 until his death in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other notable Cornish writers include <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Quiller-Couch" title="Arthur Quiller-Couch">Arthur Quiller-Couch</a>, alias "Q", novelist and literary critic, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Clemo" title="Jack Clemo">Jack Clemo</a>, deaf-blind poet, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Bottrall" title="Ronald Bottrall">Ronald Bottrall</a>, modernist poet, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephen_Hawker" title="Robert Stephen Hawker">Robert Stephen Hawker</a>, eccentric Victorian poet and priest, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Grigson" title="Geoffrey Grigson">Geoffrey Grigson</a>, poet and critic, <a href="/wiki/Silas_Hocking" class="mw-redirect" title="Silas Hocking">Silas Hocking</a>, a prolific novelist, and <a href="/wiki/D._M._Thomas" title="D. The <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a> of <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John Wesley</a> also proved to be very popular with the working classes in Cornwall in the 18th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Methodist chapels became important social centres, with male voice choirs and other church-affiliated groups playing a central role in the social lives of working-class Cornishmen. Methodism still plays a large part in the religious life of Cornwall today, although Cornwall has shared in the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> decline in British religious feeling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">the situation in Wales</a>, the churches failed to produce a translation of the Bible into the local language, and this has been seen by some as a crucial factor in the demise of the language.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2012)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\nThe Bible was translated into Cornish in 2002.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_developments">Recent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i><b>Renewed interest in Celtic Christianity</b></i>\n</p><p>In the late 20th century and early 21st century there has been a renewed interest in the older forms of Christianity in Cornwall. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cowethas_Peran_Sans&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cowethas Peran Sans (page does not exist)">Cowethas Peran Sans</a>, the Fellowship of St Piran, is one such group promoting <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Christianity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The group was founded by Andrew Phillips and membership is open to baptised Christians in good standing in their local community who support the aims of the group.\n</p><p>The aims of the group are these:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>To understand and embody the spirituality of the Celtic Saints</dd>\n<dd>To share this spirituality with others</dd>\n<dd>To use Cornwall\xe2\x80\x99s ancient Christian holy places again in worship</dd>\n<dd>To promote Cornwall as a place of Christian spiritual pilgrimage</dd>\n<dd>To promote the use of the Cornish language in prayer and worship</dd></dl>\n<p><i><b>Fry an Spyrys</b></i>\n</p><p>In 2003, a campaign group was formed called <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fry_an_Spyrys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fry an Spyrys (page does not exist)">Fry an Spyrys</a></i> (free the spirit in Cornish).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It is dedicated to disestablishing the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> in Cornwall and to forming an autonomous province of the Anglican Communion - a Church of Cornwall. Its chairman is Dr <a href="/wiki/Garry_Tregidga" title="Garry Tregidga">Garry Tregidga</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Cornish_Studies" title="Institute of Cornish Studies">Institute of Cornish Studies</a>. The Anglican Church was disestablished in Wales to form the <a href="/wiki/Church_in_Wales" title="Church in Wales">Church in Wales</a> in 1920 and in Ireland to form the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> in 1869.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_symbols">Cornish symbols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cornish symbols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_symbols" title="Cornish symbols">Cornish symbols</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Cornwall.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Flag_of_Cornwall.svg/200px-Flag_of_Cornwall.svg.png" width="200" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Flag_of_Cornwall.svg/300px-Flag_of_Cornwall.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Flag_of_Cornwall.svg/400px-Flag_of_Cornwall.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Cornwall.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flag of St Piran, used as a flag of Cornwall</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Saint_Piran%27s_Flag" title="Saint Piran&#39;s Flag">Saint Piran\'s Flag</a>, a white cross on a black background is often seen in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> shield of 15 gold <a href="/wiki/Bezants" class="mw-redirect" title="Bezants">bezants</a> on a black field is also used. Because of these two symbols black, white and gold are considered colours symbolic of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Red-billed_chough" title="Red-billed chough">chough</a> (in Cornish = <i>palores</i>) is also used as a symbol of Cornwall. In Cornish poetry the chough is used to symbolise the spirit of Cornwall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Also there is a Cornish belief that <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> lives in the form of a chough. "Chough" was also used as a nickname for Cornish people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Another animal with a deep association with Cornwall is the \xe2\x80\x9cWhite Horse of Lyonesse\xe2\x80\x9d. Arthurian legends tell of a rider escaping on a white horse as the land sunk beneath the waves, surviving and settling in Cornwall.\n</p><p>An anvil is sometimes used to symbolise <a href="/wiki/Cornish_nationalism" title="Cornish nationalism">Cornish nationalism</a>, particularly in its more extreme forms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This is a reference to \'<a href="/wiki/Michael_An_Gof" title="Michael An Gof">Michael An Gof</a>\', \'the smith\', one of two leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Rebellion_of_1497" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Rebellion of 1497">Cornish Rebellion of 1497</a>.\n</p><p>Fish, tin and copper together are used symbolically as they show the \'traditional\' three main industries of Cornwall. Tin has a special place in the Cornish culture, the \'<a href="/wiki/Stannary_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary Parliament">Stannary Parliament</a>\' and \'Cornish pennies\' are a testament to the former power of the <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Mining in Cornwall">Cornish tin industry</a>. Cornish tin is highly prized for jewellery, often of mine engines or <a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic</a> designs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Although Cornwall has no official flower many people favour the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_heath" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish heath">Cornish heath</a> (Erica vagans). In recent years <a href="/wiki/Daffodils" class="mw-redirect" title="Daffodils">daffodils</a> have been popular on the annual <a href="/wiki/Saint_Piran" title="Saint Piran">Saint Piran</a>\'s day march on Perran Sands although they are donated by a local daffodil grower and it is already considered to be the national flower of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_studies">Cornish studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cornish studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Cornish_Studies" title="Institute of Cornish Studies">Institute of Cornish Studies</a>, established in 1970, moved to the new <a href="/wiki/Combined_Universities_in_Cornwall" title="Combined Universities in Cornwall">Combined Universities in Cornwall</a> Campus at <a href="/wiki/Tremough" class="mw-redirect" title="Tremough">Tremough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penryn,_Cornwall" title="Penryn, Cornwall">Penryn</a> in October 2004: the institute is a branch of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Exeter" title="University of Exeter">University of Exeter</a>. A detailed overview of literature is provided by <a href="/wiki/Alan_M._Kent" title="Alan M. Kent">Alan M. Kent</a>\'s <i>The Literature of Cornwall</i>: it covers everything from medieval mystery plays to more recent literary works that draw on the Cornish landscape.\n</p><p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Philip_Payton" title="Philip Payton">Philip Payton</a>, professor at Exeter University\'s department of Cornish studies, has written <i>Cornwall: a History</i> as well as editing the Cornish studies series. Mark Stoyle,  Senior Lecturer in Early Modern History at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southampton" title="University of Southampton">University of Southampton</a>, asks \'Are the Cornish English?\xe2\x80\x99 in his book <i>West Britons</i>, a work on Cornish history exploring the nature of Cornishness in the early modern period. <a href="/wiki/John_Angarrack" title="John Angarrack">John Angarrack</a> of the human rights organisation <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_2000" title="Cornwall 2000">Cornwall 2000</a> has self-published two books to date, <i>Breaking the Chains</i> and <i>Our Future is History</i>: both are polemical re-examinations of Cornish history and identity, not historical works.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Old_Cornwall_Societies" title="Federation of Old Cornwall Societies">Federation of Old Cornwall Societies</a> is a group of societies of those interested in Cornwall\'s past which has published a number of books.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_art">Cornish art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cornish art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Cornish_artists,_architects_and_craftspeople" title="List of Cornish artists, architects and craftspeople">List of Cornish artists, architects and craftspeople</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people#Culture#Visual_arts" title="Cornish people">Cornish people \xc2\xa7&#160;Culture#Visual arts</a></div>\n<div class="thumb class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King-donierts-stone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 9th-century <i>Men Myghtern Doniert</i> (<a href="/wiki/King_Doniert%27s_Stone" title="King class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Three_Holes_Cross.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three Holes Cross</div></div></div>\n<p>So-called \'<a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic art</a>\' is found in Cornwall reflecting its ancient <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Brythonic</a> heritage, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_cross" title="Celtic cross">Celtic crosses</a> erected from the 10th century onwards. Many placenames are formed with the element <i><a href="/wiki/Llan_(placename_element)" class="mw-redirect" title="Llan (placename element)">Lan</a></i> of early <a href="/wiki/Cornish_saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish saints">Cornish saints</a> from Wales, Ireland, and Brittany. The activities of these saints resulted in a shared cultural inheritance which particularly includes the post-Roman corpus of literature relating to <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tristan_and_Iseult" title="Tristan and Iseult">Tristan and Iseult</a>, presumed nobility of ancient <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Cornwall boasts the highest density of traditional \'<a href="/wiki/Stone_crosses_in_Cornwall" title="Stone crosses in Cornwall">Celtic crosses</a>\' of any nation, and medieval <a href="/wiki/Holy_wells" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy wells">holy wells</a> are particularly prevalent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  The destruction of monastic institutions such as <a href="/wiki/Glasney_College" title="Glasney College">Glasney College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crantock" title="Crantock">Crantock</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> (1536\xe2\x80\x9345) is often regarded as the death knell of independence in Cornish language and culture; the very few remaining Cornish language manuscripts, including the <a href="/wiki/Miracle_plays" class="mw-redirect" title="Miracle plays">miracle plays</a> <a href="/wiki/Beunans_Ke" class="mw-redirect" title="Beunans Ke">Beunans Ke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beunans_Meriasek" title="Beunans Meriasek">Beunans Meriasek</a> are thought to have originated at these ancient centres of academic excellence. After the First World War, many crosses were erected as <a href="/wiki/War_memorials" class="mw-redirect" title="War memorials">war memorials</a> and also to celebrate events such as the <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">millennium</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Cornwall and its dramatic landscape and distinctive remoteness have produced and inspired many later artists. <a href="/wiki/John_Opie" title="John Opie">John Opie</a> was the first Cornish-born artist of note and <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a> visited in 1811. A number of London artists settled in the <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a> area in the 1880s, following the building of the Great Western Railway, who went on to form the <a href="/wiki/Newlyn_School" title="Newlyn School">Newlyn School</a>.\n</p><p>Sickert and Whistler both visited St Ives at the end of the 19th century, and the internationally famous studio potter, Bernard Leach set up his pottery in the town in 1920 <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cornwall" title="St Ives, Cornwall">St. Ives</a>. In 1928 <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nicholson" title="Ben Nicholson">Ben Nicholson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wood_(English_painter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christopher Wood (English painter)">Christopher Wood</a> visited the town and met <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wallis" title="Alfred Wallis">Alfred Wallis</a> the naive painter, native to St Ives, who was to become an important influence on a generation of British artists: particularly those who were members of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_and_Five_Society" title="Seven and Five Society">Seven and Five Society</a>.\n</p><p>At the outbreak of World War II Nicholson came to live in St Ives with his wife <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth" title="Barbara Hepworth">Barbara Hepworth</a>; staying initially with the philosopher and writer <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Stokes_(critic)" title="Adrian Stokes (critic)">Adrian Stokes (critic)</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mellis" title="Margaret Mellis">Margaret Mellis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Naum_Gabo" title="Naum Gabo">Naum Gabo</a> also joined them there as well as artists who at the time were at an earlier stage in their careers: <a href="/wiki/John_Wells_(artist)" title="John Wells (artist)">John Wells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmina_Barns-Graham" title="Wilhelmina Barns-Graham">Wilhelmina Barns-Graham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Frost" title="Terry Frost">Terry Frost</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Wynter" title="Bryan Wynter">Bryan Wynter</a>. Other artists of international repute joined the colony later: notably <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Heron" title="Patrick Heron">Patrick Heron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Hilton" title="Roger Hilton">Roger Hilton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandra_Blow" title="Sandra Blow">Sandra Blow</a>.\n</p><p>There are still a lot of artists in Cornwall many associated with the <a href="/wiki/Newlyn_Society_of_Artists" title="Newlyn Society of Artists">Newlyn Society of Artists</a>. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg/220px-Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg/330px-Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg/440px-Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Polperro_-_geograph.org.uk_-_395257.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lime-washed and slate-hung domestic vernacular architecture of various periods, Polperro</div></div></div>\n<p>Cornish vernacular architecture is characterised by its use of abundant natural stone, especially <a href="/wiki/Cornubian_batholith" title="Cornubian batholith">Cornubian granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delabole" title="Delabole">slate</a>, and local white <a href="/wiki/Whitewash" title="Whitewash">lime-washing</a> and its plain unadorned simplicity, sharing cultural and stylistic similarities, with the architecture of Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, as well as neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>. The architecture of west Cornish towns such as <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cornwall" title="St Ives, Cornwall">St Ives</a> is particularly distinctive for its use of solid granite and featuring also the type of early <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">promontory</a> <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_(religious_retreat)" title="Hermitage (religious retreat)">hermitage</a> particularly associated with <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Christianity</a>.\n</p><p>Early and continuous use of stone architecture over more than two thousand years, begins with the Romano-British enclosed courtyard houses at <a href="/wiki/Carn_Euny" title="Carn Euny">Carn Euny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chysauster" class="mw-redirect" title="Chysauster">Chysauster</a> is regionally distinct from the largely rectangular timber-derived architecture of <a href="/wiki/Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon">Saxon</a> England and often features characteristically rounded or circular forms<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> - such as the <a href="/wiki/Ringforts" class="mw-redirect" title="Ringforts">ringforts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roundhouse_(dwelling)" title="Roundhouse (dwelling)">roundhouses</a> and enclosed settlements known locally as "rounds" - the influence of which can be detected up to the building of <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Castle" title="Launceston Castle">Launceston Castle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Restormel_Castle" title="Restormel Castle">Restormel Castle</a> in the later medieval.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Morwenna,_Morwenstow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/St_Morwenna%2C_Morwenstow.jpg/220px-St_Morwenna%2C_Morwenstow.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/St_Morwenna%2C_Morwenstow.jpg/330px-St_Morwenna%2C_Morwenstow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/St_Morwenna%2C_Morwenstow.jpg/440px-St_Morwenna%2C_Morwenstow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Morwenna,_Morwenstow.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Church of St Morwenna, Morwenstow</div></div></div>\n<p>The medieval <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor_longhouse" title="Dartmoor longhouse">longhouse</a> was the typical form of housing in early Cornish \'Tre\' dispersed settlements of small hamlets of farmsteads and associated field systems apparently originating from before the time of the Norman conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor_longhouse" title="Dartmoor longhouse">longhouse</a> form is notable for its combined accommodation of humans and precious livestock under a single roof in a form found distributed across northwestern Atlantic Europe; France (<i>Longere</i>) <a href="/wiki/Brittany_(administrative_region)" title="Brittany (administrative region)">Brittany</a> (<i>Ty Hir</i>), <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>, Devon and South <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> (<i>Ty Hir</i>) .\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_font_in_St_Nonna%27s,_Altarnun_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1593217.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/The_font_in_St_Nonna%27s%2C_Altarnun_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1593217.jpg/220px-The_font_in_St_Nonna%27s%2C_Altarnun_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1593217.jpg" width="220" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/The_font_in_St_Nonna%27s%2C_Altarnun_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1593217.jpg/330px-The_font_in_St_Nonna%27s%2C_Altarnun_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1593217.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ed/The_font_in_St_Nonna%27s%2C_Altarnun_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1593217.jpg 2x" data-file-width="423" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_font_in_St_Nonna%27s,_Altarnun_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1593217.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The font of St Nonna\'s church, Altarnun</div></div></div>\n<p>Ecclesiastical architecture of Cornwall and Devon typically differs from that of the rest of southern England: most medieval churches in the larger parishes were rebuilt in the later medieval period with one or two aisles and a western tower, the aisles being the same width as the nave and the piers of the arcades being of one of a few standard types; the former monastery church at <a href="/wiki/St_German%27s_Priory" title="St German&#39;s Priory">St Germans</a> demonstrates these features over several periods as the former seat of the bishop of Cornwall. Wagon roofs often survive in these churches. The typical tower is of three stages, often with buttresses set back from the angles.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Churches of the Decorated period are relatively rare, as are those with spires. There are very few churches from the 17th and 18th centuries. There is a distinctive type of Norman font in many Cornish churches which is sometimes called the Altarnun type. The style of carving in benchends is also recognisably Cornish.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Cornwall" title="Music of Cornwall">Music of Cornwall</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LannerBandRoom.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/LannerBandRoom.jpg/220px-LannerBandRoom.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/LannerBandRoom.jpg/330px-LannerBandRoom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/LannerBandRoom.jpg/440px-LannerBandRoom.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LannerBandRoom.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lanner Band Room</div></div></div>\n<p>Cornwall has a rich and vibrant folk music tradition which has survived into the present.\n</p><p>Cornish players are regular participants in inter-Celtic festivals, and Cornwall itself has several lively inter-Celtic festivals such as <a href="/wiki/Perranporth" title="Perranporth">Perranporth</a>\'s folk festival, Lowender Peran.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cornish <a href="/wiki/Celtic_music" title="Celtic music">Celtic music</a> is a relatively large phenomenon given the size of the region. A recent tally found over 100 bands playing mostly or entirely Cornish folk music. Traditional dancing (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_dance" title="Cornish dance">Cornish dance</a>) is associated with the music. These dance events are either <i>Troyls</i>, (a dance night more similar to a <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9il%C3%AD" class="mw-redirect" title="C\xc3\xa9il\xc3\xad">ceilidh</a>) or <i><a href="/wiki/Noze_looan" class="mw-redirect" title="Noze looan">Nozow looan</a></i>, (a dance night more similar to a <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Breton</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Fest_Noz" title="Fest Noz">Fest Noz</a></i>).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aphex_Twin" title="Aphex Twin">Aphex Twin</a> is a Cornish-based electronic music artist, though he was born of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a> parents in Ireland. Many other pop musicians are based in Cornwall but many of them originate elsewhere.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lanner_and_District_Silver_Band" title="Lanner and District Silver Band">Lanner and District Silver Band</a> is a Cornish <a href="/wiki/Brass_band_(British_style)" class="mw-redirect" title="Brass band (British style)">Brass band</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Lanner,_Cornwall" title="Lanner, Cornwall">Lanner, Cornwall</a>, United Kingdom, and well known for its concerts. There are many other brass and silver bands in Cornwall, particularly in the former mining areas: <a href="/wiki/St_Dennis,_Cornwall" title="St Dennis, Cornwall">St Dennis</a> is a notable example.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_festivals" title="Cornish festivals">Cornish festivals</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stpiraninpenwith.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stpiraninpenwith.JPG/220px-Stpiraninpenwith.JPG" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stpiraninpenwith.JPG/330px-Stpiraninpenwith.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stpiraninpenwith.JPG/440px-Stpiraninpenwith.JPG 2x" data-file-width="520" data-file-height="331" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stpiraninpenwith.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Celebrating Saint Piran\'s Day</div></div></div>\n<p>There is a long tradition of processional dance and music in Cornwall. The best known tradition is the <a href="/wiki/Furry_Dance" title="Furry Dance">Helston Furry</a>. The term \'furry\' is used generally to describe such a dance or associated tune. These bands have been referred to as \'crowders and horners\' and generally have a motley mix of instruments with folk instruments such as the fiddle, bagpipe or <a href="/wiki/Crowdy-crawn" title="Crowdy-crawn">crowdy crawn</a> mixed up with brass, reed and anything that can be carried.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Padstow" title="Padstow">Padstow</a> <a href="/wiki/%27Obby_%27Oss_festival" title="&#39;Obby &#39;Oss festival">\'Obby \'Oss festival</a> takes place on 1 May, the feast of <a href="/wiki/Beltane" title="Beltane">Beltane</a> to Celtic people.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Golowan" class="mw-redirect" title="Golowan">Golowan</a> festival in <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>, which was revived in 1991, was part of a much wider tradition of midsummer festivals where bonfires were lit on hilltops on Midsummer\'s Eve. The tradition of midsummer bonfires continues, albeit to a lesser extent than when fires could be seen on every hilltop, throughout Cornwall.\n</p><p>Lowender Perran is held at the end of October in <a href="/wiki/Perranporth" title="Perranporth">Perranporth</a>. This is a gathering of musicians and dancers from the six <a href="/wiki/Celtic_nations" title="Celtic nations">Celtic nations</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornish_pasty.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cornish_pasty.jpeg/220px-Cornish_pasty.jpeg" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cornish_pasty.jpeg/330px-Cornish_pasty.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cornish_pasty.jpeg/440px-Cornish_pasty.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="1848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornish_pasty.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Cornish pasty, known traditionally as an oggy, can be found all over the world</div></div></div>\n<p>Historically Cornwall has had close links with <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and this is reflected in the music. The Cornish and <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton languages</a> were mutually intelligible until Tudor times<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and there were many Bretons living in Cornwall before the <a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a>. Myths, saints, dances and tunes are often shared with <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. It has been noted that <a href="/wiki/The_Kroaz_Du_(Black_Cross)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Kroaz Du (Black Cross)">The Kroaz Du (Black Cross)</a> flag used in medieval Brittany is the exact inverse of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Piran%27s_Flag" title="Saint Piran&#39;s Flag">Cornish flag</a>, whether there is a reason for this is unknown. Breton flags are popular in Cornwall and are often seen alongside the Cornish flag on car bumpers and at musical events. This link continues today with Cornish-Breton festivals such as \'<a href="/wiki/AberFest" title="AberFest">AberFest</a>\' in <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> (Aberfal) and the twinning of Cornish and Breton towns.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg/220px-Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg/330px-Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg/440px-Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lady_of_Cornwall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lady of Cornwall and flower girls at the 2007 Gorseth (Penzance)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gorseth_Kernow" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorseth Kernow">Gorseth Kernow</a> (or gorsedh), which was set up in 1928, is similar to the Welsh <a href="/wiki/Gorsedd" title="Gorsedd">Gorsedd</a>, and indeed was formed by the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> Gorsedd at the request of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jenner" title="Henry Jenner">Henry Jenner</a>. The Cornish Gorseth promotes the arts and the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> through competitions at the open gorseth.<sup buns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cake" title="Heavy cake">Cornish Heavy (Hevva) Cake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_fairings" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish fairings">Cornish fairings</a> (biscuit), Opinion varies as to whether or not the cream should be spread on before or after the jam.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_and_games">Sports and games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sports and games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outdoor_sports_and_games">Outdoor sports and games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Outdoor sports and games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>See <a href="/wiki/Sport_in_Cornwall" title="Sport in Cornwall">Sport in Cornwall</a>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indoor_games">Indoor games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Indoor games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Euchre" title="Euchre">Euchre</a> is a popular<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. Its origins are unclear but some claim it is a Cornish game, and was popularised in part by Cornish immigrants to the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> There are several leagues in Cornwall at present. Whist and pub quizzes are also popular in many villages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_film">Cornish film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cornish film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_film_locations" title="Cornwall film locations">Cornwall film locations</a></div>\n<p>Cornwall has a small but growing film industry, mostly focusing on the Cornish language and culture. Numerous films, short and long, have been made in Cornwall. The Cornish film industry is supported by organisations such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=War-rag&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="War-rag (page does not exist)">War-rag</a> (War-rag meaning "ahead" in Cornish).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Media_Festival" title="Celtic Media Festival">Celtic Film and Television Festival</a> includes entries from Cornish film makers, and was held in <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> in 2006. Also the Goel Fylm Kernow/<a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Film_Festival" title="Cornwall Film Festival">Cornwall Film Festival</a> is held once a year, and supports Cornish film making, including films made in the Cornish or English language.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Goel Fylm Kernow also hosts workshops, screenings and the "Govynn Kernewek" competition. In this competition applicants can present their ideas for films to be made in the Cornish language. The prizes in the competition consist of financial, material and technical support for making the film. Films made following this award include "Kernow\'s Kick Ass Kung-Fu Kweens" (2004), a <a href="/wiki/Kung_fu_film" title="Kung fu film">kung fu film</a> in Cornish.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The only known feature-length film in the Cornish language is <i><a href="/wiki/Hwerow_Hweg" title="Hwerow Hweg">Hwerow Hweg</a></i> (<i>Bitter Sweet</i>), which was filmed alongside an English version.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> However, although it did not become as popular as was hoped,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> it was nominated for Best Feature Length Film at the 2002 Celtic Film and Television Festival.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> There are a number of short films which have been made in the language.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_dress">Traditional dress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Traditional dress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_kilts_and_tartans" title="Cornish kilts and tartans">Cornish kilts and tartans</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornish_National_Tartan.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Cornish_National_Tartan.png/220px-Cornish_National_Tartan.png" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Cornish_National_Tartan.png/330px-Cornish_National_Tartan.png 1.5x, class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornish_National_Tartan.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cornwall\'s national tartan, <i>bracca</i></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SpallingLge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" data-file-width="843" data-file-height="612" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SpallingLge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bal maidens at work, showing traditional dress</div></div></div>\n<p>The "traditional dress" of Cornwall for women is a <a href="/wiki/Bal_Maiden" class="mw-redirect" title="Bal Maiden">Bal Maiden</a>\'s or fishwife\'s costume. This includes the wearing of a bonnet known as a "<a href="/wiki/Gook_(headgear)" title="Gook (headgear)">gook</a>" (which were peculiar to a district or community,) aprons and woollen <a href="/wiki/Shawls" class="mw-redirect" title="Shawls">shawls</a>.\n</p><p>For men <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishermen%27s_smocks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fishermen&#39;s smocks (page does not exist)">fishermen\'s smocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> sweaters (known as worsted-frocks in Cornwall) and long cut shirts are worn.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The adoption of the Cornish kilt has recently become popular, and these kilts are available in various <a href="/wiki/Cornish_tartans" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish tartans">Cornish tartans</a> or plain black. The first reference to a "Cornish" kilt is from 1903 when the Cornish delegate to the Celtic Congress, convening at <a href="/wiki/Caernarfon" title="Caernarfon">Caernarvon</a>, <a href="/wiki/L._C._R._Duncombe-Jewell" title="L. C. R. Duncombe-Jewell">L. C. R. Duncombe-Jewell</a>, appeared in a <a href="/wiki/Woad" class="mw-redirect" title="Woad">woad</a> blue kilt, to impress upon the delegates the Celtic character of Cornwall. Black kilts are proposed by some as the traditional version of the garment, some claiming that the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall%27s_Light_Infantry" title="Duke of Cornwall&#39;s Light Infantry">Duke of Cornwall\'s Light Infantry</a> wore black kilts on occasions in the 19th century<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> (this may have been similar to the Irish saffron kilt). The most common kilt used in Cornwall is pleated Scottish-style with a leather, <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> shield-style, sporran.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cornish_national_tartan" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish national tartan">Cornish national tartan</a> was designed by E. E Morton Nance in 1963 using colours traditionally associated with Cornwall. Fragments of tartan have been found in <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
