Ireland 800\xe2\x80\x931169</b> covers the period in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ireland" title="History of Ireland">history of Ireland</a> from the first <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> raids to the <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">Norman invasion</a>. The first two centuries of this period are characterised by Viking raids and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> settlements along the coast. Viking ports were established at <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterford" title="Waterford">Waterford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>, which became the first large towns in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.\n</p><p>Ireland consisted of many semi-independent <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BAatha" class="mw-redirect" title="T\xc3\xbaatha">t\xc3\xbaatha</a>, and during the entire period, attempts were made by various factions to gain political control over the whole of the island. For the first two centuries of this period, this was mainly a rivalry between putative <a href="/wiki/High_Kings_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="High Kings of Ireland">High Kings of Ireland</a> from the northern and southern branches of the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill">U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill</a>. The one who came closest to being de facto king over the whole of Ireland, however, was <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru" title="Brian Boru">Brian Boru</a>, the first high king in this period not belonging to the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill.\n</p><p>Following Brian\'s death at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Clontarf" title="Battle of Clontarf">Battle of Clontarf</a> in 1014, the political situation became more complex with rivalry for high kingship from several clans and dynasties. Brian\'s descendants failed to maintain a unified throne, and regional squabbling over territory led indirectly to the invasion of the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> under <a href="/wiki/Richard_de_Clare,_2nd_Earl_of_Pembroke" title="Richard de Clare, 2nd Earl of Pembroke">Richard de Clare</a> in <a href="/wiki/1169_in_Ireland" title="1169 in Ireland">1169</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toctext">Notes and references</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-20"><a href="#Notes"><span class="tocnumber">12.1</span> <span class="toctext">Notes</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-21"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">12.2</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-22"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">13</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eoin_MacNeill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Eoin_MacNeill.jpg/170px-Eoin_MacNeill.jpg" width="170" height="305" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Eoin_MacNeill.jpg/255px-Eoin_MacNeill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0e/Eoin_MacNeill.jpg 2x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="539" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eoin_MacNeill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Eoin_MacNeill" title="Eoin MacNeill">Eoin MacNeill</a>, one of the pioneers in modern studies of Irish medieval history</div></div></div>\n<p>Due to the rich amount of written sources, the study of Irish history 800\xe2\x80\x931169 has, to a large extent, focused on gathering, interpretation and <a href="/wiki/Textual_criticism" title="Textual criticism">textual criticism</a> of these. Only recently have other sources of historical knowledge received more attention, particularly <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>. Since the modern excavations of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> started in 1961, followed by similar efforts in <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterford" title="Waterford">Waterford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>, great advances have been made in the understanding of the physical character of the towns established during this period.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first part of the period from 800 to 1014 is well-studied; the "Viking age" has attracted the interest of historians for quite some time. The period between 1014 and 1169 has received less attention. In the words of Sean Duffy, this period\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>has \xe2\x80\x93 historiographically speaking \xe2\x80\x93 fallen between two stools. Historians of early medieval Ireland, seeking to conclude their narratives on a high note, have traditionally done so after recounting the death of the famous high-king Brian B\xc3\xb3rama (Boru) at the battle of Clontarf in 1014. On the other hand, historians of later medieval Ireland generally choose to begin proceedings with the English invasion of the 1160s. Eleventh- and early twelfth-century Ireland has, therefore, often assumed the character of a snappy epilogue or a lengthy prologue.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>In trying to interpret the history of early Ireland, one of the most frequently asked questions addressed by historians is how early it is possible to speak of an Irish nation, a <i>natio</i> encompassing the whole island of Ireland. Early poet-historians like <a href="/wiki/Flann_Mainistrech" class="mw-redirect" title="Flann Mainistrech">Flann Mainistrech</a> constructed a history of a monarchy of all Ireland going back to  and beyond St Patrick. Only a hundred years after Mainistrech, <a href="/wiki/Gerald_of_Wales" title="Gerald of Wales">Gerald of Wales</a> described the Irish society in his <a href="/wiki/Topographia_Hibernica" title="Topographia Hibernica">Topographia Hibernica</a> as utterly primitive and savage. At the beginning of modern scholarly interpretation of Irish history, <a href="/wiki/Eoin_MacNeill" title="Eoin MacNeill">Eoin MacNeill</a> and <a href="/wiki/G.H._Orpen" class="mw-redirect" title="G.H. Orpen">G.H. Orpen</a> came to opposite conclusions analyzing the same period. Orpen saw an anarchic country still in \xe2\x80\x98a tribal state\xe2\x80\x99; he could see no <i>natio</i>, no wider community in Ireland than the tribe. MacNeill stressed the reality of the high-kingship of Ireland and the existence of many of those institutions of government which Orpen found wanting, and claimed that Irish law had a national character.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_the_written_sources">Nature of the written sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nature of the written sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Book_of_Leinster,_folio_53.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Book_of_Leinster%2C_folio_53.jpg/220px-Book_of_Leinster%2C_folio_53.jpg" width="220" height="322" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Book_of_Leinster%2C_folio_53.jpg/330px-Book_of_Leinster%2C_folio_53.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/Book_of_Leinster%2C_folio_53.jpg 2x" data-file-width="406" data-file-height="594" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Book_of_Leinster,_folio_53.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Folio 53 from the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Leinster" title="Book of Leinster">Book of Leinster</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>A large body of contemporary and near-contemporary material on early medieval Ireland has survived. From the titles of works mentioned in these sources, it is clear that a great deal of additional material has now been lost. The surviving materials usually exist in the form of much later copies, and it is only from comparison of the various texts that the original documents can be reconstructed.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Extant <a href="/wiki/Irish_annals" title="Irish annals">Irish annals</a> are ultimately derived from the now-lost <a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_Ireland" title="Chronicle of Ireland">Chronicle of Ireland</a> which was probably being compiled in the midlands of Ireland by around 800. All include material derived from other sources, or added at a later date. The <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Ulster" title="Annals of Ulster">Annals of Ulster</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Innisfallen" class="mw-redirect" title="Annals of Innisfallen">Annals of Innisfallen</a></i> cover most of this period, but have a gap between 1132 and 1155. The <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Clonmacnoise" title="Annals of Clonmacnoise">Annals of Clonmacnoise</a></i> survive only in an eccentric 17th-century English translation, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Tigernach" title="Annals of Tigernach">Annals of Tigernach</a></i> for this period are lost with <a href="/wiki/Dubhaltach_Mac_Fhirbhisigh" title="Dubhaltach Mac Fhirbhisigh">Dubhaltach Mac Fhirbhisigh</a>\'s abbreviated copy known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicon_Scotorum" title="Chronicon Scotorum">Chronicon Scotorum</a></i> supplying only part of the missing material. The <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Four_Masters" title="Annals of the Four Masters">Annals of the Four Masters</a></i> are late, and include some material of doubtful origin. While the annals provide a considerable amount of information, they are generally terse, and most focus their attention on the doings of the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill">U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill</a> and of churchmen.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to the annals, a large number of genealogies survive, along with geographical and legal texts, poetry, sagas and hagiography.\n</p><p>In the 12th century, propaganda text like <a href="/wiki/Caithr%C3%A9im_Chellach%C3%A1in_Chaisil" title="Caithr\xc3\xa9im Chellach\xc3\xa1in Chaisil">Caithr\xc3\xa9im Chellach\xc3\xa1in Chaisil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cogad_G%C3%A1edel_re_Gallaib" title="Cogad G\xc3\xa1edel re Gallaib">Cogad G\xc3\xa1edel re Gallaib</a> were composed. Even though the historical accuracy of these accounts is dubious, the Cogad especially has had a great impact on the interpretation of Irish medieval history until recently.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_landscape_c._800">Political landscape c. 800</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political landscape c. 800">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_kingdoms_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic kingdoms of Ireland">Gaelic kingdoms of Ireland</a></div>\n<p>At the end of the 8th century, Ireland was homogeneously <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> in terms of society, culture and language. People lived in rural communities, and the only larger settlements were monastic towns of varying sizes. The monasteries played an important part in society, not just with regards to religious and cultural life, but also to economy and politics. <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianization</a> had begun in the 5th century, and by the early 9th century the island was almost entirely Christian. However, the <i><a href="/wiki/Martyrology_of_Tallaght" title="Martyrology of Tallaght">Martyrology of Tallaght</a></i> (written sometime in the 8th or 9th century) hints that <a href="/wiki/Celtic_paganism" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic paganism">paganism</a> had not yet been fully uprooted.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif/300px-Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif" width="300" height="382" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif/450px-Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif/600px-Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif 2x" data-file-width="748" data-file-height="952" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ireland_early_peoples_and_politics.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Peoples and subdivisions of early Ireland</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Eoin_MacNeill" title="Eoin MacNeill">Eoin MacNeill</a> identified the "oldest certain fact in the political history of Ireland" as the existence in late prehistory of a pentarchy, probably consisting of the <i>c\xc3\xb3iceda</i> or "fifths" of the <a href="/wiki/Ulaid" title="Ulaid">Ulaid</a> (Ulster), the <a href="/wiki/Connachta" title="Connachta">Connachta</a> (Connacht), the <a href="/wiki/Laigin" title="Laigin">Laigin</a> (Leinster), Mumu (<a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mide" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Mide">Mide</a> (Meath), although some accounts discount Mide and split Mumu in two.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This is not an accurate description of the political landscape c. 800,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup> but when discussing the political subdivisions of Ireland at this time, it is still useful to refer to this system; if Laigin and Mide are combined as Leinster, it roughly corresponds with the modern <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Ireland" title="Provinces of Ireland">four provinces of Ireland</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill">U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill</a>, divided in two main branches known as "Northern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill" and "Southern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill", was the leading dynasty in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-OCorV&amp;I_p2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OCorV&amp;I_p2-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;note 3&#93;</a></sup> The Northern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill controlled the north-western part of Ireland, and was divided into two leading branches, the <a href="/wiki/Cen%C3%A9l_Conaill" class="mw-redirect" title="Cen\xc3\xa9l Conaill">Cen\xc3\xa9l Conaill</a> in the west and <a href="/wiki/Cen%C3%A9l_nE%C3%B3gain" class="mw-redirect" title="Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain">Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain</a>, also known as the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Ailech" class="mw-redirect" title="Ailech">Ailech</a>. Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain had become the more powerful of the two in 789,<sup id="cite_ref-OCorV&amp;I_p2_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OCorV&amp;I_p2-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and had expanded east and southwards, gaining control over the important monastic centre <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a> and the large sub-kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Airg%C3%ADalla" title="Airg\xc3\xadalla">Airg\xc3\xadalla</a>. The traditional kingdom of Ulaid, dominated by <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Fiatach" title="D\xc3\xa1l Fiatach">D\xc3\xa1l Fiatach</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_nAraidi" title="D\xc3\xa1l nAraidi">D\xc3\xa1l nAraidi</a>, was now more or less confined to the area east of the <a href="/wiki/River_Bann" title="River Bann">river Bann</a>.\n</p><p>The central region of Mide had been dominated by what became known as the "southern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill" since the 7th century. Until the 8th century, the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%ADl_n%C3%81edo_Sl%C3%A1ine" title="S\xc3\xadl n\xc3\x81edo Sl\xc3\xa1ine">S\xc3\xadl n\xc3\x81edo Sl\xc3\xa1ine</a> (also known as the kingdom of Brega) was pre-eminent, but from 728 the western dynasty of <a href="/wiki/Clann_Cholm%C3%A1in" title="Clann Cholm\xc3\xa1in">Clann Cholm\xc3\xa1in</a> was dominant.\n</p><p>In Laigin, <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_D%C3%BAnlainge" title="U\xc3\xad D\xc3\xbanlainge">U\xc3\xad D\xc3\xbanlainge</a> was the dominant dynasty c. 800. They were closely associated with the large monastery of <a href="/wiki/Kildare" title="Kildare">Kildare</a>. Their main rival for dominance in Leinster, the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_Cheinnselaig" class="mw-redirect" title="U\xc3\xad Cheinnselaig">U\xc3\xad Cheinnselaig</a> had not been able to claim the title <a href="/wiki/List_of_Kings_of_Leinster" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Kings of Leinster">king of Leinster</a> since 728. The U\xc3\xad Cheinnselaig now controlled a territory in the south-eastern part of Leinster, and had close links to the monastery of <a href="/wiki/Ferns,_County_Wexford" title="Ferns, County Wexford">Ferns</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Osraige" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Osraige">kingdom of Osraige</a>, occupying roughly the same area as the present <a href="/wiki/County_Kilkenny" title="County Kilkenny">County Kilkenny</a> and western <a href="/wiki/County_Laois" title="County Laois">County Laois</a>, was considered part of Munster until the late ninth century, when it received an independent status under king <a href="/wiki/Cerball_mac_D%C3%BAnlainge" title="Cerball mac D\xc3\xbanlainge">Cerball mac D\xc3\xbanlainge</a>. Munster was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B3ganachta" title="E\xc3\xb3ganachta">E\xc3\xb3ganachta</a>, centred around <a href="/wiki/Cashel,_County_Tipperary" title="Cashel, County Tipperary">Cashel</a> and with <a href="/wiki/Emly" title="Emly">Emly</a> as ecclesiastical centre. The <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_gCais" class="mw-redirect" title="D\xc3\xa1l gCais">D\xc3\xa1l gCais</a> (not yet known under this name) had defeated the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corcu_Modruad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Corcu Modruad (page does not exist)">Corcu Modruad</a> in 744 and taken control over the area in present <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a> from which they would later rise to dominance, but were not yet a significant power in Munster. The E\xc3\xb3ganachta rivalled the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill in power and influence, and claimed suzerainty over the southern part of Ireland. This claim was in part anchored within the legendary ancient division of the island in <a href="/wiki/Leath_Cuinn" class="mw-redirect" title="Leath Cuinn">Leath Cuinn and Leath Moga</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Conn_of_the_Hundred_Battles" title="Conn of the Hundred Battles">Conn</a>\'s half" (north) and "<a href="/wiki/Mug_Nuadat" title="Mug Nuadat">Mug</a>\'s half" (south).\n</p><p>During the 7th century the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_Bri%C3%BAin" title="U\xc3\xad Bri\xc3\xbain">U\xc3\xad Bri\xc3\xbain</a> had emerged in Connacht, and since the first half of the 8th century been the dominant dynasty. U\xc3\xad Bri\xc3\xbain also influenced the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Breifne" title="Kingdom of Breifne">kingdom of Breifne</a> on the southern borders of the Northern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="First_Viking_age_.28795.E2.80.93902.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Viking_age_(795\xe2\x80\x93902)">First Viking age (795\xe2\x80\x93902)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First Viking age (795\xe2\x80\x93902)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Viking_raids">Early Viking raids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Viking raids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Early_Scandinavian_Dublin#Early_Viking_raids" title="Early Scandinavian Dublin">Early Scandinavian Dublin \xc2\xa7&#160;Early Viking raids</a></div>\n<p>The early Vikings were a group of people who originated in modern-day Denmark and Norway. In the 700s, pressure on land in Scandinavia had forced many nobles and warriors to seek land elsewhere. Some of these were younger sons, who stood to inherit nothing of their father\'s estate. Noblemen with little to lose began to gather together groups of warriors and go down the coast pillaging settlements.\n</p>\n<table class="rquote pullquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">\n\n<tbody><tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">\n<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">\xe2\x80\x9c\n</td>\n<td style="padding:0 10px;"><i>The wind is fierce tonight<br />it tosses the sea\'s white mane<br />I do not fear the coursing of a quiet sea<br />by the fierce warriors of Lothlend</i>\n</td>\n<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">\xe2\x80\x9d\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:normal; text-align:right;"><cite>\xe2\x80\x94&#160;Anonymous poem <i><a href="/wiki/Is_acher_in_ga%C3%ADth_in-nocht..." title="Is acher in ga\xc3\xadth in-nocht...">Is acher in ga\xc3\xadth in-nocht...</a></i></cite>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The first recorded <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> raid in Irish history occurred in AD <a href="/wiki/795_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="795 in Ireland">795</a> when Vikings, possibly from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a><sup id="cite_ref-Woolfp47_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfp47-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> looted the island of <a href="/wiki/Lambay_Island" title="Lambay Island">Lambay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;note 4&#93;</a></sup> This was followed by a raid on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Brega" title="Kings of Brega">Brega</a> in 798, and raids on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a> in 807.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> These early Viking raids were generally small in scale and quick.\n</p><p>These early raids interrupted the golden age of Christian Irish culture and marked the beginning of two hundred years of intermittent warfare, with waves of Viking raiders plundering monasteries and towns throughout Ireland. Most of the early raiders came from the fjords of western Norway. They are believed to have sailed first to <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, then south to <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>. The Vikings would have then sailed down the Atlantic coast of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and then over to Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Woolfp47_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woolfp47-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During these early raids the Vikings also travelled to the west coast of Ireland to the <a href="/wiki/Skellig_Islands" title="Skellig Islands">Skellig Islands</a> located off the coast of <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a>. The early raids on Ireland seem to have been aristocratic free enterprise, and named leaders appear in the Irish annals: Saxolb (Soxulfr) in <a href="/wiki/837_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="837 in Ireland">837</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turgesius" title="Turgesius">Turges</a> (\xc3\x9eurgestr) in <a href="/wiki/845_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="845 in Ireland">845</a>, Agonn (H\xc3\xa1kon) in <a href="/wiki/847_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="847 in Ireland">847</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".C3.81ed_Oirdnide"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="\xc3\x81ed_Oirdnide">\xc3\x81ed Oirdnide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: \xc3\x81ed Oirdnide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/%C3%81ed_Oirdnide" title="\xc3\x81ed Oirdnide">\xc3\x81ed Oirdnide</a> of the Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain branch of the Northern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill became King of Tara in 797, after the death of his predecessor, father-in-law and political rival <a href="/wiki/Donnchad_Midi" title="Donnchad Midi">Donnchad Midi</a>. (Duncan) This followed the classic U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill political arrangement, where over-kingship alternated regularly between Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain and Clann Cholm\xc3\xa1in of the Southern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill. During his reign he campaigned in Mide, Leinster and Ulaid to assert his authority, though unlike Donnchad (Duncan) he did not campaign in Munster.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles-Edwards" title="Thomas Charles-Edwards">Thomas Charles-Edwards</a> credits \xc3\x81ed for "the absence of any major Viking attacks on Ireland during his reign after 798".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The annals gives no reference, however, to \xc3\x81ed at any time being involved with warfare against Viking raiders.\n</p><p>\xc3\x81ed was connected to the monastic community at Armagh, and a supporter of the <i>familia</i> of <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Patrick">Patrick</a>. His rivals for supremacy within U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill, the Clann Cholm\xc3\xa1in and the Cen\xc3\xa9l Conaill, had on the other hand supported the familia of <a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">Columba</a>. During \xc3\x81ed\'s reign the Columban familia, following several Viking raids against <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a>, established a new monastery at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Kells" title="Abbey of Kells">Kells</a>, a royal site in the possession of Armagh. Byrne states that "...the foundation [of Kells] marked the resolution of any remaining rivalry between the Columban and Patrician churches...".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> That the community of Columba in 817 tried to have \xc3\x81ed excommunicated may show that not all rivalry was resolved after all.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivalry_between_north_and_south">Rivalry between north and south</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rivalry between north and south">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="rquote pullquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">\n\n<tbody><tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">\n<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">\xe2\x80\x9c\n</td>\n<td style="padding:0 10px;"><i>Is he Feidhlimidh in ri<br />dianid opair oenlaithi<br />eitrige Connacht cen cath<br />ocus Midhe do manrath<br /></i>\n<p>(Feidlimid is the king<br />For whom a single day\'s work is<br />To take the hostages of Connacht without battle<br />And to spoil Mide.)\n</p>\n</td>\n<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">\xe2\x80\x9d\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:normal; text-align:right;"><cite>\xe2\x80\x94&#160;<a href="#AU">Annals of Ulster, 840.4</a></cite>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Fedelmid_mac_Crimthainn" title="Fedelmid mac Crimthainn">Fedelmid mac Crimthainn</a> from the <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B3ganacht_Chaisil" title="E\xc3\xb3ganacht Chaisil">E\xc3\xb3ganacht Chaisil</a> acceded to the <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Munster" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Munster">kingship of Munster</a> in 820, beginning a 130-year domination by this branch of E\xc3\xb3ganachta.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_Feidlimid_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB_Feidlimid-20">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Combining military campaigns with manipulation of ecclesiastical affairs, he embarked on a policy of aggressive expansion to counter the growth in power of the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill. <a href="/wiki/Conchobar_mac_Donnchada" title="Conchobar mac Donnchada">Conchobar mac Donnchada</a> (Duncan) succeeded \xc3\x81ed Oirdnide as U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill overking in 819, and soon found himself challenged by Feidlimid, both by Feidlimid launching raids into Mide and Connacht and by him interfering (as would be the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill view) in the affairs of Armagh. Conchobar and Feidlimid met at <a href="/wiki/Birr,_County_Offaly" title="Birr, County Offaly">Birr</a> in 827 to discuss peace terms, and the very fact that "the king of Munster could force the high-king to a peace conference is indicative of Feidlimid\'s growing power".<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_Feidlimid_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB_Feidlimid-20">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Conchobar was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Niall_Caille" title="Niall Caille">Niall Caille</a> in 833. With Niall, we for the first time see a reference in the annals of a U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill leading an army against the Vikings; he defeated Viking raiders in <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a> the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He sought to further expand U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill influence in the south; in 835 he led an army to Leinster and installed <a href="/wiki/Bran_mac_F%C3%A1el%C3%A1in" title="Bran mac F\xc3\xa1el\xc3\xa1in">Bran mac F\xc3\xa1el\xc3\xa1in</a> as king of Leinster, and also invaded Mide.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This brought him into conflict with Feidlimid, however, and in 838 a conference (<i>r\xc3\xadgd\xc3\xa1l m\xc3\xb3r</i>\xe2\x80\x94"great royal meeting") between Niall and Feidlimid was held.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;note 5&#93;</a></sup> This meeting did not result in any lasting peace though; in 840 Feidlimid led an army into Mide and encamped at Tara, thereby challenging the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill also in the north. In 841, however, Feidlimid was routed in battle by Niall in Leinster. His successors in the south would not be able to challenge the north again to this extent until some 150 years later.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intensified_raiding_and_the_first_Viking_settlements_in_Ireland">Intensified raiding and the first Viking settlements in Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Intensified raiding and the first Viking settlements in Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Early_Scandinavian_Dublin#The_first_Norse_settlements_in_Dublin" title="Early Scandinavian Dublin">Early Scandinavian Dublin \xc2\xa7&#160;The first Norse settlements in Dublin</a></div>\n<p>The Viking raids on Ireland resumed in 821, and intensified during the following decades. The Vikings were beginning to establish fortified encampments, <i><a href="/wiki/Longports" class="mw-redirect" title="Longports">longports</a></i>, along the Irish coast and overwintering in Ireland instead of retreating to <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> bases. The first known longports were at <i>Linn D\xc3\xbaachaill</i> (<a href="/wiki/Annagassan" title="Annagassan">Annagassan</a>) and <i>Duiblinn</i> (on the <a href="/wiki/River_Liffey" title="River Liffey">River Liffey</a>, at or near present <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;note 6&#93;</a></sup> They were also moving further inland to attack, often using rivers such as the Shannon, and then retreating to their coastal bases. The raiding parties also increased in size, becoming regular armies\xe2\x80\x94in 837 the annals report a fleet of sixty <a href="/wiki/Longship" title="Longship">longships</a> on the Liffey, carrying 1,500 men, and another one of a similar size sailing up the river Boyne, making their way into the inland territories and launching attacks on the lands of Brega in the south of County Meath.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In general, from 837 onward larger Viking forces hit larger targets - such as the greater monastic towns of Armagh, Glendalough, Kildare, Slane, Clonard, Clonmacnoise, and Lismore - while smaller targets such as local churches with less material to be plundered may have escaped the Vikings\' attention.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vidfamne-01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Vidfamne-01.jpg/220px-Vidfamne-01.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Vidfamne-01.jpg/330px-Vidfamne-01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/Vidfamne-01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vidfamne-01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern replica of a Viking <a href="/wiki/Knarr" title="Knarr">Knarr</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>One of the first named Viking leaders was <a href="/wiki/Thorgest" class="mw-redirect" title="Thorgest">Thorgest</a> (in Latin <i>Turgesius</i>). The <a href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Four_Masters" title="Annals of the Four Masters">Annals of the Four Masters</a> connect him with attacks on <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mide" class="mw-redirect" title="Mide">Mide</a> and the church at <a href="/wiki/Clonmacnoise" title="Clonmacnoise">Clonmacnoise</a> in 844.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He was captured and drowned in <a href="/wiki/Lough_Owel" title="Lough Owel">Lough Owel</a> by <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1el_Sechnaill_mac_Ma%C3%ADl_Ruanaid" class="mw-redirect" title="M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill mac Ma\xc3\xadl Ruanaid">M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill mac Ma\xc3\xadl Ruanaid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Mide" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Mide">King of Mide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> However the existence of <a href="/wiki/Thorgest" class="mw-redirect" title="Thorgest">Thorgest</a> is uncertain as he was recorded in books written over 200 years after his death in a period of strong anti-Viking sentiment. He was often depicted as a villain and his wife was said to be a witch that would perform pagan rituals on altars. It is highly likely that this was war propaganda as we have very little evidence of his existence from his own period, and it is unlikely those close to him would record this themselves.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/848_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="848 in Ireland">848</a> a Norse army was defeated at Sciath Nechtain by <a href="/wiki/%C3%93lchobar_mac_Cin%C3%A1eda" title="\xc3\x93lchobar mac Cin\xc3\xa1eda">\xc3\x93lchobar mac Cin\xc3\xa1eda</a> of Munster and <a href="/wiki/Lorc%C3%A1n_mac_Cellaig" title="Lorc\xc3\xa1n mac Cellaig">Lorc\xc3\xa1n mac Cellaig</a> of Leinster. For the first time the leader of the Vikings is described as royalty from <a href="/wiki/Lochlann" title="Lochlann">Lochlann</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill, now High King, defeated another army at <i>Forrach</i> the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> These victories form the background of an embassy sent to the Frankish emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a>, reported in the <a href="/wiki/Annales_Bertiniani" title="Annales Bertiniani">Annales Bertiniani</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 853 <a href="/wiki/Olaf_the_White" title="Olaf the White">Olaf</a>, identified as a "son of the king of Lochlann",<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> came to Ireland. <i>Lochlann</i> has been understood as (a district of) present <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>; it is now considered more plausible that it refers to a Scandinavian colony in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Western Isles</a> of Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;note 7&#93;</a></sup> Olaf assumed leadership of the Vikings in Ireland, probably in some way shared with his kinsman <a href="/wiki/Ivar_the_Boneless" title="Ivar the Boneless">Ivar</a>, first mentioned in the Irish Annals in 857. Olaf and Ivar remained active in Ireland and around the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> for the next two decades. The descendants of Ivar, the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_%C3%8Dmair" title="U\xc3\xad \xc3\x8dmair">U\xc3\xad \xc3\x8dmair</a>, would be an important political factor for the next two centuries.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shifting_alliances_and_struggle_for_power">Shifting alliances and struggle for power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Shifting alliances and struggle for power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A significant new trait from the middle of the 9th century was that the Norse now also entered alliances with various Irish rulers. <a href="/wiki/Cerball_mac_D%C3%BAnlainge" title="Cerball mac D\xc3\xbanlainge">Cerball mac D\xc3\xbanlainge</a> had become king of Osraige in 842. Cerball had defeated Viking raiders in 846 and 847, but from 858 he is allied with Olaf and Ivar against M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill, campaigning in Leinster and Munster, and in 859 also raiding M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill\'s heartlands in Mide, though Cerball had to submit to M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill later the same year. These alliances were by no means permanent. In 860 Cerball was allied <i>with</i> M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill in a campaign against <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ed_Findliath" title="\xc3\x81ed Findliath">\xc3\x81ed Findliath</a> of the Northern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill, while Olaf and Ivar has allied themselves with \xc3\x81ed. In 870, however, Cerball and \xc3\x81ed appeared as allies in Leinster.\n</p><p>M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill had more success as high king than his predecessors Niall Caille and Conchobar Donnchada (Duncan) in dealing with the south, and forced Munster into submission in 858 and as noted above, Osraige in 859. He also asserted control over Ulaid, Leinster and Connacht, and was in his obituary in the Annals of Ulster described as <i>ri h-Erenn uile</i>, king of all Ireland. The last years of his reign he had however experienced serious opposition from his U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill kinsmen of Ailech and Brega, allied with the Norse of Dublin. <a href="/wiki/Francis_John_Byrne" title="Francis John Byrne">Byrne</a> notes: "M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill\'s unprecedented success in achieving the high-kingship of all Ireland was marred by the chronic complaint of Irish politics: having united the Ulaid, Munster, Osraige, Connacht and Leinster, he was attacked at the end of his reign by a combination of U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill kings."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\xc3\x81ed Findliath was king of Ailech and the leading king within the Northern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill. After the death of M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill he is counted in the regal lists as high king, following the established scheme where this alternated between Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain in the north and Clann Cholm\xc3\xa1in of Mide. His kingship was disputed though, and he did not come close to being an actual king over Ireland. He could count some successes against the Norse, however, most notably burning all the Norse longports in the north in 866.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> \xc3\x81ed seems to have used the opportunity while Olaf was involved in warfare in <a href="/wiki/Pictland" class="mw-redirect" title="Pictland">Pictland</a>, presumably bringing a large contingent of the Norse forces in Ireland with him. The Vikings never managed to establish permanent settlements in the north. <a href="/wiki/Donnchadh_%C3%93_Corr%C3%A1in" title="Donnchadh \xc3\x93 Corr\xc3\xa1in">\xc3\x93 Corr\xc3\xa1in</a> observes: "Ironically, [\xc3\x81ed Findliath\'s] success may have held back the economic development of the north and ultimately prevented the growth of port towns like those on the east and south coasts, on which the Leinster and Munster kings subsequently depended for much of their wealth."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The last report of Olaf is when he and Ivar returned to Dublin in 871 from <a href="/wiki/Alba" title="Alba">Alba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Ivar died in 873. In his obituary, the Annals of Ulster call Ivar "king of the Norsemen of all Ireland and Britain".<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> With their disappearance, there were frequent changes of leadership among the Norse in Ireland and a great deal of internecine conflict is reported for the following decades.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\nIn 902 <a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A1el_Finnia_mac_Flannacain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="M\xc3\xa1el Finnia mac Flannacain (page does not exist)">M\xc3\xa1el Finnia mac Flannacain</a> of Brega and <a href="/wiki/Cerball_mac_Muirec%C3%A1in" title="Cerball mac Muirec\xc3\xa1in">Cerball mac Muirec\xc3\xa1in</a> of Leinster joined forces against Dublin, and "The heathens were driven from Ireland, i.e. from the fortress of \xc3\x81th Cliath [Dublin]".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;note 8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A group of Vikings led by Hingamund who were forced out of Ireland were given permission by the Saxons to settle in <a href="/wiki/Wirral_Peninsula" title="Wirral Peninsula">Wirral</a>, in the north west of England. "The Three Fragments" refers to a distinct group of settlers living among these Vikings as "Irishmen": "Then the King, who was on the point of death, and the Queen sent messengers to the Irishmen who were among the pagans, for there were many Irish among the pagans, to say to the Irishmen, life and health to you from the King of the Saxons, who is in disease, and from his Queen, who has all authority over the Saxons, and they are certain that you are true and trusty friends to them. Therefore you should take their side; for they did not bestow any greater honour to a Saxon warror or cleric than to each warrior and cleric who came to them from Ireland, because this inimical race of pagans is equally hostile to you also." Further evidence of an Irish presence in Wirral comes from the name of the village of <a href="/wiki/Irby,_Merseyside" title="Irby, Merseyside">Irby</a> in Wirral, which means "farmstead of the Irishmen", and <a href="/wiki/St_Bridget%27s_Church,_West_Kirby" title="St Bridget&#39;s Church, West Kirby">St Bridget\'s Church, West Kirby</a> which is known to have been founded by "Christian Vikings from Ireland".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_Conquest">Failed Conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Failed Conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Vikings were able to exploit internal divisions in order to invade England and France. As Ireland was one of the most politically fractured countries at the time, it was a prime target for Viking conquest. Furthermore, Irish Kings often made alliances with foreign invaders in an attempt to weaken their domestic rivals. The Vikings were able to defeat the centralized Kingdoms of Europe, since the small ruling class was easily removed. However, Ireland was composed of more than 150 different Kingdoms ruling over small territories. This decentralized system of governance made it almost impossible to gain control of a territory, since defeated Kings were easily replaced.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_cultural_activity_and_formation_of_Irish_scholarly_diaspora">Impact on cultural activity and formation of Irish scholarly diaspora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impact on cultural activity and formation of Irish scholarly diaspora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Historians debate the consequences that the initial phases of Viking settlement had on scholarship and literary output.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Scholarly activity, for which the Irish are famous in the early Medieval period, consisted of the writing of poetry, the production of Christian devotional texts, the development of the science of <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">computus</a>, and the compilation of elaborate law tracts.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Patronage for scholars and scholarship came largely from Irish kings who regarded the presence of court scholars as part and parcel of the trappings of kingship, but also as a means of bolstering their own image through praise poetry that such scholars composed and performed. With the advent of the Vikings, patronage ties have been argued to have been loosened, thereby affecting directly the livelihood of Irish scholars. In addition, some kings would have turned their attention to more pressing (but also more profitable) pursuits, such as engaging in warfare or alliances with Vikings or other Irish kings, as well as tapping the economic benefits that Viking trade would have brought. While this was happening in Ireland, we observe a more pronounced presence of Irish scholars in Frankish Europe, and especially in circles associated with the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingian">Carolingian</a> court. Commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Irish_Diaspora" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Diaspora"><i>peregrini</i></a>, Irish scholars such as <a href="/wiki/John_Scottus_Eriugena" class="mw-redirect" title="John Scottus Eriugena">John Scottus Eriugena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedulius_Scottus" title="Sedulius Scottus">Sedulius Scottus</a> became among the most prominent and influential in ninth-century continental Europe, studying and teaching a range of subjects, from theology to political philosophy. It is tempting to link their presence on the Continent as well as the presence of scores of other Irish learned people with the impact of the Vikings on Ireland, which may have driven such people to seek employment elsewhere. If one accepts this version of events, then the Vikings can be said to have inadvertently been one of the principal catalysts for the spread of Irish culture abroad and the subsequent foundation of Irish centres on the Continent which remained influential for centuries to come.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Second_Viking_age_.28914.E2.80.93980.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Viking_age_(914\xe2\x80\x93980)">Second Viking age (914\xe2\x80\x93980)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Second Viking age (914\xe2\x80\x93980)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Norse-Gaels" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse-Gaels">Norse-Gaels</a></div>\n<div role="navigation" class="navbox" aria-labelledby="Viking_wars_in_Ireland" style="margin:0;float:right;clear:right;width:25.5em;margin-bottom:0.5em;margin-left:1em;;padding:3px"><table class="nowraplinks collapsible autocollapse navbox-inner" style="border-spacing:0;background:transparent;color:inherit"><tbody><tr><th scope="col" class="navbox-title" colspan="2" style="background-color:#C3D6EF;"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Campaignbox_Viking_wars_in_Ireland" title="Template:Campaignbox Viking wars in Ireland"><abbr title="View this template" style=";background-color:#C3D6EF;;background:none transparent;border:none;-moz-box-shadow:none;-webkit-box-shadow:none;box-shadow:none; padding:0;">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Campaignbox_Viking_wars_in_Ireland" title="Template talk:Campaignbox Viking wars in Ireland"><abbr title="Discuss this template" style=";background-color:#C3D6EF;;background:none transparent;border:none;-moz-box-shadow:none;-webkit-box-shadow:none;box-shadow:none; padding:0;">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Campaignbox_Viking_wars_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template" style=";background-color:#C3D6EF;;background:none transparent;border:none;-moz-box-shadow:none;-webkit-box-shadow:none;box-shadow:none; padding:0;">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div><div id="Viking_wars_in_Ireland" style="font-size:114%;margin:0 4em"><span style="line-height:1.6em"><a href="/wiki/Early_Scandinavian_Dublin" title="Early Scandinavian Dublin"><span class="wrap">Viking wars in Ireland</span></a></span></div></th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" class="navbox-list navbox-odd hlist" style="width:100%;padding:0px"><div style="padding:0em 0.25em">\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Strangford_Lough" title="Battle of Strangford Lough">Strangford Loch</a> (877)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Confey" title="Battle of Confey">Cenn Fuait</a> (915)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mag_Femen" title="Battle of Mag Femen">Mag Femen</a> (917)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Islandbridge" title="Battle of Islandbridge">\xc3\x81th Cliath</a> (919)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sulcoit" title="Battle of Sulcoit">Sulcoit</a> (968)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Luimnech" title="Burning of Luimnech">Luimnech</a> (968)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cathair_Cuan" title="Battle of Cathair Cuan">Cathair Cuan</a> (977)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tara_(Ireland)" title="Battle of Tara (Ireland)">Tara</a> (980)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Glenmama" title="Battle of Glenmama">Glenmama</a> (999)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Clontarf" title="Battle of Clontarf">Clontarf</a> (1014)</li></ul>\n</div></td></tr></tbody></table></div>\n<p>After having been forced to leave Dublin in 902, the descendants of Ivar, now described generically in the annals as the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_%C3%8Dmair" title="U\xc3\xad \xc3\x8dmair">U\xc3\xad \xc3\x8dmair</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;note 9&#93;</a></sup> remained active around the <a href="/wiki/Irish_sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish sea">Irish sea</a>; reports tell of their activities in <a href="/wiki/Pictland" class="mw-redirect" title="Pictland">Pictland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strathclyde" title="Strathclyde">Strathclyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Man" title="Man">Man</a>. In 914 a new Viking fleet appeared in <a href="/wiki/Waterford_Harbour" title="Waterford Harbour">Waterford Harbour</a>, and soon the U\xc3\xad \xc3\x8dmair followed, again taking control over Viking activities in Ireland. <a href="/wiki/Ragnall_ua_%C3%8Dmair" title="Ragnall ua \xc3\x8dmair">Ragnall</a> arrived with a fleet in Waterford, while <a href="/wiki/Sihtric_C%C3%A1ech" class="mw-redirect" title="Sihtric C\xc3\xa1ech">Sitric</a> landed at <i>Cenn Fuait</i> (possibly near <a href="/wiki/Leixlip" title="Leixlip">Leixlip</a>) in Leinster. <a href="/wiki/Niall_Gl%C3%BAndub" title="Niall Gl\xc3\xbandub">Niall Gl\xc3\xbandub</a> had followed Flann Sinna as U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill overking in 916, and he marched into Munster against Ragnall, but no decisive engagement followed. The men of Leinster under <a href="/wiki/Augaire_mac_Ailella" title="Augaire mac Ailella">Augaire mac Ailella</a> attacked Sithric but suffered a heavy defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Confey" title="Battle of Confey">Battle of Confey</a> or <i>Cenn Fuait</i> (917). This victory allowed Sithric to re-establish Norse control over Dublin. Ragnall left Ireland again in 918, and became king of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;note 10&#93;</a></sup>\nWith Sithric in Dublin and Ragnall in York, a Dublin-York axis developed which would have influence on both England and Ireland for the next half-century.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Viking_Ireland.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Viking_Ireland.png/220px-Viking_Ireland.png" width="220" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/15/Viking_Ireland.png 1.5x" data-file-width="292" data-file-height="274" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Viking_Ireland.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing the major Norse settlements in Ireland in the 10th Century</div></div></div>\n<p>A new and more intensive period of Viking settlement in Ireland began in 914. Between 914 and 922 the Norse established <a href="/wiki/Waterford" title="Waterford">Waterford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;note 11&#93;</a></sup> Significant excavations in Dublin and Waterford in the 20th century have unearthed much of the Viking heritage of those cities. A large amount of Viking burial stones, called the Rathdown Slabs, have been found in multiple locations across South Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Vikings founded many other coastal towns, and after several generations of coexistence and intermarriage a group of mixed Irish and Norse ethnic background arose (often called <i><a href="/wiki/Norse-Gaels" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse-Gaels">Norse-Gaels</a></i> or <i>Hiberno-Norse</i>).  Norse influence shows in the Norse-derived names of many contemporary Irish kings (e.g. Magnus, Lochlann or Sitric), and in DNA evidence in some residents of these coastal cities to this day. A genetics paper in 2006 by Dr Brian McEvoy found that most men with Irish-Viking surnames carried typically Irish genes. This suggests that Viking settlements may have had a Scandinavian elite but with most of the inhabitants being indigenous Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Niall Gl\xc3\xbandub marched on Dublin in September 919, but Sihtric met his forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Islandbridge" title="Battle of Islandbridge">battle of Islandbridge</a> or <i>\xc3\x81th Cliath</i> and inflicted on him a decisive defeat, with Niall and numerous other Irish leaders among the casualties. Dublin was secured for the Norse, and in 920 Sitric left for York and following Ragnall\'s death succeeded him as ruler there in 921. Their kinsman <a href="/wiki/Gofraid_ua_%C3%8Dmair" title="Gofraid ua \xc3\x8dmair">Gofraid</a> assumed control of Dublin. Gofraid was active as a Viking raider and slaver, but there were signs during his reign that the Norse were not just mere Vikings any more. During a raid at Armagh in 921 Gofraid "...spared the prayerhouses... ...and the sick from destruction",<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> considerations never taken by the raiders of the previous century. Another was the intense campaigns led by Dublin in eastern Ulster from 921 to 927, which appear to have aimed at conquest in order to create a Scandinavian kingdom like the one on the eastern side of the Irish sea.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Dublin\'s ambitions in Ulster were halted by a series of defeats inflicted upon the Norse by <a href="/wiki/Muirchertach_mac_N%C3%A9ill" title="Muirchertach mac N\xc3\xa9ill">Muirchertach mac N\xc3\xa9ill</a>, the son of Niall Gl\xc3\xbandub. According to Benjamin Hudson, "Muirchertach was one of the most successful generals of his day and was described as the \xe2\x80\x98Hector of the Irish\xe2\x80\x99".<sup id="cite_ref-HudsonODNB-Muirch_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HudsonODNB-Muirch-57">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In the annals, it is (Duncan) <a href="/wiki/Donnchad_Donn" title="Donnchad Donn">Donnchad Donn</a> from Clann Cholm\xc3\xa1in who is titled "high king" after Niall however, and Muirchertach did not succeed his father as king of Ailech either until 938. Apart from his victories over the Norse, Muirchertach led campaigns forcing other provincial kingdoms into submission, most notably taking the king of Munster <a href="/wiki/Cellach%C3%A1n_Caisil" title="Cellach\xc3\xa1n Caisil">Cellach\xc3\xa1n Caisil</a> captive in 941. The same year he led a fleet to the Hebrides, collecting tribute there.<sup id="cite_ref-HudsonODNB-Muirch_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HudsonODNB-Muirch-57">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;note 12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When Sihtric died in 927 Gofraid left for York, trying to assume kingship there. He was driven out by <a href="/wiki/Athelstan_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Athelstan of England">Athelstan</a>, and returned to Dublin half a year later. The Vikings of Limerick had taken Dublin in his absence. Gofraid retook the city, but the struggle between Limerick continued well after Gofraid\'s death in 934. He was succeeded by his son, <a href="/wiki/Amla%C3%ADb_mac_Gofraid_(died_941)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amla\xc3\xadb mac Gofraid (died 941)">Amla\xc3\xadb</a>, who inflicted a decisive defeat on Limerick in 937. The same year Amla\xc3\xadb went to Northumbria and allied himself with <a href="/wiki/Constantine_II_of_Scotland" title="Constantine II of Scotland">Constantine II of Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owen_I_of_Strathclyde" class="mw-redirect" title="Owen I of Strathclyde">Owen I of Strathclyde</a>. Athelstan defeated this coalition at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brunanburh" title="Battle of Brunanburh">Brunanburh</a> (937), but after Athelstan\'s death in 939 Amla\xc3\xadb became king of York. He was joined by a kinsman with the same name, <a href="/wiki/Amla%C3%ADb_Cuar%C3%A1n" title="Amla\xc3\xadb Cuar\xc3\xa1n">Amla\xc3\xadb</a> son of Sihtric, known as <i>Amla\xc3\xadb Cuar\xc3\xa1n</i>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Congalach_mac_M%C3%A1el_Mithig" class="mw-redirect" title="Congalach mac M\xc3\xa1el Mithig">Congalach mac M\xc3\xa1el Mithig</a>, known as <i>Cnogba</i>, succeeded (Duncan) Donnchad Donn as U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill overking in 944 (Muirchertag, who otherwise might have been the obvious successor, had been killed in 943). Congalach was king of Brega and a member of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%ADl_n%C3%81edo_Sl%C3%A1ine" title="S\xc3\xadl n\xc3\x81edo Sl\xc3\xa1ine">S\xc3\xadl n\xc3\x81edo Sl\xc3\xa1ine</a>, and the first of this dynasty called "High King" since <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A1ed_mac_%C3%8Drgalaig" title="Cin\xc3\xa1ed mac \xc3\x8drgalaig">Cin\xc3\xa1ed mac \xc3\x8drgalaig</a> in the early 8th century. In 944 he sacked Dublin, now ruled by <a href="/wiki/Bl%C3%A1caire_mac_Gofrith" class="mw-redirect" title="Bl\xc3\xa1caire mac Gofrith">Bl\xc3\xa1caire mac Gofrith</a>. When Amla\xc3\xadb Cuaran returned to Ireland the next year, he became ruler of Dublin and acted as an ally of Congalach in the struggle against <a href="/wiki/Ruaidr%C3%AD_ua_Canann%C3%A1in" title="Ruaidr\xc3\xad ua Canann\xc3\xa1in">Ruaidr\xc3\xad ua Canann\xc3\xa1in</a>, a rival U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill claimant for High Kingship from <a href="/wiki/Cen%C3%A9l_Conaill" class="mw-redirect" title="Cen\xc3\xa9l Conaill">Cen\xc3\xa9l Conaill</a>. This alliance did not last long after Ruaidr\xc3\xad\'s death in 950, however, and Congalach was killed in 956 in a battle against an alliance of Dublin and Leinster. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Domnall_ua_N%C3%A9ill" title="Domnall ua N\xc3\xa9ill">Domnall ua N\xc3\xa9ill</a>, and in the following decades alliances shifted constantly between the different branches of U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill, Leinster and Dublin.\n</p><p>In 980 <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1el_Sechnaill_mac_Domnaill" title="M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill mac Domnaill">M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill mac Domnaill</a> succeeded Domnall, and the same year he defeated the forces of Dublin at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tara_(Ireland)" title="Battle of Tara (Ireland)">battle of Tara</a>. Following this victory M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill forced Dublin into submission, and his half-brother, Amla\xc3\xadbs son <a href="/wiki/Gl%C3%BAniairn" title="Gl\xc3\xbaniairn">Gl\xc3\xbaniairn</a>, became ruler in Dublin.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="M.C3.A1el_Sechnaill_mac_Domnaill_and_Brian_Boru_.28980.E2.80.931022.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="M\xc3\xa1el_Sechnaill_mac_Domnaill_and_Brian_Boru_(980\xe2\x80\x931022)">M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill mac Domnaill and Brian Boru (980\xe2\x80\x931022)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill mac Domnaill and Brian Boru (980\xe2\x80\x931022)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brian_Boru,_King_of_Munster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg/220px-Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg" width="220" height="334" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9a/Brian_Boru%2C_King_of_Munster.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="266" data-file-height="404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brian_Boru,_King_of_Munster.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>18th-century <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru" title="Brian Boru">Brian Boru</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In Munster, the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Dal_gCais" class="mw-redirect" title="Dal gCais">Dal gCais</a> had grown under <a href="/wiki/Cenn%C3%A9tig_mac_Lorc%C3%A1in" title="Cenn\xc3\xa9tig mac Lorc\xc3\xa1in">Cenn\xc3\xa9tig mac Lorc\xc3\xa1in</a>, and his son <a href="/wiki/Mathgamain_mac_Cenn%C3%A9tig" title="Mathgamain mac Cenn\xc3\xa9tig">Mathgamain</a> was the first non-<a href="/wiki/E%C3%B3ganachta" title="E\xc3\xb3ganachta">E\xc3\xb3ganachta</a> ruler to be named by the annals as king of Cashel (i.e. king of Munster) in historical times.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBBrian_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBBrian-59">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> He was killed in 976, and succeeded by his brother <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boru" title="Brian Boru">Brian</a>, later famously known as <i>Brian Boru</i>.\n</p><p>Brian quickly established himself as the most powerful ruler in Munster, defeating the Norse of Limerick in 977 and the E\xc3\xb3ganachta the following year. Having gained control over Munster, he tried to extend his authority by raiding Osraige in 982 and 983, and also, according to the <a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Innisfallen" class="mw-redirect" title="Annals of Innisfallen">annals of Innisfallen</a>, entering an alliance with the Norse of <a href="/wiki/Waterford" title="Waterford">Waterford</a>, with the intention of attacking Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBBrian_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBBrian-59">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> There was such an attack, but Brian does not seem to have been involved \xe2\x80\x93 instead it was <a href="/wiki/Domnall_Claen" title="Domnall Claen">Domnall Claen</a> of Leinster who was allied with <a href="/wiki/Ivar_of_Waterford" title="Ivar of Waterford">Ivar of Waterford</a>, and they were defeated by Gl\xc3\xbaniairn and M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill.\n</p><p>M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill obviously perceived Brian as a threat, and as early as 982 raided Munster and the territory of the Dal gCais. The next two decades saw more or less constant warfare between them, mostly with Leinster as their battleground. Even if Brian never defeated M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill in battle, Brian\'s and Munster\'s influence was growing at the expense of M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill and the Southern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill. In 997 M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill was forced to acknowledge Brian\'s authority over the south of Ireland, and they formally divided Ireland according to the traditional scheme of <a href="/wiki/Leath_Cuinn" class="mw-redirect" title="Leath Cuinn">Leath Cuinn and Leath Moga</a>. In the years that followed, the two of them acted as allies in accordance with this agreement. In 999 Brian quelled a revolt against him by the men of Leinster and Dublin at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Glen_Mama" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Glen Mama">battle of Glen Mama</a>, and only restored <a href="/wiki/Sigtrygg_Silkbeard" title="Sigtrygg Silkbeard">Sigtrygg Silkbeard</a> as ruler of Dublin after he had formally submitted to Brian by handing over hostages.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBBrian_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBBrian-59">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1000, Brian turned against M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill, and by 1002 he had forced M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill to submit to him, and now claimed kingship over the whole of Ireland. In the following decade, there were several campaigns in the north to force the Ulaid and the Northern U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill into submission as well. Even if faced with multiple rebellions, both in the north and in Leinster, by 1011 he had received submission from every major regional king in Ireland, and thus earned the recognition by historians as the first real <i>king of Ireland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jaski05Brian_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaski05Brian-60">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> During his visit to Armagh in 1005, he had his secretary add a note to the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Armagh" title="Book of Armagh">Book of Armagh</a> where he is proclaimed as <i>Imperator Scottorum</i> (emperor of the Irish). According to Bart Jaski, "This can be regarded as a claim that he ruled both the Irish and the Norse in Ireland, and may even imply suzerainty over the Gaels of Scotland".<sup id="cite_ref-Jaski05Brian_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaski05Brian-60">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In his obituary in the Annals of Ulster he is styled as "over-king of the Irish of Ireland, and of the foreigners and of the Britons, the Augustus of the whole of north-west Europe".<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1012, <a href="/wiki/Flaithbertach_Ua_N%C3%A9ill" title="Flaithbertach Ua N\xc3\xa9ill">Flaithbertach Ua N\xc3\xa9ill</a> revolted against Brian, and the following year <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1el_M%C3%B3rda_mac_Murchada" title="M\xc3\xa1el M\xc3\xb3rda mac Murchada">M\xc3\xa1el M\xc3\xb3rda</a> of Leinster and Sigtrygg of Dublin did too. The latter led to the famous <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Clontarf" title="Battle of Clontarf">battle of Clontarf</a>, where Brian was killed, even if his army was victorious over M\xc3\xa1el M\xc3\xb3rda, Sigtrygg and their allies. <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Hlodvirsson,_Earl_of_Orkney" class="mw-redirect" title="Sigurd Hlodvirsson, Earl of Orkney">Sigurd Hlodvirsson, Earl of Orkney</a> as well as forces from <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Man</a> participated on the Dublin/ Leinster side, and this may, in conjunction with the propagandistic account of the battle given in the Cogad, have created the still popular myth that what took place at Clontarf <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a> 1014 was a decisive battle where the Irish defeated Viking invaders and were liberated from oppression (Duncan) <a href="/wiki/Donnchadh_%C3%93_Corr%C3%A1in" title="Donnchadh \xc3\x93 Corr\xc3\xa1in">Donnchadh \xc3\x93 Corr\xc3\xa1in</a> was one of the first to publicly debunk this national myth, in his groundbreaking <i>Ireland before the Normans</i> from 1972:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The battle of Clontarf was not a struggle between the Irish and the Norse for the sovereignty of Ireland; neither was it a great national victory which broke the power of the Norse forever (long before Clontarf the Norse had become a minor political force in Irish affairs). In fact Clontarf was part of the internal struggle for sovereignty and was essentially the revolt of the Leinstermen against the dominance of Brian, a revolt in which their Norse allies played an important but secondary role.</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Donnchadh \xc3\x93 Corr\xc3\xa1in<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;note 13&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Following Brian\'s death, M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill resumed as High King, supported by Flaithbertach ua N\xc3\xa9ill.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;note 14&#93;</a></sup> In Munster, internal strife almost immediately began between Brian\'s sons <a href="/wiki/Donnchad_mac_Briain" title="Donnchad mac Briain">Donnchad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tadc_mac_Briain" title="Tadc mac Briain">Tadc</a>, and D\xc3\xbangal Ua Donnchada of E\xc3\xb3ganachta also claimed the kingship of the province. Though Donnchad (Duncan) eventually was victorious, the descendants of Brian would not be able to make a real claim to kingship over Ireland again until <a href="/wiki/Toirdelbach_Ua_Briain" title="Toirdelbach Ua Briain">Toirdelbach Ua Briain</a>. In Leinster, the defeat at Clontarf and death of M\xc3\xa1el M\xc3\xb3rda seriously weakened the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_D%C3%BAnlainge" title="U\xc3\xad D\xc3\xbanlainge">U\xc3\xad D\xc3\xbanlainge</a>, and opened the way for a new <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_Cheinnselaig" class="mw-redirect" title="U\xc3\xad Cheinnselaig">U\xc3\xad Cheinnselaig</a> dominance in the region. Despite the defeat at Clontarf, Sigtrygg remained ruler of Dublin until 1036.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="High_kings_with_opposition_.281022_onwards.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="High_kings_with_opposition_(1022_onwards)">High kings with opposition (1022 onwards)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: High kings with opposition (1022 onwards)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="rquote pullquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; A glossing of <a href="/wiki/Baile_In_Sc%C3%A1il" class="mw-redirect" title="Baile In Sc\xc3\xa1il">Baile In Sc\xc3\xa1il</a> lists Flaitbertach Ua N\xc3\xa9ill as high king, but he proved unable even to control the north of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Neither was anyone else able to make a recognised claim for kingship over all of Ireland: according to Byrne, "what distinguished the great interregnum of 1022\xe2\x80\x9372 from other periods in Irish history is that it was recognised as such by contemporary observers".<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Flann_Mainistrech" class="mw-redirect" title="Flann Mainistrech">Flann Mainistrech</a> had written <i>R\xc3\xadg Themra t\xc3\xb3ebaige iar tain</i>, a regnal poem on the Christian (U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill) kings of Tara some time between 1014 and 1022. When he wrote in 1056 he evidently did not know any high king of Ireland, and instead lists a number of kings of the day: (Duncan) Conchobar, \xc3\x81ed, Garbith, Diarmait, Donnchad (Duncan) and two Nialls. According to Byrne, these are Conchobar Ua Ma\xc3\xadl Schechnaill of Mide, <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ed_in_Gai_Bernaig" title="\xc3\x81ed in Gai Bernaig">\xc3\x81ed Ua Conchobair</a> of Connacht, Garb\xc3\xadth Ua Cathassaig of Brega, <a href="/wiki/Diarmait_mac_Ma%C3%ADl_na_mB%C3%B3" class="mw-redirect" title="Diarmait mac Ma\xc3\xadl na mB\xc3\xb3">Diarmait mac Ma\xc3\xadl na mB\xc3\xb3</a> of Leinster, Donnchad (Duncan) Mac Briain of Munster, Niall mac M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill of Ailech and <a href="/wiki/Niall_mac_Eochada" title="Niall mac Eochada">Niall mac Eochada</a> of Ulaid.<sup id="cite_ref-Byrnep866_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrnep866-64">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term <i>r\xc3\xad \xc3\x89renn co fressarba</i> ("High kings with opposition") was used from the 12th century. According to Byrne, "it could be argued that the \'high kings with opposition\' met with opposition precisely because they tried to become kings of Ireland in a real sense. They were not less successful than their predecessors, but only seemed so in the light of the teaching of the schools".<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Following a similar line of reasoning, Byrne suggests that the focus from historians on the decline of the U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill in the 11th century may be a "tribute to the success of their own propaganda".<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> After Brian, the previous U\xc3\xad N\xc3\xa9ill monopoly of high kingship as described in poems and chronicles was anyway broken for good. The Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain suffered from internal factions, and this allowed the Ulaid, under Niall mac Eochada, to expand their influence. Niall and Diarmait mac Ma\xc3\xadl na mB\xc3\xb3 became allies, and effectively controlled the whole east coast of Ireland. This alliance helped to make it possible for Diarmait to take direct control of Dublin in 1052. Unlike M\xc3\xa1el Sechnaill in 980 or Brian in 999, he wasn\'t content with just looting the city and expelling the Hiberno-Norse ruler (<a href="/wiki/Echmarcach_mac_Ragnaill" title="Echmarcach mac Ragnaill">Echmarcach mac Ragnaill</a>); in an unprecedented move he assumed the kingship "of the foreigners" (<i>r\xc3\xadge Gall</i>) himself.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a 2010)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reform_of_the_Irish_Church">Reform of the Irish Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Ireland_(800%E2%80%931169)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reform of the Irish Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St._Malachy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/St._Malachy.jpg/220px-St._Malachy.jpg" width="220" height="328" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/St._Malachy.jpg/330px-St._Malachy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/St._Malachy.jpg/440px-St._Malachy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2296" data-file-height="3428" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St._Malachy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/St._Malachy" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Malachy">St. Malachy</a> was an influential 12th-century reformist ecclesiastic in Ireland.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Rathbreasail" class="mw-redirect" title="Synod of Rathbreasail">Synod of Rathbreasail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Kells-Mellifont" class="mw-redirect" title="Synod of Kells-Mellifont">Synod of Kells-Mellifont</a></div>\n<p>There were major reforms in the Irish church during the 12th century. These reforms have been generally interpreted as a reaction to previous secularisation, but could also be seen as a continuous development.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The reforms had consequences for, and were influenced by, relations within the church as well as secular politics.\n</p><p>Before the 11th century the church in Ireland was <a href="/wiki/Monastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monastic">monastic</a>, with bishops residing at monasteries and without a permanent <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocesan</a> structure. The circumstances surrounding the foundation of the diocese of Dublin early in the century are obscure, but at some point during the reign of Sithric Silkbeard <a href="/wiki/Donat,_Bishop_of_Dublin" class="mw-redirect" title="Donat, Bishop of Dublin">D\xc3\xban\xc3\xa1n</a> became <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Dublin" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Dublin">Bishop of Dublin</a>, thus establishing the first proper diocese in Ireland. His successor <a href="/wiki/Gilla_P%C3%A1traic" title="Gilla P\xc3\xa1traic">Gilla P\xc3\xa1traic</a> was consecrated by <a href="/wiki/Lanfranc" title="Lanfranc">Lanfranc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, and on that occasion Lanfranc sent letters to Toirdelbach Ua Briain and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gofraid_mac_Amlaib_mac_Ragnaill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gofraid mac Amlaib mac Ragnaill (page does not exist)">Gofraid</a> urging reforms, in particular regarding the consecration of bishops and the abolition of <a href="/wiki/Simony" title="Simony">simony</a>. There is no evidence of Canterbury claiming primacy over the church in Ireland prior to this, and neither Lanfranc nor <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm</a> ever made direct primatial claims for Canterbury in relation to the Irish church.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Toirdelbach appears to have responded favourably to this, and convened a synod in Dublin in 1080<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> \xe2\x80\x93 the outcome of this synod is not known. Toirdelbach may have seen cooperation with Canterbury as a way to reduce the influence from Armagh, traditionally dominated by Cen\xc3\xa9l nE\xc3\xb3gain, within the church in Ireland. Gilla P\xc3\xa1traic\'s successors <a href="/wiki/Donngus_Ua_hAingliu" title="Donngus Ua hAingliu">Donngus Ua hAingliu</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_Ua_h%C3%81ingliu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Samuel Ua h\xc3\x81ingliu (page does not exist)">Samuel Ua h\xc3\x81ingliu</a> were also consecrated in Canterbury, and so was the first bishop of Waterford, <a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A1el_%C3%8Dsu_Ua_hAinmire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="M\xc3\xa1el \xc3\x8dsu Ua hAinmire (page does not exist)">M\xc3\xa1el \xc3\x8dsu Ua hAinmire</a> in 1096. The written request for M\xc3\xa1el \xc3\x8dsu\'s consecration, as preserved in <a href="/wiki/Eadmer" title="Eadmer">Eadmer</a>\'s <i>Historia Novorum</i>, is subscribed by bishops from Munster, Mide, Dublin and Leinster.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gilla_Espaic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gilla Espaic (page does not exist)">Gilla Espaic</a>, the first bishop of Limerick, was however not consecrated in Canterbury, but probably by <a href="/wiki/Ceallach_of_Armagh" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceallach of Armagh">Cellach</a>  of Armagh.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;note 15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first of the four main synods associated with the church reforms of the 12th century took place in Cashel in 1101, at the instigation of Muirchertach Ua Briain. How many who actually attended this synod is not known, but some of its decrees have been preserved. There is a decree on simony, on prohibition for laymen to become <i>airchinnig</i> (heads of ecclesiastical establishments) and finally a decree that defines what relationships are considered to be incestuous. None of these decrees are radical, but they are generally interpreted to be in line with the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_reform" class="mw-redirect" title="Gregorian reform">Gregorian reform</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The second synod was the <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Rathbreasail" class="mw-redirect" title="Synod of Rathbreasail">Synod of Rathbreasail</a>. This synod, presided by Gilla Espaic<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">papal legate</a> and attended by fifty bishops, three hundred priests and over three thousand laymen, marked the transition of the Irish church from a monastic to a diocesan and parish-based church. It established two provinces, with archbishoprics at Armagh and Cashel, and prominence given to Armagh, making Cellach the primate of the church in Ireland. Each province consisted of twelve territorial dioceses. The see of Dublin was not included, as this was under primacy from Canterbury,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;note 16&#93;</a></sup> but a place was left open for it, in the sense that only eleven dioceses were declared under Cashel.\n</p><p>Gilla, Cellach and Cellach\'s successor M\xc3\xa1el M\xc3\xa1ed\xc3\xb3c Ua Morgair, better known as <a href="/wiki/St._Malachy" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Malachy">St. Malachy</a>, drove the reform process onwards. Malachy, in close cooperation with (Duncan) <a href="/wiki/Donnchad_Ua_Cerbaill" title="Donnchad Ua Cerbaill">Donnchad Ua Cerbaill</a>, king of Fernmag/Airgialla, established the first Irish <a href="/wiki/Cistercians" title="Cistercians">Cistercian</a> house at <a href="/wiki/Mellifont" class="mw-redirect" title="Mellifont">Mellifont</a> in 1142, and also facilitated the first <a href="/wiki/Augustinian" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustinian">Augustinian</a> community of the <a href="/wiki/Arrouaise" class="mw-redirect" title="Arrouaise">Arrouaisian</a> observance. Malachy used these as agencies of monastic reform within the Irish church.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Malachy resigned as archbishop of Armagh in 1136, but was appointed native papal legate to Ireland by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_II" title="Pope Innocent II">Innocent II</a> in 1139.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg/220px-Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg" width="220" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg/330px-Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg/440px-Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="548" data-file-height="651" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dioceses_of_Medieval_Ireland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maps of dioceses in Ireland as defined by the synod of Kells. From <i>Historical Atlas</i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Robert_Shepherd" title="William Robert Shepherd">William R. Shepherd</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>No formal attempts on getting papal approval for the structure chosen at Rathbreasail are known before Malachy sought <a href="/wiki/Pallium" title="Pallium">pallia</a> for the two incumbent archbishops at Cashel and Armagh during his trip to the Continent in 1139/40. This first bid was unsuccessful, but Malachy was told to reapply after he had gained the agreement of all Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Before undertaking his second trip to the Continent in 1148, Malachy convened a synod at <a href="/wiki/St_Patrick%27s_Island" title="St Patrick&#39;s Island">St Patrick\'s Island</a>. The main challenge must have been to reach an accommodation with Dublin, and <a href="/wiki/Tairrdelbach_Ua_Conchobair" title="Tairrdelbach Ua Conchobair">Tairrdelbach Ua Conchobair</a>, presently the most powerful king in Ireland, was eager to increase Connacht influence on the church. The solution reached was to extend the number of metropolitan sees from two to four, with <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Tuam" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Tuam">Tuam</a> and Dublin included alongside Cashel and Armagh. Malachy died on his way to meet the pope, but the message was transmitted by other means and papal approval was granted. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Eugene_III" title="Pope Eugene III">Pope Eugene III</a> appointed cardinal <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Paparoni" title="Giovanni Paparoni">John Paparo</a> as papal legate, and sent him to Ireland with pallia for the four archbishops.\n</p><p>Cardinal Paparo\'s first attempt to reach Ireland was stalled when king <a href="/wiki/Stephen_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Stephen of England">Stephen</a> refused him safe conduct through England unless he pledged himself to do nothing in Ireland that would injure England\xe2\x80\x99s interests there. This was not acceptable for Paparo, who returned to Rome. It seems likely that this was an attempt by Stephen to prevent Paparo from bringing papal confirmation for an arrangement in Ireland that would finally extinguish Canterbury\xe2\x80\x99s claims in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> In 1151 he returned and this time reached Ireland, his journey being facilitated by <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">David I of Scotland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Kells-Mellifont" class="mw-redirect" title="Synod of Kells-Mellifont">Synod of Kells-Mellifont</a> was convened in 1152, with Paparo presiding as papal legate. The decrees from the synod are no longer extant, but some information is preserved through the <a href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Four_Masters" title="Annals of the Four Masters">Annals of the Four Masters</a><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Keating" title="Geoffrey Keating">Geoffrey Keating</a>\'s <i>Foras Feasa ar \xc3\x89irinn</i>. The main result of the synod was the official papal sanctioning of the episcopal structure as created in 1111 and refined in 1148.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_invasion">Norman This was at the request of <a href="/wiki/Dermot_MacMurrough" class="mw-redirect" title="Dermot MacMurrough">Dermot MacMurrough</a> (<i>Diarmait Mac Murchada</i>), the ousted <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Leinster" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Leinster">King of Leinster</a> who sought their help in regaining his kingdom.\n</p><p>Then on 18 October 1171, <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> landed a much bigger force in Waterford to at least ensure his continuing control over the Norman force.
