in &#39;shy&#39;">\xca\x83</span><span title="/\xc9\x9b/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">\xc9\x9b</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/\xc9\x99/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">\xc9\x99</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gaeilge" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaeilge">Irish</a>: <i>Seiltis</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> spoken by <a href="/wiki/Irish_Travellers" title="Irish Travellers">Irish Travellers</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is widely known as the <a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">Cant</a>, to its native speakers in Ireland as <b>De Gammon</b>, and to the linguistic community as Shelta.<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It was often used as a <a href="/wiki/Cryptolect" class="mw-redirect" title="Cryptolect">cryptolect</a> to exclude outsiders from comprehending conversations between Travellers,<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> although this aspect is frequently over-emphasised.<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The exact number of native speakers is hard to determine due to sociolinguistic issues<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but <i><a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a></i> puts the number of speakers at 30,000 in UK, 6,000 in Ireland, and 50,000 in the US. The figure for at least the UK is dated to 1990; it is not clear if the other figures are from the same source.<sup id="cite_ref-e12_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e12-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Linguistically Shelta is today seen as a <a href="/wiki/Mixed_language" title="Mixed language">mixed language</a> that stems from a community of travelling people in Ireland that was originally predominantly <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>-speaking. The community later went through a period of widespread bilingualism that resulted in a language based heavily on <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</a> with heavy influences from Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As different varieties of Shelta display different degrees of anglicisation (see below), it is hard to determine the extent of the Irish <a href="/wiki/Substratum" class="mw-redirect" title="Substratum">substratum</a>. The <i>Oxford Companion to the English Language</i> puts it at 2,000\xe2\x80\x933,000 words.<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-2" class="reference"><a id="Names_and_etymology">Names and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The language is known by various names. People outside the Traveller community often refer to it as <i><a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">(the) Cant</a></i>, the etymology of which is a matter of debate.<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Speakers of the language refer to it as <i>(the) Cant</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Gammon</i><sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or <i>Tarri</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Amongst linguists, the name <i>Shelta</i> is the most commonly used term.<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Variants of the above names and additional names include <i>Bog Latin</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Caint\xc3\xadotar</i>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <i>Gammon</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Dolan_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dolan-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i>Sheldru</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Shelter</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Shelteroch</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>the Ould Thing</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Tinker\'s Cant</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The word Shelta appears in print for the first time in 1882 in the book <i>The Gypsies</i> by the "gypsiologist" <a href="/wiki/Charles_Leland" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Leland">Charles Leland</a>, who claimed to have discovered it as the "fifth Celtic tongue". The etymology of the word has long been a matter of debate: Modern <a href="/wiki/Celtic_studies" title="Celtic studies">Celticists</a> are convinced that Irish <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">si\xc3\xbal</i></i> <small>Irish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">[\xca\x83u\xcb\x90l\xca\xb2]</a></span> "to walk" is at the root, either via a term such as <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">si\xc3\xbalt\xc3\xb3ir</i></i> <small>Irish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Irish language text">si\xc3\xbaladh</i></i> (cf. <i>an lucht si\xc3\xbalta</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">[\xc9\x99n\xcb\xa0 l\xcb\xa0uxt\xcc\xaa \xca\x83u\xcb\x90l\xcb\xa0t\xcc\xaa\xcb\xa0\xc9\x99]</a></span>, "the walking people" (lit. <i>the people of href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Dictionary of Hiberno-English</i> cites it as possibly a corruption of the word "Celt".<sup id="cite_ref-Dolan_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dolan-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_history">Origins and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Linguist" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguist">Linguists</a> have been documenting Shelta since at least the 1870s. The first works were published in 1880 and 1882 by Charles Leland.<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Celtic language expert <a href="/wiki/Kuno_Meyer" title="Kuno Meyer">Kuno Meyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">Romani</a> expert <a href="/wiki/John_Sampson_(linguist)" title="John Sampson (linguist)">John Sampson</a> both assert that Shelta existed as far back as the 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the earliest but undocumented period linguists surmise that the Traveller community was <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>-speaking until a period of widespread <a href="/wiki/Bilingualism" class="mw-redirect" title="Bilingualism">bilingualism</a> in Irish and <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</a> (or Scots in Scotland) set in, leading to <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creolisation</a> (possibly with a trilingual stage).<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The resulting language is referred to as <i>Old Shelta</i> and it is suspected that this stage of the language displayed distinctive features, such as non-English syntactic and morphological features, no longer found in Shelta.<sup id="cite_ref-Queen&#39;s_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Queen&#39;s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a>, various sub-branches of Shelta exist. <a href="/w/index.php?title=English_Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="English Shelta (page does not exist)">English Shelta</a> is increasingly undergoing <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicisation</a>, while <a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Irish-Traveller%27s_Cant&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Irish-Traveller&#39;s Cant (page does not exist)">American Irish-Traveller\'s Cant</a>, originally also synonymous with Shelta, has by now been almost fully anglicised.<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_features">Linguistic features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Linguistic features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Sociologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociologist">Sociologist</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sharon_Gmelch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sharon Gmelch (page does not exist)">Sharon Gmelch</a> describes the Travellers\' language as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Irish Travelers use a secret argot or cant known as Gammon. It is used primarily to conceal meaning from outsiders, especially during business transactions and in the presence of police. Most Gammon utterances are terse and spoken so quickly that a non-Traveler might conclude the words merely had been garbled. Most Gammon words were formed from <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> by applying four techniques: reversal, <a href="/wiki/Metathesis_(linguistics)" title="Metathesis (linguistics)">metathesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixing</a>, and substitution. In the first, an Irish word is reversed to form a Gammon one \xe2\x80\x93 <i>mac</i>, or son, in Irish became <i>kam</i> in Gammon. In the second, consonants or consonant clusters were transposed. Thirdly, a sound or cluster of sounds were either prefixed or suffixed to an Irish word. Some of the more frequently prefixed sounds were <i>s</i>, <i>gr</i>, and <i>g</i>. For example, <i>Obair</i>, work or job, became <i>gruber</i> in Gammon. Lastly, many Gammon words were formed by substituting an arbitrary consonant or consonant cluster in an Irish word. In recent years, modern slang and <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">Romani</a> (the language of the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">gypsies</a>) words have been incorporated. The <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a> are English. The first <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a> collected from Irish Travelers was published in 1808, indicating that Gammon dates at least back to the 1700s. But many early Celtic scholars who studied it, including the eminent <a href="/wiki/Kuno_Meyer" title="Kuno Meyer">Kuno Meyer</a>, concluded it was much older.\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Thus, it is not <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutually intelligible</a> with either English or Irish, out of design.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">Lexicon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lexicon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many Shelta words have been disguised using techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Back_slang" title="Back slang">back slang</a> where sounds are transposed (for example <i>gop</i> "kiss" from Irish <i>p\xc3\xb3g</i>) or the addition of sounds (for example <i>gather</i> "father" from Irish <i>athair</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-McArthur_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McArthur-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Other examples include <i>lackin</i> or <i>lakeen</i> "girl" from Irish <i>cail\xc3\xadn</i>, and the word <i>rodas</i> "door" from Irish <i>doras</i>.\n</p><p>It also contains a certain number of introduced lexical items from Romani such as the term <i>gadje</i> "non-Traveller" or "kushti" (from the <a href="/wiki/Romanichal" title="Romanichal">Romanichal</a> word for "good"). Ten percent of the Gammon language comes from <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">Romani</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is no standard orthography. Broadly speaking, Shelta can either be written following an Irish-type orthography or an English-type orthography.
