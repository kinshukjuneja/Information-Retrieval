id="Origin_of_the_office">Origin of the office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of the office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The title "commissioner", which is a title now applied to the heads of several other major sports leagues as well as baseball, derives from its predecessor office, the National Commission. The National Commission was the ruling body of professional baseball starting with the National Agreement of 1903, which made peace between the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_baseball_in_the_United_States" title="History of baseball in the United States">History of baseball in the United States</a>). It consisted of three members: the two League presidents and a Commission chairman, whose primary responsibilities were to preside at meetings and to mediate disputes. Although the Commission chairman <a href="/wiki/August_Herrmann" title="August Herrmann">August Herrmann</a> was the nominal head of major league baseball, it was AL President <a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson" title="Ban Johnson">Ban Johnson</a> who dominated the Commission.\n</p><p>The event that would eventually lead to the appointment of a single Commissioner of Baseball was the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sox_Scandal" title="Black Sox Scandal">Black Sox Scandal</a> \xe2\x80\x94 perhaps the worst of a series of incidents in the late 1910s that jeopardized the integrity of the game. However, the motivations behind the creation of the Commissioner\'s office were more than the mere desire to rebuild public relations. The scandal had not only tarnished the image of baseball, but had brought relations between team owners and AL President Johnson to a breaking point. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Charles_Comiskey" title="Charles Comiskey">Charles Comiskey</a> was incensed at what he perceived to be Johnson\'s indifference to his suspicions that the <a href="/wiki/1919_World_Series" title="1919 World Series">1919 World Series</a> had been thrown. As a result, the National League, whose owners had never been on good terms with Johnson, agreed to invite the White Sox along with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> to join their league. The NL also unveiled plans to put a twelfth team in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>.\n</p><p>With the American League\'s status as a major league and possibly its very existence suddenly in jeopardy, the five AL owners loyal to Johnson sued for peace. Eventually, at the urging of <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> owner and Johnson loyalist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Navin" title="Frank Navin">Frank Navin</a>, a compromise was reached in late 1920 to reform the National Commission with a membership of non-baseball men.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_commissioners">Overview of commissioners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview of commissioners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis_.281920.E2.80.931944.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis_(1920\xe2\x80\x931944)">Kenesaw Mountain Landis (1920\xe2\x80\x931944)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kenesaw Mountain Landis (1920\xe2\x80\x931944)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Landis_portrait-restored.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Landis_portrait-restored.png/220px-Landis_portrait-restored.png" width="220" height="303" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Landis_portrait-restored.png/330px-Landis_portrait-restored.png 1.5x, class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Landis_portrait-restored.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Having agreed to appoint only non-baseball men to the National Commission, the owners tapped federal judge <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a>, an ardent baseball fan, to serve as the reformed commission\'s chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-blal_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Landis responded by declaring that he would only accept an appointment as sole commissioner, with nearly unlimited authority to act in the "best interests of baseball" \xe2\x80\x94 in essence, serving as an arbitrator whose decisions could not be appealed.  Finally, Landis insisted on a lifetime contract.  The owners, still reeling from the perception that the sport was crooked, readily agreed.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gambling">Gambling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gambling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Landis\'s first significant act was to deal with the Black Sox scandal. Following a trial, the eight players suspected of involvement in the fix were acquitted, including <a href="/wiki/Buck_Weaver" title="Buck Weaver">Buck Weaver</a> and superstar <a href="/wiki/Shoeless_Joe_Jackson" title="Shoeless Joe Jackson">Shoeless Joe Jackson</a>. Nevertheless, immediately following the players\' acquittal, Landis banned them all from baseball for life.  He famously declared, "Regardless of the verdict of juries, no player who throws a ballgame, no player that undertakes or promises to throw a ballgame, no player that sits in conference with a bunch of crooked players and gamblers where the ways and means of throwing a game are discussed and does not promptly tell his club about it, will ever play professional baseball."  Landis explained that even though the players had all been acquitted in court, none of them could be allowed back in the game if its image was to be restored with the public.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over the years, he dealt harshly with others proven to have thrown individual games, consorted with gamblers or engaged in actions that he felt tarnished the image of the game. Among the others he banned were <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants_(NL)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Giants (NL)">New York Giants</a> players [Phil Douglas (baseball)|Phil Douglas]] and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_O%27Connell_(baseball)" title="Jimmy O&#39;Connell (baseball)">Jimmy O\'Connell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Gene_Paulette" title="Gene Paulette">Gene Paulette</a>, Giants coach <a href="/wiki/Cozy_Dolan_(1910s_outfielder)" title="Cozy Dolan (1910s outfielder)">Cozy Dolan</a>, and (in 1943) Phillies owner <a href="/wiki/William_D._Cox" title="William D. Cox">William D. Cox</a>. He also formalized the unofficial banishments of <a href="/wiki/Hal_Chase" title="Hal Chase">Hal Chase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinie_Zimmerman" title="Heinie Zimmerman">Heinie Zimmerman</a>. In 1921, he banned Giants center fielder <a href="/wiki/Benny_Kauff" title="Benny Kauff">Benny Kauff</a> even though he had been acquitted of involvement in a car theft ring. Nonetheless, Landis was convinced Kauff was guilty and argued that players of "undesirable reputation and character" had no place in baseball.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="A_fiercely_independent_Commissioner.27s_Office"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="A_fiercely_independent_Commissioner\'s_Office">A fiercely independent Commissioner\'s Office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: A fiercely independent Commissioner&#039;s Office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The owners had initially assumed that Landis would deal with the Black Sox Scandal and then settle into a comfortable retirement as the titular head of baseball. Instead, Landis ruled baseball with an iron hand for the next 25 years. He established a fiercely independent Commissioner\'s Office that would go on to often make both players and owners miserable with decisions that he argued were in the best interests of the game. He worked to clean up the hooliganism that was tarnishing the reputation of players in the 1920s, and inserted his office into negotiations with players, where he deemed appropriate, to end a few of the labor practices of owners like <a href="/wiki/Charles_Comiskey" title="Charles Comiskey">Charles Comiskey</a> that had contributed to the players\' discontent. He also personally approved broadcasters for the World Series.\n</p><p>Landis\'s only significant rival in the early years was longtime American League founder and president <a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson" title="Ban Johnson">Ban Johnson</a>, who had been reckoned as the most powerful man in the game before Landis\'s arrival.  Johnson was as strong-willed as Landis, and a clash between the two was inevitable.  It happened in the <a href="/wiki/1924_World_Series" title="1924 World Series">1924 World Series</a>. When several Giants were implicated in a plan to bribe players on the moribund Phillies late in the season, Johnson demanded that the Series be canceled, and loudly criticized Landis\'s handling of the affair, to which Landis responded by threatening to resign. The American League owners promised to throw Johnson out of office if he stepped out of line again. <a href="/wiki/1926_in_baseball" title="1926 in baseball">Two years later</a>, when Johnson criticized Landis\'s decision to give <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tris_Speaker" title="Tris Speaker">Tris Speaker</a> an amnesty after it surfaced they had bet on a fixed game in 1919, Landis told the American League owners to choose between him and Johnson.  The owners promptly sent Johnson on a sabbatical from which he never really returned.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_baseball_color_line">The baseball color line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The baseball color line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Landis perpetuated the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_color_line" title="Baseball color line">color line</a> and prolonged the segregation of organized baseball. His successor, <a href="/wiki/Happy_Chandler" title="Happy Chandler">Happy Chandler</a>, said, "For twenty-four years Judge Landis wouldn\'t let a black man play. I had his records, and I read them, and for twenty-four years Landis consistently blocked any attempts to put blacks and whites together on a big league field."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Veeck" title="Bill Veeck">Bill Veeck</a> claimed Landis prevented him from purchasing the Phillies when Landis learned of Veeck\'s plan to integrate the team. The signing of the first black ballplayer in the modern era, <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Robinson" title="Jackie Robinson">Jackie Robinson</a>, came less than a year after Landis\'s death on Chandler\'s watch and was engineered by one of Landis\'s old nemeses, <a href="/wiki/Branch_Rickey" title="Branch Rickey">Branch Rickey</a>. Eleven weeks after Robinson\'s debut with the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a>, Veeck became the first <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> owner to break the color line.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Curbing_the_growth_of_minor_league_farm_systems">Curbing the growth of minor league farm systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Curbing the growth of minor league farm systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Landis tried to curb the growth of <a href="/wiki/Minor_League_Baseball" title="Minor League Baseball">minor league</a> farm systems by innovators such as Rickey, in the name of protecting the lower levels of professional ball. Landis argued that because a parent club could unilaterally call up players from teams which were involved in pennant races, the organization was unfairly interfering with the minor competitions. His position was that the championship of each minor league was of no less importance than the championships of the major leagues, and that minor league fans and supporters had the right to see their teams competing as best they could. Yet he also prevented the formation of a powerful third major league when he stopped <a href="/wiki/Pants_Rowland" title="Pants Rowland">Pants Rowland</a> from upgrading the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League" title="Pacific Coast League">Pacific Coast League</a> in the 1940s.\n</p><p>One of the schemes he fought was the effort by major-league teams to "cover up" players they were hiding in their farm systems. The term, not used in formal communications by league or team officials, referred to players clandestinely signed by a major-league team to a minor-league contract. Occasionally one team would serendipitously find such a player in the off-season draft, as in this occasion recorded in the book <i>Dodger Daze and Knights</i>:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>All the clubowners and managers, and Commissioner Landis, were assembled to conduct the draft. One team representative said he "claim[s] Player <a href="/wiki/Paul_Richards_(baseball)" title="Paul Richards (baseball)">[Paul] Richards</a> of Brooklyn."\n</p><p>"You can\'t do that!" barked a surprised <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Robinson" title="Wilbert Robinson">Wilbert Robinson</a>, manager of the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a>.\n</p><p>"Why not?" asked Landis.\n</p><p>"Because Brooklyn has him covered up", sputtered Robbie.\n</p><p>Most of the others broke down laughing. Even Landis smirked.\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_honors">Legacy and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Whether his decisions were praised or criticized, he was satisfied with being respected and feared. Dubbed "the baseball tyrant" by journalists of the day, his rule was absolute. In the context of ensuring the integrity of the game itself, baseball historians generally regard him as the right man at the right time when appointed, but also as a man who perhaps held office too long.\n</p><p>He was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_1944" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 1944">1944</a>, in a special election held one month after his death, and the <a href="/wiki/MLB_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="MLB Most Valuable Player Award">Most Valuable Player Award</a> in each league is officially known as the Kenesaw Mountain Landis Award in his honor.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Happy_Chandler_.281945.E2.80.931951.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Happy_Chandler_(1945\xe2\x80\x931951)">Happy Chandler War">War Department</a> began campaigning for <a href="/wiki/Happy_Chandler" title="Happy Chandler">Happy Chandler</a>\'s election to the post. Despite being the last candidate put forth in the April <a href="/wiki/1945_in_baseball" title="1945 in baseball">1945</a> meetings, he was elected by a unanimous vote of the team owners, and resigned his Senate seat in October of that year.<sup id="cite_ref-mlb_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mlb-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Chandler clashed with <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> manager <a href="/wiki/Leo_Durocher" title="Leo Durocher">Leo "the Lip" Durocher</a> over Durocher\'s association with gambling figures and his marriage to actress <a href="/wiki/Laraine_Day" title="Laraine Day">Laraine Day</a>, which came amid allegations from Day\'s ex-husband that Durocher had stolen her away from him.<sup id="cite_ref-rogers_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogers-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Before the start of the 1947 season, Chandler suspended Durocher for the entire season, citing "conduct detrimental to baseball."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Dodgers went on to win the pennant that season under replacement manager <a href="/wiki/Burt_Shotton" title="Burt Shotton">Burt Shotton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rogers_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogers-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Chandler became known as "the players\' commissioner" for his work on their behalf.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During his service, he presided over the establishment of a pension fund for players and oversaw the initial steps toward <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">integration</a> of the major leagues, beginning with the debut of <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Robinson" title="Jackie Robinson">Jackie Robinson</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> in 1947. This move was controversial with team owners, who voted 15\xe2\x80\x931 against integrating the sport in a secret January 1947 meeting. The Dodgers\' <a href="/wiki/Branch_Rickey" title="Branch Rickey">Branch Rickey</a> met with Chandler, who agreed to back the team\'s move. Chandler\'s stance was credited by many in the sports community with Chandler\'s failure to be selected for another term as Commissioner after the expiration of his first one in <a href="/wiki/1951_in_baseball" title="1951 in baseball">1951</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Chandler was fully aware that he was jeopardizing his own commissionership by stewarding the integration process. Chandler\'s attitude was a simple one, which he conveyed to Branch Rickey, and later recounted in his autobiography:<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"I\'ve already done a lot of thinking about this whole racial situation in our country. As a member of the Senate Military Affairs Committee, I got to know a lot about our casualties during the war. Plenty of Negro boys were willing to go out and fight and die for this country. Is it right when they came back to tell them they can\'t play the national pastime? You know, Branch, I\'m going to have to meet my Maker some day. And if He asks me why I didn\'t let this boy play, and I say it\'s because he\'s black, that might not be a satisfactory answer.</dd>\n<dd>If the Lord made some people black, and some white, and some red or yellow, he must have had a pretty good reason. It isn\'t my job to decide which colors can play big league baseball. It is my job to see that the game is fairly played and that everybody has an equal chance. I think if I do that, I can face my Maker with a clear conscience."</dd></dl>\n<h3><span id="Ford_Frick_.281951.E2.80.931965.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ford_Frick_(1951\xe2\x80\x931965)">Ford Frick (1951\xe2\x80\x931965)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ford Frick (1951\xe2\x80\x931965)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_(cropped_and_adjusted).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_%28cropped_and_adjusted%29.jpg/170px-Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_%28cropped_and_adjusted%29.jpg" width="170" height="325" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_%28cropped_and_adjusted%29.jpg/255px-Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_%28cropped_and_adjusted%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_%28cropped_and_adjusted%29.jpg/340px-Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_%28cropped_and_adjusted%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1663" data-file-height="3175" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ford_Frick_at_1937_All-Star_Game_(cropped_and_adjusted).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ford_Frick" title="Ford Frick">Ford Frick</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/1951_in_baseball" title="1951 in baseball">1951</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford_Frick" title="Ford Frick">Ford Frick</a> succeeded <a href="/wiki/Happy_Chandler" title="Happy Chandler">Happy Chandler</a> as commissioner of baseball. Frick\'s critics accused him of favoring the NL in his rulings, such as how the 1960s expansion teams would be stocked.\n</p><p>Frick\'s most highly criticized statement<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as commissioner was made in 1961, when several players were on a pace to break <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>\'s single-season home run record. In a press conference, Frick stated that the single-season home run record should be separated into multiple lists, based on length of season. However, as MLB did not publish an official record book at the time, Frick had no control on how publishers presented MLB records, and within a few years after <a href="/wiki/Roger_Maris" title="Roger Maris">Roger Maris</a> broke Ruth\'s record, all record books gave Maris sole credit as the single-season home run record holder.<sup id="cite_ref-Salon-Maris_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-Maris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\nLater, it was revealed that Frick had served as a <a href="/wiki/Ghostwriter" title="Ghostwriter">ghostwriter</a> for Ruth earlier in his career.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Frick successfully prevented the <a href="/wiki/Continental_League" title="Continental League">Continental League</a>, a proposed third major league, from ever playing, and introduced major league baseball to several markets in which the proposed league planned to play through expansion and relocation.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="William_Eckert_.281965.E2.80.931968.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="William_Eckert_(1965\xe2\x80\x931968)">William Eckert (1965\xe2\x80\x931968)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: William Eckert (1965\xe2\x80\x931968)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg/170px-William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg" width="170" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg/255px-William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg/340px-William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1514" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Dole_%22Spike%22_Eckert.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Eckert" title="William Eckert">William Eckert</a></div></div></div>\n<p>More than 150 names appeared on the original list of nominees for the commissionership following <a href="/wiki/Ford_Frick" title="Ford Frick">Ford Frick</a>\'s retirement. The club owners initially were unable to decide if the next commissioner should come from the ranks of the game (e.g., the president of the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American</a> or <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National Leagues</a>), or elsewhere. They finally decided that the new commissioner should have a strong business background to deal with the problems that were confronting the game at the time.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_Eckert" title="William Eckert">William Eckert</a> became a serious candidate for the commissionership only after fellow officer <a href="/wiki/Curtis_LeMay" title="Curtis LeMay">Curtis LeMay</a> gave Major League Baseball a recommendation for him. On November 17, <a href="/wiki/1965_in_baseball" title="1965 in baseball">1965</a>, by a unanimous vote of the then, 20 major league club owners, William Eckert became the fourth Commissioner of Major League Baseball.\n</p><p>When he became commissioner, Eckert had not seen a game in person in over 10 years. He was a compromise choice for the job, previously being so obscure that sportswriters nicknamed him "the Unknown Soldier."\n</p><p>He incurred the public\'s ire by refusing to cancel games after the assassinations of Sen. <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> and Rev. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a>, and team owners\' disdain because he refused to deal forcefully with substantive business issues. Anticipating a <a href="/wiki/Labor_strike" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor strike">players\' strike</a> and having no ownership confidence in his ability to handle the situation, Eckert was forced to resign at the end of the <a href="/wiki/1968_in_baseball" title="1968 in baseball">1968</a> season, although he still had three years on his contract.\n</p><p>In spite of his much publicized failures and shortcomings, William Eckert also developed more effective committee actions, streamlined business methods and helped stabilize franchises with bigger stadiums and long-term leases. In addition, Eckert worked hard toward promoting the game internationally, including a <a href="/wiki/1966_in_baseball" title="1966 in baseball">1966</a> tour of Japan by the <a href="/wiki/1966_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1966 Los Angeles Dodgers season">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Bowie_Kuhn_.281969.E2.80.931984.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bowie Kuhn (1969\xe2\x80\x931984)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bowie_Kuhn_1982.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" href="/wiki/Bowie_Kuhn" title="Bowie Kuhn">Bowie Kuhn</a>\'s tenure was marked by labor <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">strikes</a> (most notably in <a perceived to be "not in the best interests of baseball."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1970_in_baseball" title="1970 in baseball">1970</a>, he suspended star <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Denny_McLain" title="Denny McLain">Denny McLain</a> indefinitely (the suspension was later set at 3 months) due to McLain\'s involvement in a <a href="/wiki/Bookmaking" class="mw-redirect" title="Bookmaking">bookmaking</a> operation, and later suspended McLain for the rest of the season for carrying a gun. He barred both <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a> (in <a href="/wiki/1979_in_baseball" title="1979 in baseball">1979</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mickey Mantle</a> (in <a href="/wiki/1983_in_baseball" title="1983 in baseball">1983</a>) from the sport due to their involvement in <a href="/wiki/Casino" title="Casino">casino</a> promotion; neither was directly involved in gambling, and both were reinstated by Kuhn\'s successor <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ueberroth" title="Peter Ueberroth">Peter Ueberroth</a> in <a href="/wiki/1985_in_baseball" title="1985 in baseball">1985</a>.\n</p><p>Also in 1970, Kuhn described <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bouton" title="Jim Bouton">Jim Bouton</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ball_Four" title="Ball Four">Ball Four</a></i> as "detrimental to baseball" and demanded that Bouton retract it. The book has been republished several times and is now considered a classic.\n</p><p>On October 13, <a href="/wiki/1971_in_baseball" title="1971 in baseball">1971</a>, the <a href="/wiki/1971_World_Series" title="1971 World Series">World Series</a> held a night game for the first time. Kuhn, who thought that baseball could attract a larger audience by featuring a <a href="/wiki/Prime_time" title="Prime time">prime time</a> telecast (as opposed to a mid-afternoon broadcast, when most fans either worked or attended school), pitched the idea to <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_on_NBC" title="Major League Baseball on NBC">NBC</a>. An estimated 61 million people watched Game 4 on NBC; TV <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">ratings</a> for a World Series game during the daytime hours would not have approached such a record number. Kuhn\'s vision in this instance has been fulfilled, as all the World Series games are now shown in prime time.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Curt_Flood">Curt Flood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Curt Flood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flood_v._Kuhn" title="Flood v. Kuhn">Flood v. Kuhn</a></div>\n<p>On October 7, <a href="/wiki/1969_in_baseball" title="1969 in baseball">1969</a>, the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> traded <a href="/wiki/Curt_Flood" title="Curt Flood">Curt Flood</a>, catcher <a href="/wiki/Tim_McCarver" title="Tim McCarver">Tim McCarver</a>, outfielder <a href="/wiki/Byron_Browne" title="Byron Browne">Byron Browne</a>, and left-handed pitcher <a href="/wiki/Joe_Hoerner" title="Joe Hoerner">Joe Hoerner</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> for first baseman <a href="/wiki/Dick_Allen" title="Dick Allen">Dick Allen</a>, second baseman <a href="/wiki/Cookie_Rojas" title="Cookie Rojas">Cookie Rojas</a>, and right-handed ace relief pitcher <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Johnson_(baseball)" title="Jerry Johnson (baseball)">Jerry Johnson</a>.\n</p><p>However, Flood refused to report to the moribund Phillies, citing the team\'s poor record and the fact that they played in dilapidated <a href="/wiki/Connie_Mack_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Connie Mack Stadium">Connie Mack Stadium</a> before belligerent and, Flood believed, <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a> fans. Flood forfeited a relatively lucrative US$100,000 (equivalent to $667,332 as of  in 2017)<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-US_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> contract by his refusal to be traded to the Phillies.\n</p><p>In a letter to Kuhn, Flood demanded that the commissioner declare him a <a href="/wiki/Free_agent" title="Free agent">free agent</a>. Kuhn denied his request, citing the propriety of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_clause" title="Reserve clause">reserve clause</a>, which was language in contracts that essentially prevented a player from playing with another team even after his contract expired. In response, Flood filed a lawsuit against Kuhn and Major League Baseball on January 16, <a href="/wiki/1970_in_baseball" title="1970 in baseball">1970</a>, alleging that Major League Baseball had violated federal <a href="/wiki/Antitrust" class="mw-redirect" title="Antitrust">antitrust</a> laws. Even though Flood was making $90,000 (equivalent to $600,599 in 2017)<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-US_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> at the time, he likened the reserve clause to <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>. It was a controversial analogy, even among those who opposed the reserve clause.\n</p><p>The case, <i><a href="/wiki/Flood_v._Kuhn" title="Flood v. Kuhn">Flood v. Kuhn</a></i> (407 U.S. 258) eventually went to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a>. Flood\'s attorney, former Supreme Court Justice <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Goldberg" title="Arthur Goldberg">Arthur Goldberg</a>, asserted that the reserve clause depressed wages and limited players to one team for life. Major League Baseball\'s counsel countered that Commissioner Kuhn acted under the way he did \'"for the good of the game."<i></i>\n</p><p>Ultimately, the Supreme Court, acting on <i><a href="/wiki/Stare_decisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Stare decisis">stare decisis</a></i> "to stand by things decided", ruled 5\xe2\x80\x933 in favor of Major League Baseball, upholding a <a href="/wiki/1922_in_baseball" title="1922 in baseball">1922</a> ruling in the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Federal_Baseball_Club_v._National_League" title="Federal Baseball Club v. National League">Federal Baseball Club v. National League</a></i>, (259 U.S. 200.)\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_O._Finley">Charles O. Finley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Charles O. Finley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Though he had a reputation as an owners\' commissioner, Kuhn did not avoid confronting owners when he deemed it necessary. For example, he was a major adversary of <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Charles_O._Finley" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles O. Finley">Charles O. Finley</a>. A major embarrassment for baseball resulted from Finley\'s actions during the <a href="/wiki/1973_World_Series" title="1973 World Series">1973 World Series</a>. Finley forced player <a href="/wiki/Mike_Andrews" title="Mike Andrews">Mike Andrews</a> to sign a false <a href="/wiki/Affidavit" title="Affidavit">affidavit</a> saying he was injured after the reserve infielder committed two consecutive <a href="/wiki/Error_(baseball)" title="Error (baseball)">errors</a> in the 12th inning of Oakland\'s Game 2 loss to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a>. Andrews\' teammates as well as manager <a href="/wiki/Dick_Williams" title="Dick Williams">Dick Williams</a> rallied to his defense. Kuhn in return, forced Finley to reinstate Andrews. In <a href="/wiki/1976_in_baseball" title="1976 in baseball">1976</a>, when Finley attempted to sell several players to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> for $3.5 million, Kuhn blocked the deals on the grounds that they would be bad for the game. Some believe that Kuhn\'s actions were simply a revenge tactic, aimed at Finley, after Finley attempted to force an owners vote to remove Kuhn as commissioner in <a href="/wiki/1975_in_baseball" title="1975 in baseball">1975</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Kuhn.27s_war_on_drugs"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kuhn\'s_war_on_drugs">Kuhn\'s war on drugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Kuhn&#039;s war on drugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>After being in office for over ten years, Kuhn had grown a strong reputation for being hard on players who <a href="/wiki/Drug_abuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug abuse">abused drugs</a>. Kuhn was quick to punish players who used drugs with heavy fines and suspensions. <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a> catcher <a href="/wiki/Darrell_Porter" title="Darrell Porter">Darrell Porter</a> told the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> that during the winter of <a href="/wiki/1979_in_baseball" title="1979 in baseball">1979</a>\xe2\x80\x93<a href="/wiki/1980_in_baseball" title="1980 in baseball">1980</a> he became <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">paranoid</a>, convinced that Kuhn knew about his drug abuse, was trying to sneak into his house, and planned to ban him from baseball for life. Porter found himself sitting up at night in the dark watching out the front window, waiting for Kuhn to approach, clutching billiard balls and a shotgun. Ironically, when Porter was named the most valuable player of the <a href="/wiki/1982_World_Series" title="1982 World Series">1982 World Series</a> while playing for the Cardinals, Kuhn was on hand to congratulate him.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1980_in_baseball" title="1980 in baseball">1980</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_hostage_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Iranian hostage crisis">Iranian hostage crisis</a>, Kuhn sat at a baseball game with <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Denton" title="Jeremiah Denton">Jeremiah Denton</a>, a Navy admiral and former <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">POW</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam</a> who would be elected U.S. Senator later that year from the state of <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>. Recalling the event to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, Kuhn believed that <i>"that afternoon...the idea of a lifetime baseball pass was discussed,"</i> and upon their return from Iran, each of the 52 hostages was given one of these unique passes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1983_in_baseball" title="1983 in baseball">1983</a>, four players from the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Willie_Wilson_(baseball)" title="Willie Wilson (baseball)">Willie Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Martin_(baseball)" title="Jerry Martin (baseball)">Jerry Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Aikens" title="Willie Aikens">Willie Mays Aikens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vida_Blue" title="Vida Blue">Vida Blue</a> \xe2\x80\x93 were found guilty of <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> use. In addition, such established stars as <a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Jenkins" title="Ferguson Jenkins">Ferguson Jenkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Hernandez" title="Keith Hernandez">Keith Hernandez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Parker" title="Dave Parker">Dave Parker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dale_Berra" title="Dale Berra">Dale Berra</a> admitted to having problems with drugs.\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_drug_trials" title="Pittsburgh drug trials">Pittsburgh drug trials</a></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaving_office">Leaving office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Leaving office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Kuhn was both praised and attacked for the firm stand that he levied against offenders. In <a href="/wiki/1982_in_baseball" title="1982 in baseball">1982</a>, some of the owners organized a move to push him out of office. In <a href="/wiki/1983_in_baseball" title="1983 in baseball">1983</a>, Kuhn and his supporters made a last-ditch effort to renew his contract but ultimately failed. Kuhn, though, was allowed to stay for the <a href="/wiki/1984_in_baseball" title="1984 in baseball">1984</a> regular season before being replaced by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ueberroth" title="Peter Ueberroth">Peter Ueberroth</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Peter_Ueberroth_.281984.E2.80.931989.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Peter_Ueberroth_(1984\xe2\x80\x931989)">Peter Ueberroth (1984\xe2\x80\x931989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Peter Ueberroth (1984\xe2\x80\x931989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Ueberroth.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Peter_Ueberroth.jpg/170px-Peter_Ueberroth.jpg" width="170" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Peter_Ueberroth.jpg/255px-Peter_Ueberroth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/Peter_Ueberroth.jpg 2x" data-file-width="264" data-file-height="364" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Ueberroth.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Peter_Ueberroth" title="Peter Ueberroth">Peter Ueberroth</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Ueberroth" title="Peter Ueberroth">Peter Ueberroth</a> was elected to succeed <a href="/wiki/Bowie_Kuhn" title="Bowie Kuhn">Bowie Kuhn</a> on March 3, 1984 and took office on October 1 of that year. As a condition of his hiring, Ueberroth increased the commissioner\'s fining ability from US$5,000 to $250,000. His salary was raised to a reported $450,000, nearly twice what Kuhn was paid.\n</p><p>Just as Ueberroth was taking office the Major League Umpires Union was threatening to <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">strike</a> the postseason. Ueberroth managed to arbitrate the disagreement and had the umpires back to work before the <a href="/wiki/League_Championship_Series" title="League Championship Series">League Championship Series</a> were over. The next summer, Ueberroth worked behind the scenes to limit a players\' strike to one day before a new labor agreement was worked out with the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Players_Association" title="Major League Baseball Players Association">Players Association</a>.\n</p><p>During the course of his stint as commissioner, Ueberroth reinstated <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball Hall of Fame">Hall of Famers</a> <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Willie Mays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mickey Mantle</a>, who had been banned from working for Major League Baseball by Kuhn because of their associations with gambling casinos. Also, Ueberroth <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_drug_trials#Ramifications" title="Pittsburgh drug trials">suspended numerous players</a> because of <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> use, negotiated a $1.1 billion <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_on_CBS#1990\xe2\x80\x931993_version" title="Major League Baseball on CBS">television contract</a> with <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and initiated the investigation against <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rose" title="Pete Rose">Pete Rose</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Dowd_Report" title="Dowd Report">betting habits</a>. In <a href="/wiki/1985_in_baseball" title="1985 in baseball">1985</a>, Ueberroth\'s first full year in office, the League Championship Series expanded from a best-of-five series to a best-of-seven series. At his urging, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> chose to install lights at <a href="/wiki/Wrigley_Field" title="Wrigley Field">Wrigley Field</a> rather than reimburse the leagues for lost night-game revenues. Ueberroth then found a new source of income in the form of persuading large corporations to pay for the privilege of having their products endorsed by Major League Baseball.\n</p><p>However, Ueberroth, with the assistance of the owners, also facilitated <a href="/wiki/Baseball_collusion#1985\xe2\x80\x931987" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball collusion">collusion</a>, an illegal violation of the league\'s collective bargaining agreement with the players, during the 1985, 1986 and 1987 off-seasons. Players entering free-agency were prevented from both signing equitable contracts and joining the teams of their choice during this period, a strategy that union leader <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Miller" title="Marvin Miller">Marvin Miller</a> later held was "tantamount to fixing, not just games, but entire pennant races, including all post-season series."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Players_Association" title="Major League Baseball Players Association">MLBPA</a> filed <a href="/wiki/Baseball_collusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball collusion">collusion charges</a>, arguing that Ueberroth and team owners had violated the collective bargaining agreement in the <a href="/wiki/1985_in_baseball" title="1985 in baseball">1985</a>, <a href="/wiki/1986_in_baseball" title="1986 in baseball">1986</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1987_in_baseball" title="1987 in baseball">1987</a> seasons. The MLBPA won each case, resulting in "second look" free agents, and over $280 million in owner fines.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Fay_Vincent" title="Fay Vincent">Fay Vincent</a>, who followed Ueberroth\'s successor in the commissioner\'s office, laid the crippling labor problems of the early 1990s directly at the feet of Ueberroth and the owners\' collusion, holding that the collusion years constituted theft from the players.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under Ueberroth, Major League Baseball enjoyed increased attendance (record attendance four straight seasons), greater awareness of crowd control and alcohol management within ballparks, a successful and vigilant anti-drug campaign, significant industry-wide improvement in the area of fair employment, and a significantly improved financial picture for the industry. When Ueberroth took office, 21 of the 26 clubs were losing money; in Ueberroth\'s last full season--<a href="/wiki/1988_in_baseball" title="1988 in baseball">1988</a>\xe2\x80\x94all clubs either broke even or finished in the black. In <a href="/wiki/1987_in_baseball" title="1987 in baseball">1987</a>, for example, baseball as an industry showed a net profit of $21.3 million, its first profitable year since <a href="/wiki/1973_in_baseball" title="1973 in baseball">1973</a>.\n</p><p>Nonetheless, following the announcement of the first of three large awards to the players following the collusion findings, Ueberroth stepped down as commissioner before the start of the <a href="/wiki/1989_in_baseball" title="1989 in baseball">1989 regular season</a>; his contract was to have run through the end of the season. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> president <a href="/wiki/A._Bartlett_Giamatti" title="A. Bartlett Giamatti">A. Bartlett Giamatti</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="A._Bartlett_Giamatti_.281989.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="A._Bartlett_Giamatti_(1989)">A. Bartlett Giamatti (1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: A. Bartlett Giamatti (1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a> professor of <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a> who became president of the university, <a href="/wiki/A._Bartlett_Giamatti" title="A. Bartlett Giamatti">A. Bartlett Giamatti</a> had a lifelong interest in baseball (he was a die-hard <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> fan).  He became President of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> in <a href="/wiki/1986_in_baseball" title="1986 in baseball">1986</a>, and later Commissioner of Baseball in <a href="/wiki/1989_in_baseball" title="1989 in baseball">1989</a>.  During his stint as National League president, Giamatti placed an emphasis on the need to improve the environment for the fan in the ballparks.  He also decided to make umpires strictly enforce the <a href="/wiki/Balk" title="Balk">balk</a> rule, and supported "social justice" as the only remedy for the lack of presence of minority managers, coaches, or executives at any level in Major League Baseball.\n</p><p>While still serving as National League president, Giamatti suspended <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rose" title="Pete Rose">Pete Rose</a> for 30 games after Rose shoved umpire <a href="/wiki/Dave_Pallone" title="Dave Pallone">Dave Pallone</a> on April 30, 1988.  Later that year, Giamatti also suspended <a href="/wiki/1988_Los_Angeles_Dodgers_season" title="1988 Los Angeles Dodgers season">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Jay_Howell" title="Jay Howell">Jay Howell</a>, who was caught using <a href="/wiki/Pine_tar" title="Pine tar">pine tar</a> during the <a href="/wiki/1988_National_League_Championship_Series" title="1988 National League Championship Series">National League Championship Series</a>.\n</p><p>Giamatti, whose tough dealing with Yale\'s union favorably impressed Major League Baseball owners, was unanimously elected to succeed <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ueberroth" title="Peter Ueberroth">Peter Ueberroth</a> as commissioner on September 8, 1988 and assumed office on April 1, 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  Giamatti was commissioner on August 24, <a href="/wiki/1989_in_baseball" title="1989 in baseball">1989</a> when Pete Rose voluntarily agreed to permanent ineligibility from baseball.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  As reflected in the agreement with Pete Rose, Giamatti was determined to maintain the integrity of the game during his brief commissionership.\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dowd_Report" title="Dowd Report">Dowd Report</a></div>\n<p>While at his vacation home on <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha&#39;s Vineyard">Martha\'s Vineyard</a>, Giamatti, a heavy <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">smoker</a> for many years, died suddenly of a massive <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a> at the age of 51, just eight days after banishing Rose and 154 days into his tenure as commissioner.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  He became the second baseball commissioner to die in office, the first being <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a>.  Baseball\'s owners soon selected <a href="/wiki/Fay_Vincent" title="Fay Vincent">Fay Vincent</a>, Giamatti\'s close friend and baseball\'s first-ever deputy commissioner, as the new commissioner.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Fay_Vincent_.281989.E2.80.931992.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fay_Vincent_(1989\xe2\x80\x931992)">Fay Vincent (1989\xe2\x80\x931992)</span><span Giamatti">Bart Giamatti</a>\'s behest, <a href="/wiki/Fay_Vincent" title="Fay Vincent">Fay Vincent</a> accepted the position of deputy commissioner.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> He became the 8th commissioner of baseball following Giamatti\'s death on September 1, 1989, and presided over the <a href="/wiki/1989_World_Series" title="1989 World Series">1989 World Series</a>, which was interrupted by the <a href="/wiki/1989_Loma_Prieta_earthquake" title="1989 Loma Prieta earthquake">Loma Prieta earthquake</a>; the owners\' <a href="/wiki/1990_Major_League_Baseball_lockout" title="1990 Major League Baseball lockout">lockout</a> during <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">Spring training</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1990_in_baseball" title="1990 in baseball">1990</a> season; and the expulsion of <a href="/wiki/George_Steinbrenner" title="George Steinbrenner">George Steinbrenner</a> in his first year. Before accepting the job as Commissioner of Baseball, Vincent consulted with Bart Giamatti\'s widow, Toni, to make sure she thought it was appropriate for him to do so.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1990_in_baseball" title="1990 in baseball">1990</a>, National League president <a href="/wiki/Bill_White_(first_baseman)" title="Bill White (first baseman)">Bill White</a> was prepared to suspend umpire <a href="/wiki/Joe_West_(umpire)" title="Joe West (umpire)">Joe West</a> for slamming <a href="/wiki/1990_Philadelphia_Phillies_season" title="1990 Philadelphia Phillies season">Philadelphia</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Cook" title="Dennis Cook">Dennis Cook</a> to the field, but Vincent intervened and no discipline was imposed.\n</p><p>During his commissionership, Vincent made it known (e.g. while being interviewed by <a href="/wiki/Pat_O%27Brien_(television)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pat O&#39;Brien (television)">Pat O\'Brien</a> during <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_on_CBS#1990\xe2\x80\x931993_version" title="Major League Baseball on CBS">CBS</a>\' coverage of Game 4 of the <a href="/wiki/1991_World_Series" title="1991 World Series">1991 World Series</a>) that if he had the chance, he would get rid of the <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> rule.\n</p><p>Vincent has also been connected with <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rose" title="Pete Rose">Pete Rose</a>\'s lifetime banishment from baseball; however, Rose\'s banishment began while Giamatti was commissioner, not Vincent (although Vincent led the investigation and was involved in the negotiations). Vincent has publicly said he does not support Rose\'s reinstatement.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1989_World_Series">1989 World Series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 1989 World Series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1989_World_Series" title="1989 World Series">1989 World Series</a></div>\n<p>On October 17, 1989, Vincent sat in a field box behind the left <a href="/wiki/Dugout_(baseball)" title="Dugout (baseball)">dugout</a> at San Francisco\'s <a href="/wiki/Candlestick_Park" title="Candlestick Park">Candlestick Park</a>. At 5:04&#160;p.m., just prior to Game 3 of the World Series between the <a href="/wiki/1989_San_Francisco_Giants_season" title="1989 San Francisco Giants season">San Francisco Giants</a> and <a href="/wiki/1989_Oakland_Athletics_season" title="1989 Oakland Athletics season">Oakland Athletics</a>, the 6.9 <a href="/wiki/Moment_magnitude_scale" title="Moment magnitude scale">Mw</a> <a href="/wiki/1989_Loma_Prieta_earthquake" title="1989 Loma Prieta earthquake">Loma Prieta earthquake</a> hit with a maximum <a href="/wiki/Mercalli_intensity_scale" title="Mercalli intensity scale">Mercalli intensity</a> of IX (<i>Violent</i>). At approximately 5:35&#160;p.m., after coming to the conclusion that the power couldn\'t be restored before sunset, Vincent ordered the game to be postponed. According to Vincent, he had already made the decision to postpone Game 3 without telling anybody first. As a result, the umpires filed a formal protest of Vincent\'s decision. However, the game had to be postponed due to trouble with gas lines as well as the power issue.\n</p><p>The World Series ultimately resumed after a ten-day postponement (and some initial conflict between Vincent and San Francisco mayor <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Christ_Agnos" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Christ Agnos">Art Agnos</a>, who felt that the World Series ought to have been delayed much longer) on October 27, 1989. While presenting the <a href="/wiki/Commissioner%27s_Trophy_(MLB)" title="Commissioner&#39;s Trophy (MLB)">Commissioner\'s Trophy</a> to the Athletics, who wound up winning the World Series in a four-game sweep, Vincent summed up the 1989 World Series as a <i>"Remarkable World Series in many respects."</i>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1990_lockout">1990 lockout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 1990 lockout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1990_Major_League_Baseball_lockout" title="1990 Major League Baseball lockout">1990 Major League Baseball lockout</a></div>\n<p>In February 1990, owners announced that spring training would not be starting as scheduled. This occurred after <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Players_Association" title="Major League Baseball Players Association">MLBPA</a> Executive Director <a href="/wiki/Donald_Fehr" title="Donald Fehr">Donald Fehr</a> became afraid that the owners would institute a <a href="/wiki/Salary_cap" title="Salary cap">salary cap</a>. Fehr believed that a salary cap could possibly restrict the number of choices free agents could make and a pay-for-performance scale would eliminate multiyear contracts. The lockout, which was the seventh work stoppage in baseball since <a href="/wiki/1972_in_baseball" title="1972 in baseball">1972</a>, lasted 32 games and wiped out all of spring training.\n</p><p>Vincent worked with both the owners and MLBPA, and on March 19, 1990, Vincent was able to announce a new Basic Agreement (which raised the minimum major league salary from US$68,000 to $100,000 and established a six-man study committee on revenue sharing). As a consequence for the lockout, Opening Day for the <a href="/wiki/1990_in_baseball" title="1990 in baseball">1990</a> season was moved back a week to April 9, and the season was extended by three days to accommodate the normal 162-game schedule.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="George_Steinbrenner">George Steinbrenner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: George Steinbrenner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On July 30, <a href="/wiki/1990_in_baseball" title="1990 in baseball">1990</a>, Vincent banned <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> owner <a href="/wiki/George_Steinbrenner" title="George Steinbrenner">George Steinbrenner</a> from baseball for life after Steinbrenner paid Howie Spira, a small-time gambler, $40,000 for "dirt" on his outfielder <a href="/wiki/Dave_Winfield" title="Dave Winfield">Dave Winfield</a> after Winfield sued Steinbrenner for failing to pay his foundation the $300,000 guaranteed in his contract.  Steinbrenner was eventually reinstated in <a href="/wiki/1993_in_baseball" title="1993 in baseball">1993</a> (one year after Vincent left office).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Steve_Howe">Steve Howe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Steve Howe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On June 24, <a href="/wiki/1992_in_baseball" title="1992 in baseball">1992</a>, Vincent permanently suspended pitcher <a href="/wiki/Steve_Howe_(baseball)" title="Steve Howe (baseball)">Steve Howe</a> for repeated drug offenses.  Vincent was incensed when upper Yankee management (<a href="/wiki/Buck_Showalter" title="Buck Showalter">Buck Showalter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gene_Michael" title="Gene Michael">Gene Michael</a>, and Jack Lawn) agreed to testify on Howe\'s behalf, and threatened them with expulsion from the game:\n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">\xe2\x80\x9c\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">You have effectively resigned from baseball by agreeing to appear at that hearing.... you should have left your conscience and your principles outside the door.\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">\xe2\x80\x9d\n</td></tr>\n\n</tbody></table>\n<p>Ironically, the three men were unaffected by Vincent\'s hyperbole, testified for Howe as promised, and remained active in baseball.  Three months later, Vincent was removed from his job as commissioner.  An arbitrator overturned Vincent\'s suspension of Howe on November 11, 1992.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1993_expansion">1993 expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: 1993 expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In June <a href="/wiki/1991_in_baseball" title="1991 in baseball">1991</a>, Vincent declared that the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> would receive US$42 million of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>\'s $190 million in expansion revenue and that the AL would provide players in the National League expansion draft (involving the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Rockies" title="Colorado Rockies">Colorado Rockies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florida_Marlins" class="mw-redirect" title="Florida Marlins">Florida Marlins</a>).  In an attempt to win support in the American League and balance the vote, Vincent decreed that the AL owners were entitled to 22 percent of the $190 million take. This decision marked the first time in expansion history that leagues were required to share expansion revenue or provide players for another league\'s expansion draft.  Vincent said the owners expanded to raise money to pay their <a href="/wiki/Baseball_collusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball collusion">collusion</a> debt.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Realignment">Realignment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Realignment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Just prior to leaving office, Vincent had plans to realign the National League. Vincent wanted the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> to move from the <a href="/wiki/National_League_East" title="National League East">Eastern Division</a> to the <a href="/wiki/National_League_West" title="National League West">Western Division</a>. Part of the impetus for realignment was the geographically anomalous placement of the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> in the West and the Cubs and the Cardinals in the east since <a href="/wiki/1969_in_baseball" title="1969 in baseball">1969</a>. National League president <a href="/wiki/Bill_White_(first_baseman)" title="Bill White (first baseman)">Bill White</a> warned Vincent that realigning without league approval would be in violation of the National League Constitution.\n</p><p>Many thought this plan would be beneficial to the league as a whole, especially by building a regional rivalry between the new franchise in <a href="/wiki/Florida_Marlins" class="mw-redirect" title="Florida Marlins">Miami</a> and the Atlanta Braves. The Cubs, however, opposed the move, suggesting that fans in the <a href="/wiki/Central_Time_Zone" title="Central Time Zone">Central Time Zone</a> would be forced to watch more games originating on the West Coast with later broadcast times (had the realignment included the use of a balanced schedule, the Cubs would have actually played more games against teams outside their division).\n</p><p>On July 17, 1992, the Chicago Cubs sued Vincent and asked the <a href="/wiki/United_States_district_court" title="United States district court">U.S. District Court</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> for a preliminary <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">injunction</a> to prevent implementation, which was granted two weeks later. After Vincent\'s attorneys appealed, oral arguments were scheduled for August 30 that year. Ultimately, Vincent resigned before the litigation was scheduled to resume, so as a result, the Cubs dropped their suit.\n</p><p>Although Vincent\'s vision never really came into fruition, Major League Baseball did in fact realign in <a href="/wiki/1994_in_baseball" title="1994 in baseball">1994</a>, albeit in the form of three divisions in each league, and the addition of an expanded playoff format.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Vincent.27s_relationship_with_the_owners"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Vincent\'s_relationship_with_the_owners">Vincent\'s relationship with the owners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Vincent&#039;s relationship with the owners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>His relationship with baseball\'s owners was always tenuous at best; he resigned in <a href="/wiki/1992_in_baseball" title="1992 in baseball">1992</a> after the owners gave him an 18\xe2\x80\x939 no confidence vote. The owners were still angry at Vincent over his intervention during the 1990 lockout. The owners were also disappointed by dwindling television <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">ratings</a> in light of a US$1.1 billion, <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_on_CBS#1990\xe2\x80\x931993_version" title="Major League Baseball on CBS">four-year deal</a> with <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> (which ultimately cost the network $500 million) beginning in <a href="/wiki/1990_in_baseball" title="1990 in baseball">1990</a> (Vincent\'s first full season as commissioner) and upwardly spiraling salaries. (It is also important to note that CBS itself contributed to decreasing ratings thanks to the haphazard scheduling of <i><a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Game_of_the_Week" title="Major League Baseball Game of the Week">Game of the Week</a></i> broadcasts during the regular season to the point that fans grew tired of tuning into no baseball on summer Saturdays.) They also accused him of acting in a high-handed manner, especially in the Howe affair.\n</p><p>The leaders in the movement to oust Vincent were members of what <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sporting_News" class="mw-redirect" title="The Sporting News">The Sporting News</a></i> later dubbed <i>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> Gang</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bud_Selig" title="Bud Selig">Bud Selig</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a>;</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Jerry_Reinsdorf" title="Jerry Reinsdorf">Jerry Reinsdorf</a>, chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>;</li>\n<li>Stanton Cook, head of the <a href="/wiki/Tribune_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribune Company">Tribune Co.</a>, which owned the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>;</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Carl_Pohlad" title="Carl Pohlad">Carl Pohlad</a>, owner of the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Twins" title="Minnesota Twins">Minnesota Twins</a>;</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Malley" title="Peter O&#39;Malley">Peter O\'Malley</a>, the longtime majority owner of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a></li></ul>\n<p>In his farewell, Vincent said \n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">\xe2\x80\x9c\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">To do the job without angering an owner is impossible. all share "to some extent in the responsibility for the steroid era."<sup id="cite_ref-report_pg_conc_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-report_pg_conc-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Following baseball of performance-enhancing drugs, and oversaw and instituted many rule changes and penalties to that end.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bud Selig helped introduce the following changes to Major League Baseball:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Realignment of teams into three divisions per league, and Rays">Tampa Bay Devil Rays</a> (in 1998).</li>\n<li>An <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_schedule" title="Major League Baseball schedule">unbalanced schedule</a> formula that heavily favors intradivisional play (2001)</li>\n<li>Consolidation of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> offices and presidencies under the direct auspices of Major League Baseball and inclusion of all <a href="/wiki/Umpire_(baseball)" title="Umpire (baseball)">umpiring crews</a> into a common pool for AL and NL games, instead of having separate pools per league</li>\n<li>Home field advantage in the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> granted to the winner of href="/wiki/Home-field_advantage" class="mw-redirect" title="Home-field advantage">home-field advantage</a> in the World Series)</li>\n<li>Relocation of <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a> franchise to Washington, D.C., becoming the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals" title="Washington Nationals">Washington Nationals</a> (2005)</li>\n<li>Stricter <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_drug_policy" title="Major League Baseball drug policy">Major League Baseball performance-enhancing drug testing policy</a> (2005)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/World_Baseball_Classic" title="World Baseball Classic">World Baseball Classic</a> (2006)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Instant_replay" title="Instant replay">Instant replay</a> used by umpiring crew to review disputed <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home run</a> calls (2008). Expanded to all calls (except balls and strikes) starting in 2014.</li></ul>\n<p>During Selig\'s years of service, new stadiums opened in <a href="/wiki/Turner_Field" title="Turner Field">Atlanta</a>, Selig became the Commissioner Emeritus of Baseball.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Rob_Manfred_.282015.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rob_Manfred_(2015\xe2\x80\x93present)">Rob Manfred (2015\xe2\x80\x93present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Rob Manfred (2015\xe2\x80\x93present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg/220px-Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg" width="220" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg/330px-Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg/440px-Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2735" data-file-height="3104" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rob_Manfred_7-15-2014.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Rob_Manfred" title="Rob Manfred">Rob Manfred</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Rob_Manfred" title="Rob Manfred">Rob Manfred</a> was elected as the 10th commissioner of baseball on August 14, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Manfred_Election_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manfred_Election-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Manfred, who has worked full-time for Major League Baseball since 1998, was elected on the third ballot after falling one vote short of the 23 vote (3/4 super-majority)<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> threshold on the first two ballots.<sup id="cite_ref-manfred_commissioner_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manfred_commissioner-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> It was baseball\'s first contested commissioner election in 46 years<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Reinsdorf" title="Jerry Reinsdorf">Jerry Reinsdorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels" title="Los Angeles Angels">Los Angeles Angels</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Arte_Moreno" title="Arte Moreno">Arte Moreno</a> led a group in support of Boston Red Sox chairman Tom Werner, who they felt would take a tougher stance against the players in labor negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-manfred_commissioner_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manfred_commissioner-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Manfred took over from Bud Selig on January 25, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-NYDN_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYDN-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Owners.27_.22coup.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Owners\'_&quot;coup&quot;">Owners\' "coup"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Owners&#039; &quot;coup&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Tensions between commissioners and the baseball team owners who elected them, exacerbated by baseball\'s chronic labor conflicts with the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Players_Association" title="Major League Baseball Players Association">Major League Baseball Players Association</a> beginning in the 1970s, came to a head in <a href="/wiki/1992_in_baseball" title="1992 in baseball">1992</a>, when baseball owners <a href="/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence" title="Motion of no confidence">voted no confidence</a> in Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Fay_Vincent" title="Fay Vincent">Fay Vincent</a> by a tally of 18\xe2\x80\x939. The owners had a number of grievances against Vincent, especially the perception that he had been too favorable to the players during the lockout of <a href="/wiki/1990_in_baseball" title="1990 in baseball">1990</a>. Unlike his replacement Selig, Vincent stated that the owners <a href="/wiki/Baseball_collusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseball collusion">colluded</a> against the players. Vincent put it this way: "The Union basically doesn\xe2\x80\x99t trust the Ownership because collusion was a $280 million theft by Selig and <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> owner <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Reinsdorf" title="Jerry Reinsdorf">Jerry Reinsdorf</a> of that money from the players. I mean, they rigged the signing of free agents. They got caught. They paid $280 million to the players. And I think that\'s polluted labor relations in baseball ever since it happened. I think it\'s the reason union chief <a href="/wiki/Donald_Fehr" title="Donald Fehr">Donald Fehr</a> has no trust in Selig."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Vincent resigned on September 7, 1992. Selig, the longtime owner of the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a>, was appointed chairman of baseball\'s Executive Council, making him the <i>de facto</i> acting commissioner (among the potential candidates for a permanent commissioner discussed in the media were future President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, who was the managing partner for the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> from 1989 to 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/George_J._Mitchell" title="George J. Mitchell">George J. Mitchell</a>, then Majority Leader of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senate</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> While acting commissioner, Selig presided over Major League Baseball during the <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1994\xe2\x80\x9395 Major League Baseball strike">1994 player\'s strike</a>, which led to the cancellation of the <a href="/wiki/1994_World_Series" title="1994 World Series">World Series</a>.\n</p><p>Selig continued as acting commissioner until July 8, 1998, when the owners officially appointed him to the commissioner position.<sup id="cite_ref-MLB-SeligBio_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MLB-SeligBio-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\nHaving been an owner for 30 years, Selig was seen as having closer ties to the MLB team owners than previous commissioners. Selig\'s administration had many perceived successes, such as expansion and <a href="/wiki/Interleague_play" title="Interleague play">interleague play</a>.\n</p><p>In May <a href="/wiki/2008_in_baseball" title="2008 in baseball">2008</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bud_Selig" title="Bud Selig">Bud Selig</a> surpassed <a href="/wiki/Bowie_Kuhn" title="Bowie Kuhn">Bowie Kuhn</a> as the second longest-serving commissioner (including his time as "acting commissioner" from <a href="/wiki/1992_in_baseball" title="1992 in baseball">1992</a> to mid-<a href="/wiki/1998_in_baseball" title="1998 in baseball">1998</a>), behind <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a>, who died in office after 24 years of service.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\nBeginning in 2006, Selig repeatedly stated his intention to retire at the end of his contract in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\nHowever, on 17 January 2008, it was announced that Selig accepted a 3-year extension through the 2012 season <sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_challenges">Current challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Current challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A prominent issue currently faced by Major League Baseball is the usage of <a href="/wiki/Performance-enhancing_drugs" class="mw-redirect" title="Performance-enhancing drugs">performance-enhancing drugs</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Anabolic_steroids" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabolic steroids">anabolic steroids</a>, by ballplayers in the late 1990s through 2009. Addressing the issue of whether Selig should have taken alternate actions, former commissioner <a href="/wiki/Fay_Vincent" title="Fay Vincent">Fay Vincent</a> wrote in the April 24, 2006, issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i> that with most of <a href="/wiki/Barry_Bonds" title="Barry Bonds">Barry Bonds</a>\' official troubles being off the field, and with the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Players_Association" title="Major League Baseball Players Association">players\' union</a>, there is little Selig can do beyond appointing an investigating committee. Vincent said that Selig is largely "an observer of a forum beyond his reach."\n</p><p>Another challenge facing the Office of the Commissioner is competitive imbalance and struggling attendance in small markets.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="&quot;Source">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 2010, just 2 teams (<a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a>) sold out every game and many teams failed to draw 2 million fans. In the Office of the Commissioner\'s <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ribbon_Panel" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue Ribbon Panel">Blue Ribbon Panel</a> on Baseball Economics, it was found that the luxury tax was failing to correct the competitive balance of the league and several steps were needed to correct the current state of revenue generating and sharing.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Leslie_O%27Connor" title="Leslie O&#39;Connor">Leslie O\'Connor</a> (briefly served as Acting Commissioner after Landis\'s death)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commissioner_of_Baseball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Frick, Ford. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=crnl1c9JXGAC&amp;pg=PA129&amp;dq=%22Commissioner+of+Baseball%22%2B%22organized+baseball%22&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=u0gbTqyXL8O30AGh7e2WBQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CDEQ6AEwAQ#v=onepage&amp;q=%22Commissioner%20of%20Baseball%22%2B%22organized%20baseball%22&amp;f=false">"Statement of Ford Frick \xe2\x80\x93 Commissioner of Baseball"</a>.
