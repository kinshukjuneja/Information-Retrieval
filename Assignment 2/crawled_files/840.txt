For a guide to IPA symbols, see <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">Help:IPA</a>.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>J\xc3\xa8rriais</b> is the form of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman language</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. It has been in decline over the past century as <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> has increasingly become the language of education, commerce and administration. There are very few people who speak J\xc3\xa8rriais as a mother tongue and, owing to the age of the remaining speakers, their numbers decrease annually. Despite this, efforts are being made to keep the language alive.\n</p><p>A similar language, <a href="/wiki/Guern%C3%A9siais" title="Guern\xc3\xa9siais">Guern\xc3\xa9siais</a>, is spoken in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>. The language of <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sercquiais" title="Sercquiais">Sercquiais</a>, is a descendant of the J\xc3\xa8rriais brought by the Jersey colonists who settled Sark in the 16th century; and there is mutual intelligibility with the Norman language of mainland <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>.\n</p><p>J\xc3\xa8rriais is often called "Jersey French" or "Jersey Norman French" in English (though this may give the impression that the language is a <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>) and "jersiais" or "normand de Jersey" in French. J\xc3\xa8rriais is distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Legal_French" title="Jersey Legal French">Jersey Legal French</a> used for legal contracts, laws and official documents by the government and administration of Jersey. For this reason, some prefer using the term "Jersey Norman" to avoid ambiguity and to dissociate the language from standard French.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The survey of 4200 households took place in June 2012 and resulted in 2400 returns. It showed that 18% of the population could speak some J\xc3\xa8rriais words and phrases with more than 7% of those over 65 being fluent or able to speak a lot of J\xc3\xa8rriais. Two-thirds of adults said that they could not understand spoken J\xc3\xa8rriais, but more than a quarter are able to understand some, and 5% can usually or fully understand someone speaking J\xc3\xa8rriais. 4% of people said that they could write some J\xc3\xa8rriais, although under 1% could write fluently. Just under a third (32%) said that they could understand something written in J\xc3\xa8rriais.\n</p><p>These figures update those of the census (2001) which showed that approximately 3% of the island\'s population speak J\xc3\xa8rriais in their personal interactions, although research suggests that up to 15% of the population have some understanding of the language. The latest census figures also showed an increase in declarations of children speaking the language: the first such increase recorded in census figures (although this may be due to greater consciousness among parents than to language use), doubtless encouraged by the introduction of a J\xc3\xa8rriais teaching programme into Jersey schools.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Parishes_of_Jersey" title="Parishes of Jersey">parish</a> with the highest proportion (8%) of J\xc3\xa8rriais speakers is <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ouen,_Jersey" title="Saint Ouen, Jersey">Saint Ouen</a>, and that with the lowest proportion (2.1%) is <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helier" title="Saint Helier">Saint Helier</a>, although Saint Helier as the largest parish has the highest number of J\xc3\xa8rriais speakers. The number of census respondents who stated that they "usually" spoke J\xc3\xa8rriais was 113; 2,761 respondents stated that they "sometimes" spoke J\xc3\xa8rriais. A survey<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> carried out among a sample of J\xc3\xa8rriais speakers in 1996 found that 18% spoke J\xc3\xa8rriais more often than English, 66% spoke J\xc3\xa8rriais as often as English, and 16% spoke J\xc3\xa8rriais less often than English.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/States_of_Jersey" title="States of Jersey">States of Jersey</a> fund the teaching programme in schools and provide some support in terms of signage, e.g. welcome signs at harbours and airport. Ratification of the <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages</a> is under discussion. In September 2005, the States approved the development of a cultural strategy, one of whose strategic objectives was as follows:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Jersey almost lost its language in the 20th century. By 2001 there were less than 3,000 speakers of J\xc3\xa8rriais. In the 21st century strenuous efforts are being made to re-establish it. Le Don Balleine, funded by the States, is leading a programme in schools teaching J\xc3\xa8rriais. L\'Assembli\xc3\xa9e d\'J\xc3\xa8rriais promotes the language generally. Language brings distinctiveness, a sense of localness and a whole new set of skills all of which are important qualities in attracting the creative economy. It is fundamental to the Island\'s identity. This objective is to work with these organisations to help in the revival and status of the language.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In September 2009 a partnership agreement was signed by the Minister for Education, Sport and Culture and the President of Le Don Balleine to formalise the r\xc3\xb4le of L\'Office du J\xc3\xa8rriais in protecting and promoting J\xc3\xa8rriais and to develop a language plan to help make the language more prominent on a daily basis.\n</p><p>J\xc3\xa8rriais is currently classified as a "Threatened" language by <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Languages_Project" title="Endangered Languages Project">The Endangered Language Project</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>There is newspaper and radio output in the language. From 2010, <a href="/wiki/Jersey_pound#2010_issue" title="Jersey pound">Jersey banknotes</a> carry the value of the note written out in J\xc3\xa8rriais.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:left;"></div>\n<p>J\xc3\xa8rriais is recognised as a regional language by the British and Irish governments within the framework of the British-Irish Council.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A8rriais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais_literature" title="J\xc3\xa8rriais literature">J\xc3\xa8rriais literature</a></div>\n<p>The literary tradition is traced back to <a href="/wiki/Wace" title="Wace">Wace</a>, the 12th-century Jersey-born poet, although there is little surviving literature in J\xc3\xa8rriais dating to before the introduction of the first printing press in Jersey in the 1780s. The first printed J\xc3\xa8rriais appears in the first newspapers at the end of the 18th century, and the earliest identified dated example of printed poetry is a fragment by Matchi L\'G\xc3\xa9 (<a href="/wiki/Matthew_Le_Geyt" title="Matthew Le Geyt">Matthew Le Geyt</a> 1777 \xe2\x80\x93 1849) dated 1795.\n</p><p>A boom in competing newspapers and journals throughout the 19th century provided a platform for poets and writers to publish regularly \xe2\x80\x93 typically, satirical comment on the week\'s news, elections, Jersey politicians and notables.\n</p><p>The first printed anthology of J\xc3\xa8rriais poetry, <i>Rimes Jersiaises</i>, was published in 1865.\n</p><p>Influential writers include "Laelius" (Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Pipon_Marett" title="Robert Pipon Marett">Robert Pipon Marett</a> 1820 \xe2\x80\x93 1884, <a href="/wiki/Bailiff_(Channel_Islands)" title="Bailiff (Channel Islands)">Bailiff</a> of Jersey 1880 \xe2\x80\x93 1884), "A.A.L.G." (<a href="/wiki/Augustus_Aspley_Le_Gros" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus Aspley Le Gros">Augustus Aspley Le Gros</a> 1840 \xe2\x80\x93 1877), "St.-Luorenchais" (<a href="/wiki/Philippe_Langlois" title="Philippe Langlois">Philippe Langlois</a> 1817 \xe2\x80\x93 1884).\n</p><p><i>Elie</i> (<a href="/wiki/Edwin_J._Luce" class="mw-redirect" title="Edwin J. Luce">Edwin J. Luce</a> 1881 \xe2\x80\x93 1918) was editor of the French-language newspaper <i>La Nouvelle Chronique de Jersey</i>, and a poet who wrote topical poems for the newspaper. He was also active in promoting the development of drama in J\xc3\xa8rriais and organised performances, ultimately leading to the establishment of a J\xc3\xa8rriais section of the <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Eisteddfod" title="Jersey Eisteddfod">Jersey Eisteddfod</a> in 1912.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_the_Channel_Islands" title="German occupation of the Channel Islands">Occupation</a>, Nazi censors permitted little original writing to be published. However, many older pieces of literature were re-published in the newspapers as an act of cultural self-assertion and morale-boosting.\n</p><p>After the Occupation and with the re-establishment of a free press, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Le_Brocq&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Edward Le Brocq (page does not exist)">Edward Le Brocq</a> (1877\xe2\x80\x931964) revived a weekly column in 1946 with a letter from <i>Ph\'lip et Merrienne</i>, supposedly a traditional old couple who would comment on the latest news or recall time past.\n</p><p>The most influential writer of J\xc3\xa8rriais in the 20th century was a U.S. citizen, <a href="/wiki/George_F._Le_Feuvre" title="George F. Le Feuvre">George Francis Le Feuvre</a> (1891\xe2\x80\x931984) whose pen-name was "George d\'la Forge". He emigrated to North America after the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> but for almost forty years maintained a flow of articles in J\xc3\xa8rriais back to Jersey for publication in newspapers. Selections of his articles have been published in book form.\n</p><p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Le_Maistre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Frank Le Maistre (page does not exist)">Frank Le Maistre</a> (1910\xe2\x80\x932002), compiler of the dictionary <i>Dictionnaire Jersiais\xe2\x80\x93Fran\xc3\xa7ais</i>, maintained a literary output starting in the 1930s with newspaper articles under the pseudonym <i>Marie la Pie</i>, poems, magazine articles, research into toponymy and etymology.\n</p><p>Since Le Maistre, <a href="/wiki/Geraint_Jennings" title="Geraint Jennings">Geraint Jennings</a> has been influential in preserving the language by compiling thousands of pages of J\xc3\xa8rriais text online in <a href="/wiki/Les_Pages_J%C3%A8rriaises" title="Les Pages J\xc3\xa8rriaises">Les Pages J\xc3\xa8rriaises</a>, including parts of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A8rriais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg/220px-Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg/330px-Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg/440px-Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jerriais_signage_at_Jersey_supermarket.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Some bilingual (or trilingual) signage may be seen in Jersey, such as this welcome sign at a supermarket</div></div></div>\n<p>Although J\xc3\xa8rriais is now the first language of a very small minority, until the 19th century it was the everyday language of the majority of the population, and even until the Second World War up to half the population could communicate in the language. However, there is no complete Bible in J\xc3\xa8rriais (although there are versions of favourite Bible texts in J\xc3\xa8rriais), as French was, until the 20th century, the predominant language of the Church in Jersey (although sermons would be preached, or explained, in J\xc3\xa8rriais in country areas).\n</p><p>Awareness of the decline of language use became apparent in the 19th century in scholarly circles. Among foreign linguists, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Louis Lucien Bonaparte">Louis Lucien Bonaparte</a> visited Jersey and interested himself in the language and its literature. <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a>, during his exile in Jersey, took an interest in the language and numbered some J\xc3\xa8rriais writers among his circle of acquaintances and supporters.\n</p><p>Sir Robert Pipon Marett\'s prestige and influence helped to reinforce the movement toward standardisation of the writing system based on French orthography, a trend which was also helped by the nascent Norman literary revival in the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">Cotentin</a> area of mainland <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> where writers, inspired by the example of the Norman writers of Jersey and Guernsey, began their own production of literary works. However, differing (if mutually comprehensible) writing systems have been adopted in Jersey, Guernsey and mainland <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>. The question is sometimes raised as to whether J\xc3\xa8rriais should move to a writing system based on English <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>, however this would have implications for the continuity of the literary tradition over two centuries or more (note though, that the digraph "th" for the typical dental fricative of J\xc3\xa8rriais has evidently been borrowed from English orthography).\n</p><p>As English became dominant in Jersey in the 20th century, efforts were made to preserve the J\xc3\xa8rriais language. The <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Eisteddfod" title="Jersey Eisteddfod">Jersey Eisteddfod</a> has included a J\xc3\xa8rriais section since 1912. Associations were founded. <i>L\'Assembli\xc3\xa9e d\'J\xc3\xa8rriais</i> was founded in 1951, while <i>Le Don Balleine</i> is a trust set up in accordance with the will of Arthur E. Balleine (1864\xe2\x80\x931943), who bequeathed funds for the promotion of the language. <i>L\'Assembli\xc3\xa9e d\'J\xc3\xa8rriais</i> launched a quarterly magazine in 1952, which has been published since (with the occasional hiatus, and latterly under the editorship of <i>Le Don Balleine</i>); a standard grammar <i>L\xc3\xa9 J\xc3\xa8rriais pour tous</i> (by Paul Birt) appeared in 1985; cassettes, booklets and other materials have also been produced.\n</p><p>George d\'la Forge\'s maintenance of the language in the North American diaspora is not as surprising as it might seem, as considerable numbers of Jersey people had been involved in the economic development and exploitation of the New World (see <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>). Much of the concentration focused on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">cod</a> fisheries of the <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gasp\xc3\xa9 Peninsula">Gasp\xc3\xa9</a> peninsula in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, which were controlled into the early 20th century by Jersey-based companies or companies of Jersey origin employing Jersey labour. The common language of business was J\xc3\xa8rriais, and it is reported that there were still some J\xc3\xa8rriais-speakers in Gasp\xc3\xa9 villages in the 1960s. The Gaspesian expression "faire une runne" (to go and work outside the region) comes from the J\xc3\xa8rriais word "run" applied to a fishing station.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Libethes_inscription_St_Helier,_Jersey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Libethes_inscription_St_Helier%2C_Jersey.jpg/220px-Libethes_inscription_St_Helier%2C_Jersey.jpg" width="220" height="80" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Libethes_inscription_St_Helier%2C_Jersey.jpg/330px-Libethes_inscription_St_Helier%2C_Jersey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Libethes_inscription_St_Helier%2C_Jersey.jpg/440px-Libethes_inscription_St_Helier%2C_Jersey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1484" data-file-height="540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Libethes_inscription_St_Helier,_Jersey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An inscription, reading "Liberated" in J\xc3\xa8rriais, was installed at La Pi\xc3\xa8che d\xc3\xa9 l\'Av\'n\xc3\xaen in St. Helier in 2005 to mark the 60th anniversary of the Liberation.</div></div></div>\n<p>The use of J\xc3\xa8rriais is also to be noted during the German <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Channel_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupation of the Channel Islands">Occupation of the Channel Islands</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>; the local population used J\xc3\xa8rriais among themselves as a language neither the occupying Germans, nor their French interpreters, could understand. However, the social and economic upheaval of the War meant that use of English increased dramatically after the Liberation.\n</p><p>It is considered that the last monolingual adult speakers probably died in the 1950s<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, although monolingual speaking children were being received into schools in St. Ouen as late as the late 1970s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Famous J\xc3\xa8rriais speakers include <a href="/wiki/Lillie_Langtry" title="Lillie Langtry">Lillie Langtry</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Everett_Millais" title="John Everett Millais">John Everett Millais</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelite" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Raphaelite">Pre-Raphaelite</a> painter.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dictionaries">Dictionaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A8rriais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dictionaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The history of J\xc3\xa8rriais <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">dictionaries</a> goes back to 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscript</a> <a href="/wiki/Glossary" title="Glossary">glossaries</a> work of Philippe Langlois, A. A. Le Gros and Thomas Gaudin. These were later revised and expanded into the <i>Glossaire du Patois Jersiais</i> published in 1924 by La <a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_Jersiaise" title="Soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 Jersiaise">Soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 Jersiaise</a>. The 1960 <i>Glossary of Jersey French</i> (Nichol Spence) recorded J\xc3\xa8rriais in phonetic script. The 1924 Glossaire inspired the research by Frank Le Maistre that culminated in the <i>Dictionnaire Jersiais\xe2\x80\x93Fran\xc3\xa7ais</i> published in 1966 to mark the 900th anniversary of the Norman Conquest of England. The first practical English\xe2\x80\x93J\xc3\xa8rriais dictionary was the <i>English-Jersey Language Vocabulary</i> (Albert Carr\xc3\xa9 in collaboration with Frank Le Maistre and Philip de Veulle, 1972) which was itself based on the <i>Dictionnaire Jersiais\xe2\x80\x93Fran\xc3\xa7ais</i>. A children\'s picture dictionary <i>Les Preunmi\xc3\xa9 Mille Mots</i> was published by La Soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 Jersiaise in 2000. In 2005 a J\xc3\xa8rriais\xe2\x80\x93English dictionary <i>Dictionnaithe J\xc3\xa8rriais-Angliais</i> was published by La Soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 Jersiaise, in collaboration with Le Don Balleine. A revised, modernised and expanded English\xe2\x80\x93J\xc3\xa8rriais dictionary <i>Dictionnaithe Angliais-J\xc3\xa8rriais</i>, was published in 2008 by Le Don Balleine.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A8rriais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although J\xc3\xa8rriais is occasionally misleadingly described as a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Norse language">Norse</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, it would be more linguistically accurate to state that when the Norse-speaking <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> (lit. <i>North-man</i>) conquered the territory that is now called <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> they started speaking the <a href="/wiki/O%C3%AFl_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="O\xc3\xafl languages">langue d\'o\xc3\xafl</a> of their new subjects. The Norman language is therefore basically a <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance language</a> with a certain amount of vocabulary of Norse origin, plus later <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> from other languages.\n<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" has also adopted a small number of words from the <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton language</a> (e.g. <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pihangne" class="extiw"
