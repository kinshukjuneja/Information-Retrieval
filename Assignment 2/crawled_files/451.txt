title="Education in Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Wales" title="Education in Wales">Wales</a> and some other <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries, <b>sixth form</b> (sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_5" title="Key Stage 5">Key Stage 5</a>) represents the final 1-3 years of <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a> (high school), where students (typically between 16 and 18 years of age) prepare for their <a href="/wiki/GCE_Advanced_Level" title="GCE Advanced Level">A-level</a> (or equivalent) examinations.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixth_form&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term <i>sixth form</i> describes the school years numbered 12 and 13, which are called the <i>Lower Sixth</i> (L6) and <i>Upper Sixth</i> (U6) by many schools.\n</p><p>The term survives from an earlier system when the first five years of English secondary schooling were known as <i>forms</i> (which would originally have been long backless benches on which rows of pupils sat in the classroom). Pupils started their first year of secondary school in the <i>first form</i> or <a href="/wiki/First_year" class="mw-redirect" title="First year">first year</a>, and this was the <a href="/wiki/Academic_year" title="Academic year">academic year</a> in which pupils would normally become 12 years of age. Pupils would move up a form each year before entering the fifth form in the academic year in which they would have their sixteenth birthday. Those who stayed on at school to study for <a href="/wiki/A-levels" class="mw-redirect" title="A-levels">A-levels</a> moved up into the sixth form, which was divided into the Lower Sixth and the Upper Sixth. In some private schools, the term <i>Middle Sixth</i> was used in place of Upper Sixth, with the latter being used for those who stayed on for an extra term to take the entrance examinations that were previously set for candidates to <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a> or <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> universities. Other schools described these <i><a href="/wiki/Oxbridge" title="Oxbridge">Oxbridge</a></i> examination students as being in the <i>Seventh Form</i> or <i>Third Year Sixth</i>.\n</p><p>The system was changed for the 1990\xe2\x80\x931991 academic year and school years are now numbered consecutively from primary school onwards. <i><a href="/wiki/Year_One_(School)" class="mw-redirect" title="Year One (School)">Year 1</a></i> is the first year of primary school after <a href="/wiki/Reception_(School)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reception (School)">Reception</a>. The first year of secondary school (the old first form) is now known as <i><a href="/wiki/Year_Seven_(School)" class="mw-redirect" title="Year Seven (School)">Year 7</a></i>. The Lower Sixth is now <b>Year 12</b> and the Upper Sixth is <b>Year 13</b>. However, the term "Sixth Form" has still been retained as a vestige of the old system and is used as a collective term for Years 12 and 13. <a href="/wiki/Public_school_(United_Kingdom)" title="Public school (United Kingdom)">Public (fee-charging) schools</a>, along with some <a href="/wiki/State_school" title="State school">state schools</a>, tend to use the old system of numbering.\n</p><p>In some parts of the country, special <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form_college" title="Sixth form college">sixth form colleges</a> were introduced beginning in a particularly important phase of student life. A large proportion of English secondary schools no longer have an integral sixth form. This is mainly related to reforms in the later 20th century, where different political areas became a factor in the introduction of colleges instead of the original sixth forms. There are now numerous sixth form colleges throughout England and Wales, and in areas without these, sixth form schools and specialist <a href="/wiki/Further_education" title="Further education">further education</a> (FE) colleges called tertiary colleges may fill the same role.\n</p><p>Sixth form is not compulsory in England and Wales (although from 2013 onwards, people of sixth form age must remain in some form of education or training in England only, the school leaving age remains 16 in Wales); however, university entrance normally requires at least three A2-level qualifications and perhaps one AS-level. Students usually select three or four subjects from the <a href="/wiki/GCSE" class="mw-redirect" title="GCSE">GCSEs</a> they have just taken, for one "AS" year, the AS exams being taken at the end of Lower Sixth. Three subjects are then carried into the A2 year (the dropped AS being "cashed in" as a qualification) and further exams are taken at the end of that year. The marks attained in both sets of exams are converted into <a href="/wiki/UCAS" title="UCAS">UCAS</a> points, which must meet the offer made by the student\'s chosen university.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixth_form&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Northern Ireland, the equivalent of Reception is "P1", and the equivalent of the English Year 1 "P2", while the first year of secondary school is known as <i>Year 8</i> or <i>first year</i> (rather than <i>Year 7</i> as in England), and following that Lower and Upper Sixth are Year 13 and Year 14 respectively.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixth_form&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the Scottish education system, the final year of school is known as <i>Sixth Year</i> or <i>S6</i>. During this year, students typically study <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Higher_(Scottish)" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Higher (Scottish)">Advanced Higher</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Higher_(Scottish)" title="Higher (Scottish)">Higher</a> courses in a wide range of subjects, taking <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Qualifications_Authority" title="Scottish Qualifications Authority">SQA</a> exams at the end of both S5 and S6. Pupils in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> may leave once they have reached the age of 16; those who reach 16 before 30&#160;September may leave after national examinations in May, whilst those who are 16 by the end of February may leave the previous Christmas.\n</p><p>It is not essential for candidates to do a sixth year if they wish to attend a Scottish university, as they have obtained adequate Higher grades in S5 they may apply and receive acceptance, but this is conditional on being successful in the examinations. However, the vast majority of Scottish students return for S6 if they plan to attend university. Some English universities will also accept Scottish students who have obtained adequate Higher grades in S5. It was announced in December 2008 that, as from 2010, <a href="/wiki/UCAS" title="UCAS">UCAS</a> will increase the number of points awarded to those who achieve Highers and Advanced Highers.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In some cases, particularly in independent schools, the term <i>sixth form</i> is also used for the last two years of secondary education. An increasing number of independent schools are offering their students the <a href="/wiki/International_Baccalaureate_Programme" class="mw-redirect" title="International Baccalaureate Programme">International Baccalaureate Programme</a>.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable succession-box" style="margin:0.5em auto; font-size:95%;clear:both;">\n\n\n<tbody><tr style="text-align:center;">\n<td style="width:30%;" rowspan="1">Preceded&#160;by<br /><span style="font-weight: bold"><a href="/wiki/Fifth_year" title="Fifth year">Fifth year</a></span>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 40%; text-align: center;" rowspan="1"><b> Sixth year</b><br />16.5\xe2\x80\x9318\n</td>\n<td style="width: 30%; text-align: center;" rowspan="1">Succeeded&#160;by<br /><span style="font-weight: bold"><a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">Higher education</a></span>\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixth_form&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In some <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary schools</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, the sixth and seventh years are called Lower and Upper Sixth respectively. In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, it is the "+2" part of the "10+2" educational system. \n</p><p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, which previously used <i>Tingkatan Enam Bawah dan Atas</i> (Lower and Upper Sixth), switched to <i>Pra-Universiti 1</i> (Pre-University 1, replacing Lower Sixth) and <i>Pra-Universiti 2</i> (Pre-University 2, replacing Upper Sixth) to reflect that the sixth and seventh years prepare students for university. Students prepare to sit the <a href="/wiki/Sijil_Tinggi_Persekolahan_Malaysia" title="Sijil Tinggi Persekolahan Malaysia">Sijil Tinggi Persekolahan Malaysia</a> at the end of the years.\n</p><p>Similarly, the term <i>sixth form</i> is also used to define the final two years of education before entering university in <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>.\n</p><p>In Singapore, however, the equivalent of a sixth form college would be called a <i><a href="/wiki/Junior_college" title="Junior college">junior college</a></i>, where pupils take their Cambridge GCE A-levels after two years. Prior to the 1990s, these two years were known as "Pre-University" (Pre-U) 1 and 2.\n</p><p>In some college preparatory schools in the United States, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Hill_School" title="The Hill School">The Hill School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodberry_Forest_School" title="Woodberry Forest School">Woodberry Forest School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethical_Culture_Fieldston_School" title="Ethical Culture Fieldston School">Ethical Culture Fieldston School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent_School" title="Kent School">Kent School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomfret_School" title="Pomfret School">Pomfret School</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Church_Farm_School" title="Church Farm School">Church Farm School</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Haverford_School" title="Haverford School">Haverford School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Abbey_School" title="Portsmouth Abbey School">Portsmouth Abbey School</a> and more, <i>sixth form</i> refers to the final year of education prior to college. It is the equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_grade" title="Twelfth grade">twelfth grade</a> in the US education system.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, under the <a href="/wiki/Education_in_New_Zealand#Years_of_schooling" title="Education in New Zealand">old system</a> of Forms, Standards and Juniors, Sixth Form was the equivalent of Year 12 in today\'s system. Year 13 was known as Seventh Form. <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> also sometimes uses the term for Year 12, though the Australian Year 12 is equivalent to the NZ Year 13&#160;/ Seventh Form and the UK\'s Upper Sixth&#160;/ Year 13.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, sixth form comprises Year 12 and 13, which may also be referred to as Lower and Upper Six. At the end of the schooling, students sit for <a href="/wiki/GCE_Advanced_Level" title="GCE Advanced Level">Brunei-Cambridge GCE A Level</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Students may also opt to take <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Subsidiary_Level" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Subsidiary Level">Advanced Subsidiary Level</a> or AS Level halfway at the end of Lower Six or halfway through Upper Six. Sixth form is not compulsory, but a preferable choice for students wishing to continue in academic studies leading to university level.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
