<b>EXT</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EGTE</b>), formerly <i>Exeter International Airport</i>, is an airport located at <a href="/wiki/Clyst_Honiton" title="Clyst Honiton">Clyst Honiton</a> in the <a href="/wiki/East_Devon" title="East Devon">District of East Devon</a> close to the city of <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> and within the county of <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a>. In 2007 the airport handled over 1 million passengers for the first time, although passenger throughput subsequently declined. In 2016 it handled 847,257 passengers, a 3.1% increase compared with 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-stats_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Exeter has a <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Aviation Authority (United Kingdom)">CAA</a> Public Use Aerodrome Licence (Number P759) that allows flights for the public transport of passengers or for flying instruction. The airport offers both scheduled and holiday charter flights within the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exeter_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Exeter Airport is located 4 miles (6.4&#160;km) east of the city of Exeter and is approximately 170 miles (270&#160;km) west south west of London.  To the south, it is connected by the A30 dual carriageway which can be accessed from the east and the M5 in the west, just 1.5 miles (2.4&#160;km) away.  The M5 enables good links with Bristol and the Midlands. There is no railway station at the airport, and the closest station is <a href="/wiki/Cranbrook_(Devon)_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Cranbrook (Devon) railway station">Cranbrook (Devon) railway station</a> which is 2.2 miles away by road (1.5 miles as the crow flies). <a href="/wiki/Exeter_St_Davids_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Exeter St Davids railway station">Exeter St Davids railway station</a> has a bus link and is therefore easier for passengers using the airport.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exeter_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Exeter Airport was opened on 31 May 1937 and operated from a "tented" terminal before the permanent buildings were complete. <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Airways" title="Jersey Airways">Jersey Airways</a> immediately inaugurated a summer service of eight flights per week from Jersey in <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_DH.84_Dragon" class="mw-redirect" title="De Havilland DH.84 Dragon">de Havilland DH.84 Dragons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Railway_Air_Services" title="Railway Air Services">Railway Air Services</a> ran connecting flights on to Plymouth and Bristol.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime_use">Wartime use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exeter_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wartime use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg/220px-RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg/330px-RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg/440px-RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1895" data-file-height="1639" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RAF_Exeter_24_Mar_1944_Airphoto.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial photograph of RAF Exeter, 20 March 1944.</div></div></div>\n<p>In World War II <b>RAF Exeter</b> was an important <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fighter_Command" title="RAF Fighter Command">RAF Fighter Command</a> airfield during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Britain" title="Battle of Britain">Battle of Britain</a>. RAF Exeter was used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> (USAAF) <a href="/wiki/Ninth_Air_Force" title="Ninth Air Force">Ninth Air Force</a> as a <a href="/wiki/D-Day" class="mw-redirect" title="D-Day">D-Day</a> troop transport base with <a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-47_Skytrain" title="Douglas C-47 Skytrain">Douglas C-47 Skytrain</a> transports dropping paratroops near <a href="/wiki/Carentan" title="Carentan">Carentan</a> to land on the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Normandy Beachhead</a>. It was known as USAAF Station AAF-463.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_Britain">Battle of Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exeter_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Battle of Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>RAF Exeter was home to the following Squadrons of No 10 Group during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Britain" title="Battle of Britain">Battle of Britain</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li>No 213 Squadron from 18 June 1940</li>\n<li>No 87 Squadron from 5 July 1940</li>\n<li>No 601 Squadron from 7 September 1940</li></ul>\n<p>Despite efforts at camouflage, including painting the runways, Exeter attracted the <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> and administrative and technical buildings were destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="USAAF_use">USAAF use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exeter_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: USAAF use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Exeter met the requirement of basing USAAF troop carrier groups close to where units of the <a href="/wiki/101st_Airborne_Division" title="101st Airborne Division">101st Airborne Division</a> were located and within reasonable range of the expected area of operations.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="440th_Troop_Carrier_Group">440th Troop Carrier Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exeter_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 440th Troop Carrier Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:C-47s-exeter-dday.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/C-47s-exeter-dday.jpg/220px-C-47s-exeter-dday.jpg" width="220" height="90" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/C-47s-exeter-dday.jpg/440px-C-47s-exeter-dday.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="246" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:C-47s-exeter-dday.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>5 June 1944 photograph of C-47s of the <a href="/wiki/95th_Troop_Carrier_Squadron" class="mw-redirect" title="95th Troop Carrier Squadron">95th</a> and <a href="/wiki/98th_Troop_Carrier_Squadron" title="98th Troop Carrier Squadron">98th Troop Carrier Squadrons</a> at RAF Exeter with freshly applied black/white invasion stripes to aid in aircraft identification from the ground. There was insufficient space to park all the aircraft on the concrete, so many were parked on grass turf.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/440th_Airlift_Wing" title="440th Airlift Wing">440th Troop Carrier Group</a> arrived on 15 April 1944 with over 70 <a href="/wiki/C-47_Skytrain" class="mw-redirect" title="C-47 Skytrain">C-47/C-53 Skytrain</a> aircraft. There was insufficient hardstandings to accommodate all the aircraft so many had to be parked on the turf, some areas being supported by tarmac. The 440th was a group of Ninth Air Force\'s 50th Troop Carrier Wing, <a href="/wiki/IX_Troop_Carrier_Command" title="IX Troop Carrier Command">IX Troop Carrier Command</a>.  The 98th TCS remained at Exeter until 7 August when it began operating from <a href="/wiki/RAF_Ramsbury" title="RAF Ramsbury">RAF Ramsbury</a>. On 11 September the headquarters of the 440th TCG was established at the group\'s new base at <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a>, France (ALG A-62D), and the last of the air echelon left Exeter two days later.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_use">Postwar use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exeter_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Postwar use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Walrus" title="Supermarine Walrus">Walruses</a> of an RAF air-sea rescue flight were the next tenants and these were joined by a <a href="/wiki/Military_glider" title="Military glider">glider</a> training unit early in 1945.\nPost-war, Exeter was reclaimed by Fighter Command and a French <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire</a> squadron, No. 329, which came and stayed until November 1945. <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Meteor" title="Gloster Meteor">Meteors</a> and <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Mosquito" title="De Havilland Mosquito">Mosquitos</a> made a brief appearance the following spring. No. 691 Squadron\'s target-towing <a href="/wiki/A-31_Vengeance" class="mw-redirect" title="A-31 Vengeance">Vultee A-31 Vengeances</a>, which had been present for more than a year, proved to be the last RAF flying unit of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> period based at Exeter. When No. 691 Squadron departed in the summer of 1946, the station was made available for civil use, being officially transferred to the Ministry of Civil Aviation on 1 January 1947 although there was still some reserve RAF activity until the 1950s.\n</p><p>Scheduled services to the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> began in 1952 and charter flights to various locations followed. A new terminal building was opened in the early 1980s and various other improvements, including a runway extension, were carried out over following years to establish Exeter as an important airport in the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a>. Exeter was a joint RAF/Civil airfield in the 1960s.\n</p><p>On 5 January 2007 a majority share of the airport was sold by <a href="/wiki/Devon_County_Council" title="Devon County Council">Devon County Council</a> to Regional and City Airports Ltd, a consortium led by construction firm <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Beatty" title="Balfour Beatty">Balfour Beatty</a>. On 26 June 2013 the airport was bought by the Patriot Aerospace division of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Rigby" title="Peter Rigby">Rigby Group</a>, which also owns <a href="/wiki/Coventry_Airport" title="Coventry Airport">Coventry Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In August 2016, Exeter Airport recorded their highest passenger throughput in a single month since September 2008, with 100,374 passengers passing through the terminal. New services to Glasgow and the first route to be supported by the new Government Regional Air Connectivety fund to Norwich, contributed to a 19% increase in passenger numbers during the month of August.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airlines_and_destinations">Airlines and destinations</span><span
