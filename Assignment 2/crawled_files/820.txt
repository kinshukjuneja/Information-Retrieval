universities.<sup id="cite_ref-ilsole24ore.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ilsole24ore.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The city contains a picturesque Medieval and Renaissance historic class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The township of Trento encompasses the city centre as well as many suburbs of extremely varied geographical and population conditions (from the industrial suburb of Gardolo, just north of the city, to tiny mountain hamlets on Monte Bondone). Various distinctive suburbs still retain their traditional identity of rural or mountain villages.\n</p><p>Trento lies in a wide <a href="/wiki/Glacial_valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial valley">glacial valley</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">Adige valley</a>, just south of the <a href="/wiki/Dolomite_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolomite Mountains">Dolomite Mountains</a>, where the Fersina River and <a href="/wiki/Avisio_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Avisio River">Avisio</a> rivers join the <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">Adige River</a> (the second longest river in Italy). River Adige is one of the three primary south-flowing Alpine rivers; its broadly curving course alongside Trento was straightened in 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The valley is surrounded by mountains, including Vigolana (2,150&#160;m (7,050&#160;ft)), Monte Bondone (2,181&#160;m (7,156&#160;ft)), <a href="/wiki/Paganella" title="Paganella">Paganella</a> (2,124&#160;m (6,969&#160;ft)), Marzola (1,747&#160;m (5,732&#160;ft)) and <a href="/wiki/Monte_Calisio" title="Monte Calisio">Monte Calisio</a> (1,096&#160;m (3,596&#160;ft)). Nearby lakes include <a href="/wiki/Lake_Caldonazzo" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Caldonazzo">Lake Caldonazzo</a>, Lake Levico, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Garda" title="Lake Garda">Lake Garda</a> and Lake Toblino.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frazioni"><i>Frazioni</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2007, there were 112,637 people residing in Trento, of whom 48% were male and 52% were female. Minors (children ages 18 and younger) totalled 18.01 percent of the population compared to pensioners who number 19.37 percent. This compares with the Italian average of 18.06 percent (minors) and 19.94 percent (pensioners). The average age of Trento residents is 41 compared to the Italian average of 42. In the five years between 2002 and 2007, the population of Trento grew by 5.72 percent, while <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> as a whole grew by 3.56 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The current birth rate of Trento is 9.61 births per 1,000 inhabitants compared to the Italian average of 9.45 births.\n</p><p>As of  2006<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trento&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, 92.68% of the population was <a href="/wiki/Italian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian people">Italian</a>. The largest immigrant group came from other <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries (mostly <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>): 4.13%, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>: 1.08%, and the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>: 0.85%.\n</p><p>Trento Informa (a magazine distributed by the "comune") reports that in 2011 there were 117,190 people residing in Trento, of whom 48.5% aged between 45 and 65. The average age was 43.1 years. 13,535 (11.5%) were foreigners.<sup id="cite_ref-Trento_Informa_June_2012_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trento_Informa_June_2012-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a> and the low Alpine passes of <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Pass" title="Brenner Pass">Brenner</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Reschen_Pass" title="Reschen Pass">Reschen Pass</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> over the Alps are disputed. Some scholars maintain it was a <a href="/wiki/Rhaetia" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhaetia">Rhaetian</a> settlement: the Adige area was however influenced by neighbouring populations, including the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Veneti" title="Adriatic Veneti">(Adriatic) Veneti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Etruscans" class="mw-redirect" title="Etruscans">Etruscans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gauls</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> people). According to other theories, the latter did instead found the city during the 4th century BC.\n</p><p>Trento was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> in the late 1st century BC, after several clashes with the Rhaetian tribes. Before the Romans, Trento was a Celtic village. In reality, the name derives from <i>Trent</i>, which is a tribute to the Celtic god of the waters (because of the river Adige)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. The Romans gave their settlement the name <i>Tridentum</i> and is a tribute to the Roman god <a href="/wiki/Neptune_(mythology)" title="Neptune (mythology)">Neptune</a> (Tri Dentum, meaning \'Three Teeth\' because of the three hills that surround the city: the <i>Doss Trent</i>, <i>Sant\'Agata</i> and <i>San Rocco</i>). The Latin name is the source of the adjective "tridentine". On the old city hall, a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> inscription is still visible: "<i>Montes argentum mihi dant nomenque Tridentum</i>" ("Mountains give me silver and the name of Trento"), attributed to Fra\' Bartolomeo da Trento (died in 1251). Tridentum became an important stop on the <a href="/wiki/Roman_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman road">Roman road</a> that led from <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a> to <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, the independent bishopric of Trento was conquered by <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, finally becoming part of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. In 1027, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Conrad_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Conrad II, Holy Roman Emperor">Conrad II</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishops_of_Trento" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishops of Trento">Prince-Bishops of Trento</a>, who wielded both temporal and religious powers. In the following centuries, however, the sovereignty was divided between the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Trent" title="Bishopric of Trent">Bishopric of Trent</a> and the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">County of Tyrol</a> (from 1363 part of the Habsburg monarchy). Around 1200, Trento became a mining center of some significance: silver was mined from the Monte Calisio - Khalisperg, and Prince-Bishop <a href="/wiki/Federico_Wanga" title="Federico Wanga">Federico Wanga</a> issued the first mining code of the alpine region.\n</p><p>In the 14th century, the region of Trento was part of Austria. The dukes of <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> (<a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> Family) were also the counts of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">Tyrol</a> and dominated the region for six centuries (1918).\n</p><p>A dark episode in the history of Trento was the Trento <a href="/wiki/Blood_libel" title="Blood libel">blood libel</a>. When a 3-year-old Christian boy, Simonino, later known as <a href="/wiki/Simon_of_Trent" title="Simon of Trent">Simon of Trent</a>, disappeared in 1475 on the eve of <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a>, the city\'s small Jewish community was accused of killing him and draining his blood for Jewish ritual purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-iht.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iht.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Eight Jews were tortured and burned at the stake, and their families forced to convert to Christianity. The bishop of Trento, Johannes Hinderbach, had Simonino canonized and published the first book printed in Trento, <i>"Story of a Christian Child Murdered at Trento"</i>, embellished with 12 woodcuts.<sup id="cite_ref-iht.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iht.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In a governmental ceremony in the 1990s, Trento apologized to the Jewish community for this dark episode and unveiled a plaque commemorating the formal apology.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trento_woodcut.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Trento_woodcut.jpg/250px-Trento_woodcut.jpg" width="250" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Trento_woodcut.jpg/375px-Trento_woodcut.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Trento_woodcut.jpg/500px-Trento_woodcut.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1048" data-file-height="704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trento_woodcut.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>18th century copy of a late 16th-century map<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> of Trento, northeast at top, showing walled old city and original course of the Adige</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Council_of_Trent.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Council_of_Trent.JPG/220px-Council_of_Trent.JPG" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Council_of_Trent.JPG/330px-Council_of_Trent.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Council_of_Trent.JPG 2x" data-file-width="430" data-file-height="347" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Council_of_Trent.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In the 16th century, Trento became notable for the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> (1545\xe2\x80\x931563) which gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>. The adjective <i>Tridentine</i> (as in "Tridentine Mass") literally means pertaining to Trento, but can also refer to that specific event. Among the notable prince-bishops of this time were <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Clesio" title="Bernardo Clesio">Bernardo Clesio</a> (who governed the city from 1514 to 1539, and managed to steer the Council to Trento) and <a href="/wiki/Cristoforo_Madruzzo" title="Cristoforo Madruzzo">Cristoforo Madruzzo</a> (who governed from 1539 to 1567), both able European politicians and Renaissance <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanists</a>, who greatly expanded and embellished the city.\n</p><p>During this period, and as an expression of this Humanism, Trento was also known as the site of a <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> printing press. In 1558 Cardinal Madruzzo granted the privilege of printing <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Rabbinic_literature" title="Rabbinic literature">books</a> to Joseph Ottolengo, a German <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbi</a>. The actual printer was <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Marcaria" title="Jacob Marcaria">Jacob Marcaria</a>, a local physician; after his death in 1562, the activity of the press of Riva di Trento ceased. Altogether, 34 works were published in the period from 1558 to 1562, most of them bearing the coat of arms of Madruzzo.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Prince-bishops governed Trento until the Napoleonic era, when it changed hands among various states. Under the <a href="/wiki/Reichsdeputationshauptschluss" title="Reichsdeputationshauptschluss">reorganization of the Holy Roman Empire</a> in 1802, the Bishopric was secularized and annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> territories. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Pressburg_(1805)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Pressburg (1805)">Treaty of Pressburg</a> in 1805 ceded Trento to <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Sch%C3%B6nbrunn" title="Treaty of Sch\xc3\xb6nbrunn">Treaty of Sch\xc3\xb6nbrunn</a> four years later gave it to Napoleon\'s <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Napoleonic)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)">Kingdom of Italy</a>.\n</p><p>The population staged armed resistance to French domination. The resistance leader was <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Hofer" title="Andreas Hofer">Andreas Hofer</a>. During his youth, he lived in Italian Tyrol, where he learned the <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian language</a>. When Hofer recovered Trento for the Austrians (1809), he was welcomed with enthusiasm by the population of Trento. Approximately 4,000 Trentinian volunteers (<i>S\xc3\xaczzeri</i> or <i>Sch\xc3\xbctzen</i>) died in battle against the French and Bavarian troops. In 1810, Hofer was captured and brought to <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>, and was shot by French soldiers on the express order of Napoleon.\n</p><p>With Napoleon\'s defeat in 1814, Trento was again annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg Empire">Habsburg Empire</a>. Church government was finally extinguished, and Trento was henceforth governed by the secular administration of <a href="/wiki/German_Tyrol" title="German Tyrol">Tyrol</a>. In the following decades, Trento experienced a modernization of administration and economy with the first railroad in the Adige valley opening in 1859.  The entire Mediterranean basin was at risk of malaria, a factor that affected the entire Italian peninsula and this Alpine region was not spared. Even Tuscany was particularly hard hit; malaria existed far inland into the Veneto area, reaching the Italian Alps.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> From 1918 to 1940, government figures show Italy\'s malaria deaths decreased by 96%, due to the efforts of the Rockefeller Foundation and Italy\'s own malaria experts, who themselves were international leaders in malariology.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the late 19th century, Trento and <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a>, cities with ethnic Italian majorities still belonging to the Austrians, became icons of the Italian <a href="/wiki/Irredentist" class="mw-redirect" title="Irredentist">irredentist</a> movement. <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> briefly joined the staff of a local newspaper in 1909, but left Trento because they could not create an anti-Austrian group. There was dissatisfaction with the lack of provincial autonomy and the failure to establish a university for the region. Feelings of loyalty were focused on the \'father-figure\' emperor, not for Austria.  \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg/220px-Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg" width="220" height="60" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg/330px-Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg/440px-Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5366" data-file-height="1471" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Monumento_a_Cesare_Battisti_tre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mausoleum of <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Battisti_(politician)" title="Cesare Battisti (politician)">Cesare Battisti</a></div></div></div><p>The nationalist cause led Italy into <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Damiano Chiesa and the deputy in the Austrian parliament <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Battisti_(politician)" title="Cesare Battisti (politician)">Cesare Battisti</a> were two well-known local irredentists who had joined the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Italian_Army" title="Royal Italian Army">Italian Army</a> to fight against Austria-Hungary with the aim of bringing the territory of Trento into the new <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a>. The two men were taken prisoners at the nearby southern front. They were put on trial for high treason and executed in the courtyard of <a href="/wiki/Castello_del_Buonconsiglio" class="mw-redirect" title="Castello del Buonconsiglio">Castello del Buonconsiglio</a>.\n</p><p>The region was greatly affected during the war, and some of its fiercest battles were fought on the surrounding mountains in the southernmost regions and the southeast. Of a population of just less than 400,000 in the province, 55,000 men served in the Imperial and Royal Army of whom 11,000 died. Most served on the Galician front. 700 served with the Italian Army. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Trento and its Italian-speaking province, along with <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a> (Bozen) and the part of Tyrol that stretched south of the Alpine watershed (which was primarily <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>-speaking), were annexed by Italy.\n</p><p>In July 1943 Mussolini was removed as Prime Minister when the allies invaded Sicily. Italy surrendered to the Allies, and declared war on Germany. German troops promptly invaded northern Italy and the provinces of Trento, <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Belluno" title="Province of Belluno">Belluno</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol" title="South Tyrol">South Tyrol</a> became part of the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Zone_of_the_Alpine_Foothills" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Zone of the Alpine Foothills">Operation Zone of the Alpine Foothills</a>, annexed to <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a>. Some German-speakers wanted revenge upon Italian-speakers living in the area, but were mostly prevented by the occupying German troops, who still considered Mussolini head of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Social_Republic" title="Italian Social Republic">Italian Social Republic</a> and wanted to preserve good relations with the Italians. From November 1944 to April 1945, Trento was bombed as part of the so-called "Battle of the Brenner". War supplies from Germany to support the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Line" title="Gothic Line">Gothic Line</a> were for the most part routed through the rail line through the Brenner Pass. Over 6,849 sorties were flown over targets from <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Pass" title="Brenner Pass">Brenner Pass</a>, with 10,267 tons of bombs dropped. Parts of the city were hit by the Allied bombings, including the church of S. Maria Maggiore, the Church of the Annunciation and several bridges over the <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">Adige</a> river. In spite of the bombings, most of the medieval and renaissance city center was spared. It was finally liberated on 3 May 1945.\n</p><p>In 1947, Trento became the host of the <a href="/wiki/Rally_Stella_Alpina" title="Rally Stella Alpina">Rally Stella Alpina</a>.\n</p><p>Since the 1950s, the region has enjoyed prosperous growth, thanks in part to its special autonomy from the central Italian government.\n</p><p>On 4 August 2015, the cathedral tower caught fire by "spontaneous combustion". The clock stopped at 10:50 AM, a matter of minutes after the fire began.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pergola_trentino.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Pergola_trentino.JPG/220px-Pergola_trentino.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Pergola_trentino.JPG/330px-Pergola_trentino.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Pergola_trentino.JPG/440px-Pergola_trentino.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pergola_trentino.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vineyard in Trento</div></div></div><p>The city owes much of its unique economy to its position along the main communication route between Italy and Northern Europe and to the Adige river which, prior to its diversion in the mid-19th century, ran through the center of the city. The Adige river was formerly a navigable river and one of the main commercial routes in the Alps. The original course of the river is now covered by the Via Torre Vanga, Via Torre Verde and the Via Alessandro Manzoni.\n</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg/220px-Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg/330px-Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg/440px-Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trento-university-science-faculty-2010.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>University of Trento, Faculty of Science</div></div></div>\n<p>As late as World War II, Trento <a href="/wiki/Trento_DOC" title="Trento DOC">depended on wine-making</a> and silk.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The manufacturing industry installed in the post-war period has been mostly dismantled. Today, Trento thrives on commerce, services, tourism, high-quality agriculture and food industry (including wine, fruit), as a research and conference center thanks to a small but renowned <a href="/wiki/University_of_Trento" title="University of Trento">university</a>\nand internationally renowned research centers such as <i>Fondazione Bruno Kessler</i> <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.fbk.eu">[1]</a>, active in both fundamental and applied research, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110905083415/http://isig.fbk.eu/it/home">Italian-German Historical Institute</a>, the Centre for Computational and Systems Biology <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.cosbi.eu">[2]</a> and ECT* <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.ectstar.eu">[3]</a>, active in theoretical nuclear studies and part of FBK, and as logistics and transportation thoroughfare.\n</p><p>Valued pink and white <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(geology)" title="Porphyry (geology)">porphyry</a> is still excavated from some surrounding areas (Pila). This stone can be seen in many of Trento\'s buildings, both new and old.\n</p><p>The city has two long-running annual sporting events: the <a href="/wiki/Giro_al_Sas" title="Giro al Sas">Giro al Sas</a> (a 10&#160;km (6&#160;mi) professional road running competition) was first held in the city in 1907 and continues to the present,<sup id="cite_ref-Storia_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Storia-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Giro_del_Trentino" class="mw-redirect" title="Giro del Trentino">Giro del Trentino</a> is an annual <a href="/wiki/Road_cycling_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Road cycling race">road cycling race</a> which the city has hosted every year since 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_Festival_Trento">Economy Festival Trento</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy Festival Trento">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Economy festival (Festival dell\xe2\x80\x99Economia di Trento) has been brought into being in 2006 in order to enable and facilitate discussions between economists and a broad public. The aim of this festival is to put the economic terminology across to everyone. The Festival dell\xe2\x80\x99Economia di Trento takes place every year at the end of May on the historic Palazzi of the old town in Trento. \nWell known economists explain and interpret current economy issues, both from a economic-scientific as well as from a social and entrepreneurial viewpoint. In the course of the last years, numerous economy scholars and managers such as Sir <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Atkinson" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthony Atkinson">Anthony Atkinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fan_Gang" title="Fan Gang">Fan Gang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zygmunt_Bauman" title="Zygmunt Bauman">Zygmunt Bauman</a> and the Nobel Prize winner <a href="/wiki/Gary_Becker" title="Gary Becker">Gary Becker</a> took an active part in.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Trento is governed by the City Council of Trento. class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Museo_Diocesano_Tridentino.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" path of mass tourism, Trento offers rather interesting monuments. Its architecture has a unique feel, with both Italian Renaissance and Germanic influences. The city center is small, and most Late-Medieval and Renaissance buildings have been restored to their original pastel colours and wooden balconies. Part of the medieval city walls is still visible in Piazza Fiera, along with a circular tower. Once, these walls encircled the entire city and were connected to the Castello del Buonconsiglio.\nThe main monuments of the city include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Trento_Cathedral" title="Trento Cathedral">Duomo</a></b></i> (Cathedral of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vigilius" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Vigilius">Saint Vigilius</a>), a Romanesque-Gothic cathedral of the twelfth-thirteenth century, built on top of a late-Roman basilica (viewable in an underground crypt).</li>\n<li><i><b>Piazza Duomo</b></i>, on the side of the cathedral, with frescoed Renaissance buildings and the Late Baroque Fountain of Neptune (<i>Fontana di Nettuno</i>) built in 1767\xe2\x80\x931768.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Church_of_Santa_Maria_Maggiore,_Trento" class="mw-redirect" title="Church of Santa Maria Maggiore, Trento">Church of Santa Maria Maggiore</a></b> (1520), site of the preparatory congregations of the Third Council of Trent (April 1562 \xe2\x80\x93 December 1563). It was built for Bishop <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Clesio" title="Bernardo Clesio">Bernardo Clesio</a> by the architect Antonio Medaglia in Renaissance-Gothic style. The fa\xc3\xa7ade has a notable 16th-century portal, while the interior has works by <a href="/wiki/Giambettino_Cignaroli" title="Giambettino Cignaroli">Giambettino Cignaroli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gian_Battista_Moroni" class="mw-redirect" title="Gian Battista Moroni">Moroni</a>.</li>\n<li><i><b>Castello del Buonconsiglio</b></i> (<b><a href="/wiki/Buonconsiglio_Castle" title="Buonconsiglio Castle">Buonconsiglio Castle</a></b>), which includes a museum and the notable Torre dell\'Aquila, with a cycle of fine Gothic frescoes depicting the months, commissioned by the prince-bishop Georg von Lichtenstein.</li>\n<li><b>Church of San Pietro</b> (12th century) It has a neo-Gothic fa\xc3\xa7ade added in 1848\xe2\x80\x931850.</li>\n<li><b>Church of Sant\'Apollinare</b>, erected in the 13th century at the feet of the Doss Trento hill.</li>\n<li><b>Church of San Lorenzo</b> (12th century). It has a Romanesque apse.</li>\n<li><i><b>Torre Verde</b></i> (<b>Green Tower</b>), along the former transit path of the <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">Adige</a> river, is said to be where persons executed in the name of the Prince-Bishop were deposited in the river.</li>\n<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Palazzo_delle_Albere" class="mw-redirect" title="Palazzo delle Albere">Palazzo delle Albere</a></b></i> (<b>Palace of the Trees</b>), a Renaissance villa next to the Adige river built around 1550 by the Madruzzo family, now hosting a modern art museum.</li>\n<li><i><b>Palazzo Pretorio</b></i>, next to the Duomo, of the 12th century, with a bell tower (<i>Torre Civica</i>) of the thirteenth century (it now hosts a collection of baroque paintings of religious themes). It was the main Bishops\' residence until the mid-13th century.</li>\n<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Salvadori" class="mw-redirect" title="Palazzo Salvadori">Palazzo Salvadori</a></b></i> (1515).</li>\n<li><i><b>Palazzo Geremia</b></i> (late 15th century). It has a Renaissance exterior and Gothic interiors.</li>\n<li><i><b>Palazzo Lodron</b></i>, built during the Council of Trent. The interior has a large fresco cycle.</li>\n<li>Various underground remains of the streets and villas of the Roman city (in Via Prepositura and Piazza Cesare Battisti).</li></ul>\n<p>Trento also sports <a href="/wiki/Modernist" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernist">modernist</a> architecture, including the train station and the central post office, both by rationalist architect <a href="/wiki/Angiolo_Mazzoni" title="Angiolo Mazzoni">Angiolo Mazzoni</a>. In particular, the train station (1934\xe2\x80\x9336) is considered a landmark building of Italian railways architecture and combines many varieties of local stone with the most advanced building materials of the time: glass, reinforced concrete, metal. The post office was once decorated with colored windows by <a href="/wiki/Fortunato_Depero" title="Fortunato Depero">Fortunato Depero</a>, but these were destroyed during bombings in World War II. Other buildings of that time include the Grand Hotel (by G. Lorenzi) with some guest rooms furnished with futurist furniture by Depero, and the "R. Sanzio" Primary School built in 1931\xe2\x80\x9334 and designed by <a href="/wiki/Adalberto_Libera" title="Adalberto Libera">Adalberto Libera</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline"
