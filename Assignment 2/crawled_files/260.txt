border-left:thin solid lightgrey;">\n<a href="/wiki/List_of_Ambassadors_of_the_United_Kingdom_to_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Ambassadors of the United Kingdom to Ireland">Ambassador</a> Robin Barnett</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Ireland\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom relations</b>, also referred to as <b>Irish\xe2\x80\x93British relations</b>, or <b>Anglo-Irish relations</b>, are the relations between the states of <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The three <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolved administrations</a> of the United Kingdom, in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and the three <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">dependencies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="British Crown">British Crown</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, also participate in multilateral bodies created between the two states.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since at least the 1600s, all of these areas have been connected politically, reaching a height in 1801 with the creation of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>. About five-sixths of the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Island of Ireland">island of Ireland</a> seceded from the United Kingdom in 1921 as the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>. Historically, relations between the two states have been influenced heavily by issues arising from their shared (and frequently troubled) history, the independence of the Irish Free State and the governance of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. These include the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition of Ireland</a> and the terms of Ireland\'s secession, its constitutional relationship with and obligations to the UK after independence, and the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">political violence in Northern Ireland</a>. Additionally, the high level of trade between the two states, their proximate geographic location, their common status as islands in the European Union, common language and close cultural and personal links mean political developments in both states often closely follow each other.\n</p><p>Today, Irish and British citizens are accorded equivalent reciprocal rights and entitlements (with a small number of minor exceptions) and a <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a> exists between Ireland, United Kingdom, and the Crown Dependencies. The <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a> acts as an official forum for co-operation between the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Government of the United Kingdom</a> on matters of mutual interest generally, and with respect to Northern Ireland in particular. Two other bodies, the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Council" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Parliamentary_Assembly" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly</a> act as a forum for discussion between the executives and assemblies, respectively, of the region, including the devolved regions in the UK and the three Crown dependencies. Co-operation between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland, including the execution of common policies in certain areas, occurs through the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a>. In 2014, the UK Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a>, and the Irish Taoiseach <a href="/wiki/Enda_Kenny" title="Enda Kenny">Enda Kenny</a> described the relationship between the two countries as being at \'an all time high\'.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Both Ireland and the United Kingdom joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (then the <a href="/wiki/European_Communities" title="European Communities">European Communities</a>) in 1973. However, the three Crown dependencies remain outside of the EU. In June 2016, the United Kingdom held a referendum in which the majority voted to leave the European Union; but the majority of voters in Northern Ireland voted for remaining in the EU.\n</p>\n<div A <a href="/wiki/Romano-Briton" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-Briton">Romano-Briton</a>, Patricius, later known as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a>, brought Christianity to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and, following the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, missionaries from Ireland re-introduced Christianity to Britain.\n</p><p>The expansion of <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> culture into what became known as <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> (after the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Scoti" title="Scoti">Scoti</a></i>, meaning <a href="/wiki/Gael" class="mw-redirect" title="Gael">Gaels</a>) brought close <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">political and familial ties</a> between people in Ireland and people in Great Britain, lasting from the early Middle Ages to the 17th century, including a common <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic language">Gaelic language</a> spoken on both islands. <a href="/wiki/Norse-Gaels" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse-Gaels">Norse-Gaels</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Dublin" title="Kingdom of Dublin">Kingdom of Dublin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">Norman invasion of Ireland</a> added religious, political, economic and social ties between Northumbria and Wales with Leinster in <a href="/wiki/The_Pale" title="The Pale">the Pale</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tudor_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Tudor conquest of Ireland">War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plantations_of_Ireland" title="Plantations of Ireland">colonisation</a> during the 16th and 17th centuries brought Ireland securely under English control. However, this was at a cost of great resentment over <a href="/wiki/Act_for_the_Settlement_of_Ireland_1652" title="Act for the Settlement of Ireland 1652">land ownership</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penal_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Penal laws">inequitable laws</a>. This resulted in Gaelic ties between Scotland and Ireland withering dramatically over the course of the 17th century, including a divergence in the Gaelic language into two distinct languages.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Secret_societies" class="mw-redirect" title="Secret societies">Secret societies</a>, both opposing and supporting British rule through violent means, developed in the 18th century and several open rebellions were staged, most notably the <a href="/wiki/1798_Rebellion" class="mw-redirect" title="1798 Rebellion">1798 Rebellion</a>. Although Ireland gained <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_1782" title="Constitution of 1782">near-independence from Great Britain in 1782</a>, the kingdoms of the kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a> were <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1800">merged in 1801</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>.\n</p><p>19th century violent and constitutional campaigns for <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Home Rule Movement">autonomy</a> or independence culminated in <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1918" title="Irish general election, 1918">an election in 1918</a> returning almost 70% of seats to <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a>, who declared Irish independence from Britain and set up <a href="/wiki/First_D%C3%A1il" title="First D\xc3\xa1il">a parliament in Dublin</a>, and declared the independence of Ireland from the United Kingdom. A <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">war of independence</a> followed that ended with the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> of 1922, which partitioned Ireland between the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, which gained <a href="/wiki/Dominion_status" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominion status">dominion status</a> within the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, and a devolved administration in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, which remained part of the UK. In 1937, Ireland declared itself fully independent of the United Kingdom.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_landscape">Political landscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political landscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Politics of the Republic of Ireland">Politics of the Republic of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Politics of the United Kingdom">Politics of the United Kingdom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_terms.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/British_Isles_terms.gif/330px-British_Isles_terms.gif" width="330" height="467" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/British_Isles_terms.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="463" data-file-height="655" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_terms.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A political map of Ireland, the United Kingdom and the Crown Dependencies - The Irish state\'s official <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_Irish_state" title="Names of the Irish state">name is "Ireland"</a> but the description "Republic of Ireland" is used for it in the map.</div></div></div>\n<p>Today, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> contain two <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Sovereign states">sovereign states</a>: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> (alternatively described as the <i>Republic of Ireland</i>) and the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland. The United Kingdom comprises four <a href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">countries of the United Kingdom</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> All but Northern Ireland have been independent states at one point.\n</p><p>There are also three <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, in the archipelago which are not part of the United Kingdom, although the United Kingdom maintains responsibility for certain affairs such as international affairs and ensuring good governance, on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/British_crown" class="mw-redirect" title="British crown">British crown</a>, and can legislate directly for them. These participate in the shared institutions created between Ireland and the United Kingdom under the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>. The United Kingdom and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a> form what are called the <i><a href="/wiki/British_Islands" title="British Islands">British Islands</a></i>.\n</p><p>The devolved administrations of the United Kingdom and the three Crown Dependencies also participate in the shared institutions established under the Good Friday Agreement.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">British monarch</a> was <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> of all of these states and countries of the archipelago from the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Crowns" title="Union of the Crowns">Union of the Crowns</a> in 1603 until their role in Ireland became ambiguous with the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution of Ireland</a> in 1937. The remaining functions of the monarch in Ireland were transferred to the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>, with coming into effect of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Ireland Act">Republic of Ireland Act</a> in 1949.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_perspectives">Academic perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Academic perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_in_the_British_Isles#Scholarship_of_identity_and_politics_in_the_British_Isles" title="Politics in the British Isles">Politics in the British Isles \xc2\xa7&#160;Scholarship of identity and politics in the British Isles</a></div>\n<p>Several academic perspectives are important in the study and understanding of Ireland\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom relations. Important strands of scholarship include research on identity, especially <a href="/wiki/Britishness" title="Britishness">Britishness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irishness" class="mw-redirect" title="Irishness">Irishness</a>, and studies of the major political movements, such as <a href="/wiki/Separatism" title="Separatism">separatism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Unionism in the United Kingdom">unionism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Post-nationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-nationalism">post-nationalism</a> is also contemporary trend in studies of history, culture and politics in the isles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-independence_conflicts">Post-independence conflicts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-independence conflicts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_commission">Boundary commission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Boundary commission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission (Ireland)">Boundary Commission (Ireland)</a></div>\n<p>The day after the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, the Houses of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a> resolved to make an address to the King so as to opt out of the Irish Free State<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Immediately afterwards, the need to settle an agreed border between the Irish Free State and Northern Ireland arose. In response to this issue <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission (Ireland)">a commission</a> was set up involving representatives from the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_Irish_Free_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of the Irish Free State">Government of the Irish Free State</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Government of Northern Ireland">Government of Northern Ireland</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Government of the United Kingdom</a> which would chair the Commission. Ultimately and after some controversy, the present border was fixed, not by the Commission but by agreement between the United Kingdom (including Northern Ireland) and the Irish Free State.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Irish_Trade_War">Anglo-Irish Trade War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anglo-Irish Trade War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Trade_War" title="Anglo-Irish Trade War">Anglo-Irish Trade War</a></div>\n<p>A further dispute arose in 1930 over the issue of the Irish government\'s refusal to reimburse the United Kingdom with "land annuities". These annuities were derived from government financed <a href="/wiki/Soft_loan" title="Soft loan">soft loans</a> given to Irish tenant farmers before independence to allow them to buy out their farms from landlords (see <a href="/wiki/Irish_Land_Acts" title="Irish Land Acts">Irish Land Acts</a>). These loans were intended to redress <a href="/wiki/Act_for_the_Settlement_of_Ireland_1652" title="Act for the Settlement of Ireland 1652">the issue of landownership in Ireland</a> arising from the wars of the 17th century. The refusal of the Irish government to pass on monies it collected from these loans to the British government led to a retaliatory and escalating <a href="/wiki/Trade_war" title="Trade war">trade war</a> between the two states from 1932 until 1938, a period known as the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Trade_War" title="Anglo-Irish Trade War">Anglo-Irish Trade War</a> or the Economic War.\n</p><p>While the UK was less affected by the Economic War, the Irish economy was virtually crippled by the resulting <a href="/wiki/Capital_flight" title="Capital flight">capital flight</a>. Unemployment was extremely high and the effects of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> compounded the difficulties. The government urged people to support the confrontation with the UK as a national hardship to be shared by every citizen. Pressures, especially from agricultural producers in Ireland and exporters in the UK, led to an agreement between the two governments in 1938 resolving the dispute.\n</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Infant_industry_argument" title="Infant industry argument">infant industries</a> were established during this "economic war". Almost complete import substitution was achieved in many sectors.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> behind a <a href="/wiki/Trade_barrier" title="Trade barrier">protective tariff barrier</a>. These industries proved valuable during the war years as they reduced the need for imports.\nUnder the terms of resulting <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Trade_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Trade Agreement">Anglo-Irish Trade Agreement</a>, all duties imposed during the previous five years were lifted but Ireland was still entitled to impose tariffs on British imports to protect new Irish "infant" industries. Ireland was to pay a one-off \xc2\xa310 million sum to the United Kingdom (as opposed to annual repayments of \xc2\xa3250,000 over 47 more years). Arguably the most significant outcome, however, was the return of so-called "<a href="/wiki/Treaty_Ports_(Ireland)" title="Treaty Ports (Ireland)">Treaty Ports</a>", three ports in Ireland maintained by the UK as <a href="/wiki/UK_sovereign_base" class="mw-redirect" title="UK sovereign base">sovereign bases</a> under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>. The handover of these ports facilitated <a href="/wiki/Irish_neutrality_during_World_War_II" title="Irish neutrality during World War II">Irish neutrality during World War II</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and made it much harder for Britain to ensure the safety of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Conveys" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Conveys">Atlantic Conveys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Articles_2_and_3_and_the_name_Ireland">Articles 2 and 3 and the name <i>Ireland</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Articles 2 and 3 and the name Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Articles_2_and_3" class="mw-redirect" title="Articles 2 and 3">Articles 2 and 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_Irish_state" title="Names of the Irish state">Names of the Irish state</a></div>\n<p>Ireland adopted <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">a new constitution in 1937</a>. This declared Ireland to be a sovereign, independent state, but did not explicitly declare Ireland to be a republic. However, it did change the name of the state from <i>Irish Free State</i> to <i>Ireland</i> (or <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">\xc3\x89ire</i> in the Irish language). It also contained <a href="/wiki/Irredentism" title="Irredentism">irredentist</a> claims on Northern Ireland, stating that the "national territory [of the Irish state] consists of the whole island of Ireland" (Article 2). This was measured in some way by Article 3, which stated that, "Pending the re-integration of the national territory ... the laws enacted by the parliament [of Ireland] shall have the like area and extent of application as the laws of Saorstat \xc3\x89ireann" (<i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Saorst\xc3\xa1t \xc3\x89ireann</i> is the Irish language name of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>).\n</p><p>The United Kingdom initially accepted the change in the name to <i>Ireland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, it subsequently changed its practice and passed legislation providing that the Irish state could be called <i>Eire </i>(notably without a <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">fada</a></i>) in British law. For sometime, the United Kingdom was supported by some other <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth countries">Commonwealth countries</a>. However, by the mid-1960s, <i>Ireland</i> was the accepted diplomatic name of the Irish state.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>, the disagreement led to request for extradition of terrorist suspects to be struck invalid by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Ireland" title="Supreme Court of Ireland">Supreme Court of Ireland</a> unless the name <i>Ireland</i> was used. Increasingly positive relations between the two states required the two states to explore imaginative work-arounds to the disagreement. For example, while the United Kingdom would not agree to refer to <a href="/wiki/Mary_Robinson" title="Mary Robinson">Mary Robinson</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a></i> on an official visit to <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> (the first such visit in the two states\' history), they agreed to refer to her instead as "President Robinson of Ireland".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>As a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a>, Articles 2 and 3 were changed in 1999 formalising shared Irish and British citizenship in Northern Ireland, removing the irredentist claim and making provisions for common "[institutions] with executive powers and functions ... in respect of all or any part of the island."\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abdication_crisis_and_the_Republic_of_Ireland_Act">Abdication crisis and the Republic of Ireland Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Abdication crisis and the Republic of Ireland Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII_abdication_crisis" title="Edward VIII abdication crisis">Edward VIII abdication crisis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_head_of_state_from_1936_to_1949" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish head of state from 1936 to 1949">Irish head of state from 1936 to 1949</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Ireland Act">Republic of Ireland Act</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Ireland" title="Monarchy of Ireland">Monarchy of Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">Monarchy of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Edward_abdication.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Edward_abdication.png/220px-Edward_abdication.png" width="220" height="329" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Edward_abdication.png/330px-Edward_abdication.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Edward_abdication.png/440px-Edward_abdication.png 2x" data-file-width="540" data-file-height="808" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Edward_abdication.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Instrument of Abdication signed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII" title="Edward VIII">Edward VIII</a> and his three brothers</div></div></div>\n<p>The Irish Free State had been governed, at least until 1936, under a form of constitutional monarchy linked to the United Kingdom. The King had a number of symbolically important duties, including exercising the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> authority of the state, appointing the cabinet and promulgating the law. However, when <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII" title="Edward VIII">Edward VIII</a> proposed to marry <a href="/wiki/Wallis_Simpson" title="Wallis Simpson">Wallis Simpson</a>, an American <a href="/wiki/Socialite" title="Socialite">socialite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Divorc%C3%A9e" class="mw-redirect" title="Divorc\xc3\xa9e">divorc\xc3\xa9e</a>, in 1936, it caused a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_crisis" title="Constitutional crisis">constitutional crisis</a> across the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. In the chaos that ensued his abdication, the Irish Free State took the opportunity to amend its constitution and remove all of the functions of the King except one: that of representing the state abroad.\n</p><p>In 1937, <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">a new constitution</a> was adopted which entrenched the monarch\'s diminished role by transferring many of the functions performed by the King until 1936 to a new office of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>, who was declared to "take precedence over all other persons in the State". However, the 1937 constitution did not explicitly declare that the state was a republic, nor that the President was head of state. Without explicit mention, the King continued to retain his role in external relations and the Irish Free State continued to be regarded as a member of the <a href="/wiki/British_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="British Commonwealth">British Commonwealth</a> and to be associated with the United Kingdom.\n</p><p>During the period from December 1936 to April 1949, it was unclear whether or not the Irish state was a <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a> or a form of <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> and (from 1937) whether its <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> was the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> (<a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hyde" title="Douglas Hyde">Douglas Hyde</a> until 1945, and <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_T._O%27Kelly" title="Se\xc3\xa1n T. O&#39;Kelly">Se\xc3\xa1n T. O\'Kelly</a> afterwards) or the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Ireland">King of Ireland</a> (<a href="/wiki/George_VI_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George VI of the United Kingdom">George VI</a>). The exact constitutional status of the state during this period has been a matter of scholarly and political dispute.\n</p><p>The state\'s ambiguous status ended in 1949, when the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act_1948" title="Republic of Ireland Act 1948">Republic of Ireland Act</a> stripped the King of his role in external relations and declared that the state may be described as the <i>Republic of Ireland</i>. The decision to do so was sudden and unilateral. However, it did not result in greatly strained relations between Ireland and the United Kingdom. The question of the head of the Irish state from 1936 to 1949 was largely a matter of symbolism and had little practical significance. The UK response was to legislate that it would not grant Northern Ireland to the Irish state without the consent of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a> (which was unlikely to happen in <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionist</a>-majority Northern Ireland).\n</p><p>One practical implication of explicitly declaring the state to be a republic in 1949 was that it automatically terminated the state\'s membership of the <a href="/wiki/British_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="British Commonwealth">British Commonwealth</a>, in accordance with the rules in operation at the time. However, despite this, the United Kingdom legislated that Irish citizens would retain similar rights to Commonwealth subjects and were not to be regarded as foreigners.\n</p><p>The Republic of Ireland Act came into force on 18 April 1949. Ten days later, 28 April 1949, the rules of the Commonwealth of Nations were changed through the <a href="/wiki/London_Declaration" title="London Declaration">London Declaration</a> so that, when India declared itself a republic, it would not have to leave. The prospect of Ireland rejoining the Commonwealth, even today, is still occasionally raised but has never been formally considered by the Irish government.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponyms">Toponyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Toponyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/British_Isles_naming_dispute" title="British Isles naming dispute">British Isles naming dispute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terminology_of_the_British_Isles" title="Terminology of the British Isles">Terminology of the British Isles</a></div>\n<p>A minor, through recurring, source of antagonism between Britain and Ireland is the name of the archipelago in which they both are located. Commonly known as the <i>British Isles</i>, this name is opposed by some in Ireland and its use is objected to by the Irish Government.\n</p><p>A spokesman for the Irish Embassy in London recently said, "The British Isles has a dated ring to it, as if we are still part of the Empire. We are independent, we are not part of Britain, not even in geographical terms. We would discourage its usage &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93;.".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>No consensus on another name for the islands exists. In practice, the two Governments and the shared institutions of the archipelago avoid use of the term, frequently using the euphemism <i>these islands</i> in place of any term.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Troubles">The Troubles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Troubles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div id="mwe_player_0" class="PopUpMediaTransform" style="width:220px;" videopayload="&lt;div class=&quot;mediaContainer&quot; The civil rights campaign demanding an end to institutionalised discrimination against <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">nationalists</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionist</a> Government of Northern Ireland. As the violence escalated, <a href="/wiki/1969_Northern_Ireland_riots" title="1969 Northern Ireland riots">rioting and attacks</a> by nationalist and unionist groups began to de-stabilise the province and required the presence of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Banner" title="Operation Banner">British troops on the ground</a>.\n</p><p>In the wake of the riots, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> expressed its concern about the situation. In a televised broadcast, <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lynch" title="Jack Lynch">Jack Lynch</a> stated that the Irish Government could "no longer stand by" while hundreds of people were being injured. This was interpreted as a threat of military intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Exercise_Armageddon" title="Exercise Armageddon">a plan for an Irish invasion of Northern Ireland</a> was rejected by the Government of Ireland, a secret<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Irish government fund of \xc2\xa3100,000 was dedicated to helping refugees from the violence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Some more actively nationalist Irish Ministers were tried in 1970 when it emerged that some of the fund had been <a href="/wiki/Arms_Crisis" title="Arms Crisis">spent covertly on buying arms</a> for nationalists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Angry crowds burned down the <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_the_United_Kingdom,_Dublin" title="Embassy of the United Kingdom, Dublin">British Embassy in Dublin</a> in protest at the shooting by British troops of 13 civilians in Derry, Northern Ireland on <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Sunday_(1972)" title="Bloody Sunday (1972)">Bloody Sunday (1972)</a> and in 1981 protesters tried to storm the British Embassy in response to the IRA <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strike" title="Hunger strike">hunger strikes</a> of that year. In 1978, the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECHR) trial "Ireland v. the United Kingdom" ruled that the techniques used in interrogating prisoners in Northern Ireland "amounted to a practice of inhuman and degrading treatment", in breach of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>.\n</p><p>An attempt by the two governments to resolve the conflict in Northern Ireland politically in 1972 through the <a href="/wiki/Sunningdale_Agreement" title="Sunningdale Agreement">Sunningdale Agreement</a> failed due to opposition by hard-line factions in Northern Ireland. With no resolution to the conflict in sight, the Irish government established the <a href="/wiki/New_Ireland_Forum" title="New Ireland Forum">New Ireland Forum</a> in 1984 to look into solutions. While the British <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">UK Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> rejected the forum\'s proposals, it informed the British government\'s opinion and it is said to have given the Irish Taoiseach <a href="/wiki/Garret_FitzGerald" title="Garret FitzGerald">Garret FitzGerald</a> a <a href="/wiki/Mandate_(politics)" title="Mandate (politics)">mandate</a> during the negotiation of the 1985 <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a>, which was directed at resolving the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinnott1995_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinnott1995-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cochrane2001_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cochrane2001-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The 1992 <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street_Declaration" title="Downing Street Declaration">Downing Street Declaration</a> further consolidated the views of the two Governments and the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> eventually formed the basis for peace in the province.\n</p><p><span id="Reconciliation_Networking_Forum"></span>The Irish Department of Foreign Affairs established a "Reconciliation Fund" in 1982 to support organisations whose work tends to improve cross-community or North\xe2\x80\x93South relations.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Since 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs and Trade">Minister for Foreign Affairs</a> has hosted an annual "Reconciliation Networking Forum" (sometimes called the "Reconciliation Forum"; not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Forum_for_Peace_and_Reconciliation" title="Forum for Peace and Reconciliation">Forum for Peace and Reconciliation</a>) in Dublin to which such groups are invited.<sup id="cite_ref-AllainMullally2008_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllainMullally2008-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brexit">Brexit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Brexit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Irish_border_question" title="Irish border question">Irish border question</a></div>\n<p>There is <a href="/wiki/Irish_border_question" title="Irish border question">a controversy</a> about the impact that <a href="/wiki/Britain%27s_withdrawal_from_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Britain&#39;s withdrawal from the European Union">Britain\'s withdrawal from the European Union</a> will have on the <a href="/wiki/Border_between_the_Republic_of_Ireland_and_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Border between the Republic of Ireland and Northern Ireland">border between the Republic of Ireland and Northern Ireland</a>, in particular the impact it may have on the economy and people of the island were customs or immigration checks to be put in place at the border. It was prioritised as one of the three most important areas to resolve in order to reach a <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Withdrawal Agreement">Withdrawal Agreement</a>. \n</p><p>The UK voted to leave the European Union in a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">referendum on 23 June 2016</a> which would effectively make the Republic of Ireland-Northern Ireland border an <a href="/wiki/External_border_of_the_European_Union" title="External border of the European Union">external EU border</a>. All parties have stated that they want to avoid a hard border in Ireland particularly due to the sensitive nature of the border.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Good_Friday_Agreement">Good and improved relations between Ireland and the United Kingdom. A 1981 meeting between the two governments established the Anglo-Irish Intergovernmental Council. This was further developed in 1985 under the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a> whereby the two governments created the Anglo-Irish Intergovernmental Conference, under the Anglo-Irish Intergovernmental Council, as a regular forum for the two Governments to reach agreement on, "(i) political matters; (ii) security and related matters; (iii) legal matters, including the administration of justice; (iv) the promotion of cross-border co-operation." The Conference was "mainly concerned with Northern Ireland; but some of the matters under consideration will involve cooperative action in both parts of the island of Ireland, and possibly also in Great Britain." The Agreement also recommended the establishment of the Anglo-Irish Interparliamentary Body, a body where parliamentarians from the <a href="/wiki/Houses_of_the_Oireachtas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houses of the Oireachtas">Houses of the Oireachtas</a> (Ireland) and <a href="/wiki/Houses_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Houses of Parliament">Houses of Parliament</a> (United Kingdom) would regularly meet to share views and ideas. This was created in 1990 as the British-Irish Inter-Parliamentary Body.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a> culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> of 1998 that further developed the institutions established under these Anglo-Irish Agreement. New institutions were established interlocking across "strands":\n</p>\n<ul><li>Strand I: an Assembly and Executive for Northern Ireland based on the <a href="/wiki/D%27Hondt_method" title="D&#39;Hondt method">D\'Hondt system</a>;</li>\n<li>Strand II: a <a href="/wiki/North-South_Ministerial_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="North-South Ministerial Council">North-South Ministerial Council</a> to develop co-operation and common policies within the island of Ireland;</li>\n<li>Strand III:\n<ol><li>a <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Council">British-Irish Council</a> "to promote the harmonious and mutually beneficial development of the totality of relationships among the peoples of these islands"</li>\n<li>a new <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a>, established under the <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Agreement">British-Irish Agreement</a>, replaced the Anglo-Irish Intergovernmental Council and the Anglo-Irish Intergovernmental Conference.</li></ol></li></ul>\n<p>The scope of the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a> is broader that the original Conference, and is intended to "bring together the British and Irish Governments to promote bilateral co-operation at all levels on all matters of mutual interest within the competence of both Governments." The Conference also provides a joint institution for the government of Northern Ireland on non-devolved matters (or all matters when the Northern Ireland Assembly is suspended). However, the United Kingdom retains ultimate sovereignty over Northern Ireland. Representatives from Northern Ireland participate in the Conference when matters relating to Northern Ireland are concerned.\n</p><p>The members of the British-Irish Council (sometimes called the <i>Council of the Isles</i>) are representatives of the Irish and British Governments, the devolved administrations in Northern Ireland, Scotland and Wales, together with representatives of the Isle of Man and the Channel Islands. It meets regularly to discuss matters of mutual interest divided into work areas (such as energy, environment or housing) allocated to individual members to work and report on.\n</p><p>The Anglo-Irish Interparliamentary Body developed independently over the same period, eventually becoming known as the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Parliamentary_Assembly" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly</a> and including members from the devolved administrations of the UK and the Crown Dependencies.\n</p><p>The development of these institutions was supported by acts such the visit of efforts by <a href="/wiki/Mary_Robinson" title="Mary Robinson">Mary Robinson</a> (as President of Ireland) to the Queen Elizabeth II (Queen of the United Kingdom), an apology by Tony Blair (as Prime Minister of the United Kingdom) to Irish people for the failures of the British Government during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Great Famine</a> of 1845\xe2\x80\x941852 and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Ireland_Peace_Park" title="Island of Ireland Peace Park">Island of Ireland Peace Park</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II%27s_visit_to_the_Republic_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II&#39;s visit to the Republic of Ireland">state visit of Queen Elizabeth II</a> to Ireland in May 2011 \xe2\x80\x94 including the laying of a wreath at a memorial to <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">IRA</a> fighters in the Anglo-Irish war \xe2\x80\x94 symbolically sealed the change in relationships between the two states following the transfer of police and justice powers to Northern Ireland. The visit came a century after her grandfather, <a href="/wiki/King_George_V" class="mw-redirect" title="King George V">King George V</a>, was the last monarch of the United Kingdom to pay a state visit to Ireland in July 1911, while it was still part of the United Kingdom.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-operation">Co-operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Co-operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a> provides for co-operation between the Government of Ireland and the Government of the United Kingdom on all matters of mutual interest for which they have competence. Meetings take the form of <a href="/wiki/Summit_(meeting)" title="Summit (meeting)">summits</a> between the Prime Minister of the United Kingdom and the Irish Taoiseach, on an "as required" basis. Otherwise, the two governments are represented by the appropriate ministers. In light of Ireland\'s particular interest in the governance of Northern Ireland, "regular and frequent" meetings co-chaired by the Irish Minister for Foreign Affairs and the UK Secretary of State for Northern Ireland, dealing with non-devolved matters to do with Northern Ireland and non-devolved <a href="/wiki/All-Ireland" title="All-Ireland">all-Ireland</a> issues, are required to take place under the establishing treaty.\n</p><p>At these meetings, the Irish government may put forward views and proposals, however sovereignty over Northern Ireland remains with the United Kingdom. In all of the work of the Conference, "All decisions will be by agreement between both Governments [who] will make determined efforts to resolve disagreements between them." The Conference is supported by a standing secretariat at located at Belfast, Northern Ireland, dealing with non-devolved matters affecting Northern Ireland.\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".27All-islands.27_institutions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="\'All-islands\'_institutions">\'All-islands\' institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: &#039;All-islands&#039; institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Council">British-Irish Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Parliamentary_Assembly" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_(political_diagram).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/British_Isles_%28political_diagram%29.svg/330px-British_Isles_%28political_diagram%29.svg.png" width="330" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/British_Isles_%28political_diagram%29.svg/495px-British_Isles_%28political_diagram%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/British_Isles_%28political_diagram%29.svg/660px-British_Isles_%28political_diagram%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1073" data-file-height="743" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_(political_diagram).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Euler_diagram" title="Euler diagram">Euler diagram</a> showing the major political entities of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The British-Irish Council (BIC) is an <a href="/wiki/International_organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="International organisation">international organisation</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> laid out under the <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Belfast Agreement">Belfast Agreement</a> in 1998 and created by the established by the two Governments in 1999. Its members are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the two <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign</a> governments of <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the United Kingdom;</li>\n<li>the three <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolved administrations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a></li>\n<li>the three governments of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>The Council formally came into being on 2 December 1999. Its stated aim is to "promote the harmonious and mutually beneficial development of the totality of relationships among the peoples of these islands". The BIC has a standing secretariat, located in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and meets in bi-annual summits and regular sectoral meetings. Summit meetings are attended by the heads of each administrations (e.g. the Prime Minister of the United Kingdom) whereas sectoral meetings are attended by the relevant <a href="/wiki/Government_minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Government minister">ministers</a> form each administration.\n</p><p>While the Council is made up of representatives from the executive of the various administrations in the region, it does not have executive power itself. Instead, its decisions, so far as they exist, are implemented separately by each administration on the basis of consensus. Given this \xe2\x80\x94 that the Council has no means to force its member administrations into implementing programmes of action \xe2\x80\x94 the Council has been dismissed as a "talking shop" and its current role appears to be one mainly of "information exchange and consultation".<sup id="cite_ref-tonge_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tonge-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to the Council, the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Parliamentary_Assembly" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly</a> (BIPA) is composed of members of the legislative bodies in the United Kingdom, including the devolved legislatures, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, and the British <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependency" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown dependency">Crown dependencies</a>. It is the older of the two \'all-islands\' institutions (BIC and BIPA) having been founded in 1990 as the British-Irish Inter-Parliamentary Body. Its purpose is to foster common understanding between elected representatives from these jurisdictions and, while having no legislative power, it conducts parliamentary activities such as receiving oral submissions, preparing reports and debating topical issues. The Assembly meets in plenary on a bi-annual basis, alternating in venue between Britain and Ireland, and maintains on-going work in committee.\n</p><p>These institutions have been described as part of a <a href="/wiki/Confederation" title="Confederation">confederal approach</a> to the government of the British-Irish archipelago.<sup id="cite_ref-tonge_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tonge-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="All-Ireland_institutions">All-Ireland institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: All-Ireland institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg/220px-North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg/330px-North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg/440px-North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2193" data-file-height="1404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The offices of the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a> on Upper English Street, <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a> (NSMC) coordinates activity and exercises certain governmental functions across the island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. The Council is responsible for developing and executing policy in at least twelve areas of co-operation, of which:\n</p>\n<ul><li>at least six are executed separately in each jurisdiction</li>\n<li>at least six are executed by an all-Ireland "implementation body"</li></ul>\n<p>Further development of the role and function of the Council are possible "with the specific endorsement of the Northern Ireland Assembly and Oireachtas, subject to the extent of the competences and responsibility of the two Administrations."\n</p><p>The North/South Ministerial Council and the Northern Ireland Assembly are defined in the Good Friday Agreement as being "mutually inter-dependent, and that one cannot successfully function without the other." Participation in the Council is a requisite for the operation of the Northern Ireland Assembly and participation in the Northern Ireland Executive. When devolution in Northern Ireland is suspended, the powers of the Northern Ireland Executive revert to the British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference.\n</p><p>Meetings of the Council take the form of "regular and frequent" sectoral meetings between ministers from the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a>. Plenary meetings, attended by all ministers and led by the <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_and_deputy_First_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="First Minister and deputy First Minister">First Minister and deputy First Minister</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>, take place twice a year. Institutional and cross-sectoral meetings, including matters in relation to the EU or to resolved disagreements, happen "in an appropriate format" on a <i>ad hoc</i> basis. The Council has a permanent office located in <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, staffed by a standing secretariat.\n</p><p>There is no joint parliamentary forum for the island of Ireland. However, under the Good Friday Agreement, the Oireachtas and Northern Ireland Assembly are asked to consider developing one. The Agreement also contains a suggestion for the creation of a consultative forum composed of members of <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil society</a> from Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland. Under the 2007, <a href="/wiki/St._Andrew%27s_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Andrew&#39;s Agreement">St. Andrew\'s Agreement</a>, the Northern Ireland Executive agreed to support the establishment of a North/South Consultative Forum and to encourage parties in the Northern Ireland Assembly to support the creation of a North/South parliamentary forum.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Independent of the direct involvement of Government of the United Kingdom, the devolved administrations of the mainland United Kingdom and the Crown dependencies also have relationships and with Ireland.\n</p><p>For example, the Irish and Welsh governments collaborate on various economic development projects through the Ireland Wales Programme, under the <a href="/wiki/Interreg" title="Interreg">Interreg</a> initiative of the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The the <a href="/wiki/Special_EU_Programmes_Body" title="Special EU Programmes Body">Special EU Programmes Body</a>, set up under the Good Friday Agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="/wiki/File:Schengen_Area_Labelled_Map.svg" class="image"><img alt="" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Schengen_Area_Labelled_Map.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #336633; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;The <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a></div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; (EU)</div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #6666CC; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Schengen Area (non-EU)</div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #CCCC00; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;States due to join the Schengen Area</div></div></div></div>\n<p>Ireland and the United Kingdom are the only parts of the European Union not obliged to join the <a href="/wiki/Schengen_Area" title="Schengen Area">Schengen free-travel area</a>. The Crown Dependencies, which are outside of the EU, are not members either. Instead, a <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a> exists between the two states and the Crown Dependencies. The Common Travel Areas is not founded on any formal agreement between Ireland and the United Kingdom and is not provided for in legislation. Instead, it is an informal arrangement between the states. When the Schengen Area was incorporated into the European Union through the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam Treaty">Amsterdam Treaty</a>, the first formal recognition of the Common Travel areas was made though an annexed <a href="/wiki/Protocol_(treaty)" class="mw-redirect" title="Protocol (treaty)">protocol</a> exempting their obligations to join.\n</p><p>The UK\'s reluctance to join the Schengen Area, through concerns over loss of independent border controls, is usually cited as the reason for not joining. Britain argued that, for an island, frontier controls are a better and less intrusive way to prevent illegal immigration than other measures, such as identity cards, residence permits, and registration with the police. Consequent difficulties for Ireland, given its location and shared border with the United Kingdom (at which border points would have to be set up), would then make it very difficult for Ireland to join without the United Kingdom.\n</p><p>Except for a period during and in the years after World War II, neither Ireland nor the UK have placed restrictions on travel between each other for citizens resident in each others states since Irish independence. Even during wartime, when Ireland remained neutral and the United Kingdom was a belligerent during World War II, the only significant restrictions on travel between the states were an Irish prohibition on the wearing of military uniforms by British citizens when in Irish territory and the instatement of passport controls between Great Britain and the island of Ireland. When Ireland suddenly declared itself a republic in 1949, thus making it impossible to remain in the <a href="/wiki/British_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="British Commonwealth">British Commonwealth</a>, the UK government <a href="/wiki/Ireland_Act_1949" title="Ireland Act 1949">legislated that</a> even though the Republic of Ireland was no longer a <a href="/wiki/British_dominion" class="mw-redirect" title="British dominion">British dominion</a>, it would not be treated as a foreign country for the purposes of British law.\n</p><p>Prior to post-World War II, both states mutually recognised each other\'s entry visas for foreigners. However, in 1952 changes to UK law rescinded this arrangement. In 2011, the first public agreement between the British and Irish governments concerning the maintenance of the Common Travel Area was published.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The agreement, which is non-binding, envisions increased co-ordination between Irish and British immigration arrangements and that, from July 2011 Ireland would recognise UK short terms visas on an 18-month pilot basis for nationals of 16 countries. The agreement also mooted the possibility of "Common Travel Area visit visa\xe2\x80\x9d including the possibility of a pilot project.\n</p><p>There are no special arrangements for travel between the Common Travel Area and the Schengen Area and a <a href="/wiki/Schengen_visa" class="mw-redirect" title="Schengen visa">Schengen visa</a> entitle entry. However, citizens of the European Union, Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein and Switzerland <a href="/wiki/Visa_policy_in_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Visa policy in the European Union">may enter as a right</a> using only their passports.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Citizenship_and_citizens_rights">Citizenship and citizens rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The Irish Free State also issued passports from 1924 onwards.</div></div></div>\n<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">dominion</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, citizens of the Irish Free State were regarded as <a href="/wiki/British_subjects" class="mw-redirect" title="British subjects">British subjects</a> in common with all other members of the Empire. Historically, as late as 1942, British jurisprudence was that Irish citizenship "did no more than confer ... a national character as an Irish citizen within the wider British nationality". Indeed, for some years, the British authorities refused to accept Irish passports.<sup id="cite_ref-Documentsin_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Documentsin-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Starting from the basis of common citizenship, the two states to this day provide reciprocal recognition to each others\' citizens. British and Irish citizens can avail themselves of public services (for example, health care and <a href="/wiki/Social_welfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Social welfare">social welfare</a>) in each other\'s jurisdictions on an equal basis and are entitled to the right of abode, with deportation only in the most exceptional of circumstances. They each have equal voting (and standing) rights in all elections held across the United Kingdom and Ireland (except for the election of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> and referenda).\n</p><p>Northern Ireland occupies a unique location in the citizenship of the islands, with Northern Ireland people being recognised under the Good Friday Agreement as (in general terms) simultaneously British and/or Irish citizens according to their choice.\n</p><p>Both the United Kingdom and Ireland are members of the European Union. Thus, citizens of Ireland and the United Kingdom are automatically <a href="/wiki/Citizens_of_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizens of the European Union">citizens of the European Union</a> and enjoy the associated benefits. Channel Islanders and Manx people are British citizens and hence European citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> However, they are not entitled to take advantage of the freedom of movement of people or services unless they are directly connected (through birth, descent from a parent or grandparent, or five years\' residence) with the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The interaction of overlapping citizens rights and laws has led to some cases of exploitation of loopholes to avoid the intention of the law. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-seventh_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Twenty-seventh Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">Twenty-seventh Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland</a> was required to amend the potential for abuse of the rights of Irish citizenship to the right to abode in the United Kingdom. Before then, Irish citizenship was granted on the basis of <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_soli" title="Jus soli">jus soli</a></i> (i.e. being born on the island of Ireland was reason in itself to be considered an Irish citizen). <a href="/wiki/Chen_case" class="mw-redirect" title="Chen case">In one case</a>, a Chinese migrant to the UK, living in Wales on Great Britain, chose to give birth to a child in Northern Ireland, on the island of Ireland, to take advantage of Irish citizenship laws. Consequently, her child was born an Irish citizen by virtue of <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_soli" title="Jus soli">jus soli</a></i> and so was entitled to permanent abode in the UK even though the mother did not have the right to visit the Republic of Ireland. The mother and father then claimed the right to stay in the United Kingdom by virtue of being the guardians of a citizen of the European Union who would be unable to look after itself should they be deported.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. factor in the politics and labour markets of the Isles. Irish people have been the largest ethnic minority group in Britain for centuries, regularly migrating across the Irish Sea. From the earliest recorded history to the present, there has been a continuous movement of people between the islands of Ireland and Great Britain due to their proximity. This tide has ebbed and flowed in response to politics, economics and social conditions of both places. At the 2011 census, there were 869,000 Irish-born residents in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lions_vs_New_Zealand_Maori.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Lions_vs_New_Zealand_Maori.jpg/220px-Lions_vs_New_Zealand_Maori.jpg" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lions_vs_New_Zealand_Maori.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/British_and_Irish_Lions" title="British and class="mw-redirect" title="New Zealand M\xc4\x81ori rugby union team">New Zealand Maoris</a> (black) in 2005</div></div></div>\n<p>The United Kingdom and Ireland have separate media, although British television, newspapers and magazines are widely available in Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> giving people in Ireland a high level of familiarity with cultural matters in the United Kingdom. Republic of Ireland newspapers and magazines are commonly available in Northern Ireland, and the two main Irish broadsheets, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Times" title="The Irish Times">The Irish Times</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Irish_Independent" title="Irish Independent">Irish Independent</a></i> are frequently available to the diaspora in Great Britain. Certain reality TV shows have embraced the whole of the islands, for example <i><a href="/wiki/The_X_Factor_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="The X Factor (UK)">The X Factor</a></i>, seasons 3, 4 and 7 of which featured auditions in Dublin, were open to voters in the Republic,  while the show previously known as <i>Britain\'s Next Top Model</i> became <i><a href="/wiki/Britain_and_Ireland%27s_Next_Top_Model" class="mw-redirect" title="Britain and Ireland&#39;s Next Top Model">Britain and Ireland\'s Next Top Model</a></i> in 2011.\n</p><p>Ireland and the United Kingdom have agreed to a deal on the digital broadcast of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Northern_Ireland" title="BBC Northern Ireland">BBC Northern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> into the Republic of Ireland and of <a href="/wiki/RT%C3%89" class="mw-redirect" title="RT\xc3\x89">RT\xc3\x89</a> and <a href="/wiki/TG4" title="TG4">TG4</a> into Northern Ireland. <a href="/wiki/Tara_Television" title="Tara Television">Tara Television</a>, which broadcast Irish programming into Great Britain, was wound up in 2002. A replacement, under the working title of <i><a href="/wiki/RT%C3%89_International" class="mw-redirect" title="RT\xc3\x89 International">RT\xc3\x89 International</a></i>, has been postponed due to financial conditions. Instead, <a href="/wiki/RT%C3%89_Player" title="RT\xc3\x89 Player">RT\xc3\x89 Player</a> provides a subset of programming for audiences outside Ireland.\n</p><p>Some cultural events are organised for the island group as a whole. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Costa_Book_Awards" title="Costa Book Awards">Costa Book Awards</a> are awarded to authors resident in the UK or Ireland. The <a href="/wiki/Man_Booker_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Man Booker Prize">Man Booker Prize</a> is awarded to authors from the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> and Ireland. The <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Music_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury Music Prize">Mercury Music Prize</a> is handed out every year to the best album from an Irish or British musician or group.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_and_Irish_Lions" title="British and Irish Lions">British and Irish Lions</a> is a team made up of players from <a href="/wiki/England_national_rugby_union_team" title="England national rugby union team">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland_national_rugby_union_team" title="Ireland national rugby union team">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland_national_rugby_union_team" title="Scotland national rugby union team">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales_national_rugby_union_team" title="Wales national rugby union team">Wales</a> that undertakes tours of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a> rugby playing nations every four years. The <a href="/wiki/Ryder_Cup" title="Ryder Cup">Ryder Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> was originally played between a United States team and a team representing Great Britain and Ireland. From 1979 onwards this was expanded to include the whole of Europe.\n</p><p>In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_torch" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympic torch">Olympic torch</a> visited Dublin on <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics_torch_relay" title="2012 Summer Olympics torch relay">a tour of the UK</a> ahead of the <a href="/wiki/2012_Olympic_Games" class="mw-redirect" title="2012 Olympic Games">2012 Olympic Games</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. Dublin was the only place outside of the UK (apart from the traditional lighting ceremony in Greece) that the torch visited.  UK <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Culture,_Olympics,_Media_and_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Culture, Olympics, Media and Sport">Secretary of State for Culture, Olympics, Media and Sport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Hunt_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeremy Hunt (politician)">Jeremy Hunt</a>, said: "The Republic of Ireland is the only country outside the UK to be visited by the torch and rightly so, given the unique and deep ties between Ireland and the UK."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many of the countries and regions of the isles, especially <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> share a common <a href="/wiki/Celts_(modern)" title="Celts (modern)">Celtic</a> heritage, and all of these countries have branches of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_League_(political_organisation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic League (political organisation)">Celtic league</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_ties">Economic ties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Economic ties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small><i>(December 2014)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Due to the linguistic, cultural and legal (both as <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> countries) similarities between the UK and Ireland, many businesses in both countries have operations in each other country. Both countries have each other as their biggest business partner, and both in the same trade organisations include the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Trade Organisation">World Trade Organisation</a>.\n</p><p>Examples of notable British companies that have an Irish operations come as diverse as the supermarket chain <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tesco_Ireland" title="Tesco Ireland">Tesco Ireland</a>), the department store <a href="/wiki/Marks_%26_Spencer" title="Marks &amp; Spencer">Marks &amp; Spencer</a>, the commercial bank <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_Of_Scotland_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Bank Of Scotland Group">RBS</a> which trades as <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Bank" title="Ulster Bank">Ulster Bank</a>, telecoms company <a href="/wiki/BT_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="BT plc">BT</a> (<a href="/wiki/BT_Ireland" title="BT Ireland">BT Ireland</a>) and electric company <a href="/wiki/SSE_plc" title="SSE plc">SSE plc</a> (<a href="/wiki/Airtricity" title="Airtricity">Airtricity</a>). Notable Irish companies that work in the UK includes the airline <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>, fashion retailer <a href="/wiki/Primark" title="Primark">Primark</a> (founded in Dublin but now owned by <a href="/wiki/Associated_British_Foods" title="Associated British Foods">Associated British Foods</a>), food processor <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Group" title="Kerry Group">Kerry Group</a> and electric management company <a href="/wiki/ESB_Group" title="ESB Group">ESB</a>.\n</p><p>Due to the closeness, some businesses often treat both countries of trade, finance and marketing as a single unit as quoting for \xe2\x80\x9cUK and Ireland\xe2\x80\x9d rather than two separate countries.\n</p><p>However despite the closeness of the countries, the UK has criticised Ireland\'s low rate of <a href="/wiki/Corporation_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporation tax">corporation tax</a> of 12.5% against the British level of 22% as a giving Ireland a competitive advantage against the UK.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
