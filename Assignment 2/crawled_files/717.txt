(original)</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Psalm 134</b> is the 134th <a href="/wiki/Psalm" class="mw-redirect" title="Psalm">psalm</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Psalms" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Psalms">Book of Psalms</a>, generally known in English by its first verse in the <a href="/wiki/King_James_Version" title="King James Version">King James Version</a>, "Behold, bless ye the LORD, all ye servants of the LORD". The Book of Psalms is part of the <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanakh">Hebrew Bible</a> and the Christian <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>. It is <b>Psalm 133</b> in the slightly different numbering system of the Greek <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> and Latin <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> versions of the Bible. Its Latin title is "<b>Ecce nunc benedicite Dominum</b>".<sup id="cite_ref-Mediavalist_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mediavalist-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is the last of the fifteen <a href="/wiki/Song_of_Ascents" title="Song of Ascents">Songs of Ascents</a> (<i>Shir Hama\'alot</i>), and one of the three Songs of Ascents consisting of only three verses.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The psalm is a regular part of <a href="/wiki/Jewish_history" title="Jewish history">Jewish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglican</a> and Protestant liturgies. It has been set to music often and paraphrased in hymns. The short psalm is part of the daily Catholic service <a href="/wiki/Compline" title="Compline">Compline</a>, for which settings in Latin were composed by composers such as <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Luis_de_Victoria" title="Tom\xc3\xa1s Luis de Victoria">Tom\xc3\xa1s Luis de Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orlande_de_Lassus" title="Orlande de Lassus">Orlande de Lassus</a>. It is frequently used in <a href="/wiki/Evening_Prayer_(Anglican)" title="Evening Prayer (Anglican)">Anglican Evening Prayer</a>, with settings by <a href="/wiki/John_Dowland" title="John Dowland">John Dowland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Rogers_(musician)" title="Benjamin Rogers (musician)">Benjamin Rogers</a>, among others.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Psalm_134&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: King James Version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ol><li>Behold, bless ye class="smallcaps" style="font-size:83%">ORD</span> that made heaven and earth bless thee out of Zion.</li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">Interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psalm_134&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_King_James_Version" title="New King James Version">New King James Version</a> entitles this psalm "Praising the Lord in His House at Night".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nonconformist" title="Nonconformist">Nonconformist</a> minister <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Henry" title="Matthew Henry">Matthew Henry</a> notes that, as the last of the Songs of Ascents, this psalm serves as a fitting conclusion to the singing of all the Songs of Ascents in the <a href="/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem" title="Temple in Jerusalem">Temple in Jerusalem</a> which took place by day, as it exhorts "the ministers to go on with their work in the night, when the solemnities of the day were over". The psalm could also be interpreted as a "dialogue", as the <a href="/wiki/Kohen" title="Kohen">priests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Levites" class="mw-redirect" title="Levites">Levites</a> who served in the Temple are enjoined in verses 1 and 2 to spend their time during the night watch in acts of devotion rather than small talk; and in verse 3 these devotees are urged to pray for the one who enjoined them in verse 1 \xe2\x80\x93 either the <a href="/wiki/High_Priest_of_Israel" title="High Priest of Israel">High Priest</a> or a captain of the night guard.<sup id="cite_ref-henry_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-henry-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A note in the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Bible" title="Jerusalem Bible">Jerusalem Bible</a> suggests that the dialogue involves pilgrims and temple ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> preacher <a href="/wiki/Charles_Spurgeon" title="Charles Spurgeon">Charles Spurgeon</a> posits that verse 1 was recited by the festival pilgrims leaving the temple in the predawn darkness; seeing the guards with their lamps on the temple wall, they bid farewell to these loyal caretakers of the sanctuary. In return, the priests call out their own blessing for the departing pilgrims in verse 3. Spurgeon extrapolates from this the need for congregants to pray for those who minister to them, and for <a href="/wiki/Minister_(Christianity)" title="Minister (Christianity)">ministers</a> to pronounce blessings on their congregations.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Midrash_Tehillim" title="Midrash Tehillim">Midrash Tehillim</a> connects the contents of this psalm to several Jewish practices. <a href="/wiki/Johanan_bar_Nappaha" title="Johanan bar Nappaha">Rabbi Yochanan</a> says that "servants of the Lord who stand in the house of the Lord at night" mentioned in verse 1 refers to those who engage in nighttime <a href="/wiki/Torah_study" title="Torah study">Torah study</a>, which God considers in the same light "as if they occupied themselves with the priest\'s service in the house of the Lord". The midrash connects the lifting of the hands in preparation for blessing the Lord in verse 2 with the practice of lifting up the cup of wine with both hands for the recital of the <a href="/wiki/Birkat_Hamazon" title="Birkat Hamazon">Birkat Hamazon</a> (Grace after Meals). The midrash further connects this verse to the <a href="/wiki/Priestly_Blessing" title="Priestly Blessing">Priestly Blessing</a>, as Rabbi Simeon ben Pazzi says that a <a href="/wiki/Kohen" title="Kohen">Kohen</a> who has not <a href="/wiki/Ritual_washing_in_Judaism#Before_the_Priestly_Blessing" title="Ritual washing in Judaism">ritually washed his hands</a> may not lift them to invoke the Priestly Blessing.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Zohar" title="Zohar">Zohar</a> also explains verse 2 as referring to the <a href="/wiki/Kohen" title="Kohen">kohanim</a> (members of the Jewish priestly class) who bestow the Priestly Blessing upon the congregation in the <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> with raised hands. Before pronouncing the blessing, the kohanim must ritually wash their hands. They do not do so themselves; rather, the handwashing is performed by members of the <a href="/wiki/Levite" title="Levite">levitical class</a>, "who themselves are holy". If a Levite is not present in the synagogue, a <a href="/wiki/Firstborn_(Judaism)" title="Firstborn (Judaism)">firstborn son</a> pours the water, since he too is called "holy".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psalm_134&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psalm_134&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Psalm 134 is recited following the <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a> <a href="/wiki/Mincha" title="Mincha">afternoon prayer</a> between <a href="/wiki/Sukkot" title="Sukkot">Sukkot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shabbat_Hagadol" class="mw-redirect" title="Shabbat Hagadol">Shabbat Hagadol</a> (the Shabbat before <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-ency_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ency-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the <i>Siddur Avodas Yisrael</i>, the entire psalm is recited before the <a href="/wiki/Maariv" title="Maariv">evening prayer</a> on weekdays.<sup id="cite_ref-shim_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shim-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The psalm is also recited in full before engaging in <a href="/wiki/Torah_study" title="Torah study">Torah study</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Verses 1 and 2 are part of <a href="/wiki/Selichot" title="Selichot">Selichot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shim_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shim-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Ritual_washing_in_Judaism" title="Ritual washing in Judaism">ritual washing of the hands</a> before breaking bread, some say verse 2 prior to the blessing of <i>al netilat yadayim</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholic">Catholic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psalm_134&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Catholic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The psalm, mentioning "night", forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Benedictine</a> rite of the daily evening prayer <a href="/wiki/Compline" title="Compline">Compline</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_settings">Musical settings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psalm_134&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Musical settings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Among the hymns which are based on Psalm 134 is "Come, all you servants of the Lord", which <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arlo_D._Duba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arlo D. Duba (page does not exist)">Arlo D. Duba</a> wrote in 1984 to the melody <a href="/wiki/Old_Hundredth" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Hundredth">Old Hundredth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duba_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duba-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Luis_de_Victoria" title="Tom\xc3\xa1s Luis de Victoria">Tom\xc3\xa1s Luis de Victoria</a> set the psalm in Latin, <i>Ecce nunc benedicite</i>, for double choir.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Flemish composer <a href="/wiki/Orlande_de_Lassus" title="Orlande de Lassus">Orlande de Lassus</a> wrote the motet <i>Ecce nunc benedicite Dominum</i> for seven voices <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a>, using a wide range from low bass to very high soprano.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Dowland" title="John Dowland">John Dowland</a> supplied a setting in English, "Behold and have regard", to the collection <i>The Whole Booke of Psalmes</i> with works by ten composers, published in 1592 by Thomas Este.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Rogers_(musician)" title="Benjamin Rogers (musician)">Benjamin Rogers</a> set the version in the English <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>, <i>Behold, now praise the Lord</i>, for choir a cappella in the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Malcolm Hill composed a setting in English for mixed choir and organ in 1996, titled <i>Meditation on Psalm 134</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
