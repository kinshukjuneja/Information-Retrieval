5.480\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">50.211; -5.480</span></span></span></a></span></span></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>St Ives</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Porth Ia</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> meaning "St Ia\'s cove") is a seaside <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> and port in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. The town lies north of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> and west of <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a> on the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Sea" title="Celtic Sea">Celtic Sea</a>. In former times it was commercially dependent on fishing. The decline in fishing, however, caused a shift in commercial emphasis, and the town is now primarily a popular <a href="/wiki/Seaside_resort" title="Seaside resort">seaside resort</a>, notably achieving the title of Best UK Seaside Town from the British Travel Awards in both 2010 and 2011. St Ives was incorporated by Royal Charter in 1639. St Ives has become renowned for its number of artists. It was named best seaside town of 2007 by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> newspaper. It should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/St_Ive" title="St Ive">St Ive</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">village</a> and civil parish in south-east Cornwall.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Payne_memorial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/John_Payne_memorial.jpg/170px-John_Payne_memorial.jpg" width="170" height="249" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/John_Payne_memorial.jpg/255px-John_Payne_memorial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/John_Payne_memorial.jpg/340px-John_Payne_memorial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1897" data-file-height="2783" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Payne_memorial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Payne memorial, St Ives</div></div></div>\n<p>The origin of St Ives is attributed in legend to the arrival of the Irish saint <a href="/wiki/Ia_of_Cornwall" title="Ia of Cornwall">Ia of Cornwall</a>, in the 5th century. The parish church bears her name, and St Ives derives from it.<sup id="cite_ref-tde_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tde-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rocky_landscape.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Rocky_landscape.JPG/220px-Rocky_landscape.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Rocky_landscape.JPG/330px-Rocky_landscape.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Rocky_landscape.JPG/440px-Rocky_landscape.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3280" data-file-height="2460" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rocky_landscape.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rocky landscape</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/The_Sloop_Inn" title="The Sloop Inn">The Sloop Inn</a>, which lies on the wharf was a fisherman\'s pub for many centuries and is dated to "circa 1312", making it one of the oldest inns in Cornwall. The town was the site of a particularly notable atrocity during the <a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a> of 1549. The English <a href="/wiki/Provost_marshal" title="Provost marshal">provost marshal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Kingston" title="Anthony Kingston">Anthony Kingston</a>, came to St Ives and invited the <a href="/wiki/Portreeve" title="Portreeve">portreeve</a>, John Payne, to lunch at an inn. He asked the portreeve to have the <a href="/wiki/Gallows" title="Gallows">gallows</a> erected during the course of the lunch. Afterwards the portreeve and the Provost Marshal walked down to the gallows; the Provost Marshal then ordered the portreeve to mount the gallows. The portreeve was then <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanged</a> for being a "busy rebel".\n</p><p>The seal of St Ives is <i>Argent, an ivy branch overspreading the whole field Vert</i>, with the legend <i>Sigillum Burgi St. Ives in Com. Cornub. 1690</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Spanish_Armada" title="3rd Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada of 1597</a> two Spanish ships, a <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">bark</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Pinnace_(ship%27s_boat)" title="Pinnace (ship&#39;s boat)">pinnace</a> had made their way to St Ives to seek shelter from the storm which had dispersed the Spanish fleet. They were captured by the English warship <a href="/wiki/English_ship_Warspite_(1596)" title="English ship Warspite (1596)"><i>Warspite</i></a> of Sir <a href="/wiki/Walter_Raleigh" title="Walter Raleigh">Walter Raleigh</a> leaking from the same storm. The information given by the prisoners was vital on learning the Armada\'s objectives.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing">Fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St._Ives,_Porthminster_Bay,_Cornwall,_ca._1895.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/St._Ives%2C_Porthminster_Bay%2C_Cornwall%2C_ca._1895.jpg/220px-St._Ives%2C_Porthminster_Bay%2C_Cornwall%2C_ca._1895.jpg" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/St._Ives%2C_Porthminster_Bay%2C_Cornwall%2C_ca._1895.jpg/330px-St._Ives%2C_Porthminster_Bay%2C_Cornwall%2C_ca._1895.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/St._Ives%2C_Porthminster_Bay%2C_Cornwall%2C_ca._1895.jpg/440px-St._Ives%2C_Porthminster_Bay%2C_Cornwall%2C_ca._1895.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3514" data-file-height="2586" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St._Ives,_Porthminster_Bay,_Cornwall,_ca._1895.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Photochrom" title="Photochrom">Photochrom</a> of St Ives, 1895</div></div></div>\n<p>From medieval times fishing was important at St Ives; it was the most important fishing port on the north coast. The pier was built by <a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a> in 1767\xe2\x80\x9370 but has been lengthened at a later date. The octagonal lookout with a cupola belongs to Smeaton\'s design.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the decade 1747\xe2\x80\x931756 the total number of pilchards dispatched from the four principal Cornish ports of <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fowey" title="Fowey">Fowey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>, and St Ives averaged 30,000 hogsheads annually (making a total of 900 million fish). Much greater catches were achieved in 1790 and 1796. In 1847 the exports of pilchards from Cornwall amounted to 40,883 hogsheads or 122 million fish while the greatest number ever taken in one seine was 5,600 hogsheads at St Ives in 1868.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/A._K._Hamilton_Jenkin" title="A. K. Hamilton Jenkin">A. K. Hamilton Jenkin</a> describes how the St Ives fisherman strictly observed Sunday as a day of rest. St Ives was a very busy fishing port and seining was the usual method of fishing. Seining was carried out by a set of three boats of different sizes, the largest two carrying seine nets of different sizes. The total number of crew was seventeen or eighteen. However this came to an end in 1924. The bulk of the catch was exported to Italy: for example, in 1830, 6400 hogsheads were sent to Mediterranean ports. From 1829 to 1838, the yearly average for this trade was 9000 hogsheads.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While commercial fishing is much reduced, the harbour is still in use, often as well for recreational boating, tourist fishing and day trips to the nearby seal colonies on the Carrack Rocks and other locations along the coast. Recently, a class of Victorian fishing boat unique to St. Ives, known as a "jumbo," has been replicated by boatbuilder Jonny Nance to celebrate the town\'s maritime heritage. Today\'s jumbos are operated by the St. Ives Jumbo Association.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifeboat">Lifeboat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lifeboat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station" title="St Ives Lifeboat Station">St Ives Lifeboat Station</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg/220px-St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg/330px-St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg/440px-St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Ives_Lifeboat_Station_viewed_across_the_harbour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lifeboat station in the harbour</div></div></div>\n<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboat</a> was stationed in the town in 1840. In 1867 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution" title="Royal National Lifeboat Institution">Royal National Lifeboat Institution</a> built a boathouse at Porthgwidden beach. It proved to be a difficult site to launch from and in 1867 it was replaced by a building in Fore Street. In 1911 a new boathouse was built on the Quay, and then in 1993 a larger station was built at the landward end of the West Pier.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Seven crewmen died in the St Ives lifeboat tragedy of 1939. In the early hours of 23 January 1939 there was a <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Force 10</a> storm blowing with gusts up to 100 miles per hour (160&#160;km/h). The lifeboat <i>John and Sara Eliza Stych</i> was launched at 3 o\'clock to search for a ship reported in trouble off <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cornwall" title="Cape Cornwall">Cape Cornwall</a>. It rounded The island where it met the full force of the storm as it headed westwards. It capsized three times and drifted across St Ives Bay when its propeller was fouled. The first time it turned over four men were lost; the second time one more; the third time left only one man alive. He scrambled ashore when the boat was wrecked on rocks near <a href="/wiki/Godrevy" title="Godrevy">Godrevy</a> Point.<sup id="cite_ref-Bray_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bray-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_(2011)_by_Walter_Scott_(1974-_).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0b/St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_%282011%29_by_Walter_Scott_%281974-_%29.jpg/220px-St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_%282011%29_by_Walter_Scott_%281974-_%29.jpg" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0b/St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_%282011%29_by_Walter_Scott_%281974-_%29.jpg/330px-St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_%282011%29_by_Walter_Scott_%281974-_%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0b/St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_%282011%29_by_Walter_Scott_%281974-_%29.jpg/440px-St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_%282011%29_by_Walter_Scott_%281974-_%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3487" data-file-height="2740" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Ives_Harbour_Beach_(2011)_by_Walter_Scott_(1974-_).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St Ives Harbour Beach (2011) by local artist Walter Scott (1974- )</div></div></div>\n<p>The modern seaside resort developed as a result of the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_Bay_Line" title="St Ives Bay Line">St Ives Bay branch line</a> from <a href="/wiki/St_Erth" title="St Erth">St Erth</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> in 1877. With it came a new generation of Victorian seaside holidaymakers. Much of the town was built during the latter part of the 19th century. The railway, which winds along the cliffs and bays, survived the <a href="/wiki/Beeching_cuts" title="Beeching cuts">Beeching cuts</a> and has become a tourist attraction itself.\n</p><p>In 1952, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warship</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Wave" class="mw-redirect" title="HMS Wave">HMS Wave</a> ran aground near the town.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The ship was later salvaged, repaired and returned to service.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propeller</a> believed to be from HMS Wave was washed ashore in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1999, the town was the first landfall of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_11_August_1999" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar eclipse of 11 August 1999">Solar eclipse of 11 August 1999</a>. A live BBC programme with the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Moore" title="Patrick Moore">Patrick Moore</a> was clouded out and the eclipse was missed.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sharks">Sharks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sharks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On 28 July 2007 there was a suspected sighting of a <a href="/wiki/Great_white_shark" title="Great white shark">great white shark</a>. The chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Shark_Trust" title="Shark Trust">Shark Trust</a> said that "it was impossible to make a conclusive identification and that it could have also been either a <a href="/wiki/Shortfin_mako_shark" title="Shortfin mako shark">Mako</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Porbeagle" title="Porbeagle">Porbeagle</a> shark". Coastguards dismissed the claims as "scaremongering".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> On 14 June 2011 there was a suspected sighting of an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_whitetip_shark" title="Oceanic whitetip shark">oceanic whitetip shark</a>, after a boat was reportedly attacked.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The Shark Trust said that the chances of the species being in British waters were "very small".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> On 18 July 2017 a suspected blue shark was spotted close to the harbour.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> On 16 July 2018, another blue shark was spotted in the harbour, prompting the Shark Trust to ask people to stay out of the water.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg/220px-Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg/330px-Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg/440px-Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Porthgwidden_Beach_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1263150.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Porthgwidden Beach</div></div></div>\n<p>St Ives is situated on the western shore of <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_Bay" title="St Ives Bay">St Ives Bay</a>, its harbour sheltered by St Ives Island (a headland) and Smeaton\'s pier. Close to the harbour, its streets are narrow and uneven while its wider streets are in the newer parts of the town on rising ground.<sup id="cite_ref-tde_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tde-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The town has four beaches: Porthmeor a surfing beach, Porthgwidden a small sandy cove, Harbour by the working port and Porthminster which has almost half a mile of sand.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> St Ives has an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> and has some of the mildest winters and warmest summers in Britain and Northern Europe. It is therefore a popular tourist resort in the summer, and also benefits from an amount of sunshine per year that is above the national average.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/South_West_Coast_Path" title="South West Coast Path">South West Coast Path</a> passes through the town.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_administration">Politics and administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics and administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>St Ives\' local government administration has a two-tier structure consisting of two separate councils both of which are democratically elected statutory bodies which St Ives Town Council and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a>.\n</p><p>The first tier of local government in St Ives is the Town Council which is the closest tier to the general public with a membership of 16 elected town councillors from three wards. The last elections were held in May 2013. The council has responsibilities for providing grant funding to local organisations, public footpaths, bus shelters, beach patrols, traffic control and allotments. The Council is a statutory body which is consulted regarding planning decisions in the town\xe2\x80\x99s area and makes recommendations to the planning authority, Cornwall Council.\n</p><p>Before 1974, St Ives Borough Council was the principal local authority for what now forms the civil parish of St Ives. Since the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">reform of local government in 1974</a>, St Ives has an elected town council. The area overseen by the <a href="/wiki/Town_council" title="Town council">Town Council</a> of St Ives includes <a href="/wiki/Lelant" title="Lelant">Lelant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbis_Bay" title="Carbis Bay">Carbis Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halsetown" title="Halsetown">Halsetown</a> and St Ives.  The elected town council members also become charter trustees of the original borough charter (for the duration of their term in office) which dates back to 1639 entitling them to carry out various ceremonial functions such as appointing a Mayor, awarding <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_Borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom of the Borough">freedom of the borough</a> to individuals, representing the Borough at formal occasions such as <a href="/wiki/Remembrance_Sunday" title="Remembrance Sunday">Remembrance Sunday</a> wearing <a href="/wiki/Robes" class="mw-redirect" title="Robes">formal ceremonial robes</a> and using the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">Coat of Arms</a>.  Typically, the Mayor of the Town of St Ives is also the Mayor of the Borough of St Ives.  However, most of the other principal local authority functions for St Ives were undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Penwith_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Penwith District Council">Penwith District Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_County_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornwall County Council">Cornwall County Council</a>. From 1 April 2009 Penwith and the other five Cornish district councils were replaced by a unified council, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a>.\n</p><p>Like St Ives Town Council, Cornwall Council is a statutory body incorporated by Act of Parliament. Cornwall Council is the second tier of local government in the area and is a unitary authority with a far wider range of powers. The Council deals with roads, street lighting, highways, social services, children and family care, schools and public libraries. St Ives itself elects three of the 123 councillors.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Catholic_Church_of_The_Sacred_Heart,_St_Ives_-_geograph.org.uk_-_317565.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Church of the Sacred Heart (R.C.)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/St._Ia%27s_Church,_St._Ives" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Ia&#39;s Church, St. Ives">parish church</a> is dedicated to Saint <a href="/wiki/Ia_of_Cornwall" title="Ia of Cornwall">Ia of Cornwall</a>, an Irish holy woman of the 5th or 6th century, and <a href="/wiki/St_Andrew" class="mw-redirect" title="St Andrew">St Andrew</a>, the patron saint of fishermen.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 1408 the townsmen attempted to get a papal bull to authorise the consecration of their church and cemetery, but they did not achieve this, so they continued without the rights of baptism or burial. However, they undertook the building of the present church between 1410 and 1434 as a chapel of ease, St Ives being within the parish of <a href="/wiki/Lelant" title="Lelant">Lelant</a>. They were able to obtain the right to a font in 1428 but consecration of the cemetery only in 1542. For over a century the vicars of Lelant had resisted demands from the inhabitants of St Ives and Towednack for the right of sepulture but in 1542 the right was granted so the vicars transferred their residence to St Ives and abandoned the vicarage of Lelant.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> There was damage to the church in 1697 when a storm broke through the sea-wall and damaged the roof and a large window over the altar.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>There are chapels dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas" title="Saint Nicholas">St Nicholas</a> on the headland of St Ives Island and <a href="/wiki/Leonard_of_Noblac" title="Leonard of Noblac">St Leonard</a> on the quay which were used by the fishermen and have been converted for other uses.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The former chapel of St Nicholas was partially demolished by the <a href="/wiki/War_Office" title="War Office">War Office</a> in 1904<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> but rebuilt in 1909, possibly by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Harold_Sedding" title="Edmund Harold Sedding">E H Sedding</a>, from the old materials. It is plain and rectangular and has since been converted into the New Gallery.\n</p><p>The Roman Catholic Church of the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Heart" title="Sacred Heart">Sacred Heart</a> and St Ia was built in 1909 to a design by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Scoles" title="Alexander Scoles">A J C Scoles</a>. There are also two Methodist chapels, one in <a href="/wiki/Fore_Street" title="Fore Street">Fore Street</a> of 1831, and another of 1845 higher up the valley, and a Congregational chapel of 1800.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Bernard_Leach" title="Bernard Leach">Bernard Leach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dji_Hamada" title="Sh\xc5\x8dji Hamada">Sh\xc5\x8dji Hamada</a> set up the <a href="/wiki/Leach_Pottery" title="Leach Pottery">Leach Pottery</a> in 1920. Leach was a <a href="/wiki/Studio_potter" class="mw-redirect" title="Studio potter">studio potter</a> and art teacher,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and is known as the "Father of British studio pottery",<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> learned pottery under the direction of Shigekichi Urano (Kenzan VI) in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> where he also met Sh\xc5\x8dji Hamada. They promoted pottery from the point of view of Western and Eastern arts and philosophies. Leach produced work until 1972, and the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a> held an exhibition of his work in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The Leach Pottery remains operational and houses a small museum showcasing work by Leach and his students.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1928, the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wallis" title="Alfred Wallis">Alfred Wallis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nicholson" title="Ben Nicholson">Ben Nicholson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wood_(English_painter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christopher Wood (English painter)">Christopher Wood</a> met at St Ives and laid the foundation for the <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_School" title="St Ives School">artists\' colony</a>. In 1939, Ben Nicholson, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth" title="Barbara Hepworth">Barbara Hepworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naum_Gabo" title="Naum Gabo">Naum Gabo</a> settled in St Ives, attracted by its beauty. In 1993, a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tate_St_Ives" title="Tate St Ives">Tate St Ives</a>, opened. The Tate looks after the <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth_Museum" title="Barbara Hepworth Museum">Barbara Hepworth Museum</a> and her <a href="/wiki/Sculpture_garden" title="Sculpture garden">sculpture garden</a>. The town attracted artists from overseas, such as <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Sumray" title="Maurice Sumray">Maurice Sumray</a>, who moved from <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 1968,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and continues to do so today with younger artists such as Michael Polat, who took up residence there from his native Germany in 1999.\n</p><p>Before the 1940s, most artists in St Ives and West Cornwall belonged to the St Ives Society of Artists; but events in the late 1940s led to a dispute between the <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract</a> and <a href="/wiki/Figurative_art" title="Figurative art">figurative</a> artists in the group. In 1948 the abstract faction broke away to form the <a href="/wiki/Penwith_Society_of_Artists" class="mw-redirect" title="Penwith Society of Artists">Penwith Society of Artists</a> led by Barbara Hepworth and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nicholson" title="Ben Nicholson">Ben Nicholson</a>.\n</p><p>In 1962 <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Spratt" title="Frederick Spratt">Frederick Spratt</a> took a sabbatical in Britain for one year, where he lived and painted representationally in St Ives.\n</p><p>The studio pottery <a href="/wiki/Troika_Pottery" title="Troika Pottery">Troika</a> was set up in 1963.\n</p><p>A 2010, a BBC4 film, <i>The Art of Cornwall</i>, presented by <a href="/wiki/James_Fox_(art_historian)" title="James Fox (art historian)">James Fox</a> said that the St Ives artists "went on to produce some of the most exhilarating art of the twentieth century...for a few dazzling years this place was as famous as Paris, as exciting as New York and infinitely more progressive than London."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The programme explored the lives and works of the key figures and their contributions in establishing St Ives as a major centre of British art from the 1920s onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Barbara Hepworth Museum and her sculpture garden are the responsibility of Tate St Ives. It was the wish of the late sculptor to leave her work on public display in perpetuity. The St Ives Museum has exhibits illustrating local history and culture, including mining, fishing, agriculture and domestic life.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Ives,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg/220px-Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg/330px-Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg/440px-Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Knill%27s_Monument_-_geograph.org.uk_-_107848.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Knill\'s Monument, near St Ives, commemorating the mayor, John Knill</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/John_Knill" title="John Knill">John Knill</a>, a former mayor, constructed the Knill Steeple, a granite monument overlooking the town. In 1797, Knill laid down instructions for the celebration of the Knill Ceremony, which was to take place every five years on 25 July (St James\'s Day). The ceremony involves the Mayor of St Ives, a customs officer, and a vicar accompanied by two widows and ten girls who should be the "daughters of fishermen, tinners, or seamen".\n</p><p>A second celebration, of perhaps greater antiquity, is St Ives Feast, a celebration of the founding of St Ives by St Ia, which takes place on the Sunday and Monday nearest to 3 February each year. It includes a civic procession to Venton Ia, the well of St Ia, and other associated activities. It is one of the two surviving examples of <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Hurling" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Hurling">Cornish Hurling</a> (in a gentler format than its other manifestation at <a href="/wiki/St_Columb_Major" title="St Columb Major">St Columb Major</a>).\n</p><p>A third festival is the St Ives May Day, a modern revival of <a href="/wiki/West_Cornwall_May_Day_celebrations" title="West Cornwall May Day celebrations">West Cornwall May Day celebrations</a> that were once common throughout west Cornwall.\n</p><p>The St Ives September Festival celebrated its 30th anniversary in September 2008. It is one of the longest running and widest ranging Festivals of the Arts in the UK lasting for fifteen days and includes music (folk, jazz, rock, classical &amp; world), poetry, film, talks and books. It was founded in 1978 as a joint venture by local entrepreneurs and the International Musicians Seminar. Many local artists open up their studios to allow visitors to see how their art is produced. There is free music in many pubs almost every night, and concerts.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  Many events are held at the Western Hotel or St Ives Guildhall.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> St Ives has a 500-seat theatre which hosts some of the festival events.<sup id="cite_ref-41" beyond</div></div></div>\n<p>Early-20th-century figures in St Ives appear in <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a>\'s reflections contained in "<a href="/wiki/A_Sketch_of_the_Past" title="A Sketch of the Past">A Sketch of the Past</a>", from <i><a href="/wiki/Moments_of_Being" title="Moments of Being">Moments of Being</a>,</i> "...I could fill Once one of the dominant clubs in <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union_in_Cornwall" title="Rugby union in Cornwall">Cornish rugby</a>, they currently ply their trade in <a href="/wiki/Tribute_Western_Counties_West" title="Tribute Western Counties West">Tribute Western Counties West</a> (tier 7 of the <a href="/wiki/English_rugby_union_system" title="English rugby union system">English rugby union system</a>). The line was opened in 1877 by the St Ives branch railway, but became part of the Great Western Railway in 1878. A <a href="/wiki/Park_and_ride" title="Park and ride">Park-and-Ride</a> facility for visitors to St Ives runs from <a href="/wiki/Lelant_Saltings_railway_station" title="Lelant Saltings railway station">Lelant Saltings railway station</a>, which opened on 27 May 1978 specifically for this purpose. The line also links the town to nearby <a href="/wiki/Carbis_Bay" title="Carbis Bay">Carbis Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lelant" title="Lelant">Lelant</a>.\n</p><p>The town also has regular services by National Express Coach from <a href="/wiki/London_Victoria" class="mw-redirect" title="London Victoria">London Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heathrow" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathrow">Heathrow</a> and other places in Britain. Buses also connect St Ives to nearby towns and villages, such as <a href="/wiki/Zennor" title="Zennor">Zennor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Just_in_Penwith" title="St Just in Penwith">St Just</a>.\n</p><p>The nearest airports to St Ives are <a href="/wiki/Newquay_Cornwall_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Newquay Cornwall International Airport">Newquay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End_Airport" title="Land&#39;s End Airport">Land\'s End Airport</a>, near St Just.
