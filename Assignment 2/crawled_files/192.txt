href="/wiki/Leonid_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Leonid dynasty">Leonid dynasty</a>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Magnus Maximus</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Flavius Magnus Maximus Augustus</i>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Macsen Wledig</i>) (c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;335</span>\xe2\x80\x94August 28, 388) was <a href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> in the western portion of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Empire</a> from 383 to 388.\n</p><p>In 383, as commander of Britain, he usurped the throne against emperor <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a>, and by negotiation with emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>, he was made emperor in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britannia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Gaul" title="Roman Gaul">Gaul</a> the next year while Gratian\'s brother <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_II" title="Valentinian II">Valentinian II</a> retained <a href="/wiki/Roman_Italy" title="Roman Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africa_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Africa Province">Africa</a>. In 387, Maximus\'s ambitions led him to invade Italy, resulting in his defeat by <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Save" title="Battle of the Save">Battle of the Save</a> in 388. In the view of some historians, his death marked the end of direct imperial presence in Northern Gaul and Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Maximus was born c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;335</span> in <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>,  on the estates of <a href="/wiki/Count_Theodosius" title="Count Theodosius">Count Theodosius</a> (the Elder), to whom he was a nephew. Maximus was the son of the general <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flavius_Iulius_Eucherius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Flavius Iulius Eucherius (page does not exist)">Flavius Iulius Eucherius</a> and the brother of Marcellinus. Near contemporaries described his dignity as offended when lesser men were promoted to high positions.\n</p><p>Maximus was a distinguished general; he served under Count Theodosius in Africa in 373 and on the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> in 376. It is likely he also may have been a junior officer in Britain in 368, during the quelling of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Conspiracy" title="Great Conspiracy">Great Conspiracy</a>. Assigned to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a> in 380, he defeated an incursion of the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Scots</a> in 381.\n</p><p>The Western emperor <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> had become unpopular because of perceived favouritism toward <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a>--an <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">Iranian</a> speaking people (see also <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ossetians" title="Ossetians">Ossetians</a>) who were early adopters of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and migrated both east and west from their homeland--over Roman citizens.\n</p><p>In 383 Maximus was proclaimed emperor by his troops. He went to <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> to pursue his imperial ambitions, taking a large portion of the British garrison with him.\n</p><p>Following his landing in Gaul, Maximus went out to meet his main opponent, emperor Gratian, whom he defeated near <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. Gratian, after fleeing, was killed at <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> on August 25, 383. Continuing his campaign into <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, Maximus was stopped from overthrowing <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_II" title="Valentinian II">Valentinian II</a>, who was only twelve, when <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>, the Emperor in the East, sent <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Bauto" title="Flavius Bauto">Flavius Bauto</a> with a powerful force to stop him. Negotiations followed in 384 including the intervention of <a href="/wiki/Ambrose" title="Ambrose">Ambrose</a>, Bishop of Milan, leading to an accord with Valentinian II and Theodosius I in which Maximus was recognized as <a href="/wiki/Augustus_(rank)" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus (rank)">Augustus</a> in the West.\n</p><p>Maximus made his capital at Augusta Treverorum (Treves, <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a>) in Gaul, and ruled Britain, Gaul, Spain and Africa. He issued coinage and a number of edicts reorganizing Gaul\'s system of provinces. Some scholars believe Maximus may have founded the office of the <a href="/wiki/Comes_Britanniarum" title="Comes Britanniarum">Comes Britanniarum</a> as well. He became a popular emperor; <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Aurelius_Symmachus" title="Quintus Aurelius Symmachus">Quintus Aurelius Symmachus</a> delivered a <a href="/wiki/Panegyric" title="Panegyric">panegyric</a> on Maximus\'s virtues. He used <i>foederati</i> forces such as the <a href="/wiki/Alamanni" class="mw-redirect" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a> to great effect. He was also a stern persecutor of <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretics</a>. \n</p><p>It was on his orders that <a href="/wiki/Priscillian" title="Priscillian">Priscillian</a> and six companions were executed for <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>, in this case of <a href="/wiki/Priscillianism" title="Priscillianism">Priscillianism</a>, although the actual civil charges laid by Maximus himself were for the practice of <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a>. These executions went ahead despite the wishes of prominent men such as <a href="/wiki/St._Martin_of_Tours" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Martin of Tours">St. Martin of Tours</a>. Maximus\'s edict of 387 or 388, which censured Christians at Rome for burning down a Jewish <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a>, was condemned by bishop <a href="/wiki/Ambrose" title="Ambrose">Ambrose</a>, who said people exclaimed, \xe2\x80\x98the emperor has become a Jew\xe2\x80\x99.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 387 Maximus managed to force emperor <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_II" title="Valentinian II">Valentinian II</a> out of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, after which he fled to <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>.\nTheodosius and Valentinian then invaded from the east, and campaigned against Maximus in July\xe2\x80\x93August 388, their troops being led by <a href="/wiki/Richomeres" title="Richomeres">Richomeres</a> and other generals. Maximus was defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Save" title="Battle of the Save">Battle of the Save</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and retreated to <a href="/wiki/Aquileia" title="Aquileia">Aquileia</a>. Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> under <a href="/wiki/Marcomer" title="Marcomer">Marcomer</a> had taken the opportunity to invade northern Gaul, at the same time further weakening Maximus\'s position.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Andragathius" title="Andragathius">Andragathius</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_equitum" title="Magister equitum">magister equitum</a></i> of Maximus and the killer of Emperor Gratian, was defeated near <a href="/wiki/Siscia" class="mw-redirect" title="Siscia">Siscia</a>, while Maximus\'s brother, Marcellinus, fell in battle at <a href="/wiki/Poetovio" class="mw-redirect" title="Poetovio">Poetovio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Maximus surrendered in Aquileia, and although he pleaded for mercy was executed. The Senate passed a decree of <i><a href="/wiki/Damnatio_memoriae" title="Damnatio memoriae">Damnatio memoriae</a></i> against him. However, his mother and at least two daughters were spared.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Theodosius\'s trusted general <a href="/wiki/Arbogast_(general)" title="Arbogast (general)">Arbogast</a> strangled Maximus\'s son, <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Victor" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavius Victor">Flavius Victor</a>, at Trier in the fall of the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>What exactly happened to Maximus\'s family after his downfall is not recorded. He is known to have had a wife, who is recorded as having sought spiritual counsel from <a href="/wiki/St._Martin_of_Tours" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Martin of Tours">St. Martin of Tours</a> during his time at Trier.  Her ultimate fate, and even her name (but see the Welsh tradition below), have not been preserved in definitive historic records. The same is true of Maximus\'s mother and daughters, other than that they were spared by Theodosius I.\n</p><p>One of Maximus\'s daughters may have been married to Ennodius, proconsul Africae (395). Ennodius\'s grandson was <a href="/wiki/Petronius_Maximus" title="Petronius Maximus">Petronius Maximus</a>, another ill-fated emperor, who ruled in Rome for only 77 days before he was stoned to death while fleeing from the Vandals on May 24, 455. Other descendants of Ennodius, and thus possibly of Maximus, included <a href="/wiki/Olybrius" title="Olybrius">Anicius Olybrius</a>, emperor in 472, but also several consuls and bishops such as <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Felix_Ennodius" title="Magnus Felix Ennodius">St. Magnus Felix Ennodius</a> (Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a> c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;514</span>-21).  We also encounter an otherwise unrecorded daughter of Magnus Maximus, <a href="/wiki/Sevira" class="mw-redirect" title="Sevira">Sevira</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Pillar_of_Eliseg" title="Pillar of Eliseg">Pillar of Eliseg</a>, an early medieval inscribed stone in Wales which claims her marriage to <a href="/wiki/Vortigern" title="Vortigern">Vortigern</a>, king of the Britons.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_British_and_Breton_history">Role in British and Breton history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role in British and Breton history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Maximus\'s bid for imperial power in 383 coincides with the last date for any evidence of a Roman military presence in Wales, the western <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a>, and the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Deva</a>. Coins dated later than 383 have been found in excavations along <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian\'s Wall</a>, suggesting that troops were not stripped from it, as was once thought.<sup id="cite_ref-Frere1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frere1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></i>  written c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;540</span>, <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> says that Maximus left Britain not only with all of its Roman troops, but also with all of its armed bands, governors, and the flower of its youth, never to return.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Having left with the troops and senior administrators, and planning to continue as the ruler of Britain in the future, his practical course was to transfer local authority to local rulers. Welsh legend supports that this happened, with stories such as <i>Breuddwyd Macsen Wledig</i> (English: The Dream of Emperor Maximus), where he not only marries a wondrous British woman (thus making British descendants probable), but also gives her father sovereignty over Britain (thus formally transferring authority from Rome back to the Britons themselves).\n</p><p>The earliest Welsh genealogies give Maximus (referred to as <i>Macsen/Maxen Wledig</i>, or <i>Emperor Maximus</i>) the role of founding father of the dynasties of several medieval Welsh kingdoms, including those of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Powys" title="Kingdom of Powys">Powys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwent" title="Kingdom of Gwent">Gwent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He is given as the ancestor of a Welsh king on the <a href="/wiki/Pillar_of_Eliseg" title="Pillar of Eliseg">Pillar of Eliseg</a>, erected nearly 500 years after he left Britain, and he figures in lists of the <a href="/wiki/Fifteen_Tribes_of_Wales" title="Fifteen Tribes of Wales">Fifteen Tribes of Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rachel_Bromwich_2006_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rachel_Bromwich_2006-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After he became emperor of the West, Maximus would return to Britain to campaign against the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> and Scots (i.e., Irish), probably in support of Rome\'s long-standing allies the <a href="/wiki/Damnonii" title="Damnonii">Damnonii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Votadini" title="Votadini">Votadini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Novantae" title="Novantae">Novantae</a> (all located in modern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>). While there he likely made similar arrangements for a formal transfer of authority to local chiefs&#8212;the later rulers of <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a>, home to the Novantae, would claim Maximus as the founder of their line, the same as did the Welsh kings.<sup id="cite_ref-Frere1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frere1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The ninth century <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">Historia Brittonum</a></i> gives another account of Maximus and assigns him an important role:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The seventh emperor was Maximianus, He withdrew from Britain with all its military force, slew Gratianus the king of the Romans, and obtained the sovereignty of all Europe. Unwilling to send back his warlike companions to their wives, families, and possessions in Britain, he conferred upon them numerous districts from the lake on the summit of Mons Iovis, to the city called Cant Guic, and to the western Tumulus, that is Cruc Occident. These are the Armoric Britons, and they remain there to the present day. In consequence of their absence, Britain being overcome by foreign nations, the lawful heirs were cast out, till God interposed with his assistance.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Modern historians believe that this idea of mass British troop settlement in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> by Maximus may very well reflect some reality, as it accords with archaeological and other historical evidence and later Breton traditions.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a> declared independence from the Roman Empire in 407 CE, but contributed archers for <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Aetius" title="Flavius Aetius">Flavius Aetius</a>\'s defence against <a href="/wiki/Attila" title="Attila">Attila</a> the Hun, and its king <a href="/wiki/Riothamus" title="Riothamus">Riothamus</a> was subsequently mentioned in contemporary documents as an ally of Rome\'s against the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>. Despite its continued usage of two distinct languages, Breton and Gallo, and extensive invasions and conquests by Franks and Vikings, Armorica retained considerable cultural cohesion into the 13th century.\n</p><p>Maximus also established a military base in his native <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia (Spain)</a>, which persisted as a cultural entity despite occupation by the <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a> in 409, see <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Galicia" title="Kingdom of Galicia">Kingdom of Galicia</a>. This kingdom successfully resisted the Moors and subsequently initiated the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>.\n</p><p>Aetius sent large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a> to both Armorica and Galicia following the defeat of Attila at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Catalunian_Plains" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of the Catalunian Plains">Battle of the Catalunian Plains</a>. The Alans evidently assimilated quickly into the local Celtic cultures, contributing their own legends, e.g., to the Arthurian Cycle of romances.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_legend">Welsh legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Welsh legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Legendary versions of Maximus\'s career in which he marries the Welsh princess <a href="/wiki/Elen_Luyddog" class="mw-redirect" title="Elen Luyddog">Elen</a> may have circulated in popular tradition in Welsh-speaking areas from an early date. Although the story of Helen and Maximus\'s meeting is almost certainly fictional, there is some evidence for the basic claims. He is certainly given a prominent place in the earliest version of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Triads" title="Welsh Triads">Welsh Triads</a> which are believed to date from c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1100</span> and which reflect far older traditions. Welsh poetry also frequently refers to Macsen as a figure of comparison with later Welsh leaders. These legends come down to us in two separate versions.<sup id="cite_ref-Rachel_Bromwich_2006_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rachel_Bromwich_2006-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geoffrey_of_Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geoffrey of Monmouth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Llanbeblig_Hours_(f._3r.)_A_king,_possibly_Magnus_Maximus,_holding_a_sceptre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Llanbeblig_Hours_%28f._3r.%29_A_king%2C_possibly_Magnus_Maximus%2C_holding_a_sceptre.jpg/220px-Llanbeblig_Hours_%28f._3r.%29_A_king%2C_possibly_Magnus_Maximus%2C_holding_a_sceptre.jpg" width="220" height="347" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Llanbeblig_Hours_%28f._3r.%29_A_king%2C_possibly_Magnus_Maximus%2C_holding_a_sceptre.jpg/330px-Llanbeblig_Hours_%28f._3r.%29_A_king%2C_possibly_Magnus_Maximus%2C_holding_a_sceptre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Llanbeblig_Hours_%28f._3r.%29_A_king%2C_possibly_Magnus_Maximus%2C_holding_a_sceptre.jpg/440px-Llanbeblig_Hours_%28f._3r.%29_A_king%2C_possibly_Magnus_Maximus%2C_holding_a_sceptre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="570" data-file-height="900" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Llanbeblig_Hours_(f._3r.)_A_king,_possibly_Magnus_Maximus,_holding_a_sceptre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration from a 14th Century Welsh manuscript thought to intend to depict Magnus Maximus. Llanbeblig Hours (f. 3r.)</div></div></div> \n<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/38px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png" width="38" height="40" class="noviewer" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/57px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/76px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="410" data-file-height="430" /></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikisource" title="Wikisource">Wikisource</a> has original text related to this article:\n<div style="margin-left: 10px;"><b><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/History_of_the_Kings_of_Britain/Book_5" class="extiw" title="wikisource:History of the Kings of Britain/Book 5">History of the Kings of Britain/Book 5</a></b></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>\'s fictional <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i> (c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1136</span>), the basis for many English and Welsh legends, Maximianus as he calls him, was a Roman senator, a nephew of <a href="/wiki/Coel_Hen" title="Coel Hen">Coel Hen</a> through Coel\'s brother Ioelinus, and king of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Britons" title="Celtic Britons">Britons</a> following the death of <a href="/wiki/Eudaf_Hen" title="Eudaf Hen">Eudaf Hen</a>. Geoffrey writes this came about because Octavius wanted to wed his daughter to just such a powerful half-Roman-half-Briton and to give the kingship of Britain, as a dowry, to that husband, so he sent a message to Rome offering his daughter to Maximian.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Caradocus" title="Caradocus">Caradocus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_legendary_rulers_of_Cornwall" title="List of legendary rulers of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a>, had suggested and supported the marriage between Octavius\'s daughter and Maximian. Maximian accepted the offer and left Rome for Britain. Geoffrey claims further that Maximian gathered an army as he sacked <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> towns along the way. He invaded Clausentum (modern <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>) unintentionally and nearly fought the army of the Britons under <a href="/wiki/Conan_Meriadoc" title="Conan Meriadoc">Conan Meriadoc</a> before agreeing to a truce. Following further negotiations, Maximian was given the kingship of Britain and Octavius retired. Five years into his kingship, Magnus Maximus assembled a vast fleet and invaded <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, leaving Britain in the control of Caradocus. Upon reaching the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a> (historically, the region between the Loire and Seine rivers, later comprising Brittany, Normandy, Anjou, Maine and Touraine), he defeated the king and killed thousands of inhabitants. Before departing to Rome, he summoned <a href="/wiki/Conan_Meriadoc" title="Conan Meriadoc">Conanus</a>, the rebellious nephew of Octavius, and asked him to rule as king of the land, which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, or "Little Britain". Conan\'s men married native women after cutting out their tongues to preserve the purity of their language. <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> presents this legend to explain the Welsh name for Brittany, Llydaw, as originating from <i>lled-taw</i> or "half-silent". Given that Conan was well established in genealogies as the founder of Brittany, this account is certainly connected to an older tradition than Geoffrey.\n</p><p>Following the death of Caradocus, rule of Britain as regent passed to <a href="/wiki/Dionotus" title="Dionotus">Dionotus</a>, who &#8211; facing a foreign invasion &#8211; appealed to Maximus, who finally sent a man named <a href="/wiki/Gracianus_Municeps" title="Gracianus Municeps">Gracianus Municeps</a> with two legions to stop the attack. He killed many thousands before the invaders fled to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Maximus died in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> soon after and Dionotus became the official king of the Britons. Unfortunately, before he could begin his reign, Gracianus took hold of the crown and made himself king over Dionotus.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Dream_of_Macsen_Wledig">The Dream of Macsen Wledig</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Dream of Macsen Wledig">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/38px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png" width="38" height="40" class="noviewer" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/57px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Wikisource-logo.svg/76px-Wikisource-logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="410" data-file-height="430" /></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikisource" title="Wikisource">Wikisource</a> has original text related to this article:\n<div style="margin-left: 10px;"><b><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Mabinogion/The_Dream_of_Maxen_Wledig" class="extiw" title="wikisource:The Mabinogion/The Dream of Maxen Wledig">The Dream of Maxen Wledig</a></b></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Dream_of_Macsen_Wledig" class="mw-redirect" title="The Dream of Macsen Wledig">The Dream of Macsen Wledig</a></div>\n<p>Although the <i><a href="/wiki/Mabinogion" title="Mabinogion">Mabinogion</a></i> tale <i>The Dream of Macsen Wledig</i> is written in later manuscripts than Geoffrey\'s version, the two accounts are so different that scholars agree the Dream cannot be based purely on Geoffrey\'s version. The Dream\'s account also seems to accord better with details in the Triads, so it perhaps reflects an earlier tradition.\n</p><p>Macsen Wledig, the Emperor of Rome, dreams one night of a lovely maiden in a wonderful, far-off land. Awakening, he sends his men all over the earth in search of her. With much difficulty they find her in a rich castle in Wales, daughter of a chieftain based at <a href="/wiki/Segontium_Roman_Fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Segontium Roman Fort">Segontium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Caernarfon" title="Caernarfon">Caernarfon</a>), and lead the Emperor to her. Everything he finds is exactly as in his dream. The maiden, whose name is <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_the_Hosts" class="mw-redirect" title="Helen of the Hosts">Helen</a> or Elen, accepts and loves him. Because Elen is found a virgin, Macsen gives her father sovereignty over the island of Britain and orders three castles built for his bride.  In Macsen\'s absence, a new emperor seizes power and warns him not to return. With the help of men from Britain led by Elen\'s brother <a href="/wiki/Conan_Meriadoc" title="Conan Meriadoc">Conanus</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a></i>: Cynan Meriadoc, <i><a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a></i>: Conan Meriadeg), Macsen marches across Gaul and Italy and recaptures Rome. In gratitude to his British allies, Macsen rewards them with a portion of Gaul that becomes known as Brittany.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_literature">Later literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The prominent place of Macsen in history, Welsh legend and in the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_Britain" title="Matter of Britain">Matter of Britain</a> means he is often a character or referred to in historical and Arthurian fiction. Such stories include <a href="/wiki/Stephen_R._Lawhead" title="Stephen R. Lawhead">Stephen R. Lawhead</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Pendragon_Cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Pendragon Cycle">Pendragon Cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Stewart_(novelist)" title="Mary Stewart (novelist)">Mary Stewart</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollow_Hills" title="The Hollow Hills">The Hollow Hills</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Whyte" title="Jack Whyte">Jack Whyte</a>\'s Camulod Chronicles, <a href="/wiki/M_J_Trow" class="mw-redirect" title="M J Trow">M J Trow</a>\'s Britannia series, <a href="/wiki/Nancy_McKenzie" title="Nancy McKenzie">Nancy McKenzie</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Camelot" title="Queen of Camelot">Queen of Camelot</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Puck_of_Pook%27s_Hill" title="Puck of Pook&#39;s Hill">Puck of Pook\'s Hill</a></i>. The popular Welsh <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk song</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Yma_o_Hyd" title="Yma o Hyd">Yma o Hyd</a></i>, recorded by <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_Iwan" title="Dafydd Iwan">Dafydd Iwan</a> in 1981, recalls Macsen Wledig and celebrates the continued survival of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh people</a> since his days.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pillar_of_Eliseg" title="Pillar of Eliseg">Pillar of Eliseg</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_sources">Primary sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnus_Maximus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Primary sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>He is mentioned in a number of ancient and medieval sources:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a> <i>Rerum Gestarum Libri Qui Supersunt</i> XXXI.4.9</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> <i>Histories of the Kings of Britain</i> V.5-6</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> <i><a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_et_Conquestu_Britanniae" title="De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae">De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae</a></i> II.13-14</li>\n<li>\'<a href="/wiki/Nennius" title="Nennius">Nennius</a>\' <i>Historia Brittonum</i> 27;
