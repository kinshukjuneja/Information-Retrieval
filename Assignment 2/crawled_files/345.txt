style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Knight_of_the_Order_of_the_Garter" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight of the Order of the Garter">KG</a>,&#32;<a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" He was instrumental in negotiating and passing the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a> with Scotland, which created the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a>.\n</p><p>He had many other roles, including that of <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_the_Scilly_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of the Scilly Isles">Governor of Scilly</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Family_and_early_career"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Family and early career</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Exclusion_and_revolution"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Exclusion and revolution</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Career_under_William_III_and_Queen_Anne"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Career under William III and Queen Anne</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Marriage_&amp;_succession"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Marriage &amp; succession</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#Gallery"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">Gallery</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#Legacy"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">Legacy</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#Notes"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">Notes</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-8"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">7.1</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-9"><a href="#Primary_sources"><span class="tocnumber">7.2</span> <span class="toctext">Primary sources</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-10"><a href="#Secondary_sources"><span class="tocnumber">7.3</span> <span class="toctext">Secondary sources</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-11"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_early_career">Family and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>He came from an ancient <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish</a> family, being the son of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Godolphin_(1605%E2%80%931667)" title="Francis Godolphin (1605\xe2\x80\x931667)">Sir Francis Godolphin (1605\xe2\x80\x931667)</a> and nephew of the poet <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Godolphin_(poet)" title="Sidney Godolphin (poet)">Sidney Godolphin</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a> he was introduced into the royal household by King <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Favourite" title="Favourite">favourite</a> he had become, and he also entered the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> as member for <a href="/wiki/Helston_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Helston (UK Parliament constituency)">Helston</a>, in Cornwall.  Although he very seldom addressed the House, and, when he did so, only in the briefest manner, he "gradually acquired a reputation as its chief if not its only financial authority". In 1668 he was a successful intermediary between the King and his sister <a href="/wiki/Princess_Henrietta_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Princess Henrietta of England">Henrietta Anne</a> (wife of the <a href="/wiki/Philippe_I,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Philippe I, Duke of Orl\xc3\xa9ans">Duke of Orl\xc3\xa9ans</a>), in order to secure an agreement with King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a>, where Charles would reject his Dutch allies in return for French money.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1669 he was awarded a 31-year lease on all <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> mines in <a href="/wiki/Rialton" class="mw-redirect" title="Rialton">Rialton</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retraigh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Retraigh (page does not exist)">Retraigh</a> in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1670 Godolphin was appointed <a href="/wiki/Groom_of_the_Bedchamber" class="mw-redirect" title="Groom of the Bedchamber">Groom of the Bedchamber</a> along with a pension of \xc2\xa3500 per annum, holding the post until 1678.\n</p><p>Charles appointed Godolphin <a href="/wiki/Envoy-extraordinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Envoy-extraordinary">envoy-extraordinary</a> to Louis XIV in 1672 in order to reassure the French King of Charles\'s allegiance before Louis attacked the Dutch. Godolphin was with Louis in the field during the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Dutch_War" title="Franco-Dutch War">Franco-Dutch War</a> but was unimpressed with Louis\'s capabilities as a military commander.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 1679 he was appointed a member of the Privy Council, and in the September following he was promoted, along with <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Hyde,_1st_Earl_of_Rochester" title="Laurence Hyde, 1st Earl of Rochester">Viscount Hyde</a> (afterwards Earl of Rochester) and the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Spencer,_2nd_Earl_of_Sunderland" title="Robert Spencer, 2nd Earl of Sunderland">Earl of Sunderland</a>, to the chief management of affairs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusion_and_revolution">Exclusion and revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exclusion and revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although he voted for the <a href="/wiki/Exclusion_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclusion Bill">Exclusion Bill</a> in 1680 \xe2\x80\x93 which, if successfully enacted, would have prevented the Catholic Duke of York from assuming the throne \xe2\x80\x93 he was continued in office after the dismissal of Sunderland, and in September 1684 he was created <b><a href="/wiki/Baron_Godolphin" title="Baron Godolphin">Baron Godolphin</a> of Rialton</b>,.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_accessed_May_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB_accessed_May-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and succeeded Rochester as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Treasurer" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Treasurer">First Lord of the Treasury</a>. After the accession of <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II</a> he was made chamberlain to the queen, <a href="/wiki/Mary_of_Modena" title="Mary of Modena">Mary of Modena</a>, and, along with Rochester and Sunderland, enjoyed the king\'s special confidence. In 1687 he was named commissioner of the treasury. Godolphin was involved in the payment of approximately \xc2\xa3125,000 by Louis XIV to James II of money in return for James\'s support for Louis, despite Parliament voting James \xc2\xa36,000,000. The historian <a href="/wiki/David_Ogg_(historian)" title="David Ogg (historian)">David Ogg</a> has written that "James and his two ministers, Rochester and Godolphin, were prepared to barter the independence of England for a sum little more than a sixtieth part of that granted by the national legislature".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>He was one of the council of five appointed by King James to represent him in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, when he went to join the army after the landing of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William of Orange</a>, in England, and, along with <a href="/wiki/George_Savile,_1st_Marquess_of_Halifax" title="George Savile, 1st Marquess of Halifax">Lord Halifax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Finch,_2nd_Earl_of_Nottingham" title="Daniel Finch, 2nd Earl of Nottingham">Lord Nottingham</a>, he was afterwards appointed a commissioner to negotiate with the prince. On the accession of William, though he only obtained the third seat at the treasury board, he was in control of affairs. He retired in March 1690, but, was recalled in the following November and appointed first lord.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_under_William_III_and_Queen_Anne">Career under William III and Queen Anne</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career under William III and Queen Anne">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While holding this office he for several years continued, in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/John_Churchill,_1st_Duke_of_Marlborough" title="John Churchill, 1st Duke of Marlborough">John Churchill</a> (the future Duke of Marlborough), a secret correspondence with James II, and is said to have disclosed to James intelligence regarding the intended expedition against <a href="/wiki/Brest,_France" title="Brest, France">Brest</a>. Godolphin was not only a Tory by inheritance, but was thought to have a romantic admiration for the wife of James II. After <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Fenwick,_3rd_Baronet" title="Sir John Fenwick, 3rd Baronet">Fenwick</a>\'s confession in 1696 regarding the attempted assassination of William III, Godolphin, who was compromised, tendered his resignation; but when the Tories came into power in 1700, he was again appointed First Lord of the Treasury. Though not technically a favourite with <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a>, he was, after her accession, appointed Lord Treasurer, on the strong recommendation of Marlborough, and he retained this office for eight years. In 1704 he was also made a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Knight of the Garter</a>, and in December 1706 he was created <b><a href="/wiki/Viscount_Rialton" class="mw-redirect" title="Viscount Rialton">Viscount Rialton</a></b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Godolphin" title="Earl of Godolphin">Earl of Godolphin</a></b>.\n</p><p>Though a Tory, he had an active share in the intrigues which gradually led to the predominance of the Whigs in alliance with Marlborough. The influence of the Marlboroughs with the queen was, however, gradually supplanted by that of <a href="/wiki/Abigail_Masham" class="mw-redirect" title="Abigail Masham">Abigail Masham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Harley,_1st_Earl_of_Oxford_and_Mortimer" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Harley, 1st Earl of Oxford and Mortimer">Robert Harley</a> (later Earl of Oxford), and with the fortunes of the Marlboroughs those of Godolphin were indissolubly united. The services of both were so appreciated by the nation that they were able for a time to regard the loss of the queen\xe2\x80\x99s favour with indifference, and even in 1708 to procure the expulsion of Harley from office; but after the High Tory reaction which followed the impeachment of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sacheverell" title="Henry Sacheverell">Henry Sacheverell</a>, who abused Godolphin under the name of <a href="/wiki/Volpone" title="Volpone">Volpone</a>, the queen made use of the opportunity to get rid of Marlborough by abruptly dismissing Godolphin from office on 7 August 1710. He died two years later and his estate was worth more than \xc2\xa312,000.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Marriage_.26_succession"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_&amp;_succession">Marriage &amp; succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marriage &amp; succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 16 May 1675 Godolphin married <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Godolphin" title="Margaret Godolphin">Margaret Blagge</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Blagge" title="Thomas Blagge">Thomas Blagge</a>, the pious lady whose life was written by <a href="/wiki/John_Evelyn" title="John Evelyn">John Evelyn</a> in his book <i>The Life of Mrs Godolphin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> She died in childbirth in 1678 bearing his only son, and Godolphin never remarried. Margaret is buried at Breage, Cornwall, the spot marked by a small brass floor plaque erected by the Duke of Leeds. href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Whig historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Babington_Macaulay,_1st_Baron_Macaulay" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Babington Macaulay, 1st Baron Macaulay">Lord Macaulay</a> said of Godolphin in 1848:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>He was laborious, clear-headed, and profoundly versed in the details of finance. Every government, therefore, found him an useful servant; and there was nothing in his opinions or in his character which could prevent him from serving any government. \xe2\x80\x9cSidney Godolphin,\xe2\x80\x9d said Charles, \xe2\x80\x9cis never in the way, and never out of the way.\xe2\x80\x9d This pointed remark goes far to explain Godolphin\'s extraordinary success in life. He acted at different times with both the great political parties; but he never shared in the passions of either. Like most men of cautious tempers and prosperous fortunes, he had a strong disposition to support whatever existed. He disliked revolutions, and, for the same reason for which he disliked revolutions, he disliked counter-revolutions. His deportment was remarkably grave and reserved, but his personal tastes were low and frivolous; and most of the time which he could save from public business was spent in racing, cardplaying, and <a href="/wiki/Cockfighting" class="mw-redirect" title="Cockfighting">cockfighting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>In the opinion of Julian Hoppitt, Godolphin "tirelessly oversaw the dramatic expansion of key areas of the State, providing an element of integrity, continuity, and predictability in a very uncertain environment. He was in a very real sense Marlborough\'s partner and together the duumvirs oversaw the glory days of the War of the Spanish Succession. In a very real sense Marlborough\'s dismissal and Godolphin\'s death the following year marked the end of an era".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Roy Sundstrom has asserted that Godolphin is an important figure in the history of England because:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>...first he raised the money required to blunt French hegemony in Europe and thus preserved the British constitution and the protestant monarchy; second he was instrumental in planning the military and diplomatic strategy that ultimately defeated Louis XIV; third, as lord high treasurer, he worked to make the Treasury more efficient and attempted to weed out corruption\xe2\x80\x94the Treasury as he left it served England well for the remainder of the eighteenth century; fourth he was instrumental in negotiating and passing the Act of Union with Scotland which created the united kingdom of Great Britain; and fifth he negotiated the creation of a unified East India Company, which would be instrumental in establishing British rule in India.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-roman;">\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sidney_Godolphin,_1st_Earl_of_Godolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-ODNB-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-ODNB_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ODNB_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ODNB_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ODNB_1-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Roy A.
