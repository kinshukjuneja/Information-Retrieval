class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Isle of Man demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a></div>\n<p>According to the 2011 interim census,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the Isle of Man is home to 84,655 people, of whom 26,218 reside in the island\'s capital <a href="/wiki/Douglas,_Isle_of_Man" title="Douglas, Isle of Man">Douglas</a>. The largest proportion of the population was born on the island, but major settlement by <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English people</a> and others has significantly altered the demographics. According to the 2011 census, 47.6% were born in the Isle of Man, and 37.2% were born in England, with smaller numbers born elsewhere: 3.4% in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, 2.1% in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, 2.1% in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, 1.2% in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and 0.3% born in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>, with 6.1% of the population having been born elsewhere in the world.\n</p><p>Manx people living in the UK were commonly grouped by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2001">2001 census</a> under "<a href="/wiki/White_British" title="White British">White British</a>". The extremely high ratio of "come-overs" to "natives" has brought with it changes in terms of culture, identity and speech. Manx people have also made a significant contribution elsewhere through migration. The Manx have a long tradition of moving to <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> for work, hence a lot of <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> people have Manx ancestry, among them <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A lot of Manx people emigrated to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, notably to <a href="/wiki/Cuyahoga_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuyahoga County">Cuyahoga County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_County,_Ohio" title="Lake County, Ohio">Lake County, Ohio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span three vernaculars:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Goidelic language">Gaelic</a> language.</li>\n<li>English language\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Manx_English" title="Manx English">Anglo-Manx</a>, the distinctive indigenous English dialect of the Manx, now much diluted.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, the usual form of English used in the Isle of Man, especially for formal purposes.</li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>The English language is used in <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a>; the use of Manx there is restricted to a few formulaic phrases. However some Manx is used to a very limited extent in official publications, street signs etc., and education in the Manx language is offered in schools. The Manx language knowledge of most people on the island is limited to a few words.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_politics">History and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="History of the Isle of Man">History of the Isle of Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Politics of the Isle of Man">Politics of the Isle of Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Government" title="Isle of Man Government">Isle of Man Government</a></div>\n<p>The Isle of Man is one of the six <a href="/wiki/Celtic_nations" title="Celtic nations">Celtic nations</a>, and has been under <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a>, Scottish and English control for much of the past thousand years.\n</p><p>The earliest traces of people in the Isle of Man date to around 8000 BC, during the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> Period, also known as the Middle Stone Age. Small, <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomadic</a> family groups lived in camp sites, hunting wild game, fishing the rivers and coastal waters and gathering plant foods.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Neolithic period was marked by important economic and social changes. By 4000 BC, people once reliant upon the uncultivated natural resources of the land and sea had adopted cereal growing and stock rearing, using imported species of grain and animals. Large scale clearance of natural woodland provided fields for crops and animal fodder.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>, Celtic influence began to arrive on the island. Based on inscriptions, the inhabitants appear to have used a <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic</a> language; however, at some point, possibly c. 700 AD, it is assumed that Irish invasion or immigration formed the basis of a new culture, and the Manx came to speak Gaelic. This language has developed in isolation since, though it remains closely related to <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of the 8th century, <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> settlers began to arrive and establish settlements, eventually coming to dominate the island.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Norse <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Mann and the Isles">Kingdom of Mann and the Isles</a> was created by <a href="/wiki/Godred_Crovan" title="Godred Crovan">Godred Crovan</a> in 1079. The <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> had a major impact on the island, leaving behind Norse placenames, and influencing its distinctive political system, <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>, <i>\xc3\x9eingv\xc3\xb3llr</i>), which is one of the oldest parliamentary democracies in the world.\n</p><p>In 1266, under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Perth" title="Treaty of Perth">Treaty of Perth</a>, Norway\'s King <a href="/wiki/Magnus_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus VI">Magnus VI</a> ceded the isles to <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. For more than a century the Isle of Man, during the Anglo-Scottish wars, passed between Scotland and England. During this troubled period the Island was captured by the Scottish army of Robert the Bruce in 1313. Later in the 14th century, when England once more seized the Island, the Lordship \xe2\x80\x93 indeed kingship \xe2\x80\x93 was given to the Montacute family, Earls of Salisbury.\n</p><p>In 1405, the Lordship was granted to Sir John Stanley, whose descendants (later the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Derby" title="Earl of Derby">Earls of Derby</a>) ruled the Isle of Man for over 300 years. The lordship passed through a female line to the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Atholl" title="Duke of Atholl">Dukes of Atholl</a> in 1736, and was eventually <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Isle_of_Man#Revestment" title="History of the Isle of Man">purchased by the British Crown</a> in 1765.\n</p><p>Since 1866, when the Isle of Man obtained a measure of <a href="/wiki/Home_rule" title="Home rule">home rule</a>, the Manx people have developed into a modern nation with an economy based decreasingly on agriculture and fishing and increasingly first on tourism and then on financial and other services.\n</p><p>The 20th century saw a revival of interest in Manx music and dance, and in the <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx language</a>, though the last native (first language) speaker of Manx died in the 1970s. In the middle of the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a> visited, and was so distressed at the lack of support for Manx that he immediately had two recording vans sent over to record the language before it disappeared completely.\n</p><p>As the century progressed, the Manx tourist economy declined, first because of the effects of the two world wars and later as tourists began to take advantage of cheaper air travel to take European <a href="/wiki/Package_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Package holiday">package holidays</a>. The Manx government responded in the 1960s by promoting the island as an <a href="/wiki/Offshore_financial_centre" title="Offshore financial centre">offshore financial centre</a>. While this has had beneficial effects on the Manx economy, it has had its detractors, who have pointed to negative aspects such as the effects on local house prices, and perhaps also <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">money laundering</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The economic changes gave a short-lived impetus to Manx nationalism in the 1970s and 1980s, spawning <a href="/wiki/Mec_Vannin" title="Mec Vannin">Mec Vannin</a>, a nationalist group, as well as the now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Manx_National_Party" title="Manx National Party">Manx National Party</a> and <i lang="gv" title="Manx language text"><a href="/wiki/Fo_Halloo" title="Fo Halloo">Fo Halloo</a></i> ("Underground"), which mounted a direct-action campaign of spray-painting and house-burning. Nationalist politics has since declined and a number of its former proponents are now in mainstream politics.\n</p><p>The 1990s and early 21st century have seen a greater recognition of indigenous Manx culture, such as the first Manx-medium primary school, though Manx culture still remains on the margins of popular culture for the majority of Manx residents.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manx_political_parties">Manx political parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Manx political parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most Manx politicians are independents rather than <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_the_Isle_of_Man" title="List of political parties in the Isle of Man">party members</a>. Political parties such as <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Vannin_Party" title="Liberal Vannin Party">Liberal Vannin</a> (currently the only party with MHKs) and the <a href="/wiki/Manx_Labour_Party" title="Manx Labour Party">Manx Labour Party</a> (which had several MHKs in the mid 20th century) have been active in recent years.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_permits_and_immigration">Work permits and immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Work permits and immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/External_relations_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="External relations of the Isle of Man">External relations of the Isle of Man</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> has had a complicated relationship with the United Kingdom over the years \xe2\x80\x93 it is neither part of the UK, nor of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, but is a <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown Dependency</a>.\n</p><p>Manx people, as British citizens, may travel and work freely in the United Kingdom. Passports issued on the Island are marked \'British Islands \xe2\x80\x93 Isle of Man\', instead of \'United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland\', and these passports are issued to all British citizens resident on the island.\n</p><p>Manx people without a family link or past residency in the UK are restricted from exercising the right to live and work in other EU countries.\n</p><p>The Isle of Man is part of the <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a>, which means there are no immigration controls on travel to and from the UK and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>; however a work permit is generally required in order to work on the island.\n</p>\n<h2><span
