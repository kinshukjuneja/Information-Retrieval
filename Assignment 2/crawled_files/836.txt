href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in south-east <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, England, with a href="/wiki/Liskeard" title="Liskeard">Liskeard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> divided in two by the <a href="/wiki/River_Looe" title="River Looe">River Looe</a>, East Looe (Cornish: <i lang="kw">Logh</i><sup id="cite_ref-swfplacenames_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swfplacenames-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) and West Looe (Cornish: <i lang="kw">Porthbyhan</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-swfplacenames_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swfplacenames-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>, <abbr title="literal translation">lit.</abbr> "little cove") being connected by a bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Looe developed as two separate towns each with MPs and its own mayor.\n</p><p>The town centres around a small harbour and along the steep-sided valley of the River Looe which flows between East and West Looe to the sea beside a sandy beach. class="toclevel-1 tocsection-13"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-14"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-15"><a href="#General"><span class="tocnumber">6.1</span> <span class="toctext">General</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-16"><a href="#Historical_resources"><span class="tocnumber">6.2</span> <span class="toctext">Historical resources</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-17"><a href="#Miscellaneous"><span class="tocnumber">6.3</span> <span class="toctext">Miscellaneous</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory_and_foundation">Prehistory and foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prehistory and foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Archeological evidence, such as the so-called Giant\'s Hedge<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Stone_circle" title="Stone circle">stone circle</a> at Bin Down (from the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> "Bin Dun", meaning "<a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill fort</a>") on a hill above East Looe, indicates that the area around Looe was inhabited as early as <a href="/wiki/1000s_BC_(decade)" title="1000s BC (decade)">1000 BC</a>.\n</p><p>At the time of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> in 1086 the <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manor</a> of Pendrym, which included much of the site of modern-day East Looe, was still held by <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>, as part of his own <a href="/wiki/Demesne" title="Demesne">demesne</a>, which he later devolved to the Bodgrugan (<a href="/wiki/Bodrigan" title="Bodrigan">Bodrigan</a>) family. Land across the river belonged to the manors of Portalla (or Portallant) and Portbyhan (variously spelt Portbyan, Porthbyghan, Porthpyghan, among others).\n</p><p>Shutta, on the steep hillside over East Looe, is recorded as being inhabited by the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Between 1154 and 1189 <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> granted a <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">charter</a> in favour of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Bodrugan" title="Henry Bodrugan">Sir Henry Bodrugan</a> as <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">Mayor</a> of East Looe. West Looe was given free <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">borough</a> status sometime after this (the first known historical mention of the town dates from 1327) and in the 1230s East Looe secured the right to hold a weekly market and a <a href="/wiki/Michaelmas" title="Michaelmas">Michaelmas</a> <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fair</a>. East Looe\'s layout looks like a "planted borough", a concept similar to modern <a href="/wiki/New_town" class="mw-redirect" title="New town">new towns</a>, since most of its streets form a grid-like pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Low-lying parts of Looe continue to suffer frequent <a href="/wiki/Flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Flooding">flooding</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a> are very high. For practical reasons, most <a href="/wiki/Fishermen" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishermen">fishermen</a>\'s houses in ancient Looe, like elsewhere along the south coast, were constructed with their living quarters upstairs and a storage area at <a href="/wiki/Storey" title="Storey">ground level</a> below: for <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishing_tackle" title="Fishing tackle">fishing tackle</a>, <i>etc</i>; these are termed "fishermen\'s <a href="/wiki/Basement" title="Basement">cellars</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_churches">Early churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some time before 1144, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">the Order of Saint Benedict</a> occupied <a href="/wiki/Looe_Island" title="Looe Island">Looe Island</a>, building a chapel there, and the monks established a rudimentary lighthouse service using <a href="/wiki/Beacon" title="Beacon">beacons</a>. Another chapel was founded on an opposite hillside just outside West Looe; both are now marked only by ruins.\n</p><p>The parish church of East Looe was at <a href="/wiki/St_Martin_by_Looe" class="mw-redirect" title="St Martin by Looe">St Martin by Looe</a> but there was a <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapel of ease</a> in the town. <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Church,_East_Looe" title="St Mary&#39;s Church, East Looe">St Mary\'s Church, East Looe</a> was dedicated in 1259 by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bronscombe" class="mw-redirect" title="Walter Bronscombe">Walter Bronscombe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Exeter" title="Bishop of Exeter">Bishop of Exeter</a>. Despite rebuilding commencing in 1805, it has since fallen into disrepair, although the original Tower still remains. On the centre of the bridge in medieval times stood the Chapel of St Anne (dedicated in 1436): this dedication was attributed to the town chapel by <a href="/wiki/George_Oliver_(freemason)" title="George Oliver (freemason)">Dr George Oliver</a> and has been adopted ever since, displacing that of St Mary.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_pp._148-49_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_pp._148-49-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>West Looe comprised part of the <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> of <a href="/wiki/Talland" title="Talland">Talland</a> since the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, but a chapel of ease, <a href="/wiki/St_Nicholas%27_Church,_West_Looe" title="St Nicholas&#39; Church, West Looe">St Nicholas\' Church, West Looe</a> was extant before 1330 when it is recorded as being further endowed and enlarged. After spells as a <a href="/wiki/Guildhall" title="Guildhall">common hall</a> and a schoolhouse, this building has reverted to its original ecclesiastical use, having been substantially restored in 1852, 1862 and 1915.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_pp._148-49_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornish_Church_Guide_1925_pp._148-49-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_era">Medieval era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medieval era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif/220px-FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif" width="220" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif/330px-FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif/440px-FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="970" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FoweyLooeRiversMapCornwallUK.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sketchmap of the East and West Looe Rivers</div></div></div>\n<p>An early wooden bridge over the Looe River was in place by 1411, which burned down and was replaced by the first stone bridge, completed in 1436 and featured a chapel dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anne" title="Saint Anne">St Anne</a> in the middle (the current bridge, a seven-arched <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> bridge, was opened in 1853). By this time Looe had become a major port, one of Cornwall\'s largest, exporting local <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, as well as hosting thriving <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boat_building" title="Boat building">boatbuilding</a> industries. The town was able to provide some 20 ships for the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Calais_(1346)" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege of Calais (1346)">Siege of Calais</a> in 1347.\n</p><p>With effective civic leadership, Looe thrived in the Middle Ages and Tudor era, being both a busy port and situated with close access to the main road from London to <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>. By this time the <a href="/wiki/Textile_industry" title="Textile industry">textile industry</a> had come to play an important part in the town\'s economy, in addition to the traditional boatbuilding and fishing (particularly <a href="/wiki/Pilchard" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilchard">pilchards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>). Trade and transport to and from thriving <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> also contributed to the town\'s success. The Old Guildhall in East Looe is believed to have dated from around 1500.\n</p><p>The constituencies of <a href="/wiki/East_Looe_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="East Looe (UK Parliament constituency)">East Looe</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Looe_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="West Looe (UK Parliament constituency)">West Looe</a> were incorporated as <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary borough">parliamentary boroughs</a> in 1571 and 1553 respectively, surviving as <a href="/wiki/Rotten_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotten borough">rotten boroughs</a> with each returning two <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MPs</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Unreformed_House_of_Commons" title="Unreformed House of Commons">unreformed House of Commons</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Great_Reform_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Reform Act">Great Reform Act</a> of 1832. For example, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wager" title="Charles Wager">Admiral Sir Charles Wager</a>, a son and grandson of Kentish mariners, held the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district" title="Electoral district">seat</a> as MP for West Looe early in (1713\xe2\x80\x931715) and at the end (1741\xe2\x80\x931743) of his political career. The <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">seal</a> of East Looe was <a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">blazoned</a> <i>An antique one-mast vessel in it a man and boy against the side of the hulk three escutcheons each charges with three bends</i>, with the legend "Si, comunetatis de Loo". The seal of West Looe was <i>An armed man holding a bow in his right hand and an arrow in his left</i>, with the legend "Por-tu-an <i>vel</i> Wys Westlo".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1625 <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a> devastated Looe, carrying off around 80 mariners and fishermen, and taking them to North Africa to be enslaved. Forewarned of the attack, most of the inhabitants of the town were able to escape, but the town itself was torched. <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By the start of the 1800s, Looe\'s fortunes were in decline. The <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> had taken its toll on the country; in 1803, the town formed a volunteer company to man guns in defence against attack from the French. The blockade of 1808, which prevented the Looe fleet from reaching their pilchard-fishing areas, also put considerable financial strain on the community. In 1805, the old St. Mary\'s Chapel (apart from the <a href="/wiki/Bell_tower" title="Bell tower">tower</a>) had to be demolished due to dilapidation, and in 1817, the town was badly damaged by heavy storms and flooding.\n</p><p>With the building of the <a href="/wiki/Liskeard_and_Looe_Union_Canal" title="Liskeard and Looe Union Canal">Liskeard and Looe Union Canal</a> linking Looe to Liskeard in 1828, and the development of booming <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> mines in the <a href="/wiki/Caradon" title="Caradon">Caradon</a> area from 1837, Looe\'s fortunes began to revive. The Herodsfoot mine produced 13,470 tons of lead between 1848 and 1884 and more than 17 tons of silver between 1853 and 1884. The canal was used first to transport <a href="/wiki/Lime_(mineral)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lime (mineral)">lime</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> for use in Cornish farming, and later to carry copper and granite between the railhead at Liskeard (from where rail links reached to the <a href="/wiki/Cheesewring" title="Cheesewring">Cheesewring</a> on <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Moor" title="Bodmin Moor">Bodmin Moor</a>) and the port of Looe. In 1856 the large quay of East Looe was built to handle the demands of the shipping trade, and in 1860, with the canal unable to keep up with demand, a <a href="/wiki/Looe_Valley_Line" title="Looe Valley Line">railway</a> was built linking Looe to <a href="/wiki/Moorswater" title="Moorswater">Moorswater</a> near Liskeard, along the <a href="/wiki/Towpath" title="Towpath">towpath</a> of the canal, which was used less and less until, by 1910, traffic ceased entirely. The railway was later linked to <a href="/wiki/Liskeard" title="Liskeard">Liskeard</a> proper, and as the mining boom came to an end, it adapted to carry passengers in 1879.\n</p><p>In 1866, a <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboat</a> station had been established on East Looe Beach, and in 1878 a new town hall was built: Looe\'s present-day Guildhall. Around this time recommendations were made that the two towns be merged under one governing body, and despite much protest <b>Looe Urban District Council</b> was formed in 1898 <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> with jurisdiction over the communities on both sides of the River Looe.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg/220px-Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg/330px-Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg/440px-Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3240" data-file-height="4320" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Celtic_Cross_War_Memorial_Looe_-_panoramio.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The war memorial</div></div></div>\n<p>With the Victorian fashion for seaside holidays, Looe evolved as a tourist town, with nearby <a href="/wiki/Talland_Bay" title="Talland Bay">Talland Bay</a> being dubbed "the playground of Plymouth". This trend continued throughout the 20th century; more and more hotels and tourist facilities were built in the town, and Looe grew and prospered, with peaks in fishing and boatbuilding following the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World Wars</a>.\n</p><p>It is to rest in the fresh seaside air that New Zealand writer <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Mansfield" title="Katherine Mansfield">Katherine Mansfield</a> stayed in Looe for spring and summer <a href="/wiki/1918" title="1918">1918</a> in order to recover from <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>. She joined there her long-time friend the American painter <a href="/wiki/Anne_Estelle_Rice" title="Anne Estelle Rice">Anne Estelle Rice</a>, who famously painted her in red<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>. The <i>Portrait of Katherine Mansfield</i> made in Looe is now exposed since 1946 in <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_New_Zealand_Te_Papa_Tongarewa" title="Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa">Te Papa Tongarewa museum of New Zealand</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Looe_today">Looe today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Looe today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:West_Looe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/West_Looe.jpg/220px-West_Looe.jpg" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/West_Looe.jpg/330px-West_Looe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/West_Looe.jpg/440px-West_Looe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3643" data-file-height="2049" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:West_Looe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>West Looe viewed across the river from East Looe</div></div></div>\n<p>Looe remains a fishing town, and retained several fish dealers operating from the quayside of East Looe until the advent of EU regulations. With its fleet of small fishing boats returning their catches to port daily, Looe has a reputation for procuring excellent fresh fish. The town is also a centre for <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">shark</a> <a href="/wiki/Big-game_fishing" title="Big-game fishing">fishing</a>, and is the home of the <a href="/wiki/Shark_Angling_Club_of_Great_Britain" title="Shark Angling Club of Great Britain">Shark Angling Club of Great Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:View_towards_Looe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/View_towards_Looe.jpg/220px-View_towards_Looe.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/View_towards_Looe.jpg/330px-View_towards_Looe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/View_towards_Looe.jpg/440px-View_towards_Looe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:View_towards_Looe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View towards Looe, taken from near <b>Looe Island</b></div></div></div>\n<p>Nonetheless, Looe\'s main business today is tourism, with much of the town given over to hotels, <a href="/wiki/Guest_house_(lodging)" class="mw-redirect" title="Guest house (lodging)">guest houses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holiday_home" class="mw-redirect" title="Holiday home">holiday homes</a>, along with a large number of <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">pubs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurants</a> and beach equipment, ice cream and <a href="/wiki/Pasty" title="Pasty">Cornish pasty</a> vendors. Inland from Looe lie many camping and <a href="/wiki/Travel_trailer" class="mw-redirect" title="Travel trailer">caravan</a> sites, as well as the famous <a href="/wiki/Monkey_Sanctuary" title="Monkey Sanctuary">Woolly Monkey Sanctuary</a>. Other local attractions include the beaches, sailing, fishing and <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">diving</a>, and spectacular coastal walks (especially via Talland to <a href="/wiki/Polperro" title="Polperro">Polperro</a>). South East Cornwall boasts several stately homes, including <a href="/wiki/Antony_House" title="Antony House">Antony House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotehele" title="Cotehele">Cotehele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Edgcumbe_House" title="Mount Edgcumbe House">Mount Edgcumbe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lanhydrock_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Lanhydrock House">Lanhydrock House</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Eden_Project" title="Eden Project">Eden Project</a> near <a href="/wiki/St_Austell" title="St Austell">St Austell</a> which tourists can access by road.\n</p><p>Outside the busy summer months, the town remains a centre for shopping and entertainment for local villagers. Annually in late September, the town is the destination of choice for thousands of music lovers and top name performers for the <a href="/wiki/Looe_Music_Festival" title="Looe Music Festival">Looe Music Festival</a>, which takes place in temporary venues around the town, harbour and on East Looe beach.\n</p><p>There is a tradition of the townsfolk wearing <a href="/wiki/Fancy_dress" class="mw-redirect" title="Fancy dress">fancy dress</a> on <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year&#39;s Eve">New Year\'s Eve</a>, when the streets are thronged with revellers in inventive outfits.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Looe has been on the list of the top 10 places in the UK to celebrate New Year, and ranked third on the list for 2007-08. Looe is regenerating itself, like many other ports, to serve as a small cargo port. On the high ground north of East and West Looe there are many modern houses and a recreational area called \'the Downs\'.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Looe">East Looe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: East Looe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg/220px-Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg/330px-Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg/440px-Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Looe_Lifeboat_Station_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Looe <a href="/wiki/RNLI" class="mw-redirect" title="RNLI">RNLI</a> Lifeboat Station</div></div></div>\n<p>East Looe centres on its broad sandy beach, with the distinctive <a href="/wiki/Banjo_Pier" title="Banjo Pier">Banjo Pier</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Thomas_(architect)" title="Joseph Thomas (architect)">Joseph Thomas</a>, a new lifeboat station and St Mary\'s Church. Stretching back from the church is a grid of narrow streets forming the main business area of the town, packed with many small shops, restaurants and pubs and the Old Guildhall, now a museum. Along the estuary lies the quay with its fish merchants. Towards Looe Bridge lies the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Era">Victorian</a> Guildhall, and just north of the bridge the <a href="/wiki/Looe_railway_station" title="Looe railway station">railway station</a>. This is the <a href="/wiki/Terminal_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Terminal station">terminus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Looe_Valley_Line" title="Looe Valley Line">Looe Valley branch line</a> to <a href="/wiki/Liskeard_railway_station" title="Liskeard railway station">Liskeard</a>, where it connects to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Main_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Western Main Line">Great Western Main Line</a> and services to <a href="/wiki/London_Paddington_station" title="London Paddington station">London Paddington</a>.\n</p><p>On the hilltop above East Looe lies Shutta, and beyond that the Sunrising <a href="/wiki/Housing_estate" title="Housing estate">housing estate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Looe_Community_Academy" title="Looe Community Academy">Looe Community Academy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Along the cliffs to the east is Plaidy Beach, and further on the bay and village of <a href="/wiki/Millendreath" title="Millendreath">Millendreath</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:West_looe_hill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/West_looe_hill.jpg/220px-West_looe_hill.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/West_looe_hill.jpg/330px-West_looe_hill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/West_looe_hill.jpg/440px-West_looe_hill.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1126" data-file-height="845" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:West_looe_hill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View down West Looe Hill, looking towards the harbour</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Looe">West Looe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: West Looe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>West Looe spreads west from the bridge on the Polperro Road towards <a href="/wiki/Sclerder" class="mw-redirect" title="Sclerder">Sclerder</a>, and along the river south of the bridge, with hotels, restaurants and boarding houses along the waterfront and houses climbing the perilous cliff above, towards a cluster of shops and businesses and the Church of St. Nicholas.\n</p><p>Further south along the coast road is Hannafore Point, marking the edge of Looe Harbour, with to the west the wide, stony Hannafore Beach, facing across a narrow channel to Looe Island. Beyond lies the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Coast_Path" title="South West Coast Path">coastal path</a> leading to <a href="/wiki/Portnadler_Bay" title="Portnadler Bay">Portnadler Bay</a>, Talland and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Porthallow_(Talland)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Porthallow (Talland) (page does not exist)">Porthallow</a>, and then onward to Polperro. Two towers mark one end of a <a href="/wiki/Nautical_measured_mile" title="Nautical measured mile">nautical measured mile</a>, the other end is marked by two towers near Talland Bay.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Year_festivities">New Year festivities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: New Year festivities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year&#39;s Eve">New Year\'s Eve</a>, Looe provides a surprisingly exciting and large celebration. The small fishing town, usually quiet in winter, due to the largely seasonal economy, is host to an influx of visitors. People flock to the streets in their hundreds, wearing fancy dress, a tradition upheld by all ages. The crowds begin the evening in the town and slowly move towards the seafront for a fireworks display to see in the <a href="/wiki/New_Year" title="New Year">New Year</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twinning">Twinning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Twinning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Looe is <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twinned</a> with <a href="/wiki/Quiberon" title="Quiberon">Quiberon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>: <i lang="br"><b>Kiberen</b></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Category:People_from_Looe" title="Category:People from Looe">People from Looe</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Looe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
