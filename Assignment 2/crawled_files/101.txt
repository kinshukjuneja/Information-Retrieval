War">Naval</a></li></ul>\n</div></td></tr></tbody></table></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Philadelphia campaign</b> (1777&#8211;1778) was a <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> initiative in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> to gain control of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, which was then the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a>.  British General <a href="/wiki/William_Howe,_5th_Viscount_Howe" title="William Howe, 5th Viscount Howe">William Howe</a>, after unsuccessfully attempting to draw the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> under General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> into a battle in northern <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, embarked his army on transports, and landed them at the northern end of <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>.  From there, he advanced northward toward <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.  Washington prepared defenses against Howe\'s movements at <a href="/wiki/Brandywine_Creek_(Christina_River)" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandywine Creek (Christina River)">Brandywine Creek</a>, but was flanked and beaten back in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brandywine" title="Battle of Brandywine">Battle of Brandywine</a> on September 11, 1777.  After further skirmishes and maneuvers, Howe was able to enter and occupy Philadelphia.  Washington then <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Germantown" title="Battle of Germantown">unsuccessfully attacked</a> one of Howe\'s garrisons at <a href="/wiki/Germantown,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Germantown, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Germantown</a> before retreating to <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge,_Pennsylvania" title="Valley Forge, Pennsylvania">Valley Forge</a> for the winter.\n</p><p>Howe\'s campaign was controversial because, although he successfully captured the American capital of Philadelphia, he proceeded slowly and did not aid the concurrent <a href="/wiki/Saratoga_campaign" title="Saratoga campaign">campaign of John Burgoyne</a> further north, which <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Saratoga" title="Battles of Saratoga">ended in disaster at Saratoga</a> for the British, and brought <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> into the war.  General Howe resigned during the occupation of Philadelphia and was replaced by his second-in-command, General Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Henry Clinton</a>.  Clinton evacuated the troops from Philadelphia back to New York City in 1778 in order to increase that city\'s defenses against a possible Franco-American attack.  Washington harried the British army all the way across New Jersey, and successfully forced <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monmouth" title="Battle of Monmouth">a battle</a> at <a href="/wiki/Freehold_Borough,_New_Jersey" title="Freehold Borough, New Jersey">Monmouth Court House</a> that was one of the largest battles of the war.\n</p><p>At the end of the campaign the two armies were roughly in the same positions they were at its beginning.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_York_and_New_Jersey_campaign" title="New York and New Jersey campaign">New York and New Jersey campaign</a></div>\n<p>Following General <a href="/wiki/William_Howe,_5th_Viscount_Howe" title="William Howe, 5th Viscount Howe">William Howe</a>\'s successful capture of New York City, and <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>\'s successful actions at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trenton" title="Battle of Trenton">Trenton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Princeton" title="Battle of Princeton">Princeton</a>, the two armies settled into an uneasy stalemate in the winter months of early 1777.  While this time was punctuated by <a href="/wiki/Forage_War" title="Forage War">numerous skirmishes</a>, the British army continued to occupy outposts at <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick,_New_Jersey" title="New Brunswick, New Jersey">New Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perth_Amboy,_New_Jersey" title="Perth Amboy, New Jersey">Perth Amboy, New Jersey</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lord_george_germaine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Lord_george_germaine.jpg/170px-Lord_george_germaine.jpg" width="170" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Lord_george_germaine.jpg/255px-Lord_george_germaine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Lord_george_germaine.jpg/340px-Lord_george_germaine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="918" data-file-height="1502" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lord_george_germaine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lord <a href="/wiki/George_Germain,_1st_Viscount_Sackville" title="George Germain, 1st Viscount Sackville">George Germain</a></div></div></div>\n<p>General Howe had proposed to <a href="/wiki/George_Germain,_1st_Viscount_Sackville" title="George Germain, 1st Viscount Sackville">George Germain</a>, the British civilian official responsible for conduct of the war, an expedition for 1777 to capture <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, the seat of the rebellious <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a>.  Germain approved his plan, although with fewer troops than Howe requested.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He also approved plans by <a href="/wiki/John_Burgoyne" title="John Burgoyne">John Burgoyne</a> for an expedition to "force his way to <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany</a>" from <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Germain\'s approval of Howe\'s expedition included the expectation that Howe would be able to assist Burgoyne, effecting a junction at Albany between the forces of Burgoyne and troops that Howe would send north from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Howe decided by early April against taking his army overland to Philadelphia through New Jersey, as this would entail a difficult crossing of the broad <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> under hostile conditions, and it would likely require the transportation or construction of the necessary watercraft.<sup id="cite_ref-M15_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M15-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Howe\'s plan, sent to Germain on April 2, also effectively isolated Burgoyne from any possibility of significant support, since Howe would be taking his army by sea to Philadelphia, and the New York garrison would be too small for any significant offensive operations up the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> to assist Burgoyne.<sup id="cite_ref-M15_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M15-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Howe.27s_evolving_plans"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Howe\'s_evolving_plans">Howe\'s evolving plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philadelphia_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Howe&#039;s evolving plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg/170px-WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg" width="170" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg/255px-WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg/340px-WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1088" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WilliamHowe1777ColorMezzotint.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>General Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Howe,_5th_Viscount_Howe" title="William Howe, 5th Viscount Howe">William Howe</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Washington realized that Howe "certainly ought in good policy to endeavor to Cooperate with Genl. Burgoyne" and was baffled why he did not do so.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Washington at the time and historians ever since have puzzled over the reason Howe was not in place to come to the relief of General <a href="/wiki/John_Burgoyne" title="John Burgoyne">John Burgoyne</a>, whose invasion army from Canada was surrounded and captured by the Americans in October.  Historians agree that Lord Germain did a poor job in coordinating the two campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Following Howe\'s capture of New York and Washington\'s retreat across the Delaware, Howe on December 20, 1776 wrote to Germain, proposing an elaborate set of campaigns for 1777.  These included operations to gain control of the Hudson River, expand operations from the base at <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, and take the seat of the rebel Continental Congress, Philadelphia.  The latter Howe saw as attractive, since Washington was then just north of the city: Howe wrote that he was "persuaded the Principal Army should act offensively [against Philadelphia], where the enemy\'s chief strength lies."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Germain acknowledged that this plan was particularly "well digested", but it called for more men than Germain was prepared to provide.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  After the setbacks in New Jersey, Howe in mid-January 1777 proposed operations against Philadelphia that included an overland expedition and a sea-based attack, thinking this might lead to a decisive victory over the Continental Army.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  This plan was developed to the extent that in April Howe\'s army was seen constructing pontoon bridges; Washington, lodged in his winter quarters at <a href="/wiki/Morristown,_New_Jersey" title="Morristown, New Jersey">Morristown, New Jersey</a>, thought they were for eventual use on the Delaware River.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  However, by mid-May Howe had apparently abandoned the idea of an overland expedition: "I propose to invade Pennsylvania by sea ... we must probably abandon the Jersies."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Howe\'s decision to not assist Burgoyne may have been rooted in Howe\'s perception that Burgoyne would receive credit for a successful campaign, even if it required Howe\'s help; this would not help Howe\'s reputation, as the Philadelphia expedition would if it succeeded.  Historian John Alden notes the jealousies among various British leaders, saying, "It is likely that [Howe] was as jealous of Burgoyne as Burgoyne was of him and that he was not eager to do anything which might assist his junior up the ladder of military renown."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Along the same lines Don Higginbotham concludes that in Howe\'s view, "[The Hudson River campaign] was Burgoyne\'s whole show, and consequently he [Howe] wanted little to do with it. With regard to Burgoyne\'s army, he would do only what was required of him (virtually nothing)."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Howe himself wrote to Burgoyne on July 17: "My intention is for Pennsylvania, where I expect to meet Washington, but if he goes to the northward contrary to my expectations, and you can keep him at bay, be assured I shall soon be after him to relieve you."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  He sailed from New York not long after.\n</p>\n<h2><span In part as a retaliatory measure against the ongoing skirmishes, General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a> executed <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bound_Brook" title="Battle of Bound Brook">a raid against that position</a> in April 1777, in which he very nearly captured the outpost\'s commander, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Lincoln" title="Benjamin Lincoln">Benjamin Lincoln</a>. In response to this raid, Washington moved his army forward to a strongly fortified <a href="/wiki/Middlebrook_encampment" title="Middlebrook encampment">position at Middlebrook</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Watchung_Mountains" title="Watchung Mountains">Watchung Mountains</a> that commanded likely British land routes toward Philadelphia.\n</p><p>For reasons that are not entirely clear, General Howe moved a sizable army to <a href="/wiki/Millstone,_New_Jersey" title="Millstone, New Jersey">Somerset Court House</a>, south of New Brunswick. If he performed this move as a <a href="/wiki/Feint" title="Feint">feint</a> to draw Washington out from his strong position, it failed, as Washington refused to move his army out in force. Washington had intelligence that Howe had not brought the necessary equipment for either bringing or constructing watercraft, so this move seemed unlikely to him to be a move toward the Delaware River. When Howe eventually withdrew his army back toward Perth Amboy, Washington did follow. Launching a lightning strike, Howe sent forces under Cornwallis in an attempt to cut Washington off from the high ground; this attempt was foiled in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Short_Hills" title="Battle of Short Hills">Battle of Short Hills</a>. Howe then withdrew his troops to Perth Amboy, embarked them on transports, and sailed out of New York harbor, destined for Philadelphia.\n</p><p>Washington did not know where Howe was going. Considering the possibility that Howe was again feinting, and would actually sail his army up the Hudson to join with Burgoyne, he remained near New York. Only when he received word that Howe\'s fleet had reached the mouth of the Delaware, did he need to consider the defense of Philadelphia. However, the fleet did not enter the Delaware, instead continuing south. Uncertain of Howe\'s goal, which could be <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, he considered moving north to assist in the defense of the Hudson, when he learned that the fleet had entered <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>. In August, he began moving his troops south to prepare the city\'s defenses. General <a href="/wiki/John_Sullivan_(general)" title="John Sullivan (general)">John Sullivan</a>, who commanded the Continental Army\'s troops facing <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>, had, in order to capitalize on perceived weaknesses of the British position there following Howe\'s departure, attempted a raid on August 22, that failed with the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Staten_Island" title="Battle of Staten Island">Battle of Staten Island</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capture_of_Philadelphia">Capture of Philadelphia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philadelphia_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Capture of Philadelphia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See <a href="/wiki/Brandywine_order_of_battle" title="Brandywine order of battle">Brandywine order of battle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germantown_order_of_battle" title="Germantown order of battle">Germantown order of battle</a> for the organizations and lists of regiments from both armies.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg/300px-PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg" width="300" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg/450px-PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg/600px-PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1510" data-file-height="1282" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PhiladelCampaignHessianMap.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hessian_(soldiers)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hessian (soldiers)">Hessian</a> map of the campaign from August 25 \xe2\x80\x93 September 26, 1777</div></div></div><p>General Howe landed 15,000 troops in late August at the northern end of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>, about 55 miles (90&#160;km) southwest of Philadelphia. General Washington positioned 11,000 men between Howe and Philadelphia but was <a href="/wiki/Flanking_maneuver" title="Flanking maneuver">outflanked</a> and driven back at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brandywine" title="Battle of Brandywine">Battle of Brandywine</a> on September 11, 1777 and suffered over 1,000 casualties, while the British lost about half that number.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> once again abandoned the city, relocating first to <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Pennsylvania" title="Lancaster, Pennsylvania">Lancaster, Pennsylvania</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/York,_Pennsylvania" title="York, Pennsylvania">York, Pennsylvania</a>. British and Revolutionary forces maneuvered around each other west of Philadelphia for the next several days, clashing in minor encounters such as the abortive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Clouds" title="Battle of the Clouds">Battle of the Clouds</a> and the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Paoli" title="Battle of Paoli">Paoli Massacre</a>." On September 26, Howe finally outmaneuvered Washington and marched into Philadelphia unopposed. Capture of the rebel capital did not bring the end to the rebellion as the British thought it would. In 18th Century warfare, it was normal that the side who captured the opposing force\'s capital city won the war. But the war was to continue for six more years (until 1783), given the unconventional warfare tactics of the rebels at the time.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia,_PA_Oct-Nov.,_1777.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia%2C_PA_Oct-Nov.%2C_1777.jpeg/220px-Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia%2C_PA_Oct-Nov.%2C_1777.jpeg" width="220" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia%2C_PA_Oct-Nov.%2C_1777.jpeg/330px-Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia%2C_PA_Oct-Nov.%2C_1777.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia%2C_PA_Oct-Nov.%2C_1777.jpeg/440px-Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia%2C_PA_Oct-Nov.%2C_1777.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="2998" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Operations_on_the_Delaware_River_at_Philadelphia,_PA_Oct-Nov.,_1777.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Operations on the Delaware River, Oct\xe2\x80\x93Nov, 1777</div></div></div><p>After taking the city, the British garrisoned about 9,000 troops in <a href="/wiki/Germantown,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Germantown, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Germantown</a>, five miles (8&#160;km) north of Philadelphia. Washington <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Germantown" title="Battle of Germantown">unsuccessfully attacked Germantown</a> on October 4, and then retreated to watch and wait for the British to counterattack. Meanwhile, the British needed a supply route along the Delaware River to support their occupation of Philadelphia. After a prolonged defense of the river by Commodore <a href="/wiki/John_Hazelwood" title="John Hazelwood">John Hazelwood</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Navy" title="Continental Navy">Continental Navy</a> the British finally secured the river by taking forts <a href="/wiki/Fort_Mifflin" title="Fort Mifflin">Mifflin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Mercer" title="Fort Mercer">Mercer</a> in November (although the latter was not taken until after <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Red_Bank" title="Battle of Red Bank">a humiliating repulse</a>).  In early December, Washington successfully repelled a series of probes by General Howe in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Marsh" title="Battle of White Marsh">Battle of White Marsh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>General Washington\'s problems at this time were not just with the British. In the so-called <a href="/wiki/Conway_Cabal" title="Conway Cabal">Conway Cabal</a>, some politicians and officers unhappy with Washington\'s performance in the campaign secretively discussed his removal. Washington, offended by the behind-the-scenes maneuvering, laid the whole matter openly before Congress. His supporters rallied behind him, and the episode was abated.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Valley_Forge_and_Monmouth">Valley Forge and Monmouth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philadelphia_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Valley Forge and Monmouth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monmouth" title="Battle of Monmouth">Battle of Monmouth</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_order_of_battle" title="Monmouth order of battle">Monmouth order of battle</a> for the organizations and lists of regiments from both armies.</div>\n<p>Washington and his army encamped at <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a> in December 1777, about 20 miles (32&#160;km) from Philadelphia, where they stayed for the next six months. Over the winter, 2,500 men (out of 10,000) died from disease and exposure. However, the army eventually emerged from Valley Forge in good order, thanks in part to a training program supervised by <a href="/wiki/Baron_von_Steuben" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron von Steuben">Baron von Steuben</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png/300px-The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png" width="300" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png/450px-The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png/600px-The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="616" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_March_to_Valley_Forge_William_Trego.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The March to Valley Forge</i> by <a href="/wiki/William_B._T._Trego" title="William B. T. Trego">William B. T. Trego</a>, 1883</div></div></div>\n<p>Meanwhile, there was a shakeup in the British command.  General Howe resigned his command, and was replaced by Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Sir Henry Clinton</a> as commander-in-chief. France\'s entry into the war forced a change in British war strategy, and Clinton was ordered by the government to abandon Philadelphia and defend New York City, now vulnerable to French naval power.  The British sent out <a href="/wiki/Carlisle_Peace_Commission" title="Carlisle Peace Commission">a peace commission</a> headed by the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Howard,_5th_Earl_of_Carlisle" title="Frederick Howard, 5th Earl of Carlisle">Earl of Carlisle</a>, whose offers, made in June 1778 as Clinton was preparing to abandon Philadelphia, were rejected by Congress.  As the British were preparing their withdrawal, Washington sent out <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_du_Motier,_marquis_de_Lafayette" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilbert du Motier, marquis de Lafayette">Lafayette</a> on a reconnaissance mission.  Lafayette narrowly escaped a British ambush at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Barren_Hill" title="Battle of Barren Hill">Battle of Barren Hill</a>.\n</p><p>Clinton shipped many Loyalists and most of his heavy equipment by sea to New York, and evacuated Philadelphia on June 18.  Washington\'s army shadowed Clinton\'s, and Washington successfully forced a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monmouth" title="Battle of Monmouth">battle at Monmouth Courthouse</a> on June 28, the last major battle in the North. Washington\'s second-in-command, General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lee_(general)" title="Charles Lee (general)">Charles Lee</a>, who led the advance force of the army, ordered a controversial retreat early in the battle, allowing Clinton\'s army to regroup. By July, Clinton was in New York City, and Washington was again at <a href="/wiki/White_Plains,_New_York" title="White Plains, New York">White Plains, New York</a>. Both armies were back where they had been two years earlier.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philadelphia_campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Shortly after the British arrived in New York, a French fleet arrived outside its harbor, leading to a flurry of action by both sides.  The French and Americans decided to make an attempt on the British garrison at <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>; this first attempt at coordination <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rhode_Island" title="Battle of Rhode Island">was a notable failure</a>.\n</p><p>Under orders from London, Clinton reallocated some of his troops to the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, and began a program of coastal raiding from the Chesapeake to Massachusetts.  In and around New York, the armies of Clinton and Washington watched each other and skirmished, with occasional major actions like the 1779 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stony_Point" title="Battle of Stony Point">Battle of Stony Point</a> and the 1780 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Connecticut_Farms" title="Battle of Connecticut Farms">Battle of Connecticut Farms</a>.  Clinton considered making new attacks on Philadelphia, but these ideas never came to fruition.\n</p><p>The British also began a wider frontier war organized from <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a>, using Loyalist and Native American allies.  British and French forces engaged each other in the West Indies and in India beginning in 1778, and the 1779 entry of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> into the war widened the global aspects of the war even further.\n</p><p>In 1780, the British began a "<a href="/wiki/Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War">southern strategy</a>" to regain control of the rebelling colonies,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Charleston" title="Siege of Charleston">capture of Charleston, South Carolina</a>.  This effort would ultimately <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown" title="Siege of Yorktown">fail at Yorktown</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
