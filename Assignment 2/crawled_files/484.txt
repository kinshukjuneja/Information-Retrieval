<a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">Gascon</a>: <i lang="oc" title="Occitan language text">Gasconha</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Occitan" title="Help:IPA/Occitan">[\xc9\xa1as\xcb\x88ku\xc9\xb2\xc9\x94]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a>: <i lang="eu">Gaskoinia</i>) is an area of southwest France that was part of the "<a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_France" title="Provinces of France">Province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guyenne" title="Guyenne">Guyenne</a> and Gascony" prior to the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. The region is vaguely defined, and the distinction between Guyenne and Gascony is unclear; by some they are seen to overlap, while others consider Gascony a part of Guyenne. Most definitions put Gascony east and south of Bordeaux.\n</p><p>It is currently divided between the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle-Aquitaine" title="Nouvelle-Aquitaine">Nouvelle-Aquitaine</a> (departments of <a href="/wiki/Landes_(department)" title="Landes (department)">Landes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es-Atlantiques" title="Pyr\xc3\xa9n\xc3\xa9es-Atlantiques">Pyr\xc3\xa9n\xc3\xa9es-Atlantiques</a>, southwestern <a href="/wiki/Gironde" title="Gironde">Gironde</a>, and southern <a href="/wiki/Lot-et-Garonne" title="Lot-et-Garonne">Lot-et-Garonne</a>) and the region of <a href="/wiki/Occitanie" title="Occitanie">Occitanie</a> (departments of <a href="/wiki/Gers" title="Gers">Gers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hautes-Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es" title="Hautes-Pyr\xc3\xa9n\xc3\xa9es">Hautes-Pyr\xc3\xa9n\xc3\xa9es</a>, southwestern <a href="/wiki/Tarn-et-Garonne" title="Tarn-et-Garonne">Tarn-et-Garonne</a>, and western <a href="/wiki/Haute-Garonne" title="Haute-Garonne">Haute-Garonne</a>). \n</p><p>Gascony was historically inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Basque_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque people">Basque</a>-related people who appear to have spoken a language similar to <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a>. The name Gascony comes from the same root as the word Basque (see <a href="/wiki/Gascony#Wasconia" title="Gascony">Wasconia</a> below). From medieval times until today, the <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">Gascon language</a> has been spoken, although it is classified as a regional variant of the <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">Occitan language</a>.\n</p><p>Gascony is the land of <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Batz-Castelmore_d%27Artagnan" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles de Batz-Castelmore d&#39;Artagnan">d\'Artagnan</a>, who inspired <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas,_p%C3%A8re" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexandre Dumas, p\xc3\xa8re">Alexandre Dumas</a>\'s character d\'Artagnan in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Musketeers" title="The Three Musketeers">The Three Musketeers</a></i>. Gascony, with the Pyrenees mountains in the far distance</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquitania">Aquitania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aquitania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">pre-Roman times</a>, the inhabitants of Gascony were the <a href="/wiki/Aquitanian_language" title="Aquitanian language">Aquitanians</a> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Aquitani</i>), who spoke a non-<a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European language</a> related to modern Basque. \n</p><p>The Aquitanians inhabited a territory limited to the north and east by the <a href="/wiki/Garonne_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Garonne River">Garonne River</a>, to the south by the Pyrenees mountain range, and to the west by the Atlantic Ocean. The Romans called this territory Aquitania, either from the Latin word <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">aqua</i> (meaning "water"), in reference to the many rivers flowing from the Pyrenees through the area, or from the name of the Aquitanian <a href="/wiki/Ausci" title="Ausci">Ausci</a> tribe, in which case Aquitania would mean "land of the Ausci". \n</p><p>In the 50s BC, Aquitania was conquered by lieutenants of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> and became part of the Roman Empire.\n</p><p>Later, in 27 BC, during the reign of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Caesar_Augustus" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesar Augustus">Augustus</a>, the province of <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Aquitania" title="Gallia Aquitania">Gallia Aquitania</a> was created. Gallia Aquitania was far larger than the original Aquitania, as it extended north of the Garonne River, in fact all the way north to the <a href="/wiki/Loire_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Loire River">Loire River</a>, thus including the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a> that inhabited the regions between the Garonne and the Loire rivers.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Novempopulana">Novempopulana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Novempopulana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 297, as Emperor <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> reformed the administrative structures of the Roman Empire, Aquitania was split into three provinces. The territory south of the Garonne River, corresponding to the original Aquitania, was made a province called <a href="/wiki/Novempopulania" title="Novempopulania">Novempopulania</a> (that is,  "land of the nine tribes"), while the part of Gallia Aquitania north of the Garonne became the province of <a href="/wiki/Aquitanica_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquitanica I">Aquitanica I</a> and the province of <a href="/wiki/Aquitanica_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquitanica II">Aquitanica II</a>. The territory of Novempopulania corresponded quite well to what we call now Gascony.\n</p><p>The Aquitania Novempopulana or <a href="/wiki/Novempopulania" title="Novempopulania">Novempopulania</a> suffered like the rest of the Western Roman Empire from the invasions of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> tribes, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a> in 407\xe2\x80\x93409. In 416\xe2\x80\x93418, Novempopulania was delivered to the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> as their federate settlement lands and became part of the Visigoth kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>, while other than the region of the Garonne river their actual grip on the area may have been rather loose. \n</p><p>The Visigoths were defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Frankish_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankish Empire">Franks</a> in 507, and fled into Spain and <a href="/wiki/Septimania" title="Septimania">Septimania</a>. Novempopulania then became part of the <a href="/wiki/Frankish_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankish Kingdom">Frankish Kingdom</a> like the rest of southern France. However, Novempopulania was far away from the home base of the Franks in northern France, and was only very loosely controlled by the Franks. During all the troubled and historically obscure period, starting from early 5th-century accounts, the <a href="/wiki/Bagaudae" title="Bagaudae">bagaudae</a> are often cited, social uprisings against tax exaction and feudalization, largely associated to Vasconic unrest.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wasconia">Wasconia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span href="/wiki/File:Vasconia_wide_740_3_-_80.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Duchy was meant to hold sway over the Basques (<i>Vascones</i>)</div></div></div>\n<p>Old historical literature sometimes claims the Basques took control of the whole of Novempopulania in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>, founding its claims on the testimony of <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Tours" title="Gregory of Tours">Gregory of Tours</a>, on the etymological link between the words "Basque" and "Gascon" \xe2\x80\x93 both derived from "Vascones" or "Wasconia", the latter being used to name the whole of Novempopulania.\n</p><p>Modern historians reject this hypothesis, which is sustained by no archeological evidence. For Juan Jos\xc3\xa9 Larrea, and Pierre Bonnassie, "a Vascon expansionism in Aquitany is not proved and is not necessary to understand the historical evolution of this region".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This Basque-related culture and race is, whatever the origin, attested in (mainly Carolingian) Medieval documents, while their exact boundaries remain unclear ("Wascones, qui trans Garonnam et circa Pirineum montem habitant", as stated in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Frankish_Annals" title="Royal Frankish Annals">Royal Frankish Annals</a>, for one).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The word <i>Vasconia</i> evolved into <i>Wasconia</i>, and then into <i>Gasconia</i> (w- often evolved into g- under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Romance_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Romance language">Romance languages</a>, cf. warranty and guarantee, William and Guillaume). The gradual abandonment of the Basque-related Aquitanian language in favor of a local <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">vulgar Latin</a>, was not reversed. The replacing local vulgar Latin evolved into Gascon. It was heavily influenced by the original Aquitanian language (for example, Latin f- became h-, cf. Latin <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">fortia</i>, French <i lang="fr" title="French language text">force</i>, Spanish <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">fuerza</i>, Occitan <i lang="oc" title="Occitan language text">f\xc3\xb2r\xc3\xa7a</i>, but Gascon <i lang="oc" title="Occitan language text">h\xc3\xb2r\xc3\xa7a</i>). Quite paradoxically (or logically) the Basques from the French side of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(historical_territory)" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque Country (historical territory)">Basque Country</a> traditionally call anyone who does not speak Basque a Gascon.\n</p><p>Meanwhile, Viking raiders conquered several Gascon towns, among them Bayonne in 842\xe2\x80\x93844. Their attacks in Gascony may have helped the political disintegration of the Duchy until their defeat against <a href="/wiki/William_II_S%C3%A1nchez_of_Gascony" class="mw-redirect" title="William II S\xc3\xa1nchez of Gascony">William II S\xc3\xa1nchez of Gascony</a> in 982. In turn, the weakened ethnic polity known as Duchy of Wasconia/Wascones, unable to get round the general spread of feudalization, gave way to a myriad of counties founded by Gascon lords.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angevin_Empire">Angevin Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Angevin Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin Empire</a></div>\n<p>His 1152 marriage to <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Aquitaine" title="Eleanor of Aquitaine">Eleanor of Aquitaine</a> allowed the future <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> to gain control of his new wife\'s possessions of <a href="/wiki/Aquitaine" title="Aquitaine">Aquitaine</a> and Gascony. This addition to his already plentiful holdings made Henry the <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England#Henry&#39;s_claims_by_blood_and_marriage" title="Henry II of England">most powerful vassal in France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvey_p.47_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey_p.47-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg/170px-Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg" width="170" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg/255px-Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg/340px-Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1093" data-file-height="1416" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hommage_of_Edward_I_to_Philippe_le_Bel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Homage of Edward I (kneeling) to Philip IV (seated)</div></div></div> \n<p>In 1248, <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_6th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester">Simon de Montfort</a> was appointed Governor in the unsettled Duchy of Gascony. Bitter complaints were excited by de Montfort\'s rigour in suppressing the excesses of both the <a href="/wiki/Fiefdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiefdom">seigneurs</a> of the nobility and the contending factions in the <a href="/wiki/Communes_of_France" title="Communes of France">great communes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III</a> yielded to the outcry and instituted a formal inquiry into Simon\'s administration. Simon was formally acquitted of the charges, but in August 1252 he was nevertheless dismissed. Henry then himself went to Gascony, pursuing a policy of conciliation; he arranged the marriage between <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward</a>, his 14-year-old son, and <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Castile" title="Eleanor of Castile">Eleanor of Castile</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_X_of_Castile" title="Alfonso X of Castile">Alfonso X</a>. Alfonso renounced all claims to Gascony and assisted the <a href="/wiki/Plantagenets" class="mw-redirect" title="Plantagenets">Plantagenets</a> against rebels such as <a href="/wiki/Gaston_de_Bearn" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaston de Bearn">Gaston de Bearn</a>, who had taken control of the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In December 1259, <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">Louis IX of France</a> ceded to Henry land north and east of Gascony.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In return, Henry renounced his claim to many of the territories that had been lost by <a href="/wiki/King_John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King John of England">King John</a>.  \n</p><p>In May 1286, King Edward I <a href="/wiki/Homage_(feudal)" title="Homage (feudal)">paid homage</a> before the new king, <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_France" title="Philip IV of France">Philip IV of France</a>, for the lands in Gascony. However, in May 1295, Philip "confiscated" the lands. Between 1295 and 1298, Edward <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_France#War_with_the_English" title="Philip IV of France">sent three expeditionary forces</a> to recover Gascony, but Philip was able to retain most of the territory until the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1303)" title="Treaty of Paris (1303)">Treaty of Paris in 1303</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1324 when <a href="/wiki/Edward_II_of_England" title="Edward II of England">Edward II of England</a>, in his capacity as <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Aquitaine" title="Duke of Aquitaine">Duke of Aquitaine</a>, failed to pay homage to the French king after <a href="/wiki/War_of_Saint-Sardos" title="War of Saint-Sardos">a dispute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_France" title="Charles IV of France">Charles IV</a> declared the duchy forfeit at the end of June 1324, and military action by the French followed. Edward sent his wife <a href="/wiki/Isabella_of_France" title="Isabella of France">Isabella</a>, who was sister to the French king, to negotiate a settlement. The Queen departed for France on 9 March 1325, and in September was joined by her son, the heir to the throne, Prince <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward</a> (later Edward III of England). Isabella\'s negotiations were successful, and it was agreed that the young Prince Edward would perform homage in the king\'s place, which he did on 24 September and so the duchy was returned to the English crown.<sup id="cite_ref-wilson34_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilson34-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When France\'s Charles IV died in 1328 leaving only daughters, his nearest male relative was Edward III of England, the son of Isabella, the sister of the dead king; but the question arose whether she could legally transmit the inheritance of the throne of France to her son even though she herself, as a woman, could not inherit the throne. The assemblies of the French barons and prelates and the University of Paris decided that males who derive their right to inheritance through their mother should be excluded. Thus the nearest heir through male ancestry was Charles IV\'s first cousin, Philip, Count of Valois, and it was decided that he should be crowned <a href="/wiki/Philip_VI_of_France" title="Philip VI of France">Philip VI of France</a>. Philip believed that Edward III was in breach of his obligations as vassal, so in May 1337 he met with his Great Council in Paris. It was agreed that Gascony should be taken back into Philip\'s hands, thus precipitating the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred Years War">Hundred Years War</a> between England and France.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> At the end of the Hundred Years\' War, after Gascony had changed hands several times, the English were finally defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Castillon" title="Battle of Castillon">Battle of Castillon</a> on 17 July 1453; Gascony remained French from then on.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
