title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a></b> is an <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceremonial county">administrative county</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The constitutional basis for the administration of Cornwall as part of England, is disputed as Duchy Charters<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> of 1337 place the governance of Cornwall with the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> rather than the <a href="/wiki/English_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="English Crown">English Crown</a>. \n</p><p>In ethnic and cultural terms, Cornwall and its inhabitants have at various times been referred to as "foreign" to England and the <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English people</a> in various ways, including by the English themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCStoyle_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCStoyle-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> One aspect of the distinct identity of Cornwall is the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a>, which survived into the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a> and has been revived in modern times.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCStoyle_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCStoyle-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cornish_nationalism" title="Cornish nationalism">Cornish nationalists</a> argue, whether from a legal, cultural or other basis, that Cornwall should have greater autonomy than the present administrative circumstances give. A manifestation of this is the campaign for a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish assembly">Cornish assembly</a>, along the lines of the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="National Assembly of Wales">Welsh</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish</a> legislative institutions. Those who assert that Cornwall is, or ought to be, separate from England do not necessarily advocate separation from the United Kingdom. An important aim is Cornwall\'s recognition as a British "home nation" in its own right similar to how <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> are considered.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_stories_and_tales">Ancient stories and tales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient stories and tales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An ancient tale, the legend of <a href="/wiki/Brutus_of_Troy" title="Brutus of Troy">Brutus</a>, recounted by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>, makes explicit reference to a distinct origin of the Cornish people. The legend tells how <a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a> was colonised by refugees from <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> under Brutus, who renamed his new kingdom <i>Britain</i>, and how the island was subsequently divided up between his three sons, the eldest inheriting <a href="/wiki/Loegria" class="mw-redirect" title="Loegria">Loegria</a> (roughly modern England, <i>Lloegr</i> in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>), the other two <a href="/wiki/Alba" title="Alba">Albania</a> (modern Scotland, <i>Alba</i> in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Cambria" title="Cambria">Cambria</a> (modern Wales, <i>Cymru</i> in Welsh). In addition, according to the legend, a second and smaller group of Trojans arrived in Britain, led by a warrior named <a href="/wiki/Corineus" title="Corineus">Corineus</a>, to whom Brutus granted extensive estates. Just as Brutus had "called the island Britain... and his companions Britons", so Corineus called "the region of the kingdom which had fallen to his share Cornwall, after the manner of his own name, and the people who lived there... Cornishmen". This indicates that, at least as far as Geoffrey was concerned, Cornwall possessed an identity distinct from the other parts of Britain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>.  Later, it was known to the Anglo-Saxons as <i>West Wales</i>, to distinguish it from North Wales, that is, modern-day Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>Cornwall</i> is a combination of two elements.  The second derives from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Anglo-Saxon</a> word <i><a href="/wiki/Walhaz" title="Walhaz">wealh</a></i>, meaning "foreigner", "one who speaks a non-Germanic language", which also survives in the words <i>Wales</i> and <i>Welsh</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The first element "Corn", indicating the shape of the peninsula, is descended from Celtic <i>kernou</i>, an Indo-European word related to English <i>horn</i> and Latin <i>cornu</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> records a battle in 825 and quotes "The Wealas (Cornish) and the Defnas (men of Devon) fought at Gafulforda". <a href="/wiki/Gafulforda" class="mw-redirect" title="Gafulforda">Gafulforda</a> is thought to be Galford near <a href="/wiki/Lew_Trenchard" title="Lew Trenchard">Lew Trenchard</a> on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/River_Lew" title="River Lew">River Lew</a> (tributary of the Lyd), though some translations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\nrender it as <a href="/wiki/Camelford" title="Camelford">Camelford</a>, some 60&#160;km further west.\n</p><p>References in contemporary charters (for which there is either an original manuscript or an early copy regarded as authentic) show <a href="/wiki/Egbert_of_Wessex" class="mw-redirect" title="Egbert of Wessex">Egbert of Wessex</a> (802-39) granting lands in Cornwall at <a href="/wiki/Kilkhampton" title="Kilkhampton">Kilkhampton</a>, <i>Ros</i>, <a href="/wiki/Maker,_Cornwall" title="Maker, Cornwall">Maker</a>, Pawton (in <a href="/wiki/St_Breock" title="St Breock">St Breock</a>, not far from Wadebridge, head manor of <a href="/wiki/Pydarshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Pydarshire">Pydar</a> in Domesday Book), <i>Caellwic</i> (perhaps <a href="/wiki/Celliwig" title="Celliwig">Celliwig</a> or Kellywick in <a href="/wiki/Egloshayle" title="Egloshayle">Egloshayle</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Lawhitton" title="Lawhitton">Lawhitton</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sherborne" title="Sherborne">Sherborne</a> Abbey and to the Bishop of Sherborne.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> All of the identifiable locations except Pawton are in the far east of Cornwall, so these references show a degree of West Saxon control over its eastern fringes. Such control had certainly been established in places by the later ninth century, as indicated by the will of King Alfred the Great (871-99).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Apart from the reference to Egbert\'s grant at Pawton there is no indication that English rule extended deep into Cornwall at this stage and the absence of any burhs west of Lydford in the <a href="/wiki/Burghal_Hidage" title="Burghal Hidage">Burghal Hidage</a> may suggest limitations on the authority of the Kingdom of Wessex in parts of Cornwall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/King_Athelstan" class="mw-redirect" title="King Athelstan">King Athelstan</a>, who came to the throne of England in 924 CE, immediately began a campaign to consolidate his power, and by about 926 had taken control of the Kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a>, following which he established firm boundaries with other kingdoms such as <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and Cornwall. The latter agreement, according to 12th century <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> historian <a href="/wiki/William_of_Malmesbury" title="William of Malmesbury">William of Malmesbury</a>, ended rights of residence for Cornish subjects in <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>, and fixed the Cornish boundary at the east bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">River Tamar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At Easter 928, Athelstan held court at Exeter, with the Welsh and "West Welsh" subject rulers present,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and by 931 he had appointed a bishop for Cornwall within the English church (i.e. subject to the authority of the Archbishop of Canterbury).<sup id="cite_ref-orme_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orme-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Bodmin_manumissions" title="Bodmin manumissions">Bodmin manumissions</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> two to three generations later, show that the ruling class of Cornwall quickly became "Anglicised", most owners of slaves having Anglo-Saxon names (not necessarily because they were of English descent; some at least were Cornish nobles who changed their names). Among those manumitting (releasing) slaves in the Bodmin record are four English kings, but no Cornish kings, dukes or earls.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>It is clear that at this time areas beyond the core of Anglo-Saxon settlement were recognised as different by the English kings. Athelstan\'s successor, Edmund, in a charter for an estate just north of Exeter,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> styled himself as "King of the English, and ruler of this province of Britons." Edmund\'s successor Edgar styled himself, "King of the English and ruler of the adjacent nations." This was followed by king Aethelred II (978-1016) describing Cornwall not as an English shire, but as a province, or client territory.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Surviving charters issued by the Kings of England <a href="/wiki/Edmund_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Edmund I of England">Edmund I</a> (939-46), <a href="/wiki/Edgar_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Edgar of England">Edgar</a> (959-75), <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Martyr" title="Edward the Martyr">Edward the Martyr</a> (975-8), <a href="/wiki/Ethelred_the_Unready" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethelred the Unready">Aethelred II</a> (978-1016), <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Ironside" title="Edmund Ironside">Edmund II</a> (1016), <a href="/wiki/Canute_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Canute the Great">Cnut</a> (1016\xe2\x80\x9335) and <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> (1042\xe2\x80\x9366) record grants of land in Cornwall made by these kings. In contrast to the easterly concentration of the estates held or granted by English kings in the ninth century, the tenth and eleventh-century grants were widely distributed across Cornwall. As is usual with charters of this period, the authenticity of some of these documents is open to question (though Della Hooke has established high reliability for the Cornish material), but that of others (e.g., Edgar\'s grant of estates at <a href="/wiki/Tywarnhaile" class="mw-redirect" title="Tywarnhaile">Tywarnhaile</a> and <i>Bosowsa</i> to his <a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">thane</a> Eanulf in 960, Edward the Confessor\'s grant of estates at <a href="/wiki/Traboe" title="Traboe">Traboe</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trevallack&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trevallack (page does not exist)">Trevallack</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grugwith&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grugwith (page does not exist)">Grugwith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trethewey" title="Trethewey">Trethewey</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ealdred_(archbishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ealdred (archbishop)">Bishop Ealdred</a> in 1059) is not in any doubt. Some of these grants include exemptions from obligations to the crown which would otherwise accompany land ownership, while retaining others, including those regarding military service. Assuming that these documents are authentic, the attachment of these obligations to the King of England to ownership of land in Cornwall suggests that the area was under his direct rule and implies that the legal and administrative relationship between the king and his subjects was the same there as elsewhere in his kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1051, with the exile of <a href="/wiki/Godwin,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Godwin, Earl of Wessex">Godwin, Earl of Wessex</a> and his sons and the forfeiture of their earldoms, a man named <a href="/wiki/Odda,_Ealdorman_of_Devon" title="Odda, Ealdorman of Devon">Odda</a> was appointed earl over a portion of the lands thus vacated: this comprised <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, Somerset, <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, and "Wealas".<sup id="cite_ref-swanton_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swanton-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> As <i>Wealas</i> is Saxon for foreigners, this could mean "West Wales"\xe2\x80\x94that is, Cornwall\xe2\x80\x94or it could mean that he was overlord of the Cornish foreigners in Devon or elsewhere.\n</p><p>\nElizabethan historian <a href="/wiki/William_Camden" title="William Camden">William Camden</a>, in the Cornish section of his <i>Britannia</i>, notes that </p><blockquote>\n<p>"As for the Earles, none of British bloud are mentioned but onely Candorus (called by others Cadocus), who is accounted by the late writers the last Earle of Cornwall of British race".\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_conquest_and_after">Norman conquest and after</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Norman conquest and after">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cornwall was included in the survey, initiated by <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>, the first Norman king of England, which became known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i>, where it is included as being part of the Norman king\'s new domain. Cornwall was unusual as Domesday records no Saxon <i><a href="/wiki/Burh" title="Burh">burh</a></i>; a <i>burh</i> (borough) was the Saxons\' centre of legal and administrative power. Moreover, nearly all land was held by one person, William\'s half-brother <a href="/wiki/Robert_of_Mortain" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert of Mortain">Robert of Mortain</a>, who may have been the first Norman to bear the title <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Cornwall" title="Earl of Cornwall">Earl of Cornwall</a>. He held his Cornish lands not as a Tenant in Chief of the King, as was the case with other landowners, but as de facto Viceroy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>F. M. Stenton tells us<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> that the early Norman compilation known as \'The Laws of William the Conqueror\' records all regions under West Saxon Law. These included Kent, Surrey, Sussex, Berkshire, Hampshire, Wiltshire, Dorset, Somerset and Devon. Cornwall is not recorded as being under West Saxon, or English, law.\n</p><p>Ingulf was secretary to William the Conqueror and after 1066 was appointed Abbot of Croyland. When his church burned down, he established a fund raising committee to rebuild it. Ingulfs Chronicle tells us:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Having obtained this indulgence, he now opened the foundation for the new church, and sent throughout the whole of England, and into lands adjoining and beyond the sea, letters testimonial. To the Northern parts and into Scotland he sent the brothers Fulk and Oger, and into Denmark and Norway the brothers Swetman and Wulsin; while to Wales, Cornwall and Ireland he sent the brothers Augustin and Osbert." See Ingulf Chronicle of the Abbey of Croyland with the continuation of Peter of Blois, trans Henry T. Riley (London: Henry G Bohn, 1854) pp.&#160;229\xe2\x80\x93227</dd></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_of_Huntingdon" title="Henry of Huntingdon">Henry of Huntingdon</a>, writing about 1129, included Cornwall in his list of shires of England in his <i>History of the English</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On the <a href="/wiki/Hereford_Mappa_Mundi" title="Hereford Mappa Mundi">Mappa Mundi</a>, circa 1300, now in <a href="/wiki/Hereford_Cathedral" title="Hereford Cathedral">Hereford Cathedral</a>, Cornwall (as "Cornubia") is one of the very few regions within Britain to be named individually. The significance and relevance of this is unclear; the map belongs to a category of map known as <a href="/wiki/Mappa_mundi#Complex_maps" title="Mappa mundi">Complex (Great) World Maps</a> and its depiction, within such a world context, should be seen in parallel with related contemporaneous material.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The phrase "England and Cornwall" (<i>Anglia et Cornubia</i>) has been used on occasion in post-Norman official documents referring to the Duchy of Cornwall:\n</p><p>Extracted from a commission of the first Duke of Cornwall, 1351:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"25 Edw. III to "John Dabernoun, our Steward and Sheriff of Cornwall greeting. On account of certain escheats we command you that you inquire by all the means in your power how much land and rents, goods and chattels, whom and in whom, and of what value they which those persons of Cornwall and England have, whose names we send in a schedule enclosed...."</p></blockquote>\n<p><a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">William Caxton</a>\'s 1480 <i>Description of Britain</i> debated whether or not Cornwall should be shown as separate to, or part of, England.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tudor_Period">Tudor Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tudor Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Italian <a href="/wiki/Polydore_Vergil" title="Polydore Vergil">Polydore Vergil</a> in his <i>Anglica Historia</i>, published in 1535 wrote that four peoples speaking four different languages inhabited Britain:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"the whole Countrie of Britain... is divided into iiii partes; whereof the one is inhabited of <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">Englishmen</a>, the other of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottes</a>, the third of <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Wallshemen</a>, [and] the fowerthe of <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornishe people</a>, which all differ emonge them selves, either in tongue, ... in manners, or ells in lawes and ordinaunces."</p></blockquote>\n<p>During the Tudor period some travellers regarded the Cornish as a separate cultural group, from which some modern observers conclude that they were a separate ethnic group. For example, Lodovico Falier, an Italian diplomat at the Court of Henry VIII said, "The language of the English, Welsh and Cornish men is so different that they do not understand each other." He went on to give the alleged \'national characteristics\' of the three peoples, saying for example "the Cornishman is poor, rough and boorish".\n</p><p>Another example is Gaspard de Coligny Ch\xc3\xa2tillon \xe2\x80\x93 the French Ambassador in London \xe2\x80\x93 who wrote saying that England was not a united whole as it "contains Wales and Cornwall, natural enemies of the rest of England, and speaking a different language." His use of the phrase "the rest of"  implies that he believed Cornwall and Wales to be part of England in his sense of the word.\n</p><p>Some maps of the British Isles prior to the 17th century showed Cornwall (Cornubia/Cornwallia) as a territory on a par with Wales. However most post-date the incorporation of Wales as a principality of England. Examples include the maps of\n<a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Munster" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebastian Munster">Sebastian Munster</a> (1515),<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Ortelius" title="Abraham Ortelius">Abraham Ortelius</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Ruscelli" class="mw-redirect" title="Girolamo Ruscelli">Girolamo Ruscelli</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Maps that depict Cornwall as a county of the Kingdom of England and Wales include\n<a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">Gerardus Mercator</a>\'s 1564 atlas of Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Saxton" title="Christopher Saxton">Christopher Saxton</a>\'s 1579 map authorised by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A miniature "epitome" of Ortelius\' map of England &amp; Wales, published in 1595, names Cornwall; the same map displays Kent in an equivalent manner.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Maps of Britain which display Cornwall usually in their legends do not refer to Cornwall, e.g. Lily 1548.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_and_18th_centuries">17th and 18th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 17th and 18th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Recognition that several peoples lived within Britain and Ireland continued through the 17th century. For example, after the death of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> in 1603, the Venetian ambassador wrote that the late queen had ruled over five different \'peoples\': "English, Welsh, Cornish, Scottish... and Irish".\n</p><p>Writing in 1616, diplomat Arthur Hopton stated:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"England is... divided into 3 great Provinces, or Countries... every of them speaking a several and different language, as English, Welsh and Cornish."</p></blockquote>\n<p>Wales was effectively annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> in the 16th century by the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542</a>, but references to \'England\' in law were not presumed to include Wales (or indeed <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a>) until the <a href="/wiki/Wales_and_Berwick_Act_1746" title="Wales and Berwick Act 1746">Wales and Berwick Act 1746</a>. By this time the use of "England and Cornwall" (<i>Anglia et Cornubia</i>) had ceased.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Because of the tendency of historians to trust the work of their predecessors, Geoffrey of Monmouth\'s semi-fictional 12th-century <i>Historia Regum Britanniae</i> remained influential for centuries, often used by writers who were unaware that his work was the source. For example, in 1769 the antiquary <a href="/wiki/William_Borlase" title="William Borlase">William Borlase</a> wrote the following, which is actually a summary of a passage from Geoffrey [Book iii:1]:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"Of this time we are to understand what Edward I. says (Sheringham. [<i>De Anglorum Gentis Origine</i>] p. 129.) that Britain, Wales, and Cornwall, were the portion of <a href="/wiki/Belinus" title="Belinus">Belinus</a>, elder son of <a href="/wiki/Dunwallo" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunwallo">Dunwallo</a>, and that that part of the Island, afterwards called England, was divided in three shares, viz. Britain, which reached from the <a href="/wiki/River_Tweed" title="River Tweed">Tweed</a>, Westward, as far as the <a href="/wiki/River_Exe" title="River Exe">river Ex</a>; Wales inclosed by the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">Severn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Wales" title="River Dee, Wales">Dee</a>; and Cornwall from the river Ex to the <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land&#39;s End">Land\'s-End</a>".</p></blockquote>\n<p>Another 18th-century writer, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Gough_(antiquarian)" title="Richard Gough (antiquarian)">Richard Gough</a>, concentrated on a contemporary viewpoint, noting that "Cornwall seems to be another Kingdom", in his "Camden\'s Britannia", 2nd ed.(4 vols; London, 1806).\n</p><p>During the eighteenth century, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Johnson" title="Samuel Johnson">Samuel Johnson</a> created an ironic Cornish <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_independence" title="Declaration of independence">declaration of independence</a> that he used in his essay <i>Taxation no Tyranny</i><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> His irony starts:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"As political diseases are naturally contagious, let it be supposed, for a moment, that Cornwall, seized with the Philadelphian phrensy, may resolve to separate itself from the general system of the English constitution, and judge of its own rights in its own parliament. A congress might then meet at Truro, and address the other counties in a style not unlike the language of the American patriots.... We are the acknowledged descendants of the earliest inhabitants of Britain, of men, who, before the time of history, took possession of the island desolate and waste, and, therefore, open to the first occupants. Of this descent, our language is a sufficient proof, which, not quite a century ago, was different from yours."</dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Popular Cornish sentiment during the 19th century appears to have been still strong. For example, <a href="/wiki/A._K._Hamilton_Jenkin" title="A. K. Hamilton Jenkin">A. K. Hamilton Jenkin</a> records the reaction of a school pupil who was asked to describe Cornwall\'s situation replied: "he\'s kidged to a furren country from the top hand" \xe2\x80\x93 i.e., "it\'s joined to a foreign country from the upper part". This reply was "heard by the whole school with <i>much approval</i>, including old Peggy (the school-dame) herself."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The famous crime writer <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a> described Cornwall as:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"a county where, it must be remembered, a stranger is doubly a stranger, in relation to his provincial sympathies; where the national feeling is almost entirely merged into the local feeling; where a man speaks of himself as <i>Cornish</i> in much the same way that a Welshman speaks of himself as Welsh."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p><i>Chambers\' Journal</i> in 1861 described Cornwall as "one of the most un-English of English counties."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> \xe2\x80\x93 a sentiment echoed by the naturalist <a href="/wiki/W.H._Hudson" class="mw-redirect" title="W.H. Hudson">W.H. Hudson</a> who also referred to it as "un-English" and said there were:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"[few] Englishmen in Cornwall who do not experience that antipathy or sense of separation in mind from the people they live with, and are not looked upon as foreigners"<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>Until the \'Tin Duties Abolition Act 1838\', the Cornish miner was charged twice the level of taxation compared to the English miner. The English practice of charging \'foreigners\' double taxation had existed in Cornwall for over 600 years prior to the 1836 Act and was first referenced in William de Wrotham\'s letter of 1198AD, published in G.R. Lewis, The Stannaries [1908]. The campaigning West Briton Newspaper called the racially applied tax "oppresive and vexatious" [19 January 1838]. In 1856 the Westminster Parliament was still able to refer to the Cornish as <i>aboriginals</i>. In Cornish, they were called <i>kevrangow</i> (sing. <i>kevrang</i>).\n</p><p>Although the name "shire", today implies some kind of county status, hundreds in some English counties often bore the suffix \'shire\' as well (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Salford_(hundred)" class="mw-redirect" title="Salford (hundred)">Salfordshire</a>), but where English shires were split into hundreds each having their own constable, Cornish hundreds had constables at parish level.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Kevrangow were not however, English hundreds: <i>Triggshire</i> came from Tricori \'three warbands\', suggesting a military muster area capable of supporting three hundred fighting men. However it must be said that this is an inference from name alone, and does not constitute historical evidence of any fighting force raised by a Cornish hundred.\n</p><p>The Cornish kevrang replicated England\'s shire system on a smaller scale.  Although by the 15th century the shires of Cornwall had become hundreds, the administrative differences remained in place long after.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Constitutional_status_.E2.80.93_arguments_on_each_side"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_status_\xe2\x80\x93_arguments_on_each_side">Constitutional status \xe2\x80\x93 arguments on each side</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Constitutional status \xe2\x80\x93 arguments on each side">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_administrative_status">Current administrative status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Current administrative status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Along with other English counties, Cornwall was established as an <a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties_of_England" title="Administrative counties of England">administrative county</a> under the changes introduced in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>, which came into effect on 1 April 1889. This was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">non-metropolitan county</a> of Cornwall in 1974 by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, which includes it under the heading of "England".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" data-file-height="668" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EmpireNorth.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At the time of <a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>During the latter part of the pre-Norman period, the eastern seaboard of modern-day England became increasingly under the sway of the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Forkbeard" title="Sweyn Forkbeard">Sweyn Forkbeard</a>, the first Danish King of England, died a few weeks after his English opponent <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_the_Unready" title="\xc3\x86thelred the Unready">\xc3\x86thelred the Unready</a> had fled, so it is probable that he never properly took control of Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> His son <a href="/wiki/Canute" class="mw-redirect" title="Canute">Canute</a> never properly conquered or controlled Scotland or Wales,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> but he appears to have had some authority in Cornwall, for in 1027 his counsellor <a href="/wiki/Lyfing_of_Winchester" title="Lyfing of Winchester">Lyfing of Winchester</a> (already bishop of Crediton) was appointed as <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Cornwall" title="Bishop of Cornwall">bishop of Cornwall</a> (<a href="/wiki/St_Germans,_Cornwall" title="St Germans, Cornwall">St Germans</a>), beginning the merger which would later form the See of Exeter. The map pictured, by William R. Shepherd (1926), shows Cornwall as <i>not</i> part of Canute\'s realm, but this approach is not followed by more recent scholarship, such as David Hill\'s <i>An Atlas of Anglo-Saxon England</i>(1981). Ultimately, the Danes\' control of Wessex was lost in 1042 with the death of both of Canute\'s sons (Edward the Confessor retook Wessex for the Anglo-Saxons).\n</p><p>On 12 February 1857, during the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Foreshore_Case" title="Cornish Foreshore Case">Cornish Foreshore dispute</a>, the Attorney General to the Duchy of Cornwall stated that whether it was held by a viceroy, by the Crown or granted to family or favourites, the Earldom of Cornwall (<i>Comitatus Cornubi\xc7\xbd</i>) included all territorial revenues, rights and property which were held "as of the Honor". He then outlined how, when entrusted to the Crown, Cornwall was held not <i>jure coron\xc7\xbd</i> but <i>jure Comitatus</i> \xe2\x80\x93 or <i>jure Ducatus</i>, when augmented to a Duchy \xe2\x80\x93 as of the Honor <i>in manu Regis existente</i>. [See foreshore dispute papers]<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 1328 the Earldom of Cornwall, extinct since the disgrace and execution of Piers Gaveston in 1312, was recreated and awarded to <a href="/wiki/John_of_Eltham,_Earl_of_Cornwall" title="John of Eltham, Earl of Cornwall">John</a>, younger brother of <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">King Edward III</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In September 1336, shortly before he was due to marry, John died, so his heir was his brother the King, who at the beginning of March the following year proposed to Parliament that the Earldom should become a Royal Duchy, to, in the words of the Royal Declaration that preceded the Charter, "restore notable places of the realm to their pristine honours". This was agreed, and put into law by a <a href="/wiki/Materials_in_cornish_law#CHARTER_11_Edward_III._The_Great_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Materials in cornish law">charter dated 17 March 1337</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Materials_in_cornish_law#Charter_11_Edward_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Materials in cornish law">second charter</a>, immediately following the "Great Charter," attempted to clarify the Duke\'s rights specifically within the County of Cornwall. When the first Duke of Cornwall came of age in 1351, one of his first official acts was to carry out his own form of Domesday survey (Commission 25 Edward III).\n</p><p>Some say that before the creation of the Duchy, the assets of the Earl of Cornwall (including privileges such as bailiff rights, stannaries and wrecks) were subject to <a href="/wiki/Escheat" title="Escheat">Crown escheat</a>, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Edmund,_2nd_Earl_of_Cornwall" title="Edmund, 2nd Earl of Cornwall">Edmund, 2nd Earl of Cornwall</a> (died 1300).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> However, records contained within the foreshore dispute papers show that entry into Cornwall for the King\'s Escheator was often barred on grounds that the King\'s writ does not run in Cornwall. For example, records of the Launceston Eyre of 1284 show Edmund successfully resisting the King\'s attempted assertion of escheat rights over Cornwall. Edmund\'s advocate opened his plea with the words, <i>"my liege lord hols Corrnwall above the Lord King in Chief....so the Escheator of the Lord the King shall not intermeddle in anything belonging to the Sheriff of Cornwall</i>". That same year Edmund is confirmed as having \'right of wreck\' in Cornwall [Coram Regis Rolls 14 Edw.1 Easter No.99, M29d \xe2\x80\x93 Foreshore dispute papers].<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The Council for Racial Equality in Cornwall website states: "Cornwall retains a unique and distinct constitutional relationship with the Crown, based on the Duchy of Cornwall and the Stannaries. For other purposes it is recognised as a Celtic region or nation and enjoys its own national flag."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 14 July 2009, <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rogerson" title="Dan Rogerson">Dan Rogerson</a> MP, of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>, presented a Cornish \'breakaway\' bill to the Parliament in Westminster \xe2\x80\x93 \'The Government of Cornwall Bill\'. The bill proposes a devolved Assembly for Cornwall, similar to the Welsh and Scottish setup. The bill states that Cornwall should re-assert its rightful place within the United Kingdom. Rogerson argued that "that there is a political and social will for Cornwall to be recognised as its own nation. Constitutionally, Cornwall has the right to a level of self-Government. If the Government is going to recognise the right of Scotland and Wales to greater self-determination because of their unique cultural and political positions, then they should recognise ours."<sup Scotland is excluded, and so are nominally English areas then under Scottish control, such as Northumberland and most of Cumberland. Wales is also excluded, except for areas the Normans had managed to capture, such as Flintshire. Cornwall is not excluded, and, unlike, for example, the later <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> (parts of which were listed with <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>, other parts with <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>) is given a listing in the normal Domesday county-based style.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The records of the medieval eyres, the court sessions of the king\'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=Itinerant_judge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Itinerant judge (page does not exist)">itinerant judges</a>. Maitland FW (1888) <i>Select pleas of the crown</i> prints examples from Cornwall. The eyre records show Cornwall and England with common judicial arrangements from the police duties of tithings at the lowest level of administration to the highest itinerant courts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Examples are the inquiries into the use of the English-controlled port of Calais in 1474 (when officials of all counties, including Cornwall, were required to submit returns),<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> the King granting licences to trade to people in Cornwall in 1364, the Duke of Cornwall complaining in 1371 to the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">King\'s Council</a> about offences by some local men in Cornwall, and in 1380 the King\'s Council ordering the Sheriff of Cornwall to arrest and imprison an offender.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Duchy_of_Cornwall">The Duchy of Cornwall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: The Duchy of Cornwall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg/200px-Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg.png" width="200" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg/300px-Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg/400px-Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="360" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_Duke_of_Cornwall.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The banner of the Duchy of Cornwall</div></div></div>\n<p>The duchy was established in 1337 out of the former earldom of Cornwall by Edward III of England for his son, Edward, Prince of Wales, the "Black Prince", who became the first Duke of Cornwall. The Duke obtained greater rights over Cornwall than the Earls had previously exercised. These increased powers over Cornwall included the right to appoint <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="High Sheriff">Sheriffs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bona_vacantia" title="Bona vacantia">bona vacantia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Treasure_trove" title="Treasure trove">treasure trove</a>, a separate exchequer, and such forth. Most of these rights are still exercised by the Duchy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Kilbrandon Report</a> (1969&#8211;1971) into the British constitution recommends that, when referring to <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, official sources should "on all appropriate occasions" use the designation of Duchy when referring to Cornwall itself, in recognition of its "special relationship" with the Crown.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 1780 <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a> sought to curtail further the power of the Crown by removing the various principalities which he said existed as different aspects of the monarchy within the country:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>\nCross a brook, and you lose the King of England; but you have some comfort in coming again under his Majesty, though \'shorn of his beams,\' and no more than Prince of Wales. Go to the north, and you find him dwindled to a Duke of Lancaster; turn to the west of that north, and he pops upon you in the humble character of Earl of Chester. Travel a few miles on, the Earl of Chester disappears, and the King surprises you again as Count Palatine of Lancaster. If you travel beyond Mount Edgecombe, you find him once more in his incognito, and he is Duke of Cornwall... every one of those Principalities has the apparatus of a Kingdom for the jurisdiction over a few private estates, and the formality and charge of the Exchequer of Great Britain for collecting the rents of a country squire. Cornwall is the best of them.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2015)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> including <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Solidarity" title="Cornish Solidarity">Cornish Solidarity</a> and the group claiming to be the <a href="/wiki/Revived_Cornish_Stannary_Parliament" title="Revived Cornish Stannary Parliament">Revived Cornish Stannary Parliament</a>, argue that Cornwall has a <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> status apart as a sovereign Duchy extraterritorial to England. A commonly cited basis for this argument is a case of arbitration between the Crown and the Duchy of Cornwall (1856\xe2\x80\x931857) in which the Officers of the Duchy successfully argued that the Duchy enjoyed many of the rights and prerogatives of a <a href="/wiki/County_palatine" title="County palatine">County palatine</a> and that although the duke was not granted Royal Jurisdiction, was considered to be quasi-sovereign within his Duchy of Cornwall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The arbitration, as instructed by the Crown, was based on legal argument and documentation, led to the Cornwall Submarine Mines Act of 1858.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>  The Officers of the Duchy, based on its researches, made this submission:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<ol><li>"That Cornwall, like Wales, was at the time of the Conquest, and was subsequently treated in many respects as distinct from England.</li>\n<li>"That it was held by the Earls of Cornwall with the rights and prerogative of a County Palatine, as far as regarded the Seignory or territorial dominion.</li>\n<li>"That the Dukes of Cornwall have from the creation of the Duchy enjoyed the rights and prerogatives of a County Palatine, as far as regarded seignory or territorial dominion, and that to a great extent by Earls.</li>\n<li>"That when the Earldom was augmented into a Duchy, the circumstances attending to its creation, as well as the language of the Duchy Charter, not only support and confirm natural presumption, that the new and higher title was to be accompanied with at least as great dignity, power, and prerogative as the Earls enjoyed, but also afforded evidence that the Duchy was to be invested with still more extensive rights and privileges.</li>\n<li>"The Duchy Charters have always been construed and treated, not merely by the Courts of Judicature, but also by the Legislature of the Country, as having vested in the Dukes of Cornwall the whole territorial interest and dominion of the Crown in and over the entire County of Cornwall."</li></ol>\n</blockquote><p>\nHowever, the term \'<a href="/wiki/County_palatine" title="County palatine">county palatine</a>\' appears not to have been used historically of Cornwall, and the duchy did not have as much autonomy as the <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Palatine of Durham</a>, which was ruled by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Prince-Bishop of Durham</a>. However, whilst not specifically called a county palatine, the Officers of the Duchy made the observation (Duchy Preliminary Statement \xe2\x80\x93 Cornish Foreshore Dispute 1856): </p><blockquote><p>"The Dukes also had their own escheators in Cornwall, and it is deserving of notice that in the saving clause of the Act of Escheators, 1 Henry VIII., c. 8, s. 5 (as is the case in numerous other acts of Parliament), the Duchy of Cornwall is classed with counties undoubtedly palatinate."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Stannaries_and_their_revival">The Stannaries and their revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The Stannaries and their revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Stannary_Courts_and_Parliaments" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary Courts and Parliaments">Stannary Courts and Parliaments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Revived_Cornish_Stannary_Parliament" title="Revived Cornish Stannary Parliament">Revived Cornish Stannary Parliament</a></div>\n<p>In 1974, a group has claimed to be a revived Cornish <a href="/wiki/Stannary_Courts_and_Parliaments" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary Courts and Parliaments">Stannary</a> Parliament and have the ancient right of Cornish tin-miners\' assemblies to veto legislation from <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a>, although it opposed the Duchy of Cornwall. of Cornwall</a></div>\n<p>An early campaign for an independent Cornwall was put forward during the first <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Grenville,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Richard Grenville, 1st Baronet">Sir Richard Grenville, 1st Baronet</a>. He tried to use "Cornish particularist sentiment" to gather support for the Royalist cause. The Cornish were fighting for their Royalist privileges, notably the <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">Duchy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stannaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannaries">Stannaries</a> and he put a plan to the Prince which would, if implemented, have created a semi-independent Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In contrast to the arguments that Cornwall is already <i>de jure</i> autonomous, thanks to the Duchy and Stannary parliament, various ongoing political movements are seeking to <i>change</i> Cornwall\'s constitutional status. <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a>, for example, has for many years sought for Cornwall the position of a <a href="/wiki/NUTS_1_statistical_regions_of_England" title="NUTS 1 statistical regions of England">level 1 NUTS region</a>, which would put Cornwall on the same statistical level as <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">Regions of England</a>.\n</p><p>In the same vein, the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Constitutional_Convention" title="Cornish Constitutional Convention">Cornish Constitutional Convention</a> \xe2\x80\x93 composed of a number of political groups in Cornwall (including Mebyon Kernow) \xe2\x80\x93 gathered about 50,000 signatures in 2000 on a petition to create a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Assembly" title="Cornish Assembly">Cornish Assembly</a> resembling the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>. The petition was undertaken in the context of an ongoing debate on whether to devolve power to the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">English regions</a>, of which Cornwall is part of the <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West</a>. Cornwall Council\'s February 2003 <a href="/wiki/MORI" class="mw-redirect" title="MORI">MORI</a> poll showed 55% in favour of an elected, fully devolved regional assembly for Cornwall and 13% against. (Previous result: 46% in favour in 2002).<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> However the same MORI poll indicated an equal number of respondents in favour of a South West Regional Assembly ).<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The campaign had the support of all five Cornish Lib Dem MPs at the time, <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a>, and Cornwall Council.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lord_Whitty" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Whitty">Lord Whitty</a>, as Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State at the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Environment,_Transport_and_the_Regions" title="Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions">Department of Environment, Transport and the Regions</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, recognised that Cornwall has a "special case" for <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> and on a visit to Cornwall deputy Prime Minister John Prescott said "Cornwall has the strongest regional identity in the UK."\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative and Unionist Party</a> under <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> appointed <a href="/wiki/Mark_Prisk" title="Mark Prisk">Mark Prisk</a> as <a href="/wiki/Official_Opposition_frontbench" title="Official Opposition frontbench">Shadow Minister</a> for Cornwall on 26 July 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The party said that the move was aimed at putting Cornwall\'s concerns "at the heart of Conservative thinking". However, the new coalition government established in 2010 under <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a>\'s leadership did not appoint a Minister for Cornwall.\n</p><p>To some extent the moves for autonomy in Cornwall have often been tied up with cultural/linguistic revivalist organisations. Rosalie Eastlake in a 1981 paper suggested that:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"In each historic period, economic exploitation and cultural alienation succeeded one another, until the nineteenth century when the mining economy of Cornwall became an essential part of the English industrial system. The twentieth century offers either the prospect of total incorporation into England, or a cultural revival spearheaded by the several small, national and cultural organisations which now exist." <sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>Cornwall\'s distinctiveness as a <i>national</i>, as opposed to regional, minority has been periodically recognised by major British papers. For example, a <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">Guardian</a></i> editorial in 1990 pointed to these differences, and warned that they should be constitutionally recognised:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Smaller minorities also have equally proud visions of themselves as irreducibly Welsh, Irish, Manx or Cornish. If the Cornish people want autonomy and it would improve their lives, why shouldn\'t they have self-rule once again? <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, with only 400,000 people, is an independent state within the EU. Why not Cornwall?"</dd></dl>\n<p>However, in a newspaper article the Conservative MP for <a href="/wiki/Camborne_and_Redruth_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Camborne and Redruth (UK Parliament constituency)">Camborne &amp; Redruth</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Eustice" title="George Eustice">George Eustice</a>, stated in September 2014 that "However, we definitely do not need to waste money on flash new parliament buildings and yet another tier of politicians so I completely disagree with the idea of a Welsh style assembly in Cornwall."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Labour party in Cornwall also rejected the notion.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Cornish_cultural.2C_civic_and_ethnic_nationalism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_cultural,_civic_and_ethnic_nationalism">Cornish cultural, civic and ethnic nationalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_status_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cornish cultural, civic and ethnic nationalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Cornwall" title="Culture of Cornwall">Culture of Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_nationalism" title="Cornish nationalism">Cornish nationalism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a></div>\n<p>While nearly all agree that Cornwall, along with Scotland, Wales and parts of <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a> forms part of the British periphery in economic and social terms, some observers express surprise at enduring sentiments in Cornwall; Adrian Lee, for example, while considering Cornwall to be part of England, also considers it to have a unique status <i>within</i> England:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"The history of Cornwall as one of England\'s peripheral areas is relatively little known, as is the fact that it is the only part of England to have given rise to and sustained a nationalist/autonomist movement that has been neither spurious nor ephemeral."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a> will, in addition to making the legal or constitutional arguments mentioned above, stress that the Cornish are a distinct ethnic group, that people in Cornwall typically refer to \'England\' as beginning east of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">Tamar</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (December 2012)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and that there is a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a>. For the first time in a UK Census, those wishing to describe their ethnicity as Cornish were given their own code number (06) on the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2001">2001 UK Census</a> form, alongside those for people wishing to describe themselves as English, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a>, Irish or Scottish. About 34,000 people in Cornwall and 3,500 people in the rest of the UK wrote on their census forms in 2001 that they considered their ethnic group to be Cornish. This represented nearly 7% of the population of Cornwall and is therefore a significant phenomenon.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Although happy with this development, campaigners expressed reservations about the lack of publicity surrounding the issue, the lack of a clear tick-box for the Cornish option on the census and the need to deny being British to write "Cornish" in the field provided. There have been calls for the tick box option to be extended to the Cornish, however this petition did not meet with sufficient support (639 people signed up, 361 more were needed)<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> for the 2011 Census,<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> as a Welsh and English tick box option was recently agreed by the government.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
