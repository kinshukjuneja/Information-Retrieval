mw-body-content">\n</div>\n<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Hot stove league</h1>\t\t\t<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, <b>hot stove league</b> is the sport\'s off-season. The phrase does not denote an actual league, but instead calls up images of baseball fans gathering around a hot <a href="/wiki/Stove" title="Stove">stove</a> during the cold <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a> months, discussing their favorite baseball teams and players.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERielly2005261_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERielly2005261-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartinez2000284_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartinez2000284-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term has also come to refer to the wave of off-season player transactions (contract negotiations, re-signings, trades, <a href="/wiki/Free_agent" title="Free agent">free agency</a>, etc.) that occur between seasons. Since most free-agent signings and trades occur during the off-season, this time of significant player transactions (including rumors and speculation about possible trades), is often referred to as the <i>hot stove league</i> or more simply, <i>hot stove</i>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" href="#Further_reading"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Further reading</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_stove_league&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:137px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PotbellyStove.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/PotbellyStove.jpg/135px-PotbellyStove.jpg" width="135" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/PotbellyStove.jpg/203px-PotbellyStove.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/PotbellyStove.jpg/270px-PotbellyStove.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PotbellyStove.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Potbelly_stove" title="Potbelly stove">Pot-bellied stove</a></div></div></div>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/James_D._Hardy,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="James D. Hardy, Jr.">Professor James Hardy</a>, the term <i>hot stove league</i> dates from nineteenth-century small town America when, during the winter, people "gathered at the general store/post office, sat around an iron pot-bellied stove, and discussed the passing parade. Baseball, along with weather, politics, the police blotter and the churches, belonged in that company".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHardy,_Jr.20078_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHardy,_Jr.20078-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Hardy states that the term was popularly employed by sportswriters until World War II, after which rural America gave way to larger, urban centers. Baseball analysis and conversation now became the province of radio and television commentators, with off-season chatter becoming less interactive and more impersonal.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHardy,_Jr.20078-9_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHardy,_Jr.20078-9-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>According to David Anderson, the news and coverage provided by hot stove league newspaper columnists during the first half of the 20th century, when professional football and basketball had not yet come into being, gave baseball its foothold as "the national pastime and dominant professional sport for more than half of the twentieth century".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson200336_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson200336-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The hot stove league was especially important in the era of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_clause" title="Reserve clause">reserve clause</a>, when the only leverage a player had to receive more money or better contract provisions was to hold out from re-signing, refuse to play, or threaten to retire. During the off-season, when contract negotiations were underway, the rumors and reports flew fast and furious, and fans kept up with the news through hot stove league chatter. The introduction of free agency reduced the importance of the hot stove league.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson200336_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson200336-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the twenty-first century, the term is still used to describe the "endless discussions", speculation and predictions bandied about by "baseball fans, baseball columnists, baseball bloggers, baseball writers, baseball podcasters and baseball radio talk show hosts" to fill the time between the end of the Baseball <a href="/wiki/Winter_Meetings" title="Winter Meetings">Winter Meetings</a> and the start of <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">spring training</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMahony2014199_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMahony2014199-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDahlgrenSparks1992193_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDahlgrenSparks1992193-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrand2007183_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrand2007183-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/MLB_Network" title="MLB Network">MLB Network</a>\'s daily off-season show of record is called <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Stove" title="Hot Stove">Hot Stove</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_stove_league&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>Hot Stove League</b> was the name of a radio segment featuring hockey chatter and analysis that was broadcast between periods on the radio show <i><a href="/wiki/Hockey_Night_in_Canada" title="Hockey Night in Canada">Hockey Night in Canada</a></i> beginning in 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoung199087_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung199087-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The segment became a pre-game series on <a href="/wiki/CBC_Television" title="CBC Television">CBC Television</a> in the 1950s.<sup
