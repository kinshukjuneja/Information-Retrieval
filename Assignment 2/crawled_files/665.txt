Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Sheriff&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="High Sheriff">High Sheriff</a>)</span></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>high sheriff</b> is a ceremonial officer for each <a href="/wiki/Shrieval_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrieval county">shrieval county</a> of <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> or the chief <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriff</a> of a number of paid sheriffs in <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. states</a> who outranks and commands the others in their court-related functions. In Canada, the High Sheriff provides administrative services to the supreme and provincial courts.\n</p><p>The office existed in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> but was abolished there in 1926.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, the offices of high sheriff arose at the direction of the Local Government Act 1972 incepting on 1 April 1974. The purpose was to distinguish sheriffs of counties proper from sheriffs of cities and boroughs designated <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">counties corporate</a>. Except for the City of London, having <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_of_the_City_of_London" title="Sheriffs of the City of London">2 sheriffs</a>, these cities and boroughs no longer have sheriffs, leaving the remaining in England and Wales as high sheriffs. The office is now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2018)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> an unpaid privilege with ceremonial duties, the sheriffs being appointed annually by <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Warrant_(law)" title="Warrant (law)">warrant</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Most Honourable Privy Council">Privy Council</a> except in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Cornwall" title="High Sheriff of Cornwall">high sheriff</a> is appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a>. In England and Wales the office\'s civil (civil judgement) enforcement powers exist but are not exercised by <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_custom)" title="Constitutional convention (political custom)">convention</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: England, Wales and Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_(112).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_%28112%29.JPG/220px-Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_%28112%29.JPG" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_%28112%29.JPG/330px-Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_%28112%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_%28112%29.JPG/440px-Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_%28112%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1936" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Legal_Service_for_Wales_2013_(112).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>High sheriffs customarily wear velvet <a href="/wiki/Court_uniform_and_dress_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Court uniform and dress in the United Kingdom">Court Dress</a> suits on formal occasions</div></div></div>\n<p>In England, Wales and Northern Ireland the high sheriff (or in the City of London the sheriffs) are theoretically the sovereign\'s judicial representative in the county,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lieutenant">Lord Lieutenant</a> is the sovereign\'s personal and military representative.  Their jurisdictions, the <i>shrieval counties</i>, are no longer co-terminous with administrative areas, representing a mix between the <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Counties of the United Kingdom">ancient counties</a> and more recent local authority areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  The post contrasts with that of <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriff</a> in Scotland, who is a judge sitting in a <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff court</a>.\n</p><p>The word <i>sheriff</i><span id="shire_reeve"></span> is a contraction of the term <i><a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shire</a> <a href="/wiki/Reeve_(England)" title="Reeve (England)">reeve</a></i>. The term, from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>sc\xc4\xabrgerefa</i>, designated a royal official responsible for keeping the peace (a <i>reeve</i>) throughout a shire or county on behalf of the king.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The term was preserved in England notwithstanding the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>. The office of sheriff had its origins in the 10th century; the office reached the height of its power under the Norman kings. While the sheriffs originally had been men of great standing at court, the thirteenth century saw a process whereby the office devolved on significant men within each county, usually landowners.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Provisions_of_Oxford" title="Provisions of Oxford">Provisions of Oxford</a> (1258) established a yearly tenure of office. The appointments and duties of the sheriffs in England and Wales were redefined by the <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_Act_1887" title="Sheriffs Act 1887">Sheriffs Act 1887</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, on 1 April 1974, the office previously known as sheriff was retitled <i>high sheriff</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The serving high sheriff submits a list of names of possible future high sheriffs to a tribunal which chooses three names to put to the Sovereign. The nomination is made on 12 November every year and the term of office runs from 25 March, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Day" title="Lady Day">the first day of the year until 1751</a>. No person may be appointed twice in three years, unless there is no other suitable person in the county.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomination">Nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_Act_1887" title="Sheriffs Act 1887">Sheriffs Act 1887</a> (as amended) provides that sheriffs should be nominated on 12 November (<a href="/wiki/Martinmas" class="mw-redirect" title="Martinmas">Martinmas</a>), or the Monday following if it falls on a Sunday, by any two or more of the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Council" title="Lord President of the Council">Lord President of the Council</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Chief Justice of England">Lord Chief Justice of England</a>; other members of the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a>; and any two or more judges of <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">Her Majesty\'s High Court</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These amendments were in 1998, the Chancellor of the Exchequer was granted full entitlement, not merely conditional entitlement, if there is no <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Treasurer" title="Lord High Treasurer">Lord High Treasurer</a> \xe2\x80\x93 since the Treasurership is by <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_custom)" title="Constitutional convention (political custom)">constitutional convention</a> always placed into commission, and in 2006 the Lord Chancellor was removed as a nominating officer through the operation of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>These officers nominate three candidates for each county in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>), which are enrolled on a <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Remembrancer" title="Queen&#39;s Remembrancer">Queen\'s Remembrancer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Eligibility for nomination and appointment as high sheriff under the Sheriffs Act 1887 excludes peers of Parliament, members of the House of Commons, commissioners or officers of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Revenue_and_Customs" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Revenue and Customs">Her Majesty\'s Revenue and Customs</a>, officers of the Post Office and officers of the Navy, Army or Royal Air Force on full pay, clergymen (whether <a href="/wiki/Benefice" title="Benefice">beneficed</a> or not) and barristers or solicitors in actual practice.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pricking">Pricking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pricking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The practice of <i>pricking</i> is an ancient custom used to appoint the high sheriffs of England and Wales.\n</p><p>In February or March of each year, two parchments prepared the previous November are presented to the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">Sovereign</a> (who is also Duke of Lancaster) at a meeting of the Privy Council. A further parchment is drawn up in November for Cornwall and presented to the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a> (or to the Sovereign when there is no such Duke).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Certain eligible persons (High Court judges and the Privy Council) nominate candidates for each county <a href="/wiki/List_of_shrievalties" title="List of shrievalties">shrievalty</a>, one of whom is chosen for each by the Sovereign. In practice, the first name on the list is nowadays always the one chosen; the second and third names tend to become sheriffs in succeeding years, barring incapacity or death. The Sovereign signifies assent by pricking (i.e., piercing) the document with a silver <a href="/wiki/Sewing_needle" title="Sewing needle">bodkin</a> by the relevant name for each county, and signs the parchment when complete. The parchment for the Duchy of Lancaster is known as the <i>Lites</i>, and the ceremony of selection known as <i>Pricking the Lites</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-dl_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dl-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>lites</i>, meaning <i>list</i>, was once reserved for <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> the date at which the name was transferred to Lancashire is unknown.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (July 2012)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  The Lites is used for the three <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial counties</a> that fall wholly or partially within the boundaries of the historic <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">county palatine of Lancaster</a> (an <a href="/wiki/Administrative_county" title="Administrative county">administrative county</a> until 1 April 1974): <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dl_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dl-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The practice is believed to date back to a year in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a>, when, lacking a pen, she decided to use her bodkin to mark the name instead. By contrast, <a href="/wiki/John_Campbell,_1st_Baron_Campbell" title="John Campbell, 1st Baron Campbell">Lord Campbell</a> stated, perhaps without intention of publication, in February 1847, "[it began] in ancient times, sir, when sovereigns did not know how to write their names." while acquiring a prick and a signature from <a href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a> as <a href="/wiki/Albert,_Prince_Consort" title="Albert, Prince Consort">Prince Albert</a> asked him when the custom began.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The High Sheriff\'s Association argues pricking vellum ensured that the record could not be altered. Given that holders of the office often had to bear large costs and implement unpopular policies altering the choice of the monarch must sometimes have been tempting.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Declaration">Declaration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Declaration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The declaration a person must make before taking the office of high sheriff is contained in the second schedule of the Sheriffs Act 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Additional words are inserted in the case of the Duchy of Cornwall; for example, the declaration includes: "do solemnly declare that I will well and truly serve the Queen\xe2\x80\x99s Majesty and also his Royal Highness Duke of Cornwall".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities">Responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Contemporary high sheriffs have few genuine responsibilities and their functions are largely representational:<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hs_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hs-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Attendance at Royal visits to the county.</li>\n<li>Proclamation of the accession of a new Sovereign.</li>\n<li>Acting as the <a href="/wiki/Returning_officer" title="Returning officer">returning officer</a> for parliamentary elections in <a href="/wiki/County_constituency" class="mw-redirect" title="County constituency">county constituencies</a>; this role is normally delegated to an <a href="/wiki/Returning_officer#United_Kingdom" title="Returning officer">acting returning officer</a>.</li>\n<li>Attendance at the opening ceremony when a <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judge</a> goes on circuit.</li>\n<li>Historically, execution of High Court writs (although technically sheriffs may still do this, their duty to do so was effectively removed by the <a href="/wiki/Courts_Act_2003" title="Courts Act 2003">Courts Act 2003</a>).</li>\n<li>Annual appointment of <a href="/wiki/Undersheriff" title="Undersheriff">undersheriffs</a> to act as deputies.</li></ul>\n<p>Theoretical responsibilities include the well-being and protection of High Court judges, and attending them in court; and the maintenance of the loyalty of subjects to the Crown.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> However, most of the high sheriff\'s work is delegated (for example, the local police now protect judges and courts) so that in effect the post of high sheriff is essentially ceremonial.\n</p><p>The high sheriff was traditionally responsible for the maintenance of <a href="/wiki/Law_and_order_(politics)" title="Law and order (politics)">law and order</a> within the county, although most of these duties are now delegated to the police.<sup id="cite_ref-hs_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hs-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> As a result of its close links with law and order the position is frequently awarded to people with an association with law enforcement (former police officers, <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judges</a>). The high sheriff was originally allowed to kill suspects resisting arrest;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> this was still legal in the 17th century. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Coke" title="Edward Coke">Edward Coke</a> noted that when the high sheriff employed constables to assist in his duties the law was also extended to them.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers">Powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_Act_1887" title="Sheriffs Act 1887">Sheriffs Act 1887</a>, if a sheriff finds any resistance in the execution of a writ he shall "take with him the power of the county" (known as <a href="/wiki/Posse_comitatus_(common_law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Posse comitatus (common law)">posse comitatus</a>), and shall go in proper person to do execution, and may arrest the resisters and commit them to prison, and every such resister shall be guilty of a <a href="/wiki/Misdemeanor" title="Misdemeanor">misdemeanor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptional_counties">Exceptional counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exceptional counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="City_of_London">City of London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: City of London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>There are two <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_of_the_City_of_London" title="Sheriffs of the City of London">Sheriffs of the City of London</a>, elected annually by the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> <a href="/wiki/Liverymen" class="mw-redirect" title="Liverymen">Liverymen</a>; their function is similar but not equivalent to that of high sheriff since the City of London forms its own ceremonial county, thus not under the jurisdiction of <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Greater_London" title="High Sheriff of Greater London">High Sheriff of Greater London</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_of_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheriffs of London">Sheriffs of London</a> also served as sheriffs for <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a> until 1889 when the office of <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Middlesex" title="High Sheriff of Middlesex">High Sheriff of Middlesex</a> was created.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornwall">Cornwall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cornwall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Duchy of Cornwall\'s first charter in 1337 states that the <i>Shrievalty of Cornwall</i>, the right to appoint the Sheriff for the County, is vested in the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Two further charters, dated 18 March 1337 and 3 January 1338, state that no Sheriff of the King shall enter Cornwall to execute the <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">King\'s Writ</a>. The High Sheriff of Cornwall swears to serve both the reigning Monarch and Duke of Cornwall (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prince" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Prince">Crown Prince</a>). When there is no Duke of Cornwall, the Duchy Council still sits, but under the Trusteeship of the English (since 1707, British) Monarch. Only as Duchy Trustee can the Monarch appoint the Sheriff of Cornwall. Nomination and appointment generally takes place during <a href="/wiki/Hilary_Term" class="mw-redirect" title="Hilary Term">Hilary</a>, and announced via the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> Office.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-atk9_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atk9-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Durham">Durham</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Durham">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Durham" title="High Sheriff of Durham">High Sheriff of Durham</a> was appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Prince-Bishop of Durham</a> until 1836, when the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/County_palatine" title="County palatine">county palatine</a> became vested in the Crown.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Since then the High Sheriffs of Durham have been appointed in the same way as other high sheriffs in England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lancashire">Lancashire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lancashire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The right to nominate and select high sheriffs in Lancashire is vested in the <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> in right of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-atk9_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atk9-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Before 1974, this right applied only to the <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Lancashire" title="High Sheriff of Lancashire">High Sheriff of Lancashire</a>, but since the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">boundary changes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> (effective 1974), the <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Greater_Manchester" title="High Sheriff of Greater Manchester">High Sheriff of Greater Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Merseyside" title="High Sheriff of Merseyside">High Sheriff of Merseyside</a> also come under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a>. As with  other counties in England, three names are nominated to the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster">Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster</a> for Lancashire appointments;<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> the Chancellor presents these to the Monarch with his recommendation in a private audience.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> New appointments are usually announced during <a href="/wiki/Hilary_term" title="Hilary term">Hilary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-atk9_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atk9-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The nomination of sheriffs in the counties of Wales was first vested by statute in the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Wales_and_the_Marches" title="Council of Wales and the Marches">Council of Wales and the Marches</a> and the Welsh justices under <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>. With the abolition of the Council in 1689, the power of nomination was transferred to the justices of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Great_Sessions_in_Wales" title="Court of Great Sessions in Wales">Court of Great Sessions in Wales</a>. When this court was abolished in 1830, its rights were in turn transferred to the courts of <a href="/wiki/Court_of_King%27s_Bench" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of King&#39;s Bench">King\'s Bench</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exchequer_of_pleas" class="mw-redirect" title="Exchequer of pleas">Exchequer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Common_Pleas_(England)" title="Court of Common Pleas (England)">Commons Pleas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Finally, by an Act of Parliament of 1845, the nomination and appointment of sheriffs in Wales was made identical to that in England.<sup id="cite_ref-atk9_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atk9-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Sheriffs (Ireland) Act 1920 restricted the duties of the high sheriff to summoning of the county <a href="/wiki/Grand_jury" title="Grand jury">grand jury</a> and attending the judge at <a href="/wiki/Assizes_(Ireland)" title="Assizes (Ireland)">assizes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dail19260311_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dail19260311-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> the <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Justice_Act_1924" title="Courts of Justice Act 1924">Courts of Justice Act 1924</a> abolished the grand jury and the assizes;<sup id="cite_ref-dail19260311_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dail19260311-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and the office of high sheriff was formally abolished by the Court Officers Act 1926.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The office continues to exist in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the Province of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, the High Sheriff is primarily responsible for providing administrative and enforcement services to the Supreme Court of Newfoundland and the Provincial Courts. The Office of the High Sheriff administers the jury system, provides court security and executes orders and decisions of the court. These Officers act in the name of the Sheriff in accordance with directions given them and the law. They include bailiffs, Deputy Sheriffs, fee-for-service Deputy Sheriffs, and all other employees and staff of the High Sheriff. Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Officers have both the power and the duty to carry out orders of the Court. They are peace officers under the Criminal Code of Canada and have all the powers and protection of law enforcement officers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_sheriff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The position of high sheriff in the United States generally denotes the superior sheriff in a <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a>, or the head of a statewide sheriff\'s department. Such a position exists in <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> (executive high sheriff),<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_Department_of_Public_Safety#Sheriff_Division" title="Hawaii Department of Public Safety">Hawaii</a>. In <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, the ten high sheriffs are the senior law enforcement officers of each <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">county</a>, and have <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> powers throughout the state.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="New York City Sheriff">New York City Sheriff</a> functions as a high sheriff over the five counties/boroughs; each of which contains a subordinate <a href="/wiki/Undersheriff" title="Undersheriff">undersheriff</a> in charge.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a
