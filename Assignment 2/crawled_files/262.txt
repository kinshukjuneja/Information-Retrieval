Red Stockings</b> of <a href="/wiki/1869_in_baseball" title="1869 in baseball">1869</a> were <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>\'s first openly all-professional team, with ten salaried players.<sup id="cite_ref-1869RedStockings_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1869RedStockings-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <b>Cincinnati Base Ball Club</b> formed in 1866 and fielded competitive teams in the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Base_Ball_Players" title="National Association of Base Ball Players">National Association of Base Ball Players</a> (NABBP) 1867\xe2\x80\x931870, a time of a transition that ambitious <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a> businessmen and English-born ballplayer <a href="/wiki/Harry_Wright" title="Harry Wright">Harry Wright</a> shaped as much as anyone. <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> recognized those events officially by sponsoring a centennial of professional baseball in 1969.\n</p><p>Thanks partly to their on-field success and the continental scope of their tours, the Red Stockings established styles in team uniforms and team nicknames that have some currency even in the 21st century. They also established a particular color, red, as the color of Cincinnati, and they provide the ultimate origin for the use of "Red Sox" in <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball_club">Baseball club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Red_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Baseball club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Cincinnati Base Ball Club, or simply Cincinnati Club, was established July 23, 1866 at a downtown law office, drawing up a constitution and by-laws and electing officers including <a href="/wiki/Alfred_T._Goshorn" title="Alfred T. Goshorn">Alfred T. Goshorn</a>, President. A few years later Goshorn earned international fame as Director-General of the (U.S.) <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Exposition" title="Centennial Exposition">Centennial Exposition</a> held 1876 in Philadelphia. Founding member George B. Ellard also led the Union Cricket Club, and the relationship between them proved decisive for the baseball club\'s success.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1868_Reds.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/1868_Reds.jpg/200px-1868_Reds.jpg" width="200" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/1868_Reds.jpg/300px-1868_Reds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/1868_Reds.jpg/400px-1868_Reds.jpg 2x" data-file-width="730" data-file-height="535" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1868_Reds.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Cincinnati Red Stockings in 1868, one year before they turned professional.</div></div></div>\n<p>After playing four matches that summer, Cincinnati joined the NABBP for 1867 and concluded an agreement to play at the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Park_Grounds" title="Lincoln Park Grounds">Union Cricket Club grounds</a> (just west of Lincoln Park, a site now occupied by <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Museum_Center_at_Union_Terminal" title="Cincinnati Museum Center at Union Terminal">Cincinnati Museum Center at Union Terminal</a>). George Ellard\'s son says that "a great number of the cricket club members" joined and so "the team was greatly strengthened and interest in baseball gained a new impetus." Plans for a new clubhouse and "more substantical" enclosing fence were approved in April and the commercial basis was approved in June: members of both clubs admitted free to all matches; otherwise "ten cents for home matches and twenty five cents for foreign matches. Ladies free." (Ellard 23\xe2\x80\x9327).\n</p><p>The team was soon nicknamed "Red Stockings" in reference to the main feature of the uniforms designed by Ellard; long stockings were then a novelty in team uniforms.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg/220px-1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg/330px-1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg/440px-1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg 2x" data-file-width="448" data-file-height="332" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1869HarpersWeeklyBaseballRedStockings.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Harpers_Weekly" class="mw-redirect" title="Harpers Weekly">Harpers Weekly</a></i> representation of the Cincinnati Red Stockings.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Harry_Wright" title="Harry Wright">Harry Wright</a> had migrated from New York in 1866 for a job as "club pro" at the Union Cricket Club. Next year he picked up similar baseball duties, but the lingo is commonly stretched to call him a baseball "manager" from that time. His first team may have been local to a man, but he both developed and imported players to represent the club in competitive play for the 1868 season. The first team won 16 matches with regional opponents, losing only to the touring <a href="/wiki/Washington_Nationals_(NA)" title="Washington Nationals (NA)">Nationals</a> from Washington. As for most hosts on that tour, it was a "bad loss" on the scorecard but an instructive one for Cincinnati: the players, the club, the fans, and perhaps the local newspapers.  Everyone learned advanced points of play and, from their different perspectives, witnessed the gulf in playing strength.\n</p><p>About half of the 1868 Red Stockings were eastern imports, presumably compensated somehow.  The two leading batsmen, <a href="/wiki/John_Hatfield_(baseball)" title="John Hatfield (baseball)">John Hatfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Waterman" title="Fred Waterman">Fred Waterman</a> arrived from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mutuals" title="New York Mutuals">New York Mutuals</a>, one of the strongest teams anywhere and another team pushing the bounds of the amateur code.  <a href="/wiki/Asa_Brainard" title="Asa Brainard">Asa Brainard</a> had been the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Excelsiors" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Excelsiors">Brooklyn Excelsiors</a>\' regular pitcher for four seasons, succeeded in 1867 by <a href="/wiki/Candy_Cummings" title="Candy Cummings">Candy Cummings</a>.  Catcher <a href="/wiki/Doug_Allison" title="Doug Allison">Doug Allison</a> was from the Geary club of Philadelphia, one of the stronger clubs in that city. There was one local recruit, too, from the rival Buckeye club: <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Gould" title="Charlie Gould">Charlie Gould</a> at first base.  Harry Wright remained the first pitcher, sharing that position and second base with Brainard, and three other incumbents remained in the outfield and at shortstop. The 1868 team played a heavy schedule including a late eastern tour, once again dominating the western teams but losing seven of 43 matches in all.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_professional_team">First professional team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Red_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First professional team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg/220px-1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg/330px-1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg/440px-1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg 2x" data-file-width="760" data-file-height="564" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1869-cincinnati-red-stockings.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cincinnati Red Stockings in 1869.</div></div></div>\n<p>When the <a href="/wiki/NABBP" class="mw-redirect" title="NABBP">NABBP</a> permitted professional members for 1869, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Wright" title="Harry Wright">Harry Wright</a> and probably George Ellard organized a fully professional team:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> ten men on salary for eight months, March 15 to November 15. Wright played center field and coordinated the team defense, a novelty from any position. Younger brother and shortstop <a href="/wiki/George_Wright_(sportsman)" title="George Wright (sportsman)">George Wright</a>, new to the team in 1869, was its best player, maybe the best of his time.\n</p><p>The professional Cincinnati Red Stockings played their first game May 4, 1869, with a 45\xe2\x80\x939 win over the Great Westerns of Cincinnati.  The team won 57 games and lost zero, counting only matches with Association clubs. They played over 70 games counting outside teams.  Its commercial tour of continental scope, visiting both Boston and San Francisco, was unprecedented and may be essentially unrepeated.  The first season ended November 6 at home with the Cincinnatis beating the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mutuals" title="New York Mutuals">Mutuals of New York</a> 17\xe2\x80\x938.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the same regular nine, the <a href="/wiki/1870_in_baseball" title="1870 in baseball">1870</a> team continued to win regularly, perhaps 24 games before losing 8\xe2\x80\x937 in eleven innings to the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Atlantics" title="Brooklyn Atlantics">Brooklyn Atlantics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn, New York">Brooklyn</a>, June 14.  The Red Stockings remained one of the few strongest teams on the field, losing only six games, but attendance declined badly, especially at home.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perfect_season">Perfect season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Red_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Perfect season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1869, the Red Stockings posted a perfect 67\xe2\x80\x930 record (<a class="external free" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_best_Major_League_Baseball_season_win-loss_records">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_best_Major_League_Baseball_season_win-loss_records</a>), the only perfect season in professional baseball history. This was the first team to play on the East and West coasts in the same season. More than 2,000 people greeted the team when it arrived in San Francisco at 10:00&#160;p.m. \xe2\x80\x9cThey really helped nationalize the game and put Cincinnati on the map as a baseball town,\xe2\x80\x9d said Greg Rhodes, a Reds historian who wrote \xe2\x80\x9cThe First Boys of Summer\xe2\x80\x9d (Road West Publishing Company, 1994), along with Enquirer reporter John Erardi, about the 1869\xe2\x80\x931870 Red Stockings.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="1870.E2.80.931871"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1870\xe2\x80\x931871">1870\xe2\x80\x931871</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Red_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1870\xe2\x80\x931871">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On June 14, 1870, after 81 consecutive wins (<a class="external free" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_best_Major_League_Baseball_season_win-loss_records">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_best_Major_League_Baseball_season_win-loss_records</a>) since assembling the first professional team in, the Cincinnati Red Stockings lost 8\xe2\x80\x937 to the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Atlantics" title="Brooklyn Atlantics">Brooklyn Atlantics</a> before a crowd of 20,000 at the <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Grounds" title="Capitoline Grounds">Capitoline Grounds</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bob_Ferguson_(infielder)" title="Bob Ferguson (infielder)">Bob Ferguson</a> scored the winning run in the 11th inning on a hit by pitcher <a href="/wiki/George_Zettlein" title="George Zettlein">George Zettlein</a>.\n</p><p>The Executive Board now led by President A.P.C. Bonte recommended on November 21, 1870, that the club not employ a nine for 1871, for that had become too expensive. The spokesmen anticipated "a development of the amateur talent of our club, such as has not been displayed since we employed professionals." The officers subsequently decided to disband the company (the players having disbanded via the market) and a public meeting of the members put that decision into effect.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Harry_Wright" title="Harry Wright">Harry Wright</a> was hired by founder and president <a href="/wiki/Ivers_Whitney_Adams" title="Ivers Whitney Adams">Ivers Whitney Adams</a> to organize a new pro club in Boston and he signed three Cincinnati teammates to join the 1871 <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves#Boston_years" title="History of the Atlanta Braves">Boston Red Stockings</a> in the first professional league, as it turned out. Ex-Cincinnati Red Stockings moved around some (see the note on Team members) but Boston retained both Wright brothers throughout the five years of the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Professional_Base_Ball_Players" title="National Association of Professional Base Ball Players">National Association</a>.\n</p><p>The current <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> club identifies itself with the Cincinnati Red Stockings,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> despite reorganization and defections in the early years.\n</p><p>The distinct Boston Red Stockings, beginning business with half of the Cincinnati team, both followed the young tradition and spread it to Boston. Eventually the Boston Red Stockings adopted the name <a href="/wiki/Boston_Braves_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Braves (baseball)">Boston Braves</a>; the club is now based in Atlanta, and still retains red as one of its uniform colors. The <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a>, established in 1901, adopted their version of the old nickname in 1908.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Players">Players</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Red_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1869_Cincinnati_Red_Stockings_lithograph.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An 1869 lithograph of the Red Stockings\' "First Nine".</div></div></div>\n<p>Ten men composed the 1869 team and the First Nine returned for 1870 in the same roles.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Asa_Brainard" title="Asa Brainard">Asa Brainard</a>, Pitcher</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Doug_Allison" title="Doug Allison">Doug Allison</a>, Catcher</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Charlie_Gould" title="Charlie Gould">Charlie Gould</a>, First Base</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Charlie_Sweasy" title="Charlie Sweasy">Charlie Sweasy</a>, Second Base</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fred_Waterman" title="Fred Waterman">Fred Waterman</a>, Third Base</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/George_Wright_(sportsman)" title="George Wright (sportsman)">George Wright</a>, Shortstop</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Andy_Leonard" title="Andy Leonard">Andy Leonard</a>, Left Field</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Harry_Wright" title="Harry Wright">Harry Wright</a>, Center Field/Manager</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cal_McVey" title="Cal McVey">Cal McVey</a>, Right Field</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Dick_Hurley" title="Dick Hurley">Dick Hurley</a>, substitute</li></ul>\n<p>From 1867 Harry Wright fulfilled the duties of modern field managers, general managers, and traveling secretaries. In 1868 he and Brainard shared the pitcher and second base positions with Allison, Gould, and Waterman already manning the other bases. For the crosstown rival Buckeye club, Sweasy and Leonard played second and third with Hurley a substitute. Among them only Gould was a Cincinnati native; the others were from the East, presumably compensated somehow by club members if not by the clubs. (The <a href="/wiki/NABBP" class="mw-redirect" title="NABBP">Association</a> first permitted professional clubs for 1869.) Meanwhile, George Wright and McVey played in New York and Indianapolis, primarily at shortstop and pitcher.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For 1871 the Nine split between two teams in the new all-professional <a href="/wiki/NAPBBP" class="mw-redirect" title="NAPBBP">National Association</a>: Gould, the Wright brothers, and McVey with the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Atlanta_Braves#Boston_years" title="History of the Atlanta Braves">Boston Red Stockings</a>; Brainard, Allison, Sweasy, Waterman, and Leonard with the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Olympics" title="Washington Olympics">Washington Olympics</a>. Substitute Hurley is also a "major leaguer" for his brief play with the Olympics in 1872, although that club went out of business midseason and he did not return to the league. The leading substitute in the second season, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Deane" title="Harry Deane">Harry Deane</a> joined the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Wayne_Kekiongas" title="Fort Wayne Kekiongas">Fort Wayne Kekiongas</a> in 1871 and later played a full season regularly.\n</p><p>Andy Leonard rejoined Gould, the Wrights, and McVey in Boston for 1872, the first of four consecutive championship seasons there. After one miss Harry won his last two championships as a non-playing manager in 1877\xe2\x80\x931878 with Leonard and brother George still among his regulars. Gould and McVey left in 1873, although McVey returned for 1874\xe2\x80\x9375 only.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Record">Record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Red_Stockings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before Cincinnati hired its team for the 1869 season, the strongest clubs were located from Washington to <a href="/wiki/Troy,_New_York" title="Troy, New York">Troy, New York</a>.
