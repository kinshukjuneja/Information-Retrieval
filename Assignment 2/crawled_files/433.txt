the <a href="/wiki/Film_industry" title="Film industry">film industry</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. The Argentine cinema comprises the art of film and creative movies made within the nation of Argentina or by Argentine filmmakers abroad.\n</p><p>The Argentine film industry has historically been one of the three most developed in <a href="/wiki/Latin_American_cinema" title="Latin American cinema">Latin American cinema</a>, along with those produced in <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Mexico" title="Cinema of Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Brazil" title="Cinema of Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the 20th century, film production in Argentina, supported by the State and by the work of a long list of <a href="/wiki/Category:Argentine_film_directors" title="Category:Argentine film directors">directors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Argentine_actors" title="Category:Argentine actors">actors</a>, became one of the major film industries in the <a href="/wiki/Hispanophone" title="Hispanophone">Spanish-speaking world</a>.\n</p><p>Argentina has won sixteen <a href="/wiki/Goya_Award_for_Best_Spanish_Language_Foreign_Film" class="mw-redirect" title="Goya Award for Best Spanish Language Foreign Film">Goya Awards for Best Spanish Language Foreign Film</a>, which makes it the most awarded country. It is also the first Latin American country that has won <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Academy Awards</a>, in recognition of the films <i><a href="/wiki/The_Official_Story" title="The Official Story">The Official Story</a></i> (1985) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_in_Their_Eyes" title="The Secret in Their Eyes">The Secret in Their Eyes</a></i> (2009).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div photographer <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Py" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene Py">Eugene Py</a> was working for the <a href="/wiki/Belgian" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian">Belgian</a> Henri Lepage and the <a href="/wiki/Austrians" title="Austrians">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Max_Gl%C3%BCcksmann" title="Max Gl\xc3\xbccksmann">Max Gl\xc3\xbccksmann</a> at the \'Casa Lepage\', a <a Aires">Buenos Aires</a>. The three all saw the debut of the Lumi\xc3\xa8re Cin\xc3\xa9matographe in Argentina,"with a picture of the Lumi\xc3\xa9re\'s, took place on July 18, 1896"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Od%C3%A9on" class="mw-redirect" title="Teatro Od\xc3\xa9on">Teatro Od\xc3\xa9on</a>, only a year after its debut in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.\n</p><p>Lepage then imported the first French cinematographic equipment into the country and though <a href="/wiki/Eugenio_Py" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugenio Py">Eugenio Py</a> who, using a Gaumont camera in 1897, is often credited for the first Argentine film, <i><a href="/wiki/La_Bandera_Argentina" class="mw-redirect" title="La Bandera Argentina">La Bandera Argentina</a></i> (which consisted of a <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Argentina" title="Flag of Argentina">flag of Argentina</a> waving in the wind at the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Mayo" title="Plaza de Mayo">Plaza de Mayo</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> the credit belongs to German-Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Federico_Figner" title="Federico Figner">Federico Figner</a>, who screened the first three Argentine films on 24 November 1896 (shorts depicting sights of Buenos Aires). Earning renown, Py continued to produce films for exhibition at the Casa Lepage for several years, following up with <i>Viaje del Doctor Campos Salles a Buenos Aires</i> (1900, considered the country\'s first <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a>) and <i>La Revista de la Escuadra Argentina</i> (1901); by that time, the first projection halls had opened, working as part of the cross-national film production, <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">distribution</a> and <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/exhibition" class="extiw" title="wikt:exhibition">exhibition</a> system developed by Gl\xc3\xbccksmann in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_developments">Early developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Amalia_(1914,_Argentina)_-_Movie_by_Enrique_Garc%C3%ADa_Velloso.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ce/Amalia_%281914%2C_Argentina%29_-_Movie_by_Enrique_Garc%C3%ADa_Velloso.jpg" width="200" height="160" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amalia_(1914,_Argentina)_-_Movie_by_Enrique_Garc%C3%ADa_Velloso.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Amalia_(1914_film)" title="Amalia (1914 film)">Amalia</a></i> (1914), the first full-length movie of national production.</div></div></div>\n<p>Several Argentine artists continued to experime with the new invention, making news shorts and documentaries. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugenio_A._Cardini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eugenio A. Cardini (page does not exist)">Eugenio A. Cardini</a> filmed <i>Escenas Callejeras</i> (1901) and <a href="/wiki/Mario_Gallo" title="Mario Gallo">Mario Gallo</a> made the first Argentine film with a point-of-view: <i><a href="/wiki/El_fusilamiento_de_Dorrego" title="El fusilamiento de Dorrego">El fusilamiento de Dorrego</a></i> ("<a href="/wiki/Manuel_Dorrego" title="Manuel Dorrego">Dorrego</a>\'s Execution," 1908). Other directors such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernesto_Gunche&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ernesto Gunche (page does not exist)">Ernesto Gunche</a> directed early documentaries.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_Argentina" title="History of Argentina">Argentine history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Literature_of_Argentina" class="mw-redirect" title="Literature of Argentina">literature</a> provided the themes of the first years of film-making. One of the first successes of the national cinema was <i>Nobleza Gaucha</i> of 1915, inspired by <i><a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Fierro" title="Mart\xc3\xadn Fierro">Mart\xc3\xadn Fierro</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Gaucho" title="Gaucho">gaucho</a> poem by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Hern%C3%A1ndez_(writer)" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Hern\xc3\xa1ndez (writer)">Jos\xc3\xa9 Hern\xc3\xa1ndez</a>. Based on <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_M%C3%A1rmol" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 M\xc3\xa1rmol">Jos\xc3\xa9 M\xc3\xa1rmol</a>\'s novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Amalia_(1914_film)" title="Amalia (1914 film)">Amalia (1914 film)</a></i> was the first full-length movie of national production, and in 1917 <i><a href="/wiki/El_Ap%C3%B3stol" title="El Ap\xc3\xb3stol">El ap\xc3\xb3stol</a></i>, a satiric short on <a href="/wiki/President_of_Argentina" title="President of Argentina">president</a> <a href="/wiki/Hip%C3%B3lito_Yrigoyen" title="Hip\xc3\xb3lito Yrigoyen">Hip\xc3\xb3lito Yrigoyen</a>, became the first animated feature film in world cinema. Another notable 1917 debut, for Francisco Defilippis Novoa\'s <i>Flor de durazno,</i> was <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Gardel" title="Carlos Gardel">Carlos Gardel</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><div id="mwe_player_0" class="PopUpMediaTransform" style="width:250px;" videopayload="&lt;div class=&quot;mediaContainer&quot; Ferreyra">Jos\xc3\xa9 A. Ferreyra</a> began to work on producing films in Argentine cinema, releasing films such as <a href="/wiki/Palomas_rubias" title="Palomas rubias">Palomas rubias</a> (1920), <a href="/wiki/La_Gaucha" title="La Gaucha">La Gaucha</a> (1921) and <i><a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires,_ciudad_de_ensue%C3%B1o" class="mw-redirect" title="Buenos Aires, ciudad de ensue\xc3\xb1o">Buenos Aires, ciudad de ensue\xc3\xb1o</a></i> in 1922. Films that followed included <i><a href="/wiki/La_Maleva" title="La Maleva">La Maleva</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Coraz%C3%B3n_de_criolla" title="Coraz\xc3\xb3n de criolla">Coraz\xc3\xb3n de criolla</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Melenita_de_oro" title="Melenita de oro">Melenita de oro</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Leyenda_del_puente_inca" class="mw-redirect" title="Leyenda del puente inca">Leyenda del puente inca</a></i> (1923), <i><a href="/wiki/Odio_serrano" title="Odio serrano">Odio serrano</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mientras_Buenos_Aires_duerme" title="Mientras Buenos Aires duerme">Mientras Buenos Aires duerme</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arriero_de_Yacanto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arriero de Yacanto (page does not exist)">Arriero de Yacanto</a></i> (1924) and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Organito_de_la_tarde&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="El Organito de la tarde (page does not exist)">El Organito de la tarde</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mi_%C3%BAltimo_tango" class="mw-redirect" title="Mi \xc3\xbaltimo tango">Mi \xc3\xbaltimo tango</a></i> (1925).\n</p><p>In 1926, Ferreyra released <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Vuelta_al_Bul%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="La Vuelta al Bul\xc3\xadn (page does not exist)">La Vuelta al Bul\xc3\xadn</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Costurerita_que_dio_aquel_mal_paso&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="La Costurerita que dio aquel mal paso (page does not exist)">La Costurerita que dio aquel mal paso</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muchachita_de_Chiclana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Muchachita de Chiclana (page does not exist)">Muchachita de Chiclana</a></i> followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Perd%C3%B3n,_viejita" title="Perd\xc3\xb3n, viejita">Perd\xc3\xb3n, viejita</a></i> (1927). Many of these Ferreyra films featured two of the decade\'s most popular stars, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvaro_Escobar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alvaro Escobar (page does not exist)">Alvaro Escobar</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Guido&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Elena Guido (page does not exist)">Elena Guido</a>.\n</p><p>Towards the end of the decade, directors such as <a href="/wiki/Julio_Irigoyen" title="Julio Irigoyen">Julio Irigoyen</a> began to release films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Alma_en_pena" title="Alma en pena">Alma en pena</a></i> in 1928. Films such as these began to feature the Argentine culture of <a href="/wiki/Tango_dance" class="mw-redirect" title="Tango dance">tango dancing</a> into films, something which rocketed later in the 1930s after the advent of sound.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1930s.E2.80.931950s:_The_Golden_Age"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s\xe2\x80\x931950s:_The_Golden_Age">1930s\xe2\x80\x931950s: The Golden Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1930s\xe2\x80\x931950s: The Golden Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Edad_de_oro_del_cine_argentino.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c6/Edad_de_oro_del_cine_argentino.jpg/300px-Edad_de_oro_del_cine_argentino.jpg" width="300" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c6/Edad_de_oro_del_cine_argentino.jpg/450px-Edad_de_oro_del_cine_argentino.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c6/Edad_de_oro_del_cine_argentino.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="409" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Edad_de_oro_del_cine_argentino.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Golden Age of Argentine cinema. From top (left to right): <a href="/wiki/Tita_Merello" title="Tita Merello">Tita Merello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libertad_Lamarque" title="Libertad Lamarque">Libertad Lamarque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luis_Sandrini" title="Luis Sandrini">Luis Sandrini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pepe_Arias" title="Pepe Arias">Pepe Arias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mirtha_Legrand" title="Mirtha Legrand">Mirtha Legrand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zully_Moreno" title="Zully Moreno">Zully Moreno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lolita_Torres" title="Lolita Torres">Lolita Torres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Bence" title="Amelia Bence">Amelia Bence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Gardel" title="Carlos Gardel">Carlos Gardel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olga_Zubarry" title="Olga Zubarry">Olga Zubarry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mecha_Ortiz" title="Mecha Ortiz">Mecha Ortiz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Gola" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Gola">Jos\xc3\xa9 Gola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugo_del_Carril" title="Hugo del Carril">Hugo del Carril</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armando_B%C3%B3" title="Armando B\xc3\xb3">Armando B\xc3\xb3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nin%C3%AD_Marshall" title="Nin\xc3\xad Marshall">Nin\xc3\xad Marshall</a>.</div></div></div>\n<dl><dd><i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Argentine_films:1930s" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Argentine films:1930s">List of Argentine films:1930s</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>In 1930, <i><a href="/wiki/Adi%C3%B3s_Argentina" class="mw-redirect" title="Adi\xc3\xb3s Argentina">Adi\xc3\xb3s Argentina</a></i> became the first Argentine film to have a soundtrack. The film starred actresses such as <a href="/wiki/Libertad_Lamarque" title="Libertad Lamarque">Libertad Lamarque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ada_Cornaro" title="Ada Cornaro">Ada Cornaro</a> who both debuted in the film.\n</p><p>In 1931, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_A._Ferreyra" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 A. Ferreyra">Jos\xc3\xa9 A. Ferreyra</a> directed <i>Mu\xc3\xb1equitas porte\xc3\xb1as</i>, the first Argentine film to be made with <a href="/wiki/Vitaphone" title="Vitaphone">Vitaphone</a> sound synchronisation. That year, Ferreyra made a second sound film, <a href="/wiki/El_Cantar_de_mi_ciudad" class="mw-redirect" title="El Cantar de mi ciudad">El Cantar de mi ciudad</a>, encouraging other early directors to make the transition to sound.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Movietone_sound_system" title="Movietone sound system">Movietone</a> arrived in 1933 and it allowed both voice and music in motion pictures. The first two Argentine cinematographic studios were created: <a href="/wiki/Argentina_Sono_Film" title="Argentina Sono Film">Argentina Sono Film</a> was founded by <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81ngel_Mentasti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="\xc3\x81ngel Mentasti (page does not exist)">\xc3\x81ngel Mentasti</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lumit%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Lumit\xc3\xb3n">Lumit\xc3\xb3n</a> was created by a partnership led by <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Tel%C3%A9maco_Susini" title="Enrique Tel\xc3\xa9maco Susini">Enrique Susini</a>, who was instrumental in the introduction of television to Argentina in 1951.\n</p><p>The first disc-less sound film was <i>Tango</i> (1931), directed by <a href="/wiki/Luis_Maglia_Barth" class="mw-redirect" title="Luis Maglia Barth">Luis Maglia Barth</a> and a key film of the period was the tango film <a href="/wiki/Dancing_(1933_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dancing (1933 film)">Dancing</a> which saw the birth of a number of Argentine stars such as <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Bence" title="Amelia Bence">Amelia Bence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tito_Lusiardo" title="Tito Lusiardo">Tito Lusiardo</a>; other popular actors from the era included <a href="/wiki/Aida_Alberti" class="mw-redirect" title="Aida Alberti">Aida Alberti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armando_Bo" class="mw-redirect" title="Armando Bo">Armando Bo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floren_Delbene" title="Floren Delbene">Floren Delbene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Garc%C3%ADa_Buhr" title="Arturo Garc\xc3\xada Buhr">Arturo Garc\xc3\xada Buhr</a>. Two such features which have endured in local culture are <i><a href="/wiki/Honeysuckle_(film)" title="Honeysuckle (film)">Honeysuckle</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Libertad_Lamarque" title="Libertad Lamarque">Libertad Lamarque</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Casamiento_en_Buenos_Aires" class="mw-redirect" title="Casamiento en Buenos Aires">Casamiento en Buenos Aires</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Nin%C3%AD_Marshall" title="Nin\xc3\xad Marshall">Nin\xc3\xad Marshall</a>. The two 1939 films each featured themes that have become <a href="/wiki/Argentine_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Argentine music">Argentine musical</a> standards, likewise immortalizing the two leading ladies.\n</p><p>Other films included: <i><a href="/wiki/El_alma_del_bandone%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="El alma del bandone\xc3\xb3n">El alma del bandone\xc3\xb3n</a>,</i> <a href="/wiki/Mario_Soffici" title="Mario Soffici">Mario Soffici</a>, 1935; <i>La muchacha de a bordo,</i> <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Romero" title="Manuel Romero">Manuel Romero</a>, 1936; <i><a href="/wiki/Ay%C3%BAdame_a_vivir" class="mw-redirect" title="Ay\xc3\xbadame a vivir">Ay\xc3\xbadame a vivir</a></i>, 1936 by Ferreyra; <i><a href="/wiki/Besos_brujos" class="mw-redirect" title="Besos brujos">Besos brujos</a></i> (1937) by Ferreyra; <i><a href="/wiki/La_vuelta_al_nido" class="mw-redirect" title="La vuelta al nido">La vuelta al nido</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Torres_Rios" class="mw-redirect" title="Leopoldo Torres Rios">Leopoldo Torres Rios</a>, 1938) and <i><a href="/wiki/Such_Is_Life_(1939_film)" title="Such Is Life (1939 film)">Asi es la vida</a></i> (1939) directed by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Mugica" class="mw-redirect" title="Francisco Mugica">Francisco Mugica</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Manuel_Romero" title="Manuel Romero">Manuel Romero</a> was a prominent director of the mid-to-late 1930s and worked in <a href="/wiki/Comedy" title="Comedy">comedy</a> based films often with rising Argentine star <a href="/wiki/Luis_Sandrini" title="Luis Sandrini">Luis Sandrini</a> in films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quijote_del_altillo" title="Don Quijote del altillo">Don Quijote del altillo</a></i>.\n</p><p>The film industry in Argentina reached a pinnacle in the late 1930s and 1940s when an average of forty-two films were produced annually. The films usually included tango, but even when a tango theme was omitted most cinema from this period still included humble heroes and wealthy villains.<sup id="cite_ref-Karush_2010_26\xe2\x80\x9327_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karush_2010_26\xe2\x80\x9327-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In these films, it portrayed hard work and poverty as ennobling and depicted the poor as the primary beneficiaries of Juan Per\xc3\xb3n\'s economic policies. These films, in part supported by Per\xc3\xb3n, were seen as part of the political agenda of peronism.<sup id="cite_ref-Karush_2010_26\xe2\x80\x9327_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karush_2010_26\xe2\x80\x9327-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> By supporting a film industry that attacked greed and supported the working class, Per\xc3\xb3n was able to influence the attitudes of his constituency to build public appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-Karush_2010_26\xe2\x80\x9327_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karush_2010_26\xe2\x80\x9327-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The growing popularity of the <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">cinema of the United States</a>, pressure from the <a href="/wiki/Church-state_relations_in_Argentina" class="mw-redirect" title="Church-state relations in Argentina">Roman Catholic Church</a> and increasing censorship during the <a href="/wiki/Juan_Peron" class="mw-redirect" title="Juan Peron">Per\xc3\xb3n</a> presidency limited the growth of Argentine cinema somewhat, not least because harassment led to the exile of a number of prominent actors, among them <a href="/wiki/Alberto_de_Mendoza" title="Alberto de Mendoza">Alberto de Mendoza</a>, Arturo Garc\xc3\xada Buhr, <a href="/wiki/Nin%C3%AD_Marshall" title="Nin\xc3\xad Marshall">Nin\xc3\xad Marshall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Libertad_Lamarque" title="Libertad Lamarque">Libertad Lamarque</a>, whose rivalry with her colleague <a href="/wiki/Eva_Peron" class="mw-redirect" title="Eva Peron">Eva Duarte</a> turned against her when the latter became First Lady in 1946. Argentine cinema began losing viewership as foreign titles gained an increasing foothold in the Argentine market. The problem eventually became so bad that Argentina tried to curb the influx with the Cinema Law of 1957, establishing the "Instituto Nacional de Cinematograf\xc3\xada" to provide education and funding.\n</p><p>Among the era\'s most successful films were: <i>Historia de una noche,</i> <a href="/wiki/Luis_Saslavsky" title="Luis Saslavsky">Luis Saslavsky</a>, 1941; <i>La dama duende,</i> Luis Saslavsky, 1945; <i>Malambro</i> (<a href="/wiki/Lucas_Demare" title="Lucas Demare">Lucas Demare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Fregonese" title="Hugo Fregonese">Hugo Fregonese</a>, 1945); <a href="/wiki/Alb%C3%A9niz_(film)" title="Alb\xc3\xa9niz (film)">Albeniz</a> (<a href="/wiki/Luis_C%C3%A9sar_Amadori" title="Luis C\xc3\xa9sar Amadori">Luis C\xc3\xa9sar Amadori</a>) starring <a href="/wiki/Pedro_L%C3%B3pez_Lagar" title="Pedro L\xc3\xb3pez Lagar">Pedro L\xc3\xb3pez Lagar</a> (1947); <i>Pelota de trapo</i> (1948) and <i>Crimen de Oribe</i> (1950), <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Torres_R%C3%ADos" title="Leopoldo Torres R\xc3\xados">Leopoldo Torres R\xc3\xados</a>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Las_aguas_bajan_turbias" class="mw-redirect" title="Las aguas bajan turbias">Las aguas bajan turbias</a>,</i> by <a href="/wiki/Hugo_del_Carril" title="Hugo del Carril">Hugo del Carril</a>, 1952. One of the few Argentine actors who made a successful transition into directing was <a href="/wiki/Mario_Soffici" title="Mario Soffici">Mario Soffici</a>, who debuted behind the camera in 1935 to acclaim with <i>El alma del bandone\xc3\xb3n</i> and went on to become an institution in Argentine film over the next generation; among his most memorable work was the film adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Marco_Denevi" title="Marco Denevi">Marco Denevi</a>\'s bestselling mystery, <i><a href="/wiki/Rosaura_a_las_10" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosaura a las 10">Rosaura a la diez</a></i> ("Rosaura at Ten O\'Clock"), for whose 1958 screen release Soffici wrote, directed and starred.\n</p><p>In 1958, the film <i><a href="/wiki/Thunder_Among_the_Leaves" title="Thunder Among the Leaves">Thunder Among the Leaves</a></i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Armando_B%C3%B3" title="Armando B\xc3\xb3">Armando B\xc3\xb3</a> was released. The film featured the later sex-symbol <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Sarli" title="Isabel Sarli">Isabel Sarli</a> in her first starring role, and marked the beginning of her partnership with future husband Armando B\xc3\xb3, which would span almost three decades and made numerous <a href="/wiki/Sexploitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexploitation">sexploitation</a> films.<sup id="cite_ref-latsploitation_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latsploitation-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tronar_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tronar-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boom_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boom-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Now considered a classic,<sup id="cite_ref-boom_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boom-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lanoche_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lanoche-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> a scene in which she bathes in a lake was the first one to feature full frontal nudity in Argentine cinema.<sup id="cite_ref-tronar_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tronar-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RS_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RS-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The film was a highly controversial box-office success; it has been described as a "boom" and "scandalous" and shocked the mostly Catholic Argentine society.<sup id="cite_ref-boom_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boom-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lasdoce_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lasdoce-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In November 1958, <i><a href="/wiki/The_News_and_Courier" class="mw-redirect" title="The News and Courier">The News and Courier</a></i> reported "[a] saucy Latin lass has smashed South American box office records with the most daring dunking since <a href="/wiki/Hedy_Lamarr" title="Hedy Lamarr">Hedy Lamarr</a> disrobed to fame in <i><a href="/wiki/Ecstasy_(film)" title="Ecstasy (film)">Ecstasy</a></i>."<sup id="cite_ref-news_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The movie\'s premiere in <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a>, Uruguay broke box office records, and Sarli\'s bath scene "rocked some Latin American capitals".<sup id="cite_ref-news_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, Sarli was panned by fellow filmmakers for the nude scene.<sup id="cite_ref-news_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The horror genre, little explored by Argentine film-makers, was explored by Argentine director <a href="/wiki/Narciso_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_Menta" title="Narciso Ib\xc3\xa1\xc3\xb1ez Menta">Narciso Ib\xc3\xa1\xc3\xb1ez Menta</a>.\n</p><p>Television, as in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, began to exert pressure on the film market in the 1950s; on the air since the 1951 launch of Channel 7 (public television), Argentine television programming is the oldest in Latin America.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="First_.22New_Cinema.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_&quot;New_Cinema&quot;">First "New Cinema"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First &quot;New Cinema&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Grupo_Cine_Liberaci%C3%B3n" title="Grupo Cine Liberaci\xc3\xb3n">Grupo Cine Liberaci\xc3\xb3n</a></div>\n<p>Since the late 1950s a new generation of film directors took Argentine films to international film festivals. The first wave of such directors was <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Torre-Nilsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Leopoldo Torre-Nilsson">Leopoldo Torre-Nilsson</a>,who "explored aristocratic decadence",<sup id="cite_ref-argentinaautentica1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-argentinaautentica1-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Ayala" title="Fernando Ayala">Fernando Ayala</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Jose_Kohon" class="mw-redirect" title="David Jose Kohon">David Jose Kohon</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Feldman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Simon Feldman (page does not exist)">Simon Feldman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Solanas" title="Fernando Solanas">Fernando "Pino" Solanas</a>, who began by making <i>La Hora de los Hornos</i> ("Hour of the Furnaces", 1966\xe2\x80\x9368) the first documentaries on the political unrest in late-1960s Argentina (at great risk to himself).<sup id="cite_ref-argentinaautentica1_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-argentinaautentica1-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Directors such as <a href="/wiki/Tulio_Demicheli" title="Tulio Demicheli">Tulio Demicheli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Schlieper" title="Carlos Schlieper">Carlos Schlieper</a> began to emerge who often both wrote and directed them. A second generation that achieved a cinematographic style were <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mart%C3%ADnez_Su%C3%A1rez" class="mw-redirect" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Mart\xc3\xadnez Su\xc3\xa1rez">Jos\xc3\xa9 Mart\xc3\xadnez Su\xc3\xa1rez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Ant%C3%ADn" title="Manuel Ant\xc3\xadn">Manuel Ant\xc3\xadn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Favio" title="Leonardo Favio">Leonardo Favio</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s_and_1970s">1960s and 1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1960s and 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alfredo_Alcon.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alfredo_Alcon.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Period-piece master <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Torre_Nilsson" title="Leopoldo Torre Nilsson">Leopoldo la Espada">El Santo de la Espada</a></i> (1970).</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Kurt_Land" title="Kurt Land">Kurt Land</a> directed <i><a href="/wiki/El_Asalto" title="El Asalto">El asalto</a></i> in 1960 starring <a href="/wiki/Alberto_de_Mendoza" title="Alberto de Mendoza">Alberto de Mendoza</a>, a crime drama shot in black and white. <a href="/wiki/Lautaro_Mur%C3%BAa" title="Lautaro Mur\xc3\xbaa">Lautaro Mur\xc3\xbaa</a>, a Chilean actor working in Argentine cinema directed <i><a href="/wiki/Alias_Gardelito" title="Alias Gardelito">Alias Gardelito</a></i> in 1961. The film showed strong political and social undertones and is about the difficulty of living an honest life in the face of an unrelenting <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>. The title of this story is taken from the name of the great <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentine</a> <a href="/wiki/Singer" class="mw-redirect" title="Singer">singer</a> <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Gardel" title="Carlos Gardel">Carlos Gardel</a>, the idol of the <a href="/wiki/Antihero" title="Antihero">antihero</a> Toribio portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Argibay" title="Alberto Argibay">Alberto Argibay</a>. Toribio\'s goal in life is to emulate the famous singer and making his own way successfully in the music business. Yet at the same time, he does not stop his illegal means of making ends meet, <a href="/wiki/Stealing" class="mw-redirect" title="Stealing">stealing</a> and petty thievery. Films such as <i><a href="/wiki/A_hierro_muere" class="mw-redirect" title="A hierro muere">A hierro muere</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Alberto_de_Mendoza" title="Alberto de Mendoza">Alberto de Mendoza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olga_Zubarry" title="Olga Zubarry">Olga Zubarry</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Accidente_703" class="mw-redirect" title="Accidente 703">Accidente 703</a></i> in 1962 were often co-produced with <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and often featured both Argentine and Spanish born actors.\n</p><p>In 1963, <a href="/wiki/Comedy" title="Comedy">comedy</a> films became to feature in Argentine cinema, and films such as <a href="/wiki/Alias_Flequillo" title="Alias Flequillo">Alias Flequillo</a> in 1963 directed by <a href="/wiki/Julio_Saraceni" title="Julio Saraceni">Julio Saraceni</a> starred comedians such as <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Marrone" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Marrone">Jos\xc3\xa9 Marrone</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Aventuras_del_Capit%C3%A1n_Piluso_en_el_Castillo_del_Terror" title="Las Aventuras del Capit\xc3\xa1n Piluso en el Castillo del Terror">Las Aventuras del Capit\xc3\xa1n Piluso en el Castillo del Terror</a></i> starred comedians such as <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Olmedo" title="Alberto Olmedo">Alberto Olmedo</a> who appeared in the genre throughout the 1960s and 1970s appearing in 1967\'s <i><a href="/wiki/El_Andador" title="El Andador">El andador</a></i> and other slap-stick comedies.\nArgentine film and TV was largely limited to light subjects in the perilous late 1970s.\n</p><p>The trend towards <i><a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cin%C3%A9_V%C3%A9rit%C3%A9" class="extiw" title="fr:Cin\xc3\xa9 V\xc3\xa9rit\xc3\xa9">Cin\xc3\xa9 V\xc3\xa9rit\xc3\xa9</a></i> so evident in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in the early 1970s found an Argentine exponent in stage director <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Ren%C3%A1n" title="Sergio Ren\xc3\xa1n">Sergio Ren\xc3\xa1n</a>. His 1974 crime drama <i><a href="/wiki/La_tregua_(1974_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="La tregua (1974 film)">La tregua</a></i> ("The Truce"), his first foray into film, was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award">Oscar</a>. The same year, <a href="/wiki/Osvaldo_Bayer" title="Osvaldo Bayer">Osvaldo Bayer</a> cooperated with the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Province,_Argentina" title="Santa Cruz Province, Argentina">Province of Santa Cruz</a> to make <i><a href="/wiki/La_patagonia_rebelde" class="mw-redirect" title="La patagonia rebelde">La patagonia rebelde</a></i> as an homage to a violently quelled 1922 sheephands\' strike.\n</p><p>Nostalgia was captured by <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Torre_Nilsson" title="Leopoldo Torre Nilsson">Leopoldo Torre Nilsson</a>, whose reworking of Argentine literary classics like <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Mano_en_la_Trampa" class="extiw" title="es:La Mano en la Trampa">The Hand in the Trap</a></i> (1961), <i><a href="/wiki/Martin_Fierro" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Fierro">Martin Fierro</a></i> (1968), <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Siete_Locos" class="extiw" title="es:Los Siete Locos">The Seven Lunatics</a></i> (1973) and <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boquitas_Pintadas" class="extiw" title="es:Boquitas Pintadas">Painted Lips</a></i> (1974) earned him a cult following. Similar in atmosphere, Jose Martinez Suarez\'s moody <i>Los muchachos de antes no usaban arsenico</i> ("Older Men Don\'t Need Arsenic", 1975) takes a turn at murder worthy of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It was memorable as <a href="/wiki/Mario_Soffici" title="Mario Soffici">Mario Soffici</a>\'s last role.\n</p><p>Towards the mid-to-late 1960s, directors such as <a href="/wiki/Armando_Bo" class="mw-redirect" title="Armando Bo">Armando Bo</a> produced <a href="/wiki/Sex_comedies" class="mw-redirect" title="Sex comedies">sex comedies</a> which shocked the audience as they were <a href="/wiki/Soft_porn" class="mw-redirect" title="Soft porn">soft porn</a> and displayed nudity and sex not seen in the industry before. This preference continued into the 1970s, with <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Porcel" title="Jorge Porcel">Jorge Porcel</a>\'s suggestive comedies.\n</p><p>"During the early 1970\'s, Argentina came apart. Government repression was met by insurrections and terrorism. Solanas and Getino contributed by filming two documentary interviews with the exiled Peron. They also founded a magazine, Cine y liberacion. Getino directed <i><a href="/wiki/El_Familiar" title="El Familiar">El Familiar</a></i> (1972), an allegorical fiction feature on the destine of Latin America. Other film makers continued to make Peronist films, and ultra-left groups such as Cine de Base emerged."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n"In 1976, this period of militant documentary and cinematic innovation was violently ruptured by the murder/disappearance of three documentary filmmakers by the Argentine military: Gleyzer, Pablo Szir and Enrique Juarez."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Heavily censored from 1975 until about 1980, Argentine film-makers generally limited themselves to light-hearted subjects. Among the productions during that era was <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9ctor_Olivera_(film_director)" title="H\xc3\xa9ctor Olivera (film director)">H\xc3\xa9ctor Olivera</a>\'s adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Cossa" title="Roberto Cossa">Roberto Cossa</a>\'s play, <i>La nona</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Grandma_(1979_film)" title="Grandma (1979 film)">Grandma</a></i>, 1979). The dark comedy became a reference to the foreign debt interest payments that later saddled the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Argentine economy">Argentine economy</a>. One director who, even as a supporter of the military regime, delved into middle-class neuroses with frankness was <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Siro" title="Fernando Siro">Fernando Siro</a>, an inventive film-maker seemingly insensitive to many of his colleagues\' tribulations, many of whom were forced to leave during the dictatorship. Though his attitudes distanced him from his peers and public, his 1981 tragedy <i>Venido a menos</i> ("Dilapidated") continues to be influential.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_1980s">Early 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Following a loosening of restrictions in 1980, <a href="/wiki/Muck-raking" class="mw-redirect" title="Muck-raking">muck-raking</a> cinema began to make itself evident on the Argentine screen. Plunging head-long into subjects like corruption and impunity (without directly indicting those in power), <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Aristarain" title="Adolfo Aristarain">Adolfo Aristarain</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tiempo_de_revancha" class="mw-redirect" title="Tiempo de revancha">Tiempo de revancha</a></i> ("Time for Revenge", 1981), <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Ayala" title="Fernando Ayala">Fernando Ayala</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Plata_dulce" title="Plata dulce">Plata dulce</a></i> ("Sweet Money," 1982) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eduardo_Calcagno&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eduardo Calcagno (page does not exist)">Eduardo Calcagno</a>\'s <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Los_enemigos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Los enemigos (page does not exist)">Los enemigos</a></i> ("The Enemies," 1983) took hard looks at labor rights abuses, corporate corruption and the day\'s prevailing climate of fear at a time when doing so was often perilous. Petty corruption was also brought up in <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Ayala" title="Fernando Ayala">Fernando Ayala</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/El_Arreglo" class="mw-redirect" title="El Arreglo">El arreglo</a></i> ("The Deal," 1983).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_junta_cinema">Post <i>junta</i> cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post junta cinema">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A new era in Argentine cinema started after the arrival of democracy in 1983; besides a few memorable exceptions like <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Doria" title="Alejandro Doria">Alejandro Doria</a>\'s family comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Waiting_for_the_Hearse" title="Waiting for the Hearse">Esperando la carroza</a></i> ("Waiting for the Hearse", 1985), the era saw a marked decline in the popularity of slapstick comedies towards films with more serious undertones and subject matter.\n</p><p>The first group deals frankly with the repression, torture and the disappearances during the <a href="/wiki/Dirty_War" title="Dirty War">Dirty War</a> in the 1970s and early 1980s. They include: <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9ctor_Olivera_(film_director)" title="H\xc3\xa9ctor Olivera (film director)">Hector Olivera</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Funny_Dirty_Little_War_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Funny Dirty Little War (film)">Funny Little Dirty War</a></i> (1983) and the true story <i><a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Pencils_(film)" title="Night of the Pencils (film)">Night of the Pencils</a></i> (1986); <a href="/wiki/Luis_Puenzo" title="Luis Puenzo">Luis Puenzo</a>\'s Academy Award-winning <i><a href="/wiki/The_Official_Story" title="The Official Story">The Official Story</a></i> (1985); "Pino" Solanas\' <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangos_El_Exilio_de_Gardel" class="extiw" title="es:Tangos El Exilio de Gardel">Tangos</a></i> (1985) and <i><a href="/wiki/Sur_(film)" title="Sur (film)">Sur</a></i> ("South", 1987) and <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Doria" title="Alejandro Doria">Alejandro Doria</a>\'s harrowing <i>Sofia</i> (1987), among others.\n</p><p>Among films dealing with past abuses, one German-Argentine co-production that also deserves mention is <a href="/wiki/Jeanine_Meerapfel" title="Jeanine Meerapfel">Jeanine Meerapfel</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Girlfriend_(film)" title="The Girlfriend (film)">The Girlfriend</a></i> (1988), where Norwegian leading lady <a href="/wiki/Liv_Ullmann" title="Liv Ullmann">Liv Ullmann</a> is cast beside locals <a href="/wiki/Federico_Luppi" title="Federico Luppi">Federico Luppi</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cipe_Lincovski&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cipe Lincovski (page does not exist)">Cipe Lincovski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Laplace" class="mw-redirect" title="Victor Laplace">Victor Laplace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lito_Cruz" title="Lito Cruz">Lito Cruz</a>.\n</p><p>A second group of films includes portrayals of exile and homesickness, like <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Fischermann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alberto Fischermann (page does not exist)">Alberto Fischermann</a>\'s <i>Los d\xc3\xadas de junio</i> ("Days in June," 1985) and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jose_Jusid" class="mw-redirect" title="Juan Jose Jusid">Juan Jose Jusid</a>\'s <i>Made in Argentina</i> (1986), as well as plots rich in subtext, like Miguel Pereira\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ver%C3%B3nico_Cruz_(film)" title="Ver\xc3\xb3nico Cruz (film)">Ver\xc3\xb3nico Cruz</a></i> (1988), Gustavo Mosquera\'s <i>Lo que vendr\xc3\xa1</i> ("The Near Future", 1988) and a cult favorite, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Donovan" title="Martin Donovan">Martin Donovan</a>\'s English-language <i><a href="/wiki/Apartment_Zero" title="Apartment Zero">Apartment Zero</a></i> (1988). These used metaphor, life\'s imponderables and hints at wider socio-political issues to reconcile audiences with recent events.\n</p><p>This can also be said of treatments of controversial literature and painful 19th century history like <a href="/wiki/Maria_Luisa_Bemberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Luisa Bemberg">Maria Luisa Bemberg</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Camila_(film)" title="Camila (film)">Camila</a></i> (1984), <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Sorin" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlos Sorin">Carlos Sorin</a>\'s <i>A King and His Movie</i> (1985) and <a href="/wiki/Eliseo_Subiela" title="Eliseo Subiela">Eliseo Subiela</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Man_Facing_Southeast" title="Man Facing Southeast">Man Facing Southeast</a></i> (1986).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_cinema">Contemporary cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Contemporary cinema">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Argentine_films_of_the_1990s" title="List of Argentine films of the 1990s">List of Argentine films of the 1990s</a></div>\n<p>The 1990s brought another <i>New Argentine Cinema</i> wave, marked by classical cinema and a twist from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Argentine_Production&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Independent Argentine Production (page does not exist)">Independent Argentine Production</a>.\n</p><p>In 1991, Marco Bechis\' <i><a href="/wiki/Alambrado" title="Alambrado">Alambrado</a></i> ("Chicken Wire") was released. That same year, activist film-maker <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Solanas" title="Fernando Solanas">Fernando "Pino" Solanas</a> released his third major film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Journey_(1992_film)" title="The Journey (1992 film)">The Journey</a></i> (1992), a surreal overview of prevailing social conditions in Latin America. Existential angst continued to dominate the Argentine film agenda, however, with <a href="/wiki/Eliseo_Subiela" title="Eliseo Subiela">Eliseo Subiela</a>\'s <i>El lado oscuro del corazon</i> ("Dark Side of the Heart," 1992) and Adolfo Aristarain\'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Place_in_the_World_(film)" title="A Place in the World (film)">A Place in the World</a></i> (1992) - notable also for its having been nominated for an Oscar.\n</p><p>Later in the 1990s, the focus began to shift towards Argentina\'s mounting social problems, such as rising homelessness and crime. <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Agresti" title="Alejandro Agresti">Alejandro Agresti</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_vice_versa" class="mw-redirect" title="Buenos Aires vice versa">Buenos Aires vice versa</a></i> (1996) rescued the beauty of feelings in the shadows of poverty in Buenos Aires and <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Stagnaro" title="Bruno Stagnaro">Bruno Stagnaro</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pizza,_Beer,_and_Cigarettes_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pizza, Beer, and Cigarettes (film)">Pizza, Beer, and Cigarettes</a></i> (1997) looked into the human duality of even the most incorrigible and violent individuals.\n</p><p>Having an intense past and rich cultural heritage to draw on, directors continued to reach back with moody period pieces like <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Mignogna" title="Eduardo Mignogna">Eduardo Mignogna</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Flop_(1990_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flop (1990 film)">Flop</a> </i> (1990), Maria Luisa Bemberg\'s <i><a href="/wiki/De_eso_no_se_habla" class="mw-redirect" title="De eso no se habla">De eso no se habla</a></i> ("You Don\'t Discuss Certain Things," 1993, her last and one of Italian leading man\'s <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Mastroianni" title="Marcello Mastroianni">Marcello Mastroianni</a>\'s last roles, as well), Santiago Oves\' rendition of <a href="/wiki/Rodolfo_Walsh" title="Rodolfo Walsh">Rodolfo Walsh</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>-esque tale <i><a href="/wiki/Asesinato_a_distancia" class="mw-redirect" title="Asesinato a distancia">Asesinato a distancia</a></i> ("Murder from a Distance," 1998), as well as bio-pics like <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Favio" title="Leonardo Favio">Leonardo Favio</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Raging_Bull" title="Raging Bull">Raging Bull</a></i>-esque <i><a href="/wiki/Gatica,_el_mono" title="Gatica, el mono">Gatica, el mono</a></i> (1993) and Javier Torre\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Lola_Mora" title="Lola Mora">Lola Mora</a></i> (1996).\n</p><p>Political history was re-examined with films like <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eduardo_Calcagno&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eduardo Calcagno (page does not exist)">Eduardo Calcagno</a>\'s controversial take on 1970s-era Argentine film censor Paulino Tato (played by Argentina\'s most prolific character actor, <a href="/wiki/Ulises_Dumont" title="Ulises Dumont">Ulises Dumont</a>) in <i><a href="/wiki/El_Censor" class="mw-redirect" title="El Censor">El censor</a></i> (1995), Juan J. Jusid\'s indictment of the old compulsory military training system, <i><a href="/wiki/Bajo_Bandera" title="Bajo Bandera">Bajo bandera</a></i> ("At Half Mast," 1997), <a href="/wiki/Marco_Bechis" title="Marco Bechis">Marco Bechis</a>\' <i><a href="/wiki/Garage_Olimpo" class="mw-redirect" title="Garage Olimpo">Garage Olimpo</a></i> (1999), which took viewers into one of the dictatorship\'s most brutal torture dungeons and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Carlos_Desanzo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Juan Carlos Desanzo (page does not exist)">Juan Carlos Desanzo</a>\'s answer to <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Evita_(1996_film)" title="Evita (1996 film)">Evita</a></i>, his 1996 <i>Eva Per\xc3\xb3n</i> (a portrait of a far more complex first lady than the one <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lloyd_Webber" title="Andrew Lloyd Webber">Andrew Lloyd Webber</a> had taken up).\n</p><p>Popular culture had its turn on the Argentine screen. Alejandro Doria\'s <i>Cien veces no debo</i> ("I Don\'t Owe You Forever," 1990) took an irreverent peek into a typical middle-class Argentine home, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jose_Santiso&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jose Santiso (page does not exist)">Jose Santiso</a>\'s <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_mi_barrio_con_amor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="De mi barrio con amor (page does not exist)">De mi barrio con amor</a></i> ("From My Neighborhood, with Love," 1996) is a must-see for anyone planning to visit <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>\' bohemian <a href="/wiki/San_Telmo" class="mw-redirect" title="San Telmo">southside</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodolfo_Pagliere&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rodolfo Pagliere (page does not exist)">Rodolfo Pagliere</a>\'s <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_d%C3%ADa_que_Maradona_conoci%C3%B3_a_Gardel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="El d\xc3\xada que Maradona conoci\xc3\xb3 a Gardel (page does not exist)">El d\xc3\xada que Maradona conoci\xc3\xb3 a Gardel</a></i> ("The Day <a href="/wiki/Diego_Maradona" title="Diego Maradona">Maradona</a> Met <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Gardel" title="Carlos Gardel">Gardel</a>," 1996) is an inventive ode to two standards of Argentine culture.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span title="Nine Queens">Nine Queens</a></i> (2000), his gothic <i><a href="/wiki/El_Aura" class="mw-redirect" title="El Aura">El aura</a></i> (2005) and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Campanella" title="Juan Jos\xc3\xa9 Campanella">Juan Jos\xc3\xa9 Campanella</a>\'s teary <i><a href="/wiki/Son_of_the_Bride" title="Son of the Bride">Son of the Bride</a></i> (2001) have received praise and awards around the world. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Carlos_Desanzo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Juan Carlos Desanzo (page does not exist)">Juan Carlos Desanzo</a> cast <a href="/wiki/Miguel_%C3%81ngel_Sol%C3%A1" title="Miguel \xc3\x81ngel Sol\xc3\xa1">Miguel \xc3\x81ngel Sol\xc3\xa1</a> (best known for his role in <a href="/wiki/Tango_(1998_film)" title="Tango (1998 film)"><i>Tango</i></a>) as the immortal <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/El_Amor_y_el_Espanto" title="El Amor y el Espanto">El Amor y el Espanto</a></i> ("Love and Foreboding", 2001), a look at the writer\'s struggles with <a href="/wiki/Juan_Peron" class="mw-redirect" title="Juan Peron">Per\xc3\xb3n</a>-era intimidation as well as with his own insecurities.\n</p><p>Always politically active, Argentine film continues to treat hard subjects, like Spanish director <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manane_Rodr%C3%ADguez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Manane Rodr\xc3\xadguez (page does not exist)">Manane Rodr\xc3\xadguez</a>\'s look at abducted children, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Steps" title="The Lost Steps">The Lost Steps</a></i> (2001) and "Pino" Solanas\' perhaps definitive film on the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_economic_crisis_(1999%E2%80%932002)" class="mw-redirect" title="Argentine economic crisis (1999\xe2\x80\x932002)">2001 economic crisis</a>, <i>Memorias del saqueo</i> ("Memories of the Riot", 2004). <a href="/wiki/Trist%C3%A1n_Bauer" title="Trist\xc3\xa1n Bauer">Trist\xc3\xa1n Bauer</a> took audiences back to soldiers\' dehumanizing <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> experience with <i><a href="/wiki/Blessed_by_Fire" title="Blessed by Fire">Blessed by Fire</a></i> (2005) and <a href="/wiki/Adri%C3%A1n_Caetano" title="Adri\xc3\xa1n Caetano">Adri\xc3\xa1n Caetano</a> follows four football players through their 1977 escape from certain death in <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_an_Escape" title="Chronicle of an Escape">Chronicle of an Escape</a></i> (2006).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lucrecia_Martel" title="Lucrecia Martel">Lucrecia Martel</a>\'s 2001 debut feature film <i><a href="/wiki/La_Ci%C3%A9naga" title="La Ci\xc3\xa9naga">La Ci\xc3\xa9naga</a></i> ("The Swamp"), about an indulgent <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeois</a> extended family spending the summertime in a decrepit vacation home in <a href="/wiki/Salta" title="Salta">Salta</a>, was internationally highly acclaimed upon release and introduced a new and vital voice to Argentine cinema.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> For film scholar David Oubi\xc3\xb1a, it is "one of the highest achievements" of the New Argentine Cinema, coincidentally timed with Argentina\'s <a href="/wiki/December_2001_riots_in_Argentina" title="December 2001 riots in Argentina">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%932002_Argentine_great_depression" title="1998\xe2\x80\x932002 Argentine great depression">economic crisis</a> that it "became a rare expression of an extremely troubled moment in the nation\xe2\x80\x99s recent history. It is a masterpiece of singular maturity".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Martel\'s succeeding films would also receive further international acclaim, such as the adolescent drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Girl" title="The Holy Girl">The Holy Girl</a></i> (2004),<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> the psychological thriller <i><a href="/wiki/The_Headless_Woman_(2008_film)" title="The Headless Woman (2008 film)">The Headless Woman</a></i> (2008),<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Historical_period_drama" title="Historical period drama">period drama</a> adaptation <i><a href="/wiki/Zama_(2017_film)" title="Zama (2017 film)">Zama</a></i> (2017).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Responding to its sentimental public, Argentine film at times returns to subjects of the heart. <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lipszyc&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David Lipszyc (page does not exist)">David Lipszyc</a>\'s grainy portrait of depression-era Argentina, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_astillero_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="El astillero (film) (page does not exist)">El astillero</a></i> ("The Shipyard", 2000) was a hit with critics, <a href="/wiki/Paula_Hernandez" class="mw-redirect" title="Paula Hernandez">Paula Hernandez</a>\'s touching ode to immigrants, <i><a href="/wiki/Inheritance_(2001_film)" title="Inheritance (2001 film)">Inheritance</a></i> (2001), has become something of a sleeper, <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Aristarain" title="Adolfo Aristarain">Adolfo Aristarain</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Common_Places" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Places">Common Places</a></i> (2002) follows an elderly professor into retirement, <i><a href="/wiki/Cleopatra_(2003_film)" title="Cleopatra (2003 film)">Cleopatra</a></i> (2003), Eduardo Mignona\'s tale of an unlikely friendship, received numerous awards, as did <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Sor%C3%ADn" title="Carlos Sor\xc3\xadn">Carlos Sor\xc3\xadn</a>\'s touching <i><a href="/wiki/El_perro" title="El perro">El perro</a></i> ("The Dog", 2004). Emotional negativity, a staple for filmmakers anywhere, was explored in <a href="/wiki/Mario_Sabato" class="mw-redirect" title="Mario Sabato">Mario Sabato</a>\'s <i>India Pravile</i> (2003), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_D%27Intino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Francisco D&#39;Intino (page does not exist)">Francisco D\'Intino</a>\'s <i>La esperanza</i> (2005) and <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Rotter" title="Ariel Rotter">Ariel Rotter</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/El_otro" title="El otro">El otro</a></i> ("The Other", 2007) each deals with mid-life crises in very different ways. The pronounced sentimentality of the average Argentine was also the subject of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Duvall" title="Robert Duvall">Robert Duvall</a>\'s 2002 <i><a href="/wiki/Assassination_Tango" title="Assassination Tango">Assassination Tango</a></i>, a deceptively simple crime drama that shows that still waters do, indeed, run deep.\n</p><p>Buffeted by years of economic malaise and encroachment of the domestic film market by foreign (mainly, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>) titles, the Argentine film industry has been supported by the 1987 creation of the National Institute of Cinema and Audioviual Arts (<a href="/wiki/INCAA" class="mw-redirect" title="INCAA">INCAA</a>), a publicly subsidized film underwriter that, since 1987, has produced 130 full-length <a href="/wiki/Art_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Art house">art house</a> titles.\n</p><p>The decade ended on a high with the 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_in_Their_Eyes" title="The Secret in Their Eyes">The Secret in Their Eyes</a></i> receiving critical praise, winning the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Oscar for Best Foreign Language Film</a> at the <a href="/wiki/82nd_Academy_Awards" title="82nd Academy Awards">82nd Academy Awards</a>, three weeks after being awarded the <a href="/wiki/Goya_Award_for_Best_Spanish_Language_Foreign_Film" class="mw-redirect" title="Goya Award for Best Spanish Language Foreign Film">Goya Award for Best Spanish Language Foreign Film</a> of 2009.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinema_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Anthology_film" title="Anthology film">anthology film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Wild_Tales_(film)" title="Wild Tales (film)">Wild Tales</a></i> (<i>Relatos Salvajes</i> in Spanish) directed by <a href="/wiki/Dami%C3%A1n_Szifron" title="Dami\xc3\xa1n Szifron">Dami\xc3\xa1n Szifron</a> was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Best Foreign Language Film</a> at the <a href="/wiki/87th_Academy_Awards" title="87th Academy Awards">87th Academy Awards</a> and won the <a href="/wiki/Goya_Awards" title="Goya Awards">Goya Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Goya_Award_for_Best_Iberoamerican_Film" title="Goya Award for Best Iberoamerican Film">Best Iberoamerican Film</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span
