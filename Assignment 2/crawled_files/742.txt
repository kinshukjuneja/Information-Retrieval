class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parlick_234-34.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Parlick" title="Parlick">Parlick</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Forest of Bowland</b>, also known as the <b>Bowland Fells</b>, is an area of barren <a href="/wiki/Gritstone" title="Gritstone">gritstone</a> <a href="/wiki/Fell" title="Fell">fells</a>, deep valleys and <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a>, mostly in north-east <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, England with a small part in <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a> (before 1974, some of the area was in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>). It is a western <a href="/wiki/Spur_(mountain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spur (mountain)">spur</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a><sup id="cite_ref-Great_Britain_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Britain-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and was once described as the "Switzerland of England".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The Forest of Bowland has been designated an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) since 1964. \nThe Forest of Bowland AONB also includes a detached part known as the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Pendle" title="Forest of Pendle">Forest of Pendle</a> separated from the main part by the <a href="/wiki/River_Ribble" title="River Ribble">Ribble</a> Valley, and anciently a forest with its own separate history. One of the best-known features of the area is <a href="/wiki/Pendle_Hill" title="Pendle Hill">Pendle Hill</a>, which lies in Pendle Forest. There are more than 500 listed buildings and 18 scheduled monuments within the AONB.\n</p><p>Bowland survives as the north-western remainder of the ancient wilderness that once stretched over a huge part of England, encompassing the Forest of Bowland, <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Forest" title="Sherwood Forest">Sherwood Forest</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>), the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>)  and <a href="/wiki/Savernake_Forest" title="Savernake Forest">Savernake Forest</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>). While the <a href="/wiki/Trough_of_Bowland" title="Trough of Bowland">Trough of Bowland</a> (the valley and high pass connecting the <a href="/wiki/River_Wyre" title="River Wyre">Wyre</a> (at Marshaw) and Langden Brook and dividing the upland core of Bowland into two main blocks) represents the area, to many, on account of its popularity, it is in fact only a small part of the wider Forest of Bowland area.\n</p><p>The hills on the western side of the Forest of Bowland attract walkers from <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Lancaster, England">Lancaster</a> and the surrounding area. Overlooking Lancaster is <a href="/wiki/Clougha_Pike" title="Clougha Pike">Clougha Pike</a>, the western-most hill. The hills form a large <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe" title="Horseshoe">horseshoe</a> shape with its open end facing west. Clockwise from Lancaster the hills are <a href="/wiki/Clougha_Pike" title="Clougha Pike">Clougha Pike</a> (413&#160;m or 1,355&#160;ft), <a href="/wiki/Grit_Fell" title="Grit Fell">Grit Fell</a> (468&#160;m or 1,535&#160;ft), <a href="/wiki/Ward%27s_Stone" title="Ward&#39;s Stone">Ward\'s Stone</a> (561&#160;m or 1,841&#160;ft), <a href="/wiki/Wolfhole_Crag" title="Wolfhole Crag">Wolfhole Crag</a> (527&#160;m or 1,729&#160;ft), <a href="/wiki/White_Hill_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="White Hill (England)">White Hill</a> (544&#160;m or 1,785&#160;ft), Whins Brow (476&#160;m or 1,562&#160;ft), Totridge (496&#160;m or 1,627&#160;ft), <a href="/wiki/Parlick" title="Parlick">Parlick</a> (432&#160;m or 1,417&#160;ft), <a href="/wiki/Fair_Snape_Fell" title="Fair Snape Fell">Fair Snape Fell</a> (510&#160;m or 1,670&#160;ft), <a href="/wiki/Bleasdale" title="Bleasdale">Bleasdale</a> Moor (429&#160;m or 1,407&#160;ft), and <a href="/wiki/Hawthornthwaite_Fell" title="Hawthornthwaite Fell">Hawthornthwaite Fell</a> (478&#160;m or 1,568&#160;ft).  Considerable areas of the Bowland fells were used for military training during the Second World War, and there are still unexploded bombs in some areas.\n</p><p>The area contains the <a href="/wiki/Centre_points_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Centre points of the United Kingdom">geographic centre of Great Britain</a> which is close to the Whitendale Hanging Stones, around four miles (6&#160;km) north of <a href="/wiki/Dunsop_Bridge" title="Dunsop Bridge">Dunsop Bridge</a>. The historical extent of Bowland Forest is divided into two large administrative townships, Great Bowland (<a href="/wiki/Bowland_Forest_High" title="Bowland Forest High">Bowland Forest High</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bowland_Forest_Low" title="Bowland Forest Low">Bowland Forest Low</a>) and Little Bowland (<a href="/wiki/Bowland-with-Leagram" title="Bowland-with-Leagram">Bowland-with-Leagram</a>), but the modern-day AONB covers a much larger area.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Bowland" title="Lordship of Bowland">Lordship of Bowland</a></div>\n<p>A region of the British kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, Bowland was absorbed into <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a> in the 7th century. In turn, as Northumbrian influence waned, the westernmost areas of Bowland became part of <a href="/wiki/Amounderness" class="mw-redirect" title="Amounderness">Amounderness</a>, a territory forged by the Norse <i>hold</i> Agmundr, a vassal of <a href="/wiki/Eowils_and_Halfdan" title="Eowils and Halfdan">Eowils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eowils_and_Halfdan" title="Eowils and Halfdan">Halfdan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eowils_and_Halfdan" title="Eowils and Halfdan">Ingwaer</a>, co-kings of <a href="/wiki/Jorvik" class="mw-redirect" title="Jorvik">Jorvik</a>, in the early 10th century.\n</p><p>In 926, Amounderness was annexed by <a href="/wiki/Aethelstan" class="mw-redirect" title="Aethelstan">Aethelstan</a>, king of the West Saxons, as a spoil of war. In 934, he granted it to <a href="/wiki/Wulfstan_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Wulfstan I">Wulfstan I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a>. According to Aethelstan\'s grant, Amounderness at that time stretched "from the sea along the <a href="/wiki/River_Cocker_(Lancashire)" class="mw-redirect" title="River Cocker (Lancashire)">Cocker</a> to the source of that river, from that source straight to another spring which is called in <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a>, <i>"Dunshop"</i>, thus down the riverlet to the <a href="/wiki/River_Hodder" title="River Hodder">Hodder</a>, in the same direction to the <a href="/wiki/River_Ribble" title="River Ribble">Ribble</a> and thus along that river through the middle of the channel to the sea".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As such, Amounderness encompassed a significant portion of western and south-western Bowland.\n</p><p>Ekwall thus describes the eastern boundary of Amounderness as "being formed by the fells on the Yorkshire border";<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> a description which places the ancient boundary firmly within the modern-day Forest of Bowland. While it is difficult to pinpoint <i>Dunshop</i>, the confluence of the rivers Dunsop and Hodder at <a href="/wiki/Dunsop_Bridge" title="Dunsop Bridge">Dunsop Bridge</a> seems a likely locale, situated as it is close to the eastern mouth of the Trough of Bowland whose Grey Stone marks the line of the pre-1974 county boundary.\n</p><p>Contrary to the popular histories, the origins of the name "Bowland" have nothing to do with archery ("the land of the bow") or with mediaeval cattle farms or vaccaries (Old Norse, <i>buu-</i>, farmstead). The name derives from the Old Norse <i>boga-/bogi-</i>, meaning a "bend in a river". It is a 10th-century coinage used to describe the topography of the Hodder basin, with its characteristic meandering river and brooks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Ford (formerly known as Boward), a ford that sits on the wide, pronounced bend of the Hodder at its <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> with Foulscales Brook, due south-west of Newton.\n</p><p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>, Bowland was held by <a href="/wiki/Tostig" class="mw-redirect" title="Tostig">Tostig</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Godwin,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Godwin, Earl of Wessex">Godwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Wessex" title="Earl of Wessex">Earl of Wessex</a>. However, as feudal entities, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_forest" title="Royal forest">Forest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(division)" title="Liberty (division)">Liberty</a> of Bowland were created by <a href="/wiki/William_Rufus" class="mw-redirect" title="William Rufus">William Rufus</a> sometime after <a href="/wiki/Domesday" class="mw-redirect" title="Domesday">Domesday</a> and granted to his vassal <a href="/wiki/Roger_de_Poitou" class="mw-redirect" title="Roger de Poitou">Roger de Poitou</a>, possibly to reward Poitou for his role in defeating the Scots army of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Malcolm III">Malcolm III</a> in 1091\xe2\x80\x9392. In all likelihood, it was this grant that subsumed the eastern portion of Amounderness into the <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Bowland" title="Lordship of Bowland">Lordship of Bowland</a> for the first time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>By the end of the 11th century, the Forest and Liberty came into the possession of the <a href="/wiki/De_Lacy" title="De Lacy">De Lacys</a>, Lords of <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a>. In 1102, along with the grant of the adjacent fee of <a href="/wiki/Clitheroe" title="Clitheroe">Clitheroe</a> and further holdings in <a href="/wiki/Hornby,_Lancashire" class="mw-redirect" title="Hornby, Lancashire">Hornby</a> and Amounderness, they came to form the basis of what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Honor_of_Clitheroe" class="mw-redirect" title="Honor of Clitheroe">Honor of Clitheroe</a>.\n</p><p>In 1311, the Honor of Clitheroe was subsumed into the <a href="/wiki/Earldom_of_Lancaster" class="mw-redirect" title="Earldom of Lancaster">Earldom of Lancaster</a>. Between 1351 and 1661, it was administered as part of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a>. By the late 14th century, Bowland comprised a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Forest">Royal Forest</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(division)" title="Liberty (division)">Liberty</a> of ten manors spanning eight townships and four parishes and covered an area of almost 300 square miles (800&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) on the historic borders of Lancashire and Yorkshire. The manors within the Liberty  were <a href="/wiki/Slaidburn" title="Slaidburn">Slaidburn</a> (<a href="/wiki/Newton-in-Bowland" title="Newton-in-Bowland">Newton-in-Bowland</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Bradford,_Lancashire" title="West Bradford, Lancashire">West Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grindleton" title="Grindleton">Grindleton</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>) <a href="/wiki/Slaidburn" title="Slaidburn">Knowlmere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waddington,_Lancashire" title="Waddington, Lancashire">Waddington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Easington,_Lancashire" title="Easington, Lancashire">Easington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bashall_Eaves" title="Bashall Eaves">Bashall Eaves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Mitton" title="Great Mitton">Mitton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Mitton" title="Great Mitton">Withgill (Crook)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leagram" class="mw-redirect" title="Leagram">Leagram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slaidburn" title="Slaidburn">Hammerton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slaidburn" title="Slaidburn">Dunnow (Battersby)</a> .<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Pendle Forest was also part of the Honor of Clitheroe, but administered as part of the Forest of <a href="/wiki/Blackburnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackburnshire">Blackburnshire</a>, entirely in Lancashire.\n</p><p>In 1661, the 28 manors contained within the former Honor of Clitheroe, including the Forest and Liberty of Bowland, were granted by the Crown to General <a href="/wiki/George_Monck,_1st_Duke_of_Albemarle" title="George Monck, 1st Duke of Albemarle">George Monck</a> as part of the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Albemarle" title="Duke of Albemarle">Dukedom of Albermarle</a>. Monck had been a key figure in the restoration of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Bowland" title="Lordship of Bowland">Lordship of Bowland</a> then descended through the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Montagu" title="Duke of Montagu">Montagu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Buccleuch" title="Duke of Buccleuch">Buccleuch</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Towneley_(family)" class="mw-redirect" title="Towneley (family)">Towneley</a> families.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bowbearer" title="Bowbearer">Bowbearers</a> of the Forest of Bowland have been appointed since the 12th century. A Bowbearer was originally a noble who acted as ceremonial attendant to the <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Bowland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord of Bowland">Lord of Bowland</a>, latterly the king, by bearing (carrying) his hunting bow, but over the centuries the Bowbearer\'s role underwent many changes. In April 2010 it was reported that the current 16th <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Bowland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord of Bowland">Lord of Bowland</a> had revived the office of Bowbearer and appointed Robert Parker the first Bowbearer of the Forest in almost 150 years.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Forest of Bowland had its own forest courts \xe2\x80\x93<i> woodmote</i> and <i>swainmote</i> \xe2\x80\x93 from early times. These appear to have been abandoned in the 1830s around the time of Peregrine Towneley\xe2\x80\x99s acquisition of the Bowland Forest Estate. The halmote court at <a href="/wiki/Slaidburn" title="Slaidburn">Slaidburn</a> was disbanded following the abolition of copyhold by the Law of Property Act in 1922. General forest law in Britain was finally repealed by statute in 1971, more than 900 years after its introduction by the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>. The original Bowland Forest courts appear to have been held at Hall Hill near Radholme Laund before moving to <a href="/wiki/Whitewell" title="Whitewell">Whitewell</a> sometime in the 14th century.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/St_Hubert" class="mw-redirect" title="St Hubert">St Hubert</a>, the patron saint of hunting, is also patron saint of the Forest of Bowland and has a chapel dedicated to him in <a href="/wiki/Dunsop_Bridge" title="Dunsop Bridge">Dunsop Bridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This chapel was founded by Richard Eastwood of Thorneyholme, land agent to the Towneley family. Eastwood was the last known Bowbearer of the Forest of Bowland. An acclaimed breeder of racehorses and shorthorn cattle, he died in 1871 and is buried at St Hubert\'s.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The name "forest" is used in its traditional sense of "a <a href="/wiki/Royal_forest" title="Royal forest">royal hunting ground</a>", and rather than being covered by trees, much of the land is <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">heather</a> moorland and <a href="/wiki/Blanket_bog" title="Blanket bog">blanket bog</a>. In the past <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_cat" class="mw-redirect" title="Wild cat">wild cats</a> and game roamed the forest.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grouse_shooting">Grouse shooting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grouse shooting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>It is an important area for <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">grouse</a> <a href="/wiki/Driven_grouse_shooting" title="Driven grouse shooting">shooting</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Abbeystead_House" title="Abbeystead House">Abbeystead</a> estate holds the record for the <a href="/wiki/Driven_grouse_shooting" title="Driven grouse shooting">biggest grouse bag</a> in a day; when on 12 August 1915, 2,929 birds were shot by eight guns (shooters).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_areas">Protected areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protected areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>38,940.5 acres (15,759&#160;ha) of the Bowland Fells is designated a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The area is internationally important for its upland bird populations and under the <a href="/wiki/Habitats_Directive" title="Habitats Directive">Habitats Directive</a> "Bowland Fells" has been designated a <a href="/wiki/Special_Protection_Area" title="Special Protection Area">Special Protection Area</a> (designated especially for breeding <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">merlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harrier</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hen_harriers">Hen harriers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hen harriers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harrier</a> is the symbol of the AONB.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In the Forest of Bowland and other upland areas of England, the hen harrier is under threat from illegal persecution.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> None bred in England in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPBNews_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPBNews-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The RSPB takes the view that licensing of driven grouse shooting is necessary so that, for example, crimes committed on estates managed for shooting should result in the withdrawal of their right to operate.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_recovery_of_the_species">Partial recovery of the species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Partial recovery of the species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In sustainable future for Hen harriers in England and Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The project aims to work constructively with landowners and the shooting community.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_Scotland_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Scotland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 2015 a single chick fledged in the Forest.\nIn 2018 two nests were reported on land belonging to United Utilities.<sup id="cite_ref-&quot;&quot;aonb_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-&quot;&quot;aonb-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The nests have been protected by volunteers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_geology">Geography and AONB</div></div></div>\n<p>Bowland is dominated by a central upland landform of deeply incised gritstone <a href="/wiki/Fell" title="Fell">fells</a> covered with tracts of heather-covered <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> moorland and <a href="/wiki/Blanket_bog" title="Blanket bog">blanket bog</a>. The lower slopes of the fells are dotted with stone-built farms and small villages and are criss-crossed by drystone walls enclosing reclaimed moorland pasture. Steep-sided wooded valleys link the upland and lowland landscapes. In the northeast of the area are extensive coniferous plantations and the eastern limestone areas support high-quality species-rich meadows.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Caves in the area include Hell Hole, Whitewell Cave and Whitewell Pot. It is surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales" title="Yorkshire Dales">Yorkshire Dales</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Pennines" title="South Pennines">South Pennines</a>, from which the fells are separated by the <a href="/wiki/Aire_Gap" title="Aire Gap">Aire Gap</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Ribble" title="River Ribble">Ribble Valley</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_and_visitor_centre">Museum and visitor centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Museum and visitor centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Bowland Visitor Centre is located in <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Fell_Country_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Beacon Fell Country Park">Beacon Fell Country Park</a> in the AONB. It is managed by Lancashire County Council Countryside Service and provides visitors with information about the AONB and surrounding countryside.\n</p><p>There is a display on the ecology of the Forest of Bowland in <a href="/wiki/Clitheroe_Castle_Museum" title="Clitheroe Castle Museum">Clitheroe Castle Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_of_Bowland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Bowland Challenge was a fundraising event held in 2006\xe2\x80\x9311 (excluding 2010) in which teams of walkers navigated around a series of grid references over a ten-hour period.
