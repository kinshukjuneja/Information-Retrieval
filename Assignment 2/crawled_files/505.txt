class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress It is Cornwall\'s <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a>, single city, and centre for administration, leisure and retail. Truro\'s population was recorded as 18,766 in the 2011 census.<sup id="cite_ref-newcensus_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newcensus-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> People from Truro are known as Truronians.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As the southernmost city in mainland Britain, Truro grew as a centre of trade from its port and then as a <a href="/wiki/Stannary_town" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary town">stannary town</a> for the tin mining industry. Its <a href="/wiki/Truro_Cathedral" title="Truro Cathedral">cathedral</a> was completed in 1910. Places of interest include the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Museum" title="Royal Cornwall Museum">Royal Cornwall Museum</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hall_for_Cornwall" title="Hall for Cornwall">Hall for href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">15</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The origin of Truro\'s name is debated. It has been said to derive from the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> <i>tri-veru</i> meaning "three rivers", but authorities such as the <i>Oxford Dictionary of English Place Names</i> reject this theory. There are doubts about the "tru" part meaning "three". An expert on Cornish place-names, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Padel" title="Oliver Padel">Oliver Padel</a>, in <i>A Popular Dictionary of Cornish Place-names</i>, called the "three rivers" meaning "possible".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Alternatively the name may derive from <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Brythonic/tre%CE%B2" class="extiw" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Brythonic/tre\xce\xb2">tre</a>-uro</i> or similar, i. e. the settlement on the river <i>Uro</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(April 2016)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The earliest records and archaeological findings of a permanent settlement in the Truro area date from <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman times</a>. A castle was built there in the 12th century by <a href="/wiki/Richard_de_Luci" title="Richard de Luci">Richard de Luci</a>, Chief Justice of England in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a>, who for his services to the court was granted land in Cornwall, including the area surrounding the confluence of the two rivers. The town grew in the shadow of the castle and was awarded borough status to further economic activity. The castle has long since disappeared.\n</p><p>Richard de Lucy fought in Cornwall under Count Alan of Brittany after leaving Falaise late in 1138. The small <a href="/wiki/Adulterine_castle" title="Adulterine castle">adulterine castle</a> at Truro, Cornwall (originally the parish of Kenwyn), later known as "Castellum de Guelon" was probably built by him in 1139\xe2\x80\x931140. He styled himself "Richard de Lucy, de Trivereu". The castle later passed to Reginald FitzRoy (also known as Reginald de Dunstanville), an illegitimate son of Henry I, when he was invested by King Stephen as the first Earl of Cornwall. Reginald married Mabel FitzRichard, daughter of William FitzRichard, a substantial landholder in Cornwall. The 75-foot (23&#160;m)-diameter castle was in ruins by 1270 and the motte was levelled in 1840. Today <a href="/wiki/Truro_Crown_Court" title="Truro Crown Court">Truro Crown Court</a> stands on the site. In a charter of about 1170, Reginald FitzRoy confirmed to the burgesses of Truro the privileges granted by Richard de Lucy. Richard held ten <a href="/wiki/Knight%27s_fee" title="Knight&#39;s fee">knights\' fees</a> in Cornwall prior to 1135 and at his death the county still accounted for a third of his considerable total holding.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the start of the 14th century Truro was an important port, due to its inland location away from invaders, prosperity from the fishing industry, and a new role as one of Cornwall\'s <a href="/wiki/Stannary_town" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary town">stannary towns</a> for assaying and stamping <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and copper from Cornish mines. The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> brought a trade <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a> and an exodus of the population that left the town in a very neglected state. Trade gradually returned and the town regained prosperity in the <a href="/wiki/Tudors" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudors">Tudor</a> period. Local government was awarded in 1589 by a new charter granted by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>, giving Truro an elected mayor and control over the port of <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a> in the 17th century, Truro raised a sizeable force to fight for the king and a royalist mint was set up. Defeat by the Parliamentary troops came in 1646 and the mint was moved to <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>. Later in the century, Falmouth was awarded its own charter, giving it rights to its harbour and starting a long rivalry between the two towns. The dispute was settled in 1709 with control of the <a href="/wiki/River_Fal" title="River Fal">River Fal</a> divided between them. The arms of the city of Truro are "Gules the base wavy of six Argent and Azure, thereon an ancient ship of three masts under sail, on each topmast a banner of St George, on the waves in base two fishes of the second."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boscawen_1810.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Boscawen_1810.jpg/220px-Boscawen_1810.jpg" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Boscawen_1810.jpg/330px-Boscawen_1810.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Boscawen_1810.jpg/440px-Boscawen_1810.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1085" data-file-height="636" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boscawen_1810.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boscawen Street in 1810</div></div></div>\n<p>Truro prospered in the 18th\xe2\x80\x9319th centuries. Industry flourished through improved mining methods and higher prices for tin, and the town attracted wealthy mine owners. Elegant <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> <a href="/wiki/Townhouses" class="mw-redirect" title="Townhouses">townhouses</a> were built, such as those seen today in Lemon Street, named after the mining magnate and local <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Lemon" title="William Lemon">William Lemon</a>. Truro became the centre for society in the county, even dubbed "the London of Cornwall".<sup id="cite_ref-londonofcornwall_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londonofcornwall-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_Cathedral_in_1905,_before_completion_of_its_spire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Truro_Cathedral_in_1905%2C_before_completion_of_its_spire.jpg/220px-Truro_Cathedral_in_1905%2C_before_completion_of_its_spire.jpg" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/66/Truro_Cathedral_in_1905%2C_before_completion_of_its_spire.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="325" data-file-height="220" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_Cathedral_in_1905,_before_completion_of_its_spire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Cathedral in 1905, before completion of the spires</div></div></div>\n<p>Throughout those prosperous times Truro remained a social centre, and many notable people came from there. Among the noteworthy were <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lemon_Lander" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Lemon Lander">Richard Lander</a>, an explorer who discovered the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Niger" class="mw-redirect" title="River Niger">River Niger</a> in Africa and was awarded the first gold medal of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Geographical_Society" title="Royal Geographical Society">Royal Geographical Society</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Martyn" title="Henry Martyn">Henry Martyn</a>, who read mathematics at Cambridge, was ordained and became a missionary, translating the New Testament into Urdu and Persian. Others include <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a>, educated in Truro and the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Davy_lamp" title="Davy lamp">miner\'s safety lamp</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Foote" title="Samuel Foote">Samuel Foote</a>, an actor and playwright from Boscawen Street.\n</p><p>Truro\'s importance increased later in the 19th century, when it had an iron-smelting works, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">potteries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tanneries</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> arrived in the 1860s, providing a direct link to <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Truro" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric of Truro">Bishopric of Truro</a> Act 1876 gave the town a bishop, and subsequently a cathedral. The next year <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> granted Truro city status. The <a href="/wiki/New_Bridge_Street_drill_hall,_Truro" title="New Bridge Street drill hall, Truro">New Bridge Street drill hall</a> was completed in the late 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The start of the 20th century brought a decline in mining, but the city remained prosperous and continued to develop as the administrative and commercial centre of Cornwall. Today, Truro remains the county retail centre, but like other places, faces concerns over replacement of <a href="/wiki/Specialty_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Specialty store">speciality shops</a> by national <a href="/wiki/Chain_stores" class="mw-redirect" title="Chain stores">chain stores</a>, erosion of identity, and doubts about how to accommodate the growth expected in the 21st century.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_from_the_river.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Truro_from_the_river.jpg/220px-Truro_from_the_river.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Truro_from_the_river.jpg/330px-Truro_from_the_river.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Truro_from_the_river.jpg/440px-Truro_from_the_river.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_from_the_river.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/River_Kenwyn" class="mw-redirect" title="River Kenwyn">River Kenwyn</a> which converges with the Allen to become the <a href="/wiki/River_Truro" title="River Truro">River Truro</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Truro lies in the centre of western Cornwall, about 9 miles (14 kilometres) from the south coast at the confluence of the rivers <a href="/wiki/Kenwyn" title="Kenwyn">Kenwyn</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Allen_(Truro)" title="River Allen (Truro)">Allen</a>, which combine to become the <a href="/wiki/Truro_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Truro River">Truro River</a>, one of a series of creeks, rivers and <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">drowned valleys</a> leading into the <a href="/wiki/River_Fal" title="River Fal">River Fal</a> and then to the large natural harbour of <a href="/wiki/Carrick_Roads" title="Carrick Roads">Carrick Roads</a>. The river valleys form a fairly steep-sided bowl surrounding the city on the north, east and west, open to the Truro River in the south. The bowl shape, along with high precipitation that swells the rivers and a <a href="/wiki/Spring_tide" class="mw-redirect" title="Spring tide">spring tide</a> in the River Fal, were major factors in the 1988 floods that seriously damaged the city centre. Since then, flood defences have been constructed, including an emergency dam at New Mill on the River Kenwyn and a <a href="/wiki/Flood_barrier" title="Flood barrier">tidal barrier</a> on the Truro River.\n</p><p>The city is surrounded by a number of protected natural areas such as the historic parklands at <a href="/wiki/Pencalenick" class="mw-redirect" title="Pencalenick">Pencalenick</a>, and larger areas of ornamental landscape, such as <a href="/wiki/Trelissick_Garden" title="Trelissick Garden">Trelissick Garden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tregothnan" title="Tregothnan">Tregothnan</a> further down the Truro River. An area south-east of the city, around and including Calenick Creek, has been designated an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a>. Other protected areas include an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Great_Landscape_Value" title="Area of Great Landscape Value">Area of Great Landscape Value</a> comprising agricultural land and wooded valleys to the north east, and Daubuz Moors, a <a href="/wiki/Local_Nature_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Nature Reserve">Local Nature Reserve</a> alongside the River Allen close to the city centre.\n</p><p>Truro has mainly grown and developed around the historic city centre in a nuclear fashion along the slopes of the bowl valley, except for fast <a href="/wiki/Linear" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear">linear</a> development along the <a href="/wiki/A390_road" title="A390 road">A390</a> to the west, towards <a href="/wiki/Threemilestone" title="Threemilestone">Threemilestone</a>. As Truro has grown, it has incorporated a number of other settlements as suburbs or unofficial districts, including <a href="/wiki/Kenwyn" title="Kenwyn">Kenwyn</a> and Moresk to the north, Trelander to the east, Newham to the south, and Highertown, Treliske and Gloweth to the west.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography_and_economy">Demography and economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Demography and economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_pydar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Truro_pydar.jpg/220px-Truro_pydar.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Truro_pydar.jpg/330px-Truro_pydar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Truro_pydar.jpg/440px-Truro_pydar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_pydar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sunday morning on Pydar Street</div></div></div>\n<p>Truro urban statistical area, which includes parts of surrounding parishes, had a 2001 census population of 20,920.<sup id="cite_ref-population_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-population-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By 2011 the population including Threemilestone was 23,040. Truro\'s status as the county\'s prime destination for retail and leisure and administrative centre is unusual in that it is only the fourth most populous settlement in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-population_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-population-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, population growth at 10.5 per cent between 1971 and 1998 was slow compared with other Cornish towns and Cornwall as a whole.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">out of date</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Major employers in the city include the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Hospital" title="Royal Cornwall Hospital">Royal Cornwall Hospital</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Truro_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Truro College">Truro College</a>. There are about 22,000 jobs available in Truro, compared to only 9,500 economically active people living in the city. <a href="/wiki/Commuting" title="Commuting">Commutes</a> into Truro are a major factor in its traffic congestion. Average earnings are higher than in the rest of Cornwall.\n</p><p>Housing prices in Truro in the 2000s were 8 or more per cent higher than in the rest of Cornwall. Truro was named in 2006 as the top small city in the United Kingdom for increasing house prices, at 262 per cent since 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-houseprices_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-houseprices-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There is heavy demand for new housing and a call for inner-city properties to be converted into flats or houses to encourage city-centre living and reduce dependence on cars.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TruroCathedralWest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/TruroCathedralWest.jpg/170px-TruroCathedralWest.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/TruroCathedralWest.jpg/255px-TruroCathedralWest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/TruroCathedralWest.jpg/340px-TruroCathedralWest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2136" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TruroCathedralWest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The west front of <a href="/wiki/Truro_Cathedral" title="Truro Cathedral">the Cathedral</a></div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Truro\'s dominent feature is the <a href="/wiki/Gothic-revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic-revival">Gothic-revival</a> <a href="/wiki/Truro_Cathedral" title="Truro Cathedral">cathedral</a>, designed by architect <a href="/wiki/John_Loughborough_Pearson" title="John Loughborough Pearson">John Loughborough Pearson</a>, rising 249&#160;ft (76&#160;m) above the city at its highest spire.<sup id="cite_ref-cathedral_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cathedral-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It took 30 years to build, from 1880 to 1910, on the site of the old St Mary\'s Church, consecrated over 600 years earlier. Enthusiasts of <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian architecture</a> are well catered for in the city, with terraces and townhouses along Walsingham Place and Lemon Street often said to be "the finest examples of Georgian architecture west of the city of <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-westofbath_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-westofbath-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The main attraction for regional residents is the wide variety of shops. Truro has various <a href="/wiki/Chain_stores" class="mw-redirect" title="Chain stores">chain stores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Specialty_shops" class="mw-redirect" title="Specialty shops">speciality shops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Market_(place)" class="mw-redirect" title="Market (place)">markets</a> that reflect its history as a <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a>. The indoor Pannier Market is open year-round with many stalls and small businesses. The city is also popular for catering, including caf\xc3\xa9s and bistros, and for night life, with many bars, clubs and restaurants. Truro houses the <a href="/wiki/Hall_for_Cornwall" title="Hall for Cornwall">Hall for Cornwall</a>, a performing arts and entertainment venue.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Museum" title="Royal Cornwall Museum">Royal Cornwall Museum</a> is the oldest and premier museum in Cornwall for exhibitions detailing <a href="/wiki/History_of_Cornwall" title="History of Cornwall">Cornish history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Cornwall" title="Culture of Cornwall">culture</a>, with a wide range of collections such as <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>, art and geology. Among the exhibits is the so-called <a href="/wiki/Arthur_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur stone">Arthur\'s inscribed stone</a>. Truro is also noted for parks and open spaces, including Victoria Gardens, Boscawen Park and Daubuz Moors.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_piazza.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Truro_piazza.jpg/220px-Truro_piazza.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Truro_piazza.jpg/330px-Truro_piazza.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Truro_piazza.jpg/440px-Truro_piazza.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_piazza.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lemon Quay</div></div></div>\n<p>Lemon Quay is the centre of most festivities in Truro, which attracts visitors year-round with numerous different events.\n</p><p>In April, Truro prepares to partake in the <a href="/wiki/Britain_in_Bloom" title="Britain in Bloom">Britain in Bloom</a> competition, with floral displays and hanging baskets dotted around the city throughout the summer. A "continental market" also comes to Truro in the holiday-making season, featuring food and craft stalls from France, Spain, Italy, Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, Greece, etc.\n</p><p>Cornwall Pride, a <a href="/wiki/Pride_event" class="mw-redirect" title="Pride event">Pride event</a> that celebrates diversity and the <a href="/wiki/LGBT_community" title="LGBT community">LGBT community</a>, takes place on the last Saturday of August. The Truro City Carnival, held every September over a weekend, includes various arts and music performances, children\'s activities, a <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a> display, food and drinks fairs, a <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circus</a>, and a parade. A <a href="/wiki/Half-marathon" class="mw-redirect" title="Half-marathon">half-marathon</a> organised by Truro Running Club also occurs in September, running from the city centre into the country towards Kea, returning to finish at Lemon Quay.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HIGHX1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/HIGHX1.JPG/170px-HIGHX1.JPG" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/HIGHX1.JPG/255px-HIGHX1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/HIGHX1.JPG/340px-HIGHX1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1224" data-file-height="1632" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HIGHX1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Celtic_cross" title="Celtic cross">Celtic cross</a> at High Cross near the cathedral</div></div></div>\n<p>Truro marks Christmas with a Winter Festival that includes a "City of Lights" <a href="/wiki/Paper_lantern" title="Paper lantern">paper lantern</a> parade. Local schools, colleges, and community and youth groups join in.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Students at the local college in Truro have created large lanterns, complementing the work of the core artists team. There are Christmas lights throughout the city centre, with a "switch-on" event, speciality products and craft fairs, late-night shopping evenings, various cathedral events and a firework display on New Year\'s Eve. A <a href="/wiki/Christmas_tree" title="Christmas tree">Christmas tree</a> is put up on the Piazza and another outside the cathedral at <a href="/wiki/High_Cross,_Truro" title="High Cross, Truro">High Cross</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Truro was temporarily the home to the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> club <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Pirates" title="Cornish Pirates">Cornish Pirates</a>, but the team moved back<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2016)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to its original base in <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>. In April 2018, the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Stadium_for_Cornwall" title="Stadium for Cornwall">Stadium for Cornwall</a> was under discussion with <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a>, which had pledged \xc2\xa33 million in funding for the \xc2\xa314.3m project.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>. It is planned for a site in <a href="/wiki/Threemilestone" title="Threemilestone">Threemilestone</a><sup id="cite_ref-threemilestadium_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-threemilestadium-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The town has an amateur rugby union side, <a href="/wiki/Truro_RFC" title="Truro RFC">Truro RFC</a> (founded in 1885), which belongs to <a href="/wiki/Tribute_Western_Counties_West" title="Tribute Western Counties West">Tribute Western Counties West</a> and plays home games at St Clements Hill. It has hosted the <a href="/wiki/CRFU_Cornwall_Cup" title="CRFU Cornwall Cup">CRFU Cornwall Cup</a> several times.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Truro_City_F.C." title="Truro City F.C.">Truro City F.C.</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team in the <a href="/wiki/National_League_South" title="National League South">National League South</a>, is the only Cornish club ever to reach this tier of the <a href="/wiki/English_football_league_system" title="English football league system">football pyramid</a>. The club achieved national recognition by winning the <a href="/wiki/FA_Vase" title="FA Vase">FA Vase</a> in 2007, beating <a href="/wiki/A.F.C._Totton" title="A.F.C. Totton">A.F.C. Totton</a> 3\xe2\x80\x931 in only the second ever final at the new <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium" title="Wembley Stadium">Wembley Stadium</a>, and becoming the first Cornish side ever to win that award. <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_County_Cricket_Club" title="Cornwall County Cricket Club">Cornwall County Cricket Club</a> play some of their home fixtures at <a href="/wiki/Boscawen_Park" title="Boscawen Park">Boscawen Park</a>, which is also the home ground of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Truro Cricket Club (page does not exist)">Truro Cricket Club</a>. Truro Fencing Club is one of Britain\'s flagship clubs, having won numerous national championships, and had three fencers selected for Team GB at the London 2012 Olympics. Other sports amenities include a <a href="/wiki/Leisure_centre" title="Leisure centre">leisure centre</a>, golf course and tennis courts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Truro is the centre of Cornwall\'s local media. The countywide weeklies, the <i><a href="/wiki/Cornish_Guardian" title="Cornish Guardian">Cornish Guardian</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_West_Briton" title="The West Briton">The West Briton</a></i>, are based in the city, the latter serving the Truro area with its Truro and Mid-Cornwall edition. The city also houses the broadcasting studios of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Cornwall" title="BBC Radio Cornwall">BBC Radio Cornwall</a>, and those of the West district of <a href="/wiki/Westcountry_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Westcountry Television">ITV Westcountry</a>, whose main studio is now in <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> after a merger with ITV West. This closed the studio in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Westcountry_Live" title="Westcountry Live">Westcountry Live</a> programme was replaced by <a href="/wiki/The_West_Country_Tonight" class="mw-redirect" title="The West Country Tonight">The West Country Tonight</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Customs">Customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Mummers_play" title="Mummers play">mummers play</a> text attributed until recently to <a href="/wiki/Mylor,_Cornwall" title="Mylor, Cornwall">Mylor, Cornwall</a> (much quoted in early studies of folk plays, such as <i>The Mummers Play</i> by R. J. E. Tiddy \xe2\x80\x93 published posthumously in 1923 \xe2\x80\x93 and <i>The English Folk-Play</i> (1933) by E. K. Chambers), has now been shown, by genealogical and other research, to have originated in Truro around 1780.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The widespread tradition of <a href="/wiki/Nine_Lessons_and_Carols" title="Nine Lessons and Carols">Nine Lessons and Carols</a> originated in Truro, when its bishop at the time, Edward White Benson, formalised the evening singing of carols before Christmas Day<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Truro area has an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> similar to the rest of Cornwall\'s. This means even fewer extremes in temperature than in the remainder of England, marked by high rainfall, cool summers and mild winters with infrequent frosts.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable The city is divided into four <a href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (politics)">wards</a>: Boscawen, Moresk, Tregolls and Trehaverne, with 24 councillors elected for four-year terms.<sup id="cite_ref-councillors_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-councillors-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n<a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a> (a unitary authority) has its base at Lys Kernow ("Cornwall Court", formerly County Hall) west of the city centre. It administers planning, infrastructure, development and environmental issues.\n</p><p>The City Council is an elected public body forming the lowest level of government in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It is part of the parish council level, which comprises 213 parish bodies in the county of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. The layer of government directly above is <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a>, the unitary authority for Cornwall, which is directly below central id="Roads_and_bus_services">Roads and bus services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Roads and bus services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Truro is 6 miles (9.7&#160;km) from the <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30</a> <a href="/wiki/Trunk_road" title="Trunk road">trunk road</a>, to which it is connected by the A39 leading from <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penryn,_Cornwall" title="Penryn, Cornwall">Penryn</a>. Also passing through the city is the A390, from <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">Redruth</a> in the west to <a href="/wiki/Liskeard" title="Liskeard">Liskeard</a> in the east where it connects to the <a href="/wiki/A38_road" title="A38 road">A38</a>, which then goes on to <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> and further to <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5 motorway</a>. Truro is the most southerly city in the United Kingdom, just under 232 miles (373&#160;km) west south west of <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross" title="Charing Cross">Charing Cross</a>, London.\n</p><p>The city and surrounding area are served by extensive bus services, mostly operated by <a href="/wiki/First_Kernow" title="First Kernow">First Kernow</a> with routes across the city, and in and out of the city in all directions, mostly starting and terminating at Truro bus station near Lemon Quay.A permanent <a href="/wiki/Park_and_Ride" class="mw-redirect" title="Park and Ride">Park and Ride</a> scheme, known as Park for Truro, began operation in August 2008. Based at Langarth Park in <a href="/wiki/Threemilestone" title="Threemilestone">Threemilestone</a>, buses carry commuters into the city centre via <a href="/wiki/Truro_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Truro College">Truro College</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Hospital" title="Royal Cornwall Hospital">Royal Cornwall Hospital</a> Treliske, County Hall, <a href="/wiki/Truro_railway_station" title="Truro railway station">Truro railway station</a>, the Royal Cornwall Museum and Victoria Square, and now through to a second car park on the east side of Truro. Longer-distance <a href="/wiki/Coach_(vehicle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Coach (vehicle)">coaches</a> run by <a href="/wiki/National_Express_Coaches" title="National Express Coaches">National Express</a> also operate from Truro.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg/220px-Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg/330px-Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg/440px-Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1075" data-file-height="684" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carvedras_Viaduct.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway_viaducts#Carvedras" title="Cornwall Railway viaducts">Carvedras Viaduct</a>, built in 1859 by <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a>. It was replaced by a stone viaduct in 1904.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Truro_railway_station" title="Truro railway station">Truro railway station</a> is about 1&#160;km (0.6&#160;mi) from the city centre and is on the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Main_Line" title="Cornish Main Line">Cornish Main Line</a>, giving the city a direct connection to <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a>, as well as to the Midlands, North and Scotland. North-east of the station is a 28-metre-high (92-foot) stone <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaduct</a> with expansive views over the city, cathedral, and <a href="/wiki/River_Truro" title="River Truro">Truro River</a> in the distance. The viaduct\xe2\x80\x94the longest on the line\xe2\x80\x94replaced <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a>\'s wooden <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway_viaducts#Carvedras" title="Cornwall Railway viaducts">Carvedras Viaduct</a> in 1904. Connecting to the main line at Truro station is the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Line" title="Maritime Line">Maritime Line</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Branch_line" title="Branch line">branch line</a> to Falmouth in the south.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg/220px-3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg/330px-3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg/440px-3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="816" data-file-height="512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:3440_City_of_Truro_nameplate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The nameplate of preserved <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> locomotive <i><a href="/wiki/GWR_3700_Class_3440_City_of_Truro" title="GWR 3700 Class 3440 City of Truro">City of Truro</a></i>, built in 1903</div></div></div>\n<p>Truro\'s first railway station was at Highertown, which was opened in 1852 by the <a href="/wiki/West_Cornwall_Railway" title="West Cornwall Railway">West Cornwall Railway</a> and from where trains ran to <a href="/wiki/Redruth_railway_station" title="Redruth railway station">Redruth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penzance_railway_station" title="Penzance railway station">Penzance</a>. It was known as Truro Road Station; it was just west of Highertown Tunnel on the up side. The line was extended to the Truro River at Newham in 1855 Then Truro Road Station closed and Newham served as the terminus. When the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Railway" title="Cornwall Railway">Cornwall Railway</a> connected the line to Plymouth, their trains ran to the present station above the city centre. The West Cornwall Railway (WCR) then diverted most of its passenger trains to the new station, leaving Newham mainly as a goods station until it closed in 1971. The WCR became part of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a>. The route from Highertown to Newham is now a <a href="/wiki/Cycle_path" class="mw-redirect" title="Cycle path">cycle path</a>, which takes a leisurely loop through the countryside on the south side of the city. The steam locomotive, the <i><a href="/wiki/GWR_3440_City_of_Truro" class="mw-redirect" title="GWR 3440 City of Truro">City of Truro</a></i>, was built in 1903 and still runs on UK mainline and preserved railways.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_riverandboat.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Truro_riverandboat.jpg/220px-Truro_riverandboat.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Truro_riverandboat.jpg/330px-Truro_riverandboat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Truro_riverandboat.jpg/440px-Truro_riverandboat.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="532" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Truro_riverandboat.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Truro River and a ferry transporting passengers to Falmouth</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_and_river_transport">Air and river transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Air and river transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Newquay_Airport" title="Newquay Airport">Newquay Airport</a>, Cornwall\'s main airport, and is 12&#160;mi (19&#160;km) north of Truro. Truro was St Mary\'s, incorporated into the cathedral in the later 19th century. Parts of the town were in the parishes of <a href="/wiki/Kenwyn" title="Kenwyn">Kenwyn</a> and St Clement (<a href="/wiki/St_Clement,_Cornwall" title="St Clement, Cornwall">Moresk</a>) until the mid 19th century when other parishes were created. <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Church,_Truro" class="mw-redirect" title="St George&#39;s Church, Truro">St George\'s church in Truro</a>, designed by the Reverend William Haslam, vicar of <a href="/wiki/Baldhu" title="Baldhu">Baldhu</a>, was built of Cornish granite in 1855; it is lofty and imposing. The parish of St George\'s Truro was formed from part of Kenwyn in 1846. In 1865 two more parishes were created: St John\'s from part of Kenwyn and St Paul\'s from part of St Clement.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> St George\'s contains a large wall painting behind the high altar which was the work of Stephany Cooper in the 1920s. Her father Canon Cooper had been a missionary in <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">Zanzibar</a> and elsewhere. The theme of the mural painting is "Three Heavens": the first heaven has views of Zanzibar and its cathedral (a happy period in the life of the artist); the second heaven has views of the city of Truro including the cathedral, the railway viaduct and St George\'s church (another happy period in the life of the artist); the third heaven is above the others which are separated from it by the River of Life (Christ is represented bridging the river and 17 saints including St <a href="/wiki/Piran" title="Piran">Piran</a> and St Kenwyn are depicted in this part).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Hempel" title="Charles William Hempel">Charles William Hempel</a> was organist of St. Mary\'s Church for forty years from 1804, supplementing his income by teaching music. In 1805 he composed and printed <i>Psalms from the New Version for the use of the Congregation of St. Mary\'s</i>, and in 1812 <i>Sacred Melodies</i> for the same congregation. These melodies became very popular.\n</p><p>The oldest church in Truro is at <a href="/wiki/Kenwyn" title="Kenwyn">Kenwyn</a>, on the northern side of the city. It is from the 14th/15th centuries.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/St_John_the_Evangelist%27s_Church,_Truro" title="St John the Evangelist&#39;s Church, Truro">St John\'s Church</a> (dedicated to St John the Evangelist) was built in 1828 (architect P. Sambell) in the Classical style on a rectangular plan and with a gallery. Considerable alterations were carried out in the 1890s.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Church,_Truro" title="St Paul&#39;s Church, Truro">St Paul\'s Church</a> was built in 1848. The chancel was replaced in 1882\xe2\x80\x9384, the new chancel being the work of <a href="/wiki/J._D._Sedding" title="J. D. Sedding">J. D. Sedding</a>. The tower is "broad and strong" (Pevsner) and the exterior of the aisles are ornamented in Sedding\'s version of the Perpendicular style.<sup id="cite_ref-Pevsner,_N._1970_pp._234\xe2\x80\x93235_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pevsner,_N._1970_pp._234\xe2\x80\x93235-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In the parish of St Paul is the former <a href="/wiki/Convent_of_the_Epiphany" title="Convent of the Epiphany">Convent of the Epiphany</a> (Anglican) at Alverton House, Tregolls Road, an early 19th-century house. The house was extended for the convent of the Community of the Epiphany and the chapel was built in 1910 by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Harold_Sedding" title="Edmund Harold Sedding">Edmund H. Sedding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pevsner,_N._1970_pp._234\xe2\x80\x93235_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pevsner,_N._1970_pp._234\xe2\x80\x93235-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The sisterhood was founded by the Bishop of Truro, <a href="/wiki/George_Howard_Wilkinson" class="mw-redirect" title="George Howard Wilkinson">George Howard Wilkinson</a>, in 1883 and closed in 2001 when the two surviving nuns moved into care homes. The sisters were involved in pastoral and educational work and the care of the cathedral and St Paul\'s Church.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> St Paul\'s Church, built with a tower on a river bed with poor foundations, has fallen into disrepair, and is no longer in use. Services are now held at the churches of St Clement, St George, and St John. St Paul and St Clement is now a united benefice, as is St George and St John.\n</p><p>Only one of Methodist chapel remains in use, in Union Place (Truro Methodist Church), which has a broad granite front (1830, but since enlarged). There is a Quaker Meeting House built in granite (c. 1830) and numerous other churches, some meeting in their own modern buildings, e. g. St Piran\'s Roman Catholic church and All Saints, Highertown, and some in schools or halls. St Piran\'s, dedicated to Our Lady of the Portal and St Piran, was built on the site of a medieval chapel by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Steuart_Pollard" title="Margaret Steuart Pollard">Margaret Steuart Pollard</a> in 1973, for which she received the <a href="/wiki/Benemerenti_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Benemerenti Medal">Benemerenti Medal</a> from the Pope.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The Baptist church building occupies the site of the former Lake\'s pottery, one of the oldest in Cornwall.\n</p>\n<h2><span
