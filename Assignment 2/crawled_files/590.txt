The 10 IFCA Districts cover English coastal waters out to 6 nautical miles from Territorial Baselines.\nAlthough autonomous the 10 IFCAs have a shared \'vision\' to \xe2\x80\x9clead, champion and manage a sustainable marine environment and inshore fisheries, by successfully securing the right balance between social, environmental and economic benefits to ensure healthy seas, sustainable fisheries and a viable industry\xe2\x80\x9d. The IFCAs have shared powers and duties which are found in the Marine and Coastal Access Act, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under s.153 of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_and_Coastal_Access_Act_2009" title="Marine and Coastal Access Act 2009">Marine and Coastal Access Act 2009</a> (1) The authority for an IFC district must manage the exploitation of sea fisheries resources in that district. (2) In performing its duty under subsection (1), the authority for an IFC district must -\n(a) seek to ensure that the exploitation of sea fisheries resources is carried out in a sustainable way,\n(b) seek to balance the social and economic benefits of exploiting the sea fisheries resources of the district with the need to protect the marine environment from, or to promote it recovery from, the effects of such exploitation,\n(c) take any other steps which in the authority\'s opinion are necessary or expedient for the purpose of making a contribution to the achievement of sustainable development, and\n(d) seek to balance the different needs of persons engaged in the exploitation of sea fisheries resources in the district\n</p><p>IFCAs are funded by Local Authorities and this funding is, in part, underpinned by <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" title="Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">DEFRA</a> via an Area Based Grant of c.\xc2\xa35million annually.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\nSince the IFCAs were created in 2011 they have been responsible for transforming the way fishing activities are undertaken in Marine Protected Areas and have introduced 17 byelaws to protect the most vulnerable features within European Marine Sites. Southern IFCA has introduced measures which restrict Bottom Towed Fishing Gear from 25% of the entire IFCA District.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> IFCAs are also progressing measures to balance the needs of the commercial and recreational fisheries and examples of emerging plans are seen in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and Severn IFCA.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inshore_Fisheries_and_Conservation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2009/23/part/6">"Marine and Coastal Access Act 2009"</a>.
