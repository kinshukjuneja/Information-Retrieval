For a guide to IPA symbols, see <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">Help:IPA</a>.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Auregnais</b>, <b>Aoeur\'gnaeux</b> or <b>Aurignais</b> was the <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman</a> dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Aurigny</i>, Auregnais: <i>Aoeur\'gny</i> or <i>Auregny</i>). It was closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Guern%C3%A9siais" title="Guern\xc3\xa9siais">Guern\xc3\xa9siais</a> (<a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>), <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais" title="J\xc3\xa8rriais">J\xc3\xa8rriais</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sercquiais" title="Sercquiais">Sercquiais</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>) dialects of the neighbouring islands, as well as <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Continental Norman</a> on the European mainland.\n</p><p>The dialect became extinct in the 20th century. Only a few examples of Auregnais survive, mostly in place names in Alderney, and one audio recording is known to exist. Linguists have called the disappearance of Auregnais "the worst documented case of recent <a href="/wiki/Language_death" title="Language death">language extinction</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auregnais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The last known native speaker of Auregnais died around 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Linguist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Le_Maistre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Frank Le Maistre (page does not exist)">Frank Le Maistre</a>, author of the <i>Dictionnaire Jersiais-Fran\xc3\xa7ais</i>, recorded the only known audio samples of the language, which he published in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One reason for the extinction of the language was movement of the population. In particular, the influx of labourers from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> employed by the British government in the construction of the abortive harbour project and other fortifications (during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>), as well as the stationing of a sizable British garrison among the small population, served to relegate Auregnais to a lesser status for communication. It is thought that the evacuation of nearly all indigenous Auregnais to the British mainland during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> (the island was occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>) was a major factor in the final loss of the spoken language. The language has suffered further losses in recent years due to an influx of people from <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> who have moved to the <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick_of_Guernsey" title="Bailiwick of Guernsey">Bailiwick</a> both for tax purposes and to fill the local skills gap.\n</p><p>Another reason for the language\'s demise was official neglect, especially in the education sector, where it was not taught at all. This led to a situation in which, as was noted by the Guernsey newspaper <i>Le Bailliage</i> in 1880, children had ceased to speak the language among themselves \xe2\x80\x93 partly due to teachers discouraging its use in favour of standard <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>. However, along with the decline in Auregnais went the decline in the use of French. French ceased to be an <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a> in the island in 1966. The official French used in the Channel Islands (see <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Legal_French" title="Jersey Legal French">Jersey Legal French</a>) differs slightly from <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_French" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan French">Metropolitan French</a> and greatly from the vernacular Norman.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames_and_place_names">Surnames and place names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auregnais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames and place names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Traces of the language still exist in many, if not most, local <a href="/wiki/Placename" class="mw-redirect" title="Placename">placenames</a>. Many of these have been gallicised, but some notable examples include <a href="/wiki/Ortac" title="Ortac">Ortac</a> (Or\'tac), <a href="/wiki/Burhou" title="Burhou">Burhou</a> (with the <i><a href="/wiki/-hou" title="-hou">-hou</a></i> suffix) and the first element of the name "<a href="/wiki/Braye_Harbour" title="Braye Harbour"><i>Braye</i> Harbour</a>".\n</p><p>One or two words linger on in the local English, e.g. <i>vraic</i> (<a href="/wiki/Seaweed_fertiliser" title="Seaweed fertiliser">seaweed fertiliser</a> \xe2\x80\x93 a word common throughout the Channel Islands), and the pronunciation of certain local surnames, e.g. Dupont and Simon as <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[dip\xc3\xb5]</a></span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sym\xc3\xb5]</span> rather than the standard Parisian pronunciation. A few older people can still remember it being spoken, and know a word or two.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Les_Casquets">Les Casquets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auregnais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Les Casquets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Unusually, for such a small dialect, Auregnais used to have an exclave or "colony" of speakers on <a href="/wiki/Les_Casquets" title="Les Casquets">Les Casquets</a> for a number of years. <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a> based his poem <i>Les Casquets</i> on the Houguez family who actually lived on the islands for 18 years. The Houguez family came from Alderney, and the evidence points to its members being Auregnais speakers \xe2\x80\x93 in fact the daughter married a man from Alderney. During this time, they were isolated and would have had few visitors, but would have spoken Auregnais most of the time.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auregnais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Gunther, Wilf (1990).
