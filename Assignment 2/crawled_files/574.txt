Along with the <a href="/wiki/International_League" title="International League">International League</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_League_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican League (baseball)">Mexican League</a>, it is one of three leagues playing at the <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_(baseball)" title="Triple-A (baseball)">Triple-A</a> level, which is one grade below <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>. It is officially named the Pacific Coast League of Professional Baseball Clubs, Inc. Its headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/Round_Rock,_Texas" title="Round Rock, Texas">Round Rock, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Upon its founding in 1903, the Pacific Coast League fielded six teams from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific States">Pacific States</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. Today, the league is composed of 16 teams across 12 states stretching from <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Tacoma,_Washington" title="Tacoma, Washington">Tacoma, Washington</a>, to <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" class="mw-redirect" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans, Louisiana</a>. \n</p><p>The PCL was one of the premier regional baseball leagues in the first half of the 20th century. Although it was never recognized as a true major league, to which it aspired, its quality of play was considered very high. A number of top stars of the era, including <a href="/wiki/Joe_DiMaggio" title="Joe DiMaggio">Joe DiMaggio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a>, were products of the league. In 1958, with the arrival of major league teams on the west coast and the availability of televised major league games, the PCL\'s modern era began with each team signing Player Development Contracts to become <a href="/wiki/Farm_team" title="Farm team">farm teams</a> of major league clubs.\n</p><p>A league champion is determined at the end of every season. The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Seals_(baseball)" title="San Francisco Seals (baseball)">San Francisco Seals</a> won 14 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_Coast_League_champions" title="List of Pacific Coast League champions">Pacific Coast League titles</a>, the most in the league\'s history, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_(PCL)" title="Los Angeles Angels (PCL)">Los Angeles Angels</a> (12) and the <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque_Dukes" title="Albuquerque Dukes">Albuquerque Dukes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portland_Beavers" title="Portland Beavers">Portland Beavers</a> (8). After the season, the PCL champion plays in the <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_National_Championship_Game" title="Triple-A National Championship Game">Triple-A National Championship Game</a> against the International League champion to determine an overall champion of Triple-A baseball. The <a href="/wiki/Omaha_Storm_Chasers" title="Omaha Storm Chasers">Omaha Storm Chasers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River_Cats" title="Sacramento River Cats">Sacramento River Cats</a> have each won two national championships, more than any other PCL teams.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_early_history">Formation and early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Pacific Coast League was formed on December 29, 1902, when officials from the California State League (1899\xe2\x80\x931902) met in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> for the purpose of expanding the league beyond <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. Six franchises were granted. These were the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_(PCL)" title="Los Angeles Angels (PCL)">Los Angeles Angels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Oaks_(PCL)" title="Oakland Oaks (PCL)">Oakland Oaks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portland_Beavers" title="Portland Beavers">Portland Beavers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Senators_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacramento Senators (baseball)">Sacramento Senators</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Seals_(baseball)" title="San Francisco Seals (baseball)">San Francisco Seals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Indians" class="mw-redirect" title="Seattle Indians">Seattle Indians</a>. A dispute over territories owned by the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest_League" title="Pacific Northwest League">Pacific Northwest League</a>, in which the PCL had placed franchises, and the PCL\'s allowing blacklisted players to compete led to the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_(minor_league_baseball)" title="National Association (minor league baseball)">National Association</a> (<a href="/wiki/Minor_League_Baseball" title="Minor League Baseball">Minor League Baseball</a>) labeling the PCL as an outlaw league.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The mild climate of the West Coast, especially California, allowed the league to play longer seasons, sometimes starting in late February and ending as late as the beginning of December. During the 1905 season the San Francisco Seals set the all-time PCL record by playing 230 games.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Teams regularly played between 170 and 200 games in a season until the late 1950s. This allowed players, who were often career minor leaguers, to hone their skills, earn an extra month or two of pay, and reduce the need to find off-season work. These longer seasons gave owners the opportunity to generate more revenue. Another outcome was that a number of the all-time minor league records for season statistical totals are held by players from the PCL.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" March 26.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Los Angeles Angels finished the season in first place with a 133\xe2\x80\x9378 (.630) record, making them the first <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_Coast_League_champions" title="List of Pacific Coast League champions">league champions</a>.\n</p><p>In 1904, National Association President <a href="/wiki/Patrick_T._Powers" title="Patrick T. Powers">Patrick T. Powers</a> brokered terms with the PCL, clearing it of its outlaw status and designating it as a Class A league. In 1909, the league classification was raised to Double-A. In 1919, with the earlier addition of the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Bees" title="Salt Lake Bees">Salt Lake Bees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Tigers" title="Vernon Tigers">Vernon Tigers</a>, league membership reached eight teams for the first time. While the league had experienced little commercial success up to this point, the 1920s were a turning point which saw increased attendance and teams fielding star players.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> of the 1930s resulted in a lower quality of play due to the league\'s salary reduction. Still, a number of top stars, including <a href="/wiki/Joe_DiMaggio" title="Joe DiMaggio">Joe DiMaggio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Doerr" title="Bobby Doerr">Bobby Doerr</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ox_Eckhardt" title="Ox Eckhardt">Ox Eckhardt</a>, competed on PCL teams that decade. Also helping attendance was the introduction of night games. At Sacramento\'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moreing_Field&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Moreing Field (page does not exist)">Moreing Field</a>, the Sacramento Solons and the Oakland Oaks played the first night baseball game, five years before any major league night game, on June 10, 1930. The <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Stars" title="Hollywood Stars">Hollywood Stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres_(PCL)" title="San Diego Padres (PCL)">San Diego Padres</a> were added to the league in the 1930s as well.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_near-major_league">A near-major league</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: A near-major league">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the first half of the 20th century, the Pacific Coast League developed into one of the premier regional baseball leagues. The cities enfranchised by the other two high-minor leagues, the <a href="/wiki/International_League" title="International League">International League</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(20th_century)" title="American Association (20th century)">American Association</a>, were generally coordinated geographically with the major leagues, but such was not the case with the PCL. With no major league baseball team existing west of <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>, the PCL was unrivaled for American west coast baseball. Although it was never recognized as a true major league, its quality of play was considered very high. Drawing from a strong pool of talent in the area, the PCL produced many outstanding players, including such future major-league <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Hall of Famers</a> as <a href="/wiki/Joe_DiMaggio" title="Joe DiMaggio">Joe DiMaggio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Lazzeri" title="Tony Lazzeri">Tony Lazzeri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Waner" title="Paul Waner">Paul Waner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earl_Averill" title="Earl Averill">Earl Averill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Doerr" title="Bobby Doerr">Bobby Doerr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Gordon" title="Joe Gordon">Joe Gordon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Lombardi" title="Ernie Lombardi">Ernie Lombardi</a>. Amid success experienced after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, league President <a href="/wiki/Pants_Rowland" title="Pants Rowland">Pants Rowland</a> began to envision the PCL as a third major league. During 1945 the league voted to become a major league.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> were uninterested in allowing it to join their ranks.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While many PCL players went on to play in the major leagues, teams in the league were often successful enough that they could offer competitive salaries to avoid being outbid for their players\' services. Some players made a career out of the minor leagues. One of the better known was <a href="/wiki/Frank_Shellenback" title="Frank Shellenback">Frank Shellenback</a>, whose major league pitching career was brief,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but who compiled a record PCL total of 295 wins against 178 losses.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Many former major league players came to the PCL to finish their careers after their time in the majors had ended.\n</p><p>In 1952, the PCL became the only minor league in history to be given the "Open" classification, a grade above the Triple-A level. This limited the rights of major league clubs to draft players from the PCL, and was considered an act toward the circuit becoming a third major league.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sudden_decline">Sudden decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sudden decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The shift to the Open classification came just as minor league teams from coast to coast suffered a sharp drop in attendance, primarily due to the availability of major league games on television. The hammer blow to the PCL\'s major league dreams came in 1958 with the arrival of the first MLB teams on the west coast (the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a>). As a result, three of the PCL\'s flagship teams (the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_(PCL)" title="Los Angeles Angels (PCL)">Los Angeles Angels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Stars" title="Hollywood Stars">Hollywood Stars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Seals_(baseball)" title="San Francisco Seals (baseball)">San Francisco Seals</a>) were immediately forced to relocate to smaller markets. The <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Oaks_(PCL)" title="Oakland Oaks (PCL)">Oakland Oaks</a> had moved to Canada two years before the Giants arrived. The <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres_(PCL)" title="San Diego Padres (PCL)">San Diego Padres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Rainiers" title="Seattle Rainiers">Seattle Rainiers</a> suffered the same fate when they were displaced by major league teams in 1969. Additionally, the PCL lost customers to the major league teams which then occupied the same territory. The league never recovered from these blows. The Pacific Coast League reverted to Triple-A classification in 1958, where it remains to this day, and soon diminished in the public eye to nothing more than another minor league.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moving_beyond_the_coast">Moving beyond the coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Moving beyond the coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The PCL began to spread out across the nation, and internationally, in the 1950s. Previously, <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> has been the eastern-most city in the league. In 1956, the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Oaks_(PCL)" title="Oakland Oaks (PCL)">Oakland Oaks</a> relocated to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> where they became the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Mounties" title="Vancouver Mounties">Vancouver Mounties</a>, the circuit\'s first international team. Two years later, the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_(PCL)" title="Los Angeles Angels (PCL)">Los Angeles Angels</a> moved to become the <a href="/wiki/Spokane_Indians" title="Spokane Indians">Spokane Indians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Seals_(baseball)" title="San Francisco Seals (baseball)">San Francisco Seals</a> became the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Giants" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenix Giants">Phoenix Giants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The league continued to expand throughout the country in the 1960s. Clubs representing new cities during the decade included the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Rangers" title="Dallas Rangers">Dallas Rangers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denver_Bears" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver Bears">Denver Bears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_Islanders" title="Hawaii Islanders">Hawaii Islanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Indians" title="Indianapolis Indians">Indianapolis Indians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_89ers" class="mw-redirect" title="Oklahoma City 89ers">Oklahoma City 89ers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tacoma_Giants" class="mw-redirect" title="Tacoma Giants">Tacoma</a>/<a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Giants" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenix Giants">Phoenix Giants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tucson_Toros" title="Tucson Toros">Tucson Toros</a>. From 1964 to 1968 the PCL swelled to twelve teams. The <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque_Dukes" title="Albuquerque Dukes">Albuquerque Dukes</a> were one of several teams to begin play in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Several new teams arrived in the 1980s, such as the <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Cannons" title="Calgary Cannons">Calgary Cannons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs_Sky_Sox" title="Colorado Springs Sky Sox">Colorado Springs Sky Sox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Trappers" title="Edmonton Trappers">Edmonton Trappers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Stars_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Las Vegas Stars (baseball)">Las Vegas Stars</a>, but the league began to stabilize as franchise relocations became less frequent.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_expansion">Recent expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recent expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1998, the Pacific Coast League took on five teams from the disbanding <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(20th_century)" title="American Association (20th century)">American Association</a>, which had operated in the Midwest, and a sixth franchise was added to the league as an expansion team, thus providing the scheduling convenience of an even number of teams. The addition of the <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Cubs" title="Iowa Cubs">Iowa Cubs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nashville_Sounds" title="Nashville Sounds">Nashville Sounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_RedHawks" class="mw-redirect" title="Oklahoma RedHawks">Oklahoma RedHawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omaha_Royals" class="mw-redirect" title="Omaha Royals">Omaha Royals</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Zephyrs" class="mw-redirect" title="New Orleans Zephyrs">New Orleans Zephyrs</a>, and the expansion <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Redbirds" title="Memphis Redbirds">Memphis Redbirds</a> grew the league to an all-time-high 16 clubs.<sup id="cite_ref-2017RB_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017RB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The league presently stretches from <a href="/wiki/Western_Washington" title="Western Washington">Western Washington</a> to <a href="/wiki/Middle_Tennessee" title="Middle Tennessee">Middle Tennessee</a>. Despite its name, the league now has as many teams east of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a> (Memphis, Nashville, and New Orleans) as it does near the Pacific coast (Fresno, Sacramento, and Tacoma).\n</p><p>The league\'s presence in Canada diminished and ended in the early 2000s, as the Calgary Cannons moved to <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albuquerque, New Mexico</a>, to become the <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque_Isotopes" title="Albuquerque Isotopes">Albuquerque Isotopes</a> in 2003, and the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Trappers" title="Edmonton Trappers">Edmonton Trappers</a>, the circuit\'s final Canadian team moved to <a href="/wiki/Round_Rock_Express" title="Round Rock Express">Round Rock</a> in 2005. Of the cities represented in the PCL in its heyday, only <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a> remain, and even these are represented by franchises different from those that originally called these cities home. In 2005, the Pacific Coast League became the first minor league ever to achieve a season attendance of over 7 million. In 2007, league attendance reached an all-time high of 7,420,095.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2019, the team previously known as the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs_Sky_Sox" title="Colorado Springs Sky Sox">Colorado Springs Sky Sox</a> will relocate to <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio, Texas</a>, where they will continue play in the PCL as the <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Missions" title="San Antonio Missions">San Antonio Missions</a>, which had competed in the Double-A <a href="/wiki/Texas_League" title="Texas League">Texas League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This move will be accompanied by realignment in the American Conference. Nashville and Memphis will move to the Northern Division, and Oklahoma City and San Antonio will compete in the Southern Division.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In a further move, the <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Baby_Cakes" title="New Orleans Baby Cakes">New Orleans Baby Cakes</a> have requested permission to relocate to <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita, Kansas</a> as early as 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_season">Structure and season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structure and season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The league is divided into two conferences, the <b>American Conference</b> and the <b>Pacific Conference</b>. Each conference is divided into a <b>Northern Division</b> and a <b>Southern Division</b>. Each conference consists of eight teams evenly divided into four-team divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-2017season_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017season-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Each club has 140 games scheduled per season. Of these, 112 games are played against conference opponents, with 48 against teams in their division and 64 against teams in the other division. The other 28 games are played against teams in the opposing conference. These interconference games are organized as eight, three-or-four-game series. A team will play one opposite conference\'s division\'s teams at home and the other\'s on the road. Play alternates each year so that interconference opponents will have played each other at home and on the road over the span of two seasons. The season typically begins during the first week of April and concludes in the first week of September on <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">Labor Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Championship_and_interleague_play">Championship and interleague play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Championship and interleague play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg/220px-2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg/330px-2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg/440px-2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2015_AAA_All-Star_Game_dugout.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>PCL All-Stars at the 2015 <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_All-Star_Game" title="Triple-A All-Star Game">Triple-A All-Star Game</a></div></div></div>\n<p>At the end of the season, the Northern and Southern Division winners within each conference meet in a best-of-five series to determine conference champions. Then, the American and Pacific Conference winners play a best-of-five series to determine a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_Coast_League_champions" title="List of Pacific Coast League champions">league champion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2006, the PCL champion has played against the <a href="/wiki/International_League" title="International League">International League</a>\'s champion in the <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_National_Championship_Game" title="Triple-A National Championship Game">Triple-A National Championship Game</a>, a single game to determine an overall champion of Triple-A baseball. Previously, the PCL champion also competed in the <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_World_Series" title="Triple-A World Series">Triple-A World Series</a> (1983, 1998\xe2\x80\x932000), <a href="/wiki/Junior_World_Series" title="Junior World Series">Junior World Series</a> (1919), and other sporadic postseason competitions throughout the league\'s history.\n</p><p>Other interleague play occurs during the <a href="/wiki/Triple-A_All-Star_Game" title="Triple-A All-Star Game">Triple-A All-Star Game</a>. Traditionally, the game has taken place on the day after the mid-summer <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">Major League Baseball All-Star Game</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Omaha2015_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Omaha2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The game is meant to mark a symbolic halfway-point in the season (though not the mathematical halfway-point which, for most seasons, is usually one month prior). During the All-Star break, no regular-season games are scheduled for two days before the All-Star Game itself.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules">Rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Pacific Coast League plays by the same rules listed in the <i>Official Baseball Rules</i> published by Major League Baseball. One exception is the use of <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">Designated hitter</a> (DH). Whereas the application of the DH rule in Major League Baseball is determined by the identity of the home team, with the rules of the home team\'s league applying to both teams, PCL pitchers hit when both clubs are National League affiliates and they have agreed to have their pitchers hit. Two National League affiliated clubs may agree to use the DH instead. The reason for this is that as players move up and get closer to reaching the majors, teams prefer to have the rules follow (as closely as possible) those of the major leagues. The DH is always used when one or both teams are American League affiliates.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other differences lie in the use of professional baseball\'s <a href="/wiki/Pace-of-play" class="mw-redirect" title="Pace-of-play">pace-of-play</a> initiatives first implemented in 2015. A 15-second <a href="/wiki/Pitch_clock" title="Pitch clock">pitch clock</a> is used when no runners are on base; 20 seconds are allowed with runners present. Additionally, each half extra inning begins with a runner on second base, and teams are limited to six mound visits during a nine-inning game.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_teams">Current teams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a With few exceptions, most PCL champions through 1927 were simply the regular season pennant winners.<sup id="cite_ref-PCLlist_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PCLlist-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> However, a few seasons during this time did feature a postseason championship series to crown a champion. It wasn\'t until the mid-1930s that the league instituted regular postseason play that was only sporadically cancelled due to financial problems or other factors.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAlist_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAlist-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Seals_(baseball)" title="San Francisco Seals (baseball)">San Francisco Seals</a> have won 14 PCL championships, the most among all teams in the league, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_(PCL)" title="Los Angeles Angels (PCL)">Los Angeles Angels</a> (12) and the <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque_Dukes" title="Albuquerque Dukes">Albuquerque Dukes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portland_Beavers" title="Portland Beavers">Portland Beavers</a> (8). Among active PCL franchises, the <a href="/wiki/Tacoma_Rainiers" title="Tacoma Rainiers">Tacoma Rainiers</a> have won 7 championships, the most in the league, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River_Cats" title="Sacramento River Cats">Sacramento River Cats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Redbirds" title="Memphis Redbirds">Memphis Redbirds</a> (4).<sup id="cite_ref-AAAlist_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAlist-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lefty-odoul.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Frank &quot;Lefty&quot; O&#39;Doul in a New York Yankees uniform." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Lefty-odoul.jpg/220px-Lefty-odoul.jpg" width="220" height="323" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Lefty-odoul.jpg/330px-Lefty-odoul.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0b/Lefty-odoul.jpg 2x" data-file-width="381" data-file-height="559" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lefty-odoul.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lefty_O%27Doul" title="Lefty O&#39;Doul">Lefty O\'Doul</a> won the first <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Most Valuable Player Award">Pacific Coast League MVP Award</a> in 1927.<sup id="cite_ref-AwardWinners_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AwardWinners-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The PCL recognizes outstanding players and team personnel annually near the end of each season.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MVP_Award">MVP Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: MVP Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Most Valuable Player Award">Pacific Coast League Most Valuable Player Award</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Most Valuable Player Award">Most Valuable Player Award</a>, first awarded in 1927, is given to honor the best player in the league. The award is voted on by team managers, general managers, broadcasters, and media representatives from around the league, as are all PCL year-end awards.<sup id="cite_ref-AwardWinners_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AwardWinners-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitcher_of_the_Year_Award">Pitcher of the Year Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Pitcher of the Year Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Pitcher_of_the_Year_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Pitcher of the Year Award">Pacific Coast League Pitcher of the Year Award</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Pitcher_of_the_Year_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Pitcher of the Year Award">Pitcher of the Year Award</a>, first awarded in 2001, serves to recognize the league\'s best pitcher. Pitchers were eligible to win the MVP award from 1927 to 2000 as no award was designated solely for pitchers.<sup id="cite_ref-AwardWinners_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AwardWinners-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rookie_of_the_Year_Award">Rookie of the Year Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Rookie of the Year Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Rookie_of_the_Year_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Rookie of the Year Award">Pacific Coast League Rookie of the Year Award</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Rookie_of_the_Year_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Rookie of the Year Award">Rookie of the Year Award</a>, created in 1998, is given to the best player with no prior PCL experience.<sup id="cite_ref-AwardWinners_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AwardWinners-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manager_of_the_Year_Award">Manager of the Year Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Manager of the Year Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Manager_of_the_Year_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Manager of the Year Award">Pacific Coast League Manager of the Year Award</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Manager_of_the_Year_Award" title="Pacific Coast League Manager of the Year Award">Manager of the Year Award</a>, started in 1967, is given to the league\'s top manager.<sup id="cite_ref-AwardWinners_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AwardWinners-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Executive_of_the_Year_Award">Executive of the Year Award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Executive of the Year Award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Executive of the Year Award, first awarded in 1974, honors team executives who have achieved success in the area of attendance figures, promotions, and community involvement.<sup id="cite_ref-AwardWinners_26-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AwardWinners-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hall_of_fame">Hall of fame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Coast_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Hall of fame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_League_Hall_of_Fame" title="Pacific Coast League Hall of Fame">Pacific Coast League Hall of Fame</a></div>\n<p>The Pacific Coast League Hall of Fame was established in 1942 to honor league players, managers, and executives who have made significant contributions to the league\'s ideals. The Hall of Fame inducted its first class of 12&#160;men in 1943. The Hall became dormant after 1957, but was revived in 2003, the PCL\'s centennial season. Today, the Hall of Fame Committee seeks to recognize worthy players throughout the league\'s history who have made contributions to the league. New members are elected before the start of each season.<sup id="cite_ref-HallOfficial_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HallOfficial-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
