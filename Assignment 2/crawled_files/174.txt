href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The county councils created under British rule in 1899 continue to exist in Ireland, although they are now governed under legislation passed by <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas_%C3%89ireann" class="mw-redirect" title="Oireachtas \xc3\x89ireann">Oireachtas \xc3\x89ireann</a>, principally the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_2001" title="Local Government Act 2001">Local Government Act 2001</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span id="1899.E2.80.931922"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1899\xe2\x80\x931922">1899\xe2\x80\x931922</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1899\xe2\x80\x931922">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Ireland)_Act_1898" title="Local Government (Ireland) Act 1898">Local Government (Ireland) Act 1898</a> introduced county councils to Ireland. The administrative and financial business carried by county <a href="/wiki/Grand_jury" title="Grand jury">grand juries</a> and county at large presentment sessions were transferred to the new councils. Principal among these duties were the maintenance of highways and bridges, the upkeep and inspection of lunatic asylums and the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Coroner" title="Coroner">coroners</a>. The new bodies also took over some duties from <a href="/wiki/Irish_Poor_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Poor Law">poor law</a> <a href="/wiki/Board_of_guardians#Ireland" title="Board of guardians">boards of guardians</a> in relation to diseases of cattle and from the <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_peace" title="Justice of the peace">justices of the peace</a> to regulate explosives.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Irish county councils differed in constitution from those in Great Britain. Most of the council was directly elected: each county was divided by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Board_for_Ireland" title="Local Government Board for Ireland">Local Government Board for Ireland</a> into <a href="/wiki/District_Electoral_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="District Electoral Division">electoral divisions</a>, each returning a single councillor for a three-year term. In addition <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban districts</a> were to form electoral divisions: depending on population they could return multiple county councillors. The county councils were also to consist of "additional members":\n</p>\n<ul><li>The chairman of each <a href="/wiki/Rural_district" title="Rural district">rural district</a> council in the county was to be an <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_officio" class="mw-redirect" title="Ex officio">ex officio</a></i> member. Where the chairman had already been elected to the council or was disqualified, the RDC was to appoint another member of their council to be an additional member.</li>\n<li>The council could also co-opt one or two additional members for a three-year term.</li></ul>\n<p>The first county council elections were held on 6 April 1899, and the first business of their inaugural meetings being the appointment of additional members.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The triennial elections were postponed in 1914 on the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Local_Government_(Ireland)_Act,_1919" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Local Government (Ireland) Act, 1919">Local Government (Ireland) Act, 1919</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> to county councils: all councillors were to be elected by <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a> from multi-member electoral areas.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There was only one election under the new system, held in January 1920 (in urban areas) and on 2 June 1920 (in rural areas), during the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1922_to_present">1922 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1922 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> inherited the local authorities created by the United Kingdom legislation of 1898 and 1919, and elections were held on 23 June 1925. The first native legislation was the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1925" title="Local Government Act 1925">Local Government Act 1925</a>. The act abolished rural district councils (except in <a href="/wiki/County_Dublin" title="County Dublin">County Dublin</a>) and passed their powers to the county councils. At the following election all county councils were to be increased: the number of extra councillors was to be twice the number of abolished rural districts. The act set out the powers and duties of county councils and also gave the <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_the_Environment,_Community_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister for the Environment, Community and Local Government">Minister for Local Government</a> the power to dissolve councils if he was satisfied that <i>"the duties of a local council are not being duly and effectually discharged"</i>. He could order new elections to be held, or transfer the power and properties of the council <i>"to any body or persons or person he shall think fit"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The power was widely used by ministers of all parties. For example, Kerry County Council was dissolved from 1930 to 1932, and from 1945 to 1948, with commissioners appointed to perform the council\'s function.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The number of county councils was increased from twenty-seven to twenty-nine in 1994 when the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Dublin)_Act_1993" title="Local Government (Dublin) Act 1993">Local Government (Dublin) Act 1993</a> split County Dublin into three counties: <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAn_Laoghaire%E2%80%93Rathdown" title="D\xc3\xban Laoghaire\xe2\x80\x93Rathdown">D\xc3\xban Laoghaire\xe2\x80\x93Rathdown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fingal" title="Fingal">Fingal</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Dublin" title="South Dublin">South Dublin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan">Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span href="/wiki/Kinmen_County_Council" title="Kinmen County Council">Kinmen County Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lienchiang_County_Council" title="Lienchiang County Council">Lienchiang County Council</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>County councils were formed in the late 19th century. In the various <a href="/wiki/Constituent_countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Constituent countries of the United Kingdom">constituent countries of the United Kingdom</a> councils had different powers and different memberships. Following local government reforms in the 1970s, county councils no longer exist in Scotland or Northern Ireland. In England they generally form the top level in a two-tier system of administration; in Wales they are unitary authorities.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_county_councils_in_England" title="List of county councils in England">List of county councils in England</a></div>\n<p>In England county councils were introduced in 1889, and reformed in 1974. Since the mid-1990s a series of local government reorganisations has reduced the number of county councils as unitary authorities have been established in a number of areas. County councils are very large employers with a great variety of functions including education (schools and youth services), social services, highways, fire and rescue services, libraries, waste disposal, consumer services and town and country planning. Until the 1990s they also ran colleges of further education and the careers services. That decade also saw the privatisation of some traditional services, such as highway maintenance, cleaning and school meals.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>County councils were created by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>, largely taking over the administrative functions of the unelected county courts of <a href="/wiki/Quarter_sessions" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter sessions">quarter sessions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chamber_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chamber-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> County councils consisted of councillors, directly elected by the electorate; and <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">county aldermen</a>, chosen by the council itself. There was one county alderman for every three councillors (one for every six in the <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London County Council</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-lga88_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lga88-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The first elections to the councils were held at various dates in January 1889, and they served as "provisional" or shadow councils until 1 April, when they came into their powers.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Elections of all councillors and half of the aldermen took place every three years thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-lga88_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lga88-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The areas over which the councils had authority were designated as <a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties_of_England" title="Administrative counties of England">administrative counties</a>. The writ of the county councils did not extend everywhere: large towns and some historic <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">counties corporate</a> were constituted <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a> by the same act. County borough councils were independent of the council for the county in which they were geographically situated, and exercised the functions of both county and district councils.<sup id="cite_ref-chamber_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chamber-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The new system was a major modernisation, which reflected the increasing range of functions carried out by local government in late Victorian Britain. A major accretion of powers took place when education was added to county council responsibilities in 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> County councils were responsible for more strategic services in a region, with (from 1894) smaller <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban district</a> councils and <a href="/wiki/Rural_district" title="Rural district">rural district</a> councils responsible for other activities. The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1929" title="Local Government Act 1929">Local Government Act 1929</a> considerably increased the powers of county councils, who took charge of highways in rural districts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1965 there was a reduction in the number of county councils. The <a href="/wiki/London_Government_Act_1963" title="London Government Act 1963">London Government Act 1963</a> abolished those of <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a> and created the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a>. Greater London was declared to be an "area" and not to lie in any county.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In addition two pairs of administrative counties were merged to become <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_and_Isle_of_Ely" title="Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely">Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a> under recommendations made by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1958%E2%80%931967)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1958\xe2\x80\x931967)">Local Government Commission for England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> completely reorganised local authorities in England and Wales. County boroughs were abolished and the whole of England (apart from <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>) was placed in a two-tier arrangement with county councils and <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">district</a> councils. In the six largest conurbations <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan county</a> councils, with increased powers, were created. The post of county alderman was abolished, and the entire council was thereafter directly elected every four years. In 1986 the six metropolitan county councils were abolished, with their functions transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a> and joint boards.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The Local Government Act 1992 established a new <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1992)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1992)">Local Government Commission</a> whose remit was to conduct a review of the structure of local administration, and the introduction of unitary authorities where appropriate. Accordingly, the number of county councils was reduced: <a href="/wiki/Avon_(county)" title="Avon (county)">Avon</a>, Berkshire, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_(county)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland (county)">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hereford_and_Worcester" title="Hereford and Worcester">Hereford and Worcester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humberside" title="Humberside">Humberside</a> were abolished, while <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> County Council was re-established. The reforms somewhat blurred the distinction between county and district council. The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> county council became a unitary authority, renamed the "Isle of Wight Council".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, two unitary district councils added the word "county" to their titles to become "<a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a> County Council District Council" and "County of <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> District Council".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2009_reforms">2009 reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2009 reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A further <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">wave of local government reform</a> took place in April 2009 under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_and_Public_Involvement_in_Health_Act_2007" title="Local Government and Public Involvement in Health Act 2007">Local Government and Public Involvement in Health Act 2007</a>. Following invitations from central government in 2007, a number of County Councils and their associated districts examined ways in which local government provision could be rationalised, mainly in the form of abolishing the existing County and District councils and establishing one-tier authorities for all or parts of these existing counties. As a result, the status of some of these (mainly) more rural counties changed. Cornwall, Durham, Northumberland, Shropshire and Wiltshire became unitary authorities providing all services. Some of these councils have dropped the word "county" from their titles. <a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire" title="Bedfordshire">Bedfordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> County Councils were abolished with more than one unitary council established within the boundaries of the abolished council. Other county councils remained unchanged, particularly in the heavily populated parts of England such as the south-east.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>County councils existed in Northern Ireland from 1922 to 1973.\n</p><p>Following partition, six administrative counties remained within the United Kingdom as part of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. Local government came under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a>, who quickly introduced the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Government_Act_(Northern_Ireland)_1922&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Local Government Act (Northern Ireland) 1922 (page does not exist)">Local Government Act (Northern Ireland) 1922</a>, abolishing proportional representation. Electoral districts were redrawn, and a property qualification for voters introduced, ensuring Unionist controlled councils in counties with Nationalist majorities.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1968 Fermanagh County Council was reconstituted as a unitary authority. County councils were abolished under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_(Northern_Ireland)_1972" title="Local Government Act (Northern Ireland) 1972">Local Government Act (Northern Ireland) 1972</a> in 1973. The only local authorities since that date have been <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Districts of Northern Ireland">district councils</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> county councils existed from 1890 to 1975. They were created by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Scotland)_Act_1889" title="Local Government (Scotland) Act 1889">Local Government (Scotland) Act 1889</a> and reconstituted forty years later by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Scotland)_Act_1929" title="Local Government (Scotland) Act 1929">Local Government (Scotland) Act 1929</a>. County councils were abolished in 1975 when a system of large <a href="/wiki/Regions_and_districts_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Regions and districts of Scotland">regional councils</a> was introduced. Regions were themselves abolished in 1996 and replaced by the current unitary <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Scotland" title="Subdivisions of Scotland">council areas</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_3">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In Scotland control of county administration was in the hands of <a href="/wiki/Commissioners_of_Supply" class="mw-redirect" title="Commissioners of Supply">Commissioners of Supply</a>. This was a body of the principal landowners liable to pay land tax, and was unelected. The first elections to Scottish county councils took place in February 1890.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Only the councillors for the "landward" part of the county were elected however. The remainder of the council were co-opted by the town councils of the <a href="/wiki/Burgh" title="Burgh">burghs</a> in the county. Scottish county councils also differed from those in England and Wales as they were required to divide their county into districts. A district committee of the county councillors elected for the area were an independent local council for some administrative purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1930 the Scottish county councils were completely reconstituted. Their powers were increased in <a href="/wiki/Small_burgh" title="Small burgh">small burghs</a>. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Large_burgh" title="Large burgh">large burghs</a> became independent of the county for most purposes. The district committees created in 1890 were abolished and replaced by district councils, partly consisting of county councillors and partly of directly elected district councillors. Two joint county councils were created, for <a href="/wiki/Perthshire" title="Perthshire">Perthshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kinross-shire" title="Kinross-shire">Kinross-shire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nairnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Nairnshire">Nairnshire</a>. The county councils also gained the duties of the abolished education authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since 1996 <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> has been divided into unitary <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Subdivisions of Wales">principal areas</a>. Councils were designated by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Wales)_Act_1994" title="Local Government (Wales) Act 1994">legislation that created them</a> as either "county councils" or "county borough councils".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> County and county borough councils have identical powers.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_4">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Prior to 1996 local government in Wales was similar to that in England. Thus the county councils introduced in 1889 were identical to their English counterparts. The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_Wales" title="Local Government Commission for Wales">Local Government Commission for Wales</a> appointed under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1958" title="Local Government Act 1958">Local Government Act 1958</a> recommended a reduction in the number of county councils in Wales and <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire" title="Monmouthshire">Monmouthshire</a> from thirteen to seven, but reform did not take place until 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 1 April 1974 the number of counties and county councils was reduced to eight in number. Like the county councils introduced in England at the same time, the whole council was elected every four years. There was a slightly different division of powers between county and district councils, however. The county and district councils were abolished twenty-two years later, when the present system of principal areas was introduced.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the United States, most of the individual states have <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">counties</a> as a form of local government; in nine states, they are headed by a county council.  These states are South Carolina (all counties), Indiana (all but one county), Louisiana (19 parishes), Maryland (11 counties),<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Utah (7 counties), Washington (5 counties), Pennsylvania (4 counties), Ohio (2 counties), Florida (1 county), In other states, each county is headed by a <a href="/wiki/County_commission" title="County commission">county commission</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_board_of_supervisors" class="mw-redirect" title="County board of supervisors">county board of supervisors</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Freeholder_(government)" class="mw-redirect" title="Freeholder (government)">board of chosen freeholders</a> in New Jersey, a Commissioner\'s (or Quorum/Fiscal) Court in Texas, Missouri, Arkansas, and Kentucky, or a <a href="/wiki/Police_Jury" title="Police Jury">Police Jury</a> in Louisiana.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> has the weakest county governments in the nation. Counties were abolished in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> and much of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>, counties are a means of re-distributing funds authorized by the state. There is no actual county government.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a council is sometimes used in English for regional municipal bodies in other countries.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/County_council_(Norway)" title="County council (Norway)">County council
