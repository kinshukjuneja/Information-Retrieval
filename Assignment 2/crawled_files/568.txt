href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw"><b>Dowr Fala</b></i>) flows through <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, rising at Pentevale on <a href="/wiki/Goss_Moor" title="Goss Moor">Goss Moor</a> (between <a href="/wiki/St._Columb_Major" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Columb Major">St. Columb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roche,_Cornwall" title="Roche, Cornwall">Roche</a>) and reaching the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> at <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>. On or near the banks of the Fal are the castles of <a href="/wiki/Pendennis_Castle" title="Pendennis Castle">Pendennis</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Mawes_Castle" title="St Mawes Castle">St Mawes</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Trelissick_Garden" title="Trelissick Garden">Trelissick Garden</a>. The River Fal separates the <a href="/wiki/Roseland,_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Roseland, Cornwall">Roseland</a> peninsula from the rest of Cornwall. Like most of its kind on the south coast of Cornwall and Devon, the Fal estuary is a classic <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">ria</a>, or drowned river valley. The Fal estuary from Tregony to the Truro River was originally called <b>Hafaraell</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw"><b>Havarel</b></i>, meaning <i>fallow place</i>).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" tocsection-7"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The origin and meaning of the name of the river are unknown. The earliest occurrences of the name are in documents from AD 969 and 1049. <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a>, a town which was named <i>Smithwick</i> until the 17th century, is named after the River Fal.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>Fal</i> in <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> may refer to a prince, or perhaps to a spade or shovel.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The river mouth and Falmouth harbour served as an anchorage in the immediate years after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> for scores of laid-up <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> vessels (including battleships, carriers, and cruisers) awaiting sale for scrap. Its size and depth of water made it ideal for this use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The River Fal suffered a severe, high-profile pollution accident in February 1992, when a nearby tin mine was flooded. The river turned red and an extensive cleaning-up operation was needed to decontaminate the water.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> During times of reduced global trade, ships are <a href="/wiki/Reserve_fleet" title="Reserve fleet">mothballed</a> in the upper parts of the estuary of the River Fal.<sup id="cite_ref-Moored_Ships_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moored_Ships-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The ships, using the Fal estuary due to its depth and protection from the elements,<sup id="cite_ref-Moored_Ships_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moored_Ships-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> keep a <a href="/wiki/Skeleton_crew" title="Skeleton crew">skeleton crew</a> for ship maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-Moored_ships_2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moored_ships_2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A large number of ships were moored in the estuary during the <a href="/wiki/Late-2000s_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-2000s financial crisis">late-2000s financial crisis</a>, as falling global trade meant a smaller demand for trade ships. Falmouth Harbour authority charges companies for usage of the river, until the ships are either reactivated, scrapped or sold.<sup id="cite_ref-Moored_ships_2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moored_ships_2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_hydrology">Geography and hydrology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography and hydrology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The source can be traced to Pentevale, (more correctly Pentivale <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> ) (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Penfenten Fala</i>, meaning <i>head spring of the Fal</i>). The catchment of the Fal is predominantly Devonian slates, shales and grits, with granite in the upper reaches. Land use is mainly agricultural with some woodland.<sup id="cite_ref-nfra_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nfra-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tributaries of the River Fal include the <a href="/wiki/Truro_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Truro River">Truro River</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Kennal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="River Kennal (page does not exist)">River Kennal</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Penryn_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Penryn River (page does not exist)">Penryn River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carnon_River" title="Carnon River">Carnon River</a>. Several tidal creeks discharge into the River Fal including <a href="/wiki/Mylor_Creek" title="Mylor Creek">Mylor Creek</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pill_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pill Creek (page does not exist)">Pill Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penpol_Creek" class="mw-redirect" title="Penpol Creek">Penpol Creek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Restronguet_Creek" title="Restronguet Creek">Restronguet Creek</a>. The river flows through the <a href="/wiki/Carrick_Roads" title="Carrick Roads">Carrick Roads</a>, a large <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">ria</a> that creates a <a href="/wiki/Natural_harbour" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural harbour">natural harbour</a>. The waterway is navigable between Falmouth and Truro. The river is crossed by the <a href="/wiki/King_Harry_Ferry" title="King Harry Ferry">King Harry Ferry</a>, a vehicular <a href="/wiki/Cable_ferry" title="Cable ferry">chain ferry</a> that links the villages of <a href="/wiki/Feock,_Cornwall" title="Feock, Cornwall">Feock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philleigh" title="Philleigh">Philleigh</a> approximately equidistant between Truro and Falmouth.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_and_conservation">Wildlife and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wildlife and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Five <a href="/wiki/Sites_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" class="mw-redirect" title="Sites of Special Scientific Interest">Sites of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSI) have been designated along the River Fal.  The river rises on <a href="/wiki/Goss_Moor" title="Goss Moor">Goss Moor</a> which is both a <a href="/wiki/National_Nature_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="National Nature Reserve">National Nature Reserve</a> and part of a SSSI. The wetland habitats and western dry heath communities were formed, since the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, by the mining of the <a href="/wiki/Alluvial" class="mw-redirect" title="Alluvial">alluvial</a> tin deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <b>South Terras Mine</b> SSSI is also a <a href="/wiki/Geological_Conservation_Review" title="Geological Conservation Review">Geological Conservation Review</a> (GCR) site of national importance and was once a commercial <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> mine. The <b>Crowhill Valley SSSI</b> between <a href="/wiki/Grampound" title="Grampound">Grampound</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Stephen-in-Brannel" title="St Stephen-in-Brannel">St Stephen</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_woodland" title="Ancient woodland">ancient woodland</a> dominated by <a href="/wiki/Sessile_oak" class="mw-redirect" title="Sessile oak">sessile oak</a> on the slopes, and  of <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alder</a> <a href="/wiki/Carr_(topography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Carr (topography)">carr</a> on the floodplain which has a rich ground flora. The species list includes many ancient woodland indicators including <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a> such as <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmelia_endochlora&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Parmelia endochlora (page does not exist)">Parmelia endochlora</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <b>Upper Fal Estuary and Woods SSSI</b> is mostly within the tidal area of the river between <a href="/wiki/Tregony" title="Tregony">Tregony</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnaware_Point&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Turnaware Point (page does not exist)">Turnaware Point</a> and includes <a href="/wiki/Mudflats" class="mw-redirect" title="Mudflats">mudflats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">salt marsh</a> and ancient woodland. The mudflats support nationally important numbers of <a href="/wiki/Black-tailed_godwit" title="Black-tailed godwit">black-tailed godwit</a> as well as large populations of wintering waders and wildfowl such as <a href="/wiki/Curlew" title="Curlew">curlew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_plover" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden plover">golden plover</a>. A series of transition zones occur from the salt marsh to <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrubland">scrub</a>, where young trees attempt to grow despite the threat of the highest tides, to ancient woodland on the higher slopes. These zones and the estuary itself are important for <a href="/wiki/Otter" title="Otter">otter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The lowest part of the Fal along with the <a href="/wiki/Helford_River" title="Helford River">Helford River</a> is designated as the <b>Fal and Helford</b> <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Habitats_Directive" title="Habitats Directive">Habitats Directive</a> for the shallow bays and inlets, sandbanks and mudflats, and for its salt marshes. Part of the area is also designated as an <a href="/wiki/Important_Plant_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Important Plant Area">Important Plant Area</a> and a SSSI (<b>Lower Fal &amp; Helford Intertidal</b>) for the relatively undisturbed transitions from tidal mud through saltmarsh and scrub to woodland.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The River Fal is accessible for kayaking, sailing and other watersports.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Towns_and_villages_on_the_Fal">Towns and villages on the Fal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Fal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Towns and villages on the Fal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 15em;
