&#39;guy&#39;">\xc9\xa1</span><span title="/\xc9\x99l/: &#39;le&#39; in &#39;bottle&#39;">\xc9\x99l</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/i\xcb\x90/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">i\xcb\x90</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Ynys M\xc3\xb4n</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Welsh" miles (715&#160;km<sup>2</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Anglesey is by far the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Wales" title="List of islands of Wales">largest island in Wales</a> and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_the_British_Isles" title="List of islands of the British Isles">seventh largest in the British Isles</a>. Anglesey is also the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_in_the_Irish_Sea" title="List of islands in the Irish Sea">largest island in the Irish Sea by area</a>, and the second most populous island (after the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Holyhead" title="Port of Holyhead">port of Holyhead</a> handles more than 2 million passengers each year.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Menai_Suspension_Bridge" title="Menai Suspension Bridge">Menai Suspension Bridge</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a> in 1826, and the <a href="/wiki/Britannia_Bridge" title="Britannia Bridge">Britannia Bridge</a> span the <a href="/wiki/Menai_Strait" title="Menai Strait">Menai Strait</a> to connect Anglesey with the mainland.\n</p><p>Anglesey, one of the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_Wales" title="Historic counties of Wales">historic counties of Wales</a> was administered as part of <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>, but along with <a href="/wiki/Holy_Island,_Anglesey" title="Holy Island, Anglesey">Holy Island</a> and other smaller islands, it is now governed by the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Anglesey_County_Council" title="Isle of Anglesey County Council">Isle of Anglesey County Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The majority of Anglesey\'s inhabitants are <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> speakers<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Ynys M\xc3\xb4n</i>, the Welsh name for the island, is used for the <a href="/wiki/Ynys_M%C3%B4n_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Ynys M\xc3\xb4n (UK Parliament constituency)">UK Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ynys_M%C3%B4n_(Assembly_constituency)" title="Ynys M\xc3\xb4n (Assembly constituency)">National Assembly</a> constituencies. The population at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_census,_2011" title="United Kingdom census, 2011">2011 census</a> was 69,751.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><span id="Names"></span><span id="Etymology"></span>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anglesey_OS_map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Anglesey_OS_map.png/220px-Anglesey_OS_map.png" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Anglesey_OS_map.png/330px-Anglesey_OS_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Anglesey_OS_map.png/440px-Anglesey_OS_map.png 2x" data-file-width="2243" data-file-height="1449" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anglesey_OS_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ordnance Survey map of Anglesey</div></div></div>\n<p>The name of the island may be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>; either <i>\xc7\xaangullsey</i> "Hook Island"<sup id="cite_ref-Pete_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pete-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or <i>\xc7\xaanglisey</i> "\xc7\xaangli\'s Island".<sup id="cite_ref-Pete_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pete-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Roomy_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roomy-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> No record of such an \xc7\xaangli survives,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> but the place name was used in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Gwynedd_during_the_High_Middle_Ages#vikings" title="History of Gwynedd during the High Middle Ages">Viking raiders</a> as early as the 10th century and was later adopted by the Normans during their <a href="/wiki/History_of_Gwynedd_during_the_High_Middle_Ages#Norman_invasions" title="History of Gwynedd during the High Middle Ages">invasions of Gwynedd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tumultuous_Century_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tumultuous_Century-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The traditional <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a> reading the name as the "Island of the <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Angles</a> (English)"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LEnc_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LEnc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> may account for its Norman use but has no merit,<sup id="cite_ref-Roomy_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roomy-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> although the <a href="/wiki/Angles#Name" title="Angles">Angles\' name itself</a> is probably a cognate reference to the shape of the <a href="/wiki/Angeln" class="mw-redirect" title="Angeln">Angeln</a> peninsula. All of these ultimately derive from the proposed <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European root</a> <i>*ank-</i> ("to flex, bend, angle").<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Through the 18th and 19th centuries and into the 20th, it was usually spelt <b>Anglesea</b> in documents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Ynys M\xc3\xb4n</i>, the island\'s <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> name, was first recorded as <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Mona</i> by various Roman sources.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It was likewise known to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxons</a> as <i>Monez</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brittonic</a> original was in the past taken to have meant "Island of the Cow".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davy_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davy-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This view is untenable, however, according to modern scientific philology, and the etymology remains a mystery.\n</p><p>Poetic names for Anglesey include the <a href="/wiki/Old_Welsh" title="Old Welsh">Old Welsh</a> <i>Ynys Dywyll</i> (Shady or Dark Isle) for its <a href="/wiki/Nemeton" title="Nemeton">former groves</a> and <i>Ynys y Cedairn</i> (Isle of the Brave) for its royal courts;<sup id="cite_ref-LEnc_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LEnc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gerald_of_Wales" title="Gerald of Wales">Gerald of Wales</a>\' <i>M\xc3\xb4n Mam Cymru</i> ("M\xc3\xb4n, Mother of Wales") for its productivity;<sup id="cite_ref-LEnc_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LEnc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Y f\xc3\xaal Ynys</i> (Honey Isle).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg/220px-John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg/330px-John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg/440px-John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="747" data-file-height="585" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Speed_Anglesey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Speed\'s map of Anglesey, 1607</div></div></div>\n<p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic</a> monuments and <a href="/wiki/Menhir" title="Menhir">menhirs</a> are present on Anglesey, testifying to the presence of humans in <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plas_Newydd_(Anglesey)" title="Plas Newydd (Anglesey)">Plas Newydd</a> is near one of 28 <a href="/wiki/Dolmen" title="Dolmen">cromlechs</a> that remain on uplands overlooking the sea. The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Triads" title="Welsh Triads">Welsh Triads</a> claim that Anglesey was once part of the mainland.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg/220px-Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg/330px-Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg/440px-Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plas_Newydd_Anglesey_House_NW_view.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plas Newydd</div></div></div>\n<p>Historically, Anglesey has long been associated with the <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">druids</a>. In AD 60 the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> general <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Suetonius_Paulinus" title="Gaius Suetonius Paulinus">Gaius Suetonius Paulinus</a>, determined to break the power of the druids, attacked the island using his amphibious <a href="/wiki/Batavi_(Germanic_tribe)" title="Batavi (Germanic tribe)">Batavian</a> contingent as a surprise <a href="/wiki/Vanguard" title="Vanguard">vanguard</a> assault<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and then destroying the shrine and the <a href="/wiki/Nemeton" title="Nemeton">nemetons</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sacred_grove" title="Sacred grove">sacred groves</a>). News of <a href="/wiki/Boudica" title="Boudica">Boudica</a>\'s revolt reached him just after his victory, causing him to withdraw his army before consolidating his conquest. The island was finally brought into the Roman Empire by <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gnaeus Julius Agricola</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_governor" title="Roman governor">Roman governor</a> of Britain, in AD 78. During the Roman occupation, the area was notable for the mining of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. The foundations of <a href="/wiki/Caer_Gybi_(fort)" title="Caer Gybi (fort)">Caer Gybi</a> as well as a fort at <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a> are Roman, and the present road from Holyhead to <a href="/wiki/Llanfairpwllgwyngyll" title="Llanfairpwllgwyngyll">Llanfairpwllgwyngyll</a> was originally a <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads" title="Roman roads">Roman road</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The island was grouped by <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> with <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Ireland" title="Geography of Ireland">Ireland</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a>") rather than with <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Albion" title="Albion">Albion</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">British Iron Age</a> and Roman sites have been excavated and coins and ornaments discovered, especially by the 19th century <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">antiquarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Owen_Stanley" title="William Owen Stanley">William Owen Stanley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/End_of_Roman_rule_in_Britain" title="End of Roman rule in Britain">Roman departure from Britain</a> in the early 5th century, pirates from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> colonised Anglesey and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Ll%C5%B7n_Peninsula" title="Ll\xc5\xb7n Peninsula">Ll\xc5\xb7n Peninsula</a>. In response to this, <a href="/wiki/Cunedda" title="Cunedda">Cunedda ap Edern</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gododdin" title="Gododdin">Gododdin</a> warlord from <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, came to the area and began to drive the Irish out. This was continued by his son <a href="/wiki/Einion_Yrth_ap_Cunedda" title="Einion Yrth ap Cunedda">Einion Yrth ap Cunedda</a> and grandson <a href="/wiki/Cadwallon_Lawhir_ap_Einion" title="Cadwallon Lawhir ap Einion">Cadwallon Lawhir ap Einion</a>; the last Irish invaders were finally defeated in battle in 470. As an island, Anglesey was in a good defensive position, and so <a href="/wiki/Aberffraw" title="Aberffraw">Aberffraw</a> became the site of the court, or <i>Llys</i>, of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Kingdom of Gwynedd</a>. Apart from a devastating <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> raid in 853 it remained the capital until the 13th century, when improvements to the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> navy made the location indefensible. Anglesey was also briefly the most southern possession of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Norway_(872%E2%80%931397)" title="Kingdom of Norway (872\xe2\x80\x931397)">Norwegian Empire</a>.\n</p><p>After the Irish, the island was invaded by <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> \xe2\x80\x94 some of these raids were noted in famous <a href="/wiki/Saga" title="Saga">sagas</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Menai_Strait#History" title="Menai Strait">Menai Strait History</a>) \xe2\x80\x94 and by <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>, before falling to <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a> in the 13th century.\n</p><p>Anglesey (together with Holy Island) is one of the thirteen <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_Wales" title="Historic counties of Wales">historic counties of Wales</a>. In medieval times, before the conquest of Wales in 1283, <i>M\xc3\xb4n</i> often had periods of temporary independence, as it was frequently bequeathed to the heirs of kings as a sub-kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>. The last times this occurred were a few years after 1171, following the death of <a href="/wiki/Owain_Gwynedd" title="Owain Gwynedd">Owain Gwynedd</a>, when the island was inherited by <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_ab_Owain_Gwynedd" title="Rhodri ab Owain Gwynedd">Rhodri ab Owain Gwynedd</a>, and between 1246 and c. 1255, when it was granted to <a href="/wiki/Owain_Goch" class="mw-redirect" title="Owain Goch">Owain Goch</a> as his share of the kingdom. Following the conquest of Wales by <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>, Anglesey was created a county under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Rhuddlan" title="Statute of Rhuddlan">Statute of Rhuddlan</a> of 1284. Prior to this it had been divided into the <i><a href="/wiki/Cantref" title="Cantref">cantrefi</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Cantref_Aberffraw" class="mw-redirect" title="Cantref Aberffraw">Aberffraw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantref_Rhosyr" class="mw-redirect" title="Cantref Rhosyr">Rhosyr</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantref_Cemaes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cantref Cemaes (page does not exist)">Cemaes</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th-century_history">20th-century history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th-century history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, the Presbyterian minister and celebrity preacher John Williams toured the island as part of an effort to recruit young men to volunteer for a \xe2\x80\x9cjust war\xe2\x80\x9d.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> German <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">POWs</a> were kept on the island.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the end of the war, approximately 1,000 of the island\'s men had died while on active service.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1936 the <a href="/wiki/National_Society_for_the_Prevention_of_Cruelty_to_Children" title="National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children">NSPCC</a> opened its first branch on Anglesey.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>,  Anglesey received Italian <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">POWs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The island was designated a reception zone, and was home to evacuee children from Liverpool and Manchester.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The island\'s location made it an ideal place to monitor <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">German U-Boat</a> based in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>. Around half a dozen airships operated out of <a href="/wiki/RAF_Mona" title="RAF Mona">Mona</a> airfield to monitor German submarine activity.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1974, Anglesey became a district of the new large <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Wales#Counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Administrative divisions of Wales">county</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Wales)_Act_1994" title="Local Government (Wales) Act 1994">Local Government (Wales) Act 1994</a> abolished the 1974 county and the five districts on 1 April 1996. Anglesey became an independent <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a>. In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a> appointed a panel of commissioners to administer the council, thus the elected members were not in control. The commissioners remained in control until an <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Anglesey_County_Council_election,_2013" title="Isle of Anglesey County Council election, 2013">election</a> was held in May 2013, restoring an elected Council. Prior to the direct administration, there were a majority of independent councillors, though members did not generally divide along party lines, organised into five non-partisan groups on the council, containing a mix of party and independent candidates. The position remains substantially unchanged after the election, although the Labour party has formed a governing coalition with the independents.\n</p><p>The principal offices of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Anglesey_County_Council" title="Isle of Anglesey County Council">Isle of Anglesey County Council</a> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Cyngor Sir Ynys M\xc3\xb4n</i>) are in <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a> (<small>Welsh pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Welsh" title="Help:IPA/Welsh">[\xc9\xacan\xcb\x88\xc9\xa1\xc9\x9bvni]</a></span>) the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Academy_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Academy-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brittania_Bridge_Train_crossing_3.JPG" class="image"><img spaced evenly over the north of the island. The highest six are <a href="/wiki/Holyhead_Mountain" title="Holyhead Mountain">Holyhead Mountain</a>, 220 metres (720&#160;ft); <a href="/wiki/Mynydd_Bodafon" title="Mynydd Bodafon">Mynydd Bodafon</a>, 178 metres (584&#160;ft); <a href="/wiki/Llaneilian" title="Llaneilian">Mynydd Llaneilian</a>, 177 metres (581&#160;ft); <a href="/wiki/Mynydd_y_Garn" title="Mynydd y Garn">Mynydd y Garn</a>, 170 metres (560&#160;ft); <a href="/wiki/Bwrdd_Arthur" title="Bwrdd Arthur">Bwrdd Arthur</a>, 164 metres (538&#160;ft); and Mynydd Llwydiarth, 158 metres (518&#160;ft). To the south and south-east the island is separated from the Welsh mainland by the <a href="/wiki/Menai_Strait" title="Menai Strait">Menai Strait</a>, which at its narrowest point is about 250 metres (270&#160;yd) wide. In all other directions the island is surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_European_islands_by_area" title="List of European islands by area">51st largest island</a> in Europe, and just five square kilometres smaller than <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.\n</p><p>There are several small towns scattered around the island, making it quite evenly populated. The largest are <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benllech" title="Benllech">Benllech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menai_Bridge" title="Menai Bridge">Menai Bridge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amlwch" title="Amlwch">Amlwch</a>. <a href="/wiki/Beaumaris" title="Beaumaris">Beaumaris</a> (Welsh: <i>Biwmares</i>), in the east of the island, features <a href="/wiki/Beaumaris_Castle" title="Beaumaris Castle">Beaumaris Castle</a>, built by <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a> as part of his <a href="/wiki/Bastide" title="Bastide">Bastide Town</a> campaign in <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a>. Beaumaris is a <a href="/wiki/Yachting" title="Yachting">yachting</a> centre, with many boats moored in the bay or off Gallows Point. The village of <a href="/wiki/Newborough,_Anglesey" title="Newborough, Anglesey">Newborough</a> (Welsh: <i>Niwbwrch</i>), in the south, created when the townsfolk of Llanfaes were relocated to make way for the building of Beaumaris Castle, includes the site of <a href="/wiki/Llys_Rhosyr" title="Llys Rhosyr">Llys Rhosyr</a>, another of the courts of the medieval Welsh princes, which features one of the oldest courtrooms in the United Kingdom. <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a> is located in the centre and is the island\'s administrative centre. The town of <a href="/wiki/Menai_Bridge" title="Menai Bridge">Menai Bridge</a> (Welsh: <i>Porthaethwy</i>, in the southeast) expanded when the first bridge to the mainland was being built, in order to accommodate workers and construction. Until then, Porthaethwy had been one of the principal ferry crossing points from the mainland. A short distance from this town lies <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Celli_Ddu" title="Bryn Celli Ddu">Bryn Celli Ddu</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> burial mound.\n</p><p>Nearby is the village with the longest name in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Llanfairpwllgwyngyll" title="Llanfairpwllgwyngyll">Llanfairpwllgwyngyllgogerychwyrndrobwllllantysiliogogogoch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plas_Newydd_(Anglesey)" title="Plas Newydd (Anglesey)">Plas Newydd</a>, ancestral home of the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Anglesey" title="Marquess of Anglesey">Marquesses of Anglesey</a>. The town of <a href="/wiki/Amlwch" title="Amlwch">Amlwch</a> is situated in the northeast of the island and was once largely industrialised, having grown during the 18th century by supporting an important <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>-mining industry at <a href="/wiki/Parys_Mountain" title="Parys Mountain">Parys Mountain</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div title="Pentraeth">Pentraeth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaerwen" title="Gaerwen">Gaerwen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwyran" title="Dwyran">Dwyran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bodedern" title="Bodedern">Bodedern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malltraeth" title="Malltraeth">Malltraeth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhosneigr" title="Rhosneigr">Rhosneigr</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_Sea_Zoo" title="Anglesey Sea Zoo">Anglesey Sea Zoo</a> is a local tourist attraction, providing a descriptive look at local marine <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> from <a href="/wiki/Homarus_gammarus" title="Homarus gammarus">common lobsters</a> to <a href="/wiki/Conger" title="Conger">congers</a>. All the fish and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> on display are caught around the island and placed in reconstructions of their natural <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_Sea_Zoo" title="Anglesey Sea Zoo">Anglesey Sea Zoo</a> also breed commercially lobsters, for food, and <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a>, for pearls, both from local stocks. Sea salt (Halen M\xc3\xb4n, evaporated from the local sea water), now produced in a modern facility nearby, was formerly produced at the Sea Zoo site.\n</p><p>There are a few natural lakes, mostly in the west, such as <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Llywenan" title="Llyn Llywenan">Llyn Llywenan</a>, the largest on the island, Llyn Coron, and Cors Cerrig y Daran, but rivers are few and small. There are two large water supply reservoirs operated by <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Water" title="Welsh Water">Welsh Water</a>. These are <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Alaw" title="Llyn Alaw">Llyn Alaw</a> to the north of the island and <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Cefni" title="Llyn Cefni">Llyn Cefni</a> in the centre of the island, which is fed by the headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/Afon_Cefni" title="Afon Cefni">afon Cefni</a>.\n</p><p>The climate is humid (though much less so than neighbouring mountainous <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>) and generally equable, being influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>. The land is of variable quality and it was probably much more fertile in the past. Anglesey is the home of the northernmost <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a> grove in Europe and presumably in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_path">Coastal path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Coastal path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island\'s entire rural coastline has been designated an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> and features many sandy beaches, especially along its eastern coast between the towns of Beaumaris and Amlwch and along the western coast from <a href="/wiki/Ynys_Llanddwyn" title="Ynys Llanddwyn">Ynys Llanddwyn</a> through <a href="/wiki/Rhosneigr" title="Rhosneigr">Rhosneigr</a> to the little bays around Carmel Head. The northern coastline has dramatic cliffs interspersed with small bays<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_Coastal_Path" title="Anglesey Coastal Path">Anglesey Coastal Path</a> follows the entire way around the island. It is 124 miles (200&#160;km) long and passes by/through 20 towns/villages. The official start point is <a href="/wiki/St_Cybi%27s_Church,_Holyhead" class="mw-redirect" title="St Cybi&#39;s Church, Holyhead">St Cybi\'s Church, Holyhead</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Tourism is now the most significant economic activity on the island. Agriculture provides the secondary source of income for the island\'s economy, with the local dairies being some of the most productive in the region.\n</p><p>Major industry is restricted to Holyhead (Caergybi), which until 30 September 2009 supported an <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_smelting" title="Aluminium smelting">aluminium smelter</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Amlwch" title="Amlwch">Amlwch</a> area, once a major copper mining town. Nearby is the site of the former <a href="/wiki/Wylfa_Nuclear_Power_Station" title="Wylfa Nuclear Power Station">Wylfa Nuclear Power Station</a> and a former <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> extraction plant. With construction starting in 1963, the two Wylfa reactors began producing electricity in 1971. One reactor was decommissioned in 2012 and the other in 2015. The site remains a strong possibility for a replacement reactor, planned by <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">Horizon</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a>, to start production in the 2020s.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The replacement has been enthusiastically endorsed by Anglesey Council and Welsh Assembly members, but protesters have raised doubts about the economic and safety claims made for the plant.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Anglesey also has three wind farms on land,<sup id="cite_ref-energy_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and more than twenty offshore <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> near the north coast. There are plans for the world\'s first Tidal Flow turbines, near <a href="/wiki/The_Skerries,_Isle_of_Anglesey" title="The Skerries, Isle of Anglesey">The Skerries</a>, off the north coast,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and for a major <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> plant on <a href="/wiki/Holy_Island,_Anglesey" title="Holy Island, Anglesey">Holy Island</a> (Ynys Gybi). Developing such low-carbon energy assets to their full potential forms part of the Anglesey Energy Island project.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When the aluminium smelting operation closed in September 2009, it reduced its workforce from 450 to 80; this has been a major blow to the island\'s economy, especially to Holyhead. The Royal Air Force station <a href="/wiki/RAF_Valley" title="RAF Valley">RAF Valley</a> (Y Fali) is home to the RAF Fast Jet Training School and also 22 Sqn Search and Rescue Helicopters, both units providing employment for about 500 civilians. RAF Valley is now the headquarters of 22 Sqn Search and Rescue.\n</p><p>There is a wide range of smaller industries, mostly located in industrial and business parks, especially at <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaerwen" title="Gaerwen">Gaerwen</a>. These include an <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse" title="Slaughterhouse">abattoir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fine_chemical" title="Fine chemical">fine chemical</a> manufacturing, as well as factories for timber production, aluminium smelting, fish farming and food processing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The island is also on one of the major routes from Britain to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, via <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferries</a> from <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>, off the west of Anglesey on Holy Island, serving <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAn_Laoghaire" title="D\xc3\xban Laoghaire">D\xc3\xban Laoghaire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> Port.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_conservation">Ecology and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ecology and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Much of Anglesey is used for relatively intensive cattle and sheep farming. However, there are a number of important <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> sites which have protected status. In addition the several lakes all have significant ecological interest, including their support for a wide range of aquatic and semi-aquatic bird species. In the west, the <a href="/wiki/Malltraeth_Marsh" title="Malltraeth Marsh">Malltraeth Marshes</a> are believed to support an occasional visiting <a href="/wiki/Bittern" title="Bittern">bittern</a>, and the nearby estuary of the <a href="/wiki/River_Cefni" class="mw-redirect" title="River Cefni">Afon Cefni</a> supports a bird population made internationally famous by the paintings of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Tunnicliffe" title="Charles Tunnicliffe">Charles Tunnicliffe</a>, who lived for many years&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 and died \xe2\x80\x93 at <a href="/wiki/Malltraeth" title="Malltraeth">Malltraeth</a> on the Cefni estuary. The <a href="/wiki/RAF" class="mw-redirect" title="RAF">RAF</a> airstrip at Mona is a nesting site for <a href="/wiki/Skylark" class="mw-redirect" title="Skylark">skylarks</a>. The sheer cliff faces at <a href="/wiki/South_Stack" title="South Stack">South Stack</a> near <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a> provide nesting sites for huge numbers of <a href="/wiki/Auk" title="Auk">auks</a> including <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">puffins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Razorbill" title="Razorbill">razorbills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_guillemot" class="mw-redirect" title="Common guillemot">guillemots</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Chough" title="Chough">choughs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcons</a>.\n</p><p>Three sites on Anglesey are important for breeding terns&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 see <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_tern_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglesey tern colonies">Anglesey tern colonies</a>. There are significant occurrences of the <a href="/wiki/Juncus_subnodulosus-Cirsium_palustre_fen-meadow" class="mw-redirect" title="Juncus subnodulosus-Cirsium palustre fen-meadow"><i>Juncus subnodulosus</i>-<i>Cirsium palustre</i> fen-meadow</a> <a href="/wiki/Plant_association" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant association">plant association</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> characterised by certain hydrophilic grasses, sedges and forbs.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Anglesey is home to several species of tern, including the <a href="/wiki/Roseate_tern" title="Roseate tern">roseate tern</a>.\n</p><p>Anglesey is home to two of the UK\'s few remaining colonies of <a href="/wiki/Red_squirrel" title="Red squirrel">red squirrels</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Pentraeth" title="Pentraeth">Pentraeth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newborough,_Anglesey" title="Newborough, Anglesey">Newborough</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Almost the entire coastline of Anglesey is designated as an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) to protect the aesthetic appeal and variety of the island\'s coastal landscape and habitats from inappropriate development. The coastal zone of Anglesey was designated as an AONB in 1966 and was confirmed as such in 1967.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The AONB is predominantly a coastal designation, covering most of Anglesey\'s 125 miles (201&#160;km) coastline but also encompasses Holyhead Mountain and Mynydd Bodafon. Substantial areas of other land protected by the AONB form the backdrop to the coast. The AONB is about 221&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (85 sq mi) and it is the largest AONB in Wales, covering one third of the island.\n</p><p>A number of the habitats in Anglesey are afforded even greater protection through both UK and European designations because of their nature conservation value: these include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>6 candidate Special Areas of Conservation (cSACs)</li>\n<li>4 Special Protection Areas (SPAs)</li>\n<li>1 National Nature Reserve</li>\n<li>26 Sites of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI)</li>\n<li>52 Scheduled Ancient Monuments (SAMs)</li></ul>\n<p>These protected habitats support a variety of wildlife such as <a href="/wiki/Harbour_porpoise" title="Harbour porpoise">harbour porpoises</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsh_fritillary" title="Marsh fritillary">marsh fritillary</a>.\n</p><p>The AONB also takes in three sections of open, undeveloped coastline which have been designated as Heritage Coast. These non-statutory designations complement the AONB and cover about 31 miles (50&#160;km) of the coastline. The sections of Heritage Coast are:\n</p>\n<ol><li>North Anglesey 28.6&#160;km (17.8&#160;mi)</li>\n<li>Holyhead Mountain 12.9&#160;km (8.0&#160;mi)</li>\n<li>Aberffraw Bay 7.7&#160;km (4.8&#160;mi)</li></ol>\n<p>The most popular forms of recreation include sailing, angling, cycling, walking, wind surfing and jet skiing. These all place pressures and demands on the AONB; but these activities contribute to the local economy.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planned_nuclear_plant">Planned nuclear plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span id="Welsh_language">Welsh language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Welsh language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Anglesey has historically been a stronghold of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh language</a>, and according to the 2011 census it was the second most Welsh-speaking local authority area in Wales. The historical proportions of residents who could speak Welsh are as follows:\n</p>\n<ul><li>1901: 91%<sup id="cite_ref-Census-Report_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census-Report-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>1911: 89%<sup id="cite_ref-Census-Report_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census-Report-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>1921: 88%</li>\n<li>1931: 87%</li>\n<li>1951: 80%<sup id="cite_ref-Gwynedd_Council_Website_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gwynedd_Council_Website-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>1961: 75%</li>\n<li>1971: 66%</li>\n<li>1981: 61%</li>\n<li>1991: 62%</li>\n<li>2001: 60%</li>\n<li>2011: 57%</li></ul>\n<p>Today, Welsh is less widely used on the island, but it is still the dominant language in certain areas, particularly in the centre, including the town of <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a>, and some areas of the south coast. A notable example is the village of <a href="/wiki/Llanfairpwllgwyngyll" title="Llanfairpwllgwyngyll">Llanfairpwll</a>. The island\'s five secondary schools vary widely in the percentage of their pupils who come from predominantly Welsh-speaking homes, as do the percentages who can speak Welsh:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ysgol_David_Hughes" title="Ysgol David Hughes">Ysgol David Hughes</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Menai_Bridge" title="Menai Bridge">Menai Bridge</a>): 33% come from Welsh-speaking homes; 90% "can speak Welsh."<sup id="cite_ref-Estyn_Report,2012_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Estyn_Report,2012-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ysgol_Gyfun_Llangefni" title="Ysgol Gyfun Llangefni">Ysgol Gyfun Llangefni</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a>): 68% of pupils speak Welsh as their <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first language</a>; 87% of pupils take their exams through the medium of Welsh.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ysgol_Syr_Thomas_Jones" title="Ysgol Syr Thomas Jones">Ysgol Syr Thomas Jones</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Amlwch" title="Amlwch">Amlwch</a>): 34% of pupils come from Welsh-speaking homes; 82% sit the Welsh First Language <a href="/wiki/General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="General Certificate of Secondary Education">General Certificate of Secondary Education</a> (GCSE).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ysgol_Uwchradd_Bodedern" title="Ysgol Uwchradd Bodedern">Ysgol Uwchradd Bodedern</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Bodedern" title="Bodedern">Bodedern</a>): 67% of pupils come from Welsh-speaking homes; "a majority" speak Welsh fluently.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ysgol_Uwchradd_Caergybi" title="Ysgol Uwchradd Caergybi">Ysgol Uwchradd Caergybi</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>): 14% of pupils speak Welsh at home; 11% are taught the "Welsh First Language" curriculum.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The geology of Anglesey is notably complex and is frequently used for geology <a href="/wiki/Field_trip" title="Field trip">field trips</a> by schools and colleges. Younger strata in Anglesey rest upon a foundation of very old <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a> rocks that appear at the surface in four areas:\n</p>\n<ol><li>a western region including Holyhead and <a href="/wiki/Llanfaethlu" title="Llanfaethlu">Llanfaethlu</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>a central area about <a href="/wiki/Aberffraw" title="Aberffraw">Aberffraw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trefdraeth" title="Trefdraeth">Trefdraeth</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>an eastern region which includes <a href="/wiki/Newborough,_Anglesey" title="Newborough, Anglesey">Newborough</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gaerwen" title="Gaerwen">Gaerwen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pentraeth" title="Pentraeth">Pentraeth</a></li>\n<li>a coastal region at <a href="/wiki/Menai_Bridge#Glyn_Garth" title="Menai Bridge">Glyn Garth</a> between <a href="/wiki/Menai_Bridge" title="Menai Bridge">Menai Bridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beaumaris" title="Beaumaris">Beaumaris</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>These Precambrian rocks are <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phyllite" title="Phyllite">phyllites</a>, often much contorted and disturbed. The general line of strike of the formations in the island is from north-east to south-west.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>} A belt of <a href="/wiki/Granitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Granitic">granitic rocks</a> lies immediately north-west of the central Precambrian mass, reaching from <a href="/wiki/Llanfaelog" title="Llanfaelog">Llanfaelog</a> near the coast to the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Llanerchymedd" class="mw-redirect" title="Llanerchymedd">Llanerchymedd</a>. Between this granite and the Precambrian of Holyhead is a narrow tract of <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> slates and grits with Llandovery beds in places; this tract spreads out in the north of the island between <a href="/wiki/Dulas_Bay" title="Dulas Bay">Dulas Bay</a> and Carmel Point.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A small patch of Ordovician strata lies on the northern side of Beaumaris. In parts, these Ordovician rocks are much folded, crushed and metamorphosed, and they are associated with schists and altered volcanic rocks which are probably Precambrian. Between the eastern and central Precambrian masses <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> rocks are found. The <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous_Limestone" title="Carboniferous Limestone">Carboniferous Limestone</a> occupies a broad area south of <a href="/wiki/Lligwy_Bay" title="Lligwy Bay">Lligwy Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pentraeth" title="Pentraeth">Pentraeth</a>, and sends a narrow spur in a south-westerly direction by <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a> to <a href="/wiki/Malltraeth" title="Malltraeth">Malltraeth Sands</a>. The limestone is underlain on the north-west by a red basement conglomerate and yellow <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> (sometimes considered to be of <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a> age). Limestone occurs again on the north coast around <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanfihangel_Tre%27r_Beirdd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Llanfihangel Tre&#39;r Beirdd (page does not exist)">Llanfihangel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Llangoed" title="Llangoed">Llangoed</a>; and in the south-west round Llanidan near the <a href="/wiki/Menai_Strait" title="Menai Strait">Menai Strait</a>. <a href="/wiki/Puffin_Island,_Anglesey" class="mw-redirect" title="Puffin Island, Anglesey">Puffin Island</a> is made of carboniferous limestone. <a href="/wiki/Malltraeth" title="Malltraeth">Malltraeth marsh</a> is occupied by Coal Measures, and a small patch of the same formation appears near Tal-y-foel Ferry on the <a href="/wiki/Menai_Strait" title="Menai Strait">Menai Strait</a>. A patch of rhyolitic/<a href="/wiki/Felsite" title="Felsite">felsitic</a> rocks form <a href="/wiki/Parys_Mountain" title="Parys Mountain">Parys Mountain</a>, where copper and iron ochre have been worked. <a href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Serpentine group">Serpentine</a> (Mona Marble) is found near <a href="/wiki/Llanfair-yn-Neubwll" title="Llanfair-yn-Neubwll">Llanfair-yn-Neubwll</a> and upon the opposite shore in <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm191117\xe2\x80\x9318-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the name <i><a href="/wiki/GeoM%C3%B4n" title="GeoM\xc3\xb4n">GeoM\xc3\xb4n</a></i>, and in recognition of its extraordinary geological heritage, the island gained membership of the <a href="/wiki/European_Geoparks_Network" title="European Geoparks Network">European Geoparks Network</a> in spring 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Global_Network_of_National_Geoparks" class="mw-redirect" href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Anglesey is linked from <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a> to the mainland by the <a href="/wiki/Britannia_Bridge" title="Britannia Bridge">Britannia Bridge</a>, which carries the <a href="/wiki/A55_road" title="A55 road">A55</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Menai_Suspension_Bridge" title="Menai Suspension Bridge">Menai Suspension Bridge</a>, which carries the <a href="/wiki/A5_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A5 road (Great Britain)">A5</a>. The <a href="/wiki/A5025_road" title="A5025 road">A5025</a> round the northern edge of Anglesey and the <a href="/wiki/A4080_road" title="A4080 road">A4080</a> round the southern edge form a ring round the island.\n</p><p>There are six railway stations in Anglesey: <a href="/wiki/Holyhead_railway_station" title="Holyhead railway station">Holyhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valley_railway_station" title="Valley railway station">Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhosneigr_railway_station" title="Rhosneigr railway station">Rhosneigr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ty_Croes_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Ty Croes railway station">Ty Croes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bodorgan_railway_station" title="Bodorgan railway station">Bodorgan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Llanfairpwll_railway_station" title="Llanfairpwll railway station">Llanfairpwll</a>. All are on the <a href="/wiki/North_Wales_Coast_Line" title="North Wales Coast Line">North Wales Coast Line</a> and services are operated by <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Trains" title="Virgin Trains">Virgin Trains</a> to <a href="/wiki/Euston_railway_station" title="Euston railway station">London Euston</a>, and by <a href="/wiki/Transport_for_Wales_Rail" class="mw-redirect" title="Transport for Wales Rail">Transport for Wales Rail</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chester_railway_station" title="Chester railway station">Chester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Piccadilly_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Piccadilly railway station">Manchester Piccadilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_New_Street_railway_station" title="Birmingham New Street railway station">Birmingham New Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Central_railway_station" title="Cardiff Central railway station">Cardiff Central</a>. Historically the island was also served by the <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_Central_Railway" title="Anglesey Central Railway">Anglesey Central Railway</a> which ran from <a href="/wiki/Gaerwen" title="Gaerwen">Gaerwen</a> to <a href="/wiki/Amlwch" title="Amlwch">Amlwch</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Wharf_Bay_branch_line" title="Red Wharf Bay branch line">Red Wharf Bay branch line</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Holland_Arms_railway_station" title="Holland Arms railway station">Holland Arms railway station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Wharf_Bay" title="Red Wharf Bay">Red Wharf Bay</a>.\n</p><p>By air, <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_Airport" title="Anglesey Airport">Anglesey Airport</a> has a twice-daily scheduled service to <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Airport" title="Cardiff Airport">Cardiff Airport</a> where connections worldwide can be made.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Holyhead" title="Port of Holyhead">port of Holyhead</a> handles more than 2 million passengers each year. <a href="/wiki/Stena_Line" title="Stena Line">Stena Line</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Ferries" title="Irish Ferries">Irish Ferries</a> sail to <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAn_Laoghaire" title="D\xc3\xban Laoghaire">D\xc3\xban Laoghaire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, forming the principal link for surface <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> from central and northern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_and_leisure">Sport and leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sport and leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Anglesey is independently represented in the <a href="/wiki/Island_Games" title="Island Games">Island Games</a> (under its Welsh name <i>Ynys M\xc3\xb4n</i>). The team finished joint 17th in the <a href="/wiki/2009_Island_Games" title="2009 Island Games">2009 Games</a> hosted by <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x85land">\xc3\x85land</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> winning medals in <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shooting" title="Shooting">shooting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Anglesey made an unsuccessful bid to host the 2009 games, led by Ynys M\xc3\xb4n MP <a href="/wiki/Albert_Owen" title="Albert Owen">Albert Owen</a>. The island expected to benefit from more than \xc2\xa33m of spending if it had hosted the event. However, Anglesey currently lacks two facilities necessary for a successful bid, a six-lane competition swimming pool and an athletics track.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several precursors to the modern football codes were highly popular in Anglesey. They had few rules, and were quite violent. Rhys Cox, at the turn of the 18th century, described a game in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llandrygan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Llandrygan (page does not exist)">Llandrygan</a> as ending with "&#91;n&#93;umbers of players...  left here and there on the road, some having limbs broken in the struggle, others severely injured, and some carried on biers to be buried in the churchyard nearest to where they had been mortally injured." William Bulkeley, in his April 1734 diary, records that the violence of such games left no hard feelings, with both sides parting "as good friends as they came, after they had spent half an hour together cherishing their spirits with a cup of ale... having finished Easter Holydays innocently and merrily."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEETBRS2005b_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEETBRS2005b-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_football">Association football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Association football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Football arrived on the island in the 1870s. It was initially met with resistance, given its perceived (by the islanders) associations with drunkenness and rowdiness, and the lower classes. One critic dismissed it as "un-Christian practice". The Anglesey League, comprising teams from <a href="/wiki/Amlwch" title="Amlwch">Amlwch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beaumaris" title="Beaumaris">Beaumaris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menai_Bridge" title="Menai Bridge">Menai Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Llandegfan" title="Llandegfan">Llandegfan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a>, was, however, formed in the 1895\xe2\x80\x9396 football season.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPretty2005_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPretty2005-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_football_team" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglesey football team">Ynys M\xc3\xb4n</a> football team represents the island of Anglesey at the biannual Island Games, winning gold in 1999.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Llangefni_Town_F.C." title="Llangefni Town F.C.">Llangefni Town</a> are reigning <a href="/wiki/Cymru_Alliance" title="Cymru Alliance">Cymru Alliance</a> champions, only failing promotion due to the restructuring of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Premier_League" title="Welsh Premier League">Welsh Premier League</a>. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Holyhead_Hotspur" class="mw-redirect" title="Holyhead Hotspur">Holyhead Hotspur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Llanfairpwll_F.C." title="Llanfairpwll F.C.">Llanfairpwll</a> were relegated despite finishing outside the usual relegation zone at the end of the 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 season.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby_Union">Rugby Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Rugby Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Llangefni_RFC" title="Llangefni RFC">Llangefni RFC</a> is the island\'s highest competing team in the <a href="/wiki/WRU_Division_One_North" title="WRU Division One North">WRU Division One North</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Llangoed" title="Llangoed">Llangoed</a> hosts an annual <a href="/wiki/Rugby_sevens" title="Rugby sevens">rugby sevens</a> contest. Touring sides have included Manhattan RFC.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglesey_Hunt">Anglesey Hunt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Anglesey Hunt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Anglesey Hunt, formed in 1757, was the second oldest <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">fox hunting</a> association in Wales (the oldest being the Tivyside Hunt in Cardiganshire).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEETBRS2005a_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEETBRS2005a-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Every September the Anglesey Festival of Running takes place. There is a marathon, half-marathon, 10&#160;km and 5&#160;km race, as well as children\'s contests. Their slogan is <i>Run the Island</i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorsport">Motorsport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Motorsport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Anglesey_Circuit" title="Anglesey Circuit">Anglesey Circuit</a> (Welsh: Trac M\xc3\xb4n) is a fully licensed MSA and ACU championship racing circuit, it opened in 1997.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cricket">Cricket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglesey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Cricket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Beaumaris Cricket Club was formed in 1858. Clubs at <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amlwch" title="Amlwch">Amlwch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Llangefni" title="Llangefni">Llangefni</a> were formed within the following decade, but it was not until the 1880s that the sport became popular outside the upper classes. <a href="/wiki/Bodedern" title="Bodedern">Bodedern</a> Cricket Club was formed in 1947.<sup
