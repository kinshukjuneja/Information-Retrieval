Salvage may encompass towing, re-floating a vessel, or effecting repairs to a ship. Today, protecting the coastal environment from spillage of oil or other contaminants is a high priority. Before the invention of radio, salvage services would be given to a stricken vessel by any ship that happened to be passing by. Now days, most salvage is carried out by specialist salvage firms with dedicated crew and equipment.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Law_of_salvage" title="Law of salvage">The legal significance of salvage</a> is that a successful salvor is entitled to a reward, which is a proportion of the total value of the ship and its cargo. The amount of the award is determined subsequently at a "hearing on the merits" by a maritime court in accordance with Articles 13 and 14 of the <a href="/wiki/International_Convention_on_Salvage" title="International Convention on Salvage">International Salvage Convention</a> of 1989.  The common law concept of  salvage was established by the English Admiralty Court, and is defined as "a voluntary successful service provided in order to save maritime property in danger at sea, entitling the salvor to a reward"; and this definition has been further refined by the 1989 Convention.  \n</p><p>Originally, a "successful" salvage was one where at least some of the ship or cargo was saved, otherwise the principle of "No Cure, No Pay" meant that the salvor would get nothing.  In the 1970s, a number of marine casualties of single-skin-hull tankers led to serious oil spills.  Such casualties were unattractive to salvors, so the <a href="/wiki/Lloyd%27s_Open_Form" title="Lloyd&#39;s Open Form">Lloyd\'s Open Form (LOF)</a> made provision that a salvor who acts to try to prevent environmental damage will be paid, even if unsuccessful.  This <a href="/wiki/Lloyd%27s_of_London" title="Lloyd&#39;s of London">Lloyd\'s</a> initiative proved so advantageous that it was incorporated into the 1989 Convention.\n</p><p>All vessels have an international duty to give reasonable assistance to other ships in distress in order to save life, but there is no obligation to try to salve the vessel.  Any offer of salvage assistance may be refused; but if it is accepted a contract automatically arises to give the successful salvor the right to a reward under the 1989 Convention.  Typically, the ship and the salvor will sign up to an LOF agreement so that the terms of salvage are clear.  Since 2000, it has become standard to append a <a href="/wiki/Protection_and_indemnity_insurance" title="Protection and indemnity insurance">SCOPIC</a> ("Special Compensation - P&amp;I Clubs") clause to the LOF, so as to circumvent the limitations of the "Special Compensation" provisions of the 1989 Convention (pursuant to the case of the <i>Nagasaki Spirit</i>). <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. class="mw-redirect" title="Qin Shihuang">Qin Shihuang</a> (r. 221\xe2\x80\x93210 BC) assembled an expedition consisting of a thousand people for the salvage of the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Tripod_Cauldrons" title="Nine Tripod Cauldrons">Nine Tripod Cauldrons</a>. The tripods were considered important artifacts, Chinese legends credit a Xia dynasty emperor with their construction. The tripods were lost in Sishui River in present-day <a href="/wiki/Anhui_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Anhui Province">Anhui Province</a>. The salvage attempt was ultimately unsuccessful. Carvings in Han Dynasty tombs depict the salvage attempt.<sup id="cite_ref-Li_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the 11th century AD, a successful underwater salvage operation in <a href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Song Dynasty">Song China</a> (960\xe2\x80\x931279) would employ the use of <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Chinese understood the concept of buoyancy by at least the 3rd century AD; the short-lived child prodigy <a href="/wiki/Cao_Chong" title="Cao Chong">Cao Chong</a> (196\xe2\x80\x93208) weighed a large <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephant</a> by placing it on a boat in a pond and measuring the rise of the water level, then matching this weight with a boat loaded with numerous heavy objects which could be measured separately.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Between 1064 and 1067, the Puchin Bridge near Puchow, a floating bridge built some 350 years earlier across the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a>, was destroyed in a flood.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This bridge was made of boats secured by iron chains which were attached to eight different <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> statues located on each river bank, cast in the shape of recumbent <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">oxen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The flood pulled the oxen from the sandy banks into the river, where they sunk to the bottom; after this loss, the local officials issued a proclamation for submission of ideas on how to recover the statues.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The plan of the Buddhist monk Huaibing was accepted, which is described in the text <i>Liang Chi Man Chih</i> of 1192, "he used two huge boats filled with earth, cables from them being made fast to the oxen in the river bed (by the drivers). Hooks and a huge counterweight lever were also used. Then the earth in the boats was gradually taken away so that the boats floated much higher and the oxen were lifted off the river bottom."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_Period" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern Period">Early Modern Period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diving_bell" title="Diving bell">diving bells</a> were often used for salvage work. In 1658, Albrecht von Treileben was contracted by King <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustavus Adolphus">Gustavus Adolphus</a> of Sweden to salvage the warship <a href="/wiki/Vasa_(ship)" title="Vasa (ship)"><i>Vasa</i></a>, which sank in Stockholm harbor on its maiden voyage in 1628. Between 1663-1665 von Treileben\'s divers were successful in raising most of the cannon, working from a diving bell.<sup id="cite_ref-Tanuel_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tanuel-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1687, <a href="/wiki/William_Phips" title="William Phips">Sir William Phipps</a> used an inverted container to recover \xc2\xa3200,000-worth of treasure from a Spanish ship sunk off the coast of <a href="/wiki/San_Domingo" class="mw-redirect" title="San Domingo">San Domingo</a>.\n</p><p>The era of modern salvage operations was inaugurated with the development of the first surface supplied diving helmets by the inventors, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Anthony_Deane" title="Charles Anthony Deane">Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Deane_(inventor)" title="John Deane (inventor)">John Deane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Siebe" title="Augustus Siebe">Augustus Siebe</a>, in the 1830s. <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_George_(1756)" title="HMS Royal George (1756)"><i>Royal George</i></a>, a 100-gun first-rate ship of the line of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, sank undergoing routine maintenance work in 1782, and the Deane brothers were commissioned to perform salvage work on the wreck. Using their new air-pumped <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">diving helmets</a>, they managed to recover about two dozen <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannons</a>.\n</p><p>Following on from this success, Colonel of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Engineers" title="Royal Engineers">Royal Engineers</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Pasley" title="Charles Pasley">Charles Pasley</a> commenced the first large scale salvage operation in 1839. His plan was to break up the wreck of <i>Royal George</i> with gunpowder charges and then salvage as much as possible using divers.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loss_of_the_Royal_George,_at_Spithead_(1871).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Loss_of_the_Royal_George%2C_at_Spithead_%281871%29.jpg/220px-Loss_of_the_Royal_George%2C_at_Spithead_%281871%29.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Loss_of_the_Royal_George%2C_at_Spithead_%281871%29.jpg/330px-Loss_of_the_Royal_George%2C_at_Spithead_%281871%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Loss_of_the_Royal_George%2C_at_Spithead_%281871%29.jpg/440px-Loss_of_the_Royal_George%2C_at_Spithead_%281871%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="582" data-file-height="452" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loss_of_the_Royal_George,_at_Spithead_(1871).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sinking of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_George_(1756)" title="HMS Royal George (1756)"><i>Royal George</i></a></div></div></div>\n<p>Pasley\'s diving salvage operation set many diving milestones, including the first recorded use of the <a href="/wiki/Buddy_system" title="Buddy system">buddy system</a> in diving, when he ordered that his divers operate in pairs. In addition, the first emergency swimming ascent was made by a diver after his air line became tangled and he had to cut it free. A less fortunate milestone was the first medical account of a <a href="/wiki/Barotrauma#Diving_barotrauma" title="Barotrauma">diver squeeze</a> suffered by a Private Williams: the early diving helmets used had no <a href="/wiki/Non-return_valve" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-return valve">non-return valves</a>; this meant that if a hose became severed, the high-pressure air around the diver\'s head rapidly evacuated the helmet causing tremendous negative pressure that caused extreme and sometimes life-threatening effects. At the <a href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">British Association for the Advancement of Science</a> meeting in 1842, <a href="/wiki/John_Richardson_(naturalist)" title="John Richardson (naturalist)">Sir John Richardson</a> described the <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">diving apparatus</a> and treatment of diver Roderick Cameron following an injury that occurred on 14 October 1841 during the salvage operations.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pasley recovered 12 more guns in 1839, 11 more in 1840, and 6 in 1841. In 1842 he recovered only one iron 12-pounder because he ordered the divers to concentrate on removing the hull timbers rather than search for guns. Other items recovered, in 1840, included the <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_doctor" title="Ship&#39;s doctor">surgeon</a>\'s brass instruments, <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> garments of <a href="/wiki/Satin" title="Satin">satin</a> weave \'of which the silk was perfect\', and pieces of leather; but no woollen clothing.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> By 1843 the whole of the keel and the bottom timbers had been raised and the site was declared clear.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_salvage">Classification of salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification of salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barge_Salvage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d8/Barge_Salvage.jpg/220px-Barge_Salvage.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d8/Barge_Salvage.jpg/330px-Barge_Salvage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d8/Barge_Salvage.jpg/440px-Barge_Salvage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="970" data-file-height="691" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Barge_Salvage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Refloating of a 200-foot barge at the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Tampa" class="mw-redirect" title="Port of Tampa">port of Tampa</a> <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>"Salvors" are seamen and engineers who carry out salvage to vessels that they do not own, and who are not members of the vessel\'s original crew. When salving large ships, they may use <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">cranes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floating_dry_dock" class="mw-redirect" title="Floating dry dock">floating dry docks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">divers</a> to lift and repair submerged or grounded ships, preparing them to be towed by a <a href="/wiki/Tugboat" title="Tugboat">tugboat</a>. The goal of the salvage may be to repair the vessel at a <a href="/wiki/Harbour" class="mw-redirect" title="Harbour">harbour</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dry_dock" title="Dry dock">dry dock</a>, or to clear a channel for navigation. Salvage operations may also aim to prevent <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> or damage to the <a href="/wiki/Marine_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine environment">marine environment</a>. Additionally, the vessel or valuable parts of the vessel or its cargo may be recovered for resale, or for scrap.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Offshore_salvage">Offshore salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Offshore salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The refloating of ships stranded or sunk in exposed waters is called offshore salvage. In this type of salvage, vessels are exposed to waves, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents</a> and weather and are the most vulnerable and difficult to work on. They also tend to deteriorate more rapidly than such vessels in protected <a href="/wiki/Harbors" class="mw-redirect" title="Harbors">harbors</a>. Offshore salvage may provide only a short window of opportunity for the salvage team due to unusually <a href="/wiki/High_tide" class="mw-redirect" title="High tide">high tide</a> or inclement weather for instance. The work window may not come around again for as long as weeks or months and in the interim, the vessel will continue to deteriorate. As a result, it is often imperative to work quickly. Typically, offshore salvage is conducted from pre-outfitted <a href="/wiki/Salvage_tug" title="Salvage tug">salvage tugs</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Tugboat" title="Tugboat">tugboats</a>. In addition, portable <a href="/wiki/Saturation_diving" title="Saturation diving">diving</a> facilities may be transported by <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> or small boat to the work area. From a tactical point of view, working in unprotected waters is less hospitable for <a href="/wiki/Crane_vessel" title="Crane vessel">floating cranes</a>, construction tenders, <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">dredges</a> and equipment <a href="/wiki/Barges" class="mw-redirect" title="Barges">barges</a>. Plus, it is often difficult to depend upon a stable workforce (<a href="/wiki/Welders" class="mw-redirect" title="Welders">welders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carpentry" title="Carpentry">carpenters</a>, etc.) as all personnel must be present on site for the duration\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harbour_salvage">Harbour salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Harbour salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg/220px-Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg" width="220" height="269" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg/330px-Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg/440px-Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1840" data-file-height="2252" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amsterdam_canal_dinghy_salvage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A house boat dinghy being salvaged in an Amsterdam canal</div></div></div>\n<p>The term harbour salvage refers to the salvage of vessels stranded or sunk in sheltered waters. Such vessels are not normally subject to the same deterioration caused by marine and weather conditions as offshore salvage vessels are. In addition, unless the vessel to be salvaged is obstructing navigation, then there is no need to work as swiftly as in offshore salvage. Also, harbour pre-salvage survey and planning stages tend to be less time consuming and environmentally dependent. It is also easier to gain access to local labour resources and heavy equipment such as floating cranes and barges.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargo_and_equipment_salvage">Cargo and equipment salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cargo and equipment salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Saving the cargo and equipment aboard a vessel may be of higher priority than saving the vessel itself. The cargo may pose an environmental hazard or may include expensive materials such as machinery or precious metals.  In this form of salvage, the main focus is on the rapid removal of goods and may include deliberate dissection, disassembly or destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hull</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wreck_removal">Wreck removal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wreck removal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Wreck removal focuses on the removal of hazardous or unsightly <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">wrecks</a> that have little or no salvage value. Because the objectives here are not to save the vessel, the wrecks are usually refloated or removed by the cheapest and most practical method possible. In many cases, <a href="/wiki/Dangerous_goods" title="Dangerous goods">hazardous materials</a> must be removed prior to disposing of the wreck.  The most common techniques used in wreck removal are cutting the hull into easily handled sections or refloating the vessel and <a href="/wiki/Scuttling" title="Scuttling">scuttling</a> it in deeper waters.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Afloat_salvage">Afloat salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Afloat salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The salvage of a vessel that is damaged but still afloat is called afloat salvage. This type of salvage is mostly unobtrusive and involves primarily damage control work such as hull welding, stabilization (rebalancing <a href="/wiki/Ballast_tank" title="Ballast tank">ballast tanks</a> and shifting cargo) and structural bracing. In some cases, the vessel can remain underway with little disruption to its original purpose and crew.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clearance_salvage">Clearance salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Clearance salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Clearance salvage is the coordinated removal or salvage of numerous vessels in a harbor or waterway. It typically follows a catastrophic event such as a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Casus_belli" title="Casus belli">act of war</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">Pearl Harbor</a>). There may be multiple vessel obstructions with varying degrees of damage due to collision, fire or explosions.<sup id="cite_ref-Bartholomew2008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bartholomew2008-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>Tools used in marine salvage</b>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" style="width: id="Salvage_urgency_and_cost_considerations">Salvage urgency and cost considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Salvage urgency and cost considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Salvage projects may vary with respect to urgency and cost considerations. When the vessel to be returned to service is commercial, the salvage operation is typically driven by its commercial value and impact on <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">navigational waterways</a>.  Military vessels on the other hand are often salvaged at any cost\xe2\x80\x94even to exceed their operational value\xe2\x80\x94because of national prestige and anti-"abandonment" policies. Another consideration may be loss of revenue and service or the cost of the space the vessel occupies.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_salvage">Types of salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Types of salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_Rose_(1971)_ship_damage.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Typhoon_Rose_%281971%29_ship_damage.JPG/220px-Typhoon_Rose_%281971%29_ship_damage.JPG" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Typhoon_Rose_%281971%29_ship_damage.JPG/330px-Typhoon_Rose_%281971%29_ship_damage.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Typhoon_Rose_%281971%29_ship_damage.JPG/440px-Typhoon_Rose_%281971%29_ship_damage.JPG 2x" data-file-width="485" data-file-height="352" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_Rose_(1971)_ship_damage.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/USS_Regulus_(AF-57)" title="USS Regulus (AF-57)">USS <i>Regulus</i></a> hard aground in 1971 due to a typhoon: after three weeks of effort, Naval salvors deemed it unsalvageable.</div></div></div>\n<p>There are four types of salvage:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contract_salvage">Contract salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Contract salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In contract salvage the owner of the property and salvor enter into a salvage contract prior to the commencement of salvage operations and the amount that the salvor is paid is determined by the contract.  This can be a fixed amount, based on a "time and materials" basis, or any other terms that both parties agree to.  The contract may also state that payment is only due if the salvage operation is successful (a.k.a. "No Cure, No Pay"),<sup id="cite_ref-Gores1971_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gores1971-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> or that payment is due even if the operation is not successful. By far the commonest single form of salvage contract internationally is Lloyd\'s Standard Form of Salvage Agreement (2011), an English law arbitration agreement administered by the Council of Lloyd\'s, London.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_salvage">Pure salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pure salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the United States, in pure salvage (also called "merit salvage"), there is no contract between the owner of the goods and the salvor. The relationship is one which is implied by law. The salvor of property under pure salvage must bring his claim for salvage in a court which has jurisdiction, and this will award salvage <a href="/wiki/Quantum_meruit" title="Quantum meruit">based upon the "merit" of the service</a> and the value of the salvaged property.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Pure salvage claims are divided into "high-order" and "low-order" salvage. In high-order salvage, the salvor exposes himself and his crew to the risk of injury and loss or damage to his equipment in order to salvage the property that is in peril. Examples of high-order salvage are boarding a sinking ship in heavy weather, boarding a ship which is on fire, raising a ship, plane, or other sunken property, or towing a ship which is in the surf away from the shore. Low-order salvage occurs where the salvor is exposed to little or no personal risk. Examples of low-order salvage include towing another vessel in calm seas, supplying a vessel with fuel, or pulling a vessel off a sand bar. Salvors performing high order salvage receive substantially greater salvage award than those performing low order salvage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In order for a claim to be awarded three requirements must be met: The property must be in peril, the services must be rendered voluntarily (no duty to act), and finally the salvage must be successful in whole or in part.\n</p><p>There are several factors that would be considered by a court in establishing the amount of the salvor\xe2\x80\x99s award. Some of these include the difficulty of the operation, the risk involved to the salvor, the value of the property saved, the degree of danger to which the property was exposed, and the potential environmental impacts. It would be a rare case in which the salvage award would be greater than 50 percent of the value of the property salvaged. More commonly, salvage awards amount to 10 percent to 25 percent of the value of the property.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Private boat owners, to protect themselves from salvage laws in the event of a rescue, would be wise to clarify with their rescuer if the operation is to be considered salvage, or simply assistance towing.  If this is not done, the boat owner may be shocked to discover that the rescuer may be eligible for a substantial salvage award, and a lien may be placed on the vessel if it is not paid.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_salvage">Naval salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Naval salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Grapple_(ARS-53).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/USS_Grapple_%28ARS-53%29.jpg/220px-USS_Grapple_%28ARS-53%29.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/USS_Grapple_%28ARS-53%29.jpg/330px-USS_Grapple_%28ARS-53%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/USS_Grapple_%28ARS-53%29.jpg/440px-USS_Grapple_%28ARS-53%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2664" data-file-height="1990" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Grapple_(ARS-53).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/USNS_Grapple_(T-ARS-53)" title="USNS Grapple (T-ARS-53)">USNS&#160;<i>Grapple</i></a>, an example of a modern naval rescue salvage ship</div></div></div>\n<p>Several navies have Rescue Salvage vessels which are to support their fleet and to come to the aid of vessels in distress. In addition they may have Deep Salvage Units.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A DSU (salvage) is an attached unit to the US Navy .<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Technical diving marine salvage units which can operate in depths unworkable by civilian divers,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and which requires saturation diving or trimix (breathing gas) diving techniques. This type of unit is at times formed by a commercial diving company contracting to government via the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Exchange" title="Baltic Exchange">Baltic Exchange</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plunder">Plunder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Plunder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When certain vessels are lost in an unknown area, a potential salvor might discover and plunder the wreck without knowledge of the wreck\'s owner. Salvaging a foreign navy\'s vessel is against international law.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligence_salvage">Intelligence salvage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Intelligence salvage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At the height of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> the United States raised a portion of <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-129_(1960)" title="Soviet submarine K-129 (1960)">Soviet submarine&#160;<i>K-129</i></a> in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Pacific_Ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Pacific Ocean">Western Pacific Ocean</a>. The <a href="/wiki/CIA" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA">CIA</a>, who conducted the salvage under the guise of "mining the seafloor for manganese nodules" with a commercial vessel, spent over $800 million (1974 dollars) on the clandestine operation now known as <a href="/wiki/Project_Azorian" title="Project Azorian">Project Azorian</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ship_salvage_and_the_law">Ship salvage and the law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ship salvage and the law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Law_of_salvage" title="Law of salvage">Law of salvage</a></div>\n<p>Salvage law has as a basis that a salvor should be rewarded for risking his or her life and property to rescue the property of another from peril. Salvage law is in some ways similar to the wartime law of <a href="/wiki/Prize_(law)" title="Prize (law)">prize</a>, the capture, condemnation and sale of a vessel and its cargo as a spoil of war, insofar as both compensate the salvor/captors for risking life and property.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>   The two areas of law may dovetail.  For instance, a vessel taken as a prize, then recaptured by friendly forces on its way to the prize adjudication, is not deemed a prize of the rescuers (title merely reverts to the original owner).  But the rescuing vessel is entitled to a claim for salvage.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  Likewise a vessel found badly damaged, abandoned and adrift after enemy fire disabled her does not become a prize of a rescuing friendly vessel, but the rescuers may claim salvage.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A vessel is considered in peril if it is in danger or could become in danger. Examples of a vessel in peril are when it is  aground or in danger of going aground. Prior to a salvage attempt the salvor receives permission from the owner or the master to assist the  vessel. If the vessel is abandoned no permission is needed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The amount of the award depends on, in part, the value of the salved vessel, the degree of risk involved and the degree of peril the vessel was in. Legal disputes do arise from the claiming of salvage rights. To reduce the amount of a claim after an accident, boat owners or skippers often remain on board and in command of the vessel; they do everything possible to minimise further loss and seek to minimize the degree of risk the vessel is in. If another vessel offers a tow and the master or owner negotiates an hourly rate before accepting then salvage does not apply.\n</p><p>Some maritime rescue organisations, such as Britain\'s <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution" title="Royal National Lifeboat Institution">Royal National Lifeboat Institution</a>, do not insist the crews of their <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboats</a> renounce their right to claim compensation for salvage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, but should they choose to make a claim, they must pay for the use of the lifeboat and make good any damage suffered by her. Claims for salvage by lifeboat crews are rare.\n<a href="/wiki/Jetsam" class="mw-redirect" title="Jetsam">Jetsam</a> are goods that were thrown off a ship, which was in danger, to save the ship. <a href="/wiki/Flotsam" class="mw-redirect" title="Flotsam">Flotsam</a> are goods that floated off the ship while it was in danger or when it sank. Ligan or lagan are goods left in the sea on the wreck or tied to a <a href="/wiki/Buoy" title="Buoy">buoy</a> so that they can be recovered later by the owners. Derelict is abandoned <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">vessels</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a>.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> under the Merchant Shipping Act 1995, jetsam, flotsam, lagan and all other cargo and wreckage remain the property of their original owners. Anyone, including recreational divers and beachcombers, removing those goods must inform the <a href="/wiki/Receiver_of_Wreck" title="Receiver of Wreck">Receiver of Wreck</a> to avoid the accusation of theft. As the leisure activity of <a href="/wiki/Wreck_diving" title="Wreck diving">wreck diving</a> is common, there are laws to <a href="/wiki/Protection_of_Wrecks_Act_1973" title="Protection of Wrecks Act 1973">protect historic wrecks</a> of archaeological importance and the <a href="/wiki/Protection_of_Military_Remains_Act_1986" title="Protection of Military Remains Act 1986">Protection of Military Remains Act 1986</a> protects ships and aircraft that are the last resting place of the remains of members of the armed forces.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1910_Brussels_Convention_for_the_Unification_of_Certain_Rules_with_Respect_to_Assistance_and_Salvage_at_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="1910 Brussels Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules with Respect to Assistance and Salvage at Sea">1910 Brussels Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules with Respect to Assistance and Salvage at Sea</a> reflects the traditional legal principles of marine salvage.  The <a href="/wiki/1989_International_Convention_on_Salvage" class="mw-redirect" title="1989 International Convention on Salvage">1989 International Convention on Salvage</a> incorporated the essential provisions of the 1910 Convention, and added some new provisions besides. The 1989 Salvage Convention entered force on 14 July 1996 with nearly twenty parties in agreement. For states which are parties to both conventions, the 1989 Convention takes precedence over the 1910 one where their provisions are mutually incompatible.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_salvage_operations">Notable salvage operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_salvage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notable salvage operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg/220px-MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg/330px-MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg/440px-MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="675" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MV_Blue_Marlin_carrying_USS_Cole.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>USS <i>Cole</i> being carried by <a href="/wiki/MV_Blue_Marlin" title="MV Blue Marlin">MV&#160;<i>Blue Marlin</i></a></div></div></div>\n<ul><li>The largest marine salvage operation on record was the raising of the German <a href="/wiki/High_Seas_Fleet" title="High Seas Fleet">High Seas Fleet</a> which was <a href="/wiki/Scuttling_of_the_German_fleet_in_Scapa_Flow" title="Scuttling of the German fleet in Scapa Flow">scuttled at Scapa Flow</a> in 1919.  Between 1922 and 1939, 45 of the 52 warships sunk, including six battleships, five battlecruisers, five cruisers and 32 destroyers were raised from the bottom of the flow at depths of up to 45 metres, primarily by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Cox" title="Ernest Cox">Cox &amp; Danks Ltd</a> &amp; Metal Industries Ltd, and broken up for scrap.</li>\n<li>The harbor clearance and ship recovery after the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>. <a href="/wiki/USS_California_(BB-44)" title="USS California (BB-44)">USS&#160;<i>California</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_West_Virginia_(BB-48)" title="USS West Virginia (BB-48)">USS&#160;<i>West Virginia</i></a>, resting on the bottom of Pearl Harbor on 7 December 1941, were refloated and repaired.<sup id="cite_ref-Madsen2003_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madsen2003-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> They were key participants in the Battle of Surigao Strait in October 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-Morison1963_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morison1963-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> 17th-century warship <a href="/wiki/Vasa_(ship)" title="Vasa (ship)"><i>Vasa</i></a> was raised in April 1961. She had lain on the bottom of <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> harbor since her <a href="/wiki/Capsizing" title="Capsizing">capsizing</a> on her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_voyage" title="Maiden voyage">maiden voyage</a> in 1628.</li>\n<li>The raising and subsequent conservation of <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Rose" title="Mary Rose">Mary Rose</a></i>, the flagship of the navy of <a href="/wiki/King_Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Henry VIII">King Henry VIII</a>, which sank in 1545 in the <a href="/wiki/Solent" title="Solent">Solent</a>, North of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>.  As with <i>Vasa</i>, the salvage of <i>Mary Rose</i> in 1982 was an operation of immense complexity and was a major achievement in marine archaeology.  The remains of the ship, together with recovered weapons, sailing equipment and crew\xe2\x80\x99s personal effects are now on display at <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Historic_Dockyard" title="Portsmouth Historic Dockyard">Portsmouth Historic Dockyard</a> and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Mary_Rose_Museum" title="Mary Rose Museum">Mary Rose Museum</a>.</li>\n<li>In 1943-44 a famous <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> salvage engineer, Capt. Humphrey</i> in stages underwater (reducing the weight drastically) to shallower water where it was then pumped out and re-floated and towed out. Some of the techniques developed by Roen for the salvage of <i>George M. Humphrey</i> established methods which became new standards for future salvages, where before many wrecks were considered too heavy and large to salvage.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In 1968 Shipwrecks Inc., headed by <a href="/wiki/E._Lee_Spence" title="E. Lee Spence">E. Lee Spence</a>, was granted South Carolina State Salvage License #1 to salvage the wreck of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> blockade runner <a href="/wiki/SS_Georgiana" title="SS Georgiana">SS&#160;<i>Georgiana</i></a> under that state\'s new underwater antiquities act, which had been drafted and passed at the instigation of Spence, who had discovered the wreck in 1965. Spence\'s work on the wreck was some of the very first underwater archaeology done in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Spence and his crew raised over 1,000,000 individual artifacts, conservatively valued at over $12,000,000. The artifacts fell into three basic groups: medicines, munitions and merchandise. They ranged from tiny brass sewing pins and glass buttons to heavy iron cannons and included such things as cannonballs; bullets; bottles; pottery; carved bone toothbrushes; pencils; match cases; and Wedgwood china.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In 1974 the U.S. <a href="/wiki/CIA" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA">CIA</a> attempted to recover the sunken Soviet Golf-class submarine <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-129_(1960)" title="Soviet submarine K-129 (1960)"><i>K-129</i></a> in the secret and expensive intelligence operation <a href="/wiki/Project_Azorian" title="Project Azorian">Project Azorian</a>. The attempt was only partially successful.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_de_Atocha#Modern_recovery_and_legal_battle" title="Nuestra Se\xc3\xb1ora de Atocha">Nuestra Se\xc3\xb1ora de Atocha</a></i> was discovered in 1985 with recovered gold and other artifacts worth an estimated $400 million USD.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Vrouw_Maria" title="Vrouw Maria">Vrouw Maria</a></i> was discovered in 1999, and after a protracted legal battle, plans for salvage are still in the discussion and planning stage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It is known to contain priceless works of art.</li>\n<li>The search for the wreckage and <a href="/wiki/Flight_data_recorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight data recorder">flight data recorders</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_African_Airways_Flight_295" title="South African Airways Flight 295">South African Airways Flight 295</a> is at 16,000 feet (4,900&#160;m) the deepest successful marine salvage operation to date.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 12 August 2000, the Russian <a href="/wiki/Oscar-class_submarine" title="Oscar-class submarine">Oscar-class&#x20;submarine</a> <a href="/wiki/Russian_submarine_Kursk_(K-141)" title="Russian submarine Kursk (K-141)"><i>Kursk</i></a> sank in the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a> following an internal explosion, leading to the death of 118 sailors and officers.
