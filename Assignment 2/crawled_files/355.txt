Today, it is still sometimes used poetically to refer to the island. The name for <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in the Celtic languages is related to Albion: <i><a href="/wiki/Alba" title="Alba">Alba</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>, <i>Albain</i> (genitive <i>Alban</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>, <i>Nalbin</i> in <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a> and <i>Alban</i> in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>. These names were later <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_of_names" title="Latinisation of names">Latinised</a> as <i>Albania</i> and <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">Anglicised</a> as <i>Albany</i>, which were once alternative names for Scotland.\n</p><p><i>New Albion</i> and <i>Albionoria</i> ("Albion of the North") were briefly suggested as <a href="/wiki/Name_of_Canada" the name "<a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-manly.nsw.gov.au_4-0" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Add_19391_19-20.png" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Add_19391_19-20.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Vatopedi" title="Vatopedi">Codex Vatopedinus</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Ptolemy\'s map</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, labelled "<span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbb\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\x90\xcf\x89\xce\xbd</span>" (<i>Alou\xc3\xad\xc5\x8dn</i>, "Albion") and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\xb8\xce\xbf\xcf\x85\xce\xb5\xcf\x81\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1</span> (<i>Iouern\xc3\xada</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a>"). c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1300</span></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a> name for the island, <a href="/wiki/Hellenization" title="Hellenization">Hellenised</a> as <i>Alb\xc3\xad\xc5\x8dn</i> (<i><span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbb\xce\xb2\xce\xaf\xcf\x89\xce\xbd</span></i>) and Latinised as <i>Albi\xc5\x8dn</i> (<a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a> <i>Albionis</i>), derives from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language#Nasal_stems" title="Proto-Celtic language">Proto-Celtic nasal stem</a> <i>*Albi\xcc\xafi\xc5\xab</i> (<a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">oblique</a> <i>*Albiion-</i>) and survived in <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> as <i>Albu</i> (<a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a> <i>Albann</i>). The name originally referred to Britain as a whole, but was later restricted to <a href="/wiki/Caledonia" title="Caledonia">Caledonia</a> (giving the modern <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> name for Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Alba" title="Alba">Alba</a>). The root <i>*albiio-</i> is also found in <a href="/wiki/Gaulish_language" title="Gaulish language">Gaulish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galatian_language" title="Galatian language">Galatian</a> <i>albio-</i> ("world") and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> <i>elfydd</i> (<i lang="owl" title="Old Welsh language text">elbid</i>, "earth, world, land, country, district"). It may be related to other European and Mediterranean <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">toponyms</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alpes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Liban" class="mw-redirect" title="Liban">Liban</a></i>. It has two possible etymologies. It may derive from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> root <i>*albho-</i>, meaning "white" (c.f. Latin <i>albus</i>). This is perhaps in reference to the white southern shores of the island, though Celtic linguist <a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xavier_Delamarre" class="extiw" title="fr:Xavier Delamarre">Xavier Delamarre</a> argued that it originally meant "the world above, the visible world", in opposition to "the world below", i.e., the underworld. Alternatively it may derive from the Proto-Indo-European root <i>*alb-</i>, meaning "hill".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attestation">Attestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Britain_(place_name)" title="Britain (place name)">Britain (place name)</a></div>\n<p>Judging from <a href="/wiki/Avienus" title="Avienus">Avienus</a>\'s <i>Ora Maritima</i> to which it is considered to have served as a source, the <i><a href="/wiki/Massaliote_Periplus" title="Massaliote Periplus">Massaliote Periplus</a></i> (originally written in the 6th century BC, translated by <a href="/wiki/Avienus" title="Avienus">Avienus</a> at the end of the 4th century), does not use the name <i>Britannia</i>; instead it speaks of <i>n\xc4\x93sos Iern\xc5\x8dn kai Albi\xc5\x8dn\xc5\x8dn</i> "the islands of the Iernians and the Albiones".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> (ca. 320 BC), as directly or indirectly quoted in the surviving excerpts of his works in later writers, speaks of <i>Albi\xc5\x8dn and Iern\xc4\x93</i> (Britain and Ireland). \xce\xa0\xcf\x81\xce\xb5\xcf\x84\xcf\x84\xce\xb1\xce\xbd\xce\xb9\xce\xba\xce\xae</span> (<i>n\xc4\x93sos Prettanik\xc4\x93</i>, "Prettanic island") is somewhat blurry, and appears to include anything there lies beyond the Gallic bounds<br />\nAn island which the western sea surrounds,<br />\nBy giants once possessed, now few remain<br />\nTo bar thy entrance, or obstruct thy reign.<br />\nTo reach that happy shore thy sails employ<br />\nThere fate decrees to raise a second Troy<br />\nAnd found an empire in thy royal line,<br />\nWhich time shall ne\'er destroy, nor bounds confine.\n</p>\n</div><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Geoffrey of Monmouth, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/History_of_the_Kings_of_Britain/Book_1#11" class="extiw" title="s:History of the Kings of Britain/Book 1">History of the Kings of Britain/Books 1, 11</a></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>After many adventures, Brutus and his fellow <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Trojans</a> escape from <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> and "set sail with a fair wind towards the promised island".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>"The island was then called Albion, and inhabited by none but a few giants. Notwithstanding this, the pleasant situation of the places, the plenty of rivers abounding with fish, and the engaging prospect of its woods, made Brutus and his company very desirous to fix their habitation in it." After dividing up the island between themselves "at last Brutus called the island after his own name Britain, and his companions Britons; for by these means he desired to perpetuate the memory of his name".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Geoffrey goes on to recount how the last of the giants are defeated, the largest one called <a href="/wiki/Gogmagog_(folklore)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gogmagog (folklore)">Go\xc3\xabmagot</a> is flung over a cliff by <a href="/wiki/Corineus" title="Corineus">Corineus</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Norman_Albina_story"><span id="Des_grantz_geanz"></span>Anglo-Norman Albina story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anglo-Norman Albina story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Later, in the 14th century, a more elaborate tale was developed, claiming that Albina and her sisters founded Albion and procreated there a race of giants.<sup id="cite_ref-brenau_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brenau-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The "Albina story" survives in several forms, including the  <a href="/wiki/Octosyllable" title="Octosyllable">octosyllabic</a> Anglo-Norman poem "Des grantz geanz" dating to 1300&#8212;1334<sup id="cite_ref-fisher_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fisher-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup>(<a href="/w/index.php?title=Georgine_Elizabeth_Brereton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Georgine Elizabeth Brereton (page does not exist)">Georgine Elizabeth Brereton</a> ed. 1937;<sup id="cite_ref-brereton_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brereton-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Also <a href="/wiki/Achille_Jubinal" title="Achille Jubinal">Jubinal</a> ed., "Des graunz Jaianz ki primes conquistrent Bretaingne" (1842)<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>) A prose English translation is given in <a href="/wiki/Richard_Barber" title="Richard Barber">Richard Barber</a>\'s anthology (1999).<sup id="cite_ref-barber_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barber-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> According to the poem, in the 3970th <a href="/wiki/Anno_Mundi" title="Anno Mundi">year of the creation of the world</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> a king of Greece married his thirty daughters into royalty, but the haughty brides colluded to eliminate their husbands so they would be subservient to no one.  The youngest would not be party to the crime and divulged the plot, so the other princesses were confined to an unsteerable rudderless ship and set adrift, and after three days reached an uninhabited land later to be known as "England". The eldest daughter Albina (<i lang="xno" title="Anglo-Norman language text">Albine</i>) was the first to set shore and lay claim to the land, naming it after herself. At first, the women gathered acorns and fruits, but once they learned to hunt and obtain meat, it aroused their lecherous desires. As no other humans inhabited the land, they mated with evil spirits called "\'<a href="/wiki/Incubus" title="Incubus">incubi</a>", and subsequently with the sons they begot, engendering a race of giants. These giants are evidenced by huge bones which are unearthed. Brutus arrived 260 years after Albina, 1136 before the birth of Christ, but by then there were only 24 giants left, due to inner strife.<sup id="cite_ref-barber_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barber-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> As with Geoffrey of Monmouth\'s version, Brutus\'s band subsequently overtake the land, defeating <a href="/wiki/Gogmagog_(giant)" title="Gogmagog (giant)">Gogmagog</a> in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-barber_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barber-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Manuscripts_and_forms">Manuscripts and forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Manuscripts and forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The octosyllabic poem appears as a prologue to 16 out of 26 manuscripts of the Short Version of the Anglo-Norman prose <i>Brut</i>, which derives from <a href="/wiki/Wace" title="Wace">Wace</a>. Octosyllabic is not the only form the Anglo-Norman <i>Des Grantz Geanz</i>, there are five forms, the others being: the <a href="/wiki/Alexandrine" title="Alexandrine">alexandrine</a>, prose, short verse, and short prose versions.<sup id="cite_ref-fisher_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fisher-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The Latin adaptation of the Albina story, <i>De Origine Gigantum</i>, appeared soon later, in the 1330s<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> It has been edited by Carey &amp; Crick (1995),<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and translated by Ruth Evans (1998).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Diocletian.27s_daughters"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Diocletian\'s_daughters">Diocletian\'s daughters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diocletian&#039;s daughters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A variant tale occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> prose <i>Brut</i> (Brie ed., <i>The Brut or the Chronicles of England</i> 1906\xe2\x80\x931908) of the 14th century, an English rendition of the Anglo-Norman <i>Brut</i> deriving from <a href="/wiki/Wace" title="Wace">Wace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brie_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brie-38">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In the Prolog of this chronicle, it was King "Dioclician" of "Surrey" (Syria<sup id="cite_ref-baswell_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baswell-40">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>), who had 33 daughters, the eldest being called "Albyne". The princesses are all banished to Albion after plotting to murder their husbands, where they couple with the local demons; their offspring became a race of giants. The chronicle asserts that during the voyage Albyne entrusted the fate of the sisters to "Appolyn," which was the god of their faith. The Syrian king who was her father sounds much like a Roman emperor,<sup id="cite_ref-baswell_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baswell-40">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> though <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> (3rd century) would be anachronistic, and Holinshed <sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> explains this as a bungling of the legend of <a href="/wiki/Danaus" title="Danaus">Danaus</a> and his fifty daughters who founded <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span
