bridge</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Wadebridge</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Ponswad</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> and town in north <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, England, United Kingdom. The town straddles the <a href="/wiki/River_Camel" title="River Camel">River Camel</a> 5 miles (8.0&#160;km) upstream from <a href="/wiki/Padstow" title="Padstow">Padstow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The permanent population was 6,222 in the census of 2001, increasing to 7,900 in the 2011 census.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There are two electoral wards in the town (East and West). Their total population is 8,272<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Originally known as <i>Wade</i>, it was a dangerous fording point across the river until a bridge was built here in the 15th century, after which the name changed to its present form. The bridge was strategically important during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> went there to take it. Since then, it has been widened twice and refurbished in 1991.\n</p><p>Wadebridge was served by a railway station between 1834 and 1967; part of the line now forms the <a href="/wiki/Camel_Trail" title="Camel Trail">Camel Trail</a>, a recreational route for walkers, cyclists and horse riders. The town used to be a road traffic bottleneck on the <a href="/wiki/A39_road" title="A39 road">A39 road</a> until it was bypassed in 1991, and the main shopping street, Molesworth Street, is now pedestrianised.\n</p><p>The town has a <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge_School" title="Wadebridge School">secondary school</a> where several notable sports-people were educated. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Show" title="Royal Cornwall Show">Royal Cornwall Show</a> is a three-day <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_show" title="Agricultural show">agricultural show</a> held at the nearby Royal Cornwall Showground every June.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toclevel-1 tocsection-15"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">10</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The initial settlement of Wade (the name of Wadebridge before the bridge was built)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> came about due to a ford in the River Camel (Camel probably meaning "crooked one"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>). The early crossing had a chapel on each side of the river, "King\'s" chapel on the north side and "St Michael\'s" on the south side. People would pray for a safe crossing at one of the chapels before wading across at low tide, once they had made it the other side they would give thanks to God in the other chapel. In 1312 a licence was granted for a market at Wade. Wade was part of the parish of St Breock and the river separated it from the neighbouring parish of Egloshayle.\n</p><p>At some time the ford was supplemented by a ferry<sup id="cite_ref-bridges_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bridges-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> until the Reverend Thomas Lovibond (the vicar of <a href="/wiki/Egloshayle" title="Egloshayle">Egloshayle</a>) became distressed at the number of humans and animals that died during the crossing of the River Camel so he planned the building of a bridge which was completed in 1468. Wade was now known as Wadebridge. When <a href="/wiki/John_Leland_(antiquary)" title="John Leland (antiquary)">John Leland</a> travelled through Cornwall in the early 16th century he wrote that the piers were resting on packs of wool.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Begun in 1468 and completed in 1485, the bridge was traditionally known as the "Bridge on Wool" because it was reputedly built on wool sacks. In fact, however, it has been proven to be founded directly on the underlying bedrock.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><br />\nThe bridge was a strategic position in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> as in 1646 <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> came with 500 <a href="/wiki/Dragoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragoons">Dragoons</a> and 1,000 horsemen to take the bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> When the bridge was first completed tolls were charged for its maintenance. In 1853 it was widened from 3 to 5 metres (9.8 to 16.4&#160;ft). A second widening took place in 1952<sup id="cite_ref-bridges_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bridges-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and then in 1963 it was again widened taking it to 12 metres (39&#160;ft). In 1994 the bridge underwent a refurbishment to change the stone in the pavement and to create a cycle track.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A serious outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Typhoid" class="mw-redirect" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a> in 1897 caused by contamination of drinking water led to Wadebridge having its own town council as decisive action had to be taken for proper water supplies and disposal of sewage effluent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_and_Wadebridge_Railway" title="Bodmin and Wadebridge Railway">Bodmin and Wadebridge Railway</a> from Wadebridge to <a href="/wiki/Wenfordbridge" title="Wenfordbridge">Wenfordbridge</a> with branch lines to <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a> and Ruthernbridge<sup id="cite_ref-Fairclough_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairclough-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> was built at a cost of \xc2\xa335,000 following a study commissioned in 1831 by local landowner <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Molesworth,_8th_Baronet" title="Sir William Molesworth, 8th Baronet">Sir William Molesworth</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pencarrow" title="Pencarrow">Pencarrow</a>. The line was intended to carry sand from the <a href="/wiki/Camel_Estuary" class="mw-redirect" title="Camel Estuary">Camel Estuary</a> to inland farms for use as <a href="/wiki/Fertiliser" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertiliser">fertiliser</a>. It was opened on 30 September 1834 with the locomotive <i>Camel</i> pulling a train load of 400 passengers (one of the first railways in Britain to carry passengers). When the company ordered its second locomotive it came with a name plate already affixed. It had been named the <i>Elephant</i> as the makers had failed to realise that the first engine had been named after the river and not an animal.<sup id="cite_ref-bandw_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bandw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The last passenger train left <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge_railway_station" title="Wadebridge railway station">Wadebridge railway station</a> in 1967 following railway cutbacks. The railway has been transformed into the <a href="/wiki/Camel_Trail" title="Camel Trail">Camel Trail</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_and_Wenford_Railway" title="Bodmin and Wenford Railway">Bodmin and Wenford Railway</a> heritage railway runs on part of the route.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quays">Quays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Quays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Wadebridge was the highest navigable point on the <a href="/wiki/River_Camel" title="River Camel">River Camel</a> providing the main trade route before the building of the railway, and coasters would bring goods from <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> and coal from <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>. Timber was also imported from the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ncr_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncr-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> while stone from inland was sent to destination throughout England. The first locomotives used on the railway were also imported through the quay, being manufactured by Neath Abbey Ironworks,<sup id="cite_ref-bandw_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bandw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and the railway initially linked with river traffic well, having been designed to distribute sand from the river to the local farms. This commodity, brought up from <a href="/wiki/Padstow" title="Padstow">Padstow</a> in barges, had previously been taken as far as <a href="/wiki/Michaelstow" title="Michaelstow">Michaelstow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blisland" title="Blisland">Blisland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-camel_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-camel-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1880 there were quays on both sides of the river below the bridge, that on the west bank being served by the railway.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> There was also a "sand dock" constructed upstream of the bridge at the point where the Treguddick Brook (Polmorla Brook) flows into the River Camel, although this had been filled in by 1895<sup id="cite_ref-ncr_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncr-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1900s vessels such as the M.V. Florence brought cargos such as slag (for fertiliser), grain and coal. Flour was also a regular cargo brought from Ranks at Avonmouth. However, in the 1950s the river silted badly so that the ketch <i>Agnes</i> was possibly the last vessel to bring cargo to Wadebridge in 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-ncr_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncr-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eddystone_Road">Eddystone Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eddystone Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1877, after cracks appeared in the rock on which the <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a> was positioned, a new lighthouse was commissioned from <a href="/wiki/James_Nicholas_Douglass" title="James Nicholas Douglass">James Nicholas Douglass</a>. Granite quarried from <a href="/wiki/De_Lank_quarry" class="mw-redirect" title="De Lank quarry">De Lank quarry</a> was brought down to Wadebridge where stonemasons <a href="/wiki/Dovetail_joint" title="Dovetail joint">dovetailed</a> each segment of stone not only to each other but also to the courses above and below. As each layer was completed and checked to fit with the layer above, it was sent out to the Eddystone rocks by sea. The lighthouse was completed in 1882. This resulted in the road where the masons worked being called Eddystone Road.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_timeline">Historical timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Historical timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>1312 \xe2\x80\x94 Licence granted for Wade to hold a market.</li>\n<li>1455 \xe2\x80\x94 John Leland records a ferry<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Wadebridge is in the <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="North Cornwall (UK Parliament constituency)">constituency of North Cornwall</a> which is currently held by the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> MP <a href="/wiki/Scott_Mann_(politician)" title="Scott Mann (politician)">Scott Mann</a>. The main offices of the former <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="North Cornwall District Council">North Cornwall District Council</a> were at Trenant Road in the town.\n</p><p>Historically Wadebridge was part of <a href="/wiki/St_Columb_Major" title="St Columb Major">St Columb</a> Rural District Council until the creation of Wadebridge Urban District Council in April 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-ncr_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncr-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_transport">Geography and transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography and transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The town straddles the <a href="/wiki/River_Camel" title="River Camel">River Camel</a> 5 miles (8.0&#160;km) upstream from <a href="/wiki/Padstow" title="Padstow">Padstow</a><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the town centre being on the left bank of the river.\n</p><p>For many years Wadebridge was a traffic-congested town (through which the route of the <a href="/wiki/A39_road" title="A39 road">A39 trunk road</a> passed) but in 1991 the Wadebridge bypass was opened together with the <a href="/wiki/Egloshayle" title="Egloshayle">Egloshayle</a> bypass causing the two settlements to regain much of their former charm. The main shopping street in Wadebridge (Molesworth Street) has subsequently been pedestrianized through construction of an inner link road, allowing traffic-free shopping.\n</p><p>On St Swithin\'s day 1965 there was a flood in Wadebridge town after five and a half inches of rain fell in four and a half hours around high tide. The Swan Hotel on The Platt was flooded to a depth of one and a half feet.<sup id="cite_ref-camel_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-camel-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_community">Culture and community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Culture and community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg/220px-The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg/330px-The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg/440px-The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Challenge_Bridge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Challenge Bridge</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Molesworth Arms</b> is one of the oldest Inns in Wadebridge. Previously known as The Fox, The King\'s Arms and The Fountain, this coaching Inn got its current name in 1817.\n</p><p>Since 2014 the first of the annual events in and around Wadebridge has been the <b>MayPlay</b> festival, a weekend of free children\'s activities.\n</p><p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Royal_Cornwall_Agricultural_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Cornwall Agricultural Show">Royal Cornwall Agricultural Show</a></b> is held at the Royal Cornwall Showground, 1.5 miles (2.4&#160;km) west of Wadebridge over three days in early June each year. The show began in 1793 at Bodmin and was then held every year in East and West Cornwall alternately until 1960 when it came to its present site. The showground, run by the Royal Cornwall Agricultural Association, is used for many different functions from <a href="/wiki/The_Scout_Association" title="The Scout Association">Scout</a> <a href="/wiki/Jamboree_(Scouting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jamboree (Scouting)">Jamborees</a> to point-to-point horse racing.\n</p><p><b>The Big Lunch</b>, organised by the local chamber of commerce, is a free street party in the pedestrianised part of Molesworth St in the centre of Wadebridge, where around 500\xe2\x80\x93750 people get together to share food, chat, and enjoy music and other entertainment. The idea grew out of a project by the Eden Project, and was started by a former local councillor, Harriet Wild. In 2012 it also served as a celebration of the Queen\'s Jubilee.\n</p><p>Later in June, the Wadebridge Lions organise a <b>Beer Festival</b>, with brews from across Cornwall, and plenty of live music.\n</p><p>July sees the <b>Rock Oyster Festival</b> on fields just outside the town on the Camel River. Oysters are, of course, on the menu, along with bands from the local area, the South West and further afield.\n</p><p><b>Wadebridge Carnival</b> is held annually in August, with a Carnival Queen. In August there is the <b>Eglos Craft Fayre</b> at Egloshayle Church, and the <b>Cornwall Folk Festival</b><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>, one of the UK\'s longest-running folk festivals started in 1973, is held over the Bank Holiday. The likes of folk stars such as Dougie Maclean, Martyn Carthy and Cara Dillon rub shoulders with Cornish bands. The "musician\'s musician" <a href="/wiki/Wizz_Jones" title="Wizz Jones">Wiz Jones</a> and father of the Lakeman clan Geoff Lakeman are the festival\'s patrons.\n</p><p>Depending on the tides, the <b>Camel River Festival</b> is held around August or September. The main attraction is a set of raft races on the river, with bar, food, stalls and more live music.\n</p><p>In October, The <b>Bikelights</b> procession through the town centre showcases decorated bicycles and involves many youngsters. \n</p><p>In November the <b>Prime Stock Show</b> and the <b>Garden Produce Association and Chrysanthemum Show</b> are held.\n</p><p>A footbridge called the <b>Challenge Bridge</b> links the Egloshayle playing fields to the Jubilee fields on the other side of the river. The bridge was constructed in 1991 by <a href="/wiki/Anneka_Rice" title="Anneka Rice">Anneka Rice</a> and her team for the TV series "<a href="/wiki/Challenge_Anneka" title="Challenge Anneka">Challenge Anneka</a>". Locally, the bridge is known as Anneka\'s Bridge, but its real name is the Bailey Bridge.\n</p><p>The newspaper is a local edition of the weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Cornish_Guardian" title="Cornish Guardian">Cornish Guardian</a></i>.\n</p><p>The town is <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twinned</a> with  <a href="/wiki/Langueux" title="Langueux">Langueux</a> (Langaeg) in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, France.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 2013 Wadebridge was short-listed as one of Britain\'s top eco-towns<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and is home to <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge_Renewable_Energy_Network" title="Wadebridge Renewable Energy Network">Wadebridge Renewable Energy Network</a> a grass roots enterprise aiming to make the town the first solar powered and renewable energy powered town in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The town has two primary schools which have academy status, Wadebridge Primary Academy which OfSTED graded as a \xe2\x80\x98GOOD\xe2\x80\x99 school in November 2012 and St. Breock Primary School. There is also a Secondary School, <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge_School" title="Wadebridge School">Wadebridge School</a> which has a sixth form.\n</p><p>There are two health care practices: the Wadebridge and Camel Estuary Practice and the Bridge Medical Centre. There has been a group practice in Wadebridge since the early 20th century; many of the early doctors had their surgeries operating from their homes.\n</p><p>In the 1901 census the population of Wadebridge was 3470,<sup id="cite_ref-ncr_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncr-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> while in 2001 the population was 6222<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Wadebridge is home to sporting clubs including <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge_Town_F.C." title="Wadebridge Town F.C.">Wadebridge Town Football Club</a> who play at Bodieve park, <a href="/wiki/Wadebridge_Camels" title="Wadebridge Camels">Wadebridge Camels</a>, who play at the Molesworth Field in <a href="/wiki/Egloshayle" title="Egloshayle">Egloshayle</a>, and Wadebridge Cricket Club, whose main ground has been in Egloshayle Park since the 1950s. The town has a leisure centre with a programme of sports and pursuits including <a href="/wiki/Cornish_wrestling" title="Cornish wrestling">Cornish wrestling</a>.\n</p><p>The Camel estuary offers a range of water sports, including <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_skiing" title="Water skiing">water skiing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windsurfing" title="Windsurfing">windsurfing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surfing" title="Surfing">surfing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kite_surfing" class="mw-redirect" title="Kite surfing">kite surfing</a>. Golf courses close by include <a href="/wiki/Trevose" class="mw-redirect" title="Trevose">Trevose</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Minver" title="St Minver">Saint Enodoc</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Kew" title="St Kew">St Kew</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadebridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:People_from_Wadebridge" title="Category:People from Wadebridge">Category:People from Wadebridge</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gentleman_scientist" class="mw-redirect" title="Gentleman scientist">gentleman scientist</a> and surgeon <a href="/wiki/Goldsworthy_Gurney" title="Goldsworthy Gurney">Sir Goldsworthy Gurney</a>, who invented the <a href="/wiki/Bude-Light" title="Bude-Light">Bude-Light</a>, lived in Wadebridge from 1814 to 1820. A street (Goldsworthy Way) has been named after him. <a href="/wiki/Francis_Hurdon" title="Francis Hurdon">Francis Hurdon</a> (1834\xe2\x80\x931914), a prominent figure in Canadian politics, was educated in the town.\n</p><p>In media, <a href="/wiki/Michael_White_(journalist)" title="Michael White (journalist)">Michael White</a>, journalist, associate editor and former political editor of <i>The Guardian</i> was born here in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Ridgeley" title="Andrew Ridgeley">Andrew Ridgeley</a>, member of the pop music duo, <a href="/wiki/Wham!" title="Wham!">Wham!</a> and his partner <a href="/wiki/Keren_Woodward" title="Keren Woodward">Keren Woodward</a>, from the girl band <a href="/wiki/Bananarama" title="Bananarama">Bananarama</a>, live in a converted farmhouse<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> near the town.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Steven_Roberts_(British_Army_soldier)" title="Steven Roberts (British Army soldier)">Sergeant Steven Roberts</a>, the first soldier to die in the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>, was born in Wadebridge.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In sport, <a href="/wiki/Olly_Barkley" title="Olly Barkley">Olly Barkley</a>, the England <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> international player, was raised in the town, as was <a href="/wiki/Michaela_Breeze" title="Michaela Breeze">Michaela Breeze</a>, the Commonwealth <a href="/wiki/Powerlifting" title="Powerlifting">weightlifting</a> champion.
