\xce\xba\xce\xb1\xcf\x83\xcf\x83\xce\xaf\xcf\x84\xce\xb5\xcf\x81\xce\xbf\xcf\x82, <i>kass\xc3\xadteros</i> \xe2\x80\x9c<a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>\xe2\x80\x9d), are an ancient geographical name of islands that were regarded as situated somewhere near the west coasts of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cassiterides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> (430 BC) had only vaguely heard of the Cassiterides, "from which we are said to have our tin," but did not discount the islands as legendary.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Later writers \xe2\x80\x94 <a href="/wiki/Posidonius" title="Posidonius">Posidonius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and others \xe2\x80\x93 call them smallish islands off ("some way off," Strabo says) the northwest coast of the Iberian Peninsula, which contained <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> mines or, according to Strabo, tin and lead mines. A passage in Diodorus derives the name rather from their nearness to the tin districts of Northwest Iberia. <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dionysios_Periegetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Dionysios Periegetes">Dionysios Periegetes</a> mentioned them \xe2\x80\x93 the former as ten small islands in northwest Iberia far off the coast and arranged symbolically as a ring, and the latter in connection with the mythical <a href="/wiki/Hesperides" title="Hesperides">Hesperides</a>. The islands are described by <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> as rich in lead; they are mentioned last in the same paragraph he wrote about <a href="/wiki/Cadiz" class="mw-redirect" title="Cadiz">Cadiz</a> and the islands of <a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a>, and placed <i>in Celtici</i>. Following paragraphs describe the <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_de_Sein" title="\xc3\x8ele de Sein">\xc3\x8ele de Sein</a> and Britain.\n</p><p>Probably written in the first century BC, the verse <i>Circumnavigation of the World</i>, whose anonymous author is called the "<a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Scymnus" title="Pseudo-Scymnus">Pseudo-Scymnus</a>," places two tin islands in the upper part of the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a> and mentioned the market place <a href="/wiki/Osor,_Croatia" title="Osor, Croatia">Osor</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Cres" title="Cres">Cres</a>, where extraordinarily high quality tin could be bought.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, on the other hand, represents the <i>Cassiterides</i> as fronting <a href="/wiki/Celtiberia" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtiberia">Celtiberia</a>.\n</p><p>At a time when geographical knowledge of the West was still scanty, and when the secrets of the tin trade were still successfully guarded by the seamen of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C\xc3\xa1diz">Gades</a> (modern Cadiz) and others who dealt in the metal, the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greeks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greeks">Greeks</a> knew only that tin came to them by sea from the far West, and the idea of tin-producing islands easily arose. Later, when the West was better explored, it was found that tin actually came from two regions: <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, in the northwest of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon" title="Mining in Cornwall and Devon">Devon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in southwest Britain. Diodorus reports: "For there are many mines of tin in the country above <a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a> and on the islets which lie off Iberia out in the ocean and are called because of that fact the Cassiterides." According to Diodorus tin <i>also</i> came from Britannia to Gaul and thence was brought overland to <a href="/wiki/Marseilles" class="mw-redirect" title="Marseilles">Massilia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narbo" class="mw-redirect" title="Narbo">Narbo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Neither of these could be called small islands or accurately described as off the northwest coast of Iberia, and so the Greek and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> geographers did not identify either as the Cassiterides. Instead, they became a third, ill-understood source of tin, conceived of as distinct from Iberia or Britain.\n</p><p>Strabo says that a Publius Crassus was the first Roman to visit the Tin Islands and write a first-hand report. This Crassus is thought to be either the <a href="/wiki/Publius_Licinius_Crassus_Dives_(consul_97_BC)" title="Publius Licinius Crassus Dives (consul 97 BC)">Publius Licinius Crassus Dives</a> who was a governor in <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Ulterior" title="Hispania Ulterior">Hispania</a> in the 90s,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> or his <a href="/wiki/Publius_Licinius_Crassus_(son_of_triumvir)" title="Publius Licinius Crassus (son of triumvir)">grandson by the same name</a>, who in 57\xe2\x80\x9356 BC commanded <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>\'s forces in <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> which places him near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Loire" title="Loire">Loire</a> river.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_attempts_at_identification">Modern attempts at identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cassiterides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern attempts at identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Modern writers have made many attempts to identify them.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Small islands off the northwest coast of the Iberian Peninsula, the headlands of that same coast, the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, Cornwall, and the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> as a whole, have all in turn been suggested, but none suits the conditions. Neither the Iberian islands nor the Isles of Scilly contain tin, at least in significant quantities. It seems most probable, therefore, that the name <i>Cassiterides</i> represents the first vague knowledge of the Greeks that tin was found overseas, somewhere in, off, or near Western Europe.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gavin_de_Beer" title="Gavin de Beer">Gavin de Beer</a> has suggested<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> that Roger Dion had solved the puzzle<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> by bringing to bear a chance remark in <a href="/wiki/Avienus" title="Avienus">Avienus</a>\' late poem <i>Ora maritima</i>, which is based on early sources: the tin isles were in an arm of the sea within sight of wide plains and rich mines of tin and lead, and opposite two islands \xe2\x80\x94 a further one, Hibernia, and a nearer one, Britannia. "Before the estuary of the Loire became silted up in late Roman times, the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> led into a wide gulf, now represented by the lower reaches of the river Brivet<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and the marshes of the <a href="/wiki/Bri%C3%A8re" title="Bri\xc3\xa8re">Bri\xc3\xa8re</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Paimboeuf" class="mw-redirect" title="Paimboeuf">Paimboeuf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint-Nazaire" title="Saint-Nazaire">St. Nazaire</a>, in which were a number of islands. The islands and shores of this gulf, now joined together by silt, are crowded with Bronze Age foundries that worked tin and lead; <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9nestin" title="P\xc3\xa9nestin">P\xc3\xa9nestin</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and the tin headland are just north of them; and there can be no doubt that the famous tin islands were there."  De Beer confirms the location from Strabo: the Cassiterides are ten islands in the sea, north of the land of the <a href="/wiki/Artabrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Artabrians">Artabrians</a> in the northwest corner of Hispania.<br /> E. Thomas from the French <a href="/wiki/Bureau_de_recherches_g%C3%A9ologiques_et_mini%C3%A8res" class="mw-redirect" title="Bureau de recherches g\xc3\xa9ologiques et mini\xc3\xa8res">BRGM</a> showed in a 2004 report that tin mines were probably operated by the Romans at La Hye, near <a href="/wiki/Plo%C3%ABrmel" title="Plo\xc3\xabrmel">Plo\xc3\xabrmel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This tin might have transited down the <a href="/wiki/Oust" title="Oust">Oust</a> river and the <a href="/wiki/Vilaine" title="Vilaine">Vilaine</a> river to the sea, where it could be transferred on seagoing ships, possibly at <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9nestin" title="P\xc3\xa9nestin">P\xc3\xa9nestin</a>, giving some support to de Beer\'s suggestion above.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
