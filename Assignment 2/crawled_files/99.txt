pirate</i>, <a href="/wiki/Pier_Francesco_Mola" title="Pier Francesco Mola">Pier Francesco Mola</a> 1650</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Barbary pirates</b>, sometimes called <b>Barbary corsairs</b> or <b>Ottoman corsairs</b>, were <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maghrebis" title="Maghrebis">Maghrebis</a> <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a> who operated from <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, based primarily in the ports of <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9" title="Sal\xc3\xa9">Sal\xc3\xa9</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabat" title="Rabat">Rabat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>. This area was known in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary Coast</a>, a term derived from the name of its Berber inhabitants. Their predation extended throughout the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, south along <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> seaboard and into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic</a> as far north as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, but they primarily operated in the western Mediterranean. In addition to seizing <a href="/wiki/Merchant_vessel" title="Merchant vessel">merchant ships</a>, they engaged in <i><a href="/wiki/Ghazw" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghazw">Razzias</a></i>, raids on European coastal towns and villages, mainly in Italy, France, Spain, and Portugal, but also in the British Isles,<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the Netherlands, and as far away as Iceland.<sup id="cite_ref-Toll_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The main purpose of their attacks was to capture <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Slavery in the Ottoman Empire">Ottoman slave trade</a> as well as the general <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">Arab slavery market</a> in North Africa and the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While, such raids had occurred since soon after the Muslim conquest of Iberia, the terms "Barbary pirates" and "Barbary corsairs" are normally applied to the raiders active from the 16th century onwards, when the frequency and range of the slavers\' attacks increased. In that period Algiers, Tunis and Tripoli came under the sovereignty of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, either as directly administered provinces or as autonomous dependencies known as the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary States">Barbary States</a>. Similar raids were undertaken from <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Bou_Regreg" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Bou Regreg">Sal\xc3\xa9</a> and other ports in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.\n</p><p>Barbary corsairs captured thousands of merchant ships and repeatedly raided coastal towns. As a result, residents abandoned their former villages of long stretches of coast in Spain and Italy. Between 100,000 and 250,000 Iberians were enslaved by these raids.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The raids were such a problem coastal settlements were seldom undertaken until the 19th century. Between 1580 and 1680 corsairs were said to have captured about 850,000 people as slaves and from 1530 to 1780 as many as 1,250,000 people were enslaved.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, these numbers have been questioned by the historian David Earle.<sup id="cite_ref-Earle_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Earle-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Some of these corsairs were European outcasts and <a href="/wiki/Religious_conversion" title="Religious conversion">converts</a> (renegade) such as <a href="/wiki/Jack_Ward" title="Jack Ward">John Ward</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zymen_Danseker" title="Zymen Danseker">Zymen Danseker</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Toll_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa" title="Hayreddin Barbarossa">Hayreddin Barbarossa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oru%C3%A7_Reis" title="Oru\xc3\xa7 Reis">Oru\xc3\xa7 Reis</a>, Turkish Barbarossa Brothers, who took control of Algiers on behalf of the Ottomans in the early 16th century, were also notorious corsairs. The European pirates brought advanced sailing and shipbuilding techniques to the Barbary Coast around 1600, which enabled the corsairs to extend their activities into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Toll_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (March 2018)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The effects of the Barbary raids peaked in the early to mid-17th century.\n</p><p>Long after Europeans had abandoned <a href="/wiki/Oar" title="Oar">oar</a>-driven vessels in favor of sailing ships carrying tons of powerful cannon, many Barbary warships were <a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">galleys</a> carrying a hundred or more fighting men armed with <a href="/wiki/Cutlass" title="Cutlass">cutlasses</a> and small arms. The Barbary navies were not battle fleets. When they sighted a European <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigate</a>, they fled.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The scope of corsair activity began to diminish in the latter part of the 17th century,<sup id="cite_ref-Chaney_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaney-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> as the more powerful European navies started to compel the Barbary States to make peace and cease attacking their shipping. However, the ships and coasts of Christian states without such effective protection continued to suffer until the early 19th century. Following the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> in 1814\xe2\x80\x9315, European powers agreed upon the need to suppress the Barbary corsairs entirely and the threat was largely subdued. Occasional incidents occurred, including two <a href="/wiki/Barbary_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary wars">Barbary wars</a> between the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the Barbary States, until finally terminated by the <a href="/wiki/French_North_Africa" title="French North Africa">French conquest of Algiers in 1830</a>.\n</p>\n<div tocsection-15"><a href="#In_fiction"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">In fiction</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-16"><a href="#See_also"><span class="toclevel-1 tocsection-19"><a href="#Further_reading"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">Further reading</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-20"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Piracy by Muslim populations had been known in the Mediterranean since at least the 9th century and the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Crete" title="Emirate of Crete">Emirate of Crete</a>. <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> was plagued by <a href="/wiki/Saracen" title="Saracen">Saracen</a> slave raids in the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingian era">Carolingian era</a>; in 869, archbishop <a href="/wiki/Rotlandus_of_Arles" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotlandus of Arles">Rotlandus of Arles</a> was captured, and died before he could be released after the payment of a ransom in weapons, treasure and slaves. \n</p><p>In 1198 the problem of Berber piracy and slave-taking was so great that a religious order, the <a href="/wiki/Trinitarians" class="mw-redirect" title="Trinitarians">Trinitarians</a>, were founded to collect ransoms and even to exchange themselves as ransom for those captured and pressed into slavery in North Africa.  In the 14th century Tunisian corsairs became enough of a threat to provoke a Franco-Genoese attack on Mahdia in 1390, also known as the "<a href="/wiki/Barbary_Crusade" title="Barbary Crusade">Barbary Crusade</a>". <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">Morisco</a> exiles of the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> and Maghreb pirates added to the numbers, but it was not until the expansion of the Ottoman Empire and the arrival of the privateer and admiral <a href="/wiki/Kemal_Reis" title="Kemal Reis">Kemal Reis</a> in 1487 that the Barbary corsairs became a true menace to shipping from European Christian nations.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg/220px-Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg/330px-Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg/440px-Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg 2x" data-file-width="825" data-file-height="621" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Captain_walter_croker_horror_stricken_at_algiers_1815.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>British captain witnessing the miseries of Christian slaves in Algiers, 1815</div></div></div>\n<p>The Barbary pirates had long attacked English and other European shipping along the North Coast of Africa. They had been attacking English merchant and passengers ships since the 1600s. Regular fundraising for ransoms was undertaken generally by families and local church groups, who generally raised the ransoms for individuals. The government did not ransom ordinary persons. The English became familiar with <a href="/wiki/Captivity_narratives" class="mw-redirect" title="Captivity narratives">captivity narratives</a> written by Barbary pirates\' prisoners and ransomed captives, as so many people were taken. After English colonists began to go to North America and be taken captive by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a>, both the colonists and people in England had some basis for considering the meaning of captivity for a Christian in an alien society.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> the pirates attacked American merchant vessels in the Mediterranean. But, on December 20, 1777, <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a> <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_III_of_Morocco" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohammed III of Morocco">Mohammed III of Morocco</a> declared that American merchant ships would be under the protection of the sultanate and could thus enjoy safe passage into the Mediterranean and along the coast. The <a href="/wiki/Moroccan-American_Treaty_of_Friendship" class="mw-redirect" title="Moroccan-American Treaty of Friendship">Moroccan-American Treaty of Friendship</a> stands as the U.S.\'s oldest non-broken friendship <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> with a foreign power. In 1778 Morocco became the first nation to recognize the new United States.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As late as 1798, an islet near <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> was attacked by the <a href="/wiki/Beylik_of_Tunis" title="Beylik of Tunis">Tunisians</a>, and more than 900 inhabitants were taken away as slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Throughout history, geography was on the pirates\' side on the Northern coast of Africa. The coast was ideal for their wants and needs. With natural harbours often backed by lagoons, it provided a haven for guerrilla warfare, such as attacks on shipping vessels venturing through their territory. On the coast, mountainous areas provided ample reconnaissance for the corsairs as well. Ships were spotted from afar; the pirates had time to prepare their attacks and surprise the ships.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="16th_century">16th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 16th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Preveza_(1538).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Battle_of_Preveza_%281538%29.jpg/220px-Battle_of_Preveza_%281538%29.jpg" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Battle_of_Preveza_%281538%29.jpg/330px-Battle_of_Preveza_%281538%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Battle_of_Preveza_%281538%29.jpg/440px-Battle_of_Preveza_%281538%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="499" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Preveza_(1538).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Preveza" title="Battle of Preveza">Battle of Preveza</a>, 1538</div></div></div>\n<p>Moors and Turkish adventurers from the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, of whom the most successful were <a href="/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa" title="Hayreddin Barbarossa">H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aruj" class="mw-redirect" title="Aruj">Oru\xc3\xa7</a>, natives of <a href="/wiki/Mitylene" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitylene">Mitylene</a>, increased the number of raids around the turn of the 15th century. In response, Spain began to conquer the coastal towns of <a href="/wiki/Oran" title="Oran">Oran</a>, Algiers and <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>. But after Oru\xc3\xa7 was killed in battle with the Spanish in 1518, his brother H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r appealed to <a href="/wiki/Selim_I" title="Selim I">Selim I</a>, the Ottoman sultan, who sent him troops. In 1529, H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r drove the Spaniards from the rocky, fortified island in front of Algiers, and founded the Ottoman power in the region. From about 1518 till the death of <a href="/wiki/Ulu%C3%A7_Ali" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulu\xc3\xa7 Ali">Ulu\xc3\xa7 Ali</a> in 1587, Algiers was the main seat of government of the <a href="/wiki/Beylerbey" title="Beylerbey">beylerbeys</a> of northern Africa, who ruled over Tripoli, Tunisia and Algeria. From 1587 to 1659, they were ruled by Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Pasha" title="Pasha">pashas</a>, sent from <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> to govern for three years; but in the latter year a military revolt in Algiers reduced the pashas to nonentities.\n</p><p>From 1659, these African cities, although nominally part of the Ottoman Empire, were in fact military republics that chose their own rulers and lived by war booty captured from the Spanish and Portuguese. There are several cases of <a href="/wiki/Sephardic_Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Sephardic Jew">Sephardic Jews</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Sinan_Reis" title="Sinan Reis">Sinan Reis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pallache" title="Samuel Pallache">Samuel Pallache</a>, who upon <a href="/wiki/Alhambra_Decree" title="Alhambra Decree">fleeing Iberia</a> turned to attacking the Spanish Empire\'s shipping under the Ottoman flag, a profitable strategy of revenge for the <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">Inquisition</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Religious_persecution" title="Religious persecution">religious persecution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kritzler59_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kritzler59-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-plaut_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plaut-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the first period (1518\xe2\x80\x931587), the beylerbeys were admirals of the sultan, commanding great fleets and conducting war operations for political ends. They were slave-hunters and their methods were ferocious. After 1587, the sole object of their successors became plunder, on land and sea. The maritime operations were conducted by the captains, or <i>reises</i>, who formed a class or even a corporation. Cruisers were fitted out by investors and commanded by the <i>reises</i>. Ten percent of the value of the prizes was paid to the pasha or his successors, who bore the titles of <i>agha</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Dey" title="Dey">dey</a></i> or <i>bey</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg/220px-Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg/330px-Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg/440px-Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Genoise_tower_in_corsica.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Barbary pirates frequently attacked Corsica, resulting in many <a href="/wiki/Genoese_tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Genoese tower">Genoese towers</a> being erected.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1544 Hayreddin captured the island of <a href="/wiki/Ischia" title="Ischia">Ischia</a>, taking 4,000 prisoners, and enslaved some 2,000\xe2\x80\x937,000 inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Lipari" title="Lipari">Lipari</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PLZ_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PLZ-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1551 <a href="/wiki/Turgut_Reis" class="mw-redirect" title="Turgut Reis">Turgut Reis</a> enslaved the entire population of the Maltese island of <a href="/wiki/Gozo" title="Gozo">Gozo</a>, between 5,000 and 6,000, sending them to <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Tripolitania" title="Ottoman Tripolitania">Ottoman Tripolitania</a>. In 1554 corsairs under Turgut Reis sacked <a href="/wiki/Vieste" title="Vieste">Vieste</a>, beheaded 5,000 of its inhabitants, and abducted another 6,000.<sup id="cite_ref-PTZ_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PTZ-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In 1555 Turgut Reis sacked <a href="/wiki/Bastia" title="Bastia">Bastia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, taking 6,000 prisoners. In 1558, Barbary corsairs captured the town of <a href="/wiki/Ciutadella" class="mw-redirect" title="Ciutadella">Ciutadella</a> (Minorca), destroyed it, <a href="/wiki/Murdered" class="mw-redirect" title="Murdered">murdered</a> many inhabitants, and took 3,000 to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> as slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1563 Turgut Reis landed on the shores of the province of <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>, Spain, and captured coastal settlements in the area, such as <a href="/wiki/Almu%C3%B1%C3%A9car" title="Almu\xc3\xb1\xc3\xa9car">Almu\xc3\xb1\xc3\xa9car</a>, along with 4,000 prisoners. Barbary corsairs often attacked the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, and in response many coastal watchtowers and fortified <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a> were erected. The threat was so severe that residents abandoned the island of <a href="/wiki/Formentera" title="Formentera">Formentera</a>.\n</p><p>Even at this early stage, the European states fought back: <a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">Livorno</a>\'s monument <i>Quattro Mori</i> celebrates 16th-century victories against the Barbary corsairs won by the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights of Malta</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Stephen" title="Order of Saint Stephen">Order of Saint Stephen</a>, of which the Grand Duke of Tuscany <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Ferdinando I de&#39; Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Ferdinando I de\' Medici</a> was Grand Master. Another response was the construction of the original <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a>; light, fast and maneuverable galleys, designed to run down Barbary corsairs trying to get away with their loot and slaves. Other measures included coastal lookouts to give warning for people to withdraw into fortified places and rally local forces to fight the corsairs. This latter goal was especially difficult to achieve as the corsairs had the advantage of surprise; the vulnerable European Mediterranean coasts were very long and easily accessible from the north African Barbary bases, and the corsairs were careful in planning their raids.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_(c_1615)_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_%28c_1615%29_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg/220px-A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_%28c_1615%29_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_%28c_1615%29_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg/330px-A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_%28c_1615%29_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_%28c_1615%29_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg/440px-A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_%28c_1615%29_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="909" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_French_Ship_and_Barbary_Pirates_(c_1615)_by_Aert_Anthoniszoon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>A French Ship and Barbary Pirates</i> by <a href="/wiki/Aart_van_Antum" class="mw-redirect" title="Aart van Antum">Aert Anthonisz</a>, c. 1615</div></div></div>\n<p>During the first half of the 17th century, Barbary raiding was at its peak. This was due largely to the contribution of Dutch corsairs, notably <a href="/wiki/Zymen_Danseker" title="Zymen Danseker">Zymen Danseker</a> (Simon de Danser), who used the Barbary ports as bases for attacking Spanish shipping during the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Revolt" title="Dutch Revolt">Dutch Revolt</a>. They cooperated with local raiders and introduced them to the latest Dutch sailing rigs, enabling them to brave Atlantic waters.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Some of these Dutch corsairs converted to Islam and settled permanently in North Africa. Two examples are S\xc3\xbcleyman Reis, "<a href="/wiki/De_Veenboer" class="mw-redirect" title="De Veenboer">De Veenboer</a>", who became admiral of the Algerian corsair fleet in 1617, and his quartermaster Murat Reis, born <a href="/wiki/Jan_Janszoon" title="Jan Janszoon">Jan Janszoon</a>. Both worked for the notorious Dutch corsair <a href="/wiki/Zymen_Danseker" title="Zymen Danseker">Zymen Danseker</a>.\n</p><p>A notable counter action occurred in 1607, when the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Stephen" title="Order of Saint Stephen">Knights of Saint Stephen</a> (under <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Inghirami" title="Jacopo Inghirami">Jacopo Inghirami</a>) sacked <a href="/wiki/Annaba" title="Annaba">Bona</a> in Algeria, killing 470 and taking 1,464 captives.<sup id="cite_ref-PPZ_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPZ-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This victory is commemorated by a series of frescoes painted by <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_Poccetti" title="Bernardino Poccetti">Bernardino Poccetti</a> in the <i>"Sala di Bona"</i> of <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Pitti" title="Palazzo Pitti">Palazzo Pitti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 1611 Spanish galleys from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Naples</a>, accompanied by the galleys of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Malta_under_the_Order_of_Saint_John" title="History of Malta under the Order of Saint John">Knights of Malta</a>, raided the <a href="/wiki/Kerkennah_Islands" title="Kerkennah Islands">Kerkennah Islands</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and took away almost 500 Muslim captives.<sup id="cite_ref-PKZ_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PKZ-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Between 1568 and 1634 the Knights of Saint Stephen may have captured about 14,000 Muslims, with perhaps one-third taken in land raids and two-thirds taken on captured ships.<sup id="cite_ref-PKZ_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PKZ-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div title="Mercedarians">Mercedarians</a> was in ransoming Christian slaves held in Muslim hands, <i>Histoire de Barbarie et de ses Corsaires</i>, 1637</div></div></div>\n<p>Barbary corsair attacks were common in southern <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Portugal</a>, south and east <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">Italian Peninsula</a> (especially the <a href="/wiki/Tyrrhenian_Sea" title="Tyrrhenian Sea">Tyrrhenian</a> coast), <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>. They also occurred on the Atlantic northwest coast of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> as in 1617, when the North African corsairs launched their major attack in the region. They destroyed and sacked Bouzas, <a href="/wiki/Cangas_do_Morrazo" class="mw-redirect" title="Cangas do Morrazo">Cangas do Morrazo</a> and the churches of <a href="/wiki/Moa%C3%B1a" title="Moa\xc3\xb1a">Moa\xc3\xb1a</a> and Darbo.\n</p><p>Occasionally coastal raids reached farther afield. <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> was subject to <a href="/wiki/The_Turkish_abductions" class="mw-redirect" title="The Turkish abductions">raids</a> in 1627. Jan Janszoon (Murat Reis the Younger) is said to have taken 400 prisoners; 242 of the captives later were sold into slavery on the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary Coast</a>. The corsairs took only young people and those in good physical condition. All those offering resistance were killed, and the old people were gathered into a church which was set on fire. Among those captured was <a href="/wiki/%C3%93lafur_Egilsson" title="\xc3\x93lafur Egilsson">\xc3\x93lafur Egilsson</a>, who was ransomed the next year. Upon returning to Iceland, he wrote an account about his experience. Such <a href="/wiki/Captivity_narrative" title="Captivity narrative">captivity narratives</a> by Europeans who had been held in Muslim states eventually constituted a literary genre.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a> was subject to a similar attack. In June 1631 Murat Reis, with corsairs from Algiers and armed troops of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, stormed ashore at the little harbor village of <a href="/wiki/Baltimore,_County_Cork" title="Baltimore, County Cork">Baltimore, County Cork</a>. They <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Baltimore" title="Sack of Baltimore">captured almost all the villagers</a> and took them away to a life of slavery in North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The prisoners were destined for a variety of fates&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 some lived out their days chained to the oars as galley slaves, while women spent long years as <a href="/wiki/Concubines" class="mw-redirect" title="Concubines">concubines</a> in harems or within the walls of the sultan\'s palace. Only two of these captives ever returned to Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (May 2014)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>More than 20,000 captives were said to be imprisoned in Algiers alone. The rich were often able to secure release through ransom, but the poor were condemned to slavery. Their masters would on occasion allow them to secure freedom by professing Islam. A long list might be given of people of good social position, not only Italians or Spaniards, but German or English travelers in the south, who were captives for a time.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> While the chief victims were the inhabitants of the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Sicily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Naples</a> and Spain, all traders of nations which did not pay tribute for immunity or force the Barbary States to leave them alone were liable to be taken at sea. Religious orders&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Congregation_of_the_Most_Holy_Redeemer" title="Congregation of the Most Holy Redeemer">Redemptorists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Congregation_of_the_Mission" title="Congregation of the Mission">Lazarists</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 worked for the redemption of captives, and in many countries the wealthy left legacies to support such redemptions.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a Verschuier">Lieve Pietersz Verschuier</a>, <i>Dutch ships bomb Tripoli in a punitive expedition against the Barbary pirates</i>, c. 1670</div></div></div>\n<p>Barbary piracy thrived on the competition among European powers. France encouraged the corsairs against Spain, and later Britain and Holland supported them against France. By the second half of the 17th century, the greater European naval powers were able to strike back effectively enough to intimidate the Barbary States into making peace with them. However, those countries\' commercial interests benefited by the pirates continuing attacks on their competitors. As a result, they did not cooperate to impose a more general cessation of corsair activity.\n</p><p>England was the most successful of the Christian states in dealing with the corsair threat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> From the 1630s onwards England had signed peace treaties with the Barbary States on various occasions, but invariably breaches of these agreements led to renewed wars. A particular bone of contention was the tendency of foreign ships to pose as English to avoid attack. However, growing English naval power and increasingly persistent operations against the corsairs proved increasingly costly for the Barbary States. During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> a series of English expeditions won victories over raiding Barbary squadrons and mounted attacks on their home ports; these actions permanently ended the Barbary threat to English shipping. In 1675 a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> squadron led by <a href="/wiki/John_Narborough" title="John Narborough">Sir John Narborough</a> negotiated a lasting peace with Tunis and, after bombarding the city to induce compliance, with Tripoli. Peace with Sal\xc3\xa9 followed in 1676.\n</p><p>Algiers, the most powerful of the Barbary States, returned to war the following year, breaking a treaty made in 1671. After suffering defeats at the hands of an English squadron under <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Herbert,_1st_Earl_of_Torrington" title="Arthur Herbert, 1st Earl of Torrington">Arthur Herbert</a>, Algiers made peace again in 1682, in a treaty that lasted until 1816. France, which had recently emerged as a leading naval power, achieved comparable success soon afterwards. It bombarded Algiers in 1682, 1683 and 1688 to secure a lasting peace, and forced Tripoli to sue for peace by bombardment in 1686.\n</p><p>A 2016 study found that Barbary corsairs were less militarily powerful after 1675 than they were at the start of the seventeenth century.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="18th.E2.80.9319th_centuries"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="18th\xe2\x80\x9319th_centuries"><span id="18th-19th_centuries"></span><span id="Barbary_Coast_Wars"></span><span id="Barbary_Wars"></span> 18th\xe2\x80\x9319th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 18th\xe2\x80\x9319th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/First_Barbary_War" title="First Barbary War">First Barbary War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Barbary_War" title="Second Barbary War">Second Barbary War</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BainbridgeTribute.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/BainbridgeTribute.jpg/220px-BainbridgeTribute.jpg" width="220" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/BainbridgeTribute.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="322" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BainbridgeTribute.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Captain <a href="/wiki/William_Bainbridge" title="William Bainbridge">William Bainbridge</a> paying tribute to the <a href="/wiki/Dey" title="Dey">Dey</a> of Algiers, circa 1800</div></div></div>\n<p>Piracy was enough of a problem that some states entered into the redemption business. In Denmark, "At the beginning of the 18th century money was collected systematically in all churches, and a so called \'slave fund\' (slavekasse) was established by the state in 1715. Funds were brought in through a compulsory insurance sum for seafarers. 165 slaves were ransomed by this institution between 1716 and 1736."<sup id="cite_ref-madsen_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-madsen-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> "Between 1716 and 1754 19 ships from Denmark-Norway were captured with 208 men; piracy was thus a serious problem for the Danish merchant fleet."<sup id="cite_ref-madsen_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-madsen-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the late 18th century piracy began to arise again. In <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Algiers_(1783)" title="Bombardment of Algiers (1783)">1783</a> and 1784 the Spanish bombarded <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a> to end piracy. The <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Algiers_(1784)" title="Bombardment of Algiers (1784)">second time</a> <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Barcel%C3%B3" title="Antonio Barcel\xc3\xb3">Admiral Barcel\xc3\xb3</a> damaged the city so severely that the Algerian <a href="/wiki/Dey" title="Dey">Dey</a> asked <a href="/wiki/Enlightenment_in_Spain" title="Enlightenment in Spain">Spain</a> to negotiate a peace treaty. From then on Spanish vessels and coasts were safe for several years. Separately, the Danish <a href="/wiki/Action_of_16_May_1797" title="Action of 16 May 1797">attacked Tripoli in 1797</a>. \n</p><p>Until the <a href="/wiki/American_Declaration_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American Declaration of Independence">American Declaration of Independence</a> in 1776, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> treaties with the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Northwest Africa">North African</a> states protected American ships from the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary</a> corsairs. <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, which in 1777 was <a href="/wiki/Moroccan%E2%80%93American_Treaty_of_Friendship" title="Moroccan\xe2\x80\x93American Treaty of Friendship">the first independent nation to publicly recognize the United States</a>, in 1784 became the first Barbary power to seize an American vessel after the nation achieved independence. The Barbary threat led directly to the United States founding the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> in March 1794. While the United States did secure peace treaties with the Barbary states, it was obliged to pay tribute for protection from attack. The burden was substantial: in 1800 payments in ransom and tribute to the Barbary states amounted to 20% of <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States federal government</a>\'s annual expenditures.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The United States conducted the <a href="/wiki/First_Barbary_War" title="First Barbary War">First Barbary War</a> in 1801 and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Barbary_War" title="Second Barbary War">Second Barbary War</a> in 1815 to gain more favorable peace terms; it ended the payment of tribute. But, Algiers broke the 1805 peace treaty after two years, and refused to implement the 1815 treaty until compelled to do so by Britain in 1816.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> (1814\xe2\x80\x9315), which ended the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, led to increased European consensus on the need to end Barbary raiding. The sacking of <a href="/wiki/Palma,_Sardinia" class="mw-redirect" title="Palma, Sardinia">Palma</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> by a Tunisian squadron, which carried off 158 inhabitants, roused widespread indignation. Britain had by this time banned the <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a> and was seeking to induce other countries to do likewise. States that were more vulnerable to the corsairs complained that Britain cared more for ending the trade in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">African slaves</a> than stopping the enslavement of Europeans and Americans by the Barbary States.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a this objection and further the anti-slavery campaign, in 1816 Britain sent <a href="/wiki/Edward_Pellew,_1st_Viscount_Exmouth" title="Edward Pellew, 1st Viscount Exmouth">Lord Exmouth</a> to secure new concessions from <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Tripolitania" title="Ottoman Tripolitania">Tripoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beylik_of_Tunis" title="Beylik of Tunis">Tunis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Algeria" title="Ottoman Algeria">Algiers</a>, including a pledge to treat Christian captives in any future conflict as <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a> rather than slaves. He imposed peace between Algiers and the kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Piedmont-Sardinia" class="mw-redirect" title="Piedmont-Sardinia">Sardinia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Kingdom of the Two Sicilies">Sicily</a>. On his first visit, Lord Exmouth negotiated satisfactory treaties and sailed for home. While he was negotiating, a number of Sardinian fishermen who had settled at <a href="/wiki/Annaba" title="Annaba">Bona</a> on the Tunisian coast were brutally treated without his knowledge. As Sardinians they were technically under British protection, the government sent Exmouth back to secure reparation. On August 17, in combination with a Dutch squadron under Admiral Van de Capellen, Exmouth <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Algiers_(1816)" title="Bombardment of Algiers (1816)">bombarded Algiers</a>. Both Algiers and Tunis made fresh concessions as a result.\n</p><p>The Barbary states had difficulty securing uniform compliance with a total prohibition of slave-raiding, as this had been traditionally of central importance to the North African economy. Slavers continued to take captives by preying on less well-protected peoples. Algiers subsequently renewed its slave-raiding, though on a smaller scale. Europeans at the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_(1818)" title="Congress of Aix-la-Chapelle (1818)">Congress of Aix-la-Chapelle</a> in 1818 discussed possible retaliation. In 1820 a British fleet under Admiral Sir Harry Neal bombarded Algiers. Corsair activity based in Algiers did not entirely cease until France <a href="/wiki/French_Algeria#The_French_conquest_of_Algeria" title="French Algeria">conquered the state in 1830</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slaves">Slaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Slaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barbary_slaves">Barbary slaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Barbary slaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">Arab slave trade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbary_slave_trade" title="Barbary slave trade">Barbary slave trade</a></div>\n<p>According to Robert Davis, between 1 million and 1.25 million Europeans were captured by Barbary pirates and sold as slaves in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> between the 16th and 19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-researchnews.osu.edu_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-researchnews.osu.edu-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> However, to extrapolate his numbers, Davis assumes the number of European slaves captured by Barbary pirates were constant for a 250-year period, stating:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There are no records of how many men, women and children were enslaved, but it is possible to calculate roughly the number of fresh captives that would have been needed to keep populations steady and replace those slaves who died, escaped, were ransomed, or converted to Islam. On this basis it is thought that around 8,500 new slaves were needed annually to replenish numbers \xe2\x80\x93 about 850,000 captives over the century from 1580 to 1680.  By extension, for the 250 years between 1530 and 1780, the figure could easily have been as high as 1,250,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Earle_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Earle-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Davis\' numbers have been question by the historian David Earle, who said of Davis\' numbers "His figures sound a bit dodgy and I think he may be exaggerating" and cautioned that the true picture of Europeans slaves is clouded by the fact the corsairs also seized non-Christian whites from eastern Europe and black people from west Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Earle_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Earle-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition, the number of slaves traded was hyperactive, with exaggerated estimates relying on peak years to calculate averages for entire centuries, or millennia.  Hence, there were wide fluctuations year-to-year, particularly in the 18th and 19th centuries, given slave imports, and given the fact that, prior to the 1840s, there are no consistent records.  Middle East expert, John Wright, cautions that modern estimates are based on back-calculations from human observation.<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Such observations, across the late 1500s and early 1600s observers, account for around 35,000 European Christian slaves held throughout this period on the Barbary Coast, across Tripoli, Tunis, but mostly in Algiers. The majority were sailors (particularly those who were English), taken with their ships, but others were fishermen and poor coastal villagers.  However, most of these captives were people from lands close to Africa, particularly Spain and Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From bases on the Barbary coast, North Africa, the Barbary pirates raided ships traveling through the Mediterranean and along the northern and western coasts of Africa, plundering their cargo and enslaving the people they captured. From at least 1500, the pirates also conducted raids along seaside towns of Italy, Spain, France, England, the Netherlands and as far away as Iceland, capturing men, women and children. On some occasions, settlements such as <a href="/wiki/Baltimore,_County_Cork" title="Baltimore, County Cork">Baltimore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> were abandoned following the raid, only being resettled many years later. Between 1609 and 1616, England alone had 466 merchant ships lost to Barbary pirates.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While Barbary corsairs looted the cargo of ships they captured, their primary goal was to capture people for sale as slaves or for ransom. Those who had family or friends who might ransom them were held captive, but not obliged to work; the most famous of these was the author <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a>, who was held for almost five years. Others were sold into various types of servitude. Attractive women or boys could be used as <a href="/wiki/Sex_slaves" class="mw-redirect" title="Sex slaves">sex slaves</a>. Captives who converted to Islam were generally freed, since enslavement of Muslims was prohibited; but this meant that they could never return to their native countries.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg/220px-Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg" width="220" height="244" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg/330px-Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg/440px-Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4512" data-file-height="5008" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Augustins_-_Le_Sultan_du_Maroc_-_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sultan of Morocco, by <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eug\xc3\xa8ne Delacroix">Eug\xc3\xa8ne Delacroix</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Captives often suffered from privation on voyages to North Africa if taken at a distance. Those who survived the journeys were often forced to walk through town as they were taken to slave auctions. The slaves typically had to stand from eight in the morning until two in the afternoon while buyers viewed them.\nNext came the auction, where the townspeople would bid on the captives they wanted to purchase and once that was over, the governor of Algiers (the Dey) had the chance to purchase any slave he wanted for the price they were sold at the auction. During the auctions the slaves would be forced to run and jump around to show their strength and stamina. After purchase, the captives would either be held for ransom, or be put to work. Slaves were used for a wide variety of jobs, from hard manual labor to housework (the job assigned to most women slaves). At night the slaves were put into prisons called \'<b><a href="/wiki/Bagnio" title="Bagnio">bagnios</a></b>\' (derived from the Italian word <i>"bagno"</i> for <i>public bath</i>, inspired by the Turks\' use of Roman baths at Constantinople as prisons),<sup id="cite_ref-BAGNO_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BAGNO-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> which were often hot and overcrowded. However, these bagnios began improving by the 18th century. Some bagnios had chapels, hospitals, shops, and bars run by captives, though such amenities remained uncommon.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galley_slaves">Galley slaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Galley slaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although the conditions in bagnios were harsh, they were better than those endured by <a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">galley</a> slaves. Most Barbary galleys were at sea for around eighty to a hundred days a year, but when the slaves assigned to them were on land, they were forced to do hard manual labor. There were exceptions: "galley slaves of the Ottoman Sultan in Constantinople would be permanently confined to their galleys, and often served extremely long terms, averaging around nineteen years in the late seventeenth-century and early eighteenth-century periods. These slaves rarely got off the galley but lived there for years."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> During this time, rowers were shackled and chained where they sat, and never allowed to leave. Sleeping (which was limited), eating, defecation and urination took place at the seat to which they were shackled. There were usually five or six rowers on each oar. Overseers would walk back and forth and whip slaves considered not to be working hard enough.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a to the town by King <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> in 1526</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Famous_Barbary_corsairs">Famous Barbary corsairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Famous Barbary corsairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to historian <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Tinniswood" title="Adrian Tinniswood">Adrian Tinniswood</a>, the most notorious corsairs were English and European renegades who had learned their trade as <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a>, and who moved to the Barbary Coast during peacetime to pursue their trade. These outcasts brought up-to-date naval expertise to the piracy business, and enabled the corsairs to make long-distance slave-catching raids as far away as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Toll_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The English corsair <a href="/wiki/Henry_Mainwaring" title="Henry Mainwaring">Henry Mainwaring</a> later returned to England after gaining a royal pardon. He was knighted, elected to Parliament, and appointed a vice admiral of the Royal Navy.<sup id="cite_ref-Toll_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Barbarossa_brothers">The Barbarossa brothers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Barbarossa brothers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span id="Oru.C3.A7_Barbarossa"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Oru\xc3\xa7_Barbarossa">Oru\xc3\xa7 Barbarossa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Oru\xc3\xa7 Barbarossa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aruj" class="mw-redirect" title="Aruj">Aruj</a></div>\n<p>The most famous of the corsairs in North Africa were brothers Oru\xc3\xa7 and H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r Hayreddin. They, and two less well-known brothers, all became Barbary corsairs; they were called the Barbarossas (Italian for Redbeards) after the red beard of Oru\xc3\xa7, the eldest. Oru\xc3\xa7 captured the island of Djerba for the Ottoman Empire in 1502 or 1503. He often attacked Spanish territories on the coast of North Africa; during one failed attempt in 1512 he lost his left arm to a cannonball. The eldest Barbarossa also went on a rampage through Algiers in 1516, and captured the town with the help of the Ottoman Empire. He executed the ruler of Algiers and everybody he suspected would oppose him, including local rulers. He was finally captured and killed by the Spanish in 1518, and put on display.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="H.C4.B1z.C4.B1r_Hayreddin_Barbarossa"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r_Hayreddin_Barbarossa">H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r Hayreddin Barbarossa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r Hayreddin Barbarossa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa" title="Hayreddin Barbarossa">Hayreddin Barbarossa</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg/220px-Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg/330px-Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg/440px-Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg 2x" data-file-width="614" data-file-height="307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Barbaros_minyat%C3%BCr.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ottoman admiral <a href="/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa" title="Hayreddin Barbarossa">Hayreddin Barbarossa</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Oru\xc3\xa7, based mainly on land, was not the best-known of the Barbarossas. His youngest brother H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r (later called Hayreddin or Kheir ed-Din) was a more traditional corsair. He was a capable engineer and spoke at least six languages. He dyed the hair of his head and beard with <a href="/wiki/Henna#Hair_dye" title="Henna">henna</a> to redden it like Oru\xc3\xa7\'s. After capturing many crucial coastal areas, Hayreddin was appointed admiral-in-chief of the Ottoman sultan\'s fleet. Under his command the Ottoman Empire was able to gain and keep control of the eastern Mediterranean for over thirty years. Barbaros H\xc4\xb1z\xc4\xb1r Hayreddin Pasha died in 1546 of a fever, possibly the plague.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Captain_Jack_Ward">Captain Jack Ward</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Captain Jack Ward">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jack_Ward" title="Jack Ward">Jack Ward</a></div>\n<p>English corsair Jack, or John, Ward was once called "beyond doubt the greatest scoundrel that ever sailed from England" by the English ambassador to Venice. Ward was a privateer for Queen Elizabeth during her war with Spain; after the end of the war, he became a corsair. With some associates he captured a ship in about 1603 and sailed it to Tunis; he and his crew converted to <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. He was successful and became rich. He introduced heavily armed square-rigged ships, used instead of galleys, to the North African area, a major reason for the Barbary\'s future dominance of the Mediterranean. He died of plague in 1622.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sayyida_al-Hurra">Sayyida al-Hurra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_pirates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sayyida al-Hurra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sayyida_al_Hurra" title="Sayyida al Hurra">Sayyida al Hurra</a></div>\n<p>Sayyida al-Hurra was a female <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> cleric, merchant, governor of <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A9touan" title="T\xc3\xa9touan">T\xc3\xa9touan</a>, and later queen of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mernissi_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mernissi-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dict_Morocco_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dict_Morocco-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> She was born around 1485 in the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Emirate of Granada</a>, but was forced to flee to Morocco when she was very young to escape the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>. In Morocco, she gathered a crew largely of exiled <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>, and launched pirate expeditions against Spain and Portugal to avenge the Reconquista, protect Morocco from Christian pirates, and seek riches and glory. She co-founded the Barbary Corsairs with her allies the Barbarossa brothers, who divided the Mediterranean between them\xe2\x80\x94the Barbarossas and their Ottoman fleet operating in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean" title="Eastern Mediterranean">east</a>, and Sayyida al-Hurra and her Moorish and North-African pirates operating in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Mediterranean">west</a>. Sayyida al-Hurra became wealthy and renowned enough for the Sultan of Morocco, <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Abbas_Ahmad_ibn_Muhammad" title="Abu al-Abbas Ahmad ibn Muhammad">Ahmad al-Wattasi</a> to make her his queen. Notably, however, she refused to marry in his capital of <a href="/wiki/Fez,_Morocco" title="Fez, Morocco">Fez</a>, and would not get married but in T\xc3\xa9touan, of which she was governor. This was the first and only time in history that a Moroccan monarch had married away from his capital.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_famous_Barbary_corsairs">Other famous Barbary title="Livorno">Livorno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Barbary corsairs are protagonists in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Le_pantere_di_Algeri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Le pantere di Algeri (page does not exist)">Le pantere di Algeri</a> (the panthers of Algiers)</i> by <a They were featured in a number of other noted novels, including <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe" title="Robinson Crusoe">Robinson Crusoe</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Count_of_Monte_Cristo" title="The Count of Monte Cristo">The Count of Monte Cristo</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas,_p%C3%A8re" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexandre Dumas, p\xc3\xa8re">Alexandre Dumas, p\xc3\xa8re</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wind_in_the_Willows" title="The Wind in the Willows">The Wind in the Willows</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Grahame" title="Kenneth Grahame">Kenneth Grahame</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sea_Hawk" title="The Sea Hawk">The Sea Hawk</a></i> and the <i>Sword of Islam</i> by <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Sabatini" title="Rafael Sabatini">Rafael Sabatini</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Algerine_Captive" title="The Algerine Captive">The Algerine Captive</a></i> by <a Cycle</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Neal_Stephenson" title="Neal Stephenson">Neal Stephenson</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Walking_Drum" title="The Walking Drum">The Walking Drum</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Lamour" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis Lamour">Louis Lamour</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Dolittle" title="Doctor Dolittle">Doctor Dolittle</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Lofting" Cussler</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Angelique_(French_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Angelique (French series)">Ang\xc3\xa9lique in Barbary</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Anne_Golon" title="Anne Golon">Anne Golon</a>. <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a>, the Spanish author, was captive for five years as a slave in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bagnio" title="Bagnio">bagnio</a></i> of Algiers, and reflected his experience in some of his fictional (but not directly autobiographical) writings, including the Captive\'s tale in <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">Don Quixote</a></i>, his two plays set in Algiers, <i>El Trato de Argel</i> (The Treaty of Algiers) and <i>Los Ba\xc3\xb1os de Argel</i> (The Baths of Algiers), and episodes in a number of other works. In <a href="/wiki/Mozart" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>\'s opera <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Entf%C3%BChrung_aus_dem_Serail" title="Die Entf\xc3\xbchrung aus dem Serail">Die Entf\xc3\xbchrung aus dem Serail</a></i> (a <a href="/wiki/Singspiel" title="Singspiel">Singspiel</a>), two European ladies are discovered in a Turkish harem, presumably captured by Barbary corsairs. <a href="/wiki/Gioachino_Rossini" title="Gioachino Rossini">Rossini</a>\'s opera <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Italiana_in_Algeri" class="mw-redirect" title="L&#39;Italiana in Algeri">L\'Italiana in Algeri</a></i> is based on the capture of several slaves by Barbary corsairs led by the <a href="/wiki/Bey" title="Bey">bey</a> of <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>.\n</p><p>One of the stereotypical features of a pirate as portrayed in popular culture, the <a href="/wiki/Eyepatch" title="Eyepatch">eye patch</a>, may have been partially derived from the <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> corsair <a href="/wiki/Rahmah_ibn_Jabir_al-Jalahimah" class="mw-redirect" title="Rahmah ibn Jabir al-Jalahimah">Rahmah ibn Jabir al-Jalahimah</a>, who wore a patch after losing an eye in battle in the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Johnny_England&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Little Johnny England (page does not exist)">Little Johnny England</a></i> song, "Lily of Barbary", tells the story of an English man who is enslaved by Barbary corsairs and sold as a slave in Algiers. He is freed when his master dies. He becomes a merchant and buys the freedom of another English slave girl.\n</p><p>The song <i><a href="/wiki/Coast_of_High_Barbaree" title="Coast of High Barbaree">Coast of High Barbaree</a></i> tells of a sailing ship that came across a pirate ship off the Barbary Coast and defeated the pirates, who were left to drown.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
