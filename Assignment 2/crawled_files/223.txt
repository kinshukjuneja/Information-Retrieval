style="line-height:1.35em;">\n<code>51-AAA-hc</code></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Guern\xc3\xa9siais</b>, also known as <i><b>Dg\xc3\xa8rn\xc3\xa9siais</b></i>, <b>Guernsey French</b>, and <b>Guernsey Norman French</b>, is the variety of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman language</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>. It is sometimes known on the island simply as  "<a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">patois</a>". As one of the <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d&#39;o\xc3\xafl">langues d\'o\xc3\xafl</a>, it has its roots in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, but has had strong influence from both <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> at different points in its history.\n</p><p>There is <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutual intelligibility</a> (with some difficulty) with <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais" title="J\xc3\xa8rriais">J\xc3\xa8rriais</a> speakers from <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and Continental Norman speakers from <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>. Guern\xc3\xa9siais most closely resembles the Norman <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cotentinais" title="Cotentinais">Cotentinais</a> spoken at <a href="/wiki/La_Hague" title="La Hague">la Hague</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">Cotentin Peninsula</a>.\n</p><p>Guern\xc3\xa9siais has been influenced less by <a href="/wiki/Standard_French" title="Standard French">Standard French</a> than J\xc3\xa8rriais, but conversely has been influenced to a greater extent by <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>. New words have been imported for modern phenomena "le bike", "le gas-cooker".\n</p><p>There is a rich tradition of poetry in the Guernsey language. Guernsey songs were inspired by the sea, by colourful figures of speech, by traditional folk-lore, as well as by the natural environment of the island. The island\'s greatest poet was <a href="/wiki/George_M%C3%A9tivier" title="George M\xc3\xa9tivier">George M\xc3\xa9tivier</a> (1790\xe2\x80\x931881), a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a>, who influenced and inspired local poets to print and publish their traditional poetry. M\xc3\xa9tivier blended local place-names, bird and animal names, traditional sayings and orally transmitted fragments of medieval poetry to create his <i>Rimes Guernesiaises</i> (1831). <a href="/wiki/Denys_Corbet" title="Denys Corbet">Denys Corbet</a> (1826\xe2\x80\x931910) was considered the "Last Poet" of Guernsey French and published many poems in his day in his native tongue in the island newspaper and privately.\n</p><p>Wrote M\xc3\xa9tivier, <i>Que l\'lingo seit bouan ou mauvais / J\'p\xc3\xa2lron coum\'nou p\xc3\xa2lait autefais</i> (whether the "lingo" be good or bad, I\xe2\x80\x99m going to speak as we used to speak).\n</p><p>The most recent dictionary of Guern\xc3\xa9siais, <cite class="citation book"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=-S2ynAEACAAJ"><i>Dictiounnaire Angllais-guernesiais</i></a>. The Commission has operated since Liberation Day, 9 May 2013.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guern%C3%A9siais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Guernsey poet <a href="/wiki/George_M%C3%A9tivier" title="George M\xc3\xa9tivier">George M\xc3\xa9tivier</a> (1790\xe2\x80\x931881) \xe2\x80\x93 nicknamed the <i>Guernsey <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Burns</a></i>, was the first to produce a <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">dictionary</a> of the Norman language in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>, the <i>Dictionnaire Franco-Normand</i> (1870). This established the first standard <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> \xe2\x80\x93 later modified and modernised. Among his poetical works are <i>Rimes Guernesiaises</i> published in 1831.</li>\n<li>Prince <a href="/wiki/Louis_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Louis Lucien Bonaparte">Louis Lucien Bonaparte</a> published the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> by <a href="/wiki/George_M%C3%A9tivier" title="George M\xc3\xa9tivier">George M\xc3\xa9tivier</a> in Dg\xc3\xa8rn\xc3\xa9siais in London in 1863 as part of his philological research.</li>\n<li>Like M\xc3\xa9tivier, <a href="/wiki/Tam_Lenfestey" title="Tam Lenfestey">Tam Lenfestey</a> (1818\xe2\x80\x931885) published poetry in Guernsey newspapers and in book form.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Denys_Corbet" title="Denys Corbet">Denys Corbet</a> (1826\xe2\x80\x931909) described himself as the <i>Dra\xc3\xafn Rimeux</i> (last poet), but literary production continued. Corbet is best known for his poems, especially the <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a> <i>L\'Touar de Guernesy</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Picaresque" class="mw-redirect" title="Picaresque">picaresque</a> tour of the <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parishes</a> of Guernsey. As editor of the French-language newspaper <i>Le Bailliage</i>, he also wrote <a href="/wiki/Feuilleton" title="Feuilleton">feuilletons</a> in Dg\xc3\xa8rn\xc3\xa9siais under the <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a> <i>Badlagoule</i> ("chatterbox"). In 2009 the island held a special exhibition in the Forest Parish on Corbet and his work acknowledging the centenary of his death and unveiling a contemporary portrait painting of the artist by Christian Corbet a cousin to Denys Corbet.</li>\n<li>Thomas Martin (1839\xe2\x80\x931921) translated into Guern\xc3\xa9siais the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, the plays of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, twelve plays by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Corneille" title="Pierre Corneille">Pierre Corneille</a>, three plays by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Corneille" title="Thomas Corneille">Thomas Corneille</a>, twenty seven plays by <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Moli\xc3\xa8re">Moli\xc3\xa8re</a>, twenty plays by <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a> and <i>The Spanish Student</i> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Mahy" title="Thomas Henry Mahy">Thomas Henry Mahy</a> (1862\xe2\x80\x9321 April 1936) wrote <i>Dires et Pens\xc3\xa9es du Courtil Poussin</i>, a regular column in <i>La Gazette Officielle de Guernesey</i>, from 1916. A collection was published in booklet form in 1922. He was still publishing occasional pieces of poetry and prose by the start of the 1930s.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Alfred_Grut" title="Thomas Alfred Grut">Thomas Alfred Grut</a> (1852\xe2\x80\x931933) published <i>Des lures guernesiaises</i> in 1927, once again a collection of newspaper columns. He also translated some of the J\xc3\xa8rriais stories of <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Le_Sueur_Mourant" title="Philippe Le Sueur Mourant">Philippe Le Sueur Mourant</a> into Dg\xc3\xa8rn\xc3\xa9siais.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Marjorie_Ozanne" title="Marjorie Ozanne">Marjorie Ozanne</a> (1897\xe2\x80\x931973) wrote stories, published in the <i>Guernsey Evening Press</i> between 1949 and 1965. Some earlier pieces can be found in <i>La Gazette de Guernesey</i> in the 1920s.</li>\n<li>Ken Hill translated many of Marjorie Ozanne\'s short stories and poems into English with the Guernsey accent of the early 20th century.  The work was published by the Guernsey society.</li>\n<li>M\xc3\xa9tivier\'s dictionary was superseded by <a href="/wiki/Marie_de_Garis" title="Marie de Garis">Marie de Garis</a>\' (1910\xe2\x80\x932010) <i>Dictiounnaire Angllais-Guern\xc3\xa9siais</i>; first edition published in 1967, supplements 1969 and 1973, third edition 1982.</li>\n<li>When the Channel Islands were invaded by <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Dg\xc3\xa8rn\xc3\xa9siais experienced a minor revival. Many Guernsey people did not always wish the occupying forces to understand what they were saying, especially as some of the soldiers had knowledge of English.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a> includes the odd word of Dg\xc3\xa8rn\xc3\xa9siais in some of his Channel Island novels. Hugo\'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Toilers_of_the_Sea" title="Toilers of the Sea">Toilers of the Sea</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Les Travailleurs de la mer</i>), is credited with introducing the Guernesiais word for octopus <i>pieuvre</i> into the French language (standard French for octopus is <i>poulpe</i>).</li>\n<li>A collection of short stories <i>P\'tites Lures Guern\xc3\xa9siaises</i> (in Guern\xc3\xa9siais with parallel English translation) by various writers was published in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bible_translations">Bible translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guern%C3%A9siais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bible translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/George_M%C3%A9tivier" title="George M\xc3\xa9tivier">George M\xc3\xa9tivier</a> translated the Gospel of Matthew into Guern\xc3\xa9siais and it was published in London in 1863.  This is now online.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Thomas Martin translated the whole Bible into Guern\xc3\xa9siais and this has never been published.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
