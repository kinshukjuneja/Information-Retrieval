class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Saint Anthony</b> or <b>Antony</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xe1\xbc\x88\xce\xbd\xcf\x84\xcf\x8e\xce\xbd\xce\xb9\xce\xbf\xcf\x82</span> <i>Ant\xe1\xb9\x93nios</i>; <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Antonius</i>; <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic</a>: <span lang="cop">\xe2\xb2\x80\xe2\xb2\x83\xe2\xb2\x83\xe2\xb2\x81 \xe2\xb2\x80\xe2\xb2\x9b\xe2\xb2\xa7\xe2\xb2\xb1\xe2\xb2\x9b\xe2\xb2\x93</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a>&#160;</small>&#39;\'Avva Antoni\'&#39;) January 12, 251 \xe2\x80\x93 January 17, 356 was a <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism" title="Christian monasticism">monk</a> from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, revered since his death as a <a href="/wiki/Christian_saint" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian saint">saint</a>. He is distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anthony_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Saint Anthony (disambiguation)">other saints named Anthony</a> such as <span class="nowrap"><b>Anthony of Padua</b></span>, by various epithets of his own: <span class="nowrap"><b>Anthony the Great</b></span>, <span class="nowrap"><b>Anthony of Egypt</b></span>, <span class="nowrap"><b>Anthony the Abbot</b>,</span> <span class="nowrap"><b>Anthony of the Desert</b>,</span> <span class="nowrap"><b>Anthony the Anchorite</b>,</span> and <span class="nowrap"><b>Anthony of Thebes</b>.</span> For his importance among the <a href="/wiki/Desert_Fathers" title="Desert Fathers">Desert Fathers</a> and to all later <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism" title="Christian monasticism">Christian monasticism</a>, he is also known as the <span class="nowrap"><b>Father of All Monks</b></span>. His <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_the_saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Calendar of the saints">feast day</a> is celebrated on January 17 among the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> churches and on <a href="/wiki/Month_of_Tobi" class="mw-redirect" title="Month of Tobi">Tobi</a>&#160;22 in the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_calendar" title="Egyptian calendar">Egyptian calendar</a> used by the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Coptic Orthodox Church">Coptic Church</a>.\n</p><p>The biography of Anthony\'s life by <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius of Alexandria</a> helped to spread the concept of Christian <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">monasticism</a>, particularly in Western Europe via its <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> translations. He is often erroneously considered the first Christian monk, but as his biography and other sources make clear, there were many ascetics before him. Anthony was, however, the first to go into the wilderness (about <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;270), which seems to have contributed to his renown.<sup id="cite_ref-Endsjo2008_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endsjo2008-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Accounts of Anthony enduring supernatural temptation during his sojourn in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Desert" title="Eastern Desert">Eastern Desert</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> inspired the often-repeated subject of <a href="/wiki/The_temptation_of_St._Anthony_in_visual_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="The temptation of St. Anthony in visual arts">the temptation of St. Anthony</a> in Western art and literature.\n</p><p>Anthony is appealed to against infectious diseases, particularly skin diseases. In the past, many such afflictions, including <a href="/wiki/Ergotism" title="Ergotism">ergotism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erysipelas" title="Erysipelas">erysipelas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Herpes_zoster" class="mw-redirect" title="Herpes zoster">shingles</a>, were referred to as <i>St. Anthony\'s fire</i>.\n</p><p><span id="History"></span><span id="Biography"></span>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1878-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He then left to live an <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">ascetic</a> life,<sup style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1480</span></div></div></div>\n<p>For the next fifteen years, Anthony remained in the area,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1911_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1911-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> spending the first years as the disciple of another local <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> There are various legends associating Anthony with pigs: one is that he worked as a <a href="/wiki/Swineherd" title="Swineherd">swineherd</a> during this period.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Anthony is sometimes considered the first monk,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1911_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1911-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  and the first to initiate solitary desertification,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> but there were others before him. There were already <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">ascetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_religion" title="Ancient Greek religion">pagan</a> <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermits</a> (the <i><a href="/wiki/Therapeutae" title="Therapeutae">Therapeutae</a></i>), and loosely organized <a href="/wiki/Cenobitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cenobitic">cenobitic</a> communities were described by the <a href="/wiki/Hellenization" title="Hellenization">Hellenized</a> <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> <a href="/wiki/Jewish_philosophy" title="Jewish philosophy">philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Philo_of_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Philo of Alexandria">Philo of Alexandria</a> in the 1st century&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span> as long established in the harsh environment of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mareotis" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Mareotis">Lake Mareotis</a> and in other less accessible regions. Philo opined that "this class of persons may be met with in many places, for both Greece and barbarian countries want to enjoy whatever is perfectly good."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Christian ascetics such as <a href="/wiki/Thecla" title="Thecla">Thecla</a> had likewise retreated to isolated locations at the outskirts of cities. Anthony is notable for having decided to surpass this tradition and headed out into the desert proper. He left for the alkaline <a href="/wiki/Nitrian_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrian Desert">Nitrian Desert</a> (later the location of the noted monasteries of <a href="/wiki/Nitria_(monastic_site)" title="Nitria (monastic site)">Nitria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kellia" title="Kellia">Kellia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scetis" class="mw-redirect" title="Scetis">Scetis</a>) on the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Desert" title="Libyan Desert">Western Desert</a> about 95&#160;km (59&#160;mi) west of <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Alexandria" title="Early centers of Christianity">Alexandria</a>. He remained there for 13 years.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Athanasius" class="mw-redirect" title="Athanasius">Athanasius</a>, the devil fought Anthony by afflicting him with boredom, laziness, and the phantoms of women, which he overcame by the power of prayer, providing a theme for <a href="/wiki/Christian_art" title="Christian art">Christian art</a>. After that, he moved to a tomb, where he resided and closed the door on himself, depending on some local villagers who brought him food. When the devil perceived his ascetic life and his intense worship, he was envious and beat him mercilessly, leaving him unconscious. When his friends from the local village came to visit him and found him in this condition, they carried him to a church.\n</p><p>After he recovered, he made a second effort and went back into the desert to a father mountain by the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> called Pispir (now Der-el-Memun), opposite <a href="/wiki/Faiyum" title="Faiyum">Arsinoe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1911_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1911-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> There he lived strictly enclosed in an old abandoned <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> fort for some 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Athanasius" class="mw-redirect" title="Athanasius">Athanasius</a>, the devil again resumed his war against Anthony, only this time the phantoms were in the form of wild beasts, wolves, lions, snakes, and scorpions. They appeared as if they were about to attack him or cut him into pieces. But the saint would laugh at them scornfully and say, "If any of you have any authority over me, only one would have been sufficient to fight me." At his saying this, they disappeared as though in smoke. While in the fort he only communicated with the outside world by a crevice through which food would be passed and he would say a few words. Anthony would prepare a quantity of bread that would sustain him for six months. He did not allow anyone to enter his cell; whoever came to him stood outside and listened to his advice.\n</p><p>Then one day he emerged from the fort with the help of villagers, who broke down the door. By this time most had expected him to have wasted away or to have gone insane in his solitary confinement. Instead, he emerged healthy, serene, and enlightened. Everyone was amazed that he had been through these trials and emerged spiritually rejuvenated. He was hailed as a hero and from this time forth the legend of Anthony began to spread and grow. Anthony went to <a href="/wiki/Al_Fayyum" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Fayyum">Fayyum</a> and confirmed the brethren there in the <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> faith before returning to his fort.\n</p><p>Amid the <a href="/wiki/Diocletian_Persecutions" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocletian Persecutions">Diocletian Persecutions</a>, Anthony wished to become a <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyr</a> and in 311 went to <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>. He visited those who were imprisoned for the sake of <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a> and comforted them. When the Governor saw that he was confessing his <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> publicly, not caring what might happen to him, he ordered him not to show up in the city. However, the Saint did not heed his threats. He faced him and argued with him in order that he might arouse his anger so that he might be tortured and martyred, but it did not happen.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Father_of_Monks">Father of Monks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Father of Monks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:263px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antoniusaltaar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Antoniusaltaar.jpg/261px-Antoniusaltaar.jpg" width="261" height="252" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Antoniusaltaar.jpg/392px-Antoniusaltaar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Antoniusaltaar.jpg/522px-Antoniusaltaar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1446" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Antoniusaltaar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The former main altar of the hermitage church in <a href="/wiki/Warfhuizen" title="Warfhuizen">Warfhuizen</a> in the Netherlands with a mural of Anthony the Abbot and a <a href="/wiki/Reliquary" title="Reliquary">reliquary</a> with some of his <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a>. di Bologna">Pinacoteca Nazionale di Bologna</a></div></div></div>\n<p>At the end of the persecutions, Anthony returned to his old Roman fort. By this time, many more had heard of his sanctity and he had many more visitors than before. He saw these visits as interfering with his worship and went further into the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Desert" title="Eastern Desert">Eastern Desert</a>. He traveled for three days before reaching a small oasis with a spring and some <a href="/wiki/Palm_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Palm tree">palm trees</a> and chose to settle there. Disciples soon found him out and his number of visitors again continued to grow.\n</p><p>Anthony had not been the first ascetic or hermit, but he may properly be called the "Father of Monasticism" in Christianity,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1878_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1878-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> as he organized his disciples into a worshipful community and inspired similar withdrawn communities throughout Egypt and, following the spread of Athanasius\'s hagiography, the Greek and Roman world. His follower <a href="/wiki/Macarius_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Macarius the Great">Macarius the Great</a> was particularly active in continuing his legacy.\n</p><p>Anthony anticipated the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_St_Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of St Benedict">rule</a> of <a href="/wiki/Benedict_of_Nursia" title="Benedict of Nursia">Benedict</a> by about 200 years, engaging himself and his disciples in manual labor. Anthony himself cultivated a garden and wove <a href="/wiki/Juncus" title="Juncus">rush</a> mats. He and his disciples were regularly sought for words of enlightenment. These statements were later collected into the book of <i><a href="/wiki/Sayings_of_the_Desert_Fathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Sayings of the Desert Fathers">Sayings of the Desert Fathers</a></i>. Anthony himself is said to have spoken to those of a spiritual disposition personally, leaving the task of addressing the more worldly visitors to <a href="/wiki/Macarius_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Macarius the Great">Macarius</a>. On occasions, he would go to the monastery on the outskirts of the desert by the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> to visit the brethren, then return to his inner <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a>.\n</p><p>A background story of one of the surviving epistles, directed to <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_(emperor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I (emperor)">Constantine I</a>, recounts how the fame of Saint Anthony spread abroad and reached Emperor Constantine. The Emperor wrote to him offering praise and requesting prayers. The brethren were pleased with the Emperor\'s letter, but Anthony did not pay any attention to it, and he said to them, "The books of God, the King of Kings and the Lord of Lords, commands us every day, but we do not heed what they tell us, and we turn our backs on them." Under the persistence of the brethren who told him "Emperor Constantine loves the church", he accepted to write him a letter blessing him, and praying for the peace and safety of the empire and the church.\n</p><p>According to Athanasius, Saint Anthony heard a voice telling him "Go out and see." He went out and saw an <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angel</a> who wore a girdle with a cross, one resembling the holy Eskiem (<a href="/wiki/Tonsure" title="Tonsure">Tonsure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Schema_(Orthodoxy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Schema (Orthodoxy)">Schema</a>), and on his head was a head cover (Kolansowa). He was sitting while braiding palm leaves, then he stood up to pray, and again he sat to weave. A voice came to him saying, "Anthony, do this and you will rest." Henceforth, he started to wear this tunic that he saw, and began to weave palm leaves, and never was bored again. Saint Anthony prophesied about the persecution that was about to happen to the church and the control of the heretics over it, the church victory and its return to its former glory, and the end of the age. When <a href="/wiki/Macarius_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Macarius the Great">Saint Macarius</a> visited Anthony, Anthony clothed him with the monk\'s garb and foretold him what would happen to him. When the day drew near for the departure of <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Thebes" title="Paul of Thebes">Saint Paul the First Hermit</a> in the desert, Saint Anthony went to him and buried him, after clothing him in a tunic which was a present from <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">St Athanasius the Apostolic</a>, the 20th <a href="/wiki/List_of_Patriarchs_of_Alexandria" title="List of Patriarchs of Alexandria">Patriarch of Alexandria</a>.\n</p><p>In 338, he left the desert temporarily to visit Alexandria to help refute the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Arius" title="Arius">Arius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Although not particularly learned, Anthony was able to confound the Arians.<sup id="cite_ref-Gonz\xc3\xa1lez_1984_141_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonz\xc3\xa1lez_1984_141-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_days">Final days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Final days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When Saint Anthony felt that the day of his departure had approached, he commanded his disciples to give his staff to <a href="/wiki/Macarius_of_Egypt" title="Macarius of Egypt">Saint Macarius</a>, and to give one <a href="/wiki/Sheepskin_(material)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheepskin (material)">sheepskin</a> cloak to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Athanasius" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Athanasius">Saint Athanasius</a> and the other sheepskin cloak to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Serapion_of_Thmuis" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Serapion of Thmuis">Saint Serapion</a>, his disciple. He further instructed his disciples to bury his body in an unmarked, secret <a href="/wiki/Grave_(burial)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grave (burial)">grave</a>.\n</p><p>He probably spoke only his native language, <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Gonz\xc3\xa1lez_1984_141_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonz\xc3\xa1lez_1984_141-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but his sayings were spread in a <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> translation. He himself wrote letters in Coptic, seven of which are extant. His biography was written by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Athanasius" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Athanasius">Saint Athanasius</a> and titled <i>Life of Saint Anthony the Great</i>. Many stories are also told about him in various collections of sayings of the <a href="/wiki/Desert_Fathers" title="Desert Fathers">Desert Fathers</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:263px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_(Michelangelo).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg/261px-The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg" width="261" height="351" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg/392px-The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg/522px-The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_%28Michelangelo%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1785" data-file-height="2400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_(Michelangelo).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A copy by the young <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> after <a href="/wiki/The_Temptation_of_St_Anthony_(Schongauer)" title="The Temptation of St Anthony (Schongauer)">an engraving</a> by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schongauer" title="Martin Schongauer">Martin Schongauer</a> around 1487\xe2\x80\x939, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Torment_of_Saint_Anthony_(Michelangelo)" title="The Torment of Saint Anthony (Michelangelo)">The Torment of Saint Anthony</a></i>. Oil and tempera on panel. One of many artistic depictions of Saint Anthony\'s trials in the desert.</div></div></div>\n<p>Though Anthony himself did not organize or create a monastery, a community grew around him based on his example of living an <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">ascetic</a> and isolated life. Athanasius\' biography helped propagate Anthony\'s ideals. Athanasius writes, "For monks, the life of Anthony is a sufficient example of <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">asceticism</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Asceticism is a lifestyle characterized by abstinence from worldly pleasures, often for the purpose of pursuing spiritual goals.\n</p><p>The biography of Anthony\'s life by Athanasius of Alexandria helped to spread the concept of Christian monasticism, particularly in Western Europe via its Latin translations. He is often erroneously considered the first Christian monk, but as his biography and other sources make clear, there were many ascetics before him. Anthony was, however, the first to go into the wilderness (about AD 270), a geographical move that seems to have contributed to his renown.[6] Accounts of Anthony enduring supernatural temptation during his sojourn in the Eastern Desert of Egypt inspired the often-repeated subject of the temptation of St. Anthony in Western art and literature.\n</p><p>Anthony is appealed to against infectious diseases, particularly skin diseases. In the past, many such afflictions, including <a href="/wiki/Ergotism" title="Ergotism">ergotism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erysipelas" title="Erysipelas">erysipelas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shingles" title="Shingles">shingles</a>, were referred to as <i>St. Anthony\'s fire</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temptation">Temptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Temptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Temptation_of_Saint_Anthony_in_visual_arts" title="Temptation of Saint Anthony in visual arts">Temptation of Saint Anthony in visual arts</a></div>\n<p>Famously, Anthony is said to have faced a series of supernatural <a href="/wiki/Temptation" title="Temptation">temptations</a> during his pilgrimage to the desert. The first to report on the temptation was his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius of Alexandria</a>. It is possible these events, like the paintings, are full of rich metaphor or in the case of the animals of the desert, perhaps a vision or dream. Some of the stories included in Saint Anthony\'s biography are perpetuated now mostly in paintings, where they give an opportunity for artists to depict their more lurid or bizarre interpretations. Many artists, including <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schongauer" title="Martin Schongauer">Martin Schongauer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bosch" title="Hieronymus Bosch">Hieronymus Bosch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Tanning" title="Dorothea Tanning">Dorothea Tanning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">Max Ernst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonora_Carrington" title="Leonora Carrington">Leonora Carrington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dal\xc3\xad">Salvador Dal\xc3\xad</a>, have depicted these incidents from the life of Anthony; in prose, the tale was retold and embellished by <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Temptation_of_Saint_Anthony_(Flaubert)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Temptation of Saint Anthony (Flaubert)">The Temptation of Saint Anthony</a></i>.\n</p><p>Emphasis on these stories, however, did not really begin until the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, when the psychology of the individual became of greater interest.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-6" class="reference"><a his dream was a better Hermit than he.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Saint Anthony had been under the impression that he was the first person to ever dwell in the desert; however, due to the dream, Saint Anthony was called into the desert to find his "better", Saint Paul. On his way there, he ran into two <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demons</a> in the forms of a <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaur</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Satyr" title="Satyr">satyr</a>, which Western theology considers to have been temptations.\n</p><p>At any rate, he was stopped by the two demons and he asked them, "Who are you?" To that the satyr replied, "I am a corpse, one of those whom the heathen call satyrs, and by whom they are snared into idolatry." The satyr then tried to terrify the saint while the centaur acknowledged the overthrow of the gods. In the end, the centaur tried to show Saint Anthony the way to his destination while the satyr ended up asking for Saint Anthony\'s blessing.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silver_and_gold">Silver and gold</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Silver and gold">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another time Saint Anthony was travelling in the desert and found a plate of silver coins in his path.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demons_in_the_cave">Demons in the cave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Demons in the cave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Once, Saint Anthony tried hiding in a cave to escape the demons that plagued him. There were so many little demons in the cave though that Saint Anthony\'s servant had to carry him out because they had beaten him to death. When the hermits were gathered to Saint Anthony\'s corpse to mourn his death, Saint Anthony was revived. He demanded that his servants take him back to that cave where the demons had beaten him. When he got there he called out to the demons, and they came back as wild beasts to rip him to shreds. All of a sudden a bright light flashed, and the demons ran away. Saint Anthony knew that the light must have come from God, and he asked God where was he before when the demons attacked him. God replied, "I was here but I would see and abide to see thy battle, and because thou hast mainly fought and well maintained thy battle, I shall make thy name to be spread through all the world."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veneration">Veneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Veneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most of what is known about Saint Anthony comes from the <i>Life of Anthony</i>. Written in Greek around 360 by <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius of Alexandria</a>, it depicts Anthony as an illiterate and holy man who through his existence in a primordial landscape has an absolute connection to the divine truth, which always is in harmony with that of Athanasius as the biographer.<sup id="cite_ref-Endsjo2008_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endsjo2008-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Sometime before 374 it was translated into Latin by <a href="/wiki/Evagrius_of_Antioch" title="Evagrius of Antioch">Evagrius of Antioch</a>. The Latin translation helped the <i>Life</i> become one of the best known works of literature in the Christian world, a status it would hold through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  In addition to the <i>Life</i>, several surviving homilies and epistles of varying authenticity provide some additional autobiographical detail.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg/220px-Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg/330px-Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg/440px-Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="294" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vaantjeantoniuswarfhuizenkleiner_copy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pilgrimage banners from the shrine in <a href="/wiki/Warfhuizen" title="Warfhuizen">Warfhuizen</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Anthony had been secretly buried on the mountain-top where he had chosen to live. His remains were reportedly discovered in 361, and transferred to <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>. Some time later, they were taken from Alexandria to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, so that they might escape the destruction being perpetrated by invading <a href="/wiki/Saracen" title="Saracen">Saracens</a>. In the eleventh century, the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine emperor">Byzantine emperor</a> gave them to the <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Count</a> Jocelin. Jocelin had them transferred to La-Motte-Saint-Didier, which was then renamed <a href="/wiki/Saint-Antoine-en-Dauphin%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint-Antoine-en-Dauphin\xc3\xa9">Saint-Antoine-en-Dauphin\xc3\xa9</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> There, Anthony is credited with assisting in a number of miraculous healings, primarily from <a href="/wiki/Ergotism" title="Ergotism">ergotism</a>, which became known as "St. Anthony\'s Fire". He was credited by two local noblemen of assisting them in recovery from the disease. They then founded the <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Brothers_of_St._Anthony" title="Hospital Brothers of St. Anthony">Hospital Brothers of St. Anthony</a> in honor of him, who specialized in nursing the victims of skin diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Veneration of Anthony in the East is more restrained. There are comparatively few icons and paintings of him. He is, however, regarded as the "first master of the desert and the pinnacle of holy monks", and there are monastic communities of the Maronite, Chaldean, and Orthodox churches which state that they follow his monastic rule.<sup id="cite_ref-ButlerLives1991_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ButlerLives1991-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, Anthony, along with <a href="/wiki/Quirinus_of_Neuss" title="Quirinus of Neuss">Quirinus of Neuss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Cornelius" title="Pope Cornelius">Cornelius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hubertus" title="Hubertus">Hubertus</a>, was venerated as one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Holy_Marshals" title="Four Holy Marshals">Four Holy Marshals</a> (<i>Vier Marsch\xc3\xa4lle Gottes</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coptic_literature">Coptic literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_the_Great&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11"
