href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, a <b>monopsony</b> (from Ancient Greek \xce\xbc\xcf\x8c\xce\xbd\xce\xbf\xcf\x82 (<i>m\xc3\xb3nos</i>) "single" + \xe1\xbd\x80\xcf\x88\xcf\x89\xce\xbd\xce\xaf\xce\xb1 href="/w/index.php?title=Monopsony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Monopsony theory was developed by economist <a href="/wiki/Joan_Robinson" title="Joan Robinson">Joan Robinson</a> in her book <i>The Economics of Imperfect Competition</i> (1933). Economists use the term "monopsony power" in a manner similar to "monopoly power" as a shorthand reference for a scenario in which there is one dominant power in the buying relationship, so that power is able to set prices to maximize profits not subject to competitive constraints. Monopsony power exists when one buyer faces little competition from other buyers for that labor or good, so they are able to set wages and prices for the labor or goods they are buying at a level lower than would be the case in a competitive market. A classic theoretical example is a <a href="/wiki/Mining_town" class="mw-redirect" title="Mining town">mining town</a>, where the company that owns the mine is able to set wages low since they face no competition from other employers in hiring workers, because they are the only employer in the town, and geographic isolation or obstacles prevent workers from seeking employment in other locations. Other more current examples may include school districts where teachers have little mobility across districts. In such cases the district faces little competition from other schools in hiring teachers, giving the district increased power when negotiating employment terms.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Alternative terms are <a href="/wiki/Oligopsony" title="Oligopsony">oligopsony</a> or monopsonistic competition.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monopsony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The term was first introduced by <a href="/wiki/Joan_Robinson" title="Joan Robinson">Joan Robinson</a> in her influential<sup id="cite_ref-RobinsonAssessments_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobinsonAssessments-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> book, <i>The Economics of Imperfect Competition</i>, published in 1933.  Robinson credited classics scholar <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Hallward" title="Bertrand Hallward">Bertrand Hallward</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_University_of_Cambridge" class="mw-redirect" title="The University of Cambridge">the University of Cambridge</a> with coining the term.<sup id="cite_ref-jep_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jep-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Static_monopsony_in_a_labor_market">Static monopsony in a labor market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monopsony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Static monopsony in a labor market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg/300px-Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg.png" width="300" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg/450px-Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg/600px-Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="217" data-file-height="217" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Monopsony-static-partial-equilibrium.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A monopsonist employer maximizes profits by choosing the employment level <i>L</i>, that equates the marginal revenue product (<i>MRP</i>) to the marginal cost <i>MC</i>, at point <i>A</i>. In the diagram, this leads to an <i>MC</i> curve that is <i>above</i> the labor supply curve <i>S</i>.\n</p><p>The first-order condition for maximum profit is then satisfied at point <i>A</i> of the diagram, where the <i>MC</i> and <i>MRP</i>&#160; curves intersect. This determines the profit-maximising employment as <i>L</i> on the horizontal axis. The corresponding wage <i>w</i> is then obtained from the supply curve, through point <i>M</i>.\n</p><p>The monopsonistic equilibrium at <i>M</i> can be contrasted with the equilibrium that would obtain under competitive conditions. Suppose a competitor employer entered the market and offered a wage higher than that at <i>M</i>. Then every employee of the first employer would choose instead to work for the competitor. Moreover, the competitor would gain all the former profits of the first employer, minus a less-than-offsetting amount from the wage increase of the first employer\'s employees, plus profits arising from additional employees who decided to work in the market because of the wage increase. But the first employer would respond by offering an even higher wage, poaching the new rival\'s employees, and so forth. As a result, a group of perfectly competitive firms would be forced, through <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competition</a>, to intersection <i>C</i> rather than <i>M</i>. It is thus a measure of the <i>market failure</i> caused by monopsony.</div></div></div>\n<p>The lower employment and wages caused by monopsony power have two distinct effects on the economic welfare of the people involved. First, it redistributes welfare away from workers and to their employer(s). Secondly, it reduces the aggregate (or social) welfare enjoyed by both groups taken together, as the employers\' net gain is smaller than the loss inflicted on workers.\n</p><p>The diagram on the right illustrates both effects, using the standard approach based on the notion of <a href="/wiki/Economic_surplus" title="Economic surplus">economic surplus</a>. According to this notion, the workers\' economic surplus (or net gain from the exchange) is given by the area between the <i>S</i> curve and the horizontal line corresponding to the wage, up to the employment level. Similarly, the employers\' surplus is the area between the horizontal line corresponding to the wage and the <i>MRP</i> curve, up to the employment level. The <i>social</i> surplus is then the sum of these two areas.\n</p><p>Following such definitions, the grey rectangle, in the diagram, is the part of the competitive social surplus that has been redistributed from the workers to their employer(s) under monopsony. By contrast, the yellow triangle is the part of the competitive social surplus that has been lost by <i>both</i> parties, as a result of the monopsonistic restriction of employment. This is a net social loss and is called <i><a href="/wiki/Deadweight_loss" title="Deadweight loss">deadweight loss</a></i>. It is a measure of the market failure caused by monopsony power, through a wasteful misallocation of resources.\n</p><p>As the diagram suggests, the size of both effects increases with the difference between the marginal revenue product <i>MRP</i> and the market wage determined on the supply curve <i>S</i>. The market failure can only be addressed in one of two ways: either by breaking up the monopsony through anti-trust intervention, or by regulating the wage policy of firms. The most common kind of regulation is a binding <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a> higher than the monopsonistic wage.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minimum_wage">Minimum wage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monopsony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Minimum wage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg/300px-Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg.png" width="300" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg/450px-Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/da/Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg/600px-Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="217" data-file-height="217" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Monopsony-minimum-wage.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>With a binding minimum wage of <i>w<span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>\'&#160;</i> the marginal cost to the firm becomes the horizontal black <i>MC\'&#160;</i> line, and the firm <a href="/wiki/Profit_maximization" title="Profit maximization">maximises profits</a> (which it can do due to a lack of competition) at <i>A</i> with a higher employment <i>L<span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>\'.</i> However, in this example, the minimum wage is higher than the competitive one, leading to <i>involuntary unemployment</i> equal to the segment <i>AB</i>.</div></div></div>\n<p>A binding minimum wage can be introduced either directly by law or through collective bargaining laws requiring union membership.  While it is generally agreed that minimum wage price floors reduce employment<sup id="cite_ref-auto_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>, in the presence of monopsony power within the labor market the effect is reversed and a minimum wage <i>could</i> increase employment. \n</p><p>This effect is demonstrated in the diagram on the right.\n</p><p>Here the minimum wage is <i>w<span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>\',</i> higher than the monopsonistic <i>w</i>. Because of the binding effects of minimum wage and the excess supply of labor (as defined by the monopsony status), the marginal cost of labor for the firm becomes constant (the price of hiring an additional worker rather than the increasing cost as labor becomes more scarce)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>.  This means that the firm maximizes profit at the intersection of the new marginal cost line (MC\' in the diagram) and Marginal Revenue Product line (the additional revenue for selling one more unit)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>.  This is the point where it becomes more expensive to produce an additional item than is earned in revenue from selling that item.   \n</p><p>It should be noted that this condition is still inefficient compared to a competitive market.  The line segment represented by A-B shows that there are still workers who would like to find a job, but cannot due to the monopsonistic nature of this industry.  This would represent the unemployment rate for this industry. This illustrates the there will be deadweight loss in a monoposonistic labor environment regardless of minimum wage levels, however a minimum wage law can increase total employment within the industry.  \n</p><p><br />\nMore generally, a binding minimum wage modifies the form of the supply curve faced by the firm, which becomes:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span This is a highly remarkable result because it only follows under monopsony. Indeed, under competitive conditions any minimum wage higher than the market rate would actually <i>reduce</i> employment, according to classical economic models and the consensus of peer-reviewed work<sup id="cite_ref-auto_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>. Thus, spotting the effects on employment of newly introduced minimum wage regulations is among the indirect ways economists use to pin down monopsony power in selected labor markets.  This technique was used, for example in a series of studies looking at the American labor market that found monopsonies existed only in several specialized fields such as professional sports and college professors<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wage_discrimination">Wage discrimination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monopsony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wage discrimination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Just like a monopolist, a monopsonistic employer may find that its profits are maximized if it <i>discriminates</i> prices. In this case this means paying different wages to different groups of workers even if their MRP is the same, with lower wages paid to the workers who have a lower elasticity of supply of their labor to the firm.\n</p><p>Researchers have used this fact to explain at least part of the observed wage differentials whereby <a href="/wiki/Equal_pay_for_women" class="mw-redirect" title="Equal pay for women">women often earn less than men</a>, even after controlling for observed productivity differentials. Robinson\'s original application of monopsony (1938) was developed to explain wage differentials between equally productive women and men.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Ransom and Oaxaca (2004) found that women\'s wage elasticity is lower than that of men for employees at a grocery store chain in Missouri, controlling for other factors typically associated with wage determination.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Ransom and Lambson (2011) found that female teachers are paid less than male teachers due to differences in labor market mobility constraints facing women and men.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some authors have argued informally that, while this is so for <i>market</i> supply, the reverse may somehow be true of the supply to individual firms. In particular, Manning and others have shown that, in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Pay_Act_1970" title="Equal Pay Act 1970">UK Equal Pay Act</a>, implementation has led to higher employment of women. Since the Act was effectively minimum wage legislation for women, this might perhaps be interpreted as a symptom of monopsonistic discrimination.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_problems">Empirical problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monopsony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Empirical problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The simpler explanation of monopsony power in labor markets is barriers to entry on the demand side. Such barriers to entry would result in a limited number of companies competing for labor (oligopsony).  If the hypothesis was generally true, one would expect to find that wages decreased as firm size increased or, more accurately, as industry concentration increased.  However, numerous statistical studies document significant positive correlations between firm or establishment size and wages. These results are often explained as being the result of cross-industry competition.  For example, if there were only one fast food producer, that industry would be very consolidated.  But that company wouldn\'t be able to drive down wages via monopsonistic power if it were also competing against retail stores, construction, and other jobs utilizing the same labor skill set.  This finding is both intuitive (low-skilled labor can move more fluidly through different industries) and supported by the data which found that monopsony effects are limited to professional sports, and perhaps nursing, fields where skill sets limit moving to comparably paid other industries. <sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, monopsony power might also be due to circumstances affecting entry of workers on the supply side (like in the referenced case above), directly reducing the elasticity of labor supply to firms. Paramount among these are industry accreditation or licensing fees, regulatory constraints, training or education requirements, and the institutional factors that limit labor mobility between firms, including job protection legislation. \n</p><p>An alternative that has been suggested as a source of monopsony power is worker preferences over job characteristics. <sup id="cite_ref-ECJ_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ECJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jep_2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jep_2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Such job characteristics can include distance from work, type of work, location, the social environment at work, etc. If different workers have different preferences, employers could have local monopsony power over workers that strongly prefer working for them.\n</p><p>Empirical evidence of monopsony power has been relatively limited. In line with the considerations discussed above, but perhaps counter to common intuition, there is no observable monopsony power in low-skilled labor markets in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Though there has been at least one study finding monopsony power in Indonesia due to barriers to entry in developing countries. <sup id="cite_ref-CU_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CU-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Several studies expanding their view for monopsony power have found economic and labor mobility in the US precludes any detectable monopsony effects <sup id="cite_ref-ILRR_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ILRR-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> with the notable exceptions of professional sports<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and (with some disagreement <sup id="cite_ref-JLE_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JLE-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>) nursing. <sup id="cite_ref-JHE_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JHE-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HCAFR_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCAFR-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  Both of these industries have highly specialized labor conditions and are generally not substitutable.\n</p>\n<h2><span
