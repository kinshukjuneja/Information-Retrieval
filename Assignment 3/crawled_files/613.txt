search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Courts of England and Wales</b>, supported administratively by <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Courts_and_Tribunals_Service" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Courts and Tribunals Service">Her Majesty\'s Courts and Tribunals Service</a>, are the <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal</a> <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">courts</a> responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Administration_of_justice" title="Administration of justice">administration of justice</a> in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> does not have a single unified legal system&#8212;<a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> has one system, <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Scotland" title="Courts of Scotland">Scotland</a> another, and <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Courts of Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> a third. There are exceptions to this rule; for example in <a href="/wiki/Immigration_law" title="Immigration law">immigration law</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Asylum_and_Immigration_Tribunal" title="Asylum and Immigration Tribunal">Asylum and Immigration Tribunal</a>\'s jurisdiction covers the whole of the United Kingdom, while in <a href="/wiki/Employment_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Employment law">employment law</a> there is a single system of <a href="/wiki/Employment_tribunal" title="Employment tribunal">employment tribunals</a> for England, Wales, and Scotland but not Northern Ireland. Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Military_Courts_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Military Courts of the United Kingdom">Military Court Service</a> has jurisdiction over all members of the armed forces of the United Kingdom in relation to <a href="/wiki/Offences_against_military_law_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Offences against military law in the United Kingdom">offences against military law</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)" title="Court of Appeal (England and Wales)">Court of Appeal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a>, the <a href="/wiki/County_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="County Court">County Court</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Magistrates%27_court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Magistrates&#39; court (England and Wales)">magistrates\' courts</a> are administered by <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Courts_and_Tribunals_Service" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Courts and Tribunals Service">Her Majesty\'s Courts and Tribunals Service</a>, an executive agency of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Justice (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Justice</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Middlesex.guildhall.london.arp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_Guildhall" title="Middlesex navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> is the highest appeal court in almost all cases in England and Wales. Before the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a> this role was held by the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. The Supreme Court is also the highest court of appeal for <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a> matters, a role previously held by the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>.\n</p><p>The Supreme Court has a separate administration from the other courts of England and Wales, and its administration is under a Chief Executive who is appointed by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="President of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">President of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Senior_Courts_of_England_and_Wales">Senior Courts of England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Senior Courts of England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Senior Courts of England and Wales were originally created by the Judicature Acts as the "Supreme Court of Judicature". It was renamed the "Supreme Court of England and Wales" in 1981,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and again to the "Senior Courts of England and Wales" by the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a> (to distinguish it from the new <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>). It consists of the following courts:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a> (formally Her Majesty\'s Court of Appeal in England<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> (High Court, formally Her Majesty\'s High Court of Justice in England<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a></li></ul>\n<p>The Senior Courts of England and Wales, along with the Tribunals and other courts, are administered and supported by <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Courts_and_Tribunals_Service" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Courts and Tribunals Service">HM Courts and Tribunals Service</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_of_Appeal">Court of Appeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Court of data-file-width="2475" data-file-height="1470" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_the_court_system_of_England_and_Wales_2014.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic of court system for England and Wales</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal of England and Wales</a></div>\n<p>The Court of Appeal deals only with appeals from other courts or tribunals. The Court of Appeal consists of two divisions: the Civil Division hears appeals from the High Court and County Court and certain superior tribunals, while the Criminal Division may only hear <a href="/wiki/Appeals_from_the_Crown_Court" title="Appeals from the Crown Court">appeals from the Crown Court</a> connected with a trial on indictment (i.e., for a serious offence). Its decisions are binding on all courts, including itself, apart from the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Court">High Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a></div>\n<p>The High Court of Justice functions both as a civil <a href="/wiki/Trial_court" title="Trial court">court of first instance</a> and a criminal and civil <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">appellate court</a> for cases from the subordinate courts. It consists of three divisions: the Queen\'s Bench, the Chancery and the Family divisions. The divisions of the High Court are not separate courts, but have somewhat separate procedures and practices adapted to their purposes. Although particular kinds of cases will be assigned to each division depending on their subject matter, each division may exercise the jurisdiction of the High Court. However, beginning proceedings in the wrong division may result in a costs penalty.\n</p><p>The formation of The Business and Property Courts of England &amp; Wales within the High Court was announced in March 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and launched in London in July 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The courts would in future administer the specialist jurisdictions that had previously been administered in the Queen\'s Bench Division under the names of the Admiralty Court, the Commercial Court, and the Technology &amp; Construction Court, and under the Chancery Division\'s lists for Business, Company and Insolvency,  Competition, Intellectual Property, Revenue, and Trusts and Probate.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_Court">Crown Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crown Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg/220px-Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg/330px-Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg/440px-Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1760" data-file-height="1168" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Courts_Oxford_20060325.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="County Court">County Court</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Crown Court is a criminal court of both original and appellate jurisdiction which in addition handles a limited amount of civil business both at first instance and on appeal. It was established by the <a href="/wiki/Courts_Act_1971" title="Courts Act 1971">Courts Act 1971</a>. It replaced the <a href="/wiki/Assizes" title="Assizes">assizes</a> whereby High Court judges would periodically travel around the country hearing cases, and <a href="/wiki/Quarter_sessions" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter sessions">quarter sessions</a> which were periodic county courts. <a href="/wiki/The_Old_Bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="The Old Bailey">The Old Bailey</a> is the unofficial name of London\'s most famous criminal court, which is now part of the Crown Court. Its official name is the "Central Criminal Court". The Crown Court also hears appeals from <a href="/wiki/Magistrates%27_court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Magistrates&#39; court (England and Wales)">magistrates\' courts</a>.\n</p><p>The Crown Court is the only court in England and Wales that has the jurisdiction to try cases on indictment and when exercising such a role it is a superior court in that its judgments cannot be reviewed by the Administrative Court of the Queen\xe2\x80\x99s Bench Division of the High Court.\n</p><p>The Crown Court is an inferior court in respect of the other work it undertakes, viz. inter alia, appeals from the magistrates\xe2\x80\x99 courts and other tribunals.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subordinate_courts">Subordinate courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subordinate courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The most common subordinate courts in England and Wales are\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/County_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="County Court">County Court</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Family_Court_(England_and_Wales)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family Court (England and Wales)">Family Court</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Magistrates%27_court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Magistrates&#39; court (England and Wales)">Magistrates\' courts</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Youth_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Youth court">Youth courts</a></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="County_Court">County Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: County Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The County Court is a national court with a purely civil jurisdiction, sitting in 92 different towns and cities across England and Wales. As from 22 April 2014 there has been a single County Court for England and Wales where previously there was a series of courts.  The County Court is so named after the ancient sheriff\'s court held in each county, but it has no connection with it nor indeed was the jurisdiction of the county courts based on counties.\n</p><p>A County Court hearing is presided over by either a district or circuit judge and, except in a small minority of cases such as civil actions against the police, the judge sits alone as trier of fact and law without assistance from a jury. The old county courts\' divorce and family jurisdiction was passed on 22 April 2014 to the single Family Court.\n</p><p>Until unification in 2014, county courts were local courts in the sense that each one has an area over which certain kinds of jurisdiction, for example, proceedings for possession of land had to be started in the county court in whose district the property lay, but in general any county court in England and Wales could hear any action and claims were frequently transferred from court to court.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_Court">Family Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Family Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Family_Court_(England_and_Wales)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family Court (England and Wales)">Family Court</a> is a national Court and has jurisdiction to hear all Family cases in England and Wales. Local jurisdictional boundaries have disappeared and there is only one single jurisdiction for all family proceedings. The Family Court sits at many locations in England and Wales, and it usually sits at the County Courts and Magistrates Courts where family work was previously heard by county courts or <a href="/wiki/Family_proceedings_court" title="Family proceedings court">family proceedings courts</a>. Family Court judges are now more categories of judges who will be eligible to hear family cases including lay magistrates, district judges, circuit judges, and High Court judges from the Family Division.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Magistrates.27_and_youth_courts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Magistrates\'_and_youth_courts">Magistrates\' and youth courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Magistrates&#039; and youth courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Magistrates%27_court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Magistrates&#39; court (England and Wales)">Magistrates\' courts</a> are the criminal court where all criminal proceedings start. They are presided over by a bench of lay magistrates (a.k.a. <a href="/wiki/Justices_of_the_peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Justices of the peace">justices of the peace</a>), or a legally trained district judge (formerly known as a stipendiary magistrate), sitting in each <a href="/wiki/Local_justice_area" title="Local justice area">local justice area</a>. There are no <a href="/wiki/Jury" title="Jury">juries</a>. They have jurisdiction to hear minor criminal cases, as well as certain licensing appeals. Youth courts are run on similar lines to adult magistrates\' courts but deal with offenders aged between the ages of ten and seventeen inclusive. Youth courts are presided over by a specially trained subset of experienced adult magistrates or a district judge. Youth magistrates have a wider catalogue of disposals available to them for dealing with young offenders and often hear more serious cases against youths (which for adults would normally be dealt with by the Crown Court). Youth courts are not open to the public for observation, only the parties involved in a case being admitted.\n</p><p>Prior to the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Crime_and_Courts_Act_2013" title="Crime and Courts Act 2013">Crime and Courts Act 2013</a>, some magistrates\' courts were also a <a href="/wiki/Family_proceedings_court" title="Family proceedings court">family proceedings court</a> and heard Family law cases including care cases and they had the power to make adoption orders. Family cases are no longer heard by the Magistrates\' courts, instead being heard by the single Family Court established by the 2013 Act.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_courts_and_tribunals">Special courts and tribunals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Special courts and tribunals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tribunals_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Tribunals in the United Kingdom">Tribunals in the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_tribunals_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of tribunals in the United Kingdom">List of tribunals in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>In addition, there are many other specialist courts. These are often described as "tribunals" rather than courts, but the difference in name is meaningless. For example, an <a href="/wiki/Employment_Tribunal" class="mw-redirect" title="Employment Tribunal">Employment Tribunal</a> is an inferior court of record for the purposes of the law of <a href="/wiki/Contempt_of_court" title="Contempt of court">contempt of court</a>. In many cases there is a statutory right of appeal from a tribunal to a particular court or specially constituted appellate tribunal. In the absence of a specific appeals court, the only remedy from a decision of a tribunal may be via judicial review to the High Court, which will often be more limited in scope than an appeal.\n</p><p>Examples of specialist courts are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Employment_tribunal" title="Employment tribunal">Employment tribunals</a> (formerly industrial tribunals) with appeal to the <a href="/wiki/Employment_Appeal_Tribunal" title="Employment Appeal Tribunal">Employment Appeal Tribunal</a></li>\n<li>the Employment Appeal Tribunal, which is a superior court of record, and therefore not subject to judicial review, appeals go to the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/First-tier_Tribunal" title="First-tier Tribunal">First-tier Tribunal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Tribunal" title="Upper Tribunal">Upper Tribunal</a> established under the <a href="/wiki/Tribunals,_Courts_and_Enforcement_Act_2007" title="Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007">Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007</a> have absorbed the function of many pre-existing tribunals.</li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Coroners.27_courts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Coroners\'_courts">Coroners\' courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Coroners&#039; courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The post of <a href="/wiki/Coroner" title="Coroner">coroner</a> is ancient, dating from the 11th century, and coroners still sit today to determine the cause of death in situations where people have died in potentially suspicious circumstances, abroad, or in the care of central authority. They also have jurisdiction over <a href="/wiki/Treasure_trove" title="Treasure trove">treasure trove</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_courts">Ecclesiastical courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> is an established church (i.e. it is the official state church) and formerly had exclusive or non-exclusive subject matter jurisdiction over marriage and divorce cases, testamentary matters, <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamation</a>, and several other areas. Since the 19th century, the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_court" title="Ecclesiastical court">ecclesiastical courts</a> has narrowed principally to matters of church property and errant clergy. Each Diocese has a \'Chancellor\' (either a <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barrister</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solicitor" title="Solicitor">solicitor</a>) who acts as a judge in the <a href="/wiki/Consistory_court" title="Consistory court">consistory court</a> of the diocese. The <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">Bishop</a> no longer has the right to preside personally, as he formerly did.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Appeals lie to the <a href="/wiki/Arches_Court" title="Arches Court">Arches Court</a> (in Canterbury) and the <a href="/wiki/Chancery_Court_of_York" title="Chancery Court of York">Chancery Court</a> (in York), and from them to the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Ecclesiastical_Causes_Reserved" title="Court of Ecclesiastical Causes Reserved">Court of Ecclesiastical Causes Reserved</a> (CECR). From the CECR appeals lie to the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_courts">Military courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Military courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Military_courts_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Military courts of the United Kingdom">Military courts of the United Kingdom</a> (including the Summary Appeal Court, Service Civilian Court, Court Martial and Court Martial Appeal Court)</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_courts">Other courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a (ancient and rarely convened court dealing with <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_cases">Criminal cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Criminal cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are two kinds of criminal trial: \'summary\' and \'on indictment\'. For an adult, summary trials take place in a magistrates\' court, while trials on indictment take place in the Crown Court. Despite the possibility of two venues for trial, almost all criminal cases, however serious, commence in a <a href="/wiki/Magistrates%27_court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Magistrates&#39; court (England and Wales)">magistrates\' courts</a>. It is possible to start a trial for an <a href="/wiki/Indictable_offence" title="Indictable offence">indictable offence</a> by a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voluntary_bill_of_indictment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Voluntary bill of indictment (page does not exist)">voluntary bill of indictment</a>, and go directly to the Crown Court, but that would be unusual.\n</p><p>A criminal case that starts in the magistrates\' courts may begin either by the defendant being charged and then being brought forcibly before magistrates, or by summons to the defendant to appear on a certain day before the magistrates. A summons is usually confined to very minor offences. The hearing (of the charge or summons) before the magistrates is known as a "first appearance".\n</p><p>Offences are of three categories: indictable only, summary and either way. Indictable only offences such as murder and rape must be tried on indictment in the Crown Court. On first appearance, the magistrates must immediately refer the defendant to the Crown Court for trial, their only role being to decide whether to remand the defendant on bail or in custody.\n</p><p>Summary offences, such as most motoring offences, are much less serious and most must be tried in a magistrates\' court, although a few may be sent for trial to the Crown Court along with other offences that may be tried there (for example assault). The vast majority of offences are also concluded in a magistrates\' court (over 90% of cases).\n</p><p>Either way offences are intermediate offences such as theft and, with the exception of low-value criminal damage, may be tried either summarily (by magistrates) or by judge and jury in the Crown Court. If the magistrates consider that an either way offence is too serious for them to deal with, they may "decline jurisdiction" which means that the defendant will have to appear in the Crown Court. Conversely, even if the magistrates accept jurisdiction, an adult defendant has a right to compel a jury trial. Defendants under eighteen years of age do not have this right and will be tried in a <a href="/wiki/Youth_justice_in_England_and_Wales" title="Youth justice in England and Wales">youth court</a> (similar to a magistrates\' court) unless the case is homicide or else is particularly serious.\n</p><p>A magistrates\' court is made up in two ways. Either a group (known as a \'bench\') of \'lay magistrates\', or a district judge, will hear the case. A lay bench must consist of at least three magistrates. Alternatively, a case may be heard by a district judge (formerly known as a stipendiary magistrate), who will be a qualified lawyer and will sit singly, but has the same powers as a lay bench. District judges usually sit in the more busy courts in cities or hear complex cases (e.g. extradition). Magistrates have limited sentencing powers.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a>, the case is tried before a recorder (part-time judge), <a href="/wiki/Circuit_judge_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Circuit judge (UK)">circuit judge</a> or a <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="High Court of Justice of England and Wales">High Court</a> judge, and a jury. The seniority of the judge depends on the seriousness and complexity of the case. The jury is involved only if the defendant enters a plea of "not guilty".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appeals">Appeals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Appeals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From the magistrates\' courts, an appeal can be taken to the Crown Court on matters of fact and law or, on matters of law alone, to the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_Court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Administrative Court (England and Wales)">Administrative Court</a> of Queen\'s Bench Division of the High Court, which is called an appeal "by way of <a href="/wiki/Case_stated" title="Case stated">case stated</a>". The magistrates\' courts are also inferior courts and are therefore subject to <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a>.\n</p><p>The Crown Court is more complicated. When it is hearing a trial on indictment (a jury trial) it is treated as a superior court, which means that its decisions may not be judicially reviewed and appeal lies only to the Criminal Division of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a>.\n</p><p>In other circumstances (for example when acting as an appeal court from a magistrates\' court) the Crown Court is an inferior court, which means that it is subject to judicial review. When acting as an inferior court, appeals by way of case stated on matters of law may be made to the Administrative Court.\n</p><p>Appeals from the High Court, in criminal matters, lie only to the Supreme Court. Appeals from the Court of Appeal (Criminal Division) may also only be taken to the Supreme Court.\n</p><p>Appeals to the Supreme Court are unusual in that the court from which appeal is being made (either the High Court or the Court of Appeal) must certify that there is a point of law of general public importance. This additional control mechanism is not present with civil appeals and means that far fewer criminal appeals are heard by the Supreme Court.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_cases">Civil cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Civil cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Procedure_Rules_1998" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Procedure Rules 1998">Civil Procedure Rules 1998</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil</a> claims under \xc2\xa310,000 are dealt with in a <a href="/wiki/County_court" title="County court">county court</a> under the \'small claims track\'. This is generally known to the lay public as \'small claims court\' but does not exist as a separate court. Claims between \xc2\xa310,000 and \xc2\xa325,000 that are capable of being tried within one day are allocated to the \'fast track\' and claims over \xc2\xa325,000 to the \'multi track\'. These \'tracks\' are labels for the use of the court system \xe2\x80\x93 the actual cases will be heard in a county court or the High Court depending on their value.\n</p><p>For personal injury, defamation cases and in some landlord and tenant disputes the thresholds for each track have different values.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courts_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_courts_of_England_and_Wales" title="History of the courts of England and Wales">History of the courts of England and Wales</a></div>\n<p>For nearly 300 years, from the time of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> until 1362, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> was the language of the courts, rather than <a href="/wiki/English_language#French_Domination" title="English language">English</a>.
