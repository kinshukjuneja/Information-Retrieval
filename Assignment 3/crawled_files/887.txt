title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[bi.bli.j\xc9\x94.t\xc9\x9bk na.sj\xc9\x94.nal d\xc9\x99 class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The National Library of France traces its origin to the royal library founded at the <a href="/wiki/Louvre_Palace" title="Louvre Palace">Louvre Palace</a> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_V_of_France" title="Charles V of France">Charles V</a> in 1368. Charles had received a collection of manuscripts from his predecessor, <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_France" title="John II of France">John II</a>, and transferred them to the Louvre from the <a href="/wiki/Palais_de_la_Cit%C3%A9" title="Palais de la Cit\xc3\xa9">Palais de la Cit\xc3\xa9</a>. The first librarian of record was Claude Mallet, the king\'s valet de chambre, who made a sort of catalogue, <i>Inventoire des Livres du Roy nostre Seigneur estans au Chastel du Louvre</i>. Jean Blanchet made another list in 1380 and Jean de B\xc3\xa9gue one in 1411 and another in 1424. Charles V was a patron of learning and encouraged the making and collection of books. It is known that he employed <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Oresme" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas Oresme">Nicholas Oresme</a>, Raoul de Presle and others to transcribe ancient texts. At the death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI_of_France" title="Charles VI of France">Charles VI</a>, this first collection was unilaterally bought by the English regent of France, the <a href="/wiki/John_of_Lancaster,_1st_Duke_of_Bedford" title="John of Lancaster, 1st Duke of Bedford">Duke of Bedford</a>, who transferred it to England in 1424. It was apparently dispersed at his death in 1435.<sup id="cite_ref-priebe_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priebe-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII</a> did little to repair the loss of these books, but the invention of printing resulted in the starting of another collection in the Louvre inherited by <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louis XI</a> in 1461. <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII</a> seized a part of the collection of the kings of <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-staikos2012_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-staikos2012-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII</a>, who had inherited the library at <a href="/wiki/Blois" title="Blois">Blois</a>, incorporated the latter into the <i>Biblioth\xc3\xa8que du Roi</i> and further enriched it with the <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Gruuthuse" title="Louis de Gruuthuse">Gruthuyse</a> collection and with plunder from <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I</a> transferred the collection in 1534 to <a href="/wiki/Fontainebleau" title="Fontainebleau">Fontainebleau</a> and merged it with his private library. During his reign, fine bindings became the craze and many of the books added by him and <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">Henry II</a> are masterpieces of the binder\'s art.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under librarianship of <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Amyot" title="Jacques Amyot">Amyot</a>, the collection was transferred to Paris during which process many treasures were lost. <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV</a> again moved it to the <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_de_Clermont" class="mw-redirect" title="Coll\xc3\xa8ge de Clermont">Coll\xc3\xa8ge de Clermont</a> and in 1604 it was housed in the <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_la_Harpe" title="Rue de la Harpe">Rue de la Harpe</a>. The appointment of <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Auguste_de_Thou" title="Jacques Auguste de Thou">Jacques Auguste de Thou</a> as librarian initiated a period of development that made it the largest and richest collection of books in the world. He was succeeded by his son who was replaced, when executed for treason, by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bignon" title="J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Bignon">J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Bignon</a>, the first of a line of librarians of the same name. Under de Thou, the library was enriched by the collections of Queen <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de_Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Catherine de Medici">Catherine de Medici</a>. The library grew rapidly during the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIII_of_France" title="Louis XIII of France">Louis XIII</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>, due in great part to the interest of the Minister of Finance, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert">Colbert</a>, an indefatigable collectors of books.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The quarters in the Rue de la Harpe becoming inadequate, the library was again moved, in 1666, to a more spacious house in Rue Vivienne. The minister <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Michel_le_Tellier,_Marquis_de_Louvois" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Michel le Tellier, Marquis de Louvois">Louvois</a> took quite as much interest in the library as Colbert and during his administration a magnificent building to be erected in the Place Vend\xc3\xb4me was planned. The death of Louvois, however, prevented the realization of this plan. Louvois employed <a href="/wiki/Jean_Mabillon" title="Jean Mabillon">Mabillon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melchis%C3%A9dech_Th%C3%A9venot" title="Melchis\xc3\xa9dech Th\xc3\xa9venot">Th\xc3\xa9venot</a> and others to procure books from every source. In 1688, a catalogue in eight volumes was compiled.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The library opened to the public in 1692, under the administration of <a href="/wiki/Camille_le_Tellier_de_Louvois" title="Camille le Tellier de Louvois">Abb\xc3\xa9 Louvois</a>, Minister Louvois\'s son. Abb\xc3\xa9 Louvois was succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bignon" title="J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Bignon">Abb\xc3\xa9 Bignon</a>, or Bignon II as he was termed, who instituted a complete reform of the library\'s system. Catalogues were made which appeared from 1739 to 1753 in 11 volumes. The collections increased steadily by purchase and gift to the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, at which time it was in grave danger of partial or total destruction, but owing to the activities of <a href="/wiki/Antoine-Augustin_Renouard" title="Antoine-Augustin Renouard">Antoine-Augustin Renouard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Van_Praet" title="Joseph Van Praet">Joseph Van Praet</a> it suffered no injury.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The library\'s collections swelled to over 300,000 volumes during the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_(France)" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative Assembly (France)">radical phase</a> of the French Revolution when the private libraries of aristocrats and clergy were seized. After the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">French First Republic</a> in September 1792, "the Assembly declared the <i>Bibliotheque du Roi</i> to be national property and the institution was renamed the <i>Biblioth\xc3\xa8que Nationale</i>. After four centuries of control by the Crown, this great library now became the property of the French people."<sup id="cite_ref-priebe_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priebe-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a new administrative organization was established. <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> took great interest in the library and among other things issued an order that all books in provincial libraries not possessed by the <i>Biblioth\xc3\xa8que Nationale</i> should be forwarded to it, subject to replacement by exchanges of equal value from the duplicate collections, making it possible, as Napoleon said, to find a copy of any book in France in the National Library. Napoleon furthermore increased the collections by spoil from his conquests. A considerable number of these books was restored after his downfall. During the period from 1800 to 1836, the library was virtually under the control of Joseph Van Praet. At his death it contained more than 650,000 printed books and some 80,000 manuscripts.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following a series of regime changes in France, it became the <b>Imperial National Library</b> and in 1868 was moved to newly constructed buildings on the <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_Richelieu" title="Rue de Richelieu">Rue de Richelieu</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Labrouste" title="Henri Labrouste">Henri Labrouste</a>. Upon Labrouste\'s death in 1875 the library was further expanded, including the grand staircase and the Oval Room, by academic architect <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Pascal" title="Jean-Louis Pascal">Jean-Louis Pascal</a>. In 1896, the library was still the largest repository of books in the world, although it has since been surpassed by other libraries for that title.<sup id="cite_ref-world_and_its_people_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-world_and_its_people-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\nBy 1920, the library\'s collection had grown to 4,050,000 volumes and 11,000 manuscripts.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>M. Henri Lema\xc3\xaetre, a vice-president of the French Library Association and formerly librarian of the Biblioth\xc3\xa8que Nationale&#160;... outlined the story of French libraries and librarians during the German occupation, a record of destruction and racial discrimination. During 1940\xe2\x80\x931945, more than two million books were lost through the ravages of war, many of them forming the irreplaceable local collections in which France abounded. Many thousands of books, including complete libraries, were seized by the Germans. Yet French librarians stood firm against all threats, and continued to serve their readers to the best of their abilities. In their private lives and in their professional occupations they were in the van of the struggle against the Nazis, and many suffered imprisonment and death for their devotion. Despite <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> opposition they maintained a supply of books to French <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a>. They continued to supply books on various proscribed lists to trustworthy readers; and when liberation came, they were ready with their plans for rehabilitation with the creation of new book centres for the French people on lines of the English county library system.<sup id="cite_ref-Nature_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The National Library of France is a public establishment under the supervision of the <a href="/wiki/French_Ministry_of_Culture" class="mw-redirect" title="French Ministry of Culture">Ministry of Culture</a>. Its mission is to constitute collections, especially the copies of works published in France that must, by law, be deposited there, conserve them, and make them available to the public. It produces a reference catalogue, cooperates with other national and international establishments, and participates in research programs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
