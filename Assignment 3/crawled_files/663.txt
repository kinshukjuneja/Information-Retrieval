British <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a> based in London. Founded in 1930, it was the first permanent salaried orchestra in London, and is the only one of the city\'s five major symphony orchestras not to be self-governing. The BBC SO is the principal orchestra of the <a href="/wiki/British_Broadcasting_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="British Broadcasting Corporation">British Broadcasting Corporation</a> (BBC).\n</p><p>The orchestra was originally conceived in 1928 as a joint enterprise by the BBC and the conductor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Beecham" title="Thomas Beecham">Sir Thomas Beecham</a>, but the latter withdrew the next year; and the task of assembling and training the orchestra fell to the BBC\'s director of music, <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Boult" title="Adrian Boult">Adrian Boult</a>. Among its guest conductors in its first years was <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">Arturo Toscanini</a>, who judged it the finest orchestra he had ever conducted. During and after the Second World War, Boult strove to maintain standards, but the senior management of the post-war BBC did not allocate the orchestra the resources to meet competition from new and well-funded rivals.\n</p><p>After Boult\'s retirement from the BBC in 1950, the orchestra went through a fallow period. Boult\'s successor, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Sargent" title="Malcolm Sargent">Sir Malcolm Sargent</a>, was popular with the public but had poor rapport with his players, and orchestral morale dropped. Sargent\'s successor, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Schwarz_(conductor)" title="Rudolf Schwarz (conductor)">Rudolf Schwarz</a>, made little public impact, and although the BBC appointed high-profile chief conductors in the 1960s and 1970s \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Antal_Dor%C3%A1ti" title="Antal Dor\xc3\xa1ti">Antal Dor\xc3\xa1ti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Davis" title="Colin Davis">Colin Davis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre Boulez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gennady_Rozhdestvensky" title="Gennady Rozhdestvensky">Gennady Rozhdestvensky</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the BBC SO remained underfunded and could not attract enough good players to rival the leading London orchestras.\n</p><p>As a result of initiatives begun in the 1960s by the BBC controller of music <a href="/wiki/William_Glock" title="William Glock">William Glock</a>, performing standards gradually began to rise. Under <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Davis_(conductor)" title="Andrew Davis (conductor)">Andrew Davis</a> in the 1990s and <a href="/wiki/Ji%C5%99%C3%AD_B%C4%9Blohl%C3%A1vek" title="Ji\xc5\x99\xc3\xad B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek">Ji\xc5\x99\xc3\xad B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek</a> in the 2000s the orchestra prospered. By the second decade of the 21st century the BBC SO was once again regarded by critics as of first-class status. From the outset the orchestra has been known for pioneering new music, and it continues to do so, at the <a href="/wiki/The_Proms" title="The Proms">Proms</a>, in concerts at the <a href="/wiki/Barbican_Centre" title="Barbican Centre">Barbican Centre</a>, and in studio concerts from its base at <a href="/wiki/Maida_Vale_Studios" title="Maida Vale Studios">BBC Maida Vale studios</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" 2x" data-file-width="4592" data-file-height="2576" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chilly_Gonzales_with_the_BBC_Symphony_Orchestra_at_the_Barbican.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The BBC Symphony Orchestra at the Barbican in October 2012</div></div></div>\n<p>Almost from its beginning in November 1922 the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> had started broadcasting from its "<a href="/wiki/2LO" title="2LO">2LO</a>" transmitter with its own musical ensembles. The first such groups were the "2LO Dance Band", the "2LO Military Band", the "2LO Light Orchestra", and the "2LO Octette", all of which began broadcasting in 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> No concert promoter would co-operate with the BBC, regarding it as a dangerous competitor, but the <a href="/wiki/British_National_Opera_Company" title="British National Opera Company">British National Opera Company</a> allowed broadcasts of its performances from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Opera_House" title="Royal Opera House">Royal Opera House</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-k137_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k137-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Reith,_1st_Baron_Reith" title="John Reith, 1st Baron Reith">John Reith</a>, the General Manager of the BBC, invited the opera company\'s musical director, <a href="/wiki/Percy_Pitt" title="Percy Pitt">Percy Pitt</a>, to become the BBC\'s part-time musical adviser from May 1923. Later in the same year Pitt conducted the BBC\'s first broadcast symphony concert, which included <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Dvo%C5%99%C3%A1k" title="Anton\xc3\xadn Dvo\xc5\x99\xc3\xa1k">Dvo\xc5\x99\xc3\xa1k</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Dvo%C5%99%C3%A1k)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Dvo\xc5\x99\xc3\xa1k)">New World</a></i> Symphony and works by <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Sa\xc3\xabns">Saint-Sa\xc3\xabns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Elgar" title="Edward Elgar">Elgar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Maria_von_Weber" title="Carl Maria von Weber">Weber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:158px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Percy-Pitt-1910.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/49/Percy-Pitt-1910.jpg" width="156" height="164" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="156" data-file-height="164" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Percy-Pitt-1910.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Percy_Pitt" title="Percy Pitt">Percy Pitt</a>, the BBC\'s first director of music</div></div></div>\n<p>Pitt expanded the regular eight-piece studio ensemble to form <b>The Wireless Orchestra</b> of 18 players, augmented to 37 for important broadcasts.<sup id="cite_ref-k137_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k137-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> There was no thought at this stage that the BBC would maintain a full-scale symphonic orchestra of up to 100 players.  With Reith\'s approval, Pitt engaged various orchestras for a BBC concert series in 1924 at the <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Central_Hall_Westminster" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist Central Hall Westminster">Methodist Central Hall Westminster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-k137_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k137-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Pitt and <a href="/wiki/Landon_Ronald" title="Landon Ronald">Landon Ronald</a> conducted the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">Royal Albert Hall</a> Orchestra; <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Aynsley_Goossens" title="Eugene Aynsley Goossens">Eugene Goossens</a> conducted the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Harty" title="Hamilton Harty">Hamilton Harty</a> and Sir Edward Elgar conducted the orchestra of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Philharmonic_Society" title="Royal Philharmonic Society">Royal Philharmonic Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1924, the Wireless Orchestra, by then comprising 22 players, was contracted for six concerts a week. The following year, Pitt, by now working full-time for the BBC, as its director of music, augmented the ensemble to form the "Wireless Symphony Orchestra" for a new series of concerts broadcast from Covent Garden, conducted by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Walter" title="Bruno Walter">Bruno Walter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Ansermet" title="Ernest Ansermet">Ernest Ansermet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Monteux" title="Pierre Monteux">Pierre Monteux</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-k138_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k138-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1927 the BBC and Covent Garden collaborated in a series of public concerts with an orchestra of 150 players under conductors including <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Wagner" title="Siegfried Wagner">Siegfried Wagner</a>. Although the orchestra was large, it was not good. The BBC attempted to stop its contracted players sending deputies to rehearsals and even to concerts, but was unsuccessful.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;n 1&#93;</a></sup> In January 1928 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Musical_Times" title="The Musical Times">The Musical Times</a></i> protested:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The B.B.C. has been blamed for devoting too much time to the classics, and also for not giving them all that is due to them; it has been held responsible for the inferiority of the apparatus of the listener-in; it has been censured on a variety of trifling points, but never for the one heinous offence it has committed, and goes on committing: for this corporation, with all its assured and conspicuous wealth, has given and is giving us the worst orchestral performances ever heard in London. \xe2\x80\xa6 This year at Queen\'s Hall they have assembled an orchestra which sounds as if it were composed in great part of "substitutes."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In 1927, the BBC took over the responsibility for the <a href="/wiki/The_Proms" title="The Proms">Promenade Concerts</a>, widely known as "the Proms". At first <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wood" title="Henry Wood">Henry Wood</a>, the founding conductor, persuaded the corporation to engage his Queen\'s Hall Orchestra for each Prom season; from 1930 onwards, the BBC provided the orchestra.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The inadequacy of the BBC\'s players, and also of the established London orchestras, was shown up by the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Philharmonic" title="Berlin Philharmonic">Berlin Philharmonic</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Furtw%C3%A4ngler" title="Wilhelm Furtw\xc3\xa4ngler">Wilhelm Furtw\xc3\xa4ngler</a>, in two concerts in 1927. A historian of the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Hall" title="Queen&#39;s Hall">Queen\'s Hall</a>, Robert Elkin, writes, "At this period the standard of orchestral playing in London was distinctly low, and the well-drilled efficiency of the Berliners under their dynamic conductor came as something of a revelation."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These, and later concerts by the same orchestra, gained plaudits from the public and music critics at the expense of the London orchestras. The chief music critic of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Howes" title="Frank Howes">Frank Howes</a>, later commented, "the British public ... was electrified when it heard the disciplined precision of the Berlin Philharmonic... This apparently was how an orchestra could, and, therefore, ought to sound".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> After the Berliners, London heard a succession of major foreign orchestras, including the Amsterdam <a href="/wiki/Concertgebouw_Orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="Concertgebouw Orchestra">Concertgebouw Orchestra</a> under <a href="/wiki/Willem_Mengelberg" title="Willem Mengelberg">Willem Mengelberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Philharmonic" title="New York Philharmonic">Philharmonic-Symphony Orchestra of New York</a> under <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">Arturo Toscanini</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg/150px-INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg" width="150" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg/225px-INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg/300px-INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2191" data-file-height="3580" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:INF3-49_Sir_John_Reith_Artist_Wooding.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Reith,_1st_Baron_Reith" title="John Reith, 1st Baron Reith">John Reith</a>, Director-General of the BBC</div></div></div><p>\nAmong those determined that London should have a permanent orchestra of similar excellence were Reith and the conductor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Beecham" title="Thomas Beecham">Sir Thomas Beecham</a>. The latter aimed at setting up a first-rate ensemble for opera and concert performances and, though no admirer of broadcasting, he was willing to negotiate with the BBC if this gave him what he sought. Reith\'s concern was that the BBC should have a first-rate radio orchestra. The critic <a href="/wiki/Richard_Morrison_(music_critic)" title="Richard Morrison (music critic)">Richard Morrison</a> writes: </p><blockquote><p>Reith\'s BBC of the 1920s was ... imbued with an almost religious zeal for "enlightening" the public through the magical medium of the wireless. An orchestra, and particularly one that was unencumbered by commercial constraints and thus free to deliver the highest of highbrow programmes, would fit very well into that idealistic philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p> Landon Ronald brought Reith and Beecham together in April 1928; negotiations and preliminary arrangements continued for more than 18 months until it became clear that the corporation and the conductor had irreconcilable priorities for the proposed new ensemble.<sup id="cite_ref-nk_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nk-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Beecham withdrew and, as described by <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Kenyon" title="Nicholas Kenyon">Nicholas Kenyon</a>:\n</p><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>With the collapse of the Beecham scheme, the way was open for the BBC\'s music department to design an orchestral scheme truly suited to broadcasting needs \xe2\x80\x93 a plan for a 114-piece orchestra that could split into four different smaller groups, which had been devised in the autumn of 1929 by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Clark_(conductor)" title="Edward Clark (conductor)">Edward Clark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julian_Herbage" title="Julian Herbage">Julian Herbage</a> \xe2\x80\x93 and to place that orchestra\'s fortunes under the direction of the man who was to guide it with the utmost distinction for the next 20 years, the BBC\'s new director of music, Adrian Boult.<sup id="cite_ref-nk_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nk-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By the time <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Boult" title="Adrian Boult">Adrian Boult</a> succeeded Pitt as director of music for the BBC, the violinist <a href="/wiki/Albert_Sammons" title="Albert Sammons">Albert Sammons</a> and the violist <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Tertis" title="Lionel Tertis">Lionel Tertis</a> had scouted for new talent around the country on behalf of the corporation. Twenty-seven players had been offered positions in the new orchestra. Among those who joined were <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_Brain" title="Aubrey Brain">Aubrey Brain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Catterall" title="Arthur Catterall">Arthur Catterall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Cruft" title="Eugene Cruft">Eugene Cruft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidonie_Goossens" title="Sidonie Goossens">Sidonie Goossens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauri_Kennedy" title="Lauri Kennedy">Lauri Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Thurston" title="Frederick Thurston">Frederick Thurston</a>. Although many of the principals were stars recruited from the LSO, the <a href="/wiki/The_Hall%C3%A9" title="The Hall\xc3\xa9">Hall\xc3\xa9</a> and other orchestras, a high proportion of the rank and file members were fresh from music colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Boult wrote, "a brilliant group of young and inexperienced players came to sit behind the well-known old stagers."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boult-guests1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="panel of eight small photographs of men&#39;s portraits dating from the decades around 1900" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Boult-guests1.jpg/150px-Boult-guests1.jpg" width="150" height="299" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fa/Boult-guests1.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="198" data-file-height="395" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boult-guests1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Guest conducting the BBC SO in the 1930s: from top left, clockwise, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Beecham" title="Thomas Beecham">Beecham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serge_Koussevitzky" title="Serge Koussevitzky">Koussevitzky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willem_Mengelberg" title="Willem Mengelberg">Mengelberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">Toscanini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Walter" title="Bruno Walter">Walter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anton_Webern" title="Anton Webern">Webern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Weingartner" title="Felix Weingartner">Weingartner</a></div></div></div>\n<p>A substantial number of the players performed at the 1930 Promenade Concerts under Wood,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and the full BBC Symphony Orchestra gave its first concert on 22 October 1930, conducted by Boult at the Queen\'s Hall. The programme consisted of music by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a>, Saint-Sa\xc3\xabns and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">Ravel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-b99_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b99-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Of the 21 programmes in the orchestra\'s first season, Boult conducted nine and Wood five.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;n 2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The reviews of the new orchestra were enthusiastic. <i>The Times</i> wrote of its "virtuosity" and of Boult\'s "superb" conducting.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Musical Times</i> commented, "The boast of the B.B.C. that it intended to get together a first-class orchestra was not an idle one", spoke of "exhilaration at the playing", and called another concert later in the season "an occasion for national pride".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> called the playing "altogether magnificent".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> After the initial concerts Reith was told by his advisers that the orchestra had played better for Boult than anyone else. Reith asked him if he wished to take on the chief conductorship, and if so whether he would resign as director of music or occupy both posts simultaneously. Boult opted for the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-b99_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b99-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1930s, the orchestra became renowned for its high standard of playing and for performing new and unfamiliar music. The pioneering work of Boult and the BBC SO included an early performance of <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Schoenberg</a>\'s <i>Variations,</i> Op. 31, British premieres, including <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">Berg</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Wozzeck" title="Wozzeck">Wozzeck</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lyric_Suite_(Berg)" title="Lyric Suite (Berg)">Three Movements from the Lyric Suite</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-cummings_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cummings-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and world premieres, including Vaughan Williams\'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._4_(Vaughan_Williams)" title="Symphony No. 4 (Vaughan Williams)">Symphony No. 4 in F minor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anton_Webern" title="Anton Webern">Anton Webern</a> conducted eight BBC SO concerts between 1931 and 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1930s the orchestra presented rarely heard large-scale works from the past, including <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Berlioz</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Requiem_(Berlioz)" title="Requiem (Berlioz)">Grande Messe des morts</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Grande_symphonie_fun%C3%A8bre_et_triomphale" title="Grande symphonie fun\xc3\xa8bre et triomphale">Grande symphonie fun\xc3\xa8bre et triomphale</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Mahler</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Mahler)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Mahler)">Eighth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Mahler)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Mahler)">Ninth</a> Symphonies, and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Purcell" title="Henry Purcell">Purcell</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/King_Arthur_(opera)" title="King Arthur (opera)">King Arthur</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The excellence of the orchestra attracted leading international conductors. In its second season guest conductors included Richard Strauss, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Weingartner" title="Felix Weingartner">Felix Weingartner</a> and Bruno Walter,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> followed, in later seasons, by <a href="/wiki/Serge_Koussevitzky" title="Serge Koussevitzky">Serge Koussevitzky</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Beecham and Mengelberg.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Arturo Toscanini, widely regarded at the time as the world\'s leading conductor, conducted the BBC SO in 1935 and later said that it was the finest he had ever directed.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He returned to conduct the orchestra in 1937, 1938 and 1939,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and declared, "This is the orchestra I would like to take round the world."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War_and_postwar">Second World War and postwar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second World War and postwar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On the outbreak of war in September 1939 the BBC put into effect its contingency plans to minimise disruption of broadcasting. The corporation withdrew from responsibility for the Proms, with which Wood went ahead, backed by the Royal Philharmonic Society, with the LSO replacing the BBC SO.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The BBC SO was relocated from London to <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>. More than 40 players were released for active service, including the 30 youngest members; the orchestra was reduced to a complement of 70, although it was increased to 90 later in the war.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> During 1940 and 1941 Bristol suffered devastation from German air-raids, and the BBC decided to move the orchestra again. In September 1941 the BBC SO took up residence in <a href="/wiki/Bedford" title="Bedford">Bedford</a>, where it remained, giving live broadcasts and making recordings until it returned permanently to its London base at the BBC\'s <a href="/wiki/Maida_Vale_Studios" title="Maida Vale Studios">BBC Maida Vale studios</a> in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The BBC resumed its support for the Proms in 1942, with the BBC SO returning temporarily to London during the Proms seasons of 1942\xe2\x80\x9345.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> For the rest of the year, the orchestra played in the hall of <a href="/wiki/Bedford_School" title="Bedford School">Bedford School</a>, and after the launch of the <a href="/wiki/V-1_(flying_bomb)" class="mw-redirect" title="V-1 (flying bomb)">V-1</a> raids in 1944 the remaining broadcast concerts of that year\'s Proms season were performed at the <a href="/wiki/Corn_Exchange,_Bedford" title="Corn Exchange, Bedford">Bedford Corn Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Boult had striven to maintain the orchestra\'s standards and prestige during the war; as an instance of its prowess in the 1940s Kennedy cites an <a href="/wiki/HMV_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="HMV Records">HMV</a> recording of Elgar\'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._2_(Elgar)" title="Symphony No. 2 (Elgar)">Second Symphony</a> released in January 1945: "a performance that blazed with excitement and passion and is documentary evidence of the excellence of the orchestra in 1944".<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> With Reith long gone from the post of director-general, Boult found that the top management of the BBC was less concerned for the status of its Symphony Orchestra. The new director-general, <a href="/wiki/William_Haley" title="William Haley">Sir William Haley</a>, was unwilling to approve the funding needed to keep the orchestra competitive with new rivals \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Walter_Legge" title="Walter Legge">Walter Legge</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Philharmonia_Orchestra" title="Philharmonia Orchestra">Philharmonia</a> and Beecham\'s <a href="/wiki/Royal_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="Royal Philharmonic Orchestra">Royal Philharmonic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-briggs_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-briggs-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Some younger players felt that many BBC SO principals were past their best.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Steuart_Wilson" title="Steuart Wilson">Steuart Wilson</a>, the new Director of Music who had previously been married to Boult\'s wife Ann, engineered Boult\'s retirement in 1950,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Wilson had neglected to secure a successor of similar eminence to take over the orchestra. His efforts to recruit <a href="/wiki/John_Barbirolli" title="John Barbirolli">Sir John Barbirolli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Kubel%C3%ADk" title="Rafael Kubel\xc3\xadk">Rafael Kubel\xc3\xadk</a> were unsuccessful,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and he was obliged to offer the post to his third choice, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Sargent" title="Malcolm Sargent">Sir Malcolm Sargent</a>, on whatever terms Sargent demanded.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s">1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>\nSargent, an immensely popular figure with the public, was not at all popular with orchestral players, because of what a historian of the Proms has called his "autocratic and <i>prima-donna</i> attitude towards orchestral players".<sup id="cite_ref-c164_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c164-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> He offended the BBC SO players by demanding that they all stand up when he came on to the platform \xe2\x80\x93 which they firmly declined to do.<sup id="cite_ref-c164_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c164-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> He rapidly became equally unpopular with the BBC music department, ignoring its agenda and pursuing his own.<sup id="cite_ref-c164_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c164-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> He refused to join the staff of the BBC, and insisted on remaining a freelance, taking numerous external engagements to the detriment of his work with the BBC SO.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> A senior BBC manager wrote&#160;: </p><blockquote><p>Except when a Barbirolli or a <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kletzki" title="Paul Kletzki">Kletzki</a> has been in charge for a few days, the Orchestra is inferior, as an artistic instrument, to the Hall\xc3\xa9 or Philharmonia... [Sargent] is indifferent to the morale and welfare of the Orchestra and to the individual temperaments of his players as artists or as human beings.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p> It did not help that Sargent was universally acknowledged to be at his finest in choral music.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> His reputation in big works for chorus and orchestra such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dream_of_Gerontius" title="The Dream of Gerontius">The Dream of Gerontius</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Hiawatha_(Coleridge-Taylor)" title="The Song of Hiawatha (Coleridge-Taylor)">Hiawatha\'s Wedding Feast</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Belshazzar%27s_Feast_(Walton)" title="Belshazzar&#39;s Feast (Walton)">Belshazzar\'s Feast</a></i> was unrivalled, and his large-scale performances of <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">Handel</a> oratorios were assured packed houses. However, his regular programming of such works did nothing to lift the spirits of the BBC SO: orchestral musicians regarded playing the instrumental accompaniment for large choirs as drudgery.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1950s, the BBC SO, in common with the rest of the BBC\'s musical organisation, suffered from stagnation. In the words of the critic <a href="/wiki/Peter_Heyworth" title="Peter Heyworth">Peter Heyworth</a>, "the Corporation\'s music department had become a byword for its narrow-mindedness and lassitude".<sup id="cite_ref-ph_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Boult had been followed as director of music by a series of successors between 1944 and 1959 who either lacked his commitment to modern music or were actively hostile to it. Richard Howgill, who held the post from 1952 to 1959, took the view that although Webern "might have been a small composer of some significance, Schoenberg wasn\'t really a composer at all."<sup id="cite_ref-ph_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In addition to working under a conductor it disliked, the BBC SO found its role as a pioneer of progressive music gone, and its performances of the standard classics criticised as under-rehearsed (particularly during Proms seasons) compared with those given by Legge\'s Philharmonia and others.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Sargent\'s contract was not renewed in 1957, although he continued as chief conductor of the Proms until his death ten years later. Howgill appointed <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Schwarz_(conductor)" title="Rudolf Schwarz (conductor)">Rudolf Schwarz</a> as chief conductor of the BBC SO. Schwarz failed to restore orchestral standards to pre-war levels, and lacked Sargent\'s box-office appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_52-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Under Schwarz, BBC SO concerts other than the Proms drew poor houses \xe2\x80\x93 as low as 29 per cent of capacity in the 1959\xe2\x80\x9360 season.<sup id="cite_ref-k292_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k292-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The manager of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a>, Ernest Bean, spoke of "an inherited aura of mediocrity about BBC concerts which keeps people away".<sup id="cite_ref-k292_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k292-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Schwarz\'s five-year contract was not renewed when it expired.<sup id="cite_ref-rs_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s_to_1980s">1960s to 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1960s to 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:386px;max-width:386px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:99px;max-width:99px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Colin_Davis_(1967).jpg" class="image"><img alt="dark haired man, head and shoulders" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Colin_Davis_%281967%29.jpg/97px-Colin_Davis_%281967%29.jpg" width="97" height="129" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Colin_Davis_%281967%29.jpg/146px-Colin_Davis_%281967%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Colin_Davis_%281967%29.jpg/194px-Colin_Davis_%281967%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2360" data-file-height="3147" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left"><center><a href="/wiki/Colin_Davis" title="Colin Davis">Colin<br />Davis</a></center></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:174px;max-width:174px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Boulez25oct2004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="bald man, head and shoulders" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Boulez25oct2004.jpg/172px-Boulez25oct2004.jpg" width="172" height="129" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Boulez25oct2004.jpg/258px-Boulez25oct2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Boulez25oct2004.jpg/344px-Boulez25oct2004.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="300" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left"><center><a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre<br />Boulez</a></center></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:107px;max-width:107px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Gennady_Rozhdestvensky.jpg" class="image"><img alt="bald man in formal evening costume" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gennady_Rozhdestvensky.jpg/105px-Gennady_Rozhdestvensky.jpg" width="105" height="129" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gennady_Rozhdestvensky.jpg/158px-Gennady_Rozhdestvensky.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gennady_Rozhdestvensky.jpg/210px-Gennady_Rozhdestvensky.jpg 2x" data-file-width="362" data-file-height="446" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left"><center><a href="/wiki/Gennady_Rozhdestvensky" title="Gennady Rozhdestvensky">Gennady<br /> Rozhdestvensky</a></center></div></div><div style="clear:left"></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1959, the BBC appointed <a href="/wiki/William_Glock" title="William Glock">William Glock</a> as controller of music for the BBC.  During Glock\'s tenure, the profile and fortunes of the BBC SO began to rise.  Glock engaged <a href="/wiki/Antal_Dor%C3%A1ti" title="Antal Dor\xc3\xa1ti">Antal Dor\xc3\xa1ti</a> as the orchestra\'s principal conductor.  Heyworth judged that Dor\xc3\xa1ti raised standards of playing and brought new vigour to the programmes in his four years in charge (1962\xe2\x80\x931966).<sup id="cite_ref-ph_52-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Dor\xc3\xa1ti was convinced that the orchestra was stultified by concentrating on studio broadcasts, as it did except during the Proms season. He strove to free players from "slavery to the microphone", and Glock promoted a regular series of concerts at the Festival Hall.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The music critic <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sutcliffe_(opera_critic)" title="Tom Sutcliffe (opera critic)">Tom Sutcliffe</a> later wrote that Dor\xc3\xa1ti and his successors, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Davis" title="Colin Davis">Colin Davis</a> (1967\xe2\x80\x9371), <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre Boulez</a> (1971\xe2\x80\x9375) and <a href="/wiki/Gennady_Rozhdestvensky" title="Gennady Rozhdestvensky">Gennady Rozhdestvensky</a> (1978\xe2\x80\x9381) had been partly successful in improving playing standards, but had not brought the orchestra up to its original level of distinction.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nBy 1962, Glock had persuaded the BBC management to increase the orchestra\'s budget to allow for joint principals in the string sections, to attract top musicians who could play in the BBC SO without having to give up their solo or chamber careers.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The following season, he was able to engage joint principals for the wind section, including as <a href="/wiki/Jack_Brymer" title="Jack Brymer">Jack Brymer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terence_MacDonagh" title="Terence MacDonagh">Terence MacDonagh</a>, formerly members of Beecham\'s celebrated "Royal Family" in the RPO.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The problem remained that recruiting rank-and-file string players was difficult: although the BBC offered secure employment and a pension, it did not pay as well as its London rivals.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> After 1964, the BBC SO was the only one of the five London symphony orchestras that was not self-governed,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;n 3&#93;</a></sup> and some musicians felt that the BBC SO\'s constitution as a body of salaried employees, with no say in the management or repertory of the orchestra, attracted an unadventurous type of player. A former member of the BBC SO said in 1979, </p><blockquote><p>I felt I was getting too secure ... [in] the BBC Symphony you can be a poor player, but if you\'re on time and never moan at the conductor \xe2\x80\xa6 you\'ll have no trouble... I think the BBC Symphony lost some good young players because the management got their priorities wrong.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Glock was well known as a proponent of music of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Viennese_School" title="Second Viennese School">Second Viennese School</a> and their successors; earlier in his career he had been dismissed as music critic of <i>The Observer</i> for such views as "no great composer has ever cared how \'pleasant\' his music sounds".<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Under his administration, the BBC SO gave world premieres of works by composers including <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Gerhard" title="Roberto Gerhard">Roberto Gerhard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Maxwell_Davies" title="Peter Maxwell Davies">Peter Maxwell Davies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Tippett" title="Michael Tippett">Michael Tippett</a>, and UK premieres of works by, among others, <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Berio" title="Luciano Berio">Luciano Berio</a>, Boulez and <a href="/wiki/Edgard_Var%C3%A8se" title="Edgard Var\xc3\xa8se">Edgard Var\xc3\xa8se</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The policy of commissioning works, and giving UK premieres of new compositions was continued under Glock\'s successors. World or UK premieres in the 1970s included works by <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Carter" title="Elliott Carter">Elliott Carter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Ligeti" title="Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Ligeti">Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Ligeti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Witold_Lutos%C5%82awski" title="Witold Lutos\xc5\x82awski">Witold Lutos\xc5\x82awski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Olivier Messiaen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Nono_(composer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Luigi Nono (composer)">Luigi Nono</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arvo_P%C3%A4rt" title="Arvo P\xc3\xa4rt">Arvo P\xc3\xa4rt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlheinz_Stockhausen" title="Karlheinz Stockhausen">Karlheinz Stockhausen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> BBC commissions premiered by the BBC SO in the 1980s included <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Schnittke" title="Alfred Schnittke">Alfred Schnittke</a>\'s Second Symphony, <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Birtwistle" title="Harrison Birtwistle">Harrison Birtwistle</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_Dances" title="Earth Dances">Earth Dances</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Tavener" title="John Tavener">John Tavener</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Protecting_Veil" title="The Protecting Veil">The Protecting Veil</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although Glock restored the orchestra\'s reputation as Britain\'s leading modern music ensemble, the balance of programming affected the players\' capacity in the mainstream repertoire. The principal horn, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Civil" title="Alan Civil">Alan Civil</a>, recalled, "We did about eighty percent modern and twenty percent classical. The awful tragedy, for the orchestra, was that eventually we were not able to play the standard classics. We could sight-read the most fearsome contemporary piece, but a Brahms symphony \xe2\x80\x93 embarrassing!"<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The bassoon player William Waterhouse who joined the BBC SO from the LSO found the BBC\'s repertory refreshing, but the music making less impressive: "There were no potboilers, but also, I\'m afraid, no world-ranking soloists either."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Pritchard_(conductor)" title="John Pritchard (conductor)">John Pritchard</a> was principal conductor from 1982 to 1989. In <i>The Times</i>, Paul Griffiths wrote, "Sir John\'s seven-year period with the orchestra has been marked by growing orchestral confidence and many memorable performances."<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Principal guest conductors in the 1970s and 1980s included <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mackerras" title="Charles Mackerras">Sir Charles Mackerras</a> (1977\xe2\x80\x931979), Michael Gielen (1978\xe2\x80\x931981), <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Wand" title="G\xc3\xbcnter Wand">G\xc3\xbcnter Wand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Elder" title="Mark Elder">Mark Elder</a> (1982\xe2\x80\x931985) and <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_E%C3%B6tv%C3%B6s" title="P\xc3\xa9ter E\xc3\xb6tv\xc3\xb6s">P\xc3\xa9ter E\xc3\xb6tv\xc3\xb6s</a> (1985\xe2\x80\x931988).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s_and_21st_century">1990s and 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990s and 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Pritchard\'s successor was <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Davis_(conductor)" title="Andrew Davis (conductor)">Andrew Davis</a>, beginning in 1989.  He held the post until 2000, the longest-serving chief conductor since Boult.<sup id="cite_ref-finch_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finch-70">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> He was at the helm for what John Allison in <i>The Times</i> called "the valuable Barbican weekends that each January investigate another major but not fully understood 20th-century composer."<sup id="cite_ref-allison_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allison-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Noting that modern music was central to the work of Davis and the orchestra, Allison added that under Davis the orchestra took part in "once-in-a-lifetime projects such as <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Payne" title="Anthony Payne">Anthony Payne</a>\'s completion of Elgar\'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._3_(Elgar/Payne)" title="Symphony No. 3 (Elgar/Payne)">Third Symphony</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-allison_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allison-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>  Upon Davis\' departure, the orchestra appointed him its first conductor laureate.<sup id="cite_ref-finch_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finch-70">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg/170px-Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg" width="170" height="244" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg/255px-Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg/340px-Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg 2x" data-file-width="659" data-file-height="947" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Leonard_Slatkin_2004.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Leonard_Slatkin" title="Leonard Slatkin">Leonard Slatkin</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Leonard_Slatkin" title="Leonard Slatkin">Leonard Slatkin</a> succeeded Davis as chief conductor, from 2000 to 2004.   His relationship with the players was reported to be uneasy, and his choice of repertoire received criticism.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>  In February 2005, <a href="/wiki/Ji%C5%99%C3%AD_B%C4%9Blohl%C3%A1vek" title="Ji\xc5\x99\xc3\xad B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek">Ji\xc5\x99\xc3\xad B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek</a> was named the orchestra\'s next chief conductor, effective with the 2006 Proms season.  He was principal guest conductor of the orchestra from 1995 to 2000, and was the first past principal guest conductor of the orchestra to be named its chief conductor.<sup id="cite_ref-grumpy_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grumpy-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The classical repertory was regarded as one of B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek\'s strengths, but he had no reputation for conducting new works, which remained a core part of the orchestra\'s remit. He welcomed the fact that the orchestra\'s new principal guest conductor was David Robertson, a new-music expert and a prot\xc3\xa9g\xc3\xa9 of Boulez.<sup id="cite_ref-grumpy_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grumpy-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The orchestra was seen by some as "a bolshie lot" and "grumpy", but its relations with B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek were harmonious.<sup id="cite_ref-grumpy_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grumpy-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Under B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek the orchestra won glowing reviews: <i>The Times</i> referred to its "superb musicians",<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kennedy_(music_critic)" title="Michael Kennedy (music critic)">Michael Kennedy</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Telegraph" title="The Sunday Telegraph">The Sunday Telegraph</a></i> referred to a "rich and opulent [score] magnificently played by the BBC Symphony Orchestra" under B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> and another <i>Telegraph</i> critic praised the BBC SO\'s "virtuoso form".<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek, other principal guest conductors of the orchestra from this period included <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lazarev" title="Alexander Lazarev">Alexander Lazarev</a> (1992\xe2\x80\x931995) and Jukka-Pekka Saraste (2002\xe2\x80\x932005).  In October 2011, <a href="/wiki/Sakari_Oramo" title="Sakari Oramo">Sakari Oramo</a> made his first guest conducting appearance with the BBC SO,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> his first guest-conducting engagement with any London orchestra.  On the basis of this concert, in February 2012, Oramo was named the orchestra\'s 13th Chief Conductor, with an initial contract of 3 years, effective with the First Night of the 2013 Proms season.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>  In September 2015, the BBCSO announced the extension of his contract to the 2019-2020 season.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>  In August 2012, the BBC SO announced the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Semyon_Bychkov_(conductor)" title="Semyon Bychkov (conductor)">Semyon Bychkov</a> to a newly created position in the orchestra, the G\xc3\xbcnter Wand Conducting Chair.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Function_in_the_21st_century">Function in the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Function in the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg/220px-BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg/330px-BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg/440px-BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BBCSO_rehearsing_for_the_last_night_of_the_Proms.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>BBC SO rehearsing for the <a href="/wiki/Last_Night_of_the_Proms" class="mw-redirect" title="Last Night of the Proms">Last Night of the Proms</a> in September 2011</div></div></div>\n<p>The BBC SO is the associate orchestra of the <a href="/wiki/Barbican_Centre" title="Barbican Centre">Barbican Centre</a> in London, where it gives an annual season of concerts. These seasons include series of concerts devoted to individual modern composers, who have included <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_MacMillan" title="James MacMillan">James MacMillan</a>, Elliott Carter, <a href="/wiki/Sofia_Gubaidulina" title="Sofia Gubaidulina">Sofia Gubaidulina</a> and Michael Tippett.<sup id="cite_ref-biog_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biog-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-prof_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prof-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The orchestra remains the principal orchestra of the Proms, giving about a dozen concerts each season, including the first and last nights.<sup id="cite_ref-prof_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prof-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Most of its concerts are broadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_3" title="BBC Radio 3">BBC Radio 3</a>, streamed online and available as podcasts for a month after broadcast, and a number are televised: the orchestra\'s website claims that this gives the BBC "the highest broadcast profile of any UK orchestra".<sup id="cite_ref-biog_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biog-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The orchestra continues to make studio recordings for Radio 3 at the Maida Vale studios; some recording sessions are free for the public to attend.<sup id="cite_ref-biog_82-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biog-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In common with other orchestras, the BBC SO engages in educational work. According to the orchestra\'s website: "Among ongoing projects are the BBC SO Family Music Intro scheme, introducing families to live classical music, BBC SO Student Zone and the highly successful BBC SO Family Orchestra, alongside work in local schools. Total Immersion composer events also provide rich material for education work."<sup id="cite_ref-biog_82-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biog-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2000, the orchestra appointed its first associate composer, <a href="/wiki/Mark-Anthony_Turnage" title="Mark-Anthony Turnage">Mark-Anthony Turnage</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Adams_(composer)" title="John Adams (composer)">John Adams</a> became the BBC Symphony Orchestra\'s artist in association in June 2003. The composer and conductor <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Knussen" title="Oliver Knussen">Oliver Knussen</a> took up the post of artist-in-association in July 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> The orchestra\'s commitment to new music continues. In 2013, the music journalist Tom Service wrote, "I\'ve heard the BBC Symphony give concerts that I don\'t think any other orchestra in the world could do as brilliantly \xe2\x80\xa6 That supreme virtuosity in new music makes them unique among London\'s big orchestras."<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">Recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BBC_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From its first years the BBC SO was active in commercial recording studios. Under Boult it recorded a wide range of music from Bach to Mozart and Beethoven, Brahms, Wagner and Elgar. In the 1950s and 1960s it recorded a range of music with Sargent, mostly British but with several Sibelius discs in addition. With Dor\xc3\xa1ti the orchestra made recordings of works by Bart\xc3\xb3k, Gerhard and Messaien. Under Colin Davis it made its first opera sets: Mozart\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Idomeneo" title="Idomeneo">Idomeneo</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marriage_of_Figaro" title="The Marriage of Figaro">The Marriage of Figaro</a></i>, and Berlioz\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Benvenuto_Cellini_(opera)" title="Benvenuto Cellini (opera)">Benvenuto Cellini</a></i>, as well as works by Beethoven and Tippett. Under Boulez the orchestra recorded mostly twentieth century music \xe2\x80\x93 works by Bart\xc3\xb3k, Berg, Schoenberg and Boulez himself \xe2\x80\x93 and also Berlioz.<sup id="cite_ref-k499_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k499-86">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> Andrew Davis has recorded extensively with the orchestra for the Teldec label and others.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Under B\xc4\x9blohl\xc3\xa1vek the orchestra has recorded <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Martin%C5%AF" title="Bohuslav Martin\xc5\xaf">Martin\xc5\xaf</a>\'s complete symphonies, and also his <i>The Epic of Gilgamesh</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With <span class="reference-text">Despite the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wood" title="Henry Wood">Sir Henry Wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Beecham" title="Thomas Beecham">Sir Thomas Beecham</a> and others, the "deputy system" remained a traditional part of the London orchestral scene. A player booked for a concert could accept a better-paid engagement and send a substitute in his stead. The treasurer of the Royal Philharmonic Society described the system thus: "A, whom you want, signs to play at your concert. He sends B (whom you don\'t mind) to the first rehearsal. B, without your knowledge or consent, sends C to the second rehearsal. Not being able to play at the concert, C sends D, whom you would have paid five shillings to stay away."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a
