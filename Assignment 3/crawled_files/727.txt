(linguistics)">varieties</a> of <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The main, formal variety is called <b>Scottish Standard English</b> or <b>Standard Scottish English</b> (<b>SSE</b>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Scottish Standard English may be defined as "the characteristic speech of the professional class [in Scotland] and the accepted norm in schools".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IETF_language_tag" title="IETF language tag">IETF language tag</a> for "Scottish Standard English" is en-Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to distinct pronunciation, grammar and expressions, Scottish English has distinctive vocabulary, particularly pertaining to Scottish institutions such as the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">local government</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Scotland" title="Education in Scotland">education</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">legal</a> systems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Scottish Standard English is at one end of a bipolar <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_continuum" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguistic continuum">linguistic continuum</a>, with focused broad <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> at the other.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\nScottish English may be influenced to varying degrees by Scots.<sup id="cite_ref-Stuart-Smith_J._2008._p.48_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart-Smith_J._2008._p.48-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\nMany Scots speakers separate Scots and Scottish English as different <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">registers</a> depending on social circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-Aitken_A.J._1979._p.85_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aitken_A.J._1979._p.85-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Some speakers <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">code switch</a> clearly from one to the other while others <a href="/wiki/Style-shifting" class="mw-redirect" title="Style-shifting">style shift</a> in a less predictable and more fluctuating manner.<sup id="cite_ref-Aitken_A.J._1979._p.85_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aitken_A.J._1979._p.85-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Generally there is a shift to Scottish English in formal situations or with individuals of a higher social status.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Scottish English resulted from <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">language contact</a> between Scots and the <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a> of England after the 17th century. The resulting shifts to English usage by Scots-speakers resulted in many phonological compromises and lexical transfers, often mistaken for <a href="/wiki/Phonological_change" title="Phonological change">mergers</a> by linguists unfamiliar with the history of Scottish English.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, the process was also influenced by interdialectal forms, <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">hypercorrections</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spelling_pronunciation" title="Spelling pronunciation">spelling pronunciations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> (See the section on <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English_phonology" class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Reformation</a> and to the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Printing arrived in London in 1476, but the first printing press was not introduced to Scotland for another 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Texts such as the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Bible" title="Geneva Bible">Geneva Bible</a>, printed in English, were widely distributed in Scotland in order to spread Protestant doctrine.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">King James VI of Scotland</a> became King James I of England in 1603.  Since England was the larger and richer of the two Kingdoms, James moved his court to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in England.  The poets of the court therefore moved south and "began adapting the language and style of their verse to the tastes of the English market".<sup id="cite_ref-m36_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m36-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  To this event McClure attributes "the sudden and total eclipse of Scots as a literary language".<sup id="cite_ref-m36_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m36-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The continuing absence of a Scots translation of the Bible meant that <a href="/wiki/Authorized_King_James_Version" class="mw-redirect" title="Authorized King James Version">the translation of King James</a> into English was used in worship in both countries.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a> amalgamated the Scottish and English Parliaments.  However the church, educational and legal structures remained separate. This leads to important professional distinctions in the definitions of some words and terms. See <a href="/wiki/Help:Media" title="Help:Media">media help</a>.</i></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The speech of the middle classes in Scotland tends to conform to the grammatical norms of the written standard, particularly in situations that are regarded as formal. <a href="/wiki/Highland_English" title="Highland English">Highland English</a> is slightly different from the variety spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowlands</a> in that it is more phonologically, grammatically, and lexically influenced by a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Gaelic</a> <a href="/wiki/Substratum" class="mw-redirect" title="Substratum">substratum</a>. Similarly, the English spoken in the North-East of Scotland tends to follow the phonology and grammar of <a href="/wiki/Doric_dialect_(Scotland)" title="Doric dialect (Scotland)">Doric</a>.\n</p><p>Although pronunciation features vary among speakers (depending on region and social status), there are a number of phonological aspects characteristic of Scottish English:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Scottish English is a <a href="/wiki/Rhotic_and_non-rhotic_accents" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhotic and non-rhotic accents">rhotic accent</a>, meaning <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> is typically pronounced in the <a href="/wiki/Syllable_coda" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllable coda">syllable coda</a>.  The phoneme <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> may be a <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Postalveolar approximant">postalveolar approximant</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xb9]</span>, as in Received Pronunciation or General American, but speakers have also traditionally used for the same phoneme a somewhat more common <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_tap" class="mw-redirect" title="Alveolar tap">alveolar tap</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xbe]</span> or, now very rare, the <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" class="mw-redirect" title="Alveolar trill">alveolar trill</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[r]</span><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> (hereafter, &#x27e8;r&#x27e9; will be used to denote any rhotic consonant).\n<ul><li>Although other dialects have merged non-intervocalic <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\x9b/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xaa/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x8c/</span> before <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> (<a href="/wiki/Fern-fir-fur_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Fern-fir-fur merger">fern\xe2\x80\x93fir\xe2\x80\x93fur merger</a>), Scottish English makes a distinction between the vowels in <i>fern</i>, <i>fir</i>, and <i>fur</i>.</li>\n<li>Many varieties contrast <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/o/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\x94/</span> before <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> so that <i>hoarse</i> and <i>horse</i> are pronounced differently.</li>\n<li><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/or/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ur/</span> are contrasted so that <i>shore</i> and <i>sure</i> are pronounced differently, as are <i>pour</i> and <i>poor</i>.</li>\n<li><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> before <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> is strong. An <a href="/wiki/Epenthesis" title="Epenthesis">epenthetic</a> vowel may occur between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> so that <i>girl</i> and <i>world</i> are two-syllable words for some speakers. The same may occur between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/m/</span>, between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/n/</span>, and between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/m/</span>.</li></ul></li>\n<li>There is a distinction between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/hw/</span> in word pairs such as <i>witch</i> and <i>which</i>.</li>\n<li>The phoneme <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/x/</span> is common in names and in SSE\'s many Gaelic and Scots borrowings, so much so that it is often taught to incomers, particularly for "ch" in loch. Some Scottish speakers use it in words of Greek origin as well, such as technical, patriarch, etc. (Wells 1982, 408).</li>\n<li><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> is usually <a href="/wiki/Velarization" title="Velarization">velarised</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Velarized_alveolar_lateral_approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Velarized alveolar lateral approximant">dark l</a>) except in borrowings like "glen" (from Scottish Gaelic "gleann"), which had an unvelarised l in their original form. In areas where <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> was spoken until relatively recently (such as <a href="/wiki/Dumfries" title="Dumfries">Dumfries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a>) and in areas where it is still spoken (such as the <a href="/wiki/West_Highlands" class="mw-redirect" title="West Highlands">West Highlands</a>), velarisation of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> may be absent in many words in which it is present in other areas, but remains in borrowings that had velarised <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> in Gaelic, such as "loch" (Gaelic "loch") and "clan" (Gaelic "clann").</li>\n<li><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/p/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/k/</span> are not aspirated in more traditional varieties,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> but are weakly aspirated currently.</li>\n<li>The past ending <i>-ed</i> may be realised with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> where other accents use <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/d/</span>, chiefly after unstressed vowels: <i>ended</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x9bnd\xc9\xaat]</span>, <i>carried</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kar\xc9\xaat]</span></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">Vowel length</a> is generally regarded as non-phonemic, although a distinctive part of Scottish English is the <a href="/wiki/Scots_vowel_length_rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots vowel length rule">Scots vowel length rule</a> (Scobbie et al. 1999).  Certain vowels (such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span>, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/a\xc9\xaa/</span>) are generally long but are shortened before <a href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasal stop">nasals</a> and voiced <a href="/wiki/Plosive_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Plosive consonant">plosives</a>.  However, this does not occur across morpheme boundaries so that <i>need</i> contrasts with <i>kneed</i>, <i>crude</i> with <i>crude</i> and <i>side</i> with <i>sighed</i>.</li>\n<li>Scottish English has no <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x8a/</span>, instead transferring Scots <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span>.  Phonetically, this vowel may be pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x89]</span> or even <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x8f]</span>. Thus <i>pull</i> and <i>pool</i> are homophones.</li>\n<li><i>Cot</i> and <i>caught</i> are not differentiated in most Central Scottish varieties, as they are in some other varieties.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells,_pp._399_ff_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells,_pp._399_ff-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In most varieties, there is no <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc3\xa6/</span>-<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\x91\xcb\x90/</span> distinction; therefore, <i>bath</i>, <i>trap</i>, and <i>palm</i> have the same vowel.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells,_pp._399_ff_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells,_pp._399_ff-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <i>happY</i> vowel is most commonly <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/e/</span> (as in <i>face</i>), but may also be <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xaa/</span> (as in <i>kit</i>) or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> (as in <i>fleece</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb8s/</span> is often used in plural nouns where southern English has <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc3\xb0z/</span> (baths, youths, etc.); <i>with</i> and <i>booth</i> are pronounced with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb8/</span>.  (See <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation_of_English_th" class="mw-redirect" title="Pronunciation of English th">Pronunciation of English th</a>.)</li>\n<li>In colloquial speech, the <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a> may be an allophone of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> after a vowel, as in <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xcb\x88b\xca\x8c\xca\x94\xc9\x99r]</span>.  These same speakers may "drop the g" in the suffix <i>-ing</i> and <a href="/wiki/Th-glottalization" class="mw-redirect" title="Th-glottalization">debuccalise</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb8/</span> to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[h]</span> in certain contexts.</li>\n<li><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xaa/</span> may be more open <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc3\xab\xcc\x9e]</span> for certain speakers in some regions, so that it sounds more like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x9b]</span> (although <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xaa/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\x9b/</span> do not merge). Other speakers may pronounce it as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xaa]</span>, just as in many other accents, or with a schwa-like (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x99]</span>) quality. Others may pronounce it almost as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x8c]</span> in certain environments, particularly after <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/hw/</span>.</li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scottish_English_monophthongs_chart.svg" class="image"><img alt="" navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scotticism" title="Scotticism">Scotticism</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Scotticism" title="Scotticism">Scotticisms</a> are idioms or expressions that are characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>, especially when used in English.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> They are more likely to occur in spoken than written language.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Examples include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>What a dreich day!</i> meaning "What a dull, miserable, overcast day" (of weather)</li>\n<li><i>I\'m feeling quite drouthy</i> meaning "I\'m feeling quite thirsty"</li>\n<li><i>That\'s a right (</i>or <i>real) scunner!</i> meaning "That\'s extremely off-putting"</li>\n<li><i>It\'s a fair way to Skye from here</i> meaning "It\'s a good distance to Skye from here"</li>\n<li><i>The picture still looks squint</i> meaning "The picture still looks askew/awry"</li>\n<li><i>You\'d better just caw canny</i> meaning "You\'d better just go easy/Don\'t overdo it"</li>\n<li><i>His face is tripping him</i> meaning "He\'s looking fed up"</li>\n<li><i>Just play the daft laddie</i> meaning "Act ingenuously/feign ignorance"</li>\n<li><i>You\'re looking a bit peely-wally</i> meaning "You\'re looking a bit off-colour"</li>\n<li><i>That\'s outwith my remit</i> meaning "It\'s not part of my job to do that"</li>\n<li><i>It depends on what the high heid yins think</i> meaning "It depends on what the heads of the organisation/management think"</li>\n<li><i>I\'ll come round (at) the back of eight</i> meaning "I\'ll come round just after eight o\'clock"</li>\n<li><i>We\'re all <a href="/wiki/Jock_Tamson%27s_bairns" class="mw-redirect" title="Jock Tamson&#39;s bairns">Jock Tamson\'s bairns</a></i>, stock phrase meaning "None of us is better than anyone else" (i.e. socially superior)</li>\n<li><i>I kent his faither</i>, stock phrase meaning "he started off as humbly as the rest of us before achieving success"</li>\n<li><i>You\'re standing there like a stookie</i> meaning "you stand there as if incapable of stirring yourself" (like a plaster statue, a stucco figure)<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>He\'s a right sweetie-wife</i> meaning "He likes a good gossip"</li>\n<li><i>I didn\'t mean to cause a stooshie</i> meaning "I didn\'t mean to cause a major fuss/commotion"</li>\n<li><i>She was a bit pit oot when I told her</i> meaning "She was a bit upset when I told her"</li>\n<li><i>I\'m swithering whether to go</i> meaning "I\'m in two minds/uncertain as to whether to go"</li>\n<li><i>Ach, away ye go!</i> stock phrase meaning "Oh, I don\'t believe you"</li></ul>\n<p>Scotticisms are generally divided into two types:<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> covert Scotticisms, which generally go unnoticed as being particularly Scottish by those using them, and overt Scotticisms, usually used for stylistic effect, with those using them aware of their Scottish nature.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexical">Lexical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lexical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Outwith.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Outwith.JPG/150px-Outwith.JPG" width="150" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Outwith.JPG/225px-Outwith.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Outwith.JPG/300px-Outwith.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1632" data-file-height="1224" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Outwith.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of "outwith" on a sign in Scotland</div></div></div>\n<p>Scottish English has inherited a number of lexical items from Scots,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> which are less common in other forms of standard English.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>General items are <i>wee</i>, the Scots word for small (also common in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_English" title="New Zealand English">New Zealand English</a>, probably under Scottish influence); <i>bairn</i> for child (from Common Germanic,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> cf modern <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a> <i>barn</i>, <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_language" title="West Frisian language">West Frisian</a> <i>bern</i> and also used in <a href="/wiki/English_language_in_northern_England" class="mw-redirect" title="English language in northern England">Northern English dialects</a>); <i><a href="/wiki/Bonnie" title="Bonnie">bonnie</a></i> for pretty, attractive, (or good looking, handsome, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Edward_Stuart" title="Charles Edward Stuart">Bonnie Prince Charlie)</a>; <i>braw</i> for fine; <i>muckle</i> for big; <i>spail</i> for splinter, <i>snib</i> for bolt, <i>pinkie</i> for little finger, <i>janitor</i> for school caretaker (these last two are also standard in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>), <i>outwith</i>, meaning \'outside of\'; <i>cowp</i> for tip or spill, <i>fankle</i> for a tangled mess. <i>Kirk</i> for church has parallels in other Germanic languages (cf <i>kirche</i> which was also found in archaic names of some ancient churches in e.g. London). Examples of culturally specific items are <i><a href="/wiki/Hogmanay" title="Hogmanay">Hogmanay</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Caber_toss" title="Caber toss">caber</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Haggis" title="Haggis">haggis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bothy" title="Bothy">bothy</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Scone" title="Scone">scone</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Oatcake" title="Oatcake">oatcake</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Tablet_(confectionery)" title="Tablet (confectionery)">tablet</a></i>; <i>rone</i> (roof gutter); <i><a href="/wiki/Teuchter" title="Teuchter">teuchter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ned_(Scottish)" title="Ned (Scottish)">ned</a></i>, <i>numpty</i> (witless person; now more common in the rest of the UK) and <i>landward</i> (rural); <i>It\'s your shot</i> for "It\'s your turn"; and the once notorious but now redundant <i><a href="/wiki/Tawse" title="Tawse">tawse</a></i>.\n</p><p>The diminutive ending "-ie" is added to nouns to indicate smallness, as in <i>laddie</i> and <i>lassie</i> for a young boy and young girl. Other examples are <i>peirie</i> (child\'s wooden spinning top) and <i>sweetie</i> (piece of <a href="/wiki/Confectionery" title="Confectionery">confectionery</a>). The ending can be added to many words instinctively, e.g. <i>bairn</i> (see above) can become <i>bairnie</i>, a small shop can become a <i>wee shoppie</i>.\n</p><p>The use of "How?" meaning "Why?" is distinctive of Scottish, <a href="/wiki/Northern_England_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern England English">Northern English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mid_Ulster_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid Ulster English">Northern Irish English</a>. "Why not?" is often rendered as "How no?".\n</p><p>There is a range of (often anglicised) legal and administrative vocabulary inherited from Scots<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> e.g. <i>depute</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88d\xc9\x9bpjut/</span> for <i>deputy</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Not_proven" title="Not proven">proven</a></i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88pro\xcb\x90v\xc9\x99n/</span> for <i>proved</i> (standard in American English), <i>interdict</i> for \'"injunction"<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sheriff-substitute" title="Sheriff-substitute">sheriff-substitute</a></i> for "acting sheriff\'". In Scottish education a <i>short leet</i> is a list of selected job applicants, and a <i>remit</i> is a detailed job description. <i><a href="/wiki/Provost_(civil)#Scotland" title="Provost (civil)">Provost</a></i> is used for "mayor" and <i><a href="/wiki/Procurator_fiscal" title="Procurator fiscal">procurator fiscal</a></i> for "public prosecutor".\n</p><p>Often, lexical differences between Scottish English and Southern Standard English are simply differences in the distribution of shared lexis, such as <i>stay</i> for "live" (as in: <i>where do you stay?</i>).\n</p>\n<h3><span The future progressive frequently implies an assumption (<i>You\'ll be coming from Glasgow?</i>).\n</p><p>In some areas perfect aspect of a verb is indicated using "be" as auxiliary with the preposition "after" and the present participle: for example "He is after going" instead of "He has gone" (this construction is borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>).\n</p><p>The definite article tends to be used more frequently in phrases such as <i>I\'ve got the cold/the flu</i>, <i>he\'s at the school</i>, <i>I\'m away to the kirk</i>.\n</p><p>Speakers often use prepositions differently.  The compound preposition <i>off of</i> is often used (<i>Take that off of the table</i>). Scots commonly say <i>I was waiting on you</i> (meaning "waiting for you"), which means something quite different in Standard English.\n</p><p>In colloquial speech <i>shall</i> and <i>ought</i> are scarce, <i>must</i> is marginal for obligation and <i>may</i> is rare. Here are other syntactical structures:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>What age are you?</i> for "How old are you?"</li>\n<li><i>My hair is needing washed</i> or <i>My hair needs washed</i> for "My hair needs washing" or "My hair needs to be washed".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>I\'m just after telling you</i> for "I\'ve just told you".</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Amn%27t" class="mw-redirect" title="Amn&#39;t">Amn\'t</a> I invited?</i> for <i>Am I not invited?</i></li></ul>\n<p>Note that in Scottish English, the first person declarative <i>I amn\'t invited</i> and interrogative <i>Amn\'t I invited?</i> are both possible.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
