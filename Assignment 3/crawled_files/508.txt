It defines itself as "the ancient Church of this land, catholic and reformed. It proclaims and holds fast the doctrine and ministry of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Marks_of_the_Church" title="Four Marks of the Church">one, holy, catholic and apostolic</a> Church".<sup href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As with the primus of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Episcopal_Church" title="Scottish Episcopal Church">Scottish Episcopal Church</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Wales" title="Archbishop of Wales">Archbishop of Wales</a> serves concurrently as one of the six <a href="/wiki/Diocesan_bishop" title="Diocesan bishop">diocesan bishops</a>, a position currently held by <a href="/wiki/John_Davies_(bishop_of_Swansea_and_Brecon)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Davies (bishop of Swansea and Brecon)">The Most Reverend John Davies</a>.\n</p><p>Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, the Church in Wales is not an <a href="/wiki/Established_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Established church">established church</a>. <a href="/wiki/Disestablishmentarianism" title="Disestablishmentarianism">Disestablishment</a> was effected in 1920 under the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a>.\n</p><p>As a province of the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, the Church in Wales recognises the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a> as a focus of unity but without any formal authority in the Church in Wales (except for residual roles \xe2\x80\x94 in ecclesiastical court to try the archbishop, as metropolitan; and the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Notaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Notaries">notaries</a>, and the granting of <a href="/wiki/Marriage_license" title="Marriage license">Special Marriage Licences</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>). Eighteen cross-border parishes remained in the Church of England and were exempt from disestablishment.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A cleric of the Church in Wales can be appointed to posts in the Church of England, including the See of Canterbury; the former <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rowan_Williams" title="Rowan Williams">The Right Reverend Dr Rowan Williams</a> was from Wales and served as Archbishop of Wales before his appointment to Canterbury.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Official name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Church in Wales (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Yr Eglwys yng Nghymru</i>) adopted its name by accident. The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a> referred throughout to "the Church <i>in</i> Wales", the phrase being used to indicate the part of the Church of England within Wales. In 1920, a convention of the Welsh church considered what name to select and tended to favour "the Church <i>of</i> Wales". However, there were concerns that adopting a name different from that mentioned in the Act might cause legal problems. Given the situation, it seemed sensible to adopt the title "the Church <i>in</i> Wales".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Gwynedd_during_the_High_Middle_Ages#Celtic_Christian_traditions" title="Culture of Gwynedd during the High Middle Ages">Celtic Christian traditions in Gwynedd</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Wales" title="Christianity in Wales">Christianity in Wales</a> can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> period and an organised <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_polity" title="Episcopal polity">episcopal church</a> has had continuous existence in Wales since that time. Wales became a refuge for other <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Britons</a> following the pagan <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> invasion of what became <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The Welsh refused to co-operate with <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Canterbury" title="Augustine of Canterbury">Augustine of Canterbury</a>\'s mission to the Anglo-Saxons. However, a combination of other Celtic dioceses reconciling with the <a href="/wiki/See_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="See of Rome">See of Rome</a> and the English conquest of Wales meant that from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the Welsh dioceses were part of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Canterbury" title="Province of Canterbury">Province of Canterbury</a> and also in communion with the See of Rome until the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>. Afterward they were part of the Church of England until disestablishment in 1920. From the time of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>, Wales had been absorbed into England as a legal entity and the established church in Wales was the Church of England.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disestablishment">Disestablishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disestablishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg/150px-Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg" width="150" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg/225px-Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg/300px-Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="993" data-file-height="2058" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Birkenhead_by_Spy0002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>F. E. Smith, later 1st Earl of Birkenhead, <a href="/wiki/Antidisestablishmentarianism" title="Antidisestablishmentarianism">opposed disestablishment</a></div></div></div>\n<p>During the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Nonconformism" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonconformism">Nonconformist</a> churches grew rapidly in Wales, and eventually the majority of Welsh Christians were Nonconformists, although the Church of England remained the largest single denomination. By the mid-19th century the failure to appoint a Welsh-speaking bishop to any Welsh diocese for 150 years caused real resentment; disestablishment was seen as way to assert national and linguistic identity.\n</p><p>Under the influence of Nonconformist politicians such as <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a> was passed by the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Government_1905%E2%80%931915" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal Government 1905\xe2\x80\x931915">Liberal Government</a> to separate Anglicanism in Wales from the Church of England. The bill was fiercely resisted by members of the Conservative Party and blocked in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, but it was eventually passed under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act 1911</a>.\n</p><p>The opposition to disestablishment was led by the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> politician <a href="/wiki/F._E._Smith" class="mw-redirect" title="F. E. Smith">F. E. Smith</a>, who characterised the disestablishment bill as "a Bill which has shocked the conscience of every Christian community in Europe." In response to this description, the writer <a href="/wiki/G._K._Chesterton" title="G. K. Chesterton">G. K. Chesterton</a> penned the satirical poem "Antichrist, or the Reunion of Christendom: An Ode" containing the memorable retort "Chuck It, Smith".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Act both <i>disestablished</i> and <i>disendowed</i> the "Church in Wales", the term used to define the part of the Church of England which was to be separated. Disestablishment meant the end of the church\'s special legal status, and Welsh bishops were no longer entitled to sit in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> as <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Lords Spiritual</a>. As the Church in Wales became independent of the state, <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithes</a> were no longer available to the church, leaving it without a major source of income.\n</p><p>Disendowment, which was even more controversial than disestablishment, meant that the endowments of the Church in Wales were partially confiscated, and redistributed to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wales" title="University of Wales">University of Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Wales" title="Local government in Wales">local authorities</a>. Endowments before 1662 were to be confiscated; those of later date were to remain. This was justified by the theory that the pre-1662 endowments had been granted to the national church of the whole population, and hence belonged to the people as a whole rather than to the Church in Wales; understandably, this reasoning was hotly contested. The date 1662 was that of the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Uniformity_1662" title="Act of Uniformity 1662">Act of Uniformity</a> following the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a>; it was after this point that Nonconformist congregations began to develop and the Church of England ceased to be a comprehensive national church. Although secularisation of the cathedrals had been suggested, the Church in Wales retained all the ancient church buildings and the privilege of conducting legal marriages without reference to the civil registrar.\n</p><p>Due to the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in 1914, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a> was passed together with the <a href="/wiki/Suspensory_Act_1914" title="Suspensory Act 1914">Suspensory Act 1914</a>, meaning that the Welsh Church Act would not be implemented for the duration of the war. Disestablishment finally came into effect in 1920. The Church in Wales adopted a written constitution and elected a <a href="/wiki/Governing_Body_of_the_Church_in_Wales" title="Governing Body of the Church in Wales">Governing Body</a> which initially met once a year, but now meets twice annually. The Governing Body has ultimate authority "to approve liturgies, review organizational structures, and secure firm fiscal resources for the mission and ministry of the church". The Church in Wales was one of the first members of the Anglican Communion to adopt <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synodical</a> government.<sup id="cite_ref-Markham_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Markham-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_1920">Since 1920</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Since 1920">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Church_of_England_border_polls_1915%E2%80%931916" title="Church of England border polls 1915\xe2\x80\x931916">Parishes overlapping the border were allowed to vote either to accede to the Church in Wales or to continue in the Church of England</a>; so the line of disestablishment is not the same as the border between the two countries. A few districts in the former counties of <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire" title="Monmouthshire">Monmouthshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radnorshire" title="Radnorshire">Radnorshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flintshire_(historic)" title="Flintshire (historic)">Flintshire</a> remain attached to parishes in the Dioceses of <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Hereford" title="Diocese of Hereford">Hereford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Chester" title="Diocese of Chester">Chester</a> and consequently they are part of the Church of England. A complete English rural deanery with the generalised name <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">March</a> containing Oswestry and areas to the north-west of Shrewsbury, was transferred from its historic setting in the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_St_Asaph" title="Diocese of St Asaph">Diocese of St Asaph</a> to be consistent with the civil border there. The churches of St Mary, Caernarfon, and <a href="/wiki/Llangadwaladr" title="Llangadwaladr">Llangadwaladr</a>, Anglesey, were transferred from the Diocese of Chester to that of Bangor.\n</p><p>Today, the Church in Wales is fully independent of both the state and the Church of England. It is an independent member of the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, as are the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Episcopal_Church" title="Scottish Episcopal Church">Scottish Episcopal Church</a>.\n</p><p>In the first years of the 21st century, the Church in Wales has begun to engage in numerous debates. These particularly concern the appointment of women to the episcopate and the provincial recognition of the equal statuses of the Welsh and English languages in all aspects of church life.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Following disestablishment in 1920, the Church in Wales initially fared better than the Nonconformist churches, which suffered a decline during the late 20th century. The Anglican Church does not have "a single definition of \'membership\' in the Church in Wales."<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 2006 the average weekly attendance was recorded at 6,780 aged under 18 and 39,490 aged over 18. The highest attendance was at Easter, with 68,120 at worship (68,837 in 2007).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\nIn 2014, the attendance in the Church in Wales was 52,021 at Easter: a decline of about 16,000 members since 2007, but an increase from 2013. Also, in 2014, nineteen churches were closed or made redundant. Overall, in 2014, the Church in Wales reported 152,000 attenders in its parishes and congregations, compared to 105,000 in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 2015 statistics, when all "other major acts of worship" are included, the church reported having 206,000 total attenders. "Such additional services, which include civic services, family services, Remembrance, Carol and Christingle services, registered a total attendance of some 206,000 in 2015, compared with 152,000 in 2014."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the World Mission Atlas Project, the Church in Wales has 350,000 affiliates and 1,500 congregations.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Anglican_dioceses_in_the_United_Kingdom_and_Ireland" title="List of Anglican dioceses in the United Kingdom and Ireland">List of Anglican dioceses in the United Kingdom and Ireland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Church_in_Wales_flag.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Church_in_Wales_flag.svg/200px-Church_in_Wales_flag.svg.png" width="200" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Church_in_Wales_flag.svg/300px-Church_in_Wales_flag.svg.png 1.5x, class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Church_in_Wales_flag.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Church_in_Wales" title="Flag of the Church in Wales">Flag of the Church in Wales</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Polity" title="Polity">polity</a> of the Church in Wales is <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_church_governance" class="mw-redirect" title="Episcopal church governance">episcopal church governance</a>, which is the same as other Anglican churches.\n</p><p>Prior to 1920, there were four dioceses in Wales, all part of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Canterbury" title="Province of Canterbury">Province of Canterbury</a> and each led by its own <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Bangor" title="Diocese of Bangor">Diocese of Bangor</a></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_St_Asaph" title="Diocese of St Asaph">Diocese of St Asaph</a></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_St_David%27s" title="Diocese of St David&#39;s">Diocese of St David\'s</a></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Llandaff" title="Diocese of Llandaff">Diocese of Llandaff</a></li></ul>\n<p>Two additional dioceses were erected soon after the creation of the Church in Wales:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Monmouth" title="Diocese of Monmouth">Diocese of Monmouth</a> in 1921</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Swansea_and_Brecon" title="Diocese of Swansea and Brecon">Diocese of Swansea and Brecon</a> in 1923</li></ul>\n<p>Monmouth was created from one of the archdeaconries of Llandaff diocese. Swansea and Brecon was created from the eastern part of St David\'s diocese, largely corresponding to the city and county of Swansea and the traditional counties of Breconshire and Radnorshire.\n</p><p>Each diocese is divided into two or three archdeaconries, with 15 of these in total. Each has an archdeacon, who is responsible to the bishop for its administration. The archdeaconries are further divided into deaneries.\n</p><p>Each diocese has its own cathedral, the "mother church" of the diocese and the seat of the bishop. In the cathedral are held important events such as the enthronement of a new bishop. Each cathedral has a Dean, appointed to manage the cathedral, with the assistance of the Chapter. Together with the Archdeacons, the Dean of the Cathedral is one of the most prominent clerics of the diocese, after the Bishop. The Chapter is composed of the Dean and a number of Canons selected from among the clerics of the diocese.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diocesan_coats_of_arms">Diocesan coats of class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Archbishop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Until 1920 the Welsh church was part of the Church of England and under the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_bishop#Anglican" title="Metropolitan bishop">metropolitical</a> jurisdiction of the Archbishop of Canterbury. Since independence in 1920, the Church in Wales has been led by the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Wales" title="Archbishop of Wales">Archbishop of Wales</a>, who is both the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_bishop#Anglican" title="Metropolitan bishop">metropolitan bishop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Primate_(bishop)#Anglican_Communion" title="Primate (bishop)">primate</a>. The Archbishop of Wales is elected from the currently seated diocesan bishops and continues as a diocesan after election. It is a commonly held misconception that there must be six diocesan bishops in order to hold the electoral college for the Archbishop of Wales. However, the Constitution of the Church in Wales specifies no quantity of Bishops, simply referring to the electoral college as being made up of "the Bishops and the first three clerical and the first three lay Episcopal Electors on the list of each Diocese in the Church in Wales."<sup id="cite_ref-abe_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abe-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In an archiepiscopal vacancy, the senior Bishop by appointment is acting archbishop.<sup id="cite_ref-aab_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aab-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A former Archbishop of Wales, <a href="/wiki/Rowan_Williams" title="Rowan Williams">Rowan Williams</a>, became the first Welsh-born <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>. He was consecrated and enthroned as Bishop of Monmouth in 1992 and as Archbishop of Wales in 1999. He was appointed by the Queen (his appointment having been proposed by the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Appointments_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Appointments Commission">Crown Appointments Commission</a>) as Archbishop of Canterbury in July 2002.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diocesan_bishops">Diocesan bishops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Diocesan bishops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Unlike bishops in the Church of England, each bishop of the Church in Wales is elected by an "electoral college" which consists of representatives of the diocese in which a vacancy occurs, representatives of the other dioceses in Wales and all bishops of the church. In 2013 the Church in Wales officially agreed to the ordination of women as bishops, five years after a previous proposal for their ordination failed in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In descending order of seniority, the current<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2018)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> bench of Welsh bishops is:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/John_Davies_(bishop_of_Swansea_and_Brecon)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Davies (bishop of Swansea and Brecon)">John Davies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Swansea_and_Brecon" title="Bishop of Swansea and Brecon">Bishop of Swansea and Brecon</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Andrew_John" title="Andrew John">Andrew John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Bangor" title="Bishop of Bangor">Bishop of Bangor</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gregory_Cameron" title="Gregory Cameron">Gregory Cameron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_St_Asaph" title="Bishop of St Asaph">Bishop of St Asaph</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Richard_Pain" title="Richard Pain">Richard Pain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Monmouth" title="Bishop of Monmouth">Bishop of Monmouth</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Joanna_Penberthy" title="Joanna Penberthy">Joanna Penberthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_St_David%27s" title="Bishop of St David&#39;s">Bishop of St David\'s</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/June_Osborne" title="June Osborne">June Osborne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Llandaff" title="Bishop of Llandaff">Bishop of Llandaff</a></li></ul>\n<p>In cases where a see is vacant due to the death or <a href="/wiki/Translation_(ecclesiastical)" title="Translation (ecclesiastical)">translation</a> of a bishop, episcopal acts such as ordinations and confirmations are carried out by the archbishop or by another bishop appointed for that purpose by the archbishop.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assistant_bishops">Assistant bishops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Assistant bishops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Assistant bishops may be appointed within the Church in Wales. In practice, diocesan assistant bishops have only been appointed within the diocese of the archbishop, in order to assist him with diocesan episcopal functions. There are no assistant bishops at present in the Church in Wales. The previous archbishop had an assistant bishop within the Diocese of Llandaff. From April 2009 to April 2017 the post was held by <a href="/wiki/David_Wilbourne" title="David Wilbourne">David Wilbourne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Provincial_assistant_bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Provincial assistant bishop">provincial assistant bishop</a> was appointed in 1996 to provide episcopal ministry to congregations which could not accept the ministry of bishops who ordained women. <a href="/wiki/David_Thomas_(bishop)" title="David Thomas (bishop)">David Thomas</a> held the position for twelve years, retiring in 2008. At that time the Bench of Bishops decided that it would not continue to appoint a specific bishop to minister to those who cannot in conscience accept the ordination of women as priests. This point was reiterated by Barry Morgan at the Governing Body of the Church in Wales in September 2013 during the debate on whether or not the Church in Wales would ordain women to the episcopate.\n</p><p>Historically, there have been <a href="/wiki/Suffragan_bishop" title="Suffragan bishop">suffragan bishops</a> both before and since disestablishment, including two <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Swansea" title="Bishop of Swansea">Bishops of Swansea</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Maenan" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Maenan">Bishop of Maenan</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Representative_Body">Representative Body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Representative Body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Representative_Body_of_the_Church_in_Wales" title="Representative Body of the Church in Wales">Representative Body of the Church in Wales</a> is responsible for the care of the church\'s property and for funding many of the activities of the church, including support for priests\' stipends and pensions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governing_Body">Governing Body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Governing Body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Governing Body is responsible for decisions that affect the Church\'s Faith, Order and Worship. It also has powers to make regulations for the general management and good government of the Church, and its property and affairs. The Governing Body is the supreme legislature of the Church in Wales, broadly speaking the Parliament of the Church in Wales. It usually meets twice a year to receive reports and make decisions on matters brought before it.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worship_and_liturgy">Worship and liturgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Worship and liturgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Church in Wales as a whole tends to be predominantly <a href="/wiki/High_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="High Church">High Church</a>, meaning that many of the traditions are inherited from the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Movement" title="Oxford Movement">Oxford Movement</a> in more rural dioceses such as St David\'s and Bangor and especially in the industrial parishes of Llandaff and Monmouth. Although the province tends more toward liberal and <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Catholic">Anglo-Catholic</a> positions in theology and liturgy, it also has a tradition of <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">evangelicalism</a>, especially in the southern parts of Wales, and the university town of <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a>. In the 1960s there was a revival of evangelicalism within the Church in Wales and the Evangelical Fellowship of the Church in Wales exists to support such members of the church.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_of_Common_Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Book of Common Prayer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An Act of Parliament passed in the year 1563, entitled "An Act for the Translating of the Bible and the Divine Service into the Welsh Tongue," ordered that the Old and New Testament, together with the <i>Book of Common Prayer</i>, were to be translated into <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>. A translation by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Davies_(bishop)" title="Richard Davies (bishop)">Richard Davies</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/St_David%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="St David&#39;s">St David\'s</a> and the scholar <a href="/wiki/William_Salesbury" title="William Salesbury">William Salesbury</a> was published in 1567<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Toy" title="Humphrey Toy">Humphrey Toy</a> as <i>Y Llyfr Gweddi Gyffredin</i>. A new revision&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 based on the 1662 English revision and probably by <a href="/wiki/George_Griffith_(bishop)" title="George Griffith (bishop)">George Griffith</a>, Bishop of <a href="/wiki/St_Asaph" title="St Asaph">St Asaph</a>&#160;- was published in 1664.(<a href="#CITEREFMuss-Arnolt1914">Muss-Arnolt 1914</a>, Ch VII)  The 1662 book and its Welsh equivalent continued to be used,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> even after the Church in Wales was disestablished in 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="per WP:CIRCULAR (May 2018)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The Church in Wales began revising the Book of Common Prayer in the 1950s. The first material authorised for experimental use was a <a href="/wiki/Lectionary" title="Lectionary">lectionary</a> in 1956, followed by a baptism and confirmation service in 1958, an order for Holy Matrimony in 1960, and an order for the Burial of the Dead in 1962. These did not however enjoy widespread use. In 1966 an experimental order for the Holy Eucharist was authorised. This was the first to enjoy widespread use. Revision continued throughout the 60s and 70s, with an experimental version of morning and evening prayer in 1969. In 1971 a definitive version of baptism and confirmation was authorised, replacing the equivalent in the 1662 <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>. This was followed in 1974 with a definitive order for the Burial of the Dead, and in 1975 with a definitive order for Holy Matrimony. It was hoped that a new Book of Common Prayer for the church in Wales would be produced in 1981. However, in 1979 a definitive version of the Holy Eucharist failed to gain a two-thirds majority in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Clergy" title="House of Clergy">House of Clergy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Laity" title="House of Laity">House of Laity</a> at the Governing Body. A light revision of the 1966 experimental Eucharist was approved by the Governing Body, and the Book of Common Prayer for use in the Church in Wales was authorised in 1984. This Prayer Book is unique in that it is in traditional English. The Church in Wales first considered a modern language Eucharist in the early 70s but this received a lukewarm reception. A modern language Eucharist (The Holy Eucharist in modern language) was authorised alongside the new prayer book in 1984, but this did not enjoy widespread use. In 1990 new initiation services were authorised, followed in 1992 by an alternative order for morning and evening prayer<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> in 1994 by an alternative order for the Holy Eucharist, in 1995 by the alternative calendar lectionary and <a href="/wiki/Collect" title="Collect">collects</a>, and in 1998 an order for compline was produced. These enjoyed widespread use. In 2003 a new calendar and collects was made part of the Book of Common Prayer for use in the Church in Wales. This was followed in 2004 by an order for the Holy Eucharist, services for Christian initiation in 2006 and in 2009 by daily prayer. Experimental services continued, with an ordinal produced in 2004, Ministry to the Sick and Housebound in 2007, healing services in 2008, funeral services in 2009, and in 2010 marriage services which became part of the Book of Common Prayer in 2013. The ordinal was made part of the prayer book the following year. In 2017 prayers for a child were produced which were published on line.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_publications">Other publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Discontinued publications which frequently provided articles of sub-academic quality were <i><a href="/wiki/Province" title="Province">Province</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yr_Haul_%C3%A2%27r_Gangell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yr Haul \xc3\xa2&#39;r Gangell (page does not exist)">Yr Haul \xc3\xa2\'r Gangell</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Y_Llan" title="Y Llan">Y Llan</a></i>. Bi-annual news from the Governing Body meeting is released in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highlights_(church_publication)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Highlights (church publication) (page does not exist)">Highlights</a></i>. News is predominantly circulated on the Church in Wales\' Provincial and Diocesan Websites, and in various Diocesan Magazines.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Doctrine_and_practice">Doctrine and practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Doctrine and practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglican_doctrine" title="Anglican doctrine">Anglican doctrine</a></div>\n<p>Central to the teaching of the Church in Wales is the life, death and resurrection of <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>.  The basic teachings of the church, or <a href="/wiki/Catechism" title="Catechism">catechism</a>, include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Jesus Christ is fully human and fully God. He died and was resurrected from the dead.</li>\n<li>Jesus continues to provide the way to eternal life for those who believe.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testaments</a> were written by people "under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit".  The <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">Apocrypha</a> are additional books that are used in Christian worship, but not for the formation of doctrine.</li>\n<li>The two great and necessary <a href="/wiki/Anglican_sacraments" title="Anglican sacraments">sacraments</a> are <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">Holy Baptism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Holy Eucharist</a></li>\n<li>Other <a href="/wiki/Anglican_sacraments" title="Anglican sacraments">sacramental rites</a> are <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holy_orders" title="Holy orders">holy orders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matrimony" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrimony">matrimony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">reconciliation of a penitent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anointing_of_the_sick" title="Anointing of the sick">anointing of the sick</a>.</li>\n<li>Belief in <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heaven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">hell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Second_coming" class="mw-redirect" title="Second coming">Jesus\' return in glory</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>The balance of Scripture, tradition and reason as authority for faith and practice is traced to the work of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hooker" title="Richard Hooker">Richard Hooker</a>, a sixteenth-century <a href="/wiki/Apologetics" title="Apologetics">apologist</a>. In Hooker\'s model, Scripture is the primary means of arriving at doctrine, and things stated plainly in Scripture are accepted as true. Issues that are ambiguous are determined by tradition, which is checked by reason.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordination_of_women">Ordination of women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Ordination of women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A proposal to ordain women as priests was introduced and debated in 1995 after it had failed to secure a two-thirds majority in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The ordination of women to the priesthood was approved by the two-thirds majority in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The Church in Wales has ordained women as priests since 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Prior to 1997, women were permitted to serve as deacons.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The first deaconess was consecrated in 1884.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In 2013, the church voted to allow women to serve as bishops. In 2016, Canon <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Penberthy" title="Joanna Penberthy">Joanna Penberthy</a> was elected the first <a href="/wiki/Ordination_of_women" title="Ordination of women">woman bishop</a> in the church.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Penberthy was enthroned as <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_St_Davids" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of St Davids">Bishop of St Davids</a> on 11 February 2017. Also in 2017, a woman, the Very Revd <a href="/wiki/June_Osborne" title="June Osborne">June Osborne</a>, was appointed to be bishop of <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Llandaff" title="Diocese of Llandaff">Llandaff</a> becoming the second woman bishop in the Welsh church.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Same-sex_unions_and_LGBT_clergy">Same-sex unions and LGBT clergy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Same-sex unions and LGBT clergy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality_and_the_Anglican_Communion" title="Homosexuality and the Anglican Communion">Homosexuality and the Anglican Communion</a></div>\n<p>Beginning in the 1980s, the Church in Wales embarked on an increasingly open stand on various issues including economic justice, the ordination of women and inclusion of homosexual people. In some areas, such as human <a href="/wiki/Religion_and_sexuality#Christianity_and_sexuality" title="Religion and sexuality">sexuality</a>, the church establishment has faced resistance from congregations. In 2005, the church allowed gay priests to enter into <a href="/wiki/Civil_partnership_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Civil partnership in the United Kingdom">civil partnerships</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> "In the wake of civil partnerships, the Welsh Bishops sought legal advice, and told gay partnered clergy that they were welcome, as well as gay ordination candidates."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Speaking on such partnerships, it was communicated that \xe2\x80\x9cThe Church in Wales has no formal view on whether people in civil partnerships who are in a sexual relationship can serve as clergy. If the issue arises, it is up to the relevant Bishop to decide."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, the Welsh church does not require abstinence for clergy in civil unions.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Regarding transgender issues, an officer announced that the church believes transgender people "should be acknowledged and celebrated in their new gender."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Currently, "the Church has published prayers that may be said with a couple following the celebration of a [same-sex] civil partnership or civil marriage."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Currently, "the Church in Wales is much more liberal on this issue [than the Church of England]" and is discussing the possibility of blessing or performing same-sex marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In 2018, the Welsh Bishops released a statement saying it was "unjust" to not offer formal provision for same-sex marriages and civil partnerships.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Following the bishops\' announcement, the General Synod voted in favour of requesting formal provision for same-sex couples.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The Welsh Church has decided to move forward with possibly offering same-sex marriage and blessing rites for same-sex unions.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_St_Asaph" title="Diocese of St Asaph">Diocese of St. Asaph</a> provides a chaplaincy and services for LGBT people.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The Very Revd <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_John" title="Jeffrey John">Jeffrey John</a>, who is openly gay and in a <a href="/wiki/Civil_union" title="Civil union">civil union</a>, was nearly elected bishop of Llandaff when he "won more than half of a Church in Wales electoral college, but fell short of the two-thirds majority required."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>"The Anglican Church In Wales took the first steps towards allowing clergy to celebrate same sex marriage in its churches when more than half its Governing Body voted in favour of the move."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In the 2016 results, 52% of the Governing Body voted in favour of allowing same-sex marriages in church.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> "Members of the Church in Wales Governing Body voted 61 in favour of gay marriages in church, nine in favour of blessing gay partnerships and 50 for making no change."<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> As a result of the majority support for same-sex couples, but not a two-thirds majority needed to create a same-sex marriage ceremony, the church\'s Bench of Bishops affirmed members in same-sex relationships and "published a series of prayers which may be said with a couple following the celebration of a civil partnership or civil marriage."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The service, in Form One, gives God thanks "for [the two people] who have found such love and companionship in each other, that it has led them to dedicate their lives in support of one another."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecumenical_relations">Ecumenical relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ecumenical relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Like many other Anglican provinces, the Church in Wales entered into <a href="/wiki/Full_communion" title="Full communion">full communion</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholics</a>. The Church in Wales is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Porvoo_Communion" title="Porvoo Communion">Porvoo Communion</a>.  Because of the Anglo-Catholic dominance, relations with the Free Churches (formerly known during establishment times as Nonconformists), ecumenical progress has been slower in Wales than in England.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Church in Wales is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Covenanted_Churches_in_Wales" title="Covenanted Churches in Wales">Covenanted Churches in Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> A covenant (with church unity as an ultimate goal) was signed by the Church in Wales, the Methodist Church, the Presbyterian Church of Wales, the United Reformed Church, and some Baptist churches in 1982 under the title of <i>Enfys</i> ("rainbow").\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
