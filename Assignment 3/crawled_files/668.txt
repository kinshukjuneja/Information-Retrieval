class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;f&#39; in &#39;find&#39;">f</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/e\xc9\xaa/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">e\xc9\xaa</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born 8 September 1933) is an English <a href="/wiki/Playwright" title="Playwright">playwright</a> and <a href="/wiki/Novelist" title="Novelist">novelist</a>. He is best known as the author of the farce <i><a href="/wiki/Noises_Off" title="Noises Off">Noises Off</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the dramas <i><a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_(play)" title="Copenhagen (play)">Copenhagen</a></i> and  <i><a href="/wiki/Democracy_(play)" title="Democracy (play)">Democracy</a></i>.  His novels, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Towards_the_End_of_the_Morning" title="Towards the End of the Morning">Towards the End of the Morning</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Headlong_(Frayn_novel)" title="Headlong (Frayn novel)">Headlong</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Spies_(novel)" title="Spies (novel)">Spies</a></i>, have also been critical and commercial successes, making him one of the handful of writers in the English language to succeed in both drama and prose fiction.  He has also written philosophical works, such as <i>The Human Touch: Our Part in the Creation of the Universe</i> (2006).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Frayn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Frayn was born to a deaf asbestos salesman<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Mill_Hill" title="Mill Hill">Mill Hill</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> then in <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>. He grew up in <a href="/wiki/Ewell" title="Ewell">Ewell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, and was educated at <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Grammar_School" title="Kingston Grammar School">Kingston Grammar School</a>. Following two years of <a href="/wiki/National_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="National Service">National Service</a>, during which he learned Russian at the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Services_School_for_Linguists" title="Joint Services School for Linguists">Joint Services School for Linguists</a>, Frayn read Moral Sciences (<a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">Philosophy</a>) at <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_College,_Cambridge" title="Emmanuel College, Cambridge">Emmanuel College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, graduating in 1957. He then worked as a reporter and columnist for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i>, where he established a reputation as a <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirist</a> and comic writer, and began publishing his plays and novels.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Frayn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The play <i><a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_(play)" title="Copenhagen (play)">Copenhagen</a></i> deals with a historical event, a 1941 meeting  between the Danish <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Niels_Bohr" title="Niels Bohr">Niels Bohr</a> and his prot\xc3\xa9g\xc3\xa9, the German <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>, when Denmark is under German occupation, and Heisenberg is\xe2\x80\x94maybe?\xe2\x80\x94working on the development of an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a>. Frayn was attracted to the topic because it seemed to \'encapsulate something about the difficulty of knowing why people do what they do and\nthere is a parallel between that and the impossibility that Heisenberg established in physics, about ever knowing everything about the behaviour of physical objects\'.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The play explores various possibilities.\n</p><p>Frayn\'s more recent play <i>Democracy</i> ran successfully in London (the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">National Theatre</a>, 2003-4 and <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">West End</a> transfer), <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brooks_Atkinson_Theatre" title="Brooks Atkinson Theatre">Brooks Atkinson Theatre</a>, 2004-5); it dramatised the story of the German chancellor <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a> and his personal assistant, the East German spy <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Guillaume" title="G\xc3\xbcnter Guillaume">G\xc3\xbcnter Guillaume</a>. Five years later, again at the National Theatre, it was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Afterlife_(play)" title="Afterlife (play)">Afterlife</a></i>, a biographical drama of the life of the great Austrian impresario <a href="/wiki/Max_Reinhardt" title="Max Reinhardt">Max Reinhardt</a>, director of the <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a> Festival, which opened at the Lyttelton Theatre in June 2008, starring <a href="/wiki/Roger_Allam" title="Roger Allam">Roger Allam</a> as Reinhardt.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His other original plays include two evenings of short plays, <i>The Two of Us</i> and <i>Alarms and Excursions</i>, the philosophical comedies <i>Alphabetical Order</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Benefactors_(play)" title="Benefactors (play)">Benefactors</a></i>, <i>Clouds</i>, <i>Make and Break</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Here_(play)" title="Here (play)">Here</a></i>, and the farces <i>Donkeys\' Years</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Balmoral_(play)" title="Balmoral (play)">Balmoral</a></i> (also known as <i>Liberty Hall</i>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Noises_Off" title="Noises Off">Noises Off</a></i>, which critic <a href="/wiki/Frank_Rich" title="Frank Rich">Frank Rich</a> in his book <i>The Hot Seat</i> claimed "is, was, and probably always will be the funniest play written in my lifetime."\n</p><p>His novels include <a href="/wiki/Headlong_(Frayn_novel)" title="Headlong (Frayn novel)"><i>Headlong</i></a> (shortlisted for the 1999 <a href="/wiki/Booker_Prize" title="Booker Prize">Booker Prize</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tin_Men" title="The Tin Men">The Tin Men</a></i> (won the 1966 <a href="/wiki/Somerset_Maugham_Award" title="Somerset Maugham Award">Somerset Maugham Award</a>), <i>The Russian Interpreter</i> (1967, <a href="/wiki/Hawthornden_Prize" title="Hawthornden Prize">Hawthornden Prize</a>)  <i><a href="/wiki/Towards_the_End_of_the_Morning" title="Towards the End of the Morning">Towards the End of the Morning</a></i>, <i>Sweet Dreams</i>, <i>A Landing on the Sun</i>, <a href="/wiki/A_Very_Private_Life_(novel)" class="mw-redirect" title="A Very Private Life (novel)"><i>A Very Private Life</i></a>, <i>Now You Know</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Skios" title="Skios">Skios, long listed for the Man Booker Prize 2012</a></i>. His novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Spies_(novel)" title="Spies (novel)">Spies</a></i>, was long listed for the Man Booker Prize and won the <a href="/wiki/Whitbread_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Whitbread Prize">Whitbread Prize</a> for Fiction in 2002. He has also written a book about philosophy, <i>Constructions</i>, and a book of his own philosophy, <i>The Human Touch</i>.\n</p><p>His columns for <i>The Guardian</i> and <i>The Observer</i> (collected in <i>The Day of the Dog</i>, <i>The Book of Fub</i> and <i>On the Outskirts</i>) are models of the comic essay; in the 1980s a number of them were adapted and performed for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Jarvis_(actor)" title="Martin Jarvis (actor)">Martin Jarvis</a>.\n</p><p>He has also written screenplays for the films <i><a href="/wiki/Clockwise_(film)" title="Clockwise (film)">Clockwise</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/John_Cleese" title="John Cleese">John Cleese</a>, <i>First and Last</i> starring <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wilkinson" title="Tom Wilkinson">Tom Wilkinson</a>, <i>Birthday</i>, <i>Jamie on a Flying Visit</i>, and the TV series <i>Making Faces</i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Bron" title="Eleanor Bron">Eleanor Bron</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>He is now considered to be Britain\'s finest translator of <a href="/wiki/Anton_Chekhov" title="Anton Chekhov">Anton Chekhov</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Seagull" title="The Seagull">The Seagull</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Vanya" title="Uncle Vanya">Uncle Vanya</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Sisters_(play)" title="Three Sisters (play)">Three Sisters</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cherry_Orchard" title="The Cherry Orchard">The Cherry Orchard</a></i>) as well as an early untitled work, which he titled <i><a href="/wiki/Wild_Honey_(play)" title="Wild Honey (play)">Wild Honey</a></i> (other translations of the work have called it <i><a href="/wiki/Platonov_(play)" title="Platonov (play)">Platonov</a></i> or <i>Don Juan in the Russian Manner</i>) and a number of Chekhov\'s smaller plays for an evening called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sneeze_(play)" title="The Sneeze (play)">The Sneeze</a></i> (originally performed on the West End by <a href="/wiki/Rowan_Atkinson" title="Rowan Atkinson">Rowan Atkinson</a>).\n</p><p>He also translated <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Trifonov" class="mw-redirect" title="Yuri Trifonov">Yuri Trifonov</a>\'s play <i>Exchange</i>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fruits_of_Enlightenment" title="The Fruits of Enlightenment">The Fruits of Enlightenment</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Anouilh" title="Jean Anouilh">Jean Anouilh</a>\'s <i>Number One</i>.\n</p><p>In 1980, he presented the Australian journey of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Railway_Journeys_of_the_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Railway Journeys of the World">Great Railway Journeys of the World</a></i>. His journey took him from <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> to <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Pacific" title="Indian Pacific">Indian Pacific</a> with side visits to the <a href="/wiki/Lithgow_Zig_Zag" title="Lithgow Zig Zag">Lithgow Zig Zag</a> and a journey on <a href="/wiki/The_Ghan" title="The Ghan">The Ghan</a>\'s old route from <a href="/wiki/Marree,_South_Australia" title="Marree, South Australia">Marree</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a> shortly before the opening of the new line from <a href="/wiki/Tarcoola,_South_Australia" title="Tarcoola, South Australia">Tarcoola</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a>.\n</p><p>Frayn\'s wife, <a href="/wiki/Claire_Tomalin" title="Claire Tomalin">Claire Tomalin</a>, is a biographer and literary journalist.\n</p>\n<h2><span
