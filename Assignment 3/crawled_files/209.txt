Cathedral">Truro Cathedral</a> has welcome sign in several languages, including Cornish</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>history of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a></b> begins with the pre-Roman inhabitants, including speakers of a Celtic language, <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a>, that would develop into <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_Brittonic_languages" title="Southwestern Brittonic languages">Southwestern Brittonic</a> and then the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a>. Cornwall was part of the territory of the tribe of the <a href="/wiki/Dumnonii" title="Dumnonii">Dumnonii</a> that included modern-day <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>. After a period of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> rule, Cornwall reverted to rule by independent <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romano-British</a> leaders and continued to have a close relationship with <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> as well as southern Ireland, which neighboured across the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Sea" title="Celtic Sea">Celtic Sea</a>. After the collapse of <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>, the remaining territory of Cornwall came into conflict with neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>.\n</p><p>By the middle of the ninth century, Cornwall had fallen under the control of Wessex, but it kept its own culture. In 1337, the title <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a> was created by the English monarchy, to be held by the king\'s eldest son and heir. Cornwall, along with the neighbouring county of <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, maintained <a href="/wiki/Stannary_Courts_and_Parliaments" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannary Courts and Parliaments">Stannary institutions</a> that granted some local control over its most important product, tin, but by the time of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> most vestiges of Cornish autonomy had been removed as England became an increasingly centralised state under the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor dynasty</a>. Conflicts with the centre took place with the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Rebellion_of_1497" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Rebellion of 1497">Cornish Rebellion of 1497</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a> of 1549.\n</p><p>By the 18th century, Cornwall was incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> along with the rest of England and the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> had gone into steep decline. The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> brought huge change to Cornwall, as well as the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">methodism</a> among the general populace, turning the area <a href="/wiki/Nonconformist" title="Nonconformist">nonconformist</a>. Decline of mining in Cornwall resulted in mass emigration overseas and the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_diaspora" title="Cornish diaspora">Cornish diaspora</a>, as well as the start of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Revival" title="Celtic Revival">Celtic Revival</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornish_revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish revival">Cornish revival</a> which resulted in the beginnings of <a href="/wiki/Cornish_nationalism" title="Cornish nationalism">Cornish nationalism</a> in the late 20th century.\n</p><p>Cornwall\'s <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Medieval</a> history, in particular the early Welsh and Breton references to a Cornish King named <a href="/wiki/Arthur" title="Arthur">Arthur</a>, have featured in such legendary works as <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i>, predating the Arthurian legends of the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_Britain" title="Matter of Britain">Matter of Britain</a> (see the <a title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Stone_Age">Late Stone Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Late Stone Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The present human history of Cornwall begins with the reoccupation of Britain after the last Ice Age. The inhabitants may have been related to the <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberians</a> who occupied Spain and Portugal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The upland areas of Cornwall were the parts first open to settlement as the vegetation required little in the way of clearance: they were perhaps first occupied in <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> times (Palaeolithic remains are almost non-existent in Cornwall). Many <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megaliths</a> of this period exist in Cornwall and prehistoric remains in general are more numerous in Cornwall than in any other English county except <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>. The remains are of various kinds and include <a href="/wiki/Menhir" title="Menhir">menhirs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">barrows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hut_circle" title="Hut circle">hut circles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age">Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a associated with the <a href="/wiki/Beaker_culture" title="Beaker culture">Beaker culture</a>. Tin is necessary to make <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> from copper, and by about 1600 BCE the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> was experiencing a trade boom driven by the export of tin across Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This prosperity helped feed the skilfully wrought gold ornaments recovered from <a href="/wiki/Wessex_culture" title="Wessex culture">Wessex culture</a> sites.\n</p><p>There is evidence of a relatively large-scale disruption of cultural practices around the 12th century BCE that some scholars think may indicate an invasion or migration into southern Britain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age">Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_(1906)_(14591082390).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_%281906%29_%2814591082390%29.jpg/220px-The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_%281906%29_%2814591082390%29.jpg" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_%281906%29_%2814591082390%29.jpg/330px-The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_%281906%29_%2814591082390%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_%281906%29_%2814591082390%29.jpg/440px-The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_%281906%29_%2814591082390%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4120" data-file-height="2564" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Victoria_history_of_the_county_of_Cornwall_(1906)_(14591082390).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map of camps and earthworks in Cornwall</div></div></div>\n<p>Around 750 BCE the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> reached Britain, permitting greater scope of agriculture through the use of new iron ploughs and axes. The building of <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> also peaked during the <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">British Iron Age</a>. During broadly the same time (900 to 500 BCE), Celtic cultures and peoples spread across the British Isles.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">British Iron Age</a> Cornwall, like all of Britain south of the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Firth of Forth</a>, was inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (Celtic people)">Britons</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic language</a> spoken at the time, <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a>, eventually developed into several distinct tongues, including <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-payton_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-payton-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first account of Cornwall comes from the Sicilian Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> (c. 90 BCE&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 c. 30 BCE), supposedly quoting or paraphrasing the 4th-century BCE geographer <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a>, who had sailed to Britain:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The inhabitants of that part of Britain called <i>Belerion</i> (or Land\'s End) from their intercourse with foreign merchants, are civilised in their manner of life. They prepare the tin, working very carefully the earth in which it is produced ... Here then the merchants buy the tin from the natives and carry it over to <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, and after travelling overland for about thirty days, they finally bring their href="/wiki/Ogham_inscription" title="Ogham inscription">Ogham inscriptions</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Claims have been made that the Phoenicians traded directly with Cornwall for tin. There is no archaeological evidence for this and modern historians have debunked earlier antiquarian constructions of "the Phoenician legacy of Cornwall",<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> including belief that the Phoenicians even settled Cornwall.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By the time that Classical written sources appear, Cornwall was inhabited by tribes speaking <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a>. The ancient Greeks and Romans used the name <i>Belerion</i> or <i>Bolerium</i> for the south-west tip of the island of Britain, but the late-Roman source for the <a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Cosmography" title="Ravenna Cosmography">Ravenna Cosmography</a> (compiled about 700 CE) introduces a place-name <i>Puro coronavis</i>, the first part of which seems to be a misspelling of <i>Duro</i> (meaning Fort). This appears to indicate that the tribe of the <a href="/wiki/Cornovii_(Cornwall)" title="Cornovii (Cornwall)">Cornovii</a>, known from earlier Roman sources as inhabitants of an area centred on modern Shropshire, had by about the 5th century established a power-base in the south-west (perhaps at <a href="/wiki/Tintagel" title="Tintagel">Tintagel</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The tribal name is therefore likely to be the origin of <i>Kernow</i> or later <i>Curnow</i> used for Cornwall in the Cornish language. <a href="/wiki/John_Morris_(historian)" title="John Morris (historian)">John Morris</a> suggested that a contingent of the Cornovii was sent to South West Britain at the end of the Roman era, to rule the land there and keep out the invading Irish, but this theory was dismissed by Professor <a href="/wiki/Philip_Payton" title="Philip Payton">Philip Payton</a> in his book <i>Cornwall: A History</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-payton_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-payton-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Cornish Cornovii may even be a completely separate tribe, taking their name from the horn shape of the peninsula.\n</p><p>The English name, Cornwall, comes from the Celtic name, to which the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word <i>Wealas</i> "foreigner" is added.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In pre-Roman times, Cornwall was part of the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>, and was later known to the Anglo-Saxons as "<i>West</i> Wales", to distinguish it from "North Wales" (modern-day Wales).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Cornwall">Roman Cornwall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman Cornwall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a></div>\n<p>During the time of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman dominance in Britain</a>, Cornwall was rather remote from the main centres of Romanisation. The Roman road system extended into Cornwall, but the only known significant Roman sites are three forts:- Tregear near <a href="/wiki/Nanstallon" title="Nanstallon">Nanstallon</a> was discovered in early 1970s, the other two found more recently at <a href="/wiki/Restormel_Castle" title="Restormel Castle">Restormel Castle</a>, Lostwithiel (discovered 2007) and a fort near to St Andrew\xe2\x80\x99s Church in <a href="/wiki/Calstock" title="Calstock">Calstock</a> (discovered early in 2007).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A Roman style villa was found at Magor Farm near <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pottery and other evidence suggesting the presence of an ironworks have been found at the undisclosed location near St Austell, Cornwall. Experts say the discovery challenges the belief that Romans did not settle in the county and stopped in neighbouring Devon.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Furthermore, the British tin trade had been largely eclipsed by the more convenient supply from <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church,_Tintagel_(5014).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church%2C_Tintagel_%285014%29.jpg/220px-Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church%2C_Tintagel_%285014%29.jpg" width="220" height="341" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church%2C_Tintagel_%285014%29.jpg/330px-Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church%2C_Tintagel_%285014%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church%2C_Tintagel_%285014%29.jpg/440px-Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church%2C_Tintagel_%285014%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2454" data-file-height="3803" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roman_stone_in_St_Materiana%27s_Church,_Tintagel_(5014).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Roman milestone in St Materiana\'s Church, Tintagel</div></div></div>\n<p>Only a few <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads#Milestones_and_markers" title="Roman roads">Roman milestones</a> have been found in Cornwall; two have been recovered from around Tintagel in the north, one at Mynheer Farm<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> near the hill fort at <a href="/wiki/Carn_Brea,_Redruth" title="Carn Brea, Redruth">Carn Brea, Redruth</a>, another two close to <a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Mount" title="St Michael&#39;s Mount">St Michael\'s Mount</a>, one of which is preserved at <a href="/wiki/Breage_Parish_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Breage Parish Church">Breage Parish Church</a>, and one in <a href="/wiki/St_Hilary%27s_Church,_St_Hilary_(Cornwall)" title="St Hilary&#39;s Church, St Hilary (Cornwall)">St Hilary\'s Church, St Hilary (Cornwall)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The stone at <a href="/wiki/Tintagel_Parish_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Tintagel Parish Church">Tintagel Parish Church</a> bears an inscription to Imperator Caesar <a href="/wiki/Licinius" title="Licinius">Licinius</a>, and the other stone at <a href="/wiki/Trethevy" title="Trethevy">Trethevy</a> is inscribed to the Imperial Caesars <a href="/wiki/Trebonianus_Gallus" title="Trebonianus Gallus">Trebonianus Gallus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volusianus" title="Volusianus">Volusianus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Fleuriot" title="L\xc3\xa9on Fleuriot">L\xc3\xa9on Fleuriot</a>, however, Cornwall remained closely integrated with neighbouring territories by well-travelled sea routes. Fleuriot suggests that an overland route connecting <a href="/wiki/Padstow" title="Padstow">Padstow</a> with <a href="/wiki/Fowey" title="Fowey">Fowey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lostwithiel" title="Lostwithiel">Lostwithiel</a> served, in Roman times, as a convenient conduit for trade between <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a>) and the western parts of the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Archaeological sites at <a href="/wiki/Chysauster_Ancient_Village" title="Chysauster Ancient Village">Chysauster Ancient Village</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carn_Euny" title="Carn Euny">Carn Euny</a> in West Penwith and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> demonstrate a uniquely Cornish \'courtyard house\' architecture built in stone of the Roman period, entirely distinct from that of southern Britain, yet with parallels in Atlantic Ireland, North Britain and the Continent, and influential on the later development of stone-built fortified homesteads known in Cornwall as "<a href="/wiki/Ringfort" title="Ringfort">Rounds</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" wake of the Roman withdrawal from Great Britain in about 410, Saxons and other Germanic peoples were able to conquer and settle most of the east of the island over the next two centuries. In the west, Devon and Cornwall held out as the British kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>.\n</p><p>Dumnonia had close cultural contacts with Christian Ireland, Wales, Romano-Celtic <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a> via the West Atlantic trade network, and there is exceptional archaeological evidence for <a href="/wiki/Late_Antique" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antique">Late Antique</a> trading contacts at the stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Tintagel" title="Tintagel">Tintagel</a> in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-Tintagel_Castle_1999_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tintagel_Castle_1999-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Breton language is closer to Cornish than to Welsh, showing the close contacts between the areas.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Wessex">Relationship with Wessex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationship with Wessex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The early kings of Wessex are notable for their possible <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Wessex" title="List of monarchs of Wessex">prevalence of Brythonic names</a><sup id="cite_ref-Dumville_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumville-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and therefore care should be exercised in assuming a stark ethnic antipathy between emergent \'British\' and \'English\' identities, peoples and culture; rather a struggle for dominance of warring elites more or less aligned with eastern \'Germanic\' and western \'Romano-Celtic\' cultures and peoples.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumville_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumville-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Atlantic Brythons were often recorded in alliance with Scandinavian forces such as the <a href="/wiki/Danes" title="Danes">Danes</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> in Brittany, up to the period of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the early eighth century, Cornwall was probably a sub-division of Dumnonia, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> records that in 710 <a href="/wiki/Geraint_of_Dumnonia" title="Geraint of Dumnonia">Geraint</a>, king of Dumnonia, fought against <a href="/wiki/Ine_of_Wessex" title="Ine of Wessex">Ine</a>, king of Wessex. The <i><a href="/wiki/Annales_Cambriae" title="Annales Cambriae">Annales Cambriae</a></i> states that in 722 the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hehil" title="Battle of Hehil">Battle of Hehil</a> "among the Cornishmen" was won by the Britons. In the view of the historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles-Edwards" title="Thomas Charles-Edwards">Thomas Charles-Edwards</a>, this probably indicates that Dumnonia had fallen by 722, and that the British victory of that year against Wessex secured the survival of the new kingdom of Cornwall for another one hundred and fifty years. There were intermittent battles between Wessex and Cornwall for the rest of the eighth century, and <a href="/wiki/Cuthred_of_Wessex" title="Cuthred of Wessex">Cuthred</a>, king of Wessex, fought against the Cornish in 743 and 753.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, according to John Reuben Davies, Dumnonia ceased to exist around the beginning of the ninth century, but:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>The kingdom of Cornwall, on the other hand, remained as an independent British territory in the face of pressure from Wessex, cut off from fellow Brittonic-speakers in Wales and Brittany by the sea and the West Saxons.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>In 814 <a href="/wiki/Egbert_of_Wessex" class="mw-redirect" title="Egbert of Wessex">King Egbert</a> of Wessex ravaged Cornwall "from the east to the west", and the <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i> records that in 825 the Cornish fought the men of Devon. In 838 the Cornish in alliance with <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> were defeated by the West Saxons at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hingston_Down" title="Battle of Hingston Down">Battle of Hingston Down</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This was the last recorded battle between Cornwall and Wessex, and possibly resulted in the loss of Cornish independence.<sup id="cite_ref-Padel_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Padel-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 875, the <i>Annales Cambriae</i> record that king <a href="/wiki/Donyarth" title="Donyarth">Dungarth</a> of Cornwall drowned, yet <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a> had been able to go hunting in Cornwall a decade earlier suggesting Dungarth was likely an under-king. <a href="/wiki/Kenstec" title="Kenstec">Kenstec</a> (c.833-c.870) became the first <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Cornwall" title="Bishop of Cornwall">bishop of Cornwall</a> to profess obedience to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, and in the same period the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Sherborne" title="Bishop of Sherborne">bishop of Sherborne</a> was instructed to visit Cornwall annually to root out the errors of the Cornish church, further indications that Cornwall was becoming subject to Wessex in the middle of the ninth century.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CE431_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CE431-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In the 880s Alfred the Great was able to leave estates in Cornwall in his will.<sup id="cite_ref-K175_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K175-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_of_Malmesbury" title="William of Malmesbury">William of Malmesbury</a>, writing around 1120, says that in about 927 King <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelstan" title="\xc3\x86thelstan">\xc3\x86thelstan</a> of England expelled the Cornish from <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> and fixed Cornwall\'s eastern boundary at the <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">River Tamar</a>. T. M. Charles-Edwards dismisses William\'s account as an "improbable story" on the ground that Cornwall was by then firmly under English control.<sup id="cite_ref-Charles-Edwards,_pp._22,_432_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles-Edwards,_pp._22,_432-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> John Reuben Davies sees the expedition as the suppression of a British uprising, which was followed by the confinement of the Cornish beyond the <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">Tamar</a> and the creation of a separate bishopric for Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_p._343_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies_p._343-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Although English kings granted land in the eastern part in the ninth century, no grants are recorded in the western half until the mid-tenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Padel_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Padel-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cornwall now acquired Anglo-Saxon administrative features such as the hundred system. Unlike Devon, Cornwall\'s culture was not anglicised. Most people still spoke Cornish, and place-names are still mainly Brittonic.<sup id="cite_ref-Charles-Edwards,_pp._22,_432_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles-Edwards,_pp._22,_432-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davies_p._343_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies_p._343-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In 944 \xc3\x86thelstan\'s successor, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Edmund I of England">Edmund I</a>, styled himself \'King of the English and ruler of this province of the Britons\'.<sup id="cite_ref-T287_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-T287-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The antiquarian <a href="/wiki/William_Camden" title="William Camden">William Camden</a> wrote in his book <i>Britannia</i> in 1607:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>As for those Cornwallians, although they stoutly bent all their force together in defence of their Countrey, yet soone became they subject to the Saxons, as who neither matched then in number, neither was their Countrey sufficiently fenced by nature to defend them.<sup id="cite_ref-camden_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-camden-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cornish_Church">The Cornish Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Cornish Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Cornwall" title="Christianity in Cornwall">Christianity in Cornwall</a></div>\n<p>The first centuries after the Romans left are known as the \'age of the saints\', as <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Christianity</a> and a revival of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_art" title="Celtic art">Celtic art</a> spread from Ireland, Wales and Scotland into Great Britain, <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, and beyond. According to tradition the area was evangelised in the 5th and 6th centuries by the children of <a href="/wiki/Brychan" title="Brychan">Brychan Brycheiniog</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a> from Ireland. <a href="/wiki/List_of_Cornish_Saints" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Cornish Saints">Cornish saints</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Piran" title="Saint Piran">Piran</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Meriasek" class="mw-redirect" title="St Meriasek">Meriasek</a>, or Geraint exercised a religious and arguably political influence; they were often closely connected to the local civil rulers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and in some cases were kings themselves. There was an important monastery at <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a> and sporadically, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Cornwall" title="Bishop of Cornwall">Cornish bishops</a> are named in various records.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_(New_Minster_Liber_Vitae)_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944,_f_30v.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_%28New_Minster_Liber_Vitae%29_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944%2C_f_30v.jpg/170px-Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_%28New_Minster_Liber_Vitae%29_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944%2C_f_30v.jpg" width="170" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_%28New_Minster_Liber_Vitae%29_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944%2C_f_30v.jpg/255px-Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_%28New_Minster_Liber_Vitae%29_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944%2C_f_30v.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_%28New_Minster_Liber_Vitae%29_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944%2C_f_30v.jpg/340px-Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_%28New_Minster_Liber_Vitae%29_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944%2C_f_30v.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="732" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Will_of_Alfred_the_Great_(New_Minster_Liber_Vitae)_-_BL_Stowe_MS_944,_f_30v.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Will of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>, AD 873\xe2\x80\x93888 (11th-century copy, <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> Stowe MS 944, ff. 29v\xe2\x80\x9333r)<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>By the 880s more Saxon priests were being appointed to the Church in Cornwall and they controlled some church estates like Polltun, Caellwic and Landwithan (Pawton, in <a href="/wiki/St_Breock" title="St Breock">St Breock</a>; perhaps <a href="/wiki/Celliwig" title="Celliwig">Celliwig</a> (Kellywick in <a href="/wiki/Egloshayle" title="Egloshayle">Egloshayle</a>?); and <a href="/wiki/Lawhitton" title="Lawhitton">Lawhitton</a>. Eventually they passed these over to Wessex kings. However, according to <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>\'s will the amount of land he owned in Cornwall was very small.<sup id="cite_ref-K175_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K175-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> West of the Tamar <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a> only owned a small area in the <a href="/wiki/Stratton,_Cornwall" title="Stratton, Cornwall">Stratton</a> region, plus a few other small estates around <a href="/wiki/Lifton,_Devon" title="Lifton, Devon">Lifton</a> on Cornish soil east of the Tamar). These were provided to him through the Church whose Canterbury appointed priesthood was increasingly English dominated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The early organisation and affiliations of the Church in Cornwall are unclear, but in the mid-9th century it was led by a Bishop <a href="/wiki/Kenstec" title="Kenstec">Kenstec</a> with his see at <i>Dinurrin</i>, a location which has sometimes been identified as <a href="/wiki/Bodmin" title="Bodmin">Bodmin</a> and sometimes as <a href="/wiki/Gerrans" title="Gerrans">Gerrans</a>. Kenstec acknowledged the authority of <a href="/wiki/Ceolnoth" title="Ceolnoth">Ceolnoth</a>, bringing Cornwall under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>. In the 920s or 930s King Athelstan established a bishopric at <a href="/wiki/St_German%27s_Priory" title="St German&#39;s Priory">St Germans</a> to cover the whole of Cornwall, which seems to have been initially subordinated to the see of <a href="/wiki/Sherborne" title="Sherborne">Sherborne</a> but emerged as a full bishopric in its own right by the end of the 10th century. The first few bishops here were native Cornish, but those appointed from 963 onwards were all English. From around 1027 the see was held jointly with that of <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Crediton" title="Bishop of Crediton">Crediton</a>, and in 1050 they were merged to become the diocese of <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Exeter" title="Bishop of Exeter">Exeter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-T287_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-T287-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_11th_century">The 11th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The 11th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EmpireNorth.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/EmpireNorth.JPG/220px-EmpireNorth.JPG" width="220" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/EmpireNorth.JPG/330px-EmpireNorth.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/EmpireNorth.JPG/440px-EmpireNorth.JPG 2x" data-file-width="674" data-file-height="668" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EmpireNorth.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At the time of <a href="/wiki/Cnut_the_Great" title="Cnut the Great">King Cnut</a>, Wales and Cornwall fell outside his British realms</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1013 Wessex was conquered by a Danish army under the leadership of the Viking leader and King of Denmark <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Forkbeard" title="Sweyn Forkbeard">Sweyn Forkbeard</a>. Sweyn annexed Wessex to his Viking empire which included Denmark and Norway. He did not, however, annex Cornwall, Wales and Scotland, allowing these "client nations" self-rule in return for an annual payment of tribute or "danegeld". Between 1013 and 1035 Cornwall, Wales, much of Scotland and Ireland were not included in the territories of King <a href="/wiki/Canute_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Canute the Great">Canute the Great</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The chronology of English expansion into Cornwall is unclear, but it had been absorbed into England by the reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> (1042\xe2\x80\x931066), when it apparently formed part of <a href="/wiki/Godwin,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Godwin, Earl of Wessex">Godwin\'s</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold\'s</a> earldom of Wessex.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The records of Domesday Book show that by this time the native Cornish landowning class had been almost completely dispossessed and replaced by English landowners, the largest of whom was Harold Godwinson himself.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> continued to be spoken, particularly in west and mid Cornwall, and acquired a number of characteristics establishing its identity as a separate language from <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>. However, Cornwall showed a very different type of settlement pattern from that of Saxon Wessex and places continued, even after 1066, to be named in the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> Cornish tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Mills argues that the Breton rulers of Cornwall, as allies of the Normans, brought about an \'Armorican Return\' <sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> with Cornu-Breton retaining its status as a prestige language.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Post_Norman_conquest_.281066.E2.80.931485.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_Norman_conquest_(1066\xe2\x80\x931485)">Post Norman conquest (1066\xe2\x80\x931485)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Post Norman conquest (1066\xe2\x80\x931485)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tintagel_b.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tintagel_b.jpg/220px-Tintagel_b.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tintagel_b.jpg/330px-Tintagel_b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tintagel_b.jpg/440px-Tintagel_b.jpg 2x" data-file-width="472" data-file-height="708" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tintagel_b.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tintagel_Castle" title="Tintagel Castle">Tintagel Castle</a>: part of ruined curtain wall</div></div></div>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/William_Worcester" title="William Worcester">William Worcester</a>, writing in the 15th century, <a href="/wiki/Cadoc_of_Cornwall" title="Cadoc of Cornwall">Cadoc</a> (Cornish: <i>Kadog</i>) was a survivor of the Cornish royal line<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and was appointed as the first Earl of Cornwall by William the Conqueror following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Brian_of_Brittany" title="Brian of Brittany">Brian of Brittany</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Eudes,_Count_of_Penthi%C3%A8vre" class="mw-redirect" title="Eudes, Count of Penthi\xc3\xa8vre">Eudes, Count of Penthi\xc3\xa8vre</a>, defeated a second raid in the southwest of England, launched from Ireland by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold</a>\'s sons in 1069.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Brian was granted lands in Cornwall but by 1072 he had probably returned to Brittany: he died without issue<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.\n</p><p>Much of the land in Cornwall was seized and transferred into the hands of a new Norman aristocracy, with the lion\'s share going to <a href="/wiki/Robert,_Count_of_Mortain" title="Robert, Count of Mortain">Robert, Count of Mortain</a>, half-brother of <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">King William</a> and the largest landholder in England after the king. Some land was held by King William and by existing monasteries - the remainder by the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Exeter" title="Diocese of Exeter">Bishop of Exeter</a>, and a single manor each by <a href="/wiki/Juhel_de_Totnes" title="Juhel de Totnes">Judhael of Totnes</a> and Gotshelm<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> (brother of <a href="/wiki/Walter_de_Claville" title="Walter de Claville">Walter de Claville</a>).\n</p><p>Robert eventually displaced the Cornish Earl though nothing is known of Cadoc apart from what <a href="/wiki/William_Worcester" title="William Worcester">William Worcester</a> says four centuries later. Four Norman castles were built in east Cornwall at different periods, at <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Castle" title="Launceston Castle">Launceston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trematon" title="Trematon">Trematon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restormel_Castle" title="Restormel Castle">Restormel</a> and Tintagel. A new town grew up around the castle and this became the capital of the county. On several occasions over the following centuries noblemen were created <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Cornwall" title="Earl of Cornwall">Earl of Cornwall</a>, but each time their line soon died out and the title lapsed until revived for a new appointee. In 1336, <a href="/wiki/Edward,_the_Black_Prince" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward, the Black Prince">Edward, the Black Prince</a> was named <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a>, a title that has been awarded to the eldest son of the Sovereign since 1421.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A popular Cornish literature, centred on the religious-themed <a href="/wiki/Mystery_play" title="Mystery play">mystery plays</a>, emerged in the 14th century (see <a href="/wiki/Cornish_literature" title="Cornish literature">Cornish literature</a>) based around <a href="/wiki/Glasney_College" title="Glasney College">Glasney College</a>\xe2\x80\x94the college established by the Bishop of Exeter in the 13th century<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.\n</p><p>It has been claimed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as one of the great ironies of history that three <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish-speaking</a> Cornishmen brought the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> back from the verge of extinction - <a href="/wiki/John_of_Cornwall_(grammarian)" title="John of Cornwall (grammarian)">John of Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Trevisa" title="John Trevisa">John Trevisa</a> and Richard Pencrych.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Trevisa" title="John Trevisa">John of Trevisa</a> was a Cornish cleric instrumental in translation of the Bible into English under <a href="/wiki/John_Wycliffe" title="John Wycliffe">John Wycliffe</a>\'s proto-Reformation and, ironically for a Cornish-speaker, is the third most cited source for the very first appearance of many words in the English language. He also added many notes to his translation c.1387 of the <a href="/wiki/Polychronicon" class="mw-redirect" title="Polychronicon">Polychronicon</a> relating to the geography and culture of Cornwall.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tudor_and_Stuart_period">Tudor and Stuart period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tudor and Stuart period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="1485.E2.80.931558"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1485\xe2\x80\x931558">1485\xe2\x80\x931558</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1485\xe2\x80\x931558">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Tudor_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor England">Tudor England</a></div>\n<p>The general tendency of administrative centralisation under the Tudor dynasty began to undermine Cornwall\'s distinctive status. For example, under the Tudors, the practice of distinguishing between some laws, such as those related to the tin industry, that applied simply <i>in Anglia</i> or <i>in Anglia et Cornubia</i> (in England and Cornwall) ceased.<sup id="cite_ref-DaviesIsles_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DaviesIsles-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Rebellion_of_1497" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Rebellion of 1497">Cornish Rebellion of 1497</a> originated among Cornish tin miners who opposed the raising of taxes by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a> to make war on <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. This levy was resented for the economic hardship it would cause; it also intruded on a special Cornish tax exemption. The rebels marched on London, gaining supporters as they went, but were defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Deptford_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Deptford Bridge">Battle of Deptford Bridge</a>.\n</p><p>The Cornish also rose up in the <a href="/wiki/Prayer_Book_Rebellion" title="Prayer Book Rebellion">Prayer Book Rebellion</a> of 1549. Much of south-western Britain rebelled against the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Uniformity_1549" title="Act of Uniformity 1549">Act of Uniformity 1549</a>, which introduced the obligatory use of the Protestant <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>. Cornwall was mostly Catholic in sympathy at this time; the Act was doubly resented in Cornwall because the Prayer Book was in English only and most <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a> at this time spoke the Cornish language rather than English. They therefore wished church services to continue to be conducted in Latin; although they did not understand this language either, it had the benefit of long-established tradition and lacked the political and cultural connotations of the use of English. Twenty percent of the Cornish population are believed to have been killed during 1549: it is one of the major factors that contributed to the decline in the Cornish language.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="English_Civil_War_.281642.E2.80.931649.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Civil_War_(1642\xe2\x80\x931649)">English Civil War (1642\xe2\x80\x931649)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: English Civil War (1642\xe2\x80\x931649)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG/180px-English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG" width="180" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG/270px-English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG/360px-English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="627" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:English_civil_war_map_1642_to_1645.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maps of territory held by Royalists (red) and Parliamentarians (green), 1642\xe2\x80\x931645</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_in_the_English_Civil_War" title="Cornwall in the English Civil War">Cornwall in the English Civil War</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/First_English_Civil_War" title="First English Civil War">First English Civil War</a></div>\n<p>Cornwall played a significant role during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, as it was a <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalist</a> enclave in the generally <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Parliamentarian</a> south-west. The reason for this was that Cornwall\'s rights and privileges were tied up with the royal <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">Duchy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stannaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannaries">Stannaries</a> and so the Cornish saw the King as protector of their rights and Ducal privileges. The strong local Cornish identity also meant the Cornish would resist any meddling in their affairs by any outsiders. The English Parliament wanted to reduce royal power. Parliamentary forces invaded Cornwall three times and burned the Duchy archives. In 1645 Cornish Royalist leader <a href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Grenville,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Richard Grenville, 1st Baronet">Sir Richard Grenville, 1st Baronet</a> made <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Cornwall" title="Launceston, Cornwall">Launceston</a> his base and he stationed Cornish troops along the <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">River Tamar</a> and issued them with instructions to keep "all foreign troops out of Cornwall". Grenville tried to use "Cornish particularist sentiment" to muster support for the Royalist cause and put a plan to the Prince which would, if implemented, have created a semi-independent Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_and_19th_centuries">18th and 19th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 18th and 19th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c6/Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg/350px-Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg" width="350" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c6/Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg/525px-Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c6/Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg/700px-Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2340" data-file-height="1899" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grose-map-cornwall-q80-2340x1899.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1783 map of Cornwall.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1755_Tsunami">1755 Tsunami</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 1755 Tsunami">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 1 November 1755 at 09:40 the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_earthquake" class="mw-redirect" title="Lisbon earthquake">Lisbon earthquake</a> caused a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> to strike the Cornish coast at around 14:00. The epicentre was approximately 250 miles (400&#160;km) off <a href="/wiki/Cape_St_Vincent" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape St Vincent">Cape St Vincent</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> coast, over 1,000 miles (1,600&#160;km)  south west of the Lizard. At <a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Mount" title="St Michael&#39;s Mount">St Michael\'s Mount</a>, the sea rose suddenly and then retired, ten minutes later it rose 6&#160;ft (1.8&#160;m) very rapidly, then ebbed equally rapidly, and continued to rise and fall for five hours. The sea rose 8&#160;ft (2.4&#160;m) in <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> and 10&#160;ft (3.0&#160;m) at <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">Newlyn</a>. The same effect was reported at St Ives and Hayle. The 18th-century French writer, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Boscowitz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arnold Boscowitz (page does not exist)">Arnold Boscowitz</a>, claimed that "great loss of life and property occurred upon the coasts of Cornwall".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developments_in_tin_mining">Developments in tin mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Developments in tin mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg/220px-Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg/330px-Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg/440px-Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithick1803Locomotive.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Richard_Trevithick" title="Richard Trevithick">Richard Trevithick</a>\'s steam engine.</div></div></div>\n<p>At one time the Cornish were the world\'s foremost experts of mining (See <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon" title="Mining in Cornwall and Devon">Mining in Cornwall and Devon</a> ) and a <a href="/wiki/Camborne_School_of_Mines" title="Camborne School of Mines">School of Mines</a> was established in 1888. As Cornwall\'s reserves of tin began to be exhausted, many <a href="/wiki/Cornish_diaspora" title="Cornish diaspora">Cornishmen emigrated</a> to places such as the Americas, Australia, New Zealand and South Africa where their skills were in demand.\n</p><p>There is no current tin mining undertaken in Cornwall. However, a popular legend says that wherever you may go in the world, if you see a hole in the ground, you\'ll find a Cornishman at the bottom of it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Several Cornish mining words are in use in English language mining terminology, such as <a href="/wiki/Costean" title="Costean">costean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gunnies" title="Gunnies">gunnies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vug" title="Vug">vug</a>.\n</p><p>Since the decline of tin mining, agriculture and fishing, the area\'s economy has become increasingly dependent on tourism\xe2\x80\x94some of Britain\'s most spectacular coastal scenery can be found here. However, Cornwall is one of the poorest parts of Western Europe and it has been granted <a href="/wiki/Objective_1" class="mw-redirect" title="Objective 1">Objective 1</a> status by the EU.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Politics.2C_religion_and_administration"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics,_religion_and_administration">Politics, religion and administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Politics, religion and administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cornwall and Devon were the site of a <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_uprising_in_Cornwall_of_1715" title="Jacobite uprising in Cornwall of 1715">Jacobite rebellion in 1715</a> led by <a href="/wiki/James_Paynter_(Jacobite)" title="James Paynter (Jacobite)">James Paynter</a> of St. Columb. This coincided with the larger and better-known <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_Rising_of_1715" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacobite Rising of 1715">"Fifteen Rebellion"</a> which took place in Scotland and the north of England. However, the Cornish uprising was quickly quashed by the authorities. James Paynter was tried for High Treason but claiming his right as a Cornish tinner was tried in front of a jury of other Cornish tinners and was cleared.\n</p><p>Industrialised communities have long appeared to weaken the pre-eminence of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, and as the Cornish people were readily involved in mining, a rift developed between the Cornish people and their <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> clergy in the early 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Resisting the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">established church</a>, many ordinary Cornish people were <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> or non-religious until the late 18th century, when <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a> was introduced to Cornwall during a series of visits by <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wesley" title="Charles Wesley">Charles Wesley</a>. Methodist separation from the Church of England was made formal in 1795.\n</p><p>In 1841 there were ten <a href="/wiki/Hundreds_of_Cornwall" title="Hundreds of Cornwall">hundreds of Cornwall</a>: <a href="/wiki/Stratton_(hundred)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratton (hundred)">Stratton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lesnewth_(hundred)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lesnewth (hundred)">Lesnewth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triggshire" title="Triggshire">Trigg</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wivelshire" title="Wivelshire">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wivelshire" title="Wivelshire">West Wivelshire</a>; <a href="/wiki/Powdershire" class="mw-redirect" title="Powdershire">Powder</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pydarshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Pydarshire">Pydar</a>; <a href="/wiki/Kerrier_(hundred)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kerrier (hundred)">Kerrier</a>; <a href="/wiki/Penwith_(hundred)" class="mw-redirect" title="Penwith (hundred)">Penwith</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Scilly" class="mw-redirect" title="Scilly">Scilly</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shire</a> suffix has been attached to several of these, notably: the first three formed Triggshire; East and West appear to be divisions of <a href="/wiki/Wivelshire" title="Wivelshire">Wivelshire</a>; Powdershire and Pydarshire. The old names of Kerrier and Penwith have been re-used for modern <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">local government districts</a>. The ecclesiastical division within Cornwall into rural deaneries used versions of the same names though the areas did not correspond exactly: Trigg Major, Trigg Minor, East Wivelshire, West Wivelshire, Powder, Pydar, Kerrier and Penwith were all deaneries of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Exeter" title="Diocese of Exeter">Diocese of Exeter</a> but boundaries were altered in 1875 when five more deaneries were created (from December 1876 all in the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Truro" title="Diocese of Truro">Diocese of Truro</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_and_21st_centuries">20th and 21st centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 20th and 21st centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A revival of interest in Cornish studies began in the early 20th century with the work of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jenner" title="Henry Jenner">Henry Jenner</a> and the building of links with the other five Celtic nations.\n</p><p>A political party, <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a>, was formed in 1951 to attempt to serve the interests of Cornwall and to support greater <a href="/wiki/Cornish_self-government_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish self-government movement">self-government</a> for the county. The party has had elected a number of members to county, district, town and parish councils but has had no national success, although the more widespread use of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Piran%27s_Flag" title="Saint Piran&#39;s Flag">Flag of St Piran</a> has been accredited to this party.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>There have been some developments in the recognition of Cornish identity or <a href="/wiki/Ethnicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnicity">ethnicity</a>. In 2001 for the first time in the UK the inhabitants of Cornwall could record their ethnicity as Cornish on the national census, and in 2004 the schools census in Cornwall carried a Cornish option as a subdivision of white British. On 24 April 2014 it was announced that Cornish people will be granted minority status under the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/Framework_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_National_Minorities" title="Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities">Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_minority_status_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_minority_status-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
