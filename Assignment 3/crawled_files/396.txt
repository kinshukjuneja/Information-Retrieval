"Pop" and "<a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>" were roughly synonymous terms until the late 1960s, when they became increasingly differentiated from each other.\n</p><p>Although much of the music that appears on <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">record charts</a> is seen as pop music, the genre is distinguished from chart music. Pop music is eclectic, and often borrows elements from other styles such as <a href="/wiki/Urban_contemporary" title="Urban contemporary">urban</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_music_(genre)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin music (genre)">Latin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>; nonetheless, there are core elements that define pop music. Identifying factors include generally short to medium-length songs written in a basic format (often the <a href="/wiki/Verse%E2%80%93chorus_form" title="Verse\xe2\x80\x93chorus form">verse-chorus structure</a>), as well as common use of repeated choruses, melodic tunes, and <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" Stephen Millward define pop music as "a body of music which is distinguishable from popular, jazz, and folk musics".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\nAccording to <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Pete Seeger</a>, pop music is "professional music which draws upon both folk music and fine arts music".<sup id="cite_ref-pc1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pc1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\nAlthough pop music is seen as just the <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">singles charts</a>, it is not the sum of all chart music. The music charts contain songs from a variety of sources, including <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Novelty_song" title="Novelty song">novelty songs</a>. As a genre, Pop music is seen to exist and develop separately.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, the term "pop music" may be used to describe a distinct genre, designed to appeal to all, often characterized as "instant singles-based music aimed at teenagers" in contrast to rock music as "album-based music for adults".<sup id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pop music continuously evolves along with the term\'s definition. According to <i>The New Grove Dictionary Of Music and Musicians</i>, popular music is defined as "the music since industrialization in the 1800s that is most in line with the tastes and interests of the urban middle class."<sup id="cite_ref-What_Is_Pop_Music_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-What_Is_Pop_Music-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The term "pop song" was first used in 1926, in the sense of a piece of music "having popular appeal".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Hatch and Millward indicate that many events in the history of recording in the 1920s can be seen as the birth of the modern pop music industry, including in <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hillbilly_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Hillbilly music">hillbilly music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rolling_stones_-_11_luglio_2006_-_san_siro.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Oxford Dictionary of however, pop has had the special meaning of non-classical mus[ic], usually in the form of songs, performed by such artists as <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a>, etc."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <i>Grove Music Online</i> also states that "[...] in the early 1960s, [the term] \'pop music\' competed terminologically with <a href="/wiki/Beat_music" title="Beat music">beat music</a> [in England], while in the USA its coverage overlapped (as it still does) with that of \'rock and roll\'".<sup id="cite_ref-Grove_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nFrom about 1967, the term \xe2\x80\x9cpop music\xe2\x80\x9d was increasingly used in opposition to the term <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a>, a division that gave generic significance to both terms.<sup id="cite_ref-Gloag983_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloag983-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> While rock aspired to <a href="/wiki/Authenticity_(philosophy)" title="Authenticity (philosophy)">authenticity</a> and an expansion of the possibilities of popular music,<sup id="cite_ref-Gloag983_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloag983-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> pop was more commercial, ephemeral, and accessible.<sup id="cite_ref-Warner2003_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warner2003-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> According to British musicologist <a href="/wiki/Simon_Frith" title="Simon Frith">Simon Frith</a>, pop music is produced "as a matter of <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">enterprise</a> not art", and is "designed to appeal to everyone" but "doesn\'t come from any particular place or mark off any particular taste". Frith adds that it is "not driven by any significant ambition except profit and commercial reward [...] and, in musical terms, it is essentially conservative". It is, "provided from on high (by record companies, radio programmers, and concert promoters) rather than being made from below&#160;... Pop is not a <a href="/wiki/DIY" class="mw-redirect" title="DIY">do-it-yourself</a> music but is professionally produced and packaged".<sup id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> </p><div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/50px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png" width="50" height="50" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/75px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/100px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="160" data-file-height="160" /></div></div></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist" style="line-height:1.1em"><div class="haudio">\n<div style="padding:4px 0"><a href="/wiki/File:Righteous_Brothers_-_You%27ve_Lost_That_Lovin%27_Feelin%27.ogg" title="File:Righteous Brothers - You&#39;ve Lost That Lovin&#39; audience, rather than to a particular sub-culture or ideology, and an emphasis on craftsmanship rather than formal "artistic" qualities.<sup id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Music scholar Timothy Warner said it typically has an emphasis on recording, production, and technology, rather than live performance; a tendency to reflect existing trends rather than <a href="/wiki/Progressive_music" title="Progressive music">progressive developments</a>; and aims to encourage dancing or uses dance-oriented rhythms.<sup id="cite_ref-Warner2003_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warner2003-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The main medium of pop music is the song, often between two and a half and three and a half minutes in length, generally marked by a consistent and noticeable <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">rhythmic element</a>, a mainstream style and a simple traditional <a href="/wiki/Song_structure_(popular_music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Song structure (popular music)">structure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Common variants include the verse-chorus form and the <a href="/wiki/Thirty-two-bar_form" title="Thirty-two-bar form">thirty-two-bar form</a>, with a focus on <a href="/wiki/Melody" title="Melody">melodies</a> and catchy <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">chorus</a> that contrasts melodically, rhythmically and <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmonically</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Verse_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Verse (music)">verse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The beat and the melodies tend to be simple, with limited harmonic accompaniment.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The lyrics of modern pop songs typically focus on simple themes&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 often love and romantic relationships&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 although there are notable exceptions.<sup id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Harmony_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Harmony (music)">Harmony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chord_progression" title="Chord progression">chord progressions</a> in pop music are often "that of <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a> European <a href="/wiki/Tonality" title="Tonality">tonality</a>, only more simple-minded."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Clich\xc3\xa9s include the <a href="/wiki/Close_and_open_harmony" title="Close and open harmony">barbershop quartet-style harmony</a> (i.e. ii \xe2\x80\x93 V \xe2\x80\x93 I) and <a href="/wiki/Blues_scale" title="Blues scale">blues scale</a>-influenced harmony.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> There was a lessening of the influence of traditional views of the <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_fifths" title="Circle of fifths">circle of fifths</a> between the mid-1950s and the late 1970s, including less predominance for the <a href="/wiki/Dominant_(music)" title="Dominant (music)">dominant</a> <a href="/wiki/Diatonic_function" class="mw-redirect" title="Diatonic function">function</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_influence">Development and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stylistic_evolution">Stylistic evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stylistic evolution">edit</a><span <a href="/wiki/Indie_pop" title="Indie pop">Indie pop</a></div>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="44" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>is missing information about the 1980s\xe2\x80\x932000s</b>.<span Early pop music drew on the <a href="/wiki/Sentimental_ballad" title="Sentimental ballad">sentimental ballad</a> for its form, gained its use of vocal harmonies from <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul music</a>, instrumentation from <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a>, orchestration from <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, tempo from <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance music</a>, backing from <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a>, rhythmic elements from <a href="/wiki/Hip-hop_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip-hop music">hip-hop music</a>, and spoken passages from <a href="/wiki/Rap_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Rap music">rap</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Firth2001_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (February 2017)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In the 1960s, the majority of mainstream pop music fell in two categories: guitar, drum and bass groups or singers backed by a traditional orchestra.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Since early in the decade, it was common for pop producers, songwriters, and engineers to freely experiment with musical form, <a href="/wiki/Orchestration" title="Orchestration">orchestration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reverberation" title="Reverberation">unnatural reverb</a>, and other sound effects. Some of the best known examples are <a href="/wiki/Phil_Spector" title="Phil Spector">Phil Spector</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Wall_of_Sound" title="Wall of Sound">Wall of Sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Meek" title="Joe Meek">Joe Meek</a>\'s use of homemade electronic sound effects for acts like <a href="/wiki/The_Tornados" title="The Tornados">the Tornados</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlake200945_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlake200945-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, pop music on radio and in both American and British film moved away from refined <a href="/wiki/Tin_Pan_Alley" title="Tin Pan Alley">Tin Pan Alley</a> to more eccentric songwriting and incorporated reverb-drenched rock guitar, symphonic strings, and horns played by groups of properly arranged and rehearsed studio musicians.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the mid-1960s, pop music made repeated forays into new sounds, styles, and techniques that inspired public discourse among its listeners. The word "progressive" was frequently used, and it was thought that every song and single was to be a "progression" from the last.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewittHellier2015162_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewittHellier2015162-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">Music critic</a> <a href="/wiki/Simon_Reynolds" title="Simon Reynolds">Simon Reynolds</a> writes that beginning with 1967, a divide would exist between "progressive" pop and "mass/chart" pop, a separation which was "also, broadly, one between boys and girls, middle-class and working-class."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2006398_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2006398-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Before the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_pop" title="Progressive pop">progressive pop</a> of the late 1960s, performers were typically unable to decide on the artistic content of their music.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWillis2014217_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWillis2014217-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Assisted by the mid-1960s economic boom, record labels began investing in artists, giving them the freedom to experiment, and offering them limited control over their content and marketing.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> This situation fell in disuse after the late 1970s and would not reemerge until the rise of <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> stars.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2016202-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Indie_pop" title="Indie pop">Indie pop</a>, which developed in the late 1970s, marked another departure from the glamour of contemporary pop music, with guitar bands formed on the then-novel premise that one could record and release their own music without having to procure a <a href="/wiki/Record_contract" class="mw-redirect" title="Record contract">record contract</a> from a major label.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, pop music worldwide was permeated by <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Christgau_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christgau-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_Reports" title="Scientific Reports">Scientific Reports</a></i> study that examined over 464,000 recordings of popular music recorded between 1955 and 2010 found less variety in pitch progressions, growing average loudness levels,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> less diverse instrumentation and recording techniques, and less timbral variety, which declined after reaching a peak in the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-Matson_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matson-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s John Matson reported that this "seems to support the popular anecdotal observation that pop music of yore was "better", or at least more varied, than today\xe2\x80\x99s top-40 stuff."<sup id="cite_ref-Matson_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matson-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 2018, researchers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Irvine" title="University of California, Irvine">University of California, Irvine</a>, concluded that pop music has become \'sadder\' over the last 30 years. The elements of <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happiness</a> and brightness have eventually been replaced with the electronic beats making the pop music more \'sad yet danceable\'.<sup
