lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The list of <b>Lakes and lochs of the United Kingdom</b> is a link page for some large lakes of the United Kingdom (<a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>), including <a href="/wiki/Loch" title="Loch">lochs</a> fully enclosed by land.\n</p><p>Lakes in Scotland are called <i>lochs</i>, and in Northern Ireland <i><a href="/wiki/Lough" class="mw-redirect" title="Lough">loughs</a></i> (pronounced the same way, i.e. (/l\xc9\x92x/)). In Wales a lake is also called a <i>llyn</i>. The words "loch" and "lough", in addition to referring to bodies of freshwater ("lakes"), are also applied to bodies of brackish water or seawater, which in other countries or contexts may be called <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Firth" title="Firth">firth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bay</a> etc.  In particular, the term "sea-loch" is used in Scotland in this way, as the English language equivalent of \'fjord\'. (There are many examples, including Loch Carron, Loch Torridon etc.) \n</p><p>Some of the largest lakes in England and Wales are man-made <a href="/wiki/Reservoir_(water)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reservoir (water)">reservoirs</a>, or lakes whose size has been increased by damming.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox"
