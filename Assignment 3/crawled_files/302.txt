Microsoft Corporation</b></i>, 35 F.3d 1435 (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">9th Cir.</a> 1994),<sup id="cite_ref-courtopinion_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtopinion-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> lawsuit in which Apple Computer, Inc. (now <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>) sought to prevent <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">Hewlett-Packard</a> from using visual <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">graphical user interface</a> (GUI) elements that were similar to those in Apple\'s <a href="/wiki/Apple_Lisa" title="Apple Lisa">Lisa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Macintosh">Macintosh</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The court ruled that, "Apple cannot get patent-like protection for the idea of a graphical user interface, or the idea of a desktop metaphor [under copyright law]...".<sup id="cite_ref-courtopinion_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtopinion-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the midst of the <i>Apple v. Microsoft</i> lawsuit, <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a> also sued Apple alleging that Mac\'s GUI was heavily based on Xerox\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-xeroxsuesapple_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xeroxsuesapple-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The district court dismissed Xerox\'s claims without addressing whether Apple\'s GUI infringed Xerox\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Apple lost all claims in the <i>Microsoft</i> suit except for the ruling that the trash can icon and folder icons from Hewlett-Packard\'s <a href="/wiki/NewWave_(HP_software)" class="mw-redirect" title="NewWave (HP software)">NewWave</a> windows application were infringing. The lawsuit was filed in 1988 and lasted four years; the decision was affirmed on appeal in 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-courtopinion_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtopinion-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and Apple\'s appeal to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> was denied.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Computer,_Inc._v._Microsoft_Corp.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Apple had agreed to license certain parts of its GUI to Microsoft for use in <a href="/wiki/Windows_1.0" title="Windows 1.0">Windows 1.0</a>, but when Microsoft made changes in <a href="/wiki/Windows_2.0" title="Windows 2.0">Windows 2.0</a> adding overlapping windows and other features found in the Macintosh GUI, Apple filed suit. Apple added additional claims to the suit when Microsoft released <a href="/wiki/Windows_3.0" title="Windows 3.0">Windows 3.0</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Apple claimed the "<a href="/wiki/Look_and_feel" title="Look and feel">look and feel</a>" of the Macintosh operating system, taken as a whole, was protected by <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a>, and that each individual element of the interface (such as the existence of windows on the screen, the rectangular appearance of windows, windows could be resized, overlap, and have title bars) was not as important as all these elements taken together. After oral arguments, the court insisted on an analysis of specific GUI elements that Apple claimed were infringements. Apple listed 189 GUI elements; the court decided that 179 of these elements had been licensed to Microsoft in the Windows 1.0 agreement and most of the remaining 10 elements were not copyrightable\xe2\x80\x94either they were unoriginal to Apple, or they were <a href="/wiki/Idea-expression_divide" class="mw-redirect" title="Idea-expression divide">the only possible way of expressing a particular idea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Midway through the suit, <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a> filed a lawsuit against Apple claiming Apple had infringed copyrights Xerox held on <i>its</i> GUIs. Xerox had invited the <a href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Macintosh">Macintosh</a> design team to view their GUI computers at the <a href="/wiki/PARC_(company)" title="PARC (company)">PARC</a> research lab; these visits had been very influential on the development of the Macintosh GUI. Xerox\'s lawsuit appeared to be a defensive move to ensure that if <i>Apple v. Microsoft</i> established that "look and feel" was copyrightable, then Xerox would be the primary beneficiary, rather than Apple. The Xerox case <a href="/wiki/PARC_(company)#Adoption_by_Apple" title="PARC (company)">was dismissed</a>, for a variety of legal reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_case">Court case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Computer,_Inc._v._Microsoft_Corp.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Court case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The district court ruled that it would require a standard of "virtual identity" between Windows and the Macintosh at trial in order for Apple to prove copyright infringement. Apple believed this to be too narrow of a standard and that a more broad "look and feel" was all that should be necessary at trial. As a result, both parties agreed that a jury trial was unnecessary given the rulings, and Apple filed an appeal to the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals in order to have the district court\'s characterization overruled.<sup id="cite_ref-seattlearticle_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seattlearticle-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the district court ruled in favor of Microsoft, Apple appealed the decision arguing that the district court only considered infringements on the individual elements of Apple\'s GUI, rather than the interface as a whole. The appeals court almost entirely affirmed the ruling of the district court, establishing that, "almost all the similarities spring either from the license or from basic ideas and their obvious expression... illicit copying could occur only if the works as a whole are virtually identical."<sup id="cite_ref-courtopinion_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtopinion-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, the circuit court did reverse the district court\'s decision not to award attorney\'s fees to Microsoft, clarifying and sending the case back to the district court to resolve the issue.\n</p><p>Citing <i><a href="/wiki/Brown_Bag_Software_v._Symantec_Corp." title="Brown Bag Software v. Symantec Corp.">Brown Bag Software v. Symantec Corp.</a></i>, the circuit court dissected the GUI in order to separate expression from ideas (as expression, but not ideas, are covered by copyright law).<sup id="cite_ref-courtopinion_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtopinion-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-baker_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The court outlined five ideas that it identified as basic to a GUI desktop: windows, icon images of office items, manipulations of icons, menus, and the opening and closing of objects.<sup id="cite_ref-courtopinion_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtopinion-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The court established that Apple could not make copyright claims based on these ideas and could only make claims on the precise expression of them.\n</p><p>The court also pointed out that many of Apple\'s claims fail on an originality basis. Apple admittedly licensed many of its representations from Xerox, and copyright protection only extends to original expression. Apple returned to its "complete look and feel" argument, stating that while the individual components were not original, the complete GUI was. The court rejected these arguments because the parts were not original.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Computer,_Inc._v._Microsoft_Corp.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Much of the court\'s ruling was based on the original licensing agreement between Apple and Microsoft for Windows 1.0, and this fact made the case more of a contractual matter than of copyright law, to the chagrin of Apple. This also meant that the court avoided a more far-reaching "look and feel copyright" precedent ruling. However, the case did establish that the analytic dissection (rather than the general "look and feel") of a user interface is vital to any copyright decision on such matters.\n</p><p>In 1998, three years after the lawsuit was decided, all lingering infringement questions against Microsoft regarding the Lisa and Macintosh GUI as well as Apple\'s <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Canyon_Company" title="San Francisco Canyon Company">"QuickTime piracy" lawsuit</a> against Microsoft were settled in direct negotiations.\nApple agreed to make Internet Explorer their default browser, to the detriment of <a href="/wiki/Netscape" title="Netscape">Netscape</a>.\nMicrosoft agreed to continue developing <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office" title="Microsoft Office">Microsoft Office</a> and other software for the Mac over the next five years. Microsoft also purchased $150 million of nonvoting Apple stock.
