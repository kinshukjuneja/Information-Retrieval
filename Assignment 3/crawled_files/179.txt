title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>tumulus</i>, meaning \'mound\', and sometimes translated as <i><a href="/wiki/Ayre_(landform)" title="Ayre (landform)">ayre</a></i>, is a <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(sediment)" class="mw-redirect" title="Deposition (sediment)">deposition</a> <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">landform</a> in which an island is attached to the mainland by a narrow piece of land such as a <a href="/wiki/Spit_(landform)" title="Spit (landform)">spit</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bar_(landform)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bar (landform)">bar</a>. Once attached, the island is then known as a <a href="/wiki/Tied_island" title="Tied island">tied island</a>. reduced and therefore deposition of sand occurs.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_diffraction_and_refraction">Wave diffraction and refraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tombolo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wave diffraction and refraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>True tombolos are formed by <a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean surface waves">wave</a> <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a>. As waves near an island, they are slowed by the shallow water surrounding it. These waves then bend around the island to the opposite side as they approach. The wave pattern created by this water movement causes a convergence of <a href="/wiki/Longshore_drift" title="Longshore drift">longshore drift</a> on the opposite side of the island. The beach sediments that are moving by lateral transport on the lee side of the island will accumulate there, conforming to the shape of the wave pattern. In other words, the waves sweep sediment together from both sides. Eventually, when enough sediment has built up, the beach shoreline, known as a spit, will connect with an island and form a tombolo.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unidirectional_longshore_drift">Unidirectional longshore drift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tombolo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Unidirectional longshore drift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Longshore_drift" title="Longshore drift">longshore drift</a> from one single or a dominant direction, like at <a href="/wiki/Chesil_Beach" title="Chesil Beach">Chesil Beach</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spurn_Head" class="mw-redirect" title="Spurn Head">Spurn Head</a>, the flow of material is along the coast in a movement which is not determined by the now <a href="/wiki/Tied_island" title="Tied island">tied island</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Portland" title="Isle of Portland">Portland</a>, which it has reached. In this and similar cases, while the strip of beach material connected to the Island may be technically called a tombolo because it links the island to the land, it is better thought of in terms of its formation \xe2\x80\x93 as a <a href="/wiki/Spit_(landform)" title="Spit (landform)">spit</a> or otherwise.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology_and_sediment_distribution">Morphology and sediment distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tombolo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Morphology and sediment distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Tombolos are more prone to natural fluctuations of profile and area as a result of tidal and weather events than a normal beach is. Because of this susceptibility to weathering, tombolos are sometimes made more sturdy through the construction of roads or parking lots. The sediments that make up a tombolo are coarser towards the bottom and finer towards the surface. It is easy to see this pattern when the waves are destructive and wash away finer grained material at the top, revealing coarser sands and cobbles as the base. \n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sea_level#Changes_through_geologic_time" title="Sea level">Sea level rise</a> may also contribute to accretion, as material is pushed up with rising sea levels. This is the case with <a href="/wiki/Chesil_Beach" title="Chesil Beach">Chesil Beach</a> (which connects the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Portland" title="Isle of Portland">Isle of Portland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>), notable because the shingle ridge is parallel rather than at right angles to the coast.\n</p><p>Tombolos demonstrate the sensitivity of shorelines. A small piece of land, such as an island, can change the way that waves move, leading to different deposition of sediments.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_tombolos">List of
