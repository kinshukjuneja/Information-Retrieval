href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">royal house</a> that ruled Hanover, Great Britain, and Ireland at various times during the 17th through 20th centuries.  The house originated in 1635 as a <a href="/wiki/Cadet_branch" title="Cadet branch">cadet branch</a> of the House of <a href="/wiki/Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg</a>, growing in prestige until Hanover <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Hanover" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of Hanover">became an Electorate</a> in 1692.  <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">George I</a> became the first Hanoverian monarch of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a> in 1714. At <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Victoria</a>\'s death in 1901, the throne of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> passed to her eldest son <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">Edward VII</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="House of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">House of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a>.  The last reigning members of the House lost the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick" title="Duchy of Brunswick">Duchy of Brunswick</a> in 1918 when Germany became a republic.\n</p><p>The formal name of the house was the <b>House of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg, Hanover line</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_hanover_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica_hanover-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The senior line of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg, which ruled <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Brunswick-Wolfenb%C3%BCttel" title="Principality of Brunswick-Wolfenb\xc3\xbcttel">Brunswick-Wolfenb\xc3\xbcttel</a>, became extinct in 1884. The House of Hanover is now the only surviving branch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Welf" title="House of Welf">House of Welf</a>, which is the senior branch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Este" title="House of Este">House of Este</a>.  The current head of the House of Hanover is <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ernst_August_of_Hanover_(born_1954)" title="Prince Ernst August of Hanover (born 1954)">Ernst August, Prince of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">George, Duke of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg</a>, is considered the first member of the House of Hanover.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Duchy of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">Duchy of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg</a> was divided in 1635, George inherited the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Calenberg" title="Principality of Calenberg">Principality of Calenberg</a> and moved his residence to Hanover. His son, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Louis,_Duke_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Christian Louis, Duke of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">Christian Louis</a> inherited the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_L%C3%BCneburg" title="Principality of L\xc3\xbcneburg">Principality of L\xc3\xbcneburg</a> from George\'s brother.  Calenberg and L\xc3\xbcneburg were then shared between George\'s sons until united in 1705 under his grandson, also called George, who subsequently became <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">George I of Great Britain</a>. All held the title <i>Duke of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg</i>. George died in 1641 and was succeeded by:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Louis,_Duke_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Christian Louis, Duke of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">Christian Louis</a>, 1st son of Duke George, Prince of Calenberg (1641\xe2\x80\x931648) and Prince of L\xc3\xbcneburg (1648\xe2\x80\x931665). He relinquished Calenburg when he became Prince of L\xc3\xbcneburg.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/George_William,_Duke_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="George William, Duke of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">George William</a>, 2nd son of Duke George, Prince of Calenberg (1648\xe2\x80\x931665) and Prince of L\xc3\xbcneburg (1665\xe2\x80\x931705). He relinquished Calenburg when he became Prince of L\xc3\xbcneburg on the death of his brother, Christian Louis.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/John_Frederick,_Duke_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="John Frederick, Duke of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">John Frederick</a>, 3rd son of Duke George, Prince of Calenberg (1665\xe2\x80\x931679).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ernest_Augustus,_Elector_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Ernest Augustus, Elector of Brunswick-L\xc3\xbcneburg">Ernest Augustus</a>, 4th son of Duke George, Prince of Calenberg (1679\xe2\x80\x931698). He became Prince of Calenberg on the death of his brother John Frederick. He was elevated to <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">prince-elector</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> in 1692. Ernest Augustus\'s wife, <a href="/wiki/Sophia_of_Hanover" title="Sophia of Hanover">Sophia of the Palatinate</a>, was declared heiress of the throne of England by the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1701" title="Act of Settlement 1701">Act of Settlement of 1701</a>, which decreed <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholics">Roman Catholics</a> could not accede to the throne. Sophia was at that time the senior eligible Protestant descendant of <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I of England</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">George Louis</a>, son of Duke Ernest Augustus and Sophia, became Elector and Prince of Calenberg in 1698 and Prince of L\xc3\xbcneburg when his uncle George William died in 1705. He inherited his mother\'s claim to the throne of Great Britain when she died in 1714.</li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Monarchs_of_Great_Britain.2C_Ireland.2C_and_Hanover"></span><span class="mw-headline" Osnabr\xc3\xbcck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Hanover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Hanover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dukes of Brunswick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1884, the senior branch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Welf" title="House of Welf">House of Welf</a> became extinct. By semi-<a href="/wiki/Salic_law" title="Salic law">Salic law</a>, the House of Hanover would have acceded to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick" title="Duchy of Brunswick">Duchy of Brunswick</a>, but there had been strong Prussian pressure against having George V of Hanover or his son, the <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Augustus,_3rd_Duke_of_Cumberland" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernest Augustus, 3rd Duke of Cumberland">Duke of Cumberland</a>, succeed to a member state of the German Empire, at least without strong conditions, including swearing to the German constitution. By a law of 1879, the Duchy of Brunswick established a temporary council of regency to take over at the Duke\'s death, and if necessary appoint a regent.\n</p><p>The Duke of Cumberland proclaimed himself Duke of Brunswick at the Duke\'s death, and lengthy negotiations ensued, but were never resolved. <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albrecht_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Albrecht of Prussia">Prince Albert of Prussia</a> was appointed regent; after his death in 1906, <a href="/wiki/Duke_John_Albert_of_Mecklenburg" title="Duke John Albert of Mecklenburg">Duke John Albert of Mecklenburg</a> succeeded him. The Duke of Cumberland\'s eldest son died in a car accident in 1912; the father renounced Brunswick in favor of his younger son <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Augustus,_Duke_of_Brunswick" title="Ernest Augustus, Duke of Brunswick">Ernest Augustus</a>, who married the Kaiser\'s daughter <a href="/wiki/Princess_Viktoria_Luise_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Princess Viktoria Luise of Prussia">Victoria Louise</a> the same year, swore allegiance to the German Empire, and was allowed to ascend the throne of the Duchy in November 1913. He was a major-general during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>; but he was overthrown as Duke of Brunswick in 1918. His father was also deprived of his British titles in 1919, for "bearing arms against Great Britain".\n</p><p>After having left <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_Palace" title="Brunswick Palace">Brunswick Palace</a>, the duke and his family moved back to their exile seat <i>Cumberland Castle</i> at <a href="/wiki/Gmunden" title="Gmunden">Gmunden</a>, Austria, but in 1924 he received <a href="/wiki/Blankenburg_Castle_(Harz)" title="Blankenburg Castle (Harz)">Blankenburg Castle</a> and some other estates in a settlement with the <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Brunswick" title="Free State of Brunswick">Free State of Brunswick</a>, and moved there in 1930. A few days before Blankenburg was handed over to the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> by British and US forces in late 1945, to become part of <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, the family was able to quickly move to <a href="/wiki/Marienburg_Castle_(Hanover)" title="Marienburg Castle (Hanover)">Marienburg Castle (Hanover)</a> with all their furniture, transported by British army trucks, on the order of King <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Duke Ernest Augustus died at Marienburg Castle in 1953. His <a href="/wiki/Herrenhausen_Palace" title="Herrenhausen Palace">Herrenhausen Palace</a> in Hanover had been completely destroyed during World War II. His eldest son, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ernest_Augustus_of_Hanover_(1914%E2%80%931987)" title="Prince Ernest Augustus of Hanover (1914\xe2\x80\x931987)">Prince Ernest Augustus</a>, sold his remaining property at <a href="/wiki/Herrenhausen_Gardens" title="Herrenhausen Gardens">Herrenhausen Gardens</a> in 1961, but kept the nearby <i>Princely House</i>, a small palace built in 1720 by George I for his daughter Anna Louise. It is now his grandson <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ernst_August_of_Hanover_(born_1983)" title="Prince Ernst August of Hanover (born 1983)">Ernest Augustus</a>\'s private home, along with Marienburg Castle.\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px"><div style="width: 155px">\n\t\t\t<div class="thumb" style="width: 150px;"><div succession to the Hanoverian Throne">Line of succession to the Hanoverian Throne</a>\n</p><p>The family has been resident in Austria since 1866 and thus took on Austrian nationality besides their German and British. Since the later king <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Augustus,_King_of_Hanover" title="Ernest Augustus, King of Hanover">Ernest Augustus</a> had been created <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cumberland_and_Teviotdale" title="Duke of Cumberland and Teviotdale">Duke of Cumberland and Teviotdale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Armagh" class="mw-redirect" title="Earl of Armagh">Earl of Armagh</a> by his father <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III</a> in 1799, these British peerages were inherited by his descendants. In 1914 the title of a <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince of Great Britain and Ireland">Prince of Great Britain and Ireland</a> was additionally granted to the members of the house by King <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">George V</a>. These peerages and titles however were suspended under the <a href="/wiki/Titles_Deprivation_Act_1917" title="Titles Deprivation Act 1917">Titles Deprivation Act 1917</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, the title <i>Royal Prince of Great Britain and Ireland</i> had been entered into the family\'s German passports, together with the German titles, in 1914. After the <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%9319" title="German Revolution of 1918\xe2\x80\x9319">German Revolution of 1918\xe2\x80\x9319</a>, with the abolishment of nobility\'s privileges,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> titles officially became parts of the last name. So, curiously, the British prince\'s title is still part of the family\'s last name in their German passports, while it is no longer mentioned in their British documents.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 29 August 1931, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Augustus,_Duke_of_Brunswick" title="Ernest Augustus, Duke of Brunswick">Ernest Augustus, Duke of Brunswick</a>, as head of the House of Hanover, declared the formal resumption, for himself and his dynastic descendants, of use of his former British princely title as a secondary <a href="/wiki/Pretender" title="Pretender">title of pretense</a>, which style, "Royal Prince of Great Britain and Ireland", his grandson, the current head of the house, also called <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ernst_August_of_Hanover_(born_1954)" title="Prince Ernst August of Hanover (born 1954)">Ernest Augustus</a>, continues to claim.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He has the right to petition under the <a href="/wiki/Titles_Deprivation_Act_1917" title="Titles Deprivation Act 1917">Titles Deprivation Act 1917</a> for the restoration of his ancestors\' suspended British peerages <i>Duke of Cumberland and Teviotdale</i> and <i>Earl of Armagh</i>, but he has not done so. His father, another <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ernest_Augustus_of_Hanover_(1914%E2%80%931987)" title="Prince Ernest Augustus of Hanover (1914\xe2\x80\x931987)">Ernest Augustus</a>, did, however, successfully claim British nationality after World War II by virtue of a hitherto overlooked (and since repealed) provision of the <a href="/wiki/Sophia_Naturalization_Act_1705" title="Sophia Naturalization Act 1705">Sophia Naturalization Act 1705</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> According to the decision taken by a court of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, all family members bear the last name <i><a href="/wiki/House_of_Guelph" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Guelph">Guelph</a></i> in the UK and are styled <a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highnesses</a> in their documents.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>, in the UK six and in the US thirteen towns named Brunswick, furthermore one each in Australia and New Zealand, and worldwide more than fifty towns named Victoria.
