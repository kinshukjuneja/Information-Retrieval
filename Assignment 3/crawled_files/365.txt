Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(July 2015)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A <b>personal union</b> is the combination of two or more <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">states</a> that have the same <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> while their boundaries, laws, and interests remain distinct.<sup id="cite_ref-oppenheim1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oppenheim1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Real_union" title="Real union">real union</a>, by contrast, would involve the constituent states being to some extent interlinked, such as by sharing governmental institutions. In a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federation</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary state</a>, a central (federal) government spanning all member states exists, with the degree of self-governance distinguishing the two. The ruler in a personal union does not need to be a hereditary monarch.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term was coined by German jurist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Stephan_P%C3%BCtter" title="Johann Stephan P\xc3\xbctter">Johann Stephan P\xc3\xbctter</a>, introducing it into <i>Elementa iuris publici germanisi</i> (Elements of German Public Law) of 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Personal unions can arise for several reasons, ranging from coincidence (a woman who is already married to a <a href="/wiki/King" title="King">king</a> becomes <a href="/wiki/Queen_regnant" title="Queen regnant">queen regnant</a>, and their child inherits the crown of both countries; the King of one country inherits the crown of another country) to virtual <a href="/wiki/Annexation" title="Annexation">annexation</a> (where a personal union sometimes was seen as a means of preventing uprisings). They can also be <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codified</a> (i.e., the constitutions of the states clearly express that they shall share the same person as head of state) or non-codified, in which case they can easily be broken (e.g., by the death of the monarch when the two states have different <a href="/wiki/Order_of_succession" title="Order of succession">succession laws</a>).\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a> are independent states that share the same person as monarch.\n</p><p>Because presidents of republics are ordinarily chosen from within the citizens of the state in question, the concept of personal union has almost never crossed over from monarchies into republics, with the rare exception of the President of France being a <a href="/wiki/Co-Princes_of_Andorra" title="Co-Princes of Andorra">co-prince of Andorra</a>. In 1860 <a href="/wiki/Marthinus_Wessel_Pretorius" title="Marthinus Wessel Pretorius">Marthinus Wessel Pretorius</a> was simultaneously elected as the president of <a href="/wiki/South_African_Republic" title="South African Republic">Transvaal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orange_Free_State" title="Orange Free State">Orange Free State</a> and he tried to unify the two countries but his mission failed and led to the <a href="/wiki/Transvaal_Civil_War" title="Transvaal Civil War">Transvaal Civil War</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox"
