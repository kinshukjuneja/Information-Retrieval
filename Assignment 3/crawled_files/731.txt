title="Temperate climate">Temperate</a>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> located off the north-western coast of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a>. With a total area of approximately 248,532 square kilometres (95,960&#160;sq&#160;mi), the UK occupies the major part of the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> and includes the island of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the north-eastern one-sixth of the island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and many smaller surrounding islands.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The mainland areas lie between latitudes 49\xc2\xb0N and 59\xc2\xb0N (the <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Shetland Islands">Shetland Islands</a> reach to nearly 61\xc2\xb0N), and longitudes 8\xc2\xb0W to 2\xc2\xb0E. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Greenwich_Observatory" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Greenwich Observatory">Royal Greenwich Observatory</a>, in <a href="/wiki/South_East_(London_sub_region)" class="mw-redirect" title="South East (London sub region)">South East London</a>, is the defining point of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Meridian">Prime Meridian</a>.\n</p><p>The UK lies between the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, and comes within 35&#160;km (22&#160;mi) of the north-west coast of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, from which it is separated by the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>. It shares a 499&#160;km international land boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Channel_Tunnel" title="Channel Tunnel">Channel Tunnel</a> bored beneath the English Channel, now links the UK with France.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a> are covered in their own respective articles, <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom#Geography_of_dependent_territories" title="Geography of the United Kingdom">see below</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The total area of the United Kingdom according to the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a> is 248,532 square kilometres (95,960&#160;sq&#160;mi), comprising the island of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the northeastern one-sixth of the island of Ireland (Northern Ireland) and many smaller islands. <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_England" title="Geography of England">England</a> is the largest <a href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">country of the United Kingdom</a>, at 132,938 square kilometres (51,330&#160;sq&#160;mi) accounting for just over half the total area of the UK. <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Scotland" title="Geography of Scotland">Scotland</a> at 80,239 square kilometres (30,980&#160;sq&#160;mi), is second largest, accounting for about a third of the area of the UK. <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Wales" title="Geography of Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Ireland" title="Geography of Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> are much smaller, covering 21,225 and 14,130 square kilometres (8,200 and 5,460&#160;sq&#160;mi) respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-area_4-0" class="reference"><a The UK is the world\'s 80th largest country by land area and the 10th largest in Europe (if European Russia is included).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_geography">Physical geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Uk_topo_en.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Uk_topo_en.jpg/220px-Uk_topo_en.jpg" width="220" height="340" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Uk_topo_en.jpg/330px-Uk_topo_en.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Uk_topo_en.jpg/440px-Uk_topo_en.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="3089" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Uk_topo_en.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>UK\'s topography</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">physical geography</a> of the UK varies greatly. <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_England" title="Geography of England">England</a> consists of mostly lowland terrain, with upland or mountainous terrain only found north-west of the <a href="/wiki/Tees-Exe_line" title="Tees-Exe line">Tees-Exe line</a>. The upland areas include the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_York_Moors" title="North York Moors">North York Moors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exmoor" title="Exmoor">Exmoor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a>. The lowland areas are typically traversed by ranges of low hills, frequently composed of <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, and flat plains. <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Scotland" title="Geography of Scotland">Scotland</a> is the most mountainous country in the UK and its physical geography is distinguished by the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Boundary Fault</a> which traverses the Scottish mainland from <a href="/wiki/Helensburgh" title="Helensburgh">Helensburgh</a> to <a href="/wiki/Stonehaven" title="Stonehaven">Stonehaven</a>. The faultline separates the two distinctively different regions of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> to the north and west, and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowlands</a> to the south and east. The Highlands are predominantly mountainous, containing the majority of Scotland\'s mountainous landscape, while the Lowlands contain flatter land, especially across the <a href="/wiki/Central_Lowlands" title="Central Lowlands">Central Lowlands</a>, with upland and mountainous terrain located at the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Uplands" title="Southern Uplands">Southern Uplands</a>. <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Wales" title="Geography of Wales">Wales</a> is mostly mountainous, though <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">south Wales</a> is less mountainous than <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">north</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mid_Wales" title="Mid Wales">mid Wales</a>. <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Ireland" title="Geography of Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> consists of mostly hilly landscape and its geography includes the <a href="/wiki/Mourne_Mountains" title="Mourne Mountains">Mourne Mountains</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a>, at 388 square kilometres (150&#160;sq&#160;mi), the largest body of water in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The overall <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> of the UK was shaped by a combination of forces including <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a> in northern and western areas.\n</p><p>The tallest mountain in the UK (and British Isles) is <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nevis" title="Ben Nevis">Ben Nevis</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Mountains" title="Grampian Mountains">Grampian Mountains</a>, Scotland. The longest river is the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a> which flows from <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> into England. The largest lake by surface area is <a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a> in Northern Ireland, though Scotland\'s <a href="/wiki/Loch_Ness" title="Loch Ness">Loch Ness</a> has the largest volume.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Great_Britain" title="Geology of Great Britain">Geology of Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Ireland" title="Geology of Ireland">Geology of Ireland</a></div>\n<p>The geology of the UK is complex and diverse, a result of it being subject to a variety of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Plate tectonic">plate tectonic</a> processes over a very extended period of time. Changing latitude and sea levels have been important factors in the nature of sedimentary sequences, whilst successive continental collisions have affected its <a href="/wiki/Geological_structure_of_Great_Britain" title="Geological structure of Great Britain">geological structure</a> with major faulting and folding being a legacy of each <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogeny</a> (mountain-building period), often associated with <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanic</a> activity and the metamorphism of existing rock sequences. As a result of this eventful geological history, the UK shows a rich variety of <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscapes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Toghill_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toghill-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Precambrian">Precambrian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Precambrian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The oldest rocks in the British Isles are the <a href="/wiki/Lewisian_gneiss" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewisian gneiss">Lewisian gneisses</a>, metamorphic rocks found in the far north west of Scotland and in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> (with a few small outcrops elsewhere), which date from at least 2,700 Ma (Ma = million years ago). South and east of the gneisses are a complex mixture of rocks forming the North West <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grampian" title="Grampian">Grampian</a> Highlands in Scotland. These are essentially the remains of folded <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> that were deposited between 1,000 Ma and 670 Ma over the gneiss on what was then the floor of the <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Ocean" title="Iapetus Ocean">Iapetus Ocean</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeozoic">Palaeozoic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Palaeozoic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>At 520 Ma, what is now Great Britain was split between two <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>; the north of Scotland was located on the continent of <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> at about 20\xc2\xb0 south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, while the rest of the country was on the continent of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a>. In Gondwana, England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> were largely submerged under a shallow sea studded with <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> islands. The remains of these islands underlie much of central England with small outcrops visible in many places.\n</p><p>About 500 Ma southern Britain, the east coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and south-east <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> broke away from Gondwana to form the continent of <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a>, which by 440 Ma had drifted to about 30\xc2\xb0 south. During this period north Wales was subject to <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanic</a> activity. The remains of these volcanoes are still visible, one example of which is <a href="/wiki/Rhobell_Fawr" title="Rhobell Fawr">Rhobell Fawr</a> dating from 510 Ma. Large quantities of volcanic <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> and ash known as the <a href="/wiki/Borrowdale_Volcanics" class="mw-redirect" title="Borrowdale Volcanics">Borrowdale Volcanics</a> covered the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> and this can still be seen in the form of mountains such as <a href="/wiki/Helvellyn" title="Helvellyn">Helvellyn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scafell_Pike" title="Scafell Pike">Scafell Pike</a>.\n</p><p>Between 425 and 400 Ma <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a> had joined with the continent of <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a>, and the combined landmass collided with <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> at about 20\xc2\xb0 south, joining the southern and northern halves of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> together. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Orogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Caledonian Orogeny">Caledonian Orogeny</a> produced an <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alpine</a>-style mountain range in much of north and west Britain.\n</p><p>The collision between continents continued during the <a href="/wiki/Devonian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Devonian period">Devonian period</a>, producing uplift and subsequent erosion, resulting in the deposition of numerous sedimentary rock layers in lowlands and seas. The <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a> and the contemporary volcanics and marine sediments found in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> originated from these processes.\n</p><p>Around 360 Ma Great Britain was lying at the equator, covered by the warm shallow waters of the <a href="/wiki/Rheic_Ocean" title="Rheic Ocean">Rheic Ocean</a>, during which time the Carboniferous Limestone was deposited, as found in the <a href="/wiki/Mendip_Hills" title="Mendip Hills">Mendip Hills</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>. Later, <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a> formed and the sediments deposited were colonised by <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rain_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Rain forest">rain forest</a>. It was in this environment that the <a href="/wiki/Coal_Measures" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal Measures">Coal Measures</a> were formed, the source of the majority of Britain\'s extensive <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> reserves.\n</p><p>Around 280 Ma the <a href="/wiki/Variscan_orogeny" title="Variscan orogeny">Variscan orogeny</a> mountain-building period occurred, again due to collision of continental plates, causing major deformation in south west England. The general region of Variscan folding was south of an east\xe2\x80\x93west line roughly from south <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>. Towards the end of this period <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> was formed beneath the overlying rocks of <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, now exposed at <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Moor" title="Bodmin Moor">Bodmin Moor</a>.\n</p><p>By the end of the Carboniferous period the various continents of the Earth had fused to form the super-continent of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>. <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> was located in the interior of Pangea where it was subject to a hot arid desert climate with frequent flash floods leaving deposits that formed beds of red <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> rock.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mesozoic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>As Pangaea drifted during the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a>, Great Britain moved away from the equator until it was between 20\xc2\xb0 and 30\xc2\xb0 north. The remnants of the Variscan uplands in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> to the south were eroded down, resulting in layers of the New Red Sandstone being deposited across central England.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> began to break up at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> period. Sea levels rose and Britain drifted on the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a> to between 31\xc2\xb0 and 40\xc2\xb0 north. Much Britain was under water again, and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> were deposited and can now be found underlying much of England from the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Hills" title="Cleveland Hills">Cleveland Hills</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Coast" title="Jurassic Coast">Jurassic Coast</a> in <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Dorset" title="Geology of Dorset">Dorset</a>. These include <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greensand_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Greensand (geology)">greensands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oolite" title="Oolite">oolitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_Hills" class="mw-redirect" title="Cotswold Hills">Cotswold Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corallian_limestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Corallian limestone">corallian limestone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_White_Horse" title="Vale of White Horse">Vale of White Horse</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Limestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Portland Limestone">Isle of Portland</a>. The burial of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> below the mud of the sea floor during this time resulted in the formation of <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_oil" title="North Sea oil">North Sea oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Geological_map_-_William_Smith,_1815_-_BL.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Geological_map_-_William_Smith%2C_1815_-_BL.jpg/220px-Geological_map_-_William_Smith%2C_1815_-_BL.jpg" width="220" height="310" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Geological_map_-_William_Smith%2C_1815_-_BL.jpg/330px-Geological_map_-_William_Smith%2C_1815_-_BL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Geological_map_-_William_Smith%2C_1815_-_BL.jpg/440px-Geological_map_-_William_Smith%2C_1815_-_BL.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1366" data-file-height="1924" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Geological_map_-_William_Smith,_1815_-_BL.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1815 Geological by <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_(geologist)" title="William Smith (geologist)">William Smith</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The modern continents having formed, the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> saw the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, gradually separating northern Scotland from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The land underwent a series of uplifts to form a fertile plain. After 20 million years or so, the seas started to flood the land again until much of Britain was again below the sea, though sea levels frequently changed. <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">Chalk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flints</a> were deposited over much of Great Britain, now notably exposed at the <a href="/wiki/White_Cliffs_of_Dover" title="White Cliffs of Dover">White Cliffs of Dover</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Sisters,_Sussex" title="Seven Sisters, Sussex">Seven Sisters</a>, and also forming <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cenozoic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Between 63 and 52 Ma, the last volcanic rocks in Great Britain were formed. The major eruptions at this time produced the <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">Antrim Plateau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> columns of the <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway" title="Giant&#39;s Causeway">Giant\'s Causeway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lundy_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Lundy Island">Lundy Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel" title="Bristol Channel">Bristol Channel</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Orogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpine Orogeny">Alpine Orogeny</a> that took place in Europe about 50 Ma, was responsible for the folding of strata in southern England, producing the <a href="/wiki/London_Basin" title="London Basin">London Basin</a> <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">syncline</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weald-Artois_Anticline" class="mw-redirect" title="Weald-Artois Anticline">Weald-Artois Anticline</a> to the south, the <a href="/wiki/North_Downs" title="North Downs">North Downs</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Downs" title="South Downs">South Downs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiltern_Hills" title="Chiltern Hills">Chiltern Hills</a>.\n</p><p>During the period the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> formed, Britain was <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">uplifted</a>. Some of this uplift was along old lines of weakness left from the Caledonian and Variscan Orogenies long before. The uplifted areas were then eroded, and further sediments, such as the <a href="/wiki/London_Clay" title="London Clay">London Clay</a>, were deposited over southern England.\n</p><p>The major changes during the last 2 million years were brought about by <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">several recent ice ages</a>. The most severe was the <a href="/wiki/Anglian_Stage" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglian Stage">Anglian Glaciation</a>, with ice up to 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft) thick that reached as far south as <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>. This took place between about 478,000 to 424,000 years ago, and was responsible for the diversion of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> onto its present course. During the most recent <a href="/wiki/Devensian_glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Devensian glaciation">Devensian glaciation</a>, which ended a mere 10,000 years ago, the icesheet reached south to <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>. Among the features left behind by the ice are the <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a> of the west coast of Scotland, the <a href="/wiki/Glaciated_valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciated valley">U-shaped valleys</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacial_erratic" title="Glacial erratic">erratics</a> (blocks of rock) that have been transported from the <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and deposited on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>.\n</p><p>Amongst the most significant geological features created during the last twelve thousand years are the <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> deposits of Scotland, and of coastal and upland areas of England and Wales.\n</p><p>At the present time Scotland is continuing to rise as a result of the weight of Devensian ice being lifted. Southern and eastern England is sinking, generally estimated at 1&#160;mm (1/25&#160;inch) per year, with the London area sinking at double the speed partly due to the continuing <a href="/wiki/Compaction_(geology)" title="Compaction (geology)">compaction</a> of the recent clay deposits.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountains_and_hills">Mountains and hills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mountains and hills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BenNevis2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/BenNevis2005.jpg/220px-BenNevis2005.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/BenNevis2005.jpg/330px-BenNevis2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/BenNevis2005.jpg/440px-BenNevis2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BenNevis2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At 1,345&#160;metres, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nevis" title="Ben Nevis">Ben Nevis</a> is the highest peak in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountains_and_hills_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of mountains and hills of the United Kingdom">List of mountains and hills of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The ten tallest <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a> in the UK are all found in Scotland. In general the south of the country is warmer than the north, and the west wetter than the east. Due to the warming influence of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>, the UK is significantly warmer than some other locations at similar latitude, such as <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.\n</p><p>The prevailing winds are southwesterly, from the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a>. More than 50% of the days are overcast.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> There are few natural hazards, although there can be strong winds and floods, especially in winter.\n</p><p>Average annual rainfall varies from over 3,000&#160;mm (118.1&#160;in) in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a> down to 553&#160;mm (21.8&#160;in) in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>. The county of <a href="/wiki/Essex,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Essex, England">Essex</a> is one of the driest in the UK, with an average annual rainfall of around 600&#160;mm (23.6&#160;in), although it typically rains on over 100 days per year. In some years rainfall in Essex can be below 450&#160;mm (17.7&#160;in), less than the average annual rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a>.\n</p><p>The highest temperature recorded in the UK was 38.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (101.3&#160;\xc2\xb0F) at <a href="/wiki/Brogdale" title="Brogdale">Brogdale</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Faversham" title="Faversham">Faversham</a>, in the county of <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, on 10 August 2003. The lowest was \xe2\x88\x9227.2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9217.0&#160;\xc2\xb0F) recorded at <a href="/wiki/Braemar" title="Braemar">Braemar</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Mountains_(Scotland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grampian Mountains (Scotland)">Grampian Mountains</a>, Scotland, on 11 February 1895 and 10 January 1982 and <a href="/wiki/Altnaharra" title="Altnaharra">Altnaharra</a>, also in Scotland, on 30 December 1995.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_geography">Human geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Politics of the United Kingdom">Politics of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National_government">National government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: National government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The UK is governed as a whole by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>. Of the four countries that make the UK, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> have devolved administrations and parliaments/assembly:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Northern Ireland \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a></li>\n<li>Scotland \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a></li>\n<li>Wales \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a></li></ul>\n<p>England has no devolved system of government<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>that is, the Parliament of the United Kingdom serves as (and historically was) the English Parliament. It is governed by UK government ministers and legislated for by the UK parliament. Within England, London has a devolved assembly but proposals for elected <a href="/wiki/Regional_Assemblies_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Assemblies in England">Regional Assemblies in England</a> were rejected in the first referendum covering <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>. <i>See <a href="/wiki/Government_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of England">Government of England</a>.</i>\n</p><p>The UK (specifically, Northern Ireland) has an international land boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> of 499&#160;km.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There is also a boundary between the jurisdiction of France and the UK on the Channel Tunnel.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">Local government in England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">Local government in Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Wales" title="Local government in Wales">Local government in Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Local government in Northern Ireland">Local government in Northern Ireland</a></div>\n<p>Each part of the UK is subdivided in further local governmental regions:\n</p>\n<ul><li>England: <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">Unitary Authorities</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">district councils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish councils</a></li>\n<li>Wales: <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Subdivisions of Wales">Principal areas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_council" title="Community council">communities</a></li>\n<li>Scotland: <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Scotland" title="Subdivisions of Scotland">Council areas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_council" title="Community council">communities</a></li>\n<li>Northern Ireland: <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Districts of Northern Ireland">Districts</a></li></ul>\n<p>Historically the UK was divided into counties or <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shires</a>: administrative areas through which all civil responsibilities of the <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> were passed. Each county or shire had a <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> as its administrative centre and was divided into individual <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parishes</a> that were defined along <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastic">ecclesiastic</a> boundaries.\n</p><p>Between 1889 (1890 in Scotland) and 1974, the political boundaries were based on the traditional counties, but due to changes in population centres, the traditional counties became impractical as local government areas in certain highly urbanised areas. The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> created a new system of administrative counties, designed to take account of the widely differing populations across different parts of the country.\n</p><p>In the 1990s further population growth led to more political changes on a local level.  <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">Unitary authorities</a> were formed across the entirety of Scotland and Wales, and in larger <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> in England. Many unpopular administrative counties were also abolished at this time, leading to a mixture of two-tier and single-purpose authorities. Further reorganisations are planned if and when regional assemblies in England are revisited in the future.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_geography">Economic geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Economic geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_geography_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Economic geography of the United Kingdom">Economic geography of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>The economic geography of the UK reflects not only its current position in the global economy, but its long history both as a trading nation and an <a href="/wiki/British_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="British empire">imperial power</a>.\n</p><p>The UK led the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> and its highly urban character is a legacy of this, with all its major cities being current or former centres of various forms of manufacturing. However, this in turn was built on its exploitation of natural resources, especially <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_industry">Primary industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Primary industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The UK\'s primary industry was once dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> industry, heavily concentrated in the north, the <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">Midlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">south Wales</a>. This is all but gone and the major primary industry is <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_oil" title="North Sea oil">North Sea oil</a>. Its activity is concentrated on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Continental_Shelf" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Continental Shelf">UK Continental Shelf</a> to the north-east of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The UK\'s heavy manufacturing drove the industrial revolution. A map of the major UK cities gives a good picture of where this activity occurred, in particular <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>. Today there is no heavy manufacturing industry in which UK-based firms can be considered world leaders. However, areas of the UK still have a notable manufacturing base, including the <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">Midlands</a> which remains a strong manufacturing centre, and the <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West</a> which accounts for 60% of the United Kingdom\'s manufacturing output.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> More recently, high technology firms have concentrated largely along the <a href="/wiki/M4_motorway" title="M4 motorway">M4 motorway</a>, partly because of access to <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a>, but also because of agglomeration economies.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finance_and_services">Finance and services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Finance and services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Once, every large city had a <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange" title="Stock exchange">stock exchange</a>. Now, the UK financial industry is concentrated overwhelmingly in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf" title="Canary Wharf">Canary Wharf</a>, with back office and administrative operations often dispersed around the south of England. London is one of the world\'s great financial centres and is usually referred to as a <a href="/wiki/World_city" class="mw-redirect" title="World city">world city</a>. There is also a significant legal and <a href="/wiki/Ebusiness" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebusiness">ebusiness</a> industry in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_disparity">Regional disparity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Regional disparity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The effect of changing economic fortune has contributed to the creation of the so-called <a href="/wiki/North-South_divide_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="North-South divide in the United Kingdom">North-South divide</a>, in which decaying industrial and ex-industrial areas of Northern England, Scotland and Wales contrast with the wealthy, finance and technology-led southern economy. This has led successive governments to develop <a href="/wiki/Regional_policy" title="Regional policy">regional policy</a> to try to rectify the imbalance. However, this is not to say that the north-south divide is uniform; some of the worst pockets of deprivation can be found in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, whilst parts of <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a> are very wealthy. Nor is the <a href="/wiki/North-South_divide_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="North-South divide in the United Kingdom">North-South divide</a> limited to the economic sphere; cultural and political divisions weigh heavily too.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_resources">Natural resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Natural resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Economy of the United Kingdom">Economy of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>Historically, much of the United Kingdom was <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forested</a>. Since <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> times, man has <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforested</a> much of the United Kingdom.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> is <a href="/wiki/Intensive_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Intensive agriculture">intensive</a>, highly <a href="/wiki/Mechanisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanisation">mechanised</a>, and efficient by <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> standards, producing about 60% of food needs with only 1% of the labour force. It contributes around 2% of <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a>. Around two thirds of production is devoted to <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, one third to <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">arable</a> crops.\n</p><p>In 1993, it was estimated that <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> was:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">Arable land</a>: 25%</li>\n<li><a title="Forest">Forests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">Woodland</a>: 10%</li>\n<li>Other: 19%</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">Irrigated</a>: 1,080&#160;km\xc2\xb2</li></ul>\n<p>The UK has a variety of natural resources including:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Geological: <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silica" class="mw-redirect" title="Silica">silica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_salt" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock salt">rock salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/China_clay" class="mw-redirect" title="China clay">china clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>.</li>\n<li>Agricultural:  <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic sheep">sheep</a></li></ul>\n<p>The UK has large <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> reserves; primary energy production accounts for 10% of <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a>, one of the highest shares of any <a href="/wiki/Industrial_nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial nation">industrial nation</a>. Due to the island location of the UK, the country has great potential for generating electricity from <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a>, although these have not yet been exploited on a commercial basis.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_issues">Current issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Current issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>England is one of the most densely populated countries/regions in the world, and the most densely populated major nation in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The high population density (especially in the southeast of England) coupled with a changing climate, is likely to put extreme pressure on the United Kingdom\'s water resources in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The United Kingdom is reducing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> gas emissions. It has met <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> target of a 12.5% reduction from 1990 levels and intends to meet the legally binding target of a 20% cut in emissions by 2010. By 2015, to recycle or compost at least 33% of household waste. Between 1998-99 and 1999\xe2\x80\x932000, household <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> increased from 8.8% to 10.3% respectively.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline"
