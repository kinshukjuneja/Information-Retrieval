S. Gilbert</h1>\t\t\t<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">\n\t\n\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_S._Gilbert_(1878).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/William_S._Gilbert_%281878%29.jpg/300px-William_S._Gilbert_%281878%29.jpg" width="300" height="385" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/William_S._Gilbert_%281878%29.jpg/450px-William_S._Gilbert_%281878%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/William_S._Gilbert_%281878%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="616" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_S._Gilbert_(1878).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>W. S. Gilbert</center></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Sir William Schwenck Gilbert</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;n 1&#93;</a></sup> (18 November 1836&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 29 May 1911) was an English <a href="/wiki/Dramatist" class="mw-redirect" title="Dramatist">dramatist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">librettist</a>, poet and illustrator best known for his <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">collaboration</a> with composer <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Sullivan" title="Arthur Sullivan">Arthur Sullivan</a>, which produced fourteen <a href="/wiki/Comic_opera" title="Comic opera">comic operas</a>. The most famous of these include <i><a href="/wiki/H.M.S._Pinafore" title="H.M.S. Pinafore">H.M.S. Pinafore</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pirates_of_Penzance" title="The Pirates of Penzance">The Pirates of Penzance</a></i> and one of the most frequently performed works in the history of musical theatre, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The popularity of these works was supported for over a century by year-round performances of them, in Britain and abroad, by the repertory company that Gilbert, Sullivan and their producer <a href="/wiki/Richard_D%27Oyly_Carte" title="Richard D&#39;Oyly Carte">Richard D\'Oyly Carte</a> founded, the <a href="/wiki/D%27Oyly_Carte_Opera_Company" title="D&#39;Oyly Carte Opera Company">D\'Oyly Carte Opera Company</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Savoy_opera" title="Savoy opera">Savoy operas</a> continue to be frequently performed in the English-speaking world and beyond.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gilbert\'s creative output included over 75 plays and <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretti</a>, and numerous short stories, poems and lyrics, both comic and serious. After brief careers as a government clerk and a lawyer, Gilbert began to focus, in the 1860s, on writing light verse, including his <i><a href="/wiki/Bab_Ballads" title="Bab Ballads">Bab Ballads</a></i>, short stories, theatre reviews and illustrations, often for <i><a href="/wiki/Fun_(magazine)" title="Fun (magazine)">Fun</a></i> magazine. He also began to write <a href="/wiki/Victorian_burlesque" title="Victorian burlesque">burlesques</a> and his first comic plays, developing a unique absurdist, inverted style that would later be known as his "topsy-turvy" style. He also developed a <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realistic</a> method of stage direction and a reputation as a strict theatre director. In the 1870s, Gilbert wrote 40 plays and libretti, including his <a href="/wiki/German_Reed_Entertainments" title="German Reed Entertainments">German Reed Entertainments</a>, several blank-verse "fairy comedies", some serious plays, and his first five collaborations with Sullivan: <i><a href="/wiki/Thespis_(opera)" title="Thespis (opera)">Thespis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_Jury" title="Trial by Jury">Trial by Jury</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Sorcerer" title="The Sorcerer">The Sorcerer</a>, H.M.S. Pinafore</i> and <i>The Pirates of Penzance</i>. In the 1880s, Gilbert focused on the Savoy operas, including <i><a href="/wiki/Patience_(opera)" title="Patience (opera)">Patience</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iolanthe" title="Iolanthe">Iolanthe</a></i>, <i>The Mikado</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Yeomen_of_the_Guard" title="The Yeomen of the Guard">The Yeomen of the Guard</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gondoliers" title="The Gondoliers">The Gondoliers</a></i>.\n</p><p>In 1890, after this long and profitable creative partnership, Gilbert quarrelled with Sullivan and their producer, <a href="/wiki/Richard_D%27Oyly_Carte" title="Richard D&#39;Oyly Carte">Richard D\'Oyly Carte</a>, concerning expenses at the Savoy Theatre; the dispute is referred to as the "carpet quarrel". Gilbert won the ensuing lawsuit, but the argument caused hurt feelings among the partnership. Although Gilbert and Sullivan were persuaded to collaborate on two last operas, they were not as successful as the previous ones. In later years, Gilbert wrote several plays, and a few operas with other collaborators. He retired, with his wife Lucy, and their ward, <a href="/wiki/Nancy_McIntosh" title="Nancy McIntosh">Nancy McIntosh</a>, to a country estate, <a href="/wiki/Grim%27s_Dyke" title="Grim&#39;s Dyke">Grim\'s Dyke</a>. He was <a href="/wiki/Knight_bachelor" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight bachelor">knighted</a> in 1907. Gilbert died of a heart attack while attempting to rescue a young woman to whom he was giving a swimming lesson in the lake at his home.\n</p><p>Gilbert\'s plays inspired other dramatists, including <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and his comic operas with Sullivan inspired the later development of American <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical theatre</a>, especially influencing Broadway librettists and lyricists. According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cambridge_History_of_English_and_American_Literature" title="The Cambridge History of English and American Literature">The Cambridge History of English and American Literature</a></i>, Gilbert\'s "lyrical facility and his mastery of metre raised the poetical quality of comic opera to a position that it had never reached before and has not reached since".<sup id="cite_ref-CambridgeHistory_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CambridgeHistory-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="toccolours" style="float: right; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em; font-size: 85%; background:#c6dbf7; color:black; width:30em; max-width: 40%;" cellspacing="5">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;"><i>"No sooner had the learned judge pronounced this sentence than the poor soul stooped down, and taking off a heavy boot, flung it at my head, as a reward for my eloquence on her behalf; accompanying the assault with a torrent of invective against my abilities as a counsel, and my line of defence."</i>\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;">\xe2\x80\x94 <i><b>My Maiden Brief</b></i><sup id="cite_ref-MMB_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MMB-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n<p><i>(Gilbert claimed this incident was autobiographical.)</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Gilbert was born at 17 <a href="/wiki/Southampton_Street,_London" title="Southampton Street, London">Southampton Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strand,_London" title="Strand, London">Strand</a>, London. His father, also named <a href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(author)" title="William Gilbert (author)">William</a>, was briefly a <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_doctor" title="Ship&#39;s doctor">naval surgeon</a>, who later became a writer of novels and short stories, some of which were illustrated by his son. Gilbert\'s mother was the former Anne Mary Bye Morris (1812\xe2\x80\x931888), the daughter of Thomas Morris, an apothecary.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert\'s parents were distant and stern, and he did not have a particularly close relationship with either of them.  They quarrelled increasingly, and following the break-up of their marriage in 1876, his relationships with them, especially his mother, became even more strained.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert had three younger sisters, two of whom were born outside England because of the family\'s travels during these years: Jane Morris (b. 1838 in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, Italy \xe2\x80\x93 1906), who married Alfred Weigall, a miniatures painter; Mary Florence (b. 1843 in <a href="/wiki/Boulogne" class="mw-redirect" title="Boulogne">Boulogne</a>, France \xe2\x80\x93 1911); and Anne Maude (1845\xe2\x80\x931932). The younger two never married.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> . Gilbert was nicknamed "Bab" as a baby, and then "Schwenck", after his father\'s godparents.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a child, Gilbert travelled to Italy in 1838 and then France for two years with his parents, who finally returned to settle in London in 1847. He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Boulogne-sur-Mer" title="Boulogne-sur-Mer">Boulogne</a>, France, from the age of seven (he later kept his diary in French so that the servants could not read it),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> then at Western Grammar School, <a href="/wiki/Brompton,_Kensington" class="mw-redirect" title="Brompton, Kensington">Brompton</a>, London, and then at the <a href="/wiki/Great_Ealing_School" title="Great Ealing School">Great Ealing School</a>, where he became head boy and wrote plays for school performances and painted scenery. He then attended <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King\'s College London</a>, graduating in 1856. He intended to take the examinations for a commission in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Artillery" title="Royal Artillery">Royal Artillery</a>, but with the end of the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, fewer recruits were needed, and the only commission available to Gilbert would have been in a <a href="/wiki/Line_regiment" title="Line regiment">line regiment</a>. Instead he joined the <a href="/wiki/British_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="British Civil Service">Civil Service</a>: he was an assistant clerk in the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_Office_(United_Kingdom)" title="Privy Council Office (United Kingdom)">Privy Council</a> Office for four years and hated it. In 1859 he joined the <a href="/wiki/Volunteer_Force_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Volunteer Force (Great Britain)">Militia</a>, a part-time volunteer force formed for the defence of Britain, with which he served until 1878 (in between writing and other work), reaching the rank of Captain.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;n 2&#93;</a></sup> In 1863 he received a bequest of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\xc2\xa3</a>300 that he used to leave the civil service and take up a brief career as a <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barrister</a> (he had already entered the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Temple" title="Inner Temple">Inner Temple</a> as a student), but his legal practice was not successful, averaging just five clients a year.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg/220px-Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg" width="220" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg/330px-Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg/440px-Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg 2x" data-file-width="641" data-file-height="552" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gentle_Alice_Brown.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of Gilbert\'s illustrations for his <a href="/wiki/Bab_Ballads" title="Bab Ballads">Bab Ballad</a> "Gentle Alice Brown"</div></div></div>\n<p>To supplement his income from 1861 on, Gilbert wrote a variety of stories, comic rants, grotesque illustrations, theatre reviews (many in the form of a parody of the play being reviewed),<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and, under the pseudonym "Bab" (his childhood nickname), illustrated poems for several comic magazines, primarily <i><a href="/wiki/Fun_(magazine)" title="Fun (magazine)">Fun</a></i>, started in 1861 by <a href="/wiki/Henry_James_Byron" title="Henry James Byron">H. J. Byron</a>. He published stories, articles, and reviews in papers such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Cornhill_Magazine" title="Cornhill Magazine">Cornhill Magazine</a>, London Society, Tinsley\'s Magazine</i> and <i>Temple Bar</i>. In addition, Gilbert was the London correspondent for <i>L\'Invalide Russe</i> and a drama critic for the <i>Illustrated London Times</i>. In the 1860s he also contributed to <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hood" title="Tom Hood">Tom Hood</a>\'s Christmas annuals, to <i>Saturday Night</i>, the <i>Comic News</i> and the <i>Savage Club Papers</i>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> newspaper in 1870 sent him to France as a war correspondent reporting on the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gilberts1867.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Gilberts1867.gif/170px-Gilberts1867.gif" width="170" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0a/Gilberts1867.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="211" data-file-height="255" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gilberts1867.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gilbert and his wife, Lucy, in 1867</div></div></div>\n<p>The poems, illustrated humorously by Gilbert, proved immensely popular and were reprinted in book form as the <i><a href="/wiki/Bab_Ballads" title="Bab Ballads">Bab Ballads</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;n 3&#93;</a></sup> He would later return to many of these as source material for his plays and comic operas. Gilbert and his colleagues from <i>Fun</i>, including <a href="/wiki/Thomas_William_Robertson" title="Thomas William Robertson">Tom Robertson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hood" title="Tom Hood">Tom Hood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clement_Scott" title="Clement Scott">Clement Scott</a> and <a href="/wiki/F._C._Burnand" title="F. C. Burnand">F. C. Burnand</a> (who defected to <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i> in 1862) frequented the Arundel Club, the <a href="/wiki/Savage_Club" title="Savage Club">Savage Club</a>, and especially Evans\'s caf\xc3\xa9, where they had a table in competition with the <i>Punch</i> \'Round table\'.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;n 4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After a relationship in the mid-1860s with the novelist <a href="/wiki/Annie_Hall_Cudlip" title="Annie Hall Cudlip">Annie Thomas</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert married Lucy Agnes Turner, whom he called "Kitty", in 1867; she was 11 years his junior. He wrote many affectionate letters to her over the years. Gilbert and Lucy were socially active both in London and later at <a href="/wiki/Grim%27s_Dyke" title="Grim&#39;s Dyke">Grim\'s Dyke</a>, often holding dinner parties and being invited to others\' homes for dinner, in contrast to the picture painted by fictionalisations such as the film <i><a href="/wiki/Topsy-Turvy" title="Topsy-Turvy">Topsy-Turvy</a></i>. The Gilberts had no children, but they had many pets, including some exotic ones.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_plays">First plays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First plays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Gilbert wrote and directed a number of plays at school, but his first professionally produced play was <i>Uncle Baby</i>, which ran for seven weeks in the autumn of 1863.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;n 5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png/250px-Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png" width="250" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png/375px-Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png/500px-Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png 2x" data-file-width="2481" data-file-height="2408" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pantomimes_of_1866_-_Hush-a-Bye_Baby.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Hush-a-Bye Baby, On the Tree Top</i> \xe2\x80\x93 an 1866 pantomime by Gilbert and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Millward" title="Charles Millward">Charles Millward</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1865\xe2\x80\x9366, Gilbert collaborated with Charles Millward on several <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomimes</a>, including one called <i>Hush-a-Bye, Baby, On the Tree Top, or, Harlequin Fortunia, King Frog of Frog Island, and the Magic Toys of Lowther Arcade</i> (1866).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert\'s first solo success came a few days after <i>Hush-a-Bye Baby</i> premiered. His friend and mentor, Tom Robertson, was asked to write a pantomime but did not think he could do it in the two weeks available, and so he recommended Gilbert instead. Written and rushed to the stage in 10 days, <i><a href="/wiki/Dulcamara,_or_the_Little_Duck_and_the_Great_Quack" title="Dulcamara, or the Little Duck and the Great Quack">Dulcamara, or the Little Duck and the Great Quack</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Victorian_burlesque" title="Victorian burlesque">burlesque</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Donizetti" title="Gaetano Donizetti">Gaetano Donizetti</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/L%27elisir_d%27amore" title="L&#39;elisir d&#39;amore">L\'elisir d\'amore</a></i>, proved extremely popular. This led to a long series of further Gilbert opera burlesques, <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomimes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Farce" title="Farce">farces</a>, full of awful <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">puns</a> (traditional in burlesques of the period),<sup id="cite_ref-LaViv_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaViv-28">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> though showing, at times, signs of the satire that would later be a defining part of Gilbert\'s work.<sup id="cite_ref-CambridgeHistory_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CambridgeHistory-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;n 6&#93;</a></sup> For instance:\n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; The piece was Gilbert\'s biggest success to date, running for over 100 nights and being frequently revived and played continuously in the provinces for three years thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Victorian theatre, "[to degrade] high and beautiful themes&#160;... had been the regular proceeding in burlesque, and the age almost expected it"<sup id="cite_ref-CambridgeHistory_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CambridgeHistory-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, Gilbert\'s burlesques were considered unusually tasteful compared to the others on the London stage. <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Goldberg" title="Isaac Goldberg">Isaac Goldberg</a> wrote that these pieces "reveal how a playwright may begin by making burlesque of opera and end by making opera of burlesque."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  Gilbert would depart even further from the burlesque style from about 1869 with plays containing original plots and fewer puns.  His first full-length prose comedy was <i><a href="/wiki/An_Old_Score" title="An Old Score">An Old Score</a></i> (1869).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_Reed_entertainments_and_other_plays_of_the_early_1870s">German Reed entertainments and other plays of the early 1870s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: German Reed entertainments and other plays of the early 1870s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="toccolours" style="float: right; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em; font-size: 85%; background:#c6dbf7; color:black; width:28em; max-width: 40%;" cellspacing="5">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;">C<small>HRYSAL</small>: This hound abused me!\n<p>Z<small>ORAM</small>: He insulted me;<br />\nB<small>OTH</small>: Our honour must be satisfied!<br />\n<i>(They cross swords.)</i><br />\nG<small>\xc3\x89LANOR</small>: No, no\xe2\x80\x94\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Attend to me. Zoram, your hand!<br />\nZ<small>ORAM</small>: With pleasure. <i>(Shaking hands with Chrysal.)</i>\n</p>\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;">\xe2\x80\x94 <b><i>The Palace of Truth</i> (1870)</b>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Theatre, at the time Gilbert began writing, had fallen into disrepute. Badly translated and adapted French <a href="/wiki/Operetta" title="Operetta">operettas</a> and poorly written, prurient <a href="/wiki/Victorian_burlesque" title="Victorian burlesque">Victorian burlesques</a> dominated the London stage. As <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Bond" title="Jessie Bond">Jessie Bond</a> vividly described it, "stilted tragedy and vulgar farce were all the would-be playgoer had to choose from, and the theatre had become a place of evil repute to the righteous British householder."<sup id="cite_ref-JessieBond_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JessieBond-33">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Bond created the mezzo-soprano roles in most of the Gilbert and Sullivan operas, and is here leading into a description of Gilbert\'s role reforming the Victorian theatre. <sup id="cite_ref-JessieBond_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JessieBond-33">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 1869 to 1875, Gilbert joined with one of the leading figures in theatrical reform, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_German_Reed" title="Thomas German Reed">Thomas German Reed</a> (and his wife <a href="/wiki/Priscilla_Horton" title="Priscilla Horton">Priscilla</a>), whose <a href="/wiki/German_Reed_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="German Reed Entertainment">Gallery of Illustration</a> sought to regain some of theatre\'s lost respectability by offering family entertainments in London.<sup id="cite_ref-JessieBond_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JessieBond-33">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  So successful were they that by 1885 Gilbert stated that original British plays were appropriate for an innocent 15-year-old girl in the audience.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;n 7&#93;</a></sup>  Three months before the opening of Gilbert\'s last burlesque (<i>The Pretty Druidess</i>), the first of his pieces for the Gallery of Illustration, <i><a href="/wiki/No_Cards" title="No Cards">No Cards</a></i>, was produced. Gilbert created six <a href="/wiki/German_Reed_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="German Reed Entertainment">musical entertainments</a> for the German Reeds, some with music composed by Thomas German Reed.<sup id="cite_ref-PlayList_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PlayList-35">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The environment of the German Reeds\' intimate theatre allowed Gilbert quickly to develop a personal style and freedom to control all aspects of production, including set, costumes, direction and stage management.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> These works were a success,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> with Gilbert\'s first big hit at the Gallery of Illustration, <i><a href="/wiki/Ages_Ago" title="Ages Ago">Ages Ago</a></i>, opening in 1869. <i>Ages Ago</i> was also the beginning of a collaboration with the composer <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Clay" title="Frederic Clay">Frederic Clay</a> that would last seven years and produce four works.<sup id="cite_ref-CrowtherAgesAgo_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CrowtherAgesAgo-38">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> It was at a rehearsal for <i>Ages Ago</i> that Clay formally introduced Gilbert to his friend, Arthur Sullivan.<sup id="cite_ref-CrowtherAgesAgo_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CrowtherAgesAgo-38">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;n 8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The Bab Ballads and Gilbert\'s many early musical works gave him much practice as a lyricist even before his collaboration with Sullivan.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ages_Ago.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Ages_Ago.png/250px-Ages_Ago.png" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Ages_Ago.png/375px-Ages_Ago.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Ages_Ago.png/500px-Ages_Ago.png 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ages_Ago.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Ages_Ago" title="Ages Ago">Ages Ago</a></i>, 1870</div></div></div>\n<p>Many of the plot elements of the German Reed Entertainments (as well as some from his earlier plays and Bab Ballads) would be reused by Gilbert later in the <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a> operas. These elements include paintings coming to life (<i>Ages Ago</i>, used again in <i><a href="/wiki/Ruddigore" title="Ruddigore">Ruddigore</a></i>), a deaf nursemaid binding a respectable man\'s son to a "pirate" instead of to a "pilot" by mistake (<i><a href="/wiki/Our_Island_Home" title="Our Island Home">Our Island Home</a></i>, 1870, reused in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pirates_of_Penzance" title="The Pirates of Penzance">The Pirates of Penzance</a></i>), and the forceful mature lady who is "an acquired taste" (<i><a href="/wiki/Eyes_and_No_Eyes" title="Eyes and No Eyes">Eyes and No Eyes</a></i>, 1875, reused in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> During this time, Gilbert perfected the \'topsy-turvy\' style that he had been developing in his Bab Ballads, where the humour was derived by setting up a ridiculous premise and working out its logical consequences, however absurd.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mike_Leigh" title="Mike Leigh">Mike Leigh</a> describes the "Gilbertian" style as follows: "With great fluidity and freedom, [Gilbert] continually challenges our natural expectations. First, within the framework of the story, he makes bizarre things happen, and turns the world on its head. Thus the Learned Judge marries the Plaintiff, the soldiers metamorphose into aesthetes, and so on, and nearly every opera is resolved by a deft moving of the goalposts&#160;... His genius is to fuse opposites with an imperceptible sleight of hand, to blend the surreal with the real, and the caricature with the natural. In other words, to tell a perfectly outrageous story in a completely deadpan way."<sup id="cite_ref-MikeLeigh_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MikeLeigh-43">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News,_March_22,_1873.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_March_22%2C_1873.PNG/250px-The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_March_22%2C_1873.PNG" width="250" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_March_22%2C_1873.PNG/375px-The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_March_22%2C_1873.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_March_22%2C_1873.PNG/500px-The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_March_22%2C_1873.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1931" data-file-height="1373" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Happy_Land_-_Illustrated_London_News,_March_22,_1873.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scene from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Happy_Land" title="The Happy Land">The Happy Land</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Illustrated_London_News" title="The Illustrated London News">The Illustrated London News</a></i>, 22 March 1873, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/D._H._Friston" class="mw-redirect" title="D. H. Friston">D. H. Friston</a></div></div></div>\n<p>At the same time, Gilbert created several "fairy comedies" at the <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Haymarket Theatre">Haymarket Theatre</a>. This series of plays was founded upon the idea of self-revelation by characters under the influence of some magic or some supernatural interference.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The first was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Palace_of_Truth" title="The Palace of Truth">The Palace of Truth</a></i> (1870), based partly on a story by <a href="/wiki/Madame_de_Genlis" class="mw-redirect" title="Madame de Genlis">Madame de Genlis</a>.  In 1871, with <i><a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_and_Galatea_(play)" title="Pygmalion and Galatea (play)">Pygmalion and Galatea</a></i>, one of seven plays that he produced that year, Gilbert scored his greatest hit to date.  Together, these plays and their successors such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wicked_World" title="The Wicked World">The Wicked World</a></i> (1873), <i><a href="/wiki/Sweethearts_(play)" title="Sweethearts (play)">Sweethearts</a></i> (1874), and <i><a href="/wiki/Broken_Hearts" title="Broken Hearts">Broken Hearts</a></i> (1875), did for Gilbert on the dramatic stage what the German Reed entertainments had done for him on the musical stage: they established that his capabilities extended far beyond burlesque, won him artistic credentials, and demonstrated that he was a writer of wide range, as comfortable with human drama as with farcical humour. The success of these plays, especially <i>Pygmalion and Galatea</i>, gave Gilbert a prestige that would be crucial to his later collaboration with as respected a musician as Sullivan.<sup id="cite_ref-Wren_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wren-45">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During this period, Gilbert also pushed the boundaries of how far satire could go in the theatre. He collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Arthur_%C3%A0_Beckett" title="Gilbert Arthur \xc3\xa0 Beckett">Gilbert Arthur \xc3\xa0 Beckett</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Happy_Land" title="The Happy Land">The Happy Land</a></i> (1873), a political satire (in part, a parody of his own <i>The Wicked World</i>), which was briefly banned because of its unflattering caricatures of <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">Gladstone</a> and his ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Realm_of_Joy" title="The Realm of Joy">The Realm of Joy</a></i> (1873) was set in the lobby of a theatre performing a scandalous play (implied to be the <i>Happy Land</i>), with many jokes at the expense of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chamberlain" title="Lord Chamberlain">Lord Chamberlain</a> (the "Lord High Disinfectant", as he\'s referred to in the play).<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Charity_(play)" title="Charity (play)">Charity</a></i> (1874), however, Gilbert uses the freedom of the stage in a different way: to provide a tightly written critique of the contrasting ways in which Victorian society treated men and women who had sex outside of marriage, which anticipated the \'problem plays\' of <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">Shaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Ibsen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CrowtherCharity_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CrowtherCharity-48">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_director">As director</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As director">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="toccolours" style="float: right; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em; font-size: 85%; background:#c6dbf7; color:black; width:30em; max-width: 40%;" cellspacing="5">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;">"It is absolutely essential to the success of this piece that it should be played with the most perfect earnestness and gravity throughout. There should be no exaggeration in costume, makeup or demeanour; and the characters, one and all, should appear to believe, throughout, in the perfect sincerity of their words and actions. Directly the actors show that they are conscious of the absurdity of their utterances the piece begins to drag."\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;">\xe2\x80\x93 <b>Preface to <i><a href="/wiki/Engaged_(play)" title="Engaged (play)">Engaged</a></i></b>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Once he became established, Gilbert was the stage director for his plays and operas and had strong opinions on how they should best be performed.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> He was strongly influenced by the innovations in "stagecraft", now called stage direction, by the playwrights <a href="/wiki/James_Planch%C3%A9" title="James Planch\xc3\xa9">James Planch\xc3\xa9</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Thomas_William_Robertson" title="Thomas William Robertson">Tom Robertson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JessieBond_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JessieBond-33">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert attended rehearsals directed by Robertson to learn this art first-hand from the older director, and he began to apply it in some of his earliest plays.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> He sought realism in acting, settings, costumes and movement, if not in content of his plays (although he did write a romantic comedy in the "naturalist" style, as a tribute to Robertson, <i><a href="/wiki/Sweethearts_(play)" title="Sweethearts (play)">Sweethearts</a></i>), shunned self-conscious interaction with the audience, and insisted on a style of portrayal in which the characters were never aware of their own absurdity, but were coherent internal wholes.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png/170px-Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png" width="170" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png/255px-Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png/340px-Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png 2x" data-file-width="2008" data-file-height="2712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ironmaster_of_the_Savoy.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"The Ironmaster at the Savoy" (1884): Gilbert with the mallet of discipline; <a href="/wiki/Richard_D%27Oyly_Carte" title="Richard D&#39;Oyly Carte">Carte</a> reacts</div></div></div>\n<p>In Gilbert\'s 1874 burlesque, <i><a href="/wiki/Rosencrantz_and_Guildenstern_(Gilbert)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosencrantz and Guildenstern (Gilbert)">Rosencrantz and Guildenstern</a></i>, the character Hamlet, in his speech to the players, sums up Gilbert\'s theory of comic acting: "I hold that there is no such antick fellow as your bombastical hero who doth so earnestly spout forth his folly as to make his hearers believe that he is unconscious of all incongruity".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Robertson "introduced Gilbert both to the revolutionary notion of disciplined rehearsals and to mise-en-sc\xc3\xa8ne or unity of style in the whole presentation \xe2\x80\x93 direction, design, music, acting."<sup id="cite_ref-MikeLeigh_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MikeLeigh-43">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Like Robertson, Gilbert demanded discipline in his actors.  He required that his actors know their words perfectly, enunciate them clearly and obey his stage directions, which was something quite new to many actors of the day.<sup id="cite_ref-Cox-Ife1_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cox-Ife1-53">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> A major innovation was the replacement of the star actor with the disciplined ensemble, "raising the director to a new position of dominance" in the theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-utility_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utility-54">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> "That Gilbert was a good director is not in doubt. He was able to extract from his actors natural, clear performances, which served the Gilbertian requirements of outrageousness delivered straight."<sup id="cite_ref-MikeLeigh_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MikeLeigh-43">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gilbert prepared meticulously for each new work, making models of the stage, actors and set pieces, and designing every action and bit of business in advance.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert would not work with actors who challenged his authority.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> George Grossmith wrote that, at least sometime, "Mr. Gilbert is a perfect autocrat, insisting that his words should be delivered, even to an inflection of the voice, as he dictates. He will stand on the stage beside the actor or actress, and repeat the words with appropriate action over and over again, until they are delivered as he desires them to be."<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Even during long runs and revivals, Gilbert closely supervised the performances of his plays, making sure that the actors did not make unauthorised additions, deletions or paraphrases.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert was famous for demonstrating the action himself, even as he grew older.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;n 9&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert himself went on stage in a number of productions throughout his lifetime, including several performances as the Associate in <i><a href="/wiki/Trial_by_Jury" title="Trial by Jury">Trial by Jury</a></i>, as substitute for an ailing actor in his play <i><a href="/wiki/Broken_Hearts" title="Broken Hearts">Broken Hearts</a></i>, and in charity matinees of his one-act plays, such as King Claudius in <i>Rosencrantz and Guildenstern</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaboration_with_Sullivan">Collaboration with Sullivan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Collaboration with Sullivan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Arthur_Seymour_Sullivan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Sir_Arthur_Seymour_Sullivan.jpg/170px-Sir_Arthur_Seymour_Sullivan.jpg" width="170" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Sir_Arthur_Seymour_Sullivan.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="219" data-file-height="279" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Arthur_Seymour_Sullivan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sir Arthur Sullivan</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_collaborations_amidst_other_works">First collaborations amidst other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First collaborations amidst other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1871, John Hollingshead commissioned Gilbert to work with Sullivan on a holiday piece for Christmas, <i><a href="/wiki/Thespis_(opera)" title="Thespis (opera)">Thespis, or The Gods Grown Old</a></i>, at the <a href="/wiki/Gaiety_Theatre,_London" title="Gaiety Theatre, London">Gaiety Theatre</a>. <i>Thespis</i> outran five of its nine competitors for the 1871 holiday season, and its run was extended beyond the length of a normal run at the Gaiety,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  However, nothing more came of it at that point, and Gilbert and Sullivan went their separate ways. Gilbert worked again with Clay on <i><a href="/wiki/Happy_Arcadia" title="Happy Arcadia">Happy Arcadia</a></i> (1872), and with <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Cellier" title="Alfred Cellier">Alfred Cellier</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Topsyturveydom" title="Topsyturveydom">Topsyturveydom</a></i> (1874), as well as writing several farces, operetta libretti, <a href="/wiki/Extravaganza" title="Extravaganza">extravaganzas</a>, fairy comedies, adaptations from novels, translations from the French, and the dramas described above. Also in 1874, he published his last contribution for <i>Fun</i> magazine (<i>"Rosencrantz and Guildenstern"</i>), after a gap of three years, then resigned due to disapproval of the new owner\'s other publishing interests.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png/220px-Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png" width="220" height="106" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png/330px-Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png/440px-Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png 2x" data-file-width="3414" data-file-height="1650" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thespis_-_Illustrated_London_News_Jan_6_1872.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of <i>Thespis</i> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Illustrated_London_News" title="The Illustrated London News">The Illustrated London News</a></i>, 6 January 1872</div></div></div>\n<p>It would be nearly four years after <i>Thespis</i> was produced before the two men worked together again. In 1868, Gilbert had published a short comic sketch in <i>Fun</i> magazine titled "Trial by Jury: An Operetta". In 1873, Gilbert was asked by the theatrical manager, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Rosa" title="Carl Rosa">Carl Rosa</a>, to write a work for his planned 1874 season. Gilbert expanded <i>Trial</i> into a one-act libretto. However, Rosa\'s wife <a href="/wiki/Euphrosyne_Parepa-Rosa" title="Euphrosyne Parepa-Rosa">Euphrosyne Parepa-Rosa</a>, a childhood friend of Gilbert\'s, died after an illness in 1874 and Rosa dropped the project.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Later in 1874 Gilbert offered the libretto to <a href="/wiki/Richard_D%27Oyly_Carte" title="Richard D&#39;Oyly Carte">Richard D\'Oyly Carte</a>, but Carte could not use the piece at that time. By early 1875, Carte was managing the <a href="/wiki/Royalty_Theatre" title="Royalty Theatre">Royalty Theatre</a>, and he needed a short opera to be played as an afterpiece to <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Offenbach" title="Jacques Offenbach">Offenbach</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/La_P%C3%A9richole" title="La P\xc3\xa9richole">La P\xc3\xa9richole</a></i>. He contacted Gilbert, asked about the piece, and suggested Sullivan to set the work. Sullivan was enthusiastic, and <i><a href="/wiki/Trial_by_Jury" title="Trial by Jury">Trial by Jury</a></i> was composed in a matter of weeks. The little piece was a runaway hit, outlasting the run of <i>La P\xc3\xa9richole</i> and being revived at another theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;n 10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy,_Engaged.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy%2C_Engaged.jpg/250px-W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy%2C_Engaged.jpg" width="250" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy%2C_Engaged.jpg/375px-W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy%2C_Engaged.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy%2C_Engaged.jpg/500px-W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy%2C_Engaged.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3952" data-file-height="3116" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:W.S._Gilbert%27s_burlesque_comedy,_Engaged.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Engaged_(play)" title="Engaged (play)">Engaged</a></i> (1877) was Gilbert\'s most successful non-musical play</div></div></div>\n<p>Gilbert continued his quest to gain respect in and respectability for his profession. One thing that may have been holding dramatists back from respectability was that plays were not published in a form suitable for a "gentleman\'s library", as, at the time, they were generally cheaply and unattractively published for the use of actors rather than the home reader. To help rectify this, at least for himself, Gilbert arranged in late 1875 for publishers Chatto and Windus to print a volume of his plays in a format designed to appeal to the general reader, with an attractive binding and clear type, containing Gilbert\'s most respectable plays, including his most serious works, but mischievously capped off with <i>Trial by Jury</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the success of <i>Trial by Jury</i>, there were discussions towards reviving <i>Thespis</i>, but Gilbert and Sullivan were not able to agree on terms with Carte and his backers. The score to <i>Thespis</i> was never published, and most of the music is now lost. It took some time for Carte to gather funds for another Gilbert and Sullivan opera, and in this gap Gilbert produced several works including <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Cobb" title="Tom Cobb">Tom Cobb</a></i> (1875), <i><a href="/wiki/Eyes_and_No_Eyes" title="Eyes and No Eyes">Eyes and No Eyes</a></i> (1875, his last German Reed Entertainment), and <i><a href="/wiki/Princess_Toto" title="Princess Toto">Princess Toto</a></i> (1876), his last and most ambitious work with Clay, a three-act comic opera with full orchestra, as opposed to the shorter works for much reduced accompaniment that came before. Gilbert also wrote two serious works during this time, <i><a href="/wiki/Broken_Hearts" title="Broken Hearts">Broken Hearts</a></i> (1875) and <i><a href="/wiki/Dan%27l_Druce,_Blacksmith" title="Dan&#39;l Druce, Blacksmith">Dan\'l Druce, Blacksmith</a></i> (1876).<sup id="cite_ref-PlayList_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PlayList-35">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Also during this period, Gilbert wrote his most successful comic play, <i><a href="/wiki/Engaged_(play)" title="Engaged (play)">Engaged</a></i> (1877), which inspired <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Importance_of_Being_Earnest" title="The Importance of Being Earnest">The Importance of Being Earnest</a></i>. <i>Engaged</i> is a parody of romantic drama written in the "topsy-turvy" satiric style of many of Gilbert\'s Bab Ballads and the <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Opera" class="mw-redirect" title="Savoy Opera">Savoy Operas</a>, with one character pledging his love, in the most poetic and romantic language possible, to every single woman in the play; the "innocent" Scottish rustics being revealed to be making a living through throwing trains off the lines and then charging the passengers for services, and, in general, romance being gladly thrown over in favour of monetary gain. <i>Engaged</i> continues to be performed today by both professional and amateur companies.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;n 11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peak_collaborative_years">Peak collaborative years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Peak collaborative years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg/220px-Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg" width="220" height="276" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg/330px-Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg/440px-Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2372" data-file-height="2972" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sorc-Pin-Trial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An early poster showing scenes from the first three <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a> operas after <i><a href="/wiki/Thespis_(opera)" title="Thespis (opera)">Thespis</a></i></div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a></div>\n<p>Carte finally assembled a syndicate in 1877 and formed the Comedy Opera Company to launch a series of original English comic operas, beginning with a third collaboration between Gilbert and Sullivan, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sorcerer" title="The Sorcerer">The Sorcerer</a></i>, in November 1877. This work was a modest success,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/H.M.S._Pinafore" title="H.M.S. Pinafore">H.M.S. Pinafore</a></i> followed in May 1878. Despite a slow start, mainly due to a scorching summer, <i>Pinafore</i> became a red-hot favourite by autumn. After a dispute with Carte over the division of profits, the other Comedy Opera Company partners hired thugs to storm the theatre one night to steal the sets and costumes, intending to mount a rival production. The attempt was repelled by stagehands and others at the theatre loyal to Carte, and Carte continued as sole impresario of the newly renamed <a href="/wiki/D%27Oyly_Carte_Opera_Company" title="D&#39;Oyly Carte Opera Company">D\'Oyly Carte Opera Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Indeed, <i>Pinafore</i> was so successful that over a hundred unauthorised productions sprang up in America alone. Gilbert, Sullivan and Carte tried for many years to control the American performance copyrights over their operas, without success.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png/220px-The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png/330px-The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png/440px-The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="2969" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Princess_-_W._S._Gilbert.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gilbert reworked his 1870 farce, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Princess_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Princess (play)">The Princess</a></i>, illustrated here, into <i><a href="/wiki/Princess_Ida" title="Princess Ida">Princess Ida</a></i> (1884).</div></div></div>\n<p>For the next decade, the <a href="/wiki/Savoy_opera" title="Savoy opera">Savoy Operas</a> (as the series came to be known, after the <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Theatre" title="Savoy Theatre">theatre</a> Carte later built to house them) were Gilbert\'s principal activity. The successful comic operas with Sullivan continued to appear every year or two, several of them being among the longest-running productions up to that point in the history of the musical stage.<sup id="cite_ref-beauty_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beauty-74">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;n 12&#93;</a></sup> After <i>Pinafore</i> came <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pirates_of_Penzance" title="The Pirates of Penzance">The Pirates of Penzance</a></i> (1879), <i><a href="/wiki/Patience_(opera)" title="Patience (opera)">Patience</a></i> (1881), <i><a href="/wiki/Iolanthe" title="Iolanthe">Iolanthe</a></i> (1882), <i><a href="/wiki/Princess_Ida" title="Princess Ida">Princess Ida</a></i> (1884, based on Gilbert\'s earlier farce, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Princess_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Princess (play)">The Princess</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i> (1885), <i><a href="/wiki/Ruddigore" title="Ruddigore">Ruddigore</a></i> (1887), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Yeomen_of_the_Guard" title="The Yeomen of the Guard">The Yeomen of the Guard</a></i> (1888) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gondoliers" title="The Gondoliers">The Gondoliers</a></i> (1889). Gilbert not only directed and oversaw all aspects of production for these works, but he actually <i>designed</i> the costumes himself for <i>Patience</i>, <i>Iolanthe</i>, <i>Princess Ida</i>, and <i>Ruddigore</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>  He insisted on precise and authentic sets and costumes, which provided a foundation to ground and focus his absurd characters and situations.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg/170px-The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg" width="170" height="252" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg/255px-The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg/340px-The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg 2x" data-file-width="376" data-file-height="558" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Mikado_Three_Little_Maids.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lithograph from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>During this time, Gilbert and Sullivan also collaborated on one other major work, the oratorio <i><a href="/wiki/The_Martyr_of_Antioch" title="The Martyr of Antioch">The Martyr of Antioch</a></i>, premiered at the Leeds music festival in October 1880. Gilbert arranged the original epic poem by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hart_Milman" title="Henry Hart Milman">Henry Hart Milman</a> into a libretto suitable for the music, and it contains some original work. During this period, also, Gilbert occasionally wrote plays to be performed elsewhere\xe2\x80\x93both serious dramas (for example <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ne%27er-Do-Weel" class="mw-redirect" title="The Ne&#39;er-Do-Weel">The Ne\'er-Do-Weel</a></i>, 1878; and <a href="/wiki/Gretchen_(play)" title="Gretchen (play)">Gretchen</a>, 1879) and humorous works (for example <i><a href="/wiki/Foggerty%27s_Fairy" title="Foggerty&#39;s Fairy">Foggerty\'s Fairy</a></i>, 1881). However, he no longer needed to turn out multiple plays each year, as he had done before. Indeed, during the more than nine years that separated <i>The Pirates of Penzance</i> and <i>The Gondoliers</i>, he wrote just three plays outside of the partnership with Sullivan.<sup id="cite_ref-PlayList_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PlayList-35">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Only one of these works, <i>Comedy and Tragedy</i>, proved successful.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Although <i>Comedy and Tragedy</i> had a short run due to the lead actress refusing to act during <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">Holy Week</a>, the play was revived regularly. With respect to <i><a href="/wiki/Brantinghame_Hall" title="Brantinghame Hall">Brantinghame Hall</a></i>, Stedman writes, "It was a failure, the worst failure of Gilbert\'s career."<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1878, Gilbert realised a lifelong dream to play <a href="/wiki/Harlequinade" title="Harlequinade">Harlequin</a>, which he did at the Gaiety Theatre as part of an amateur charity production of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Forty_Thieves" title="The Forty Thieves">The Forty Thieves</a></i>, partly written by himself.   Gilbert trained for Harlequin\'s stylised dancing with his friend <a href="/wiki/John_D%27Auban" title="John D&#39;Auban">John D\'Auban</a>, who had arranged the dances for some of his plays and would choreograph most of the Gilbert and Sullivan operas.<sup id="cite_ref-Timesobit_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timesobit-80">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tm_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tm-81">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>  Producer <a href="/wiki/John_Hollingshead" title="John Hollingshead">John Hollingshead</a> later remembered, "the gem of the performance was the grimly earnest and determined Harlequin of W. S. Gilbert. It gave me an idea of what <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> would have made of the character."<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Another member of the cast recalled that Gilbert was tirelessly enthusiastic about the piece and often invited the cast to his home for dinner extra rehearsals. "A pleasanter, more genial, or agreeable companion than he was it would have been difficult, if not impossible, to find."<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> In 1882, Gilbert had a telephone installed in his home and at the prompt desk at the Savoy Theatre, so that he could monitor performances and rehearsals from his home study. Gilbert had referred to the new technology in <i>Pinafore</i> in 1878, only two years after the device was invented and before London even had telephone service.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carpet_quarrel_and_end_of_the_collaboration">Carpet quarrel and end of the collaboration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Carpet quarrel and end of the collaboration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Gilbert sometimes had a strained working relationship with Sullivan, partly because each man saw himself allowing his work to be subjugated to the other\'s, and partly due to their opposing personalities.  Gilbert was often confrontational and notoriously thin-skinned, though prone to acts of extraordinary kindness, while Sullivan eschewed conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpet_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpet-85">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In addition, Gilbert imbued his libretti with "topsy-turvy" situations in which the social order was turned upside down. After a time, these subjects were often at odds with Sullivan\'s desire for realism and emotional content.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> In addition, Gilbert\'s political satire often poked fun at those in the circles of privilege, while Sullivan was eager to socialise among the wealthy and titled people who would become his friends and patrons.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;n 13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WSG_by_Holl.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/WSG_by_Holl.jpg/220px-WSG_by_Holl.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/WSG_by_Holl.jpg/330px-WSG_by_Holl.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/WSG_by_Holl.jpg/440px-WSG_by_Holl.jpg 2x" data-file-width="956" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WSG_by_Holl.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Holl" title="Frank Holl">Frank Holl</a> (1886) in the <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Portrait Gallery (United Kingdom)">National Portrait Gallery</a>, London, next to <a href="/wiki/John_Everett_Millais" title="John Everett Millais">Millais</a>\' 1888 portrait of Sullivan.</div></div></div>\n<p>Throughout their collaboration, Gilbert and Sullivan disagreed several times over the choice of a subject. After both <i>Princess Ida</i> and <i>Ruddigore</i>, which were less successful than the seven other operas from <i>H.M.S. Pinafore</i> to <i>The Gondoliers</i>, Sullivan asked to leave the partnership, saying that he found Gilbert\'s plots repetitive and that the operas were not artistically satisfying to him. While the two artists worked out their differences, Carte kept the Savoy open with revivals of their earlier works. On each occasion, after a few months\' pause, Gilbert responded with a libretto that met Sullivan\'s objections, and the partnership continued successfully.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpet_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpet-85">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 1890, during the run of <i>The Gondoliers</i>, however, Gilbert challenged Carte over the expenses of the production. Among other items to which Gilbert objected, Carte had charged the cost of a new carpet for the Savoy Theatre lobby to the partnership.<sup id="cite_ref-Ford_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ford-90">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert believed that this was a maintenance expense that should be charged to Carte alone. Gilbert confronted Carte, who refused to reconsider the accounts. Gilbert stormed out and wrote to Sullivan that "I left him with the remark that it was a mistake to kick down the ladder by which he had risen".<sup id="cite_ref-Carpet_85-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpet-85">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Helen_Carte" title="Helen Carte">Helen Carte</a> wrote that Gilbert had addressed Carte "in a way that I should not have thought you would have used to an offending menial."<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> As the scholar Andrew Crowther has commented:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>After all, the carpet was only one of a number of disputed items, and the real issue lay not in the mere money value of these things, but in whether Carte could be trusted with the financial affairs of Gilbert and Sullivan. Gilbert contended that Carte had at best made a series of serious blunders in the accounts, and at worst deliberately attempted to swindle the others. It is not easy to settle the rights and wrongs of the issue at this distance, but it does seem fairly clear that there was something very wrong with the accounts at this time. Gilbert wrote to Sullivan on 28 May 1891, a year after the end of the "Quarrel", that Carte had admitted "an unintentional overcharge of nearly \xc2\xa31,000 in the electric lighting accounts alone."<sup id="cite_ref-Carpet_85-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpet-85">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>Gilbert brought suit, and after <i>The Gondoliers</i> closed in 1891, he withdrew the performance rights to his libretti, vowing to write no more operas for the Savoy.<sup id="cite_ref-vowed_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vowed-92">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert next wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mountebanks_(opera)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mountebanks (opera)">The Mountebanks</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Cellier" title="Alfred Cellier">Alfred Cellier</a> and the flop <i><a href="/wiki/Haste_to_the_Wedding" title="Haste to the Wedding">Haste to the Wedding</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/George_Grossmith" title="George Grossmith">George Grossmith</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-PlayList_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PlayList-35">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and Sullivan wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Haddon_Hall_(opera)" title="Haddon Hall (opera)">Haddon Hall</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Grundy" title="Sydney Grundy">Sydney Grundy</a>. Gilbert eventually won the lawsuit and felt vindicated, but his actions and statements had been hurtful to his partners. Nevertheless, the partnership had been so profitable that, after the financial failure of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_English_Opera_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal English Opera House">Royal English Opera House</a>, Carte and his wife sought to reunite the author and composer.<sup id="cite_ref-vowed_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vowed-92">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg/300px-Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg" width="300" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg/450px-Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg/600px-Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3019" data-file-height="1259" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Utopia_Limited_Poster.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Drawing Room Scene from <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia,_Limited" title="Utopia, Limited">Utopia, Limited</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1891, after many failed attempts at reconciliation by the pair, <a href="/wiki/Chappell_%26_Co." title="Chappell &amp; Co.">Tom Chappell</a>, the music publisher responsible for printing the Gilbert and Sullivan operas, stepped in to mediate between two of his most profitable artists, and within two weeks had succeeded.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>  Two more operas resulted: <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia,_Limited" title="Utopia, Limited">Utopia, Limited</a></i> (1893) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grand_Duke" title="The Grand Duke">The Grand Duke</a></i> (1896). Gilbert also offered a third libretto to Sullivan (<i><a href="/wiki/His_Excellency_(opera)" title="His Excellency (opera)">His Excellency</a></i>, 1894), but Gilbert\'s insistence on casting <a href="/wiki/Nancy_McIntosh" title="Nancy McIntosh">Nancy McIntosh</a>, his proteg\xc3\xa9e from <i>Utopia</i>, led to Sullivan\'s refusal.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> <i>Utopia</i>, concerning an attempt to "anglicise" a south Pacific island kingdom, was only a modest success, and <i>The Grand Duke</i>, in which a theatrical troupe, by means of a "statutory duel" and a conspiracy, takes political control of a grand duchy, was an outright failure. After that, the partnership ended for good.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Sullivan continued to compose comic opera with other librettists but died four years later. In 1904, Gilbert would write, "...&#160;Savoy opera was snuffed out by the deplorable death of my distinguished collaborator, Sir Arthur Sullivan. When that event occurred, I saw no one with whom I felt that I could work with satisfaction and success, and so I discontinued to write <i>libretti</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9c/The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png/220px-The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png" width="220" height="306" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9c/The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png/330px-The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9c/The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png/440px-The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png 2x" data-file-width="2910" data-file-height="4052" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Story_of_the_Mikado_-_Frontispiece.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frontispiece by <a href="/wiki/Alice_B._Woodward" title="Alice B. Woodward">Alice B. Woodward</a> to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Story_of_the_Mikado" class="mw-redirect" title="The Story of the Mikado">The Story of the Mikado</a></i>, Gilbert\'s last literary work, published posthumously in 1921.</div></div></div>\n<p>Gilbert built the <a href="/wiki/Garrick_Theatre" title="Garrick Theatre">Garrick Theatre</a> in 1889.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The Gilberts moved to <a href="/wiki/Grim%27s_Dyke" title="Grim&#39;s Dyke">Grim\'s Dyke</a> in <a href="/wiki/Harrow,_London" title="Harrow, London">Harrow</a> in 1890, which he purchased from Robert Heriot, to whom the artist <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Goodall" title="Frederick Goodall">Frederick Goodall</a> had sold the property in 1880.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> In 1891, Gilbert was appointed <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Peace">Justice of the Peace</a> for <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> After casting Nancy McIntosh in <i>Utopia, Limited</i>, he and his wife developed an affection for her, and she eventually gained the status of an unofficially adopted daughter, moving to Grim\'s Dyke to live with them. She continued living there, even after Gilbert died, until Lady Gilbert\'s death in 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> A statue of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, carved by Danish sculptor <a href="/wiki/Caius_Gabriel_Cibber" title="Caius Gabriel Cibber">Caius Gabriel Cibber</a> in 1681, was moved in 1875 from <a href="/wiki/Soho_Square" title="Soho Square">Soho Square</a> to an island in the lake at Grim\'s Dyke, where it remained when Gilbert purchased the property.<sup id="cite_ref-Cibber_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cibber-101">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> On Lady Gilbert\'s direction, it was restored to Soho Square in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News,_Feb_8_1873.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_Feb_8_1873.PNG/220px-Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_Feb_8_1873.PNG" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_Feb_8_1873.PNG/330px-Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_Feb_8_1873.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_Feb_8_1873.PNG/440px-Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News%2C_Feb_8_1873.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1903" data-file-height="1442" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wicked_World_-_Illustrated_London_News,_Feb_8_1873.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/D._H._Friston" class="mw-redirect" title="D. H. Friston">D. H. Friston</a>\'s illustration of a scene from Gilbert\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wicked_World" title="The Wicked World">The Wicked World</a></i> (1873), which he reworked into <i><a href="/wiki/Fallen_Fairies" title="Fallen Fairies">Fallen Fairies</a></i> (1909)</div></div></div>\n<p>Although Gilbert announced a retirement from the theatre after the short run of his last work with Sullivan, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grand_Duke" title="The Grand Duke">The Grand Duke</a></i> (1896) and the poor reception of his 1897 play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fortune_Hunter" title="The Fortune Hunter">The Fortune Hunter</a></i>, he produced at least three more plays over the last dozen years of his life, including an unsuccessful opera, <i><a href="/wiki/Fallen_Fairies" title="Fallen Fairies">Fallen Fairies</a></i> (1909), with <a href="/wiki/Edward_German" title="Edward German">Edward German</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert also continued to supervise the various revivals of his works by the D\'Oyly Carte Opera Company, including its London Repertory seasons in 1906\xe2\x80\x9309.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> His last play, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hooligan" title="The Hooligan">The Hooligan</a></i>, produced just four months before his death, is a study of a young condemned thug in a prison cell.  Gilbert shows sympathy for his protagonist, the son of a thief who, brought up among thieves, kills his girlfriend.  As in some earlier work, the playwright displays "his conviction that nurture rather than nature often accounted for criminal behaviour".<sup id="cite_ref-Bradley100_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley100-105">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>  The grim and powerful piece became one of Gilbert\'s most successful serious dramas, and experts conclude that, in those last months of Gilbert\'s life, he was developing a new style, a "mixture of irony, of social theme, and of grubby realism,"<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> to replace the old "Gilbertianism" of which he had grown weary.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> In these last years, Gilbert also wrote children\'s book versions of <i><a href="/wiki/H.M.S._Pinafore" title="H.M.S. Pinafore">H.M.S. Pinafore</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i> giving, in some cases, backstory that is not found in the librettos.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gilbert was <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighted</a> on 15 July 1907 in recognition of his contributions to drama.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Sullivan had been knighted for his contributions to music almost a quarter of a century earlier, in 1883. Gilbert was, however, the first British writer ever to receive a knighthood for his plays alone\xe2\x80\x94earlier dramatist knights, such as <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Davenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir William Davenant">Sir William Davenant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Vanbrugh" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir John Vanbrugh">Sir John Vanbrugh</a>, were knighted for political and other services.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg/220px-Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg/330px-Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg/440px-Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2646" data-file-height="2645" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Memorial_to_WS_Gilbert_v4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Memorial to W. S. Gilbert on <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Embankment" title="Victoria Embankment">Victoria Embankment</a>, London by <a href="/wiki/George_Frampton" title="George Frampton">George Frampton</a>, 1914</div></div></div>\n<p>On 29 May 1911, Gilbert was about to give a swimming lesson to two young women, Winifred Isabel Emery (1890\xe2\x80\x931972),<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> and 17-year-old <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Preece" title="Patricia Preece">Ruby Preece</a><sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> in the lake of his home, <a href="/wiki/Grim%27s_Dyke" title="Grim&#39;s Dyke">Grim\'s Dyke</a>, when Preece got into difficulties and called for help.<sup id="cite_ref-Ford_90-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ford-90">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert dived in to save her but suffered a heart attack in the middle of the lake and died at the age of 74.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> He was cremated at <a href="/wiki/Golders_Green_Crematorium" title="Golders Green Crematorium">Golders Green</a> and his ashes buried at the churchyard of <a href="/wiki/St_John_the_Evangelist,_Great_Stanmore" title="St John the Evangelist, Great Stanmore">St. John\'s Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanmore" title="Stanmore">Stanmore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The inscription on Gilbert\'s memorial on the south wall of the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Embankment" title="Thames Embankment">Thames Embankment</a> in London reads: "His Foe was Folly, and his Weapon Wit".<sup id="cite_ref-MikeLeigh_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MikeLeigh-43">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> There is also a memorial plaque at All Saints\' Church, Harrow Weald.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personality">Personality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Gilbert was known for being sometimes prickly.  Aware of this general impression, he claimed that "If you give me your attention",<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Misanthrope" class="mw-redirect" title="Misanthrope">misanthrope</a>\'s song from <i><a href="/wiki/Princess_Ida" title="Princess Ida">Princess Ida</a></i>, was a satiric self-reference, saying: "I thought it my duty to live up to my reputation."<sup id="cite_ref-Prickly_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prickly-120">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> However, many people have defended him, often citing his generosity.  Actress <a href="/wiki/May_Fortescue" title="May Fortescue">May Fortescue</a> recalled, "His kindness was extraordinary.  On wet nights and when rehearsals were late and the last buses were gone, he would pay the cab-fares of the girls whether they were pretty or not, instead of letting them trudge home on foot&#160;... He was just as large-hearted when he was poor as when he was rich and successful.  For money as money he cared less than nothing.  Gilbert was no plaster saint, but he was an ideal friend."<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> The journalist Frank M. Boyd wrote:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I fancy that seldom was a man more generally given credit for a personality quite other than his own, than was the case with Sir W. S. Gilbert&#160;... Till one actually came to know the man, one shared the opinion held by so many, that he was a gruff, disagreeable person; but nothing could be less true of the really great humorist. He had rather a severe appearance&#160;... and like many other clever people, he had precious little use for fools of either sex, but he was at heart as kindly and lovable a man as you could wish to meet.<sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png/170px-Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png" width="170" height="299" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png/255px-Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png/340px-Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png 2x" data-file-width="552" data-file-height="972" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ws_gilbert_cartoon.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Caricature from <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i>, 1881</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg/170px-WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg" width="170" height="295" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg/255px-WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg/340px-WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="648" data-file-height="1125" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WSGilbert_by_Spy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gilbert by the cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Ward" title="Leslie Ward">\'Spy\'</a>, 1880s</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Jessie_Bond" title="Jessie Bond">Jessie Bond</a> wrote that Gilbert "was quick-tempered, often unreasonable, and he could not bear to be thwarted, but how anyone could call him unamiable I cannot understand."<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> George Grossmith wrote to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> that, although Gilbert had been described as an autocrat at rehearsals, "That was really only his manner when he was playing the part of stage director at rehearsals. As a matter of fact, he was a generous, kind true gentleman, and I use the word in the purest and original sense."<sup id="cite_ref-GG1911_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG1911-124">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Aside from his occasional creative disagreements with, and eventual rift from, Sullivan, Gilbert\'s temper led to the loss of friendships with a number of people. For instance, he quarrelled with his old associate <a href="/wiki/C._H._Workman" class="mw-redirect" title="C. H. Workman">C. H. Workman</a>, over the firing of Nancy McIntosh from the production of <i>Fallen Fairies</i>, and with actress <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Hodson" title="Henrietta Hodson">Henrietta Hodson</a>. He also saw his friendship with theatre critic <a href="/wiki/Clement_Scott" title="Clement Scott">Clement Scott</a> turn bitter.  However, Gilbert could be extraordinarily kind. During Scott\'s final illness in 1904, for instance, Gilbert donated to a fund for him, visited nearly every day, and assisted Scott\'s wife,<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> despite having not been on friendly terms with him for the previous sixteen years.<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, Gilbert had written several plays at the behest of comic actor <a href="/wiki/Edward_Askew_Sothern" title="Edward Askew Sothern">Ned Sothern</a>. However, Sothern died before he could perform the last of these, <i>Foggerty\'s Fairy</i>.  Gilbert purchased the play back from his grateful widow.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup>  According to one London society lady:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[Gilbert]\'s wit was innate, and his rapier-like retorts slipped out with instantaneous ease.  His mind was naturally fastidious and clean; he never asserted himself, never tried to make an effect.  He was great-hearted and most understanding, with an underlying poetry of fancy that made him the most delicious companion.  They spoke of his quick temper, but that was entirely free from malice or guile.  He was soft-hearted as a babe, but there was nothing of the hypocrite about him.  What he thought he said on the instant, and though by people of sensitive vanity this might on occasion be resented, to a sensitiveness of a finer kind it was an added link, binding one to a faithful, valued friend.<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>As the writings about Gilbert by husband and wife <a href="/wiki/Seymour_Hicks" title="Seymour Hicks">Seymour Hicks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ellaline_Terriss" title="Ellaline Terriss">Ellaline Terriss</a> (frequent guests at his home) vividly illustrate, Gilbert\'s relationships with women were generally more successful than his relationships with men.<sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup>  According to <a href="/wiki/George_Grossmith" title="George Grossmith">George Grossmith</a>, Gilbert "was to those who knew him a courteous and amiable gentleman \xe2\x80\x93 a gentleman without veneer."<sup id="cite_ref-Prickly_120-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prickly-120">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup>  Grossmith and many others wrote of how Gilbert loved to amuse children:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>During my dangerous illness, Mr. Gilbert never failed a day to come up and enquire after me&#160;... and kept me in roars of laughter the whole time&#160;... But to see Gilbert at his best, is to see him at one of his juvenile parties. Though he has no children of his own, he loves them, and there is nothing he would not do to please them. I was never so astonished as when on one occasion he put off some of his own friends to come with Mrs. Gilbert to a juvenile party at my own house.<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Gilbert\'s niece Mary Carter confirmed, "...&#160;he loved children very much and lost no opportunity of making them happy&#160;... [He was] the kindest and most human of uncles."<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup>  Grossmith quoted Gilbert as saying, "<a href="/wiki/Deer-stalking" class="mw-redirect" title="Deer-stalking">Deer-stalking</a> would be a very fine sport if only the deer had guns."<sup id="cite_ref-GG1911_124-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GG1911-124">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W._S._Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cultural_influence_of_Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Cultural influence of Gilbert and Sullivan">Cultural influence of Gilbert and Sullivan</a></div>\n<p>In 1957, a review in <i>The Times</i> explained "the continued vitality of the Savoy operas" as follows:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[T]hey were never really contemporary in their idiom&#160;... Gilbert and Sullivan\'s [world], from the first moment was obviously not the audience\'s world, [it was] an artificial world, with a neatly controlled and shapely precision which has not gone out of fashion \xe2\x80\x93 because it was never in fashion in the sense of using the fleeting conventions and ways of thought of contemporary human society&#160;... The neat articulation of incredibilities in Gilbert\'s plots is perfectly matched by his language&#160;... His dialogue, with its primly mocking formality, satisfies both the ear and the intelligence.  His verses show an unequalled and very delicate gift for creating a comic effect by the contrast between poetic form and prosaic thought and wording&#160;... How deliciously [his lines] prick the bubble of sentiment.  Gilbert had many imitators, but no equals, at this sort of thing&#160;... [Of] equal importance&#160;... Gilbert\'s lyrics almost invariably take on extra point and sparkle when set to Sullivan\'s music&#160;... The two men together remain endlessly and incomparably delightful&#160;... Light, and even trifling, though [the operas] may seem upon grave consideration, they yet have the shapeliness and elegance that can make a trifle into a work of art.<sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gilbert-library.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Gilbert-library.jpg/220px-Gilbert-library.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/70/Gilbert-library.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="303" data-file-height="220" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gilbert-library.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gilbert in his library</div></div></div>\n<p>Gilbert\'s legacy, aside from building the <a href="/wiki/Garrick_Theatre" title="Garrick Theatre">Garrick Theatre</a> and writing the Savoy Operas and other works that are still being performed or in print nearly 150 years after their creation, is felt perhaps most strongly today through his influence on the American and British musical theatre. The innovations in content and form of the works that he and Sullivan developed, and in Gilbert\'s theories of acting and stage direction, directly influenced the development of the modern musical throughout the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-PeterDowns_133-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PeterDowns-133">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> Gilbert\'s lyrics employ punning, as well as complex internal and two and three-syllable rhyme schemes, and served as a model for such 20th century Broadway librettists and lyricists as <a href="/wiki/P.G._Wodehouse" class="mw-redirect" title="P.G. Wodehouse">P.G. Wodehouse</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-135" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-135">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">Cole Porter</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-136" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-136">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ira_Gershwin" title="Ira Gershwin">Ira Gershwin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-137" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-137">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_Hart" title="Lorenz Hart">Lorenz Hart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Hammerstein_II" title="Oscar Hammerstein II">Oscar Hammerstein II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PeterDowns_133-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PeterDowns-133">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gilbert\'s influence on the English language has also been marked, with well-known phrases such as "A policeman\'s lot is not a happy one", "<a href="/wiki/Short,_sharp_shock" title="Short, sharp shock">short, sharp shock</a>", "What never? Well, hardly ever!",<sup id="cite_ref-138" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-138">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> and "let the punishment fit the crime" arising from his pen.<sup id="cite_ref-140" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-140">&#91;n 14&#93;</a></sup> In addition, biographies continue to be written about Gilbert\'s life and career,<sup id="cite_ref-141" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-141">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup> and his work is not only performed, but frequently <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parodied</a>, pastiched, quoted and imitated in comedy routines, film, television and other popular media.<sup id="cite_ref-PeterDowns_133-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PeterDowns-133">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-142" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-142">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ian Bradley, in connection with the 100th anniversary of Gilbert\'s death in 2011 wrote:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There has been much discussion about Gilbert\'s proper place in British literary and dramatic history. Was he essentially a writer of burlesque, a satirist, or, as some have argued, the forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_the_absurd" class="mw-redirect" title="Theatre of the absurd">theatre of the absurd</a>?&#160;... Perhaps he stands most clearly in that distinctively English satirical tradition which stretches back to <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>.&#160;... Its leading exponents lampoon and send up the major institutions and public figures of the day, wielding the weapon of grave and temperate irony with devastating effect, while themselves remaining firmly within the Establishment and displaying a deep underlying affection for the objects of their often merciless attacks. It is a combination that remains a continuing enigma.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradley100_105-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley100-105">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span
