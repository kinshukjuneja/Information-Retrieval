He was an editor, journalist, psychogeographer, broadcaster and writer of creative non-fiction, as well as being a lecturer at <a href="/wiki/Swansea_University" title="Swansea University">Swansea University</a> and director of the creative writing programme there.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nigel_Jenkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jenkins was born on 20 July 1949 in <a href="/wiki/Gorseinon" title="Gorseinon">Gorseinon</a>, Wales, and was brought up on a farm on the former Kilvrough estate on the <a href="/wiki/Gower_Peninsula" title="Gower Peninsula">Gower Peninsula</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>. He was educated at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Essex" title="University of Essex">University of Essex</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nigel_Jenkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jenkins first came to prominence as one of the Welsh Arts Council\'s <i>Three Young Anglo-Welsh Poets</i> (the title of a 1974 collection featuring Jenkins, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Curtis_(Welsh_poet)" title="Tony Curtis (Welsh poet)">Tony Curtis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Bush" title="Duncan Bush">Duncan Bush</a> \xe2\x80\x93 all winners of the Council\'s Young Poets Prize). In 1976, he was given an Eric Gregory Award by the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Authors" title="Society of Authors">Society of Authors</a>.\n</p><p>Jenkins would go on to publish several collections of poetry over the course of his life, including, in 2002, the first haiku collection from a Welsh publisher (<i>Blue: 101 Haiku, Senryu and Tanka</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His poetry has been translated into French, German, Hungarian, Dutch and Russian, and his translations of modern Welsh poetry have appeared in numerous magazines and anthologies worldwide, including <i>The Bloodaxe Anthology of Modern Welsh Poetry</i> (2003). In 1998, the Russian journal <i>Literatura Innostranya</i> (Foreign Literature) published a selection of his poems, translated into Russian, for a feature on his work. He also composed poetry for public places \xe2\x80\x93 executed in stone, steel, neon, glass and other materials \xe2\x80\x93 in response to commissions from various public bodies.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg/220px-Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg/330px-Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg/440px-Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Poem_for_the_Good_Settler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\'Poem for the Good Settler\', Swansea</div></div></div>\n<p>A former newspaper journalist, Jenkins was an accomplished writer of prose. In 1996, he won the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Book_of_the_Year" title="Wales Book of the Year">Wales Book of the Year</a> prize for his travel book <i><a href="/wiki/Gwalia_in_Khasia" title="Gwalia in Khasia">Gwalia in Khasia</a></i> (1995) \xe2\x80\x93 the story of the Welsh Calvinistic Methodists\' Mission to the Khasi Hills in north-east India (1841\xe2\x80\x931969). Jenkins also edited an accompanying anthology of poetry and prose from the Khasi Hills, entitled <i>Khasia in Gwalia</i>. In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Gomer_Press" title="Gomer Press">Gomer Press</a> published a selection of his essays and articles as <i>Footsore on the Frontier</i> and, in 2008, <i>Real Swansea</i> \xe2\x80\x93 the first of his three contributions to <a href="/wiki/Seren_Books" title="Seren Books">Seren\'s</a> series of <a href="/wiki/Psychogeography" title="Psychogeography">psychogeographic</a> guide books \xe2\x80\x93 was released to much acclaim. A second volume (<i>Real Swansea Two</i>) was published in 2012, followed by a third, posthumous volume in 2014 (<i>Real Gower</i>), completing an unintended trilogy.\n</p><p>During his career, Jenkins proved himself to be a proficient editor, lending his keen editorial eye to a number of prominent projects and publications, including <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Encyclopedia of Wales">The Welsh Academy Encyclopaedia of Wales</a></i>, published by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wales_Press" title="University of Wales Press">University of Wales Press</a> in 2008. A highly respected pioneer of the haiku in Wales, he also co-edited the country\'s first national anthology of haiku poetry, <i>Another Country</i> (Gomer Press), in 2011.\n</p><p>Jenkins was a lecturer on Swansea University\'s Creative and Media Writing programme and, at the time of his death, lived in <a href="/wiki/Mumbles" title="Mumbles">Mumbles</a>, Swansea.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nigel_Jenkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jenkins died in the T\xc5\xb7 Olwen Hospice in Swansea on 28 January 2014, aged 64, following a short illness.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His funeral was held at St. Mary\'s Church, <a href="/wiki/Pennard" title="Pennard">Pennard</a>, on the morning of 10 February 2014. With the church at capacity, the ceremony was relayed by audio link-up to hundreds of mourners gathered in the nearby community hall. Jenkins was then buried in the graveyard of St. Mary\'s, the same resting place as fellow poets <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Watkins" title="Vernon Watkins">Vernon Watkins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harri_Webb" title="Harri Webb">Harri Webb</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nigel_Jenkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In July 2014, The H\'mm Foundation published <i>Encounters with Nigel</i>, an anthology of critical essays, creative pieces and tributes to Jenkins from fellow writers, former students and family members. The anthology was the third in the H\'mm Foundation\'s <i>Encounters</i> series, following publications dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">Dylan</a> and <a href="/wiki/R._S._Thomas" title="R. S. Thomas">R. S. Thomas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It was launched at <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas_Centre" title="Dylan Thomas Centre">Dylan Thomas Centre</a> on 19 July 2014 as part of <i>Cofio Nigel</i>, an event celebrating Jenkins\' life.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The punk band <a href="/wiki/Helen_Love" title="Helen Love">Helen Love</a> name-checked Jenkins on their single \'Where Dylan Thomas Talks To Me\', released in November 2014. The song revealed the band\'s desire to see the cycle path from Mumbles to Swansea being renamed \'The Nigel Jenkins Way\', with lead singer Love seeing it as a fitting tribute to "a fantastic writer and poet, a maverick, a punk rocker, somebody Swansea should be really proud of."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nigel_Jenkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r853264625">.mw-parser-output .refbegin{font-size:90%;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .refbegin-hanging-indents>ul{list-style-type:none;margin-left:0}.mw-parser-output .refbegin-hanging-indents>ul>li,.mw-parser-output .refbegin-hanging-indents>dl>dd{margin-left:0;padding-left:3.2em;text-indent:-3.2em;list-style:none}.mw-parser-output .refbegin-100{font-size:100%}</style><div class="refbegin columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 35em;
