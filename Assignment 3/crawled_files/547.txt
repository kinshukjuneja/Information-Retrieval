title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A <b>unitary state</b> is a <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> governed as a single power in which the <a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">central government</a> is ultimately supreme and any <a href="/wiki/Administrative_division" title="Administrative division">administrative divisions</a> (sub-national units) exercise only the powers that the central government chooses to delegate. The majority of states in the world have a unitary system of government. Of the 193 <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations" title="Member states of the United Nations">UN member states</a>, 165 are governed as unitary states.\n</p><p>In a unitary state, sub-national units are created and abolished (an example being the 22 mainland <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">regions of France</a> being merged into 13), and their powers may be broadened and narrowed, by the central government. Although <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">political power</a> may be delegated through <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a> to <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local governments</a> by <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a>, the central government remains supreme; it may abrogate the acts of devolved governments or curtail their powers.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/The_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="The United Kingdom">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland</a> is an example of a unitary state. <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> have a degree of autonomous devolved power, but such power is delegated by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>, which may enact laws unilaterally altering or abolishing devolution (<a href="/wiki/Devolved_English_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Devolved English Parliament">England does not have any devolved power</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Many unitary states have no areas possessing a degree of autonomy.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In such countries, sub-national regions cannot decide their own laws. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Kingdom of Norway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Unitary states are contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federations</a>, or federal states.  In such states, the sub-national governments share powers with the central government as equal actors through a written <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a>, to which the consent of both is required to make amendments. This means that the sub-national units have a right of existence and powers that cannot be unilaterally changed by the central government.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox"
