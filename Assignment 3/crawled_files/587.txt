a <a href="/wiki/Commercial_district" title="Commercial district">commercial</a> <a href="/wiki/Business_estate" class="mw-redirect" title="Business estate"> estate</a> and locality in between <a href="/wiki/Poplar,_London" title="Poplar, London">Poplar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millwall" title="Millwall">Millwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limehouse,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Limehouse, href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Tower_Hamlets" title="London Borough of Tower Hamlets">London Borough of Tower Hamlets</a>. It is one of the main <a href="/wiki/Financial_centres" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial centres">financial centres</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>, and contains many of Europe\'s tallest buildings, including the 2nd-tallest in the UK, <a href="/wiki/1_Canada_Square" class="mw-redirect" title="1 Canada Square">1 Canada Square</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Canary Wharf is 97 acres (39 hectares) and contains around 16,000,000 square feet (1,500,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) of office and retail space.  It comprises many open areas, including <a href="/wiki/Canada_Square" title="Canada Square">Canada Square</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabot_Square" title="Cabot Square">Cabot Square</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westferry_Circus" title="Westferry Circus">Westferry Circus</a>.  Together with <a href="/wiki/Heron_Quays" title="Heron Quays">Heron Quays</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_India_Quay" title="West India Quay">West India Quay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wood_Wharf" title="Wood href="/wiki/Cabot_Square" title="Cabot Square">Cabot Square</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Canary Wharf is located on the <a href="/wiki/West_India_Docks" title="West India Docks">West India Docks</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Dogs" title="Isle of Dogs">Isle of Dogs</a>.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>West India Dock Company</dt></dl>\n<p>From 1802 to the late 1980s, the Canary Wharf Estate was apart of <a href="/wiki/Millwall" title="Millwall">Millwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limehouse" title="Limehouse">Limehouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poplar,_London" title="Poplar, London">Poplar</a> and was one of the busiest docks in the world. After the 1960s, the port industry began to decline, leading to all the docks being closed by 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-Port_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Port-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-history_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> West India Docks was primarily developed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Milligan" title="Robert Milligan">Robert Milligan</a> (c. 1746\xe2\x80\x931809) who set up the West India Dock Company.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Port of London Authority</dt></dl>\n<p>West India Dock was by this time owned by the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_London_Authority" title="Port of London Authority">Port of London Authority</a> in 1909. Canary Wharf itself takes its name from No. 32 berth of the West Wood Quay of the Import Dock. This was built in 1936 for Fruit Lines Ltd, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Fred._Olsen_%26_Co." title="Fred. Olsen &amp; Co.">Fred Olsen Lines</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> fruit trade. The Canary islands were so named after the large dogs found there by the Spanish (Gran Canaria from Canine) and as it is located on the Isle of Dogs, the quay and warehouse were given the name Canary Wharf.<sup id="cite_ref-Brit_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>London Docklands Development Corporation</dt></dl>\n<p>After the docks closed in 1980, the British Government adopted policies to stimulate redevelopment of the area, including the creation of the <a href="/wiki/London_Docklands_Development_Corporation" title="London Docklands Development Corporation">London Docklands Development Corporation</a> (LDDC) in 1981 and the granting of <a href="/wiki/Urban_Enterprise_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban Enterprise Zone">Urban Enterprise Zone</a> status to the Isle of Dogs in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-history_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Canary Wharf of today began when <a href="/wiki/Michael_von_Clemm" title="Michael von Clemm">Michael von Clemm</a>, former chairman of <a href="/wiki/Credit_Suisse_First_Boston" title="Credit Suisse First Boston">Credit Suisse First Boston</a> (CSFB), came up with the idea to convert Canary Wharf into a <a href="/wiki/Back_office" title="Back office">back office</a>. Further discussions with <a href="/wiki/G_Ware_Travelstead" class="mw-redirect" title="G Ware Travelstead">G Ware Travelstead</a> led to proposals for a new business district and included the LDDC developing a cheap light metro scheme, called the <a href="/wiki/Docklands_Light_Railway" title="Docklands Light Railway">Docklands Light Railway</a> to make use a large amount of redundant railway infrastructure and to improve access. \n</p><p>The project was sold to the Canadian company <a href="/wiki/Olympia_%26_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympia &amp; York">Olympia &amp; York</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and construction began in 1988, master-planned by <a href="/wiki/Skidmore,_Owings_%26_Merrill" title="Skidmore, Owings &amp; Merrill">Skidmore, Owings &amp; Merrill</a> with <a href="/wiki/Yorke_Rosenberg_Mardall" title="Yorke Rosenberg Mardall">Yorke Rosenberg Mardall</a> as their UK advisors, and subsequently by Koetter Kim. The first buildings were completed in 1991, including <a href="/wiki/1_Canada_Square" class="mw-redirect" title="1 Canada Square">1 Canada Square</a>, which became the UK\'s tallest building at the time and a symbol of the regeneration of Docklands. By the time it opened, the London commercial property market had collapsed, and Olympia and York Canary Wharf Limited filed for bankruptcy in May 1992.\n</p><p>Initially, the City of London saw Canary Wharf as an existential threat. It modified its planning laws to expand the provision of new offices in the City of London, for example, creating offices above railway stations (Blackfriars) and roads (<a href="/wiki/125_London_Wall" title="125 London Wall">Alban Gate</a>). The resulting oversupply of office space contributed to the failure of the Canary Wharf project.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Canary Wharf Group</dt></dl>\n<p>In December 1995 an international consortium, backed by the former owners of <a href="/wiki/Olympia_%26_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympia &amp; York">Olympia &amp; York</a> and other investors, bought the scheme. The new company was called Canary Wharf Limited, and later became <a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf_Group" title="Canary Wharf Group">Canary Wharf Group</a>.\n</p><p>In 1997, some residents living on the Isle of Dogs launched a lawsuit against Canary Wharf Ltd for private nuisance because the tower interfered with TV signals. The residents lost the case.<sup id="cite_ref-hunter01_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hunter01-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Recovery in the property market generally, coupled with continuing demand for large floorplate Grade A office space, slowly improved the level of interest. A critical event in the recovery was the much-delayed start of work on the <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_Line_Extension" title="Jubilee Line Extension">Jubilee Line Extension</a>, which the government wanted ready for the <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">Millennium</a> celebrations.\n</p><p>In March 2004, Canary Wharf Group plc. was taken over by a consortium of investors, backed by its largest shareholder Glick Family Investments<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and led by <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a> using a vehicle named Songbird Estates plc.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tallest_buildings">Tallest buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Square</dt></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Canada_Square" title="Canada Square">Canada Square</a> is one of the central squares at Canary Wharf.  It is a large open space with grass, except during the winter when it is coverted into an ice rink.  The square is named after Canada, because the original developers of modern Canary Wharf, Olympia &amp; York, wanted to reflect their heritage.  Underneath the square is Canada Place shopping mall.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Westferry Circus</dt></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Westferry_Circus" title="Westferry Circus">Westferry Circus</a> is on the west side of Canary Wharf. It is a garden at ground level, and below is a roundabout allowing traffic to flow through.  The garden is enclosed by bespoke hand-crafted ornamental railings and entrance gates by artist Giuseppe Lund. The area has a long history, dating back to 1812, when the Poplar and Greenwich Roads Company operated a horse ferry between Greenwich and the Isle of Dogs. It operated on the West Ferry and East Ferry Roads, which the names survived. Westferry Circus was chosen as the name for the roundabout and park by virtue of its proximity to Westferry Road.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Cabot Square</dt></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Cabot_Square" title="Cabot Square">Cabot Square</a> is one of the biggest squares at Canary Wharf, with a large fountain at the centre. The inner perimeter has additional fountains covered by trees. The square has large cirular glass ventilation holes to allow gases to escape from the underneath car park. The square is named after <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> and his son Sebastian, who were Italian explorers who settled in England in 1484.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Churchill Place</dt></dl>\n<p>Churchill Place is an area on the east side of Canary Wharf.  It is named after <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Columbus Courtyard</dt></dl>\n<p>A small square on the west side of Canary Wharf. Named after <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> who was the Italian navigator who discovered America. The first phase of Canary Wharf was completed in 1992, 500 years after Columbus\' discovery and was a tribute to his achievements.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Chancellor Passage</dt></dl>\n<p>A passage way south of Cabot Square.  Named after <a href="/wiki/Richard_Chancellor" title="Richard Chancellor">Richard Chancellor</a> who sailed with Sir John Willoughby from Greenwich on their voyage through the White Sea to Moscow.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Wren Landing</dt></dl>\n<p>Small area north of Cabot Square. Leads to North Dock footbridge towards Port East. The station has two platforms in use, is in <a href="/wiki/Travelcard_Zone_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Travelcard Zone 2">Travelcard Zone 2</a>, and is on the <a href="/wiki/Lewisham" title="Lewisham">Lewisham</a> branch, between Canary Wharf and <a href="/wiki/South_Quay" class="mw-redirect" title="South Quay">South Quay</a>. The station was moved 200 metres south (to fit inside the new buildings) and a longer platform was built at this new site to accommodate three-unit trains planned as part of the DLR Capacity Enhancement; the station re-opened on 18 December 2002.\n</p><p>While <a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf_DLR_station" title="Canary Wharf DLR station">Canary Wharf Station</a> had been part of the original DLR plans, but the station was not ready when the DLR opened in August 1987. It was originally planned that the station would be similar to the original station at <a href="/wiki/Heron_Quays" title="Heron Quays">Heron Quays</a>, with 2 small platforms either side of the tracks. The station is located on the DLR between Heron Quays station and the <a href="/wiki/West_India_Quay_station" class="mw-redirect" title="West India Quay station">West India Quay station</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Travelcard_Zone_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Travelcard Zone 2">Travelcard Zone 2</a>.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>London Underground</dt></dl>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG/220px-CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG/330px-CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG/440px-CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CanaryWharfTubeA.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The interior of Canary Wharf tube station.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf_tube_station" title="Canary Wharf tube station">Canary Wharf tube station</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf_tube_station" title="Canary Wharf tube station">Canary Wharf Underground station</a> is a 2 platform station designed by Norman Foster and opened in 1999 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_Line_Extension" title="Jubilee Line Extension">Jubilee Line Extension</a> from Charing Cross to Stratford.
