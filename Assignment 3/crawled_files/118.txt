class="toclevel-1 tocsection-22"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">18</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip,_c.1725..jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip%2C_c.1725..jpg/220px-West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip%2C_c.1725..jpg" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip%2C_c.1725..jpg/330px-West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip%2C_c.1725..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip%2C_c.1725..jpg/440px-West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip%2C_c.1725..jpg 2x" data-file-width="1065" data-file-height="865" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:West_prospect_of_Gloucester_by_Kip,_c.1725..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jan_Kip_and_Leonard_Knyff" class="mw-redirect" title="Jan Kip and Leonard Knyff">Kip</a>\'s West prospect of Gloucester, c. 1725, emphasises the causeway and bridges traversing the water meadows of the floodplain.</div></div></div>\n<p>The origins of the name Gloucester are related to <i>Caerloyw</i>, its name in modern <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Language">Welsh</a>. The name \'caerloyw\' is composed of two parts: caer (meaning fort, stronghold or castle, and whose cognate is she suffix "cester", both deriving from the Latin <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra"><i>castrum</i></a>) and \'loyw\', a linquistic mutation of \'gloyw\', meaning bright or shining. The name Gloucester thus means roughly "bright fort". There are various appellations of the city\'s name in history, such as Caer Glow, Gleawecastre, Gleucestre as an early <a href="/wiki/Brython" class="mw-redirect" title="Brython">British</a> settlement is not confirmed by direct evidence. However, Gloucester was the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> municipality of Colonia Nervia Glevensium, or <a href="/wiki/Glevum" title="Glevum">Glevum</a>, built in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Nerva" title="Nerva">Nerva</a>. Parts of the walls can be traced, and a number of remains and coins have been found, though inscriptions are scarce. In <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">Historia Brittonum</a></i>, a fabled account of the early rulers of Britain, <a href="/wiki/Vortigern" title="Vortigern">Vortigern</a>\'s grandfather, Gloiu (Gloyw Wallt Hir in Welsh, meaning "Gloiu Long-hair"), is given as the founder of Gloucester.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Part of the foundations of Roman Gloucester can be seen today in Eastgate Street (near Boots), while Roman tombstones and a range of other Roman artefacts can be seen in Gloucester City Museum.\n</p><p>After the withdrawal on the Roman Empire in the late 4th Century the town returned to the control of Celtic Dubonni tribe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> By the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>, Gloucester is shown as part of <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Deorham" title="Battle of Deorham">Battle of Deorham</a> in 577 until 584, when it came under the control of <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>. The name Gloucester derives from the Anglo-Saxon for fort (<a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>ceaster</i>) preceded by Celtic name, which derived from the Roman stem Glev- (pronounced glaiw).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><i>Claudia Castra</i> is mentioned in the 18th Century as possible Latin name related to the city.<sup id="cite_ref-Claudia_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Claudia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gloucester was captured by the <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> in 577. Its situation on a navigable river, and the foundation in 681 of the abbey of St Peter by <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_of_Mercia" title="\xc3\x86thelred of Mercia">\xc3\x86thelred</a>, favoured the growth of the town; and before the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a> of England, Gloucester was a borough governed by a <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">portreeve</a>, with a castle which was frequently a royal residence, and a mint. In the early 10th century the remains of <a href="/wiki/Oswald_of_Northumbria" title="Oswald of Northumbria">Saint Oswald</a> were brought to a small church in Gloucester, bringing many pilgrims to the town. The core street layout is thought to date back to the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ethelfleda" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethelfleda">Ethelfleda</a> in late Saxon times.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg/220px-Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg" width="220" height="289" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg/330px-Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg/440px-Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg 2x" data-file-width="535" data-file-height="703" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Gloucester_in_1805_engraved_by_J.Roper_from_a_drawing_by_G.Cole.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gloucester in 1805</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1051 <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> held court at Gloucester and was threatened there by an army led by <a href="/wiki/Godwin,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Godwin, Earl of Wessex">Godwin, Earl of Wessex</a>, but the incident resulted in a standoff rather than a battle. A unique coin, dated to 1077\xe2\x80\x9380, was discovered, just north of the city, in November 2011. It features the name of the moneyer Silacwine and its place of minting. The <a href="/wiki/Portable_Antiquities_Scheme" title="Portable Antiquities Scheme">Portable Antiquities Scheme</a> said that, until the coin was discovered, there had been no known examples of <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William I</a> coins minted in Gloucester in this period.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the Norman Conquest, <a href="/wiki/William_Rufus" class="mw-redirect" title="William Rufus">William Rufus</a> made <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fitzhamon" title="Robert Fitzhamon">Robert Fitzhamon</a> the first baron or overlord of Gloucester. Fitzhamon had a military base at <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Castle" title="Cardiff Castle">Cardiff Castle</a>, and for the succeeding years the history of Gloucester was closely linked to that of Cardiff. During <a href="/wiki/The_Anarchy" title="The Anarchy">the Anarchy</a>, Gloucester was a centre of support for the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Matilda" title="Empress Matilda">Empress Matilda</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> who was supported in her claim to the throne by her half-brother, Fitzhamon\'s grandson, <a href="/wiki/Robert,_1st_Earl_of_Gloucester" title="Robert, 1st Earl of Gloucester">Robert, 1st Earl of Gloucester</a> (also known as Robert of Gloucester). After this period of strife ended with the marriage of Matilda to <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I of England</a>, Henry granted Robert possession of <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Castle" title="Cardiff Castle">Cardiff Castle</a>, and it later passed to Mathilda\'s son <a href="/wiki/Robert_Curthose" title="Robert Curthose">Robert Curthose</a> and his son, <a href="/wiki/William_Fitz_Robert,_2nd_Earl_of_Gloucester" title="William Fitz Robert, 2nd Earl of Gloucester">William Fitz Robert, 2nd Earl of Gloucester</a>. The story of the Anarchy is vividly told in a series of nineteenth-century paintings by <a href="/wiki/William_Burges" title="William Burges">William Burges</a> at the Castle.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">King Henry II</a> granted Gloucester its first charter in 1155, which gave the burgesses the same liberties as the citizens of London and <a href="/wiki/Winchester" title="Winchester">Winchester</a>. A a second charter of Henry II gave them freedom of passage on the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a>. The first charter was confirmed in 1194 by <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">King Richard I</a>. The privileges of the borough were greatly extended by the charter of <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">King John</a> (1200), which gave freedom from toll throughout the kingdom and from pleading outside the borough.\n</p><p>In 1216 <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">King Henry III</a>, aged only ten years, was crowned with a gilded iron ring in the Chapter House of Gloucester Cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gloucester\'s significance in the Middle Ages is underlined by the fact that it had a number of monastic establishments, including St Peter\'s Abbey founded in 679 (later <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Cathedral" title="Gloucester Cathedral">Gloucester Cathedral</a>), the nearby <a href="/wiki/St_Oswald%27s_Priory,_Gloucester" title="St Oswald&#39;s Priory, Gloucester">St Oswald\'s Priory, Gloucester</a> founded in the 880s or 890s, Llanthony Secunda Priory, founded 1136 as a retreat for a community of Welsh monks (now near the western bypass),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> the Franciscan Greyfriars community founded in 1231 (near <a href="/wiki/Eastgate_Shopping_Centre,_Gloucester" title="Eastgate Shopping Centre, Gloucester">Eastgate Shopping Centre</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and the Dominican Blackfriars community founded in 1239 (Ladybellegate Street).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It also has some very early churches including <a href="/wiki/St_Mary_de_Lode_Church,_Gloucester" title="St Mary de Lode Church, Gloucester">St Mary de Lode Church, Gloucester</a> near the Cathedral and the Norman <a href="/wiki/St_Mary_de_Crypt_Church,_Gloucester" title="St Mary de Crypt Church, Gloucester">St Mary de Crypt Church, Gloucester</a> in Southgate Street. Additionally, there is evidence of a <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a> community Gloucester as early as 1158-1159, who lived around present day East Gate Street and had a synagogue on the north side.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> the main export was wool which came from the <a href="/wiki/Cotswolds" title="Cotswolds">Cotswolds</a> and was processed in Gloucester; other exports included <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> (tools and weapons). Gloucester also had a large fishing industry at that time. In 1223 thatched roofs were banned<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> after a massive fire that destroyed a part of Gloucester.\n</p><p>One of the most significant periods in Gloucester\'s history began in 1378 when <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II of England</a> convened Parliament in the city. Parliaments were held there until 1406 under <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">Henry IV of England</a>. The Parliament Rooms at the Cathedral remain testimony to this important time.\n</p><p>Gloucester was incorporated by <a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">King Richard III</a> in 1483, the town being made a <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">county</a> in itself. This charter was confirmed in 1489 and 1510, and other charters of incorporation were received by Gloucester from <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth I</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">King James I</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Gloucester was the site of the execution by burning of <a href="/wiki/John_Hooper_(bishop)" title="John Hooper (bishop)">John Hooper</a>, Bishop of Gloucester in the time of <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Queen Mary</a> in 1555. In 1580, Gloucester was awarded the status of a <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a> by Queen Elizabeth I.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Gloucester" title="Siege of Gloucester">Siege of Gloucester</a> in 1643 was a battle of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> in which the besieged parliamentarians emerged victorious.\n</p><p>The sixteenth and seventeenth centuries saw the foundation of two of Gloucester\'s grammar schools: <a href="/wiki/The_Crypt_School" title="The Crypt School">the Crypt School</a> in 1539 and <a href="/wiki/Sir_Thomas_Rich%27s_School" title="Sir Thomas Rich&#39;s School">Sir Thomas Rich\'s School</a> in 1666. Both still flourish as grammar schools today, along with <a href="/wiki/Ribston_Hall" title="Ribston Hall">Ribston Hall</a> and the <a href="/wiki/High_School_for_Girls,_Gloucester" title="High School for Girls, Gloucester">High School for Girls, Gloucester</a> (Denmark Road).\n</p><p>Gloucester\'s most important citizens include <a href="/wiki/Robert_Raikes" title="Robert Raikes">Robert Raikes</a> (founder of the Sunday School movement) who is still commemorated by the name of a pub in Southgate Street. Its most infamous citizen was <a href="/wiki/Fred_West" title="Fred West">Fred West</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/2007_United_Kingdom_Floods" class="mw-redirect" title="2007 United Kingdom Floods">July 2007</a>, Gloucester was hit badly by a <a href="/wiki/Summer_2007_United_Kingdom_floods#Gloucestershire" class="mw-redirect" title="Summer 2007 United Kingdom floods">flood</a> that struck Gloucestershire and its surrounding areas. Hundreds of homes were flooded, but the event was most memorable because of its wider impact&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 about 40,000 people were without power for 24 hours, and the entire city (plus surrounding areas) was without piped water for 17 days.\n</p><p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Day" title="Gloucester Day">Gloucester Day</a> was revived as an annual day of celebration of Gloucester\'s history and culture. The day originally dates from the lifting of the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Gloucester" title="Siege of Gloucester">Siege of Gloucester</a> in 1643, during which the city held out against Royalist forces during the <a href="/wiki/First_English_Civil_War" title="First English Civil War">First English Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc1-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Bishop of Worcester; right: the arms of the city of Gloucester</div></div></div>\n<p>Gloucester is one of few cities in England with the distinction of having two coats of arms. The first consists of three chevrons surrounded by ten roundels. The chevrons come from the arms of the <a href="/wiki/De_Clare" title="De Clare">Clare family</a>, who were earls of Gloucester from the 12th to the 14th centuries, while the roundels come from the arms of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishop of Worcester</a>, whose bishopric historically encompassed Gloucester. This coat is the older of the two, though it is usually termed the "Commonwealth coat", as it was not officially granted to the city until 1652, during the Commonwealth period. The crest and supporters (lions bearing broadswords and trowels) were also adopted at this time, along with the motto <i>Fides Invicta Triumphat</i> ("unconquered faith triumphs", in reference to the royalist siege withstood by the city in 1643).\n</p><p>The second coat, termed the "Tudor coat", was granted in 1538. It features the roses of York and Lancaster, the boar\'s head of Richard III, a ceremonial sword and cap, and two horseshoes surrounded by nails, to represent Gloucester\'s historical association with ironworking.\n</p><p>Though grants made by Commonwealth heralds were nullified after the restoration, the Commonwealth coat continued to be used by the city rather than the Tudor coat. The Commonwealth coat, along with the crest and supporters, was legally granted to the city by letters patent dated 16 April 1945. As of 24 April 2017, Quedgeley is now classed as a town.<sup id="cite_ref-Quedgeley_Town_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quedgeley_Town-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gloucester_Docks_at_Night.jpeg/220px-Gloucester_Docks_at_Night.jpeg" width="220" height="117" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gloucester_Docks_at_Night.jpeg/440px-Gloucester_Docks_at_Night.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="4208" data-file-height="2243" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gloucester_Docks_at_Night.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gloucester Docks at night</div></div></div>\n<p>Gloucester is the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, and is the 53rd largest settlement in the United Kingdom by population.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In 2002, its population was 110,600.<sup id="cite_ref-Center_for_Cities_2014_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Center_for_Cities_2014-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The 2011 census recorded that the city had a population of 121,921 and by 2016 its population was estimated to be 128,488.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The city\'s urban area extends beyond its boundaries, with <a href="/wiki/Category:Areas_of_Gloucester" title="Category:Areas of Gloucester">several outlying districts</a>. The 2011 census gave the population of the Gloucester Urban Area as 150,053, absorbing areas such as <a href="/wiki/Brockworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Brockworth">Brockworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Churchdown" title="Churchdown">Churchdown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The city is located on the eastern bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a>, sheltered by the <a href="/wiki/Cotswolds" title="Cotswolds">Cotswolds</a> to the east, while the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Malvern_Hills_AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="Malvern Hills AONB">Malvern Hills</a> rise to the west and north, respectively. Gloucester is a port, linked via the <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_and_Sharpness_Canal" title="Gloucester and Sharpness Canal">Gloucester and Sharpness Canal</a> which runs from Gloucester\'s docks to the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Estuary" title="Severn Estuary">Severn Estuary</a>, allowing larger ships to reach the docks than would be possible on the tidal reaches of the river itself, which go well north of the city to Haw Bridge. The wharfs, warehouses and the docks themselves fell into disrepair until their renovation in the 1980s. They now form a public open space. Some warehouses now house the <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Waterways_Museum" title="Gloucester Waterways Museum">Gloucester Waterways Museum</a>, others were converted into residential flats, shops and bars. Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Soldiers_of_Gloucestershire_Museum" title="Soldiers of Gloucestershire Museum">Soldiers of Gloucestershire Museum</a> is located in the Custom House. Next to the museum is Gloucester Yacht Club. The port still houses the most inland <a href="/wiki/RNLI" class="mw-redirect" title="RNLI">RNLI</a> <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboat</a> in the United Kingdom.\n</p><p>Gloucester is made up of a variety of neighbourhoods, some of which correspond to electoral divisions of the City Council.\n</p>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" Because of this it has its own town council.<sup id="cite_ref-Quedgeley_Town_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quedgeley_Town-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_belt">Green belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Green belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_and_Cheltenham_Green_Belt" title="Gloucester and Cheltenham Green Belt">Gloucester and Cheltenham Green Belt</a></div>\n<p>The city itself contains no green belt; however it is bordered to the north east by the green belt in the surrounding Tewkesbury district, helping to maintain local green space, prevent further urban sprawl and unplanned expansion towards <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Innsworth" title="Innsworth">Innsworth</a>, as well as protecting smaller nearby villages such as <a href="/wiki/Churchdown" title="Churchdown">Churchdown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badgeworth" title="Badgeworth">Badgeworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shurdington" title="Shurdington">Shurdington</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Twigworth" title="Twigworth">Twigworth</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gloucester_Cathedral_exterior_front.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gloucester_Cathedral_exterior_front.jpg/220px-Gloucester_Cathedral_exterior_front.jpg" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gloucester_Cathedral_exterior_front.jpg/330px-Gloucester_Cathedral_exterior_front.jpg 1.5x, <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gloucester_Cathedral_exterior_front.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Cathedral" title="Gloucester Cathedral">Gloucester Cathedral</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Cathedral" title="Gloucester Cathedral">Gloucester Cathedral</a>, in the north of the city near the river, originates in the foundation of an abbey dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> in 681. It is the burial place of <a href="/wiki/Edward_II_of_England" title="Edward II of England">King Edward II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_de_Lacy_(died_1085)" title="Walter de Lacy (died 1085)">Walter de Lacy</a>. The cathedral (mainly its cloisters) was used for corridor scenes in the films <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher&#39;s Stone (film)">Harry Potter and the Philosopher\'s Stone</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Chamber_of_Secrets_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets (film)">Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Half-Blood_Prince_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Half-Blood Prince (film)">Harry Potter and the Half Blood Prince</a></i>. The crypt was used for a scene in <i>Sherlock Christmas special</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Attached to the deanery is the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> prior\'s chapel. In St Mary\'s Square outside the Abbey gate, the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Gloucester" title="Bishop of Gloucester">Bishop of Gloucester</a>, Bishop <a href="/wiki/John_Hooper_(bishop)" title="John Hooper (bishop)">John Hooper</a>, was martyred under <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Queen Mary I</a> in 1555.\n</p><p>A good number of <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor period</a> gabled and <a href="/wiki/Half_timbered" class="mw-redirect" title="Half timbered">half timbered</a> houses survive from earlier periods of Gloucester\'s history. At the point where the four principal streets intersected stood the Tolsey (town hall), which was replaced by a modern building in 1894. None of the old public buildings are left except for the <a href="/wiki/The_New_Inn,_Gloucester" title="The New Inn, Gloucester">New Inn</a> in Northgate Street. It is a timbered house, with strong, massive external galleries and courtyards. It was built around 1450 by John Twyning, a monk.<sup id="cite_ref-eh_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eh-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:King%27s_Square,_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/King%27s_Square%2C_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg/200px-King%27s_Square%2C_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg" width="200" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/King%27s_Square%2C_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg/300px-King%27s_Square%2C_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/King%27s_Square%2C_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg/400px-King%27s_Square%2C_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King%27s_Square,_Gloucester_-_geograph.org.uk_-_469564.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kings Square (1976)</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Kings_Square,_Gloucester" title="Kings Square, Gloucester">Kings Square</a> is at the heart of the city centre and occupies what was once a cattle market and bus station. Officially opened in 1972, it was the centrepiece of a radical redesign of the city, The Jellicoe Plan, which was first proposed in 1961. It stands beside the Debenham\'s (formerly Bon March\xc3\xa9) store built in the early 1960s. Many of the features of the redevelopment have since been dismantled; the <a href="/wiki/Brutalist_architecture" title="Brutalist architecture">brutalist</a> concrete fountains in the middle of the square have gone and the overhead roadways which linked three multi storey car parks around the centre have been either closed or dismantled. The main bus station received a Civic Trust Award in 1963 but has since been demolished, with a new bus station being constructed on the same site during 2018. In 2012 a \xc2\xa360 million plan was unveiled to revamp the square.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In 2014 the prominent Golden Egg restaurant was demolished and a new look public space was created. A prior archaeological dig revealed a Roman house underneath.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An indoor market opened in Eastgate Street in 1968, followed by the Eastgate Shopping Centre in 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kings_Walk_Shopping_Centre,_Gloucester" title="Kings Walk Shopping Centre, Gloucester">Kings Walk Shopping Centre</a> was built between 1969 and 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The corner of Eastgate Street and Brunswick Road was redeveloped around this time; Roman remains unearthed below street level in 1974 may be seen through a glass observation panel outside the Boots building, which opened in 1980. The <a href="/wiki/HSBC" title="HSBC">HSBC</a> building on the Cross was renovated and a modern extension added to the Westgate Street aspect in 1972 which received a Civic Trust Award. <a href="/wiki/Sainsbury%27s" title="Sainsbury&#39;s">Sainsbury\'s</a> opened a supermarket in Northgate Street in 1970; it retains its original interior. Opposite, <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a> opened a large two-storey supermarket in June 1976 on the site of a demolished chapel. This is now occupied by <a href="/wiki/Wilko_(retailer)" title="Wilko (retailer)">Wilkinson\'s</a> after Tesco moved to <a href="/wiki/Quedgeley" title="Quedgeley">Quedgeley</a> in 1984. Asda opened its first store in Gloucester in Bruton Way in 1983.\n</p><p>Gloucester Leisure Centre opened on the corner of Eastgate Street and Bruton Way in September 1974 and was redeveloped and rebranded (as "GL1") in August 2002. <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Central_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Gloucester Central railway station">Gloucester Central railway station</a> was rebuilt in 1977 to serve both the original traffic to that railway station and the services from the closed <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Eastgate_railway_station" title="Gloucester Eastgate railway station">Gloucester Eastgate railway station</a> (former <a href="/wiki/Midland_Railway" title="Midland Railway">Midland Railway</a>) which had stood on another site further east along the same road. Opposite the station stands one of the city\'s largest office blocks, Twyver House, opened in 1968, which houses the regional Land Registry. The main shopping streets were pedestrianised in the late 1980s.\n</p><p>The 1966 <i>Heights Plan for Gloucester</i> sought to restrict construction of tall buildings and defend spiritual values by protecting views of <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Cathedral" title="Gloucester Cathedral">Gloucester Cathedral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The tower of <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_Royal_Hospital" title="Gloucestershire Royal Hospital">Gloucestershire Royal Hospital</a>, started in 1970 and completed in August 1975, can be seen from miles around. In Brunswick Road, a brown concrete tower, which housed classrooms at the <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_College" title="Gloucestershire College">Gloucestershire College of Arts and Technology</a> (now moved to a site near Llanthony Bridge). The tower was added incongruously to the existing 1930s Technical College buildings in 1971 which has now been demolished. Clapham Court, a tall block of flats, stands in Columbia Close, between London Road and Kingsholm Road. It was built in 1963 and stands on what was once Columbia Street in a small district formerly known as Clapham.\n</p><p>Other features of interest include the museum and school of art and science, the former <a href="/wiki/HM_Prison_Gloucester" title="HM Prison Gloucester">county jail</a> (on the site of a <a href="/wiki/Saxon_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon people">Saxon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> castle), the Shire Hall (now headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_County_Council" title="Gloucestershire County Council">County Council</a>) and the Whitefield memorial church. A park in the south of the city contains a spa, a <a href="/wiki/Chalybeate" title="Chalybeate">chalybeate</a> spring having been discovered in 1814. West of this, across the canal, are the remains (a gateway and some walls) of <a href="/wiki/Llanthony_Secunda" title="Llanthony Secunda">Llanthony Secunda Priory</a>, a cell of the <a href="/wiki/Llanthony_Priory" title="Llanthony Priory">mother abbey</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Ewyas" title="Vale of Ewyas">Vale of Ewyas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire" title="Monmouthshire">Monmouthshire</a>, which in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV_of_England" title="Edward IV of England">King Edward IV</a> became the secondary establishment.\n</p>\n<h2><span This is followed by a parade of serving forces, veterans and cadets through the city centre to the docks for a family day with military and military-related charity displays and entertainment in Back Badge Square in front of the Soldiers of Gloucestershire Museum.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nature_in_Art" title="Nature in Art">Nature in Art</a> is a gallery dedicated to the display of works of art inspired by the natural world.\n</p><p>A popular and well known rhyme about the city: <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Foster" title="Doctor Foster">Doctor Foster</a> went to Gloucester in a shower of rain, he stood in a puddle right up to his middle and never went there again.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloucester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_churches_in_Gloucestershire" title="List of churches in Gloucestershire">List of churches in Gloucestershire</a></div>\n<p>Gloucester has many churches, and historically has also had many dissenting chapels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Gloucester was the host of the first <a href="/wiki/Sunday_school" title="Sunday school">Sunday school</a> in England; this was founded by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Raikes" title="Robert Raikes">Robert Raikes</a> in 1780. It was built on the site of an ancient <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> temple which became the first Christian church in Britain</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/St_Mary_de_Crypt_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="St Mary de Crypt Church">St Mary de Crypt</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 with a cruciform structure of the 12th century. It has later additions, such as the tower. Also the site of the Schoolroom in which the <a href="/wiki/The_Crypt_School,_Gloucester" class="mw-redirect" title="The Crypt School, Gloucester">Crypt School</a> was formed</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Tower,_Gloucester" title="St Michael&#39;s Tower, Gloucester">St Michael\'s Church</a> \xe2\x80\x93 said to have been connected with St Peter\'s ancient abbey</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/St_Nicholas%27_Church,_Gloucester" title="St Nicholas&#39; Church, Gloucester">St Nicholas\'s Church</a> \xe2\x80\x93 founded by the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> but with many additions since then.</li></ul>\n<p>In the neighbourhood around St Mary de Crypt there are slight remains of Greyfriars and <a href="/wiki/Blackfriars,_Gloucester" title="Blackfriars, Gloucester">Blackfriars</a> monasteries, and also of the city wall. Under the Golden Fleece (The Monks Bar) and Saracen\'s Head inns early vaulted cellars still remains. In addition, in the city is <a href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_Church,_Gloucester" title="St Peter&#39;s Church, Gloucester">St Peter\'s Roman Catholic Church</a>, a Grade II* listed building.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the construction of the Boots store on the corner of Brunswick Road and Eastgate Street in 1974, Roman remains were found. These can be seen through a glass case on the street. At the back of the Gloucester Furniture Exhibition Centre part of the city\'s South Gate can be seen.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span Junction 12 serves south Gloucester and Quedgeley. Junction 11a serves central Gloucester and junction 11 serves north Gloucester. The <a href="/wiki/A38_road" title="A38 road">A38</a> runs north\xe2\x80\x93south through Gloucester connecting the city with <a href="/wiki/Tewkesbury" title="Tewkesbury">Tewkesbury</a> and Bristol. The <a href="/wiki/A40_road" title="A40 road">A40</a> runs west to east, connecting Gloucester with <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a> to the east (via a dual carriageway section known as <i>The Golden Valley Bypass</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a> to the West. The Severn is split into two branches at this point, so the road crosses first onto <a href="/wiki/Alney_Island" title="Alney Island">Alney Island</a> and then onto the western bank. A <a href="/wiki/Over_Bridge" title="Over Bridge">road bridge</a> on this western side at Over, built by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a> in 1829, still stands, notable for its very flat arch construction, but its fragility and narrow width means it is no longer used for traffic, and since 1974 it has been paralleled by a modern road bridge. There is a rail crossing, also across Alney Island, which was the lowest on the river until the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Railway_Bridge" title="Severn Railway Bridge">Severn Railway Bridge</a> in 1879, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Tunnel" title="Severn Tunnel">Severn Tunnel</a> in 1886, although following the dismantling of the former in 1970 Gloucester once again has the furthest downstream rail bridge crossing of the Severn.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gloucester_railway_station" title="Gloucester railway station">Gloucester railway station</a> has frequent trains to <a href="/wiki/London_Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="London Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reading_railway_station" title="Reading railway station">Reading</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Temple_Meads_railway_station" title="Bristol Temple Meads railway station">Bristol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Central_railway_station" title="Cardiff Central railway station">Cardiff Central</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Nottingham railway station">Nottingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_New_Street_railway_station" title="Birmingham New Street railway station">Birmingham</a>. Buses are run by <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach_West" title="Stagecoach West">Stagecoach West</a>, operating from its depot on London Road. For longer, slower journeys, <a href="/wiki/National_Express_Coaches" title="National Express Coaches">National Express</a> operate a number of services including the 444 to London and the 222 to Heathrow and Gatwick airports. Hunts Grove railway station is proposed to open on the southern edge of the city near <a href="/wiki/Quedgeley" title="Quedgeley">Quedgeley</a> as part of metrowest plans to extend Bristol commuter services to the city.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gloucester is linked to the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Estuary" title="Severn Estuary">Severn Estuary</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_and_Sharpness_Canal" title="Gloucester and Sharpness Canal">Gloucester and Sharpness Canal</a>, which is navigable by small <a href="/wiki/Coastal_trading_vessel" title="Coastal trading vessel">coasters</a>. The city is linked to the <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Warwickshire" title="River Avon, Warwickshire">River Avon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stourport-on-Severn" title="Stourport-on-Severn">Stourport-on-Severn</a> by the navigable part of the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a>, which is navigable by river craft of a few hundred tonnes\' <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">displacement</a>. Gloucester Docks mark the Normal Tidal Limit (NTL) of the river.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Gloucester was formerly linked to <a href="/wiki/Ledbury" title="Ledbury">Ledbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hereford" title="Hereford">Hereford</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire_and_Gloucestershire_Canal" title="Herefordshire and Gloucestershire Canal">Herefordshire and Gloucestershire Canal</a>; and subsequently by the <a href="/wiki/Ledbury_and_Gloucester_Railway" title="Ledbury and Gloucester Railway">Ledbury and Gloucester Railway</a>, which used the southern section of the former canal, until it also closed in 1964. This canal is now being restored, and the restored canal basin in the Gloucester suburb of Over is a local attraction.\n</p><p>The nearest commercial airport with scheduled services is <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Airport" title="Bristol Airport">Bristol Airport</a>, around 50 miles to the south. <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_Airport" title="Gloucestershire Airport">Gloucestershire Airport</a>, located some 8 miles to the east, currently offers no scheduled flights.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport,_Gloucestershire,_England_24May2017_arp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport%2C_Gloucestershire%2C_England_24May2017_arp.jpg/220px-Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport%2C_Gloucestershire%2C_England_24May2017_arp.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport%2C_Gloucestershire%2C_England_24May2017_arp.jpg/330px-Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport%2C_Gloucestershire%2C_England_24May2017_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport%2C_Gloucestershire%2C_England_24May2017_arp.jpg/440px-Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport%2C_Gloucestershire%2C_England_24May2017_arp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2018" data-file-height="1419" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aerial_of_Gloucestershire_Airport,_Gloucestershire,_England_24May2017_arp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_Airport" title="Gloucestershire Airport">Gloucestershire Airport</a> in 2017, looking east. In 1926 the Gloucestershire Aircraft Company at <a href="/wiki/Brockworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Brockworth">Brockworth</a> changed its name to the <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Aircraft_Company" title="Gloster Aircraft Company">Gloster Aircraft Company</a> because international customers claimed that the name "Gloucestershire" was too difficult to spell. A sculpture in the city centre celebrates Gloucester\'s aviation history and its involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Whittle" title="Frank Whittle">Frank Whittle</a>\'s pioneering <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Aircraft_Company#1941_\xe2\x80\x93_turbojet" title="Gloster Aircraft Company">turbojet engine</a> powered the <a href="/wiki/Gloster_E28/39" class="mw-redirect" title="Gloster E28/39">first British jet aircraft</a>, which first flew at the company\'s airfield at <a href="/wiki/Brockworth,_Gloucestershire#Gloster_Aircraft_Company" title="Brockworth, Gloucestershire">Brockworth</a>. This is commemorated by the pub "The Whittle" at Gloucester Business Park, which now occupies the site. Roads in the business park are named after other Gloster aircraft and a small statue overlooks the site of the old main runway. <a href="/wiki/Messier-Dowty" class="mw-redirect" title="Messier-Dowty">Messier-Dowty</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a> plant and <a href="/wiki/GE_Aviation" title="GE Aviation">GE Aviation</a> <a href="/wiki/Dowty_Rotol" title="Dowty Rotol">Dowty Propellers</a> plants are on the outskirts of the city.\n</p><p>The large insurer <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_Insurance" title="Ecclesiastical Insurance">Ecclesiastical Insurance</a> is based in the city, as is its owner, the charity <a href="/wiki/Allchurches_Trust" title="Allchurches Trust">Allchurches Trust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lloyds_Banking_Group" title="Lloyds Banking Group">Lloyds Banking Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/TSB_Bank_(United_Kingdom)" title="TSB Bank (United Kingdom)">TSB Bank</a> each have an office in <a href="/wiki/Barnwood" title="Barnwood">Barnwood</a>, the former previously having been the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_%26_Gloucester" title="Cheltenham &amp;
