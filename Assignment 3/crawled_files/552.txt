(2) <a href="/wiki/Distribution_of_income" class="mw-redirect" title="Distribution of income">distribution of income</a>, and (3) <a href="/wiki/Economic_stability" title="Economic links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The proper role of government provides a starting point for the analysis of public finance. In theory, under certain circumstances, <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">private markets</a> will allocate goods and services among individuals efficiently (in the sense that no waste occurs and that individual tastes are matching with the economy\'s productive abilities). If private markets were able to provide efficient outcomes and if the distribution of income were socially acceptable, then there would be little or no scope for government. In many cases, however, conditions for private market efficiency are violated. For example, if many people can enjoy the same good at the same time (non-rival, non-excludable consumption), then private markets may supply too little of that good. National defense is one example of non-rival consumption, or of a <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public good</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Market_failure" title="Market failure">Market failure</a>" occurs when private markets do not allocate goods or services efficiently. The existence of market failure provides an efficiency-based rationale for collective or governmental provision of goods and services.<sup id="cite_ref-HewettR_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HewettR-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Externalities" class="mw-redirect" title="Externalities">Externalities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public goods</a>, informational advantages, strong economies of scale, and network effects can cause market failures. Public provision via a government or a voluntary association, however, is subject to other inefficiencies, termed "<a href="/wiki/Government_failure" title="Government failure">government failure</a>."\n</p><p>Under broad assumptions, government decisions about the efficient scope and level of activities can be efficiently separated from decisions about the design of taxation systems (Diamond-Mirrlees separation). In this view, <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a> programs should be designed to maximize social benefits minus costs (<a href="/wiki/Cost-benefit" class="mw-redirect" title="Cost-benefit">cost-benefit</a> analysis), and then revenues needed to pay for those expenditures should be raised through a <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxation</a> system that creates the fewest efficiency losses caused by <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(economics)" title="Distortion (economics)">distortion</a> of economic activity as possible. In practice, government <a href="/wiki/Budgeting" class="mw-redirect" title="Budgeting">budgeting</a> or <a href="/wiki/Public_budgeting" title="Public budgeting">public budgeting</a> is substantially more complicated and often results in inefficient practices.\n</p><p>Government can pay for spending by borrowing (for example, with <a href="/wiki/Government_bond" title="Government bond">government bonds</a>), although borrowing is a method of distributing tax burdens through time rather than a replacement for taxes. A <a href="/wiki/Government_budget_deficit" class="mw-redirect" title="Government budget deficit">deficit</a> is the difference between government spending and revenues.  The accumulation of deficits over time is the total public <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>. Deficit finance allows governments to smooth tax burdens over time, and gives governments an important <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_policy" title="Fiscal policy">fiscal policy</a> tool. Deficits can also narrow the options of successor governments.\n</p><p>Public finance is closely connected to issues of <a href="/wiki/Income_distribution" title="Income distribution">income distribution</a> and social equity. Governments can reallocate income through <a href="/wiki/Transfer_payments" class="mw-redirect" title="Transfer payments">transfer payments</a> or by designing tax systems that treat high-income and low-income households differently.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Public_choice" title="Public choice">public choice</a> approach to public finance seeks to explain how self-interested voters, politicians, and bureaucrats actually operate, rather than how they should operate.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_finance_management">Public finance management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Public finance management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Collection of sufficient resources from the economy in an appropriate manner along with allocating and use of these resources efficiently and effectively constitute good financial management. Resource generation, resource allocation and expenditure management (resource utilization) are the essential components of a <b>public financial management</b> system.\n</p><p>The following subdivisions form the subject matter of public finance.\n</p>\n<ol><li>Public expenditure</li>\n<li>Public revenue</li>\n<li>Public debt</li>\n<li>Financial administration</li>\n<li>Federal finance</li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_expenditures">Government expenditures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Government expenditures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_spending" title="Government spending">Government spending</a></div>\n<p>Economists classify government expenditures into three main types. Government purchases of goods and services for current use are classed as <a href="/wiki/National_Income_and_Product_Accounts#Accounting_for_national_product:_the_right_side_of_the_report" title="National Income and Product Accounts">government consumption</a>. Government purchases of goods and services intended to create future benefits&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 such as infrastructure investment or research spending&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 are classed as <a href="/wiki/National_Income_and_Product_Accounts#Accounting_for_national_product:_the_right_side_of_the_report" title="National Income and Product Accounts">government investment</a>. Government expenditures that are not purchases of goods and services, and instead just represent transfers of money&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 such as social security payments&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 are called <a href="/wiki/Transfer_payment" title="Transfer payment">transfer payments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_operations">Government operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Government operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_operations" title="Government operations">Government operations</a></div>\n<p>Government operations are those activities involved in the running of a <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> or a functional equivalent of a state (for example, <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secessionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Secessionist">secessionist</a> movements or <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary" title="Revolutionary">revolutionary</a> movements) for the purpose of producing <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizen">citizens</a>.  Government operations have the power to make, and the authority to enforce rules and laws within a civil, <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious">religious</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academic" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic">academic</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organization</a> or group.<sup id="cite_ref-Columbia_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Income_distribution">Income distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Income distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Income_distribution" title="Income distribution">Income distribution</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Redistribution_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Redistribution (economics)">Redistribution (economics)</a></div>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Income_distribution" title="Income distribution">Income distribution</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Some forms of government expenditure are specifically intended to <a href="/wiki/Transfer_payment" title="Transfer payment">transfer</a> income from some groups to others. For example, governments sometimes transfer income to people that have suffered a loss due to natural disaster. Likewise, public <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a> programs transfer wealth from the young to the old. Other forms of government expenditure which represent purchases of goods and services also have the effect of changing the income distribution. For example, engaging in a <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a> may transfer wealth to certain sectors of society. <a href="/wiki/Public_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Public education">Public education</a> transfers wealth to families with children in these schools. Public <a href="/wiki/Road_construction" class="mw-redirect" title="Road construction">road construction</a> transfers wealth from people that do not use the roads to those people that do (and to those that build the roads).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">Income Security</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Employment_insurance" class="mw-redirect" title="Employment insurance">Employment insurance</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Health_Care" class="mw-redirect" title="Health Care">Health Care</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Public_financing_of_campaigns" class="mw-redirect" title="Public financing of campaigns">Public financing of campaigns</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financing_of_government_expenditures">Financing of government expenditures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Financing of navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_revenue" title="Government revenue">Government revenue</a></div>\n<p>Government expenditures are financed primarily in three ways:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Government_revenue" title="Government revenue">Government revenue</a>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">Taxes</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Non-tax_revenue" to finance its activities can have important effects on the distribution of income and wealth (<a href="/wiki/Income_redistribution" class="mw-redirect" title="Income redistribution">income redistribution</a>) and on the efficiency of markets (<a href="/wiki/Effect_of_taxes_and_subsidies_on_price" title="Effect of taxes and subsidies on price">effect of taxes on market prices and efficiency</a>).  The issue of how taxes affect income distribution is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Tax_incidence" title="Tax incidence">tax incidence</a>, which examines the distribution of tax burdens after market adjustments are taken into account.  Public finance research also analyzes effects of the various types of taxes and types of borrowing as well as administrative concerns, such as tax enforcement.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxes">Taxes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Taxes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">Tax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_capacity" title="Fiscal capacity">Fiscal capacity</a></div>\n<p>Taxation is the central part of modern public finance. Its significance arises not only from the fact that it is by far the most important of all revenues but also because of the gravity of the problems created by the present day tax burden.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The main objective of taxation is raising revenue. A high level of taxation is necessary in a welfare State to fulfill its obligations. Taxation is used as an instrument of attaining certain social objectives i.e. as a means of redistribution of wealth and thereby reducing inequalities. Taxation in a modern Government is thus needed not merely to raise the revenue required to meet its ever-growing expenditure on administration and social services but also to reduce the inequalities of income and wealth. Taxation is also needed to draw away money that would otherwise go into consumption and cause inflation to rise.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A tax is a financial charge or other <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">levy</a> imposed on an individual or a <a href="/wiki/Juristic_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Juristic person">legal entity</a> by a <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> or a functional equivalent of a state (for example, <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secessionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Secessionist">secessionist</a> movements or <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary" title="Revolutionary">revolutionary</a> movements). Taxes could also be imposed by a <a href="/wiki/Subnational_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="Subnational entity">subnational entity</a>. Taxes consist of <a href="/wiki/Direct_tax" title="Direct tax">direct tax</a> or <a href="/wiki/Indirect_tax" title="Indirect tax">indirect tax</a>, and may be paid in <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> or as <i><a href="/wiki/Corv%C3%A9e" title="Corv\xc3\xa9e">corv\xc3\xa9e</a></i> labor. A tax may be defined as a "pecuniary burden laid upon individuals or property to support the government [&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.] a payment exacted by legislative authority."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  A tax "is not a voluntary payment or donation, but an enforced contribution, exacted pursuant to legislative authority" and is "any contribution imposed by government [&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.] whether under the name of toll, tribute, tallage, gabel, impost, duty, custom, excise, subsidy, aid, supply, or other name."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>There are various types of taxes, broadly divided into two heads&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 direct (which is proportional) and indirect tax (which is differential in nature):</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stamp_duty" title="Stamp duty">Stamp duty</a>, levied on documents</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Excise" title="Excise">Excise</a> tax (tax levied on production for sale, or sale, of a certain good)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Sales_tax" title="Sales tax">Sales tax</a> (tax on business transactions, especially the <a href="/wiki/Sales" title="Sales">sale</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a>)\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Value_added_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Value added tax">Value added tax</a> (VAT) is a type of sales tax</li>\n<li>Services taxes on specific services</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Road_tax" title="Road tax">Road tax</a>; <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_excise_duty" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicle excise duty">Vehicle excise duty</a> (UK), Registration Fee (USA), Regco  (Australia), Vehicle Licensing Fee (Brazil) etc.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gift_tax" title="Gift tax">Gift tax</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Duty_(economics)" title="Duty (economics)">Duties</a> (taxes on importation, levied at <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Corporate_income_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporate income tax">Corporate income tax</a> on <a href="/wiki/Corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporations">corporations</a> (<a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(business)" title="Incorporation (business)">incorporated</a> entities)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Wealth_tax" title="Wealth tax">Wealth tax</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Personal_income_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal income tax">Personal income tax</a> (may be levied on individuals, families such as the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_joint_family" title="Hindu joint family">Hindu joint family</a> in India, <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_association" title="Voluntary association">unincorporated associations</a>, etc.)</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Debt">Debt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Debt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">Government debt</a></div>\n<p>Governments, like any other legal entity, can take out <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loans</a>, issue <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> and make <a href="/wiki/Invest#Finance" class="mw-redirect" title="Invest">financial investments</a>.  Government debt (also known as public debt or national debt) is <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a>) owed by any level of <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>; either <a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">central or federal government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipal government">municipal government</a> or <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a>. Some local governments issue bonds based on their taxing authority, such as <a href="/wiki/Tax_increment_financing" title="Tax increment financing">tax increment bonds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Revenue_bond" title="Revenue bond">revenue bonds</a>.\n</p><p>As the government represents the people, government debt can be seen as an indirect debt of the <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxpayers</a>.  Government debt can be categorized as <a href="/wiki/Internal_debt" title="Internal debt">internal debt</a>, owed to lenders within the country, and <a href="/wiki/External_debt" title="External debt">external debt</a>, owed to foreign lenders. Governments usually borrow by issuing <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Government_bond" title="Government bond">government bonds</a> and bills. Less creditworthy countries sometimes borrow directly from <a href="/wiki/Commercial_bank" title="Commercial bank">commercial banks</a> or international institutions such as the International Monetary Fund or the World Bank.\n</p><p>Most government budgets are calculated on a cash basis, meaning that revenues are recognized when collected and outlays are recognized when paid.  Some consider all government liabilities, including future <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a> payments and payments for goods and services the government has contracted for but not yet paid, as government debt.  This approach is called accrual accounting, meaning that obligations are recognized when they are acquired, or accrued, rather than when they are paid. This constitutes public debt.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seigniorage">Seigniorage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Seigniorage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seigniorage" title="Seigniorage">Seigniorage</a></div>\n<p>Seigniorage is the net <a href="/wiki/Revenue" title="Revenue">revenue</a> derived from the issuing of <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a>. It arises from the difference between the <a href="/wiki/Face_value" title="Face value">face value</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bank_note" class="mw-redirect" title="Bank note">bank note</a> and the cost of producing, distributing and eventually retiring it from circulation.  Seigniorage is an important source of revenue for some <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">national banks</a>, although it provides a very small proportion of revenue for advanced industrial countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_finance_through_state_enterprise">Public finance through state enterprise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public finance through state enterprise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/State-owned_enterprise" title="State-owned enterprise">State-owned enterprise</a></div>\n<p>Public finance in centrally planned economies has differed in fundamental ways from that in market economies. Some state-owned enterprises generated profits that helped finance government activities. The government entities that operate for profit are usually manufacturing and financial institutions, services such as nationalized healthcare do not operate for a profit to keep costs low for consumers. The Soviet Union relied heavily on turnover taxes on retail sales. Sale of natural resources, and especially petroleum products, were an important source of revenue for the Soviet Union.\n</p><p>In market-oriented economies with substantial state enterprise, such as in Venezuela, the state-run oil company PSDVA provides revenue for the government to fund its operations and programs that would otherwise be profit for private owners. In various mixed economies, the revenue generated by state-run or state-owned enterprises are used for various state endeavors; typically the revenue generated by state and government agencies goes into a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_wealth_fund" title="Sovereign wealth fund">sovereign wealth fund</a>. An example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Permanent_Fund" title="Alaska Permanent Fund">Alaska Permanent Fund</a> and Singapore\'s <a href="/wiki/Temasek_Holdings" title="Temasek Holdings">Temasek Holdings</a>.\n</p><p>Various <a href="/wiki/Market_socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Market socialist">market socialist</a> systems or proposals utilize revenue generated by state-run enterprises to fund social dividends, eliminating the need for taxation altogether.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_finance_statistics_and_methodology">Government finance statistics and methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Government finance statistics and methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Macroeconomic data to support public finance economics are generally referred to as fiscal or government finance statistics (GFS). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/gfs/manual/index.htm"><i>The Government Finance Statistics Manual 2001 (GFSM 2001)</i></a> is the internationally accepted methodology for compiling fiscal data.  It is consistent with regionally accepted methodologies such as the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20100529171142/http://circa.europa.eu/irc/dsis/nfaccount/info/data/esa95/esa95-new.htm"><i>European System of Accounts 1995</i></a> and consistent with the methodology of the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090430043521/http://unstats.un.org/unsd/sna1993/toctop.asp?L1=4"><i>System of National Accounts (SNA1993)</i></a> and broadly in line with its most recent update, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://unstats.un.org/unsd/nationalaccount/sna2008.asp"><i>SNA2008</i></a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_the_public_sector">Measuring the public sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Measuring the public sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The size of governments, their institutional composition and complexity, their ability to carry out large and sophisticated operations, and their impact on the other sectors of the economy warrant a well-articulated system to measure government economic operations.\n</p><p>The <i>GFSM 2001</i> addresses the institutional complexity of government by defining various levels of government. The main focus of the <i>GFSM 2001</i> is the general government sector defined as the group of entities capable of implementing public policy through the provision of primarily non market  goods and services and the <a href="/wiki/Redistribution_of_income_and_wealth" title="Redistribution of income and wealth">redistribution of income and wealth</a>, with both activities supported mainly by compulsory levies on other sectors. The <i>GFSM 2001</i> disaggregates the general government into subsectors: central government, state government, and local government (See Figure 1). The concept of general government does not include <a href="/wiki/Government-owned_corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Government-owned corporation">public corporations</a>. The general government plus the public corporations comprise the public sector (See Figure 2).\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Government.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Economic Structure of the General Government." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/General_Government.jpg/440px-General_Government.jpg" width="440" height="304" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/General_Government.jpg/660px-General_Government.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/General_Government.jpg 2x" data-file-width="802" data-file-height="555" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Government.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 1: General Government</b> (IMF <i>Government Finance Statistics Manual 2001</i>(Washington, 2001) pp.13</div></div></div></div>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Public_Sector.png" class="image"><img alt="Economic Structure of the Public Sector." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Public_Sector.png/440px-Public_Sector.png" width="440" height="328" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Public_Sector.png/660px-Public_Sector.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/73/Public_Sector.png 2x" data-file-width="674" data-file-height="502" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Public_Sector.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 2: Public Sector</b>(IMF <i>Government Finance Statistics Manual 2001</i>(Washington, 2001) pp.15</div></div></div></div>\n<p>The general government sector of a nation includes all non-private sector institutions, organisations and activities. The general government sector, by convention, includes all the public corporations that are not able to cover at least 50% of their costs by sales, and, therefore, are considered non-market producers.<sup id="cite_ref-gen_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the European System of Accounts,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> the sector \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral government\xe2\x80\x9d has been defined as containing:\n</p>\n<ul><li>\xe2\x80\x9cAll institutional units which are other non-market producers whose output is intended for individual and collective consumption, and mainly financed by compulsory payments made by units belonging to other sectors, and/or all institutional units principally engaged in the redistribution of national income and wealth\xe2\x80\x9d.<sup id="cite_ref-gen_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Therefore, the main functions of general government units are&#160;:\n</p>\n<ul><li>to organise or redirect the flows of money, goods and services or other assets among corporations, among households, and between corporations and households; in the purpose of social justice, increased efficiency or other aims legitimised by the citizens; examples are the redistribution of national income and wealth, the corporate income tax paid by companies to finance unemployment benefits, the social contributions paid by employees to finance the pension systems;</li>\n<li>to produce goods and services to satisfy households\' needs (e.g. state health care) or to collectively meet the needs of the whole community (e.g. defence, public order and safety).<sup id="cite_ref-gen_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The general government sector, in the European System of Accounts, has four sub-sectors:\n</p>\n<ol><li>central government</li>\n<li>state government</li>\n<li>local government</li>\n<li>social security funds</li></ol>\n<p>"Central government"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> consists of all administrative departments of the state and other central agencies whose responsibilities cover the whole economic territory of a country, except for the administration of social security funds.\n</p><p>"State government"<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> is defined as the separate institutional units that exercise some government functions below those units at central government level and above those units at local government level, excluding the administration of social security funds.\n</p><p>"Local government"<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> consists of all types of public administration whose responsibility covers only a local part of the economic territory, apart from local agencies of social security funds.\n</p><p>"Social security fund"<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> is a central, state or local institutional unit whose main activity is to provide social benefits. It fulfils the two following criteria:\n</p>\n<ul><li>by law or regulation (except those about government employees), certain population groups must take part in the scheme and have to pay contributions;</li>\n<li>general government is responsible for the management of the institutional unit, for the payment or approval of the level of the contributions and of the benefits, independent of its role as a supervisory body or employer.</li></ul>\n<p>The <i>GFSM 2001</i> framework is similar to the financial accounting of businesses. For example, it recommends that governments produce a full set of financial statements including the statement of government operations (akin to the <a href="/wiki/Income_statement" title="Income statement">income statement</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Balance_sheet" title="Balance sheet">balance sheet</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Cash_flow_statement" title="Cash flow statement">cash flow statement</a>. Two other similarities between the GFSM 2001 and business financial accounting are the recommended use of <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_Cash_Method_and_Accrual_Method_of_accounting" class="mw-redirect" title="Comparison of Cash Method and Accrual Method of accounting">accrual accounting</a> as the basis of recording and the presentations of stocks of assets and liabilities at <a href="/wiki/Market_value" title="Market value">market value</a>. It is an improvement on the prior methodology&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <i>Government Finance Statistics Manual 1986</i> \xe2\x80\x93 based on cash flows and without a balance sheet statement.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Users_of_GFS">Users of GFS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Users of GFS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <i>GFSM 2001</i> recommends standard tables including standard fiscal indicators that meet a broad group of users including policy makers, researchers, and investors in sovereign debt.\nGovernment finance statistics should offer data for topics such as the fiscal architecture, the measurement of the efficiency and effectiveness of government expenditures, the economics of taxation, and the structure of public financing. The <i>GFSM 2001</i> provides a blueprint for the compilation, recording, and presentation of revenues, expenditures, stocks of assets, and stocks of liabilities. The <i>GFSM 2001</i> also defines some indicators of effectiveness in government\xe2\x80\x99s expenditures, for example the compensation of employees as a percentage of expense. The <i>GFSM 2001</i> includes a functional classification of expense as defined by the Classification of Functions of Government (COFOG) .\n</p><p>This functional classification allows policy makers to analyze expenditures on categories such as health, education, social protection, and environmental protection.\nThe financial statements can provide investors with the necessary information to assess the capacity of a government to service and repay its debt, a key element determining sovereign risk, and risk premia. Like the risk of default of a private corporation, sovereign risk is a function of the level of debt, its ratio to liquid assets, revenues and expenditures, the expected growth and volatility of these revenues and expenditures, and the cost of servicing the debt. The government\xe2\x80\x99s financial statements contain the relevant information for this analysis.\n</p><p>The government\xe2\x80\x99s balance sheet presents the level of the debt; that is the government\xe2\x80\x99s liabilities. The memorandum items of the balance sheet provide additional information on the debt including its maturity and whether it is owed to domestic or external residents. The balance sheet also presents a disaggregated classification of financial and non-financial assets.\n</p><p>These data help estimate the resources a government can potentially access to repay its debt. The statement of operations  (\xe2\x80\x9cincome statement\xe2\x80\x9d) contains the revenue and expense accounts of the government. The revenue accounts are divided into subaccounts, including the different types of taxes, social contributions, dividends from the public sector, and royalties from natural resources. Finally, the interest expense account is one of the necessary inputs to estimate the cost of servicing the debt.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiscal_data_using_the_GFSM_2001_methodology">Fiscal data using the <i>GFSM 2001</i> methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fiscal data using the GFSM 2001 methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>GFS can be accessible through several sources. The International Monetary Fund publishes GFS in two publications: <i>International Financial Statistics</i> and the <i>Government Finance Statistics Yearbook</i>. The World Bank gathers information on external debt. On a regional level, the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (Dibidami ) compiles general government account data for its members, and Eurostat, following a methodology compatible with the GFSM 2001, compiles GFS for the members of the European Union.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
