class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Union_Flag" class="mw-redirect" title="Union Flag">Union Flag</a> is one of the most potent symbols of Britishness.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200521_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200521-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Britishness</b> is the state or quality of being British,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oed_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or of embodying British characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It comprises the claimed qualities that bind and distinguish the <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British people</a> and form the basis of their unity and identity,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWrightGamble200932_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWrightGamble200932-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and the expressions of <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Culture of the United Kingdom">British culture</a>\xe2\x80\x94such as habits, behaviours, or symbols\xe2\x80\x94that have a common, familiar or iconic quality readily identifiable with the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. Dialogue about the legitimacy and authenticity of Britishness is intrinsically tied with power relations and politics;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200511\xe2\x80\x9312_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200511\xe2\x80\x9312-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> in terms of <a href="/wiki/Nationhood" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationhood">nationhood</a> and belonging, expressing or recognising one\'s Britishness provokes a range of responses and attitudes, such as advocacy, indifference, or rejection.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200511\xe2\x80\x9312_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200511\xe2\x80\x9312-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the term \'Britishness\' "[sprang] into political and academic prominence" only in the late 20th century,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWrightGamble2009149_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWrightGamble2009149-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> its origins lie with the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> in 1707. It was used with reference to Britons collectively as early as 1682,<sup id="cite_ref-oed_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the historian <a href="/wiki/Linda_Colley" title="Linda Colley">Linda Colley</a> asserts that it was after the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a> that the ethnic groups of Great Britain began to assume a "layered" identity\xe2\x80\x94to think of themselves as simultaneously British but also <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, and/or <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColley199212\xe2\x80\x9313_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColley199212\xe2\x80\x9313-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In this formative period, Britishness was "closely bound up with <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColley19928_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColley19928-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Oxford English Dictionary Online</i> dates the first known use of the term Britishness to refer to the state of being British to a June 1857 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Putnam%27s_Monthly_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Putnam&#39;s Monthly Magazine">Putnam\'s Monthly Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the late 20th century, the exploration and proliferation of Britishness became directly associated with a desire to define, sustain or restore a homogeneous British identity or allegiance to Britain, prompting debate. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Life_in_the_United_Kingdom_test" title="Life in the United Kingdom test">Life in the United Kingdom test</a>\xe2\x80\x94reported as a test of one\'s Britishness\xe2\x80\x94has been described as controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">UK Independence Party</a> have asserted that Britishness is tied with inclusive <a href="/wiki/Civic_nationalism" title="Civic nationalism">civic nationalism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> whereas the <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Racial_Equality" title="Commission for Racial Equality">Commission for Racial Equality</a> reported that Scots, Welsh, Irish and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_minorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic minorities">ethnic minorities</a> may feel quite divorced from Britishness because of ethnic English dominance; <a href="/wiki/Gwynfor_Evans" title="Gwynfor Evans">Gwynfor Evans</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_nationalism" title="Welsh nationalism">Welsh nationalist</a> politician, said that "Britishness is a political synonym for Englishness which extends English culture over the Scots, Welsh, and the Irish".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Historians Graham Macphee and Prem Poddar state that Britishness and Englishness are invariably conflated as they are both tied to the identity of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and UK; slippage between the two words is common.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\nWith regards to a proposed oath of allegiance for school leavers, historian <a href="/wiki/David_Starkey" title="David Starkey">David Starkey</a> argued that it is impossible to teach Britishness because "a British nation doesn\'t exist".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britishness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Government perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>, made a speech in 2006 to promote the idea of Britishness.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Brown\'s speech to the <a href="/wiki/Fabian_Society" title="Fabian Society">Fabian Society</a>\'s Britishness Conference proposed that <b>British values</b> demand a new constitutional settlement and symbols to represent a modern patriotism, including a new youth community service scheme and a \'<a href="/wiki/British_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="British Day">British Day</a>\' to celebrate.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the central issues identified at the Fabian Society conference was how the English identity fits within the framework of a devolved UK. Does England require a new constitutional settlement for instance?<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nThe <a href="/wiki/British_government" class="mw-redirect" title="British government">British government</a> has sought to promote Britishness with the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Day_(United_Kingdom)" title="Armed Forces Day (United Kingdom)">Veterans\' Day</a> (now called <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Day_(United_Kingdom)" title="Armed Forces Day (United Kingdom)">Armed Forces Day</a>), first held on 27 June 2006. As well as celebrating the achievements of members of the armed forces, at the first event for the celebration Brown said: </p><blockquote><p>Scots and people from the rest of the UK share the purpose\xe2\x80\x94that Britain has something to say to the rest of the world about the values of freedom, democracy, and the dignity of the people that you stand up for. So at a time when people can talk about football and devolution and money, it is important that we also remember the values that we share in common.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Critics have argued that Brown\'s sudden interest in the subject had more to do with countering English opposition to a <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> Member of Parliament becoming Prime Minister.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> newspaper\'s <i>Comment Central</i> asked readers to define Britishness in five little words. The winning suggestion was "No motto please, we\'re British".<sup id="cite_ref-Hurst_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurst-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A duty to promote <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> forms a key part of the "duty to actively promote fundamental British values in schools" in the United Kingdom in accordance with section 78 of the <a href="/wiki/Education_Act_2002" title="Education Act 2002">Education Act 2002</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Education" title="Department for Education">Department for Education</a>\xe2\x80\x99s advice for maintained schools in 2014, \xe2\x80\x9cSchools should promote the fundamental British values of democracy, the rule of law, individual liberty, and mutual respect and tolerance of those with different faiths and beliefs\xe2\x80\x9d.<sup id="cite_ref-DfE2014_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DfE2014-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Government\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/CONTEST" title="CONTEST"><i>Prevent</i> strategy</a> of 2011 was cited as the source of this list of values, but that strategy also contained a slightly different list: \xe2\x80\x9cdemocracy, rule of law, equality of opportunity, freedom of speech and the rights of all men and \nwomen to live free from persecution of any kind.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The 2018 version of the <a href="/wiki/CONTEST" title="CONTEST">CONTEST</a> strategy codified the list as:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the rule of law</li>\n<li>individual liberty</li>\n<li>democracy</li>\n<li>mutual respect, tolerance and understanding of different faiths and beliefs.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The same advice stated that UK schools must:\n</p>\n<ul><li>encourage respect for democracy and support for participation in the democratic processes</li>\n<li>[ensure pupils acquire] an understanding of how citizens can influence decision-making through the democratic process</li></ul>\n<p>for example by\n</p>\n<ul><li>[including] in suitable parts of the curriculum, as appropriate for the age of pupils, material on the strengths, advantages and disadvantages of democracy, and how democracy and the law works in Britain, in contrast to other forms of government in other countries;</li>\n<li>[ensuring] that all pupils within the school have a voice that is listened to, and demonstrate how democracy works by actively promoting democratic processes, such as a school council whose members are voted for by the pupils.<sup id="cite_ref-DfE2014_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DfE2014-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnicity_and_social_trends">Ethnicity and social trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britishness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ethnicity and social trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Not all people residing in England and the United Kingdom are <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">White</a> due to immigration from other countries. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a> in England, around 85.4% of residents are White (British, Irish, other European), 7.8% Asian (mainly <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asian</a>), 3.5% Black, 2.3% are of <a href="/wiki/Mixed-race" class="mw-redirect" title="Mixed-race">Mixed-race</a> heritage, 0.4% Arab, and 0.6% identified as Other ethnicity, with a significantly higher ethnic minority population in large cities such as <a href="/wiki/London#Ethnic_groups" title="London">London</a>.\n</p><p>A survey conducted in 2007 found that the majority of people in many non-white ethnic minority groups living in Great Britain described their national identity as British, English, Scottish or Welsh. This included almost nine in ten (87%) of people with <a href="/wiki/British_Mixed-Race" class="mw-redirect" title="British Mixed-Race">mixed heritage</a>, 85% of <a href="/wiki/British_African-Caribbean_community" class="mw-redirect" title="British African-Caribbean community">Black Caribbean people</a>, 80% of <a href="/wiki/British_Pakistanis" title="British Pakistanis">Pakistanis</a> and 78% of <a href="/wiki/British_Bangladeshi" title="British Bangladeshi">Bangladeshis</a>. Non-whites were more likely to describe themselves as British than whites. One-third of people from the <a href="/wiki/White_British" title="White British">White British</a> group described themselves as British; the remaining two third of respondents identified themselves as <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, Welsh, or Scottish ethnic groups. <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A study conducted for the <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Racial_Equality" title="Commission for Racial Equality">Commission for Racial Equality</a> (CRE) in 2005 found that, in England, the majority of ethnic minority participants identified primarily as British, whereas ethnic English participants identified as English first and British second. In <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the majority of both white and ethnic minority participants identified as Welsh or Scottish first and British second, although they saw no incompatibility between the two identities.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200537_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality200537-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Other research conducted for the CRE found that white participants felt that there was a threat to Britishness from large-scale immigration, the "unfair" claims that they perceived ethnic minorities made on the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>, a rise in <a href="/wiki/Value_pluralism" title="Value pluralism">moral pluralism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">political correctness</a>. Much of this frustration was found to be targeted at <a href="/wiki/Muslims" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslims">Muslims</a> rather than minorities in general. Muslim participants in the study reported feeling victimised and stated that they felt that they were being asked to choose between Muslim and British identities, whereas they saw it possible to be both at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality20054_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECommission_for_Racial_Equality20054-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Within_the_United_Kingdom">Within the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britishness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Within the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britishness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/English_national_identity" title="English national identity">English national identity</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britishness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_national_identity" title="Scottish national identity">Scottish national identity</a></div>\n<table class="wikitable" style="float:right;">\n\n<tbody><tr>\n<th style="background:#e9e9e9; text-align:center;" rowspan="2">Identity\n</th>\n<th style="background:#e9e9e9; text-align:center;" colspan="4">National Identity in Scotland from 1997\xe2\x80\x932003<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>1997\n</th>\n<th>1999\n</th>\n<th>2001\n</th>\n<th>2003\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background:#e9e9e9;">Scottish not British\n</th>\n<td>23\n</td>\n<td>32\n</td>\n<td>36\n</td>\n<td>31\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background:#e9e9e9;">More Scottish than British\n</th>\n<td>38\n</td>\n<td>35\n</td>\n<td>30\n</td>\n<td>34\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background:#e9e9e9;">Equally Scottish and British\n</th>\n<td>27\n</td>\n<td>22\n</td>\n<td>24\n</td>\n<td>22\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background:#e9e9e9;">More British than Scottish\n</th>\n<td>4\n</td>\n<td>3\n</td>\n<td>3\n</td>\n<td>4\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background:#e9e9e9;">British not Scottish\n</th>\n<td>4\n</td>\n<td>4\n</td>\n<td>3\n</td>\n<td>4\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>There is evidence that <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">people in Scotland</a> are increasingly likely to describe themselves as Scottish, and less likely to say they are British. A 2006 study by social scientists at the Universities of Edinburgh, Dundee, St Andrews and Lancaster shows that more than eight out of ten people in Scotland saw themselves as Scottish. At the same time, there has been a long-term decline in Scots defining themselves as British, although more than half of the people in the survey saw themselves as British.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a> in Scotland:<sup id="cite_ref-nationalidentity_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nationalidentity-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>62% identified themselves as <b>Scottish only</b></li>\n<li>18% identified themselves as <b>Scottish and British</b></li>\n<li>8% identified themselves as <b>British only</b></li></ul>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Member of the Scottish Parliament">MSP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Secretary_for_Justice" title="Cabinet Secretary for Justice">Cabinet Secretary for Justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_MacAskill" title="Kenny MacAskill">Kenny MacAskill</a> gave the following submission to the <a href="/wiki/UK_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Parliament">UK Parliament</a>\'s Joint Committee on Human Rights in March 2008 discussing a British Bill of Rights:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>What is meant by Britishness? Is there a concept of Britishness? Yes, just as there is a concept of being Scandinavian. We eat fish and chips, we eat chicken masala, we watch EastEnders. Are [the SNP] British? No, we are not. We consider ourselves Scottish.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britishness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_national_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Welsh national identity (page does not exist)">Welsh national identity</a></div>\n<p>Similar to Scotland, results from the Annual Population Survey (APS) conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a>, show that the majority of people residing in Wales describe themselves as <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  Respondents were asked whether they considered their national identity to be \'Welsh\', or \'Non-Welsh\' (defined as: \'English\', \'Scottish\', \'Irish\', \'British\' or \'Other\').  In June 2017, 63.2% of respondents in Wales defined their national identity as \'Welsh\'.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_politics">Identity and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Britishness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Identity and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In a 1998 poll, 37% of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> voters stated themselves to be "Scottish, not British", the rest demonstrating some form of British identity, with the most popular choice being "More Scottish than British" (41%).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> This conclusion was again put forward in 2002, with similar figures cited.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> However, the British Social Attitudes Survey of 2007 found that only 21% of Scots saw themselves as \'Equally Scottish and British\', with less than half choosing British as a secondary identity.<sup id="cite_ref-BSA2007_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSA2007-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The report concluded that 73% of respondents saw themselves as \'only\' or \'mainly\' Scottish.<sup id="cite_ref-BSA2007_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSA2007-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
