class="mw-redirect" title="Departments of the United Kingdom Government">British government department</a> responsible for implementing the defence policy set by <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Her Majesty\'s Government</a> and is the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a>.\n</p><p>The MOD states that its principal objectives are to defend the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland</a> and its interests and to strengthen international peace and stability.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> With the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the MOD does not foresee any short-term conventional military threat; rather, it has identified <a href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" class="mw-redirect" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_terrorism" class="mw-redirect" title="International terrorism">international terrorism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Failed_state" title="Failed state">failed and failing states</a> as the overriding threats to <a href="/wiki/National_interest" title="National interest">Britain\'s interests</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The MOD also manages day-to-day running of the armed forces, contingency planning and defence procurement.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 1920s and 1930s, British <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servants</a> and politicians, looking back at the performance of the state during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, concluded that there was a need for greater co-ordination between the three services that made up the armed forces of the United Kingdom\xe2\x80\x94the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>. The formation of a united ministry of defence was rejected by <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George\'s</a> coalition government in 1921; but the <a href="/wiki/Chiefs_of_Staff_Committee" title="Chiefs of Staff Committee">Chiefs of Staff Committee</a> was formed in 1923, for the purposes of inter-service co-ordination. As rearmament became a concern during the 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a> created the position of <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Co-ordination_of_Defence" title="Minister for Co-ordination of Defence">Minister for Co-ordination of Defence</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ernle_Chatfield,_1st_Baron_Chatfield" title="Ernle Chatfield, 1st Baron Chatfield">Lord Chatfield</a> held the post until the fall of <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Neville Chamberlain\'s</a> government in 1940; his success was limited by his lack of control over the existing Service departments and his limited political influence.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, on forming his government in 1940, created the office of Minister of Defence to exercise ministerial control over the Chiefs of Staff Committee and to co-ordinate defence matters. The post was held by the Prime Minister of the day until <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee\'s</a> government introduced the Ministry of Defence Act of 1946. The new ministry was headed by a Minister of Defence who possessed a seat in the Cabinet. The three existing service Ministers\xe2\x80\x94the <a href="/wiki/First_Lord_of_the_Admiralty" title="First Lord of the Admiralty">First Lord of the Admiralty</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_War" title="Secretary of State for War">Secretary of State for War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Air" title="Secretary of State for Air">Secretary of State for Air</a>\xe2\x80\x94remained in direct operational control of their respective services, but ceased to attend Cabinet.\n</p><p>From 1946 to 1964 five Departments of State did the work of the modern Ministry of Defence: the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Admiralty" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Admiralty">Admiralty</a>, the <a href="/wiki/War_Office" title="War Office">War Office</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Air_Ministry" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Air Ministry">Air Ministry</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Ministry_of_Aviation" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Ministry of Aviation">Ministry of Aviation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(1947%E2%80%9364)" title="Ministry of Defence (1947\xe2\x80\x9364)">an earlier form</a> of the Ministry of Defence. These departments merged in 1964; the defence functions of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Aviation_Supply&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ministry of Aviation Supply (page does not exist)">Ministry of Aviation Supply</a> merged into the Ministry of Defence in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span Defence">Secretary of State for Defence</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>.\n</p><p>The CDS is supported by the Vice Chief of the Defence Staff (VCDS) who deputises and is responsible for the day-to-day running of the armed services aspect of the MOD through the Central Staff, working closely alongside the Permanent Secretary. href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Together the Chiefs of Staff form the <a href="/wiki/Chiefs_of_Staff_Committee" title="Chiefs of Staff Committee">Chiefs of Staff Committee</a> These forces must be capable of representing Britain as lead nation in any coalition operations.</li>\n<li>The ability, at longer notice, to deploy forces in a large-scale operation while running a concurrent small-scale operation.</li></ul>\n<p>The MOD has since been regarded as a leader in elaborating the post-Cold War organising concept of "<a href="/wiki/Defence_diplomacy" title="Defence diplomacy">defence diplomacy</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defence_and_Security_Review_2010" title="Strategic Defence and Security Review 2010">Strategic Defence and Security Review 2010</a>, Prime Minister David Cameron signed a <a href="/wiki/The_Lancaster_House_Treaties_(2010)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lancaster House Treaties (2010)">50-year treaty</a> with French President Nicolas Sarkozy that would have the two countries co-operate intensively in military matters.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The UK is establishing air and naval bases in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>, located in the UAE and Bahrain.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> A presence in Oman is also being considered.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defence_and_Security_Review_2015" title="Strategic Defence and Security Review 2015">Strategic Defence and Security Review 2015</a> included \xc2\xa3178 billion investment in new equipment and capabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The review set a defence policy with four primary missions for the Armed Forces:<sup id="cite_ref-SDSR2015_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SDSR2015-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Defend and contribute to the security and resilience of the UK and Overseas Territories.</li>\n<li>Provide the nuclear deterrent.</li>\n<li>Contribute to improved understanding of the world through strategic intelligence and the global defence network.</li>\n<li>Reinforce international security and the collective capacity of our allies, partners and multilateral institutions.</li></ul>\n<p>The review stated the Armed Forces will also contribute to the government\xe2\x80\x99s response to crises by being prepared to:<sup id="cite_ref-SDSR2015_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SDSR2015-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Support humanitarian assistance and disaster response, and conduct rescue missions.</li>\n<li>Conduct strike class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the threat of direct conventional military confrontation with In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dannatt,_Baron_Dannatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Dannatt, Baron Dannatt">Sir Richard Dannatt</a>, then head of the British Army, predicted British forces to be involved in combating "predatory non-state actors" for the foreseeable future, in what he called an "era of persistent conflict". He told the <a href="/wiki/Chatham_House" title="Chatham House">Chatham House</a> think tank that the fight against <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">al-Qaeda</a> and other militant <a strategic threat;<sup id="cite_ref-N000346_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N000346-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-N000345_24-0" class="reference"><a class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> stating that the <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Defence_Materiel" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief of Defence Materiel">Chief of Defence Materiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_O%27Donoghue" title="Kevin O&#39;Donoghue">General Sir Kevin O\'Donoghue</a>, had instructed staff within <a href="/wiki/Defence_Equipment_and_Support" title="Defence Equipment and Support">Defence Equipment and Support</a> (DE&amp;S) through an internal memorandum to re-prioritise the approvals process to focus on supporting current operations over the next three years; deterrence related programmes; those that reflect defence obligations both contractual or international; and those where production contracts are already signed. The report also cites concerns over potential cuts in the defence science and technology research budget; implications of inappropriate estimation of Defence Inflation within budgetary processes; underfunding in the Equipment Programme; and a general concern over striking the appropriate balance over a short-term focus (Current Operations) and long-term consequences of failure to invest in the delivery of future UK defence capabilities on future combatants and campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-CommitteePub_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CommitteePub-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a> of land and foreshore (either freehold or leasehold) at April 2014, which was valued at "about \xc2\xa320&#160;billion". The total annual cost to support the defence estate is "in excess of \xc2\xa33.3 billion".<sup id="cite_ref-LHbulletin_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHbulletin-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The defence estate is divided as training areas &amp; ranges (84.0%), research &amp; development (5.4%), airfields (3.4%), barracks &amp; camps (2.5%), storage &amp; supply depots (1.6%), and other (3.0%).<sup id="cite_ref-LHbulletin_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHbulletin-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> These are largely managed by the <a href="/wiki/Defence_Infrastructure_Organisation" title="Defence Infrastructure Organisation">Defence Infrastructure Organisation</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_Building">Main Building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Main Building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_Main_Building_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence Main Building (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence Main Building (United Kingdom)</a></div>\n<p>The headquarters of the MOD are in <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a> and is known as <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_Main_Building_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence Main Building (United Kingdom)">MOD Main Building</a>. This structure is <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical</a> in style and was originally built between 1938 and 1959 to designs by <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Harris" title="Vincent Harris">Vincent Harris</a> to house the <a href="/wiki/Air_Ministry" title="Air Ministry">Air Ministry</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a>. A major refurbishment of the building was completed under a <a href="/wiki/Private_Finance_Initiative" class="mw-redirect" title="Private Finance Initiative">Private Finance Initiative</a> contract by <a href="/wiki/Skanska" title="Skanska">Skanska</a> in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The northern entrance in <a href="/wiki/Horse_Guards_Avenue" title="Horse Guards Avenue">Horse Guards Avenue</a> is flanked by two monumental statues, <i>Earth</i> and <i>Water</i>,  by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wheeler_(sculptor)" title="Charles Wheeler (sculptor)">Charles Wheeler</a>. Opposite stands the <a href="/wiki/Gurkha" title="Gurkha">Gurkha</a> Monument, sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Jackson_(sculptor)" title="Philip Jackson (sculptor)">Philip Jackson</a> and unveiled in 1997 by <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a>. Within it is the Victoria Cross and George Cross Memorial, and nearby are memorials to the <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm_Memorial" title="Fleet Air Arm Memorial">Fleet Air Arm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force_Memorial" title="Royal Air Force Memorial">RAF</a> (to its east, facing the riverside).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII\'s</a> wine cellar at the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Whitehall" title="Palace of Whitehall">Palace of Whitehall</a>, built in 1514\xe2\x80\x931516 for <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wolsey" title="Thomas Wolsey">Cardinal Wolsey</a>, is in the basement of Main Building, and is used for entertainment.  The entire vaulted brick structure of the cellar was encased in steel and concrete and relocated nine feet to the west and nearly 19 feet (5.8&#160;m) deeper in 1949, when construction was resumed at the site after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. This was carried out without any significant damage to the structure.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraud">Fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Fraud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Foxley" title="Gordon Foxley">Gordon Foxley</a></div>\n<p>The most notable fraud conviction has been that of <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Foxley" title="Gordon Foxley">Gordon Foxley</a>, Director of Ammunition Procurement at the Ministry of Defence from 1981 to 1984. Police claimed he received at least \xc2\xa33.5m in total in corrupt payments, such as substantial bribes from overseas arms contractors aiming to influence the allocation of contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-Mike_Hall_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mike_Hall-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germ_and_chemical_warfare_tests">Germ and chemical warfare tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Germ and chemical warfare tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A government report covered by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> newspaper in 2002 indicated that between 1940 and 1979, the Ministry of Defence "turned large parts of the country into a giant laboratory to conduct a series of secret <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">germ warfare</a> tests on the public" and many of these tests "involved releasing potentially dangerous chemicals and micro-organisms over vast swathes of the population without the public being told."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The Ministry of Defence claims that these trials were to simulate germ warfare and that the tests were harmless. However, families who have been in the area of many of the tests are experiencing children with birth defects and physical and mental handicaps and many are asking for a public inquiry. The report estimated these tests affected  millions of people, including during one period between 1961 and 1968 where "more than a million people along the south coast of England, from <a href="/wiki/Torquay" title="Torquay">Torquay</a> to the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a>, were exposed to bacteria including <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">e.coli</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_atrophaeus" title="Bacillus atrophaeus">bacillus globigii</a></i>, which mimics <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>." Two scientists commissioned by the Ministry of Defence stated that these trials posed no risk to the public. This was confirmed by Sue Ellison, a representative of the <a href="/wiki/Defence_Science_and_Technology_Laboratory" title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory">Defence Science and Technology Laboratory</a> at <a href="/wiki/Porton_Down" title="Porton Down">Porton Down</a> who said that the results from these trials "will save lives, should the country or our forces face an attack by chemical and biological weapons."  Asked whether such tests are still being carried out, she said: "It is not our policy to discuss ongoing research." It is unknown whether or not the harmlessness of the trials was known at the time of their occurrence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Territorial The government eventually backed down and restored the funding. The TA provides a small percentage of the UK\'s operational troops. Its members train on weekly evenings and monthly weekends, as well as two-week exercises generally annually and occasionally bi-annually for troops doing other courses. The cuts would have meant a significant loss of personnel and would have had adverse effects on recruitment.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overspending">Overspending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Overspending">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2013 it was found that the Ministry of Defence had overspent on its equipment budget by \xc2\xa36.5bn on orders that could take up to 39 years to fulfil.
