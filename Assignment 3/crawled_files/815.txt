present</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Media&#160;type</th><td>\nBook, television</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><i><b>Guinness World Records</b></i>, known from its inception in 1955 until 2000 as <i><b>The Guinness Book of Records</b></i> and in previous United States editions as <i><b>The Guinness Book of World Records</b></i>, is a <a href="/wiki/Reference_book" class="mw-redirect" title="Reference book">reference book</a> published annually, listing <a href="/wiki/World_record" title="World record">world records</a> both of human achievements and the extremes of the natural world. The brainchild of Sir <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Beaver" title="Hugh Beaver">Hugh Beaver</a>, the book was co-founded by brothers <a href="/wiki/Norris_McWhirter" title="Norris McWhirter">Norris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ross_McWhirter" title="Ross McWhirter">Ross McWhirter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a>, London in August 1954.\n</p><p>The book itself holds a world record, as the best-selling copyrighted book of all time. As of the 2019 edition, it is now in its 64th  year of publication, published in 100 countries and 23 languages. The international franchise has extended beyond print to include television series and museums. The popularity of the franchise has resulted in <i>Guinness World Records</i> becoming the primary international authority on the cataloguing and verification of a huge number of world records; the organisation employs official record adjudicators authorised to verify the authenticity of the setting and breaking of records.<sup id="cite_ref-faq_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div href="/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 10 November 1951, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Beaver" title="Hugh Beaver">Sir Hugh Beaver</a>, then the managing director of the <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">Guinness</a> Breweries,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> went on a shooting party in the <a href="/wiki/North_Slob" title="North Slob">North Slob</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/River_Slaney" title="River Slaney">River Slaney</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>, Ireland. After missing a shot at a <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Golden_Plover" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurasian Golden Plover">golden plover</a>, he became involved in an argument over which was the fastest <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (food)">game bird</a> in Europe, the golden plover or the <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">red grouse</a> (it is the plover<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>). That evening at <a href="/wiki/Castlebridge" title="Castlebridge">Castlebridge</a> House, he realized that it was impossible to confirm in reference books whether or not the golden plover was Europe\'s fastest game bird.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Beaver knew that there must be numerous other questions debated nightly in pubs throughout Ireland and abroad, but there was no book in the world with which to settle arguments about records. He realised then that a book supplying the answers to this sort of question might prove successful.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Beaver\'s idea became reality when Guinness employee <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Chataway" title="Christopher Chataway">Christopher Chataway</a> recommended University friends <a href="/wiki/Norris_McWhirter" title="Norris McWhirter">Norris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ross_McWhirter" title="Ross McWhirter">Ross McWhirter</a>, who had been running a fact-finding agency in London. The twin brothers were commissioned to compile what became <i>The Guinness Book of Records</i> in August 1954. A thousand copies were printed and given away.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the founding of <i>The Guinness Book of Records</i> at 107 <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a>, London, the first 198-page edition was bound on 27 August 1955 and went to the top of the British best seller lists by Christmas. The following year, it launched in the US, and sold 70,000 copies. Since then, <i>Guinness World Records</i> has gone on to become a record breaker in its own right; with sales of more than 100 million copies in 100 different countries and 37 languages, <i>Guinness World Records</i> is the world\'s best selling copyrighted book ever.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div time for Christmas. The McWhirters continued to compile it for many years. Both brothers had an encyclopedic memory; on the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Record_Breakers" title="Record Breakers">Record Breakers</a></i>, based upon the book, they would take questions posed by children in the audience on various world records and were able to give the correct answer. <a href="/wiki/Ross_McWhirter#Assassination" title="Ross McWhirter">Ross McWhirter was assassinated</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a> in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Following Ross\' assassination, the feature in the show where questions about records posed by children were answered was called <i>Norris on the Spot</i>.\n</p><p>Guinness Superlatives (later Guinness World Records) Limited was formed in 1954 to publish the first book. <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Publishing" title="Sterling Publishing">Sterling Publishing</a> owned the rights to the <i>Guinness</i> book in the US for decades. The group was owned by Guinness PLC and subsequently <a href="/wiki/Diageo" title="Diageo">Diageo</a> until 2001, when it was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Gullane_Entertainment" title="Gullane Entertainment">Gullane Entertainment</a>. Gullane was itself purchased by <a href="/wiki/HIT_Entertainment" title="HIT Entertainment">HIT Entertainment</a> in 2002. In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Apax_Partners" title="Apax Partners">Apax Partners</a> purchased HiT and subsequently sold Guinness World Records in early 2008 to the <a href="/wiki/Jim_Pattison_Group" title="Jim Pattison Group">Jim Pattison Group</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Ripley_Entertainment" title="Ripley Entertainment">Ripley Entertainment</a>, which is licensed to operate Guinness World Records\' Attractions. With offices in New York City and Tokyo, Guinness World Records\' global headquarters remain in London, while its museum attractions are based at Ripley headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>, US.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg/210px-Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg" width="210" height="319" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg/315px-Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg/420px-Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1320" data-file-height="2004" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lucky_Diamond_Rich_face.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lucky_Diamond_Rich" title="Lucky Diamond Rich">Lucky Diamond Rich</a> is "the world\'s most tattooed person", and has tattoos covering his entire body. He holds the Guinness World Records title as of  2006<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Recent editions have focused on record feats by person competitors. Competitions range from obvious ones such as <a href="/wiki/Olympic_weightlifting" title="Olympic weightlifting">Olympic weightlifting</a> to the longest <a href="/wiki/Egg_tossing" title="Egg tossing">egg tossing</a> distances, or for longest time spent playing <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto_IV" title="Grand Theft Auto IV">Grand Theft Auto IV</a></i> or the number of hot dogs that can be consumed in three minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Besides records about competitions, it contains such facts such as the heaviest tumour,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> the most poisonous fungus,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> the shortest river (<a href="/wiki/Roe_River" title="Roe River">Roe River</a>), the longest-running soap opera<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and the most valuable life-insurance policy,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> among others. Many records also relate to the youngest people to have achieved something, such as the youngest person to visit all nations of the world (<a href="/wiki/Maurizio_Giuliano" title="Maurizio Giuliano">Maurizio Giuliano</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Each edition contains a selection of the records from the Guinness World Records database, as well as select new records, with the criteria for inclusion changing from year to year.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The retirement of Norris McWhirter from his consulting role in 1995 and the subsequent decision by Diageo Plc to sell The Guinness Book of Records brand have shifted the focus of the books from text-oriented to illustrated reference. A selection of records are curated for the book from the full archive but all existing Guinness World Records titles can be accessed by creating a login on the company\'s website. Applications made by individuals for existing record categories are free of charge. There is an administration fee of $5 to propose a new record title.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A number of spin-off books<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and television series have also been produced.\n</p><p><i>Guinness World Records</i> bestowed the record of "Person with the most records" on <a href="/wiki/Ashrita_Furman" title="Ashrita Furman">Ashrita Furman</a> of Queens, NY in April 2009. At that time, he held 100 records.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2005, Guinness designated 9 November as <i>International Guinness World Records Day</i> to encourage breaking of world records.<sup id="cite_ref-Guinness2006_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness2006-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, an estimated 100,000 people participated in over 10 countries. Guinness reported 2,244 new records in 12 months, which was a 173% increase over the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-Guinness2006_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness2006-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In February 2008, <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> aired <i>The Top 100 Guinness World Records of All Time</i> and Guinness World Records made the complete list available on their website.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:right;"></div>\n<h2><span the company providing adjudicators to events to determine the veracity of record attempts. The list of records which the Guinness World Records covers is not fixed; records may be added and also removed for various reasons. The public are invited to submit applications for records, which can be either the bettering of existing records or substantial achievements which could constitute a new record.<sup id="cite_ref-faq_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The company also provides corporate services for companies to "harness the power of record-breaking to deliver tangible success for their businesses."<sup id="cite_ref-corporate_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corporate-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_and_safety_issues">Ethical and safety issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ethical and safety issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Guinness_Beer_Record_1977.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Guinness_Beer_Record_1977.jpg/220px-Guinness_Beer_Record_1977.jpg" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2b/Guinness_Beer_Record_1977.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="310" data-file-height="268" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Guinness_Beer_Record_1977.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Steven Petrosino drinking 1 litre of beer in 1.3 seconds in June 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Petrosino set record times for 250 ml, 500 ml and 1.5 litres as well, but Guinness accepted only the record for one litre. They later dropped all beer and alcohol records from their compendium in 1991, and reinstated the records in 2008.</div></div></div>\n<p>Guinness World Records states several types of records it will not accept for ethical reasons, such as those related to the killing or harming of animals.<sup id="cite_ref-isitarecord_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isitarecord-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several world records that were once included in the book have been removed for ethical reasons, including concerns for the well being of potential record breakers. For example, following publication of a "heaviest fish" record, many fish owners overfed their pets beyond the bounds of what was healthy, and therefore such entries were removed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Guinness Book also dropped records within their "eating and drinking records" section of Human Achievements in 1991 over concerns that potential competitors could harm themselves and expose the publisher to potential <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">litigation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-discontinue_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discontinue-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> These changes included the removal of all <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Distilled beverage">spirit</a>, wine, and beer drinking records, along with other unusual records for consuming such unlikely things as bicycles and trees.<sup id="cite_ref-discontinue_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discontinue-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Other records, such as <a href="/wiki/Sword_swallowing" title="Sword swallowing">sword swallowing</a> and rally driving (on public roads), were closed from further entry as the current holders had performed beyond what are considered safe human tolerance levels.\n</p><p>There have been instances of closed records being reopened. For example, the sword swallowing record was listed as closed in the 1990 Guinness Book of World Records, but the <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records_Primetime" title="Guinness World Records Primetime">Guinness World Records Primetime</a></i> TV show, which started in 1998, accepted three sword swallowing challenges (and so did the 2007 edition of the <i>Guinness World Records</i> onwards). Similarly, the speed beer drinking records which were dropped from the book in 1991, reappeared 17 years later in the 2008 edition, but were moved from the "Human Achievements" section of the older book<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> to the "Modern Society" section of the newer edition.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of  2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, it is required in the guidelines of all "large food" type records that the item be fully edible, and distributed to the public for consumption, to prevent food wastage.<sup id="cite_ref-faq_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chain_letter" title="Chain letter">Chain letters</a> are also not allowed: "Guinness World Records does not accept any records relating to chain letters, sent by post or e-mail. If you receive a letter or an e-mail, which may promise to publish the names of all those who send it on, please destroy it, it is a hoax. No matter if it says that Guinness World Records and the postal service are involved, they are not."<sup id="cite_ref-faq_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulty_in_defining_records">Difficulty in defining records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Difficulty in defining records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For some potential categories, <i>Guinness World Records</i> has declined to list some records that are too difficult or impossible to determine. For example, its website states: "We do not accept any claims for beauty as it is not objectively measurable."<sup id="cite_ref-isitarecord_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isitarecord-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 10 December 2010, <i>Guinness World Records</i> stopped its new "<a href="/wiki/Dreadlock" class="mw-redirect" title="Dreadlock">dreadlock</a>" category after investigation of its first and only female title holder, Asha Mandela, determining it was impossible to judge this record accurately.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Verifying_existing_records">Verifying existing records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Verifying existing records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Guinness World Records</i> website publishes selected records and is not supposed to be used for the record verification purposes, as it explains: "There are more than 40,000 current records in our database and we try our best to feature as many as possible online. We currently include over 15,000 records online which we update every week, so make sure to check the site regularly!" The book printed annually contains only 4,000 records. The only way to verify a record is to contact Guinness, and the average response time is twelve weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guinness_World_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:6764_Guinness.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/6764_Guinness.JPG/220px-6764_Guinness.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/6764_Guinness.JPG/330px-6764_Guinness.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/46/6764_Guinness.JPG 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:6764_Guinness.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Guinness Museum in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1976, a <i>Guinness Book of World Records</i> museum opened in the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>. Speed shooter <a href="/wiki/Bob_Munden" title="Bob Munden">Bob Munden</a> then went on tour promoting <i>The Guinness Book of World Records</i> by performing his record fast draws with a standard weight single-action revolver from a western movie type holster. His fastest time for a draw was 0.02 seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Among exhibits were life-size statues of the world\'s tallest man (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Wadlow" title="Robert Wadlow">Robert Wadlow</a>) and world\'s largest earth worm, an X-ray photo of a sword swallower, repeated lightning strike victim <a
