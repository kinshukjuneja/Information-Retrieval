class="mw-headline" id="County_constituencies_and_borough_constituencies">County constituencies and borough constituencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_constituencies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: County constituencies and borough constituencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><span id="Types_of_constituency"></span>\nHouse of Commons, Northern Ireland Assembly, Scottish Parliament and Welsh Assembly constituencies are designated as either <span id="County"></span><i>county</i> or <span id="Borough"></span><i>borough</i> constituencies, except that in Scotland the term <span id="Burgh"></span><i>burgh</i> is used instead of <i>borough</i>.  Since the advent of <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a>, the differences between county and borough constituencies are slight. Formerly (see <a href="#History">below</a>) the franchise differed, and there were also <i>county borough</i> and <i>university</i> constituencies.\n</p><p>Borough constituencies are predominantly <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> while county constituencies are predominantly <a href="/wiki/Rural" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural">rural</a>. There is no definitive statutory criterion for the distinction; the Boundary Commission for England has stated that, "as a general principle, where constituencies contain more than a small rural element they should normally be designated as county constituencies. Otherwise they should be designated as borough constituencies."<sup id="cite_ref-bce2007_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bce2007-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland, all House of Commons constituencies are county constituencies except those in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> and three urban areas of <a href="/wiki/Lanarkshire" title="Lanarkshire">Lanarkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Returning_officer" title="Returning officer">returning officer</a> will typically be a local Council\'s <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Executive">Chief Executive</a><sup id="cite_ref-scc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or Head of Legal Services. The role, however, is separate from these posts, and can be held by any person appointed by the Council. The spending limits for election campaigns are different in the two, the reasoning being that candidates in county constituencies tend to need to travel further.\n</p>\n<table id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_constituencies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Unreformed_House_of_Commons" title="Unreformed House of Commons">Unreformed House of Commons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Franchise_in_the_United_Kingdom_1885%E2%80%931918" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary Franchise in the United Kingdom 1885\xe2\x80\x931918">Parliamentary Franchise in the United Kingdom 1885\xe2\x80\x931918</a></div>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_England" title="House of Commons of England">House of Commons of England</a>, each <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">English county</a> elected two "knights of the shire" while each enfranchised <a href="/wiki/Ancient_borough" title="Ancient borough">borough</a> elected "burgesses" (usually two, sometimes four, and in a few cases one). <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542">From 1535</a> each <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_Wales" title="Historic counties of Wales">Welsh county</a> and borough was represented, by one knight or burgess.   The franchise was restricted differently in different types of constituency; in county constituencies <a href="/wiki/Forty_shilling_freeholders" class="mw-redirect" title="Forty shilling freeholders">forty shilling freeholders</a> (i.e. landowners) could vote, while in boroughs the franchise varied from <a href="/wiki/Potwalloper" title="Potwalloper">potwallopers</a>, giving many residents votes, to <a href="/wiki/Rotten_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotten borough">rotten boroughs</a> with hardly any voters. <span id="county_borough"></span> A <i>county borough</i> was the constituency of a <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">county corporate</a>, combining the franchises of both county and borough. Until 1950 there were also <a href="/wiki/University_constituency" title="University constituency">university constituencies</a>, which gave graduates an additional representation.\n</p><p>Similar distinctions applied in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Commons" title="Irish House of Commons">Irish House of Commons</a>, while the non-university elected members of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a> were called Shire Commissioners and Burgh Commissioners.  After the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a>, Scottish burghs were grouped into <a href="/wiki/District_of_burghs" title="District of burghs">districts of burghs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">Parliament of Great Britain</a>, except that <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Edinburgh (UK Parliament constituency)">Edinburgh was a constituency in its own right</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union 1800</a>, smaller Irish boroughs were disenfranchised, while most others returned only one MP to the United Kingdom Parliament.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a> reduced the number of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary borough">parliamentary boroughs</a> by eliminating the rotten boroughs. It also divided larger counties into two two-seat <span id="Division"></span><i>divisions</i>, the boundaries of which were defined in the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Boundaries_Act_1832" title="Parliamentary Boundaries Act 1832">Parliamentary Boundaries Act 1832</a>, and gave seven counties a third member.  The <a href="/wiki/Redistribution_of_Seats_Act_1885" title="Redistribution of Seats Act 1885">Redistribution of Seats Act 1885</a> equalised the population of constituencies; it split larger boroughs into multiple single-member constituencies, reduced smaller boroughs from two seats each to one, split each two-seat county and division into two single-member constituencies, and each three-seat county into single-member constituencies.\n</p><p>The House of Commons (Redistribution of Seats) Act 1958, eliminated the previous common electoral quota for the whole United Kingdom and replaced it with four separate national minimal seat quotas for the respective Boundaries commissions to work to, as a result the separate national electoral quotas came into effect: England 69,534; Northern Ireland 67,145, Wales 58,383 and in Scotland only 54,741 electors.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_constituencies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Voting_System_and_Constituencies_Act_2011" title="Parliamentary Voting System and Constituencies Act 2011">Parliamentary Voting System and Constituencies Act 2011</a> gives the Boundary Commissions for <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_for_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission for England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_for_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission for Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_for_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission for Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_for_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission for Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> the power to create names for constituencies, and does not provide a set of statutory guidelines for the Commissions to follow in doing so.\n</p><p>Constituency names are geographic, and "should normally reflect the main population centre(s) contained in the constituency". <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_direction" title="Cardinal direction">Compass points</a> are used to distinguish constituencies from each other when a more suitable label cannot be found. Where used, "The compass point reference used will generally form a prefix in cases where the rest of the constituency name refers to the county area or a local council, but a suffix where the rest of the name refers to a population centre." This is the reason for the difference in naming between, for example, <a href="/wiki/North_Shropshire_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="North Shropshire (UK Parliament constituency)">North Shropshire</a> (a county constituency) and <a href="/wiki/Reading_West_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Reading West (UK Parliament constituency)">Reading West</a> (a borough constituency).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="House_of_Commons_constituencies">House of Commons constituencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_constituencies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: House of Commons constituencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 United Kingdom general election</a>, the House of Commons had <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_Kingdom_Parliament_constituencies" class="mw-redirect" title="List of United Kingdom Parliament constituencies">646 constituencies</a> covering the whole of the United Kingdom. This rose to 650 in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 election</a>. Each constituency elects one <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP) by the "<a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting" title="First-past-the-post voting">first-past-the-post</a>" system of election.\n</p><p>The House of Commons is one of the two chambers of the <a href="/wiki/Bicameralism" title="Bicameralism">bicameral</a> <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>, the other being the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_Building" title="Scottish Parliament Building">Scottish Parliament Building</a> is in the <a href="/wiki/Holyrood_(Edinburgh)" class="mw-redirect" title="Holyrood (Edinburgh)">Holyrood</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, while the main meeting place of the Parliament of the United Kingdom is the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>.\n</p><p>There are 73 Holyrood constituencies covering <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and each elects one <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Member of the Scottish Parliament">Member of the Scottish Parliament</a> (MSP) by the <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting" title="First-past-the-post voting">first-past-the-post system</a>. Also, the constituencies are grouped into eight electoral regions, and each of these regions elects seven <a href="/wiki/Additional_member_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Additional member system">additional members</a>, to produce a form or degree of <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">mixed-member proportional representation</a>.\n</p><p>The existing constituencies were created, effectively, for the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_election,_1999" title="Scottish Parliament election, 1999">first general election</a> of the Scottish Parliament, in 1999. When created, all but two had the names and boundaries of Westminster constituencies. The two exceptions were the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_(Scottish_Parliament_constituency)" title="Orkney (Scottish Parliament constituency)">Orkney Holyrood constituency</a>, covering the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands_council_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands council area">Orkney Islands council area</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Shetland_(Scottish_Parliament_constituency)" title="Shetland (Scottish Parliament constituency)">Shetland Holyrood constituency</a>, covering the <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands_council_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Shetland Islands council area">Shetland Islands council area</a>. For Westminster elections, these <a href="/wiki/Council_areas_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Council areas of Scotland">council areas</a> were covered (and still are covered) by the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_and_Shetland_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Orkney and Shetland (UK Parliament constituency)">Orkney and Shetland Westminster constituency</a>.\n</p><p>In 1999, under the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the expectation was that there would be a permanent link between the boundaries of Holyrood constituencies and those of Westminster constituencies. This link was broken, however, by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_(Constituencies)_Act_2004" title="Scottish Parliament (Constituencies) Act 2004">Scottish Parliament (Constituencies) Act 2004</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> which enabled the creation of a new set of Westminster constituencies without change to Holyrood constituencies. The new Westminster boundaries became effective for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">United Kingdom general election, 2005</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_Assembly_constituencies"><span id="Wales"></span> Welsh Assembly constituencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_constituencies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Welsh Assembly constituencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are 40 <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_constituencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly constituencies">Welsh Assembly constituencies</a> covering <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and each elects one <a href="/wiki/Assembly_Member_(Welsh_Assembly)" class="mw-redirect" title="Assembly Member (Welsh Assembly)">Assembly Member</a> (AM) by the <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting" title="First-past-the-post voting">first-past-the-post system</a>. Also, the constituencies are grouped into five electoral regions, and each of these regions elects four <a href="/wiki/Additional_member_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Additional member system">additional members</a>, to produce a form or degree of <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">mixed-member proportional representation</a>.\n</p><p>The current set of Assembly constituencies is the second to be created. The first was created for the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_election,_1999" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly election, 1999">first general election</a> of the Assembly, in 1999.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Parliament_constituencies">European Parliament constituencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_constituencies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: European Parliament constituencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The United Kingdom elects its <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">Members of the European Parliament (MEPs)</a> through twelve multimember <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_constituency" title="European Parliament constituency">constituencies</a>. One, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="Northern Ireland (European Parliament constituency)">Northern Ireland</a>, uses <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a>, while the eleven covering <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> use the <a href="/wiki/D%27Hondt_method" title="D&#39;Hondt method">d\'Hondt method</a> of <a href="/wiki/Party-list_proportional_representation" title="Party-list proportional representation">party-list proportional representation</a>.\n</p><p>For its first <a href="/wiki/Elections_to_the_European_Parliament" title="Elections to the European Parliament">European Parliamentary elections</a> in <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1979_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 1979 (United Kingdom)">1979</a> Great Britain was divided into a number of single-member <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting" title="First-past-the-post voting">first-past-the Post</a> constituencies, matching the way Westminster MPs are elected. Following the decision that all MEPs should be elected by some form of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> <a href="/wiki/Blair_ministry" title="Blair ministry">government</a> passed the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliamentary_Elections_Act_1999" title="European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999">European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999</a>, creating the current eleven constituencies on Great Britain, which were first used in <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1999_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 1999 (United Kingdom)">1999</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/South_West_England_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="South West England (European Parliament constituency)">South West England constituency</a> was expanded from the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2004_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 2004 (United Kingdom)">2004 elections</a> onward to include <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, the only <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territory" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territory">British overseas territory</a> that is part of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>,  following a court case.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
