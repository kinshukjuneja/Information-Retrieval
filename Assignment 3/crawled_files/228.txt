The lines represents the 20 <a href="/wiki/Gal_(unit)" title="Gal (unit)">mGal</a> <a href="/wiki/Bouguer_anomaly" title="Bouguer anomaly">Bouguer anomaly</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Cornubian batholith</b> is a large mass of <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> rock, formed about 280 million years ago, which lies beneath much of the south-western peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. The main exposed masses of granite are seen at <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Moor" title="Bodmin Moor">Bodmin Moor</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Austell" title="St Austell">St Austell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carnmenellis" title="Carnmenellis">Carnmenellis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land&#39;s End">Land\'s End</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>. The intrusion is associated with significant quantities of <a href="/wiki/Minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerals">minerals</a> particularly <a href="/wiki/Cassiterite" title="Cassiterite">cassiterite</a>, an ore of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> which has been mined since about 2000 BC. Other minerals include <a href="/wiki/China_clay" class="mw-redirect" title="China clay">china clay</a> and ores of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>.\n</p><p>It takes its name from <i>Cornubia</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> name for <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent_and_geometry">Extent and geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extent and geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SW_England_Bouguer_gravity_anomaly_map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/SW_England_Bouguer_gravity_anomaly_map.png/320px-SW_England_Bouguer_gravity_anomaly_map.png" width="320" height="290" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7d/SW_England_Bouguer_gravity_anomaly_map.png 1.5x" data-file-width="371" data-file-height="336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SW_England_Bouguer_gravity_anomaly_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bouguer gravity anomaly map over southwest England showing the linear negative anomalies associated with the Cornubian batholith and the Haig Fras granite</div></div></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">batholith</a> is a large mass of <a href="/wiki/Intrusive_rock" title="Intrusive rock">intrusive rock</a> formed from the crystallization of molten rock beneath the Earth\'s surface (<a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a>). From <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">gravity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth&#39;s magnetic field">magnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysical data</a>, the batholith is interpreted to extend from about 8\xc2\xb0W, more than 100&#160;km southwest of the Isles of Scilly, to the eastern edge of Dartmoor. The negative gravity anomaly, caused by the relatively low density of the granites compared to average <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>, is linear and trends WSW-ENE, parallel with that associated with the <a href="/wiki/Haig_Fras" title="Haig Fras">Haig Fras</a> granite.<sup id="cite_ref-Edwards_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The shape of the <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">batholith</a> and the relationship between the individual <a href="/wiki/Pluton" title="Pluton">plutons</a> and the main mass of the granite remained entirely speculative until gravity data began to be used to constrain the thickness and shape of the batholith by modelling. The initial work by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Bott" title="Martin Bott">Martin Bott</a> suggested that the batholith had an overall trapezoidal shape with a base to the batholith at about 10&#8211;12&#160;km. However, current understanding of granite pluton shape suggest that most are either <a href="/wiki/Laccolith" title="Laccolith">laccolithic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lopolith" title="Lopolith">lopolithic</a>. Comparisons with other examples would suggest that the thicknesses of the individual plutons would be in the range 3&#8211;5&#160;km, based on their outcrop widths.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The volume of the batholith was estimated in 1989 to be around 68,000 cubic kilometres.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hound_Tor,_Manaton_(4265).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hound_Tor%2C_Manaton_%284265%29.jpg/220px-Hound_Tor%2C_Manaton_%284265%29.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hound_Tor%2C_Manaton_%284265%29.jpg/330px-Hound_Tor%2C_Manaton_%284265%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hound_Tor%2C_Manaton_%284265%29.jpg/440px-Hound_Tor%2C_Manaton_%284265%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hound_Tor,_Manaton_(4265).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hound Tor on Dartmoor showing horizontal and vertical joints in the granite</div></div></div>\n<p>The Cornubian batholith formed during the early <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> period, from about 300 to 275 <a href="/wiki/Year#SI_prefix_multipliers" title="Year">Ma</a> (million years ago) at a late stage in the mountain-building event known as the <a href="/wiki/Variscan_orogeny" title="Variscan orogeny">Variscan orogeny</a> as a suite of late <a href="/wiki/Orogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Orogenic">orogenic</a> granites. It has been suggested that stretching of the Earth\'s crust (<a href="/wiki/Crustal_extension" class="mw-redirect" title="Crustal extension">crustal extension</a>) allowed the granitic magmas to move to higher levels in the crust. Evidence from <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> isotopes suggests that the magmas that formed the batholith were mainly the result of <a href="/wiki/Partial_melting" title="Partial melting">partial melting</a> of the lower <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> with a minor component of basaltic magma from a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> source. This lower crustal source is likely to have consisted of both <a href="/wiki/Metasedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Metasedimentary">metasedimentary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metavolcanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Metavolcanic">metavolcanic</a> rocks, of <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic</a> age (2500 million to 542 million years old).<sup id="cite_ref-Darbyshire_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Darbyshire-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As the molten rock cooled to around 1000 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">celsius</a> it solidified and crystallised and fractures along vertical joints formed. Over time the slate and sandstone rocks covering the granite were eroded exposing the granite in areas such as Dartmoor and Bodmin Moor. The granite also expanded and horizontal joints were formed. These joints are most clearly seen on exposed pieces of rock such as the <a href="/wiki/Tor_(rock_formation)" title="Tor (rock formation)">Tors</a> of Dartmoor and Bodmin Moor. As the granite erodes further, blocks of eroded granite known as clitter are left.<sup id="cite_ref-hesketh_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hesketh-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timing">Timing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Timing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An approximate age of emplacement for the Cornubian batholith was known before <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a> methods became commonplace, from the observed relationships with the <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> rocks - both those which formed before the intrusion and those which were laid down after it. The youngest rocks into which the granites intrude are the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> Crackington and Bealsmill formations of <a href="/wiki/Namurian" title="Namurian">Namurian</a> to lower <a href="/wiki/Westphalian_(stage)" title="Westphalian (stage)">Westphalian</a> age.<sup id="cite_ref-Page_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Page-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Page2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Page2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This provides a lower bound for the timing of granite emplacement of about 310 Ma.\n</p><p>The earliest instance of granitic <a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">clasts</a> (fragments of the granite which have weathered away and become part of a new sedimentary rock) in younger sedimentary sequences is from the Late Permian <a href="/wiki/Exeter_Group" title="Exeter Group">St. Cyres beds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This gives an upper bound of about 250 Ma.\n</p><p>The inferred age of emplacement from this evidence of Late Carboniferous to Early Permian has been confirmed by radiometric dating, although it has shown that the individual intrusions were emplaced over a significant time interval. The earliest dated major intrusion is the Carnmenellis pluton at 293.1\xc2\xb13 Ma. The youngest dated granite is the southern lobe of the Land\'s End pluton, intruded at 274.5\xc2\xb11.4 Ma.<sup id="cite_ref-Chen_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The earliest recorded magmatic activity is the intrusion of the small Hemerdon pluton on the southwestern flank of the Dartmoor pluton at 298.3\xc2\xb12.3 Ma. There is no apparent systematic variation in age of plutons compared to their position within the batholith. This suggests that the batholith grew by the coalescence of a series of separate intrusions over a period of about 25 Ma.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism_of_emplacement">Mechanism of emplacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mechanism of emplacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As with all large intrusive bodies, the method by which the batholith\'s <a href="/wiki/Methods_of_pluton_emplacement" title="Methods of pluton emplacement">plutons were emplaced</a> is a matter of debate, due to the space problem of adding such large masses to the upper crust.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Four main mechanisms have been proposed; <a href="/wiki/Stoping_(geology)" title="Stoping (geology)">stoping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diapirism" class="mw-redirect" title="Diapirism">diapirism</a>, extensional faulting and uplift of the overlying <a href="/wiki/Country_rock_(geology)" title="Country rock (geology)">country rock</a> over a laccolithic <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sill</a> with a relatively small vertical <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dyke</a> feeder.\n</p><p>Evidence for a stoping mechanism has been described locally from the margin of the Tregonning intrusion, where a series of intrusive sheets extend out from the roof zone of the intrusion into the country-rock.<sup id="cite_ref-Leboutillier_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leboutillier-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Although the Land\'s End pluton was once thought to have a diapiric origin, its emplacement is now interpreted to have been accommodated by fault movements during regional extension.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plutons">Plutons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a comprise the Cornubian Batholith can be broadly subdivded into five main lithologies: Two mica, Muscovite, Biotite, Tourmaline and Topaz granites, each named for their distinguishing mineral(s) <sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chappell_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chappell-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stone_2000_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stone_2000-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>. The two-mica and muscovite granites are older, and can be found in the Carnmenellis, Bodmin and Isles of Scilly, whereas the younger biotite and tourmaline granites are found within the Land\'s End, St. Austell and Dartmoor plutons. Topaz granites outcrop within the Tregonning, Land\'s End and St Austell plutons.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dartmoor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This is the largest exposed area of granite which also forms the easternmost development of the batholith. The granite consists of two main types, coarse-grained granite with abundant large <a href="/wiki/Alkali_feldspar" title="Alkali feldspar">alkali feldspar</a> <a href="/wiki/Megacryst" title="Megacryst">megacrysts</a> and coarse-grained granite with few megacrysts. To the southwest there is an area of coarse-grained granite with small megacrysts and several small exposures of fine-grained granite, particularly in the southeastern part of the outcrop.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Interpretation of the gravity field over this pluton suggests that it is\nsheet-like with a thickness of nearly 10&#160;km, and a root at its southern end extending down to about 17&#160;km depth, which may represent the conduit that carried the magma to shallow crustal levels. It appears to have been intruded along the interface between Devonian and Carboniferous rocks.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Uranium-lead_dating" class="mw-redirect" title="Uranium-lead dating">Uranium-lead dating</a> of <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">Monazite</a> from this intrusion gives emplacement ages of 278.2\xc2\xb10.8 Ma and 280.4\xc2\xb11.2 Ma.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bodmin_Moor">Bodmin Moor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bodmin Moor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Bodmin Moor pluton consists mainly of coarse-grained granite with abundant small megacrysts. Towards the centre and the western margin of the outcrop there are smaller bodies of fine-grained granite.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Gravity data suggest that this pluton is in the form of a south-southeastward thickening wedge, reaching a maximum of about 7&#160;km.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Monazite from this pluton gives an emplacement age of 291.4\xc2\xb10.8 Ma.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="St_Austell">St Austell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: St Austell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The St Austell pluton consists of coarse-grained megacrystic granite with large megacrysts at the western and eastern ends of the outcrop. The central part of the pluton is also coarse-grained but lacks the megacrysts. Between the central megacryst poor and the large megacryst rich coarse-grained granites at the western end, a medium-grained granite is developed with lithium-mica. Smaller bodies of fine-grained granite are found in the central part of the outcrop and at the western end.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Gravity data indicate that this pluton is wedge-shaped similar to that at Bodmin.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Monazite gives an emplacement age of 281.8\xc2\xb10.4 Ma for this pluton.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carnmenellis">Carnmenellis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Carnmenellis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Carnmenellis pluton and the smaller intrusion of Carn Brea appear to be part of a single intrusive body. The central part of the Carnmenellis outcrop is a medium-grained granite with few megacrysts. The bulk of the main outcrop and the Carn Brea and Carn Marth masses consist of coarse-grained megacrystic granite with small megacrysts. Small bodies of fine-grained granite are found towards the west of the Carnemellis outcrop. The shape of this pluton is interpreted to be a sheet about 3&#160;km thick with a near central root extending down to about 7&#160;km depth.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Boreholes at <a href="/wiki/Rosemanowes" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosemanowes">Rosemanowes</a> that extend to over 2.5&#160;km deep have shown very little variation in the <a href="/wiki/Petrography" title="Petrography">petrographic</a> composition of the granite with depth in this pluton.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Monazite gives an emplacement age of 293.7\xc2\xb10.6 Ma for this pluton.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tregonning-Godolphin">Tregonning-Godolphin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tregonning-Godolphin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Tregonning Granite and Godolphin Granite are two distinct granite bodies on the southern coast of Cornwall. The Tregonning Granite is mainly a medium-grained lithium-mica granite with a body of fine-grained granite developed towards the northwest of the outcrop.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> . It has a unique chemistry and differs from the granite found within the Carnmenellis and Land\'s End granites, likely forming in a different manner <sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>. The Godolphin Granite is mineralogically and chemically similar to the nearby Carnmenellis Granite, although is finer grained.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Land.27s_End"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Land\'s_End">Land\'s End</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Land&#039;s End">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Land\'s End pluton is mainly a coarse-grained granite with abundant large megacrysts. There is an area in the centre which is poor in megacrysts and there are several small and medium-sized masses of fine-grained granite throughout the outcrop.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Dating has been carried out on <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a> and monazite samples, from a fine-grained granite and the main coarse-grained granite respectively. These give emplacement ages of 279.3\xc2\xb10.4 Ma for the fine-grained granite and 274.8\xc2\xb10.5 Ma for the main phase granite. This difference is consistent with fine-grained granite being a roof-pendant to the coarse-grained main phase granite intrusion.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isles_of_Scilly">Isles of Scilly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Isles of Scilly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg/220px-Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg/330px-Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg/440px-Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tresco_-_aerial_photo6_edit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Looking across Tresco, the second-largest of the Scilly Isles</div></div></div>\n<p>The Isles of Scilly all have granite bedrock. The dominant rock type is a megacrystic biotite granite, although the megacrysts are relatively small. In the centre of the pluton a medium-grained granite is developed with few megacrysts, more tourmaline and less biotite than the main variety.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stone_1989_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stone_1989-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Monazite from this pluton suggest an emplacement age of 290.3\xc2\xb10.6 Ma.<sup id="cite_ref-Chen_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haig_Fras">Haig Fras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Haig Fras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Haig_Fras#Geology" title="Haig Fras">Haig Fras \xc2\xa7&#160;Geology</a></div>\n<p>This submarine outcrop, 45&#160;km in length, lies 95&#160;km northwest of the Scilly Isles, rising at one point to 38&#160;m below sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-JNCC_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JNCC-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In contrast to most granites of the Cornubian batholith the granites here are fine to medium-grained and generally lack megacrysts. It was intruded at 277 Ma and is considered most likely to be a separate but related intrusive body that runs parallel to the Cornubian batholith.<sup id="cite_ref-Edwards_1991_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards_1991-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jones_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_intrusions">Other intrusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other intrusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Minor granitic intrusions are present throughout the peninsula.  In some cases granitic bodies have been recognised from the mineralization above them, even if the intrusion itself has not been encountered.\n</p><p>A series of minor intrusions are found within the country rock and the granites themselves. Common types are <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aplite" title="Aplite">aplites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elvan" title="Elvan">elvans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UCL_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCL-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mullis_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullis-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineralogy_and_chemistry_of_the_granite_and_other_rocks">Mineralogy and chemistry of the granite and other rocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mineralogy and chemistry of the granite and other rocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Granite">Granite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Granite">edit</a><span href="/wiki/Luxullianite" title="Luxullianite">Luxullianite</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tourmaline" title="Tourmaline">tourmalinized</a> granite</div></div></div>\n<p>The main rock forming the batholith is <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, which formed when <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> cooled slowly, covered by 2\xe2\x80\x933,000 metres of slate and sandstone. The slow cooling gave time for crystals to form in the granite which are large enough to see with the naked eye, giving it a granular appearance. These grains are mainly of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biotite" title="Biotite">biotite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hesketh_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hesketh-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-westwood_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-westwood-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The granite is generally coarse grained and in some places very coarse-grained, or <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatitic</a> (grains larger than 3&#160;cm). Large <a href="/wiki/Phenocrysts" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenocrysts">phenocrysts</a>, several centimetres long, of K-feldspar, are a distinctive feature.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The chemistry and mineralogy of the granites vary from one location to another, but they are all classified on the <a href="/wiki/Granite#Chappell_&amp;_White_classification_system" title="Granite">Chappell &amp; White classification</a> as being <i>S-type</i>, which means that they are ultimately derived from <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> <a href="/wiki/Protolith" title="Protolith">protolith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chappell_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chappell-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineralogy">Mineralogy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mineralogy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Cornubian intrusions are mostly made up of two-<a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a> granite (containing both <a href="/wiki/Muscovite" title="Muscovite">muscovite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biotite" title="Biotite">biotite</a>). <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">Li</a>-mica granite forms a less common type found only in the St. Austell pluton and some smaller intrusions.<sup id="cite_ref-Chappell_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chappell-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Many of the granites contain large alkali feldspar phenocrysts.<sup id="cite_ref-Dangerfield_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dangerfield-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In some locations the original granite has been modified to form a <a href="/wiki/Tourmaline" title="Tourmaline">tourmaline</a>-bearing granite called <a href="/wiki/Luxullianite" title="Luxullianite">luxullianite</a>. This tourmalinisation occurred during the late stages of cooling of the granite, as feldspar and mica were partly replaced by tourmaline.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The two-mica granites are strongly <a href="/wiki/Peraluminous_rock" title="Peraluminous rock">peraluminous</a> \xe2\x80\x93 they have a high ratio of aluminium oxide to sodium and potassium oxides. They also have a low ratio of sodium to potassium and overall high level of alkalis. The granites are highly enriched in <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> and moderately so in <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a>, tin, <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>. Given the overall chemistry, the levels of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> are also high. Strontium, <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a> and the elements from <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> are relatively depleted. This chemistry is consistent with partial melting of a source consisting of <a href="/wiki/Greywacke" title="Greywacke">greywackes</a> (a variety of sandstone). The conditions under which the melts formed is modelled to be temperature of 770&#160;\xc2\xb0C and a <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">confining pressure</a> of 50 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chappell_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chappell-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Differences in chemistry have been identified between an earlier group of plutons (Isles of Scilly, Carnmenellis &amp; Bodmin Moor) and a later group (Land\'s End, St Austell &amp; Dartmoor). The early group of granites contain more aluminium than the later set and have steeper slopes on plots of <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a> against <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>. The later group contain more common basic microgranite <a href="/wiki/Xenolith" title="Xenolith">xenoliths</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stone_2000_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stone_2000-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The granites are generally rich in ammonium compared to average granites worldwide. There is also considerable variation between the individual plutons, with an average of 11 <a href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> for Dartmoor compared to 94 ppm for Bodmin Moor. The concentration of ammonium in these granites correlates well with both their initial <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">87</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Sr</span>/<span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">86</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Sr</span> ratios and their <a href="/wiki/Peraluminous" class="mw-redirect" title="Peraluminous">peraluminosity</a>. The relatively high ammonium content is interpreted to indicate that the granites were derived from a sedimentary protolith, or have been contaminated from such a source after emplacement to high crustal levels.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_metamorphic_and_metasomatic_rocks">Associated metamorphic and metasomatic rocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Associated metamorphic and metasomatic rocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Around the edges of many of the plutons the country rocks have been transformed by heat in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Contact_metamorphism" class="mw-redirect" title="Contact metamorphism">contact metamorphism</a>. The effects of this can be seen up to a distance of 4 miles from the granite in an area called the metamorphic aureole. The effect of this process depends on the type of rocks which were heated and their distance from the intrusion. Fine grained sedimentary rocks were transformed into <a href="/wiki/Hornfels" title="Hornfels">hornfels</a> and minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Amphibole" title="Amphibole">amphibole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a>. At greater distances from the plutons, the only evidence of metamorphism is spotting in these rocks. <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">Garnet</a> developed in <a href="/wiki/Calcareous" title="Calcareous">calcareous</a> rocks as well as <a href="/wiki/Amphibole" title="Amphibole">amphibole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a>. The metamorphosis of  <a href="/wiki/Greenschist" title="Greenschist">greenstones</a> has generally led to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Hornblende" title="Hornblende">hornblende</a>-<a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">plagioclase</a> rocks.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A feature of the granites of the batholith is the high concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">volatile</a> components. Fluids rich in these have strongly affected the country rock and locally the granites themselves in a process called metasomatism. The first phase recognised is alkali metasomatism (where alkaline components are enhanced), which occurred within and at the margins of the granite. <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">Potassium</a>\xe2\x80\x93<a href="/wiki/Metasomatism" title="Metasomatism">metasomatism</a> was followed by <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>\xe2\x80\x93metasomatism. Finally acid metasomatism (enrichment of acid components and depletion of alkalis) took place leading to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Greisen" title="Greisen">greisen</a> and tourmalines.<sup id="cite_ref-Pirajno_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pirajno-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_formation">Mineral formation</span><span Moor china clay pit showing <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_mining" title="Hydraulic mining">hydraulic mining</a> in progress</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Cornwall_and_Devon" title="Mining in Cornwall and Devon">Mining in Cornwall and Devon</a></div>\n<p>Large <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> deposits are found in the vicinity of the batholith and these have been mined for thousands of years. The area has been famous for its tin since about 2000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Emsley_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The minerals formed when fluids escaped along fractures in the hot granite as it cooled and are typically found in <a href="/wiki/Vein_(geology)" title="Vein (geology)">veins</a> or washed into streams to form <a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hesketh_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hesketh-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The mineral deposits are associated with multiple <a href="/wiki/Lode" title="Lode">lodes</a> and fractures that dip steeply and cut across both the granites and the country rocks. The ore-bearing lodes are up to several kilometres long and are 0.5 to 3m wide on average.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stages_of_mineralization">Stages of mineralization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Stages of mineralization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are four recognised stages of <a href="/wiki/Mineralization_(geology)" title="Mineralization (geology)">mineralization</a> associated with different conditions as the granite slowly cooled. Each stage is associated with different temperatures, economic deposits of different metals and different <a href="/wiki/Gangue" title="Gangue">gangue</a> minerals. The first three stages have been connected to the intrusion and cooling of the batholith, while the fourth stage may have been driven by the heat production associated with <a href="/wiki/Radioactive" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> materials in the granite.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Stage 1 \xe2\x80\x93 exoskarns&#160;</dt>\n<dd>The earliest phase of mineralization occurred during the intrusion of the granite. Hot water from the magma which was rich in silica, iron, aluminium, and magnesium mixed and dissolved <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metabasalt" class="mw-redirect" title="Metabasalt">metabasalts</a>, and converted them into <a href="/wiki/Skarn" title="Skarn">exoskarns</a> in a process called <a href="/wiki/Metasomatism" title="Metasomatism">metasomatism</a>, where the chemical composition of rocks is changed by hot water or other fluids.  Typical minerals formed at this time include <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">garnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epidote" title="Epidote">epidote</a>, chlorine-rich <a href="/wiki/Amphiboles" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphiboles">amphiboles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malayaite" title="Malayaite">malayaite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vesuvianite" title="Vesuvianite">vesuvianite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siderite" title="Siderite">siderite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Axinite" title="Axinite">axinite</a>. The skarns may contain economic amounts of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>. These minerals were formed at about the same time as the crystallisation of the pluton they are associated with. Temperatures associated with this stage  were 375 \xe2\x80\x93 450&#160;\xc2\xb0C.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Stage 2 \xe2\x80\x93 greisen bordered veins and tourmaline veins and breccias&#160;</dt>\n<dd>The second recognised phase of mineralization involved the granite being transformed by high-temperature late magmatic fluids rich in volatiles to form <a href="/wiki/Greisen" title="Greisen">greisen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tourmaline" title="Tourmaline">tourmaline</a>. Veins of <a href="/wiki/Cassiterite" title="Cassiterite">cassiterite</a> (tin oxide) and <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolframite</a> (a mineral containing iron, manganese and tungsten) are found associated with the greisens, the former deposited by high salinity, low <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, fluids and the latter by low salinity, high <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> fluids. <a href="/wiki/Muscovite" title="Muscovite">Muscovites</a> within the greisens give cooling ages similar to magmatic muscovites in the relevant granite.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Stage 3 \xe2\x80\x93 the main phase of mineralization&#160;</dt>\n<dd>The third and main phase of mineralization occurred at a later stage, and at lower temperatures (200 \xe2\x80\x93 400&#160;\xc2\xb0C) as the granite intrusion cooled. Fluids circulating within the country rock leached out tin, copper and arsenic and deposited them in veins which generally lie on an east-west axis. The typical fill of these veins is <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>-tourmaline-<a href="/wiki/Chlorite_group" title="Chlorite group">chlorite</a>-sulfide-<a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a>, with tin, copper, lead, zinc, iron and arsenic sulfides. This mineralization is 25&#8211;40 Ma later than the intrusion age for the Carnmenellis pluton. These veins are the main economically useful source of minerals.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Stage 4 \xe2\x80\x93 crosscourses&#160;</dt>\n<dd>The fourth and final stage of mineralization was the lowest temperature phase (100 \xe2\x80\x93 170&#160;\xc2\xb0C) and is associated with veins containing multiple metallic elements (lead, zinc, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>). The veins lie on a north-south or northwest-southeast axis and are known as \'crosscourses\' because they cross-cut the earlier E-W trending veins. Gangue minerals include quartz, <a href="/wiki/Barite" class="mw-redirect" title="Barite">barite</a> and fluorite. Studies on quartz <a href="/wiki/Fluid_inclusions" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluid inclusions">fluid inclusions</a> have shown that the fluids causing this stage are similar in composition to deep sedimentary <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brines</a> which are rich in sodium, calcium and chlorine. This brine came from Permo-Triassic sedimentary rocks that once covered the entire area. These rocks are still preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Approaches" title="Southwest Approaches">Southwest Approaches</a> to the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>. Evidence of seawater influence suggests that the mineralization did not start until the Late <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> marine incursion at the earliest. The high heatflow from the granites helped drive fluid circulation.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gleeson_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gleeson-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China_clay">China clay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornubian_batholith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: China clay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png/220px-SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png/330px-SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png/440px-SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png 2x" data-file-width="3194" data-file-height="1900" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SW_England_China_Clay_Pits.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite image of south-west England showing lighter areas that mark the location of china clay workings (labelled)</div></div></div>\n<p>Large economic deposits of <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">china clay</a> are found in places across the south-west peninsula, in particular Lee Moor on the western edge of Dartmoor and the St Austell district.<sup id="cite_ref-sw-regional_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sw-regional-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The china clay was formed by the alteration of feldspars in a process known as kaolinization. There is still debate about the origin and age of these deposits but they are commonly thought to arise from the circulation of <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_water" title="Meteoric water">meteoric water</a> (water from rain or snow) at a late stage during cooling of the batholith.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesley_10-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesley-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> One theory is that the kaolinization resulted from intense <a href="/wiki/Supergene_(geology)" title="Supergene (geology)">supergene</a> <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> in a tropical to warm
