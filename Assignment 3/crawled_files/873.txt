this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>High Court of Justiciary</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Courts_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Supreme Courts of Scotland">supreme</a> <a href="/wiki/Scottish_criminal_law" title="Scottish criminal law">criminal court</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The High Court is both a <a href="/wiki/Court_of_first_instance" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of first instance">trial court</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Court_of_appeal" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of appeal">court of appeal</a>. As a trial court, the High Court sits on circuit at <a href="/wiki/Parliament_House,_Edinburgh" title="Parliament House, Edinburgh">Parliament House</a> or the former Sheriff Court building in Edinburgh, or in dedicated buildings in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>. The High Court sometimes sits in various smaller towns in Scotland, where it uses the local <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheriff Court">sheriff court</a> building. As an appeal court the High Court sits only in Edinburgh.\n</p><p>On one occasion the High Court of Justiciary sat outside Scotland, at <a href="/wiki/Zeist" title="Zeist">Zeist</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103_bombing_trial" title="Pan Am Flight 103 bombing trial">Pan Am Flight 103 bombing trial</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Court_in_the_Netherlands" title="Scottish Court in the Netherlands">Scottish Court in the Netherlands</a>. At Zeist the High Court sat both as a trial court, and an appeal court for the initial appeal by <a href="/wiki/Abdelbaset_al-Megrahi" title="Abdelbaset al-Megrahi">Abdelbaset al-Megrahi</a>.\n</p><p>The president of the High Court is the <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Court_of_Session" title="Lord President of the Court of Session">Lord Justice General</a>, who holds office <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_officio_member" title="Ex officio member">ex officio</a></i> by virtue of being <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Court_of_Session" title="Lord President of the Court of Session">Lord President of the Court of Session</a>, and his depute is the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_Clerk" title="Lord Justice Clerk">Lord Justice Clerk</a>. The remaining judges are the <a href="/wiki/Senator_of_the_College_of_Justice" title="Senator of the College of Justice"><i>Lords Commissioners of Jusiticiary</i></a>, who hold office <i>ex officio</i> by virtue of being appointed as <a href="/wiki/Senator_of_the_College_of_Justice" title="Senator of the College of Justice">Senators of the College of Justice</a> and judges of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a>. As a court of first instance <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_jury_in_Scotland" title="Trial by jury in Scotland">trials are usually heard with a jury of 15</a> and a single Lord Commissioner of Justiciary; the jury can <a href="/wiki/Conviction" title="Conviction">convict</a> on a majority verdict. In some cases, such as the trial of <a href="/wiki/Abdelbaset_al-Megrahi" title="Abdelbaset al-Megrahi">Abdelbaset al-Megrahi</a>&#160;and&#160;<a href="/wiki/Lamin_Khalifah_Fhimah" title="Lamin Khalifah Fhimah">Lamin Khalifah Fhimah</a> for the bombing of <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103" title="Pan Am Flight 103">Pan Am Flight 103</a>, a trial can be heard by a <a href="/wiki/Bench_(law)" title="Bench (law)">bench</a> of judges alone; sitting without a jury.\n</p><p>As an appeal court the hearings are always without a jury, with two judges sitting to hear an appeal against <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">sentence</a>, and three judges sit to hear an appeal against <a href="/wiki/Conviction" title="Conviction">conviction</a>. The High Court will hear appeals from the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff courts</a> of Scotland where the trial was under <a href="/wiki/Solemn_proceedings" title="Solemn proceedings">solemn proceedings</a>; the High Court will also hear referrals on <a href="/wiki/Point_of_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Point of law">points of law</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_Appeal_Court" title="Sheriff Appeal Court">Sheriff Appeal Court</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Summary_offence" title="Summary offence">summary proceedings</a> in the Sheriff courts and <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_peace_court" title="Justice of the peace court">justice of the peace court</a>s.\n</p><p>Cases can be remitted to the High Court by the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff courts</a> after <a href="/wiki/Conviction" title="Conviction">conviction</a> for <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">sentencing</a>, where a sheriff believes that their sentencing powers are inadequate. The High Court can impose a <a href="/wiki/Life_sentence" class="mw-redirect" title="Life sentence">life sentence</a> but the sheriff has a limit of five years sentencing; both can issue an unlimited <a href="/wiki/Fine_(penalty)" title="Fine (penalty)">fine</a>.\n</p><p>As of May 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_justice-general" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord justice-general">Lord Justice General</a> was <a href="/wiki/Colin_Sutherland,_Lord_Carloway" title="Colin Sutherland, Lord Carloway">Lord Carloway</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_Clerk" title="Lord Justice Clerk">Lord Justice Clerk</a> was <a href="/wiki/Leeona_Dorrian,_Lady_Dorrian" title="Leeona Dorrian, Lady Dorrian">Lady Dorrian</a>, and there were a total of 35 Lords Commissioners of Justiciary.\n</p>\n<div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Justiciar">Justiciar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Justiciar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The origins derive from the <a href="/wiki/Justiciar" title="Justiciar">Justiciar</a> and <a href="/wiki/College_of_Justice" title="College of Justice">College of Justice</a>, as well as from the medieval <a href="/wiki/Noble_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble court">royal courts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barony_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Barony court">barony courts</a>. The medieval <a href="/wiki/Justiciar" title="Justiciar">Justiciar</a> (royal judge) took its name from the justices who originally travelled around Scotland hearing cases on circuit or \'ayre\'. From 1524, the Justiciar or a depute was required to have a "permanent base" in Edinburgh.<sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_journal_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eb_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Scottish_monarchs" title="List of Scottish monarchs">King of Scots</a> sometimes sat in judgment of cases in the early King\'s Court, and it appears that appeals could be taken from the King\'s Court to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a> in civil cases but not in criminal ones. In 1532 the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Justice" title="College of Justice">College of Justice</a> was founded, separating <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal</a> jurisdiction between two distinct courts. The King\'s Court was, however, normally the responsibility of the Justiciar. The Justiciar normally appointed several deputes to assist in the administration of justice, and to preside in his absence.  A legally qualified <a href="/wiki/Clerk_of_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Clerk of court">clerk</a> advised the Justiciar and his deputes as they were generally <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Scotland" title="Peerage of Scotland">noblemen</a> and often not <a href="/wiki/Legal_education" title="Legal education">legally qualified</a>. This clerk prepared all the <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictments</a> and was keeper of the records. Eventually the influence of the clerk increased until the clerk gained both a vote in the court, and a seat on the <a href="/wiki/Bench_(law)" title="Bench (law)">bench</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_Clerk" title="Lord Justice Clerk">Justice-Clerk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_journal_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-administration_journal_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-administration_journal-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Courts_Act_1672">Courts Act 1672</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Courts Act 1672">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The High Court in its modern form was founded in 1672 by the Courts Act 1672,<sup id="cite_ref-act1672_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act1672-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> when five of the <i><a href="/wiki/Senator_of_the_College_of_Justice" title="Senator of the College of Justice">Lords of Session</a></i> (judges of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a>) were added as permanent judges of the <i>Justice Court</i>. Previously the Lord Justice General had appointed deputes to preside in the court. From 1672 to 1887, the High Court consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Justice General">Lord Justice General</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_Clerk" title="Lord Justice Clerk">Lord Justice Clerk</a>, and five <a href="/wiki/Senator_of_the_College_of_Justice" title="Senator of the College of Justice">Lords of Session</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_journal_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Court Act 1672 also gave statutory effect to the position of the Lord Justice Clerk, and the Lord Justice-General was made president of the Court, and the Justice-Clerk vice-president. During the period when the office of Lord Justice-General was held by noblemen the Lord Justice-Clerk was virtual head of the Justiciary Court.<sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_journal_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaty_of_Union">Treaty of Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treaty of Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Article XIX of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union" title="Treaty of Union">Treaty of Union</a> that united Scotland and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> into <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> preserved the High Court of Justiciary, though now the High Court was subject to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">Parliament of Great Britain</a> which could enact "...regulations for the better administration of Justice". Dominic Scullion, writing in the <i>Aberdeen Student Law Review</i> in 2010, identified that the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Union of England and Scotland</a> saw an increase in references to <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a> and cases in the reports of the High Court. However, Scullion identified that it was only in the latter half of the 20th Century that the judgments of the High Court were directly influenced by English decisions and <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-scullion_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scullion-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The High Court of Justiciary remained the final authority on all matters of <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a> after the Act of Union, though the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">Parliament of Great Britain</a> appears to have had appellate jurisdiction through the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">judicial functions of the House of Lords</a> this appeared to have little effect in practice.<sup id="cite_ref-scullion_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scullion-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_journal_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1713 a case (<i>Magistrates of Elgin v. Ministers of Elgin</i>) was heard by the House of Lords which overturned a decision of the High Court. However, in 1781 the House of Lords resolved that there could be no appeal from the High Court, as no right of appeal had existed beyond the Court beyond the Treaty of Union.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_Century">19th Century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 19th Century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unification_of_judiciary">Unification of judiciary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unification of judiciary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1830 the Court of Session Act 1830 united the offices of Lord President of the Court of Session and Lord Justice General, with the person appointed as Lord President assuming the office of Lord Justice General <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_officio" class="mw-redirect" title="Ex officio">ex officio</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-session_act_1830_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-session_act_1830-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1834 the five <a href="/wiki/Senator_of_the_College_of_Justice" title="Senator of the College of Justice">Lords of Session</a> who were appointed as Lords Commissioners of Justiciary were paid an additional allowance of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\xc2\xa3</a>600 over their basic salary of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\xc2\xa3</a>2,000.<sup id="cite_ref-hansard_sinclair_1834_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hansard_sinclair_1834-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wages_1834_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wages_1834-11">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Select_committee_(United_Kingdom)" title="Select committee (United Kingdom)">Select Committee</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> was appointed to investigate the <a href="/wiki/Remuneration" title="Remuneration">remuneration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Working_time" title="Working time">working conditions</a> of the Lords of Session and Lords Commissioners of Justiciary. The Select Committee recommended that all the Lords of Session should be made Lords Commissioners of Justiciary and that the additional allowance be abolished. At the same time the Committee recommended that the basic salary of a Senator be increased to <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\xc2\xa3</a>3,000.<sup id="cite_ref-report_judges_salaries_1834_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-report_judges_salaries_1834-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The membership of the court remained unchanged until 1887 when all of the <a href="/wiki/Senator_of_the_College_of_Justice" title="Senator of the College of Justice">Senators of the College of Justice</a> were made Lords Commissioners of Justiciary, by the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_act_1887_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_act_1887-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Writing in 1896, Charles Pearson attested that no appeal was competent from the High Court to the House of Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-administration_journal_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-administration_journal-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Supremacy_of_High_Court">Supremacy of High Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Supremacy of High Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The House of Lords made a final determination in the case of <i>Mackintosh v. Lord Advocate (1876) 2 App.&#160;Cas. 41</i>&#160;that it had no jurisdiction over criminal appeals, as it had inherited the power of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a> to hear civil appeals, but that the pre-union Parliament did not have any jurisdiction to hear criminal appeals.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_Century">20th Century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 20th Century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span id="Criminal_Procedure_.28Scotland.29_Act_1995"></span><span class="mw-headline" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1913, Edwin Keedy, writing in the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Institute_of_Criminal_Law_and_Criminology" class="mw-redirect" title="Journal of the American Institute of Criminal Law and Criminology">Journal of the American Institute of Criminal Law and Criminology</a></i>, would affirm that the High Court "is the Supreme Court for the trial of criminal causes".<sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_journal_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The supremacy of the High Court was affirmed by Section 124 of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995, which stated:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...every interlocutor and sentence pronounced by the High Court under this Part of this Act shall be final and conclusive and not subject to review by any court whatsoever...</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Section 124, Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_devolution">Scottish devolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Scottish devolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Scottish_devolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish devolution">Scottish devolution</a> and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a> introduced the right to refer <a href="/wiki/Points_of_law" Privy Council</a>. Such points of law related to <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">compatibility issues</a> or related to <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution issues</a>. Devolution issues are concerned with the legislative competence of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and the executive functions of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-criminaljustice_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criminaljustice-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-practice_direction10_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-practice_direction10-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_Century">21st Century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 21st Century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_Court_in_the_Netherlands">Scottish Court in the Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Scottish Court in the Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>From May 2000 until March 2002 the High Court of Justiciary sat as the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Court_in_the_Netherlands" title="Scottish Court in the Netherlands">Scottish Court in the Netherlands</a> to try <a href="/wiki/Abdelbaset_al-Megrahi" title="Abdelbaset al-Megrahi">Abdelbaset al-Megrahi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lamin_Khalifah_Fhimah" title="Lamin Khalifah Fhimah">Lamin Khalifah Fhimah</a> for the bombing of <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103" title="Pan Am Flight 103">Pan Am Flight 103</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103_bombing_trial" title="Pan Am Flight 103 bombing trial">Pan Am Flight 103 bombing trial</a> required a <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Government of the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_Netherlands" title="Cabinet of the Netherlands">Government of the Kingdom of the Netherlands</a> which created <a href="/wiki/Extraterritoriality" title="Extraterritoriality">extraterritoriality</a> for the Scottish Court, with <a href="/wiki/Camp_Zeist" class="mw-redirect" title="Camp Zeist">Camp Zeist</a> in <a href="/wiki/Utrecht_(province)" title="Utrecht (province)">Utrecht</a> (a disused <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force base</a>) made a subject of <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-panam_treaty_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-panam_treaty-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Legal effect was given to the treaty in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> by the High Court of Justiciary Order 1998, an <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a>. The order empowered the Lord Justice Clerk to appoint three Lords Commissioners of Justiciary to sit as <a href="/wiki/Bench_trial" title="Bench trial">bench trial</a> as both <a href="/wiki/Trier_of_fact" title="Trier of fact">trier of fact</a> and for determining any <a href="/wiki/Points_of_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Points of law">points of law</a>. The High Court had full authority to determine <a href="/wiki/Contempt_of_court" title="Contempt of court">contempt of court</a> relating to the proceedings.<sup id="cite_ref-SI_1998_2251_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SI_1998_2251-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the conviction, which was upheld on appeal of Abdelbaset al-Megrahi, the Scottish Court in the Netherlands ceased to sit. Subsequent appeals were heard in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The jurisdiction of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in human rights and devolution issues was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-reformact2005_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformact2005-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Such a transfer was not without controversy, as commentators, including the <a href="/wiki/Law_Society_of_Scotland" title="Law Society of Scotland">Law Society of Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Advocate_General_for_Scotland" title="Advocate General for Scotland">Advocate General for Scotland</a>, noted that this notionally placed an <a href="/wiki/English_court" class="mw-redirect" title="English court">English court</a> in a position of superiority to the High Court.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 2013, the Supreme Court\'s guidance on its jurisdiction over Scottish appeals stated that:<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The changes to the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, first, in Scottish criminal cases as a result of the Scotland Act 2012 (\xe2\x80\x98the 2012 Act\xe2\x80\x99) which ensured that the High Court of Justiciary retained the power ultimately to resolve cases once the Supreme Court has determined the legal question at issue</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>The Jurisdiction of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom in Scottish Appeals</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Section 35 of the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_2012" title="Scotland Act 2012">Scotland Act 2012</a> modified the procedure for referrals by removing the ability of the Supreme Court to determine the final judgment of the case; in essence a criminal case cannot be <i>remitted</i> to the Supreme Court. The Scotland Act 2012 requires that once the point of law has been decided upon by the Supreme Court, it is for the High Court to resolve the case. An issue can be referred to the Supreme Court either by the Lords Commissioners of Justiciary who are presiding, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Advocate" title="Lord Advocate">Lord Advocate</a>, or the Advocate General for Scotland. Though where two or more Lords Commissioners are presiding they may determine the human rights issue without referral to the Supreme Court.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remit_and_jurisdiction">Remit and jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Remit and jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_instance_jurisdiction">First instance jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: First instance jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The High Court has jurisdiction over all crimes in Scotland unless restricted by statute. The High Court has <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_jurisdiction" title="Exclusive jurisdiction">exclusive jurisdiction</a> over serious crimes such as <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a> and, in practice, deals with <a href="/wiki/Armed_robbery" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed robbery">armed robbery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drug_trafficking" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug trafficking">drug trafficking</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">sexual offences involving children</a> (over which it <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_jurisdiction" title="Concurrent jurisdiction">shares jurisdiction</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff court</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-criminaljustice_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criminaljustice-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Court_entrance_-_geograph.org.uk_-_495852.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Court_entrance_-_geograph.org.uk_-_495852.jpg/230px-Court_entrance_-_geograph.org.uk_-_495852.jpg" width="230" height="182" class="thumbimage" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Court_entrance_-_geograph.org.uk_-_495852.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Court entrance the High Court of Justiciary situated at the <a href="/wiki/Saltmarket" title="Saltmarket">Saltmarket</a> in Glasgow</div></div></div>\n<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Trial_court" title="Trial court">court of first instance</a> the court sits regularly in various places in Scotland, with permanent seats in Edinburgh, Glasgow and Aberdeen. There are sittings when required in <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton" title="Dumbarton">Dumbarton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanark" title="Lanark">Lanark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livingston,_West_Lothian" title="Livingston, West Lothian">Livingston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paisley,_Renfrewshire" title="Paisley, Renfrewshire">Paisley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-highcourt_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highcourt-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Trials in the High Court are usually <a href="/wiki/Jury_trial" title="Jury trial">jury trials</a>, with a single Lord Commissioner of Justiciary presiding (although two or more judges may sit in important or difficult cases) with a <a href="/wiki/Jury" title="Jury">jury</a> of fifteen individuals; in Scotland this is known as <a href="/wiki/Solemn_proceedings" title="Solemn proceedings">solemn proceedings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-highcourt_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highcourt-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scottish legal system</a>, the jury can convict on a majority <a href="/wiki/Verdict" title="Verdict">verdict</a> of at least eight jurors, and need not return a unanimous verdict. The Scottish legal system also permits a verdict of \'<a href="/wiki/Not_proven" title="Not proven">not proven</a>\' as well as verdicts of \'guilty\' or \'not guilty\'. Juries may add a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/rider">rider to their verdict</a> as additional commentary on their verdict. The \'not proven\' verdict may be given when a jury is not prepared to utter \'not guilty\' or where the jury has lingering doubts; however if eight jurors cannot agree on an accused\'s guilt or on an <a href="/wiki/Alternative_verdict" class="mw-redirect" title="Alternative verdict">alternative verdict</a>, then the accused will be <a href="/wiki/Acquitted" class="mw-redirect" title="Acquitted">acquitted</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jurymanual2016_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jurymanual2016-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cases in the High Court are prosecuted in the <a href="/wiki/Public_interest" title="Public interest">public interest</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Advocate" title="Lord Advocate">Lord Advocate</a>, who is usually represented in such cases by <a href="/wiki/Advocate_Depute" class="mw-redirect" title="Advocate Depute">Advocates Depute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crownoffice_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crownoffice-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Private_prosecution" title="Private prosecution">private prosecution</a> can be brought before the High Court, but this is very rare and difficult as it requires the concurrence of the Lord Advocate and for the High Court to issue a <i>bill for criminal letters</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-crim_proc_journal_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crim_proc_journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>739</span></sup> When families of the victims of the <a href="/wiki/2014_Glasgow_bin_lorry_crash" title="2014 Glasgow bin lorry crash">2014 Glasgow bin lorry crash</a> applied for such a bill, their request was denied by the High Court in 2016 on the basis that there was insufficient evidence. The Lord Justice Clerk, <a href="/wiki/Leeona_Dorrian,_Lady_Dorrian" title="Leeona Dorrian, Lady Dorrian">Lady Dorrian</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Menzies,_Lord_Menzies" title="Duncan Menzies, Lord Menzies">Lord Menzies</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Drummond_Young,_Lord_Drummond_Young" title="James Drummond Young, Lord Drummond Young">Lord Drummond Young</a> further concluded that the case did not present special circumstances to enable granting of the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bail" title="Bail">Bail</a> can be granted by the High Court to any <a href="/wiki/Criminal_charge" title="Criminal charge">accused person</a> and "bail is to be granted to an accused person except where there is good reason for refusing bail." The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bail,_Judicial_Appointments_etc._(Scotland)_Act_2000&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bail, Judicial Appointments etc. (Scotland) Act 2000 (page does not exist)">Bail, Judicial Appointments etc. (Scotland) Act 2000</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Act of the Scottish Parliament">Act of the Scottish Parliament</a>, had removed the previous restrictions on bail that meant that <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a> were not ordinarily bailable.<sup id="cite_ref-act_2000_c9_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act_2000_c9-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> However, a person could be bailed when accused of these of crimes on application of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Advocate" title="Lord Advocate">Lord Advocate</a> or by a decision of the High Court itself.<sup id="cite_ref-south_africa_compare2_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-south_africa_compare2-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Criminal_Proceedings_etc._(Reform)_(Scotland)_Act_2007&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Criminal Proceedings etc. (Reform) (Scotland) Act 2007 (page does not exist)">Criminal Proceedings etc. (Reform) (Scotland) Act 2007</a> did reintroduce restrictions on the granting of bail by requiring exceptional circumstances to be shown when a person is accused of violent, sexual, or drugs offences, and they have a prior conviction for a similar offence.<sup id="cite_ref-act_2007_c6_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act_2007_c6-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Scotland, the focus is normally for those who are opposed to bail to convince the courts that bail should not be granted.<sup id="cite_ref-south_africa_compare2_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-south_africa_compare2-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> With the <a href="/wiki/Procurator_fiscal" title="Procurator fiscal">procurator fiscal</a> given guidance to use the nature and gravity of an offence as grounds to oppose bail.<sup id="cite_ref-regulations_c8_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-regulations_c8-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sentencing_on_conviction_by_sheriff_court">Sentencing on conviction by sheriff court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sentencing on conviction by sheriff court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following a conviction under solemn proceedings in a <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff court</a>, Section 195 of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 allows a sheriff to remit the case to the High Court for sentencing, should the sheriff believes their powers of sentencing to be inadequate for the crime committed.<sup id="cite_ref-act_1995_c46_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act_1995_c46-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A sheriff in solemn proceedings can impose a maximum sentence of up to 5 years <a href="/wiki/Imprisonment" title="Imprisonment">imprisonment</a> or an unlimited <a href="/wiki/Fine_(penalty)" title="Fine (penalty)">fine</a>, and the High Court can impose a <a href="/wiki/Life_sentence" class="mw-redirect" title="Life sentence">life sentence</a> (unless a lesser maximum sentence is prescribed by statute) as well as an unlimited fine. Once a case is remitted, the High Court can treat the case as if it had been tried before a Lord Commissioner of Justiciary.<sup id="cite_ref-sheriffs_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheriffs-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-criminaljustice_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criminaljustice-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span Edinburgh</div></div></div>\n<p>Leave to appeal is granted by a Lord Commissioner of Justiciary in <a href="/wiki/Chambers_(law)" title="Chambers (law)">chambers</a> under sections 106 and 107 of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 when a person is convicted in <a href="/wiki/Solemn_procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="Solemn procedure">solemn procedure</a> in either the High Court or <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff courts</a>, with the High Court sitting as the Appeal Court.<sup id="cite_ref-act_1995_c46_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act_1995_c46-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>Sections 106 and 107</span></sup>\n</p><p>Appeals against convictions or sentence in <a href="/wiki/Summary_offence" title="Summary offence">summary procedure</a> heard in sheriff courts and <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_peace_courts" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the peace courts">justice of the peace courts</a> are now heard by the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_Appeal_Court" title="Sheriff Appeal Court">Sheriff Appeal Court</a>. However, referrals on <a href="/wiki/Question_of_law" title="Question of law">points of law</a> may be heard in the High Court from the Sheriff Appeal Court with the permission of the High Court. Two judges sit to hear an appeal against sentence, and three judges sit to hear an appeal against conviction. The High Court of Justiciary sits as an appeal court in Edinburgh.<sup id="cite_ref-criminaljustice_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criminaljustice-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-highcourt_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highcourt-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The High Court, as a collegiate court, has the ability to convene a <a href="/wiki/Bench_(law)" title="Bench (law)">bench</a> of greater numbers of Lords Commissioners of Justiciary to overturn decisions and <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a> established by previous appeals. Such a decision is made by the High Court on its own initiative. It is possible for the entire High Court to sit in determination of an appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In exceptional circumstances, a person may petition the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Criminal_Cases_Review_Commission" title="Scottish Criminal Cases Review Commission">Scottish Criminal Cases Review Commission</a>, who have the authority to refer an appeal back to the High Court of Justiciary, if the Commission determine that a miscarriage of justice has or might have occurred.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under Section 35 of the Scotland Act 2012, the High Court as an Appeal Court will also hear referrals on <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">compatibility issues</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_Appeal_Court" title="Sheriff Appeal Court">Sheriff Appeal Court</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff courts</a>, and from cases being heard at first-instance by a single Lord Commissioner of Justiciary. The High Court can then make a determination on that issue, or it can refer the matter to the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appeals_from_the_High_Court">Appeals from the High Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Appeals from the High Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Devolution_and_human_rights_issues">Devolution and human rights issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Devolution and human rights issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The High Court of Justiciary has the final authority on matters of criminal law in Scotland, and thus no appeal beyond the High Court is possible on the grounds of sentence or conviction.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> However, it is possible to refer a <a href="/wiki/Point_of_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Point of law">point of law</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> relating to <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">compatibility issues</a> or relating to <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution issues</a>. Devolution issues are concerned with the legislative competence of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and the executive functions of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-criminaljustice_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criminaljustice-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-practice_direction10_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-practice_direction10-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Such referrals are made to the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom under Schedule 6 of the Scotland Act 1998 or Section 288A of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Criminal_Procedure_(Scotland)_Act_1995&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (page does not exist)">Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-act_1995_c46_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act_1995_c46-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-scotlandact1998_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotlandact1998-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-reformact2005_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformact2005-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> For a referral to proceed permission must be granted by two or more Lords Commissioners of Justiciary, or by the Supreme Court itself.<sup id="cite_ref-practice_direction10_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-practice_direction10-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The most frequent devolution issues raised related to <a href="/wiki/Article_6_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 6</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, which mandates the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_a_fair_trial" title="Right to a fair trial">right to a fair trial</a>, and the role of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Advocate" title="Lord Advocate">Lord Advocate</a> who is both the chief <a href="/wiki/Prosecutor" title="Prosecutor">public prosecutor</a> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>. Under the Scotland Act 1998 the Lord Advocate could do nothing that was incompatible with the European Convention on Human Rights, and should his actions be deemed incomparable then they were <a href="/wiki/Void_(law)" title="Void (law)">null and void</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This led to the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Cadder_v_HM_Advocate" title="Cadder v HM Advocate">Cadder v HM Advocate</a></i> where the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom ruled that <a href="/wiki/Police_Scotland" title="Police Scotland">the police in Scotland</a> could not question a <a href="/wiki/Suspect" title="Suspect">suspect</a> without granting that person access to a <a href="/wiki/Solicitor" title="Solicitor">solicitor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This was one case, along with <i><a href="/wiki/Fraser_v_HM_Advocate" title="Fraser v HM Advocate">Fraser v HM Advocate</a></i>, that led the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> to raise concerns with <a href="/wiki/HM_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="HM Government">HM Government</a> that it appeared that "virtually any objection, challenge, or point of law can be characterised as a devolution issue." Thus undermining the High Court\'s final jurisdiction in criminal matters.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_2012" title="Scotland Act 2012">Scotland Act 2012</a> modified provisions around devolution issues by no longer renderring null and void those actions of the Lord Advocate that were incompatible with the European Convention, but still allowing a right to appeal against those actions on grounds of incompatibility.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom_2">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Supreme Court of the United Kingdom was established by the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a>, and is the highest court in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> for <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil cases</a> and those matters relating to human rights and devolution.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Prior to the establishment of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom devolution issues were decided by the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>, whose members were the <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" title="Lords of Appeal in Ordinary">Lords of Appeal in Ordinary</a> (who exercised the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">judicial functions of the House of Lords</a>.) However, the two bodies were legally and constitutionally separate.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Courts">European Courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: European Courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the most exceptional of circumstances, an appeal may be made to the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> (ECJ) of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Justice_of_the_European_Union" title="Court of Justice of the European Union">Court of Justice of the European Union</a> (CJEU), subject to an application for permission from both the High Court of Justiciary and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Supreme Court">United Kingdom Supreme Court</a> (UKSC) being granted, or to the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECtHR), or both.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acts_of_Adjournal">Acts of Adjournal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Acts of Adjournal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Adjournal" title="Act of Adjournal">Act of Adjournal</a></div>\n<p>The High Court of Justiciary as a Court, or the Lord Justice General, Lord Justice Clerk and Lords Commissioners of Justiciary as a body, have the power to regulate <a href="/wiki/Criminal_procedure" title="Criminal procedure">criminal procedure</a> in the criminal courts in Scotland: regulations can be made for the High Court, sheriff courts (summary and solemn procedures), and the justice of the peace courts. Such regulations are promulgated by <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Adjournal" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Adjournal">Acts of Adjournal</a>, which take the form of <a href="/wiki/Subordinate_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Subordinate legislation">subordinate legislation</a> as <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Statutory_Instrument" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Statutory Instrument">Scottish Statutory Instruments</a>, under powers granted by Section 305 of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-act_1995_c46_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act_1995_c46-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>Section 305</span></sup> Schedule 6 of the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a> also grants that Acts of Adjournal can be used to regulate the procedure for referring a <a href="/wiki/Question_of_law" title="Question of law">question of law</a> relating to a <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution issue</a> to either the High Court or the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-scotlandact1998_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotlandact1998-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>Schedule 6</span></sup>\n</p><p>Section 305 of the 1995 Act states:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>(1) The High Court may by Act of Adjournal\xe2\x80\x94\n</p><dl><dd>(a) regulate the practice and procedure in relation to criminal procedure;</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>(b) make such rules and regulations as may be necessary or expedient to carry out the purposes and accomplish the objects of any enactment (including an enactment in this Act) in so far as it relates to criminal procedure;</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>(c) subject to subsection (5)<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup> below, to fix and regulate the fees payable in connection with summary criminal proceedings; and</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>(d) make provision for the application of sums paid under section 220 of this Act and for any matter incidental thereto.</dd></dl>\n<p>(2) The High Court may by Act of Adjournal modify, amend or repeal any enactment (including an enactment in this Act) in so far as that enactment relates to matters with respect to which an Act of Adjournal may be made under subsection (1) above...\n</p>\n<div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Section 305, <i>Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995</i><sup id="cite_ref-act_1995_c46_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act_1995_c46-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>Section 305</span></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Thus the Lord Justice General, Lord Justice Clerk, and Lords Commissioners of Justiciary have the power to modify and amend <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>, where that primary legislation deals with a matter of criminal procedure. The Criminal Courts Rules Council on 8 February 2016 considered Section 288BA of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (which prescribes rules for <a href="/wiki/Docket_(court)" title="Docket (court)">dockets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictments</a> for <a href="/wiki/Sexual_offences" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual offences">sexual offences</a>) and asked the Lord President\'s Private Office to consider if this could be modified by Act of Adjournal.<sup id="cite_ref-rules-2016-feb_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules-2016-feb-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> A draft Act of Adjournal was also prepared in 2011 to amend the 1995 Act as the Rules Council was awaiting primary legislation, and the Rules Council agreed to proceed with the Act of Adjournal.<sup id="cite_ref-rules-2011-oct_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules-2011-oct-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The Act of Adjournal amended the 1995 Act by adding Sections 75C and 137ZB to enable the court to discharge, vary and change the diet (sittings) of a case.<sup id="cite_ref-adjournal-diets_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adjournal-diets-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rights_of_audience">Rights of audience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Rights of audience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Members of the <a href="/wiki/Faculty_of_Advocates" title="Faculty of Advocates">Faculty of Advocates</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Advocate" title="Advocate">advocates</a> or <a href="/wiki/Counsel" title="Counsel">counsel</a>, and as of 1990 also some <a href="/wiki/Solicitors" class="mw-redirect" title="Solicitors">solicitors</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Solicitor-advocate" class="mw-redirect" title="Solicitor-advocate">solicitor-advocates</a>, have practically exclusive <a href="/wiki/Right_of_audience" class="mw-redirect" title="Right of audience">right of audience</a> rights of audience in the court.<sup id="cite_ref-abouthigh_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abouthigh-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Until 1990 only advocates had any right of audience before the High Court, but the Law Reform (Miscellaneous Provisions) (Scotland Act) 1990 allowed solicitors to apply for enhanced rights and become solicitor-advocates.<sup id="cite_ref-rightsaudience2_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rightsaudience2-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judges_and_office_holders">Judges and office holders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Judges and office holders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="President_and_judges">President and judges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: President and judges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The court\'s <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Justice">president</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Court_of_Session" title="Lord President of the Court of Session">Lord Justice General</a>; the second most senior judge is the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_Clerk" title="Lord Justice Clerk">Lord Justice Clerk</a>; and a further 35 <a href="/wiki/Senators_of_the_College_of_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Senators of the College of Justice">Senators of the College of Justice</a> hold office as Lords Commissioners of Justiciary. The total numbers of judges is fixed by Section 1 of the Court of Session Act 1988, subject to amendment by <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a> (the last order was made in 2016 and increased the number of judges to 35.)<sup id="cite_ref-act1988p1_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act1988p1-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2016-ssi-423_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2016-ssi-423-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Judges are appointed for life, subject to dismissal if they are found unfit for office, and subject to a compulsory retirement age of 75.<sup id="cite_ref-retirement1993_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retirement1993-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The court is a unitary collegiate court, with all judges other than the <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Court_of_Session" title="Lord President of the Court of Session">Lord Justice General</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_Clerk" title="Lord Justice Clerk">Lord Justice Clerk</a> holding the same rank and title: <a href="/wiki/Lord_Commissioner_of_Justiciary" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Commissioner of Justiciary">Lord Commissioner of Justiciary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-highcourt_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highcourt-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> There are 35,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> in addition to a number of temporary judges; these temporary judges can be <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_principal" title="Sheriff principal">sheriffs principal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheriff Court">sheriffs</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Advocate" title="Advocate">advocates</a> in private practice. The judges sit also in the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a>, where they are known as <i>Lords of Council and Session</i>; in the Court of Session the Lord Justice General is called the <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Court_of_Session" title="Lord President of the Court of Session">Lord President of the Court of Session</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Head_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Head-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_Justice_General">Lord Justice General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Lord Justice General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Court_of_Session" title="Lord President of the Court of Session">Lord President of the Court of Session</a></div>\n<p>The Lord Justice General is the most senior judge of the High Court of Justiciary. The Lord Justice General will sit as chairperson in the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Criminal_Appeal" title="Court of Criminal Appeal">Court of Criminal Appeal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-highcourt_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highcourt-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_Justice_Clerk">Lord Justice Clerk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Lord Justice Clerk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lord_Justice_Clerk" title="Lord Justice Clerk">Lord Justice Clerk</a></div>\n<p>The Justice Clerk is the second most senior judge of the High Court, and deputises for the Lord Justice General when the latter is absent, or is unable to fulfil his duties, or when there is a vacancy for Lord Justice General. The Lord Justice Clerk will sit as chairperson in the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Criminal_Appeal" title="Court of Criminal Appeal">Court of Criminal Appeal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-highcourt_26-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highcourt-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lords_Commissioners_of_Justiciary">Lords Commissioners of Justiciary</span><span Mulholland\n</td>\n<td>Lord Commissioner of Justiciary\n</td>\n<td>2016\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appointment">Appointment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Appointment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>To be eligible for appointment as a Lord Commissioner of Justiciary, or temporary judge, a person must have served at least 5 years as <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_principal" title="Sheriff principal">sheriff principal</a>; or been an <a href="/wiki/Advocate" title="Advocate">advocate</a> for 5 years, or a <a href="/wiki/Solicitor" title="Solicitor">solicitor</a> with 5 years rights of audience before the Court of Session or High Court of Justiciary; or been a <a href="/wiki/Writer_to_the_Signet" class="mw-redirect" title="Writer to the Signet">Writer to the Signet</a> for 10 years (having passed the exam in civil law at least 2 years before application.)<sup id="cite_ref-senator_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-senator-59">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eligibility_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eligibility-60">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Appointments are made by the <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">First Minister of Scotland</a> on the recommendation of the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Appointments_Board_for_Scotland" title="Judicial Appointments Board for Scotland">Judicial Appointments Board for Scotland</a>. The Judicial Appointments Board has statutory authority to make recommendations under Sections 9 to 27 of the Judiciary and Courts (Scotland) Act 2008 (as amended by the Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014).<sup id="cite_ref-act2008board_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act2008board-61">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Appointments to the Inner House are made by the Lord President and Lord Justice Clerk, with the consent of the Scottish Ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-act1988p1_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act1988p1-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Temporary judges can also be appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_ministers" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish ministers">Scottish Ministers</a> provided that person would other be eligible for appointment as permanent judge of the High Court. Originally the power was granted by Section 35 of the Law Reform (Miscellaneous Provisions) (Scotland) Act 1990,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> but the enactment was repealed and replaced by Section 123 of the Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014. Such temporary judges are appointed for a period of 5 years.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-63">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Section 123 of Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014 allows the Lord Justice General to appoint former Senators, and <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">former Justices of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>, to the High Court provided they are under 75 years of age. The tenure of such appointments is determined by the Lord Justice General.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-63">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Brian_Gill,_Lord_Gill" title="Brian Gill, Lord Gill">Lord Gill</a>, Lord Justice General from 2012\xe2\x80\x932015, issued guidance in 2013 on the use temporary judges which stipulated that:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Temporary judges will be used only where there are, for reasons of a temporary nature, an insufficient number of permanent judges to meet the demands of business and the Lord President has approved their use.</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Lord Gill, <i>Guidelines for the Use of Temporary Judges</i> (2013)</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Further stating that the preference would be to allocate business to temporary judges who were already, had previously been, a <i>judicial office holder</i> (namely, <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_principal" title="Sheriff principal">sheriff principal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">sheriff</a>); as opposed to using temporary judges who were practising <a href="/wiki/Faculty_of_Advocates" title="Faculty of Advocates">advocates</a> or <a href="/wiki/Law_Society_of_Scotland" title="Law Society of Scotland">solicitor-advocates</a>. Lord Gill\'s guidance allows for such judges to be allocated to any first instance business of the High Court, but requires the approval of the Lord Justice General for their deployment in the Appeal Court.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Removal_from_office">Removal from office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Removal from office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Lord Justice General, Lord Justice Clerk and the Lords Commissioners of Justiciary can only be removed office after a tribunal has been convened to examine their fitness for office. The tribunal is convened at the request of the Lord Justice General (in his capacity as Lord President,) or in other circumstances if the First Minister sees fit. However, the First Minister must consult the Lord Justice General (or the Lord Justice Clerk, if the Lord Justice Generalis under investigation). Should the tribunal recommend their dismissal the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> can resolve that the First Minister make a recommendation to <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">the Monarch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-removal_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-removal-65">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-independence_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independence-66">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_Clerk_of_Session_and_Justiciary">Principal Clerk of Session and Justiciary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Principal Clerk of Session and Justiciary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The administration of the court is part of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Courts_and_Tribunals_Service" title="Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service">Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service</a>, and is led by the <a href="/wiki/Principal_Clerk_of_Session_and_Justiciary" title="Principal Clerk of Session and Justiciary">Principal Clerk of Session and Justiciary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The Principal Clerk is responsible for the administration of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Courts_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Supreme Courts of Scotland">Supreme Courts of Scotland</a> and their associated staff. As of  April&#160;2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=High_Court_of_Justiciary&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> the Principal Clerk was Graeme Marwick, who was also Director of the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service.<sup id="cite_ref-currentclerk_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-currentclerk-68">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
