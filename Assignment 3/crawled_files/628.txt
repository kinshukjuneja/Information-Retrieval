title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> each having their own systems of <a href="/wiki/Publicly_funded_healthcare" class="mw-redirect" title="Publicly funded healthcare">publicly funded healthcare</a>, funded by and accountable to separate governments and parliaments, together with smaller private sector and voluntary provision. As a result of each country having different policies and priorities, a variety of differences now exist between these systems.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite there being separate health services for each country, the performance of the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_(England)" title="National Health Service (England)">National Health Service</a> (NHS) across the UK can be measured for the purpose of making international comparisons. In a 2014 report by the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Fund" title="Commonwealth Fund">Commonwealth Fund</a> ranking developed-country healthcare systems, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> was ranked the best healthcare system in the world overall and in the following categories: Quality of Care (i.e. effective, safe, coordinated, patient-oriented), Access to Care, Efficiency, and Equity.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The UK\'s <a href="/wiki/Palliative_care" title="Palliative care">palliative care</a> has also been ranked as the best in the world by the <a href="/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit" title="Economist Intelligence Unit">Economist Intelligence Unit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, in 2005-09 cancer survival rates lagged ten years behind the rest of Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> although survival rates continue to increase.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2015, the UK was 14th (out of 35) in the annual <a href="/wiki/Euro_health_consumer_index" title="Euro health consumer index">Euro health consumer index</a>. It was criticised for its poor accessibility and "an autocratic top-down management culture".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The index has in turn been criticized by academics, however.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The total expenditure on healthcare as a proportion of GDP in 2013 was 8.5%, below the OECD average of 8.9% and considerably less than comparable economies such as France (10.9%), Germany (11.0%), Netherlands (11.1%), Switzerland (11.1%) and the USA (16.4%).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The percentage of healthcare provided directly by the state is higher than most European countries, which have insurance-based healthcare with the state providing for those who cannot afford insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Common features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Each NHS system uses <a href="/wiki/General_Practitioner" class="mw-redirect" title="General Practitioner">General Practitioners</a> (GPs) to provide <a href="/wiki/Primary_healthcare" title="Primary healthcare">primary healthcare</a> and to make referrals to further services as necessary. Hospitals then provide more specialist services, including care for patients with psychiatric illnesses, as well as direct access to <a href="/wiki/Accident_and_Emergency" class="mw-redirect" title="Accident and Emergency">Accident and Emergency</a> (A&amp;E) departments. Community pharmacies are privately owned but have contracts with the relevant health service to supply prescription drugs.\n</p><p>The public healthcare system also provides free (at the point of service) ambulance services for emergencies, when patients need the specialist transport only available from ambulance crews or when patients are not fit to travel home by public transport. These services are generally supplemented when necessary by the voluntary ambulance services (<a href="/wiki/British_Red_Cross" title="British Red Cross">British Red Cross</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews_Ambulance_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="St Andrews Ambulance Association">St Andrews Ambulance Association</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_John_Ambulance" title="St John Ambulance">St John Ambulance</a>). In addition, patient transport services by air are provided by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Ambulance_Service" title="Scottish Ambulance Service">Scottish Ambulance Service</a> in Scotland and elsewhere by county or regional air ambulance trusts (sometimes operated jointly with local police helicopter services<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>) throughout England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In specific emergencies, emergency air transport is also provided by naval, military and air force aircraft of whatever type might be appropriate or available on each occasion,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and dentists can only charge NHS patients at the set rates for each country. Patients opting to be treated privately do not receive any NHS funding for the treatment. About half of the income of dentists in England comes from work sub-contracted from the NHS,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> however not all dentists choose to do NHS work.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_medicine">Private medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Private medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Private_medicine_in_the_UK" title="Private medicine in the UK">Private medicine in the UK</a></div>\n<p>Private medicine, where patients, or their insurers, pay for treatment in the UK is a niche market. Some is provided by NHS hospitals.  Private providers also contract with the NHS, especially in England, to provide treatment for NHS patients, particularly in mental health and planned surgery. \n</p><p>Patients also go abroad for treatment. In 2014 about 48,000 went abroad for treatment and about 144,000 in 2016.  This may be driven by increasing waiting times for NHS treatment, but will also include migrants who may return to their home country for treatment, especially childbirth.  It also includes fertility services, dentistry and cosmetic surgery which may not be available on the NHS.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> See <a (\xc2\xa3122.5 billion<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> in 2017-18).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commissioning">Commissioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commissioning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In April 2013, under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_Act_2012" title="Health and Social Care Act 2012">Health and Social Care Act 2012</a>, a reorganisation of the NHS took place regarding the administration of the NHS. <a href="/wiki/Primary_care_trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary care trust">Primary care trusts</a> (PCTs) and <a href="/wiki/Strategic_health_authority" title="Strategic health authority">strategic health authorities</a> (SHAs) were abolished, and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Clinical_commissioning_group" title="Clinical commissioning group">clinical commissioning groups</a> (CCGs).<sup id="cite_ref-structure2013_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-structure2013-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> CCGs now commission most of the hospital and community NHS services in the local areas for which they are responsible.<sup id="cite_ref-CCGoutcomes_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCGoutcomes-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Commissioning involves deciding what services a population is likely to need, and ensuring that there is provision of these services.<sup id="cite_ref-NHS_CB_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHS_CB-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The CCGs are overseen by <a href="/wiki/NHS_England" title="NHS England">NHS England</a>, formally known as the NHS Commissioning Board (NHS CB) which was established on 1 October 2012 as an executive non-departmental public body.<sup id="cite_ref-NHS_CB_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHS_CB-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> NHS England also has the responsibility for commissioning primary care services - <a href="/wiki/General_Practitioners" class="mw-redirect" title="General Practitioners">General Practitioners</a>, opticians and <a href="/wiki/NHS_dentistry" title="NHS dentistry">NHS dentistry</a>, as well as some specialised hospital services. Services commissioned include general practice physician services (most of whom are private businesses working under contract to the NHS), community nursing, local clinics and mental health services.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Provider trusts are NHS bodies delivering health care service. They are involved in agreeing major capital and other health care spending projects in their region.<sup id="cite_ref-structure2013_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-structure2013-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/NHS_trust" title="NHS trust">NHS trusts</a> are care deliverers  which spend money allocated to them by CCG\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Secondary care (sometimes termed acute health care) can be either elective care or emergency care and providers may be in the public or private sector.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare_in_Northern_Ireland">Healthcare in Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Healthcare in Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland">Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland</a></div>\n<p>The biggest part of healthcare in Northern Ireland is provided by <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland">Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland</a>. Though this organization does not use the term \'National Health Service\', it is still sometimes referred to as the \'NHS\'.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare_in_Scotland">Healthcare in Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Healthcare in Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Scotland" title="Healthcare in Scotland">Healthcare in Scotland</a></div>\n<p>The majority of healthcare in Scotland is provided by <a href="/wiki/NHS_Scotland" title="NHS Scotland">NHS Scotland</a>; Scotland\'s current national system of publicly funded healthcare was created in 1948 at the same time as those in Northern Ireland and in England and Wales, incorporating and expanding upon services already provided by local and national authorities as well as private and charitable institutions. It remains a separate body from the other public health systems in the United Kingdom although this is often not realised by patients when "cross-border" or emergency care is involved due to the level of co-operation and co-ordination, occasionally becoming apparent in cases where patients are repatriated by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Ambulance_Service" title="Scottish Ambulance Service">Scottish Ambulance Service</a> to a hospital in their country of residence once essential treatment has been given but they are not yet fit to travel by non-ambulance transport.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare_in_Wales">Healthcare in Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Healthcare in Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Wales" title="Healthcare in Wales">Healthcare in Wales</a></div>\n<p>The majority of healthcare in Wales is provided by <a href="/wiki/NHS_Wales" title="NHS Wales">NHS Wales</a>. This body was originally formed as part of the same <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS structure</a> for <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> created by the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_Act_1946" title="National Health Service Act 1946">National Health Service Act 1946</a> but powers over the NHS in Wales came under the Secretary of State for Wales in 1969<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and, in turn, responsibility for NHS Wales was passed to the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly">Welsh Assembly</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly Government">Welsh Assembly Government</a> under devolution in 1999.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_between_the_healthcare_systems_in_the_United_Kingdom">Comparisons between the healthcare systems in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Comparisons between the healthcare systems in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences">Differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Differences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Telephone_advisory_services">Telephone advisory services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Telephone advisory services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Each NHS system has developed ways of offering access to non-emergency medical advice. People in England<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and Scotland can access these services by dialling the free-to-call <a href="/wiki/NHS_111" title="NHS 111">111</a> number. Scotland\'s service is run by <a href="/wiki/NHS24" class="mw-redirect" title="NHS24">NHS24</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The telephone number for <a href="/wiki/NHS_Direct_Wales" title="NHS Direct Wales">NHS Direct Wales</a>/Galw Iechyd Cymru<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> is 0845 4647, but this service intends to offer access through the 111 number from some point in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Best_practice_and_cost_effectiveness">Best practice and cost effectiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Best practice and cost effectiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In England and Wales, the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Health_and_Clinical_Excellence" class="mw-redirect" title="National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence">National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence</a> (NICE) sets guidelines for medical practitioners as to how various conditions should be treated and whether or not a particular treatment should be funded. These guidelines are established by panels of medical experts who specialise in the area being reviewed.\n</p><p>In Scotland, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Medicines_Consortium" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Medicines Consortium">Scottish Medicines Consortium</a> advises NHS Boards there about all newly licensed medicines and formulations of existing medicines as well as the use of antimicrobiotics but does not assess vaccines, branded generics, non-prescription-only medicines (POMs), blood products and substitutes or diagnostic drugs. Some new drugs are available for prescription more quickly than in the rest of the United Kingdom. At times this has led to complaints.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_control">Cost control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cost control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Audit_Office_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Audit Office (United Kingdom)">National Audit Office</a> reports annually on the summarised consolidated accounts of the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_(England)" title="National Health Service (England)">NHS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Audit_Scotland" title="Audit Scotland">Audit Scotland</a> performs the same function for <a href="/wiki/NHS_Scotland" title="NHS Scotland">NHS Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since January 2007, the NHS have been able to claim back the cost of treatment, and for ambulance services, for those who have been paid personal injury compensation.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parking_charges">Parking charges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Parking charges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Parking charges at hospitals have been abolished in Scotland (except for 3 PFI hospitals)<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and have also been abolished in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Parking charges continue to be in place at many hospitals in England.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Prescribed_drugs">Prescribed drugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Prescribed drugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In a sample of 13 developed countries the UK was 9th in its population weighted usage of medication in 14 classes in both 2009 and 2013. The drugs studied were selected on the basis that the conditions treated had high incidence, prevalence and/or mortality, caused significant long-term morbidity and incurred high levels of expenditure and significant developments in prevention or treatment had been made in the last 10 years. The study noted considerable difficulties in cross border comparison of medication use.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> no longer have <a href="/wiki/Prescription_charges" title="Prescription charges">Prescription charges</a>. However, in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, a prescription charge of \xc2\xa38.60 is payable per item as of  April 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  though patients under 16 years old (16\xe2\x80\x9318 years if still in full-time education) or over 60 years getting prescribed drugs are exempt from paying as are people with certain medical conditions, those on low incomes or in receipt of certain benefits, and those prescribed drugs for contraception.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">UK permanent residents</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> do not pay the real cost of the medicines and so for some prescribed medicines that can be bought over the counter without a prescription, for example aspirin, it can be much cheaper to purchase these without a prescription. <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">UK permanent residents</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> who must pay can (instead of paying for each medical item individually) purchase a three-month Prescription Prepayment Certificate (PPC) costing \xc2\xa329.10. This saves the patient money where the patient needs three or more items in three months. There is also a 12-month PPC certificate costing \xc2\xa3104.00 which saves patient\'s money if 12 or more items are needed in 12 months. There are no prescription charges anywhere in the UK for medicines administered at a hospital, by a doctor or at an NHS walk-in centre.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_private_sector_in_public_healthcare">Role of private sector in public healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Role of private sector in public healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>From the birth of the NHS in 1948, <a href="/wiki/Private_medicine_in_the_UK" title="Private medicine in the UK">private medicine</a> has continued to exist, paid for partly by private insurance. Provision of private healthcare acquired by means of private health insurance, funded as part of an employer funded healthcare scheme or paid directly by the customer, though provision can be restricted for those with conditions such as <a href="/wiki/AIDS" class="mw-redirect" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>/<a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In recent years, despite some evidence that a large proportion of the public oppose such involvement,<sup id="cite_ref-BMA_2007_survey_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMA_2007_survey-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> the private sector has been used to increase NHS capacity. In addition, there is some relatively minor sector crossover between public and private provision with it possible for some NHS patients to be treated in private healthcare facilities<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and some NHS facilities let out to the private sector for privately funded treatments or for pre- and  post-operative care.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> However, since private hospitals tend to manage only routine operations and lack a level 3 critical care unit (or <a href="/wiki/Intensive_therapy_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Intensive therapy unit">intensive therapy unit</a>), unexpected emergencies may lead to the patient being transferred to an NHS hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When the <a href="/wiki/Blair_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Blair government">Blair government</a> expanded the role of the private sector within the NHS in England,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> the Scottish government reduced the role of the private sector within public healthcare in Scotland<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and planned legislation to prevent the possibility of private companies running GP practices in future.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Later, however in an attempt to comply with the Scottish Treatment Time Guarantee, a 12-week target for inpatient or day-case patients waiting for treatment, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Lothian" title="NHS Lothian">NHS Lothian</a> spent \xc2\xa311.3 million on private hospital treatment for NHS patients in 2013-14.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_and_performance_of_healthcare_since_devolution">Funding and performance of healthcare since devolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_care_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Funding and performance of healthcare since devolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In January 2010 the <a href="/wiki/Nuffield_Trust" title="Nuffield Trust">Nuffield Trust</a> published a comparative study of NHS performance in England and the devolved administrations since <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a>, concluding that while Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland have had higher levels of funding per capita than England, with the latter having fewer doctors, nurses and managers per head of population, the English NHS is making better use of the resources by delivering relatively higher levels of activity, crude productivity of its staff, and lower waiting times.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> However, the Nuffield Trust quickly issued a clarifying statement in which they admitted that the figures they used to make comparisons between Scotland and the rest of the United Kingdom were inaccurate due to the figure for medical staff in Scotland being overestimated by 27 per cent.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Using revised figures for medical staffing, Scotland\'s ranking relative to the other devolved nations on crude productivity for medical staff changes, but there is no change relative to England.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The Nuffield Trust study was comprehensively criticised by the BMA which concluded "whilst the paper raises issues which are genuinely worth debating in the context of devolution, these issues do not tell the full story, nor are they unambiguously to the disadvantage of the devolved countries. The emphasis on policies which have been prioritised in England such as maximum waiting times will tend to reflect badly on countries which have prioritised spending increases in other areas including non-health ones.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 2014 the Nuffield Trust produced a further comparative report "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nuffieldtrust.org.uk/compare-UK-health">The four health systems of the UK: How do they compare?</a>" which concluded that despite the widely publicised policy differences there was little sign that any one country was moving ahead of the others consistently across the available indicators of performance. It also complained that there was an increasingly limited set of comparable data on the four health systems of the UK which made comparison difficult.\n</p><p>In February 2016 the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> published a review which concluded that performance of the NHS in Wales was little different from that in the rest of the UK. They described performance across the UK as "fairly mediocre" saying that great policies were not being translated into great practices.  They suggested that GPs should be more involved in health boards and that resources should be shifted out of hospitals.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
