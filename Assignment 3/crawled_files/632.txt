The score forms part of the film\'s <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">soundtrack</a>, which also usually includes pre-existing music, <a href="/wiki/Dialogue" title="Dialogue">dialogue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sound_effects" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound effects">sound effects</a>, and comprises a number of orchestral, instrumental, or choral pieces called <a href="/wiki/Music_cue" class="mw-redirect" title="Music cue">cues</a>, which are timed to begin and end at specific points during the film in order to enhance the dramatic narrative and the emotional impact of the scene in question.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Scores are written by one or more <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composers</a>, under the guidance of, or in collaboration with, the film\'s <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">director</a> or <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">producer</a> and are then usually performed by an ensemble of musicians&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 most often comprising an orchestra or band, instrumental soloists, and choir or vocalists&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 and recorded by a <a href="/wiki/Sound_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound engineer">sound engineer</a>.\n</p><p>Film scores encompass an enormous variety of styles of music, depending on the nature of the films they accompany. The majority of scores are orchestral works rooted in <a href="/wiki/Western_classical_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Western classical music">Western classical music</a>, but many scores are also influenced by <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Blues music">blues</a>, <a href="/wiki/New-age_music" title="New-age music">new-age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">ambient music</a>, and a wide range of ethnic and <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world music</a> styles. Since the 1950s, a growing number of scores have also included <a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">electronic elements</a> as part of the score, and many scores written today feature a hybrid of <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestral</a> and electronic instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the invention of <a href="/wiki/Digital_data" title="Digital data">digital technology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">audio sampling</a>, many modern films have been able to rely on digital samples to imitate the sound of live instruments, and many scores are created and performed wholly by the composers themselves, by using sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Scorewriter" title="Scorewriter">music composition software</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Songs" class="mw-redirect" title="Songs">Songs</a> are usually not considered part of the film\'s score,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> although songs do also form part of the film\'s soundtrack.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Although some songs, especially in <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">musicals</a>, are based on thematic ideas from the score (or vice versa), scores usually do not have lyrics, except for when sung by choirs or soloists as part of a cue. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> songs which are "<a href="/wiki/Diegesis#Film_sound_and_music" title="Diegesis">needle dropped</a>" into a specific scene in film for added emphasis are not considered part of the score, although occasionally the score\'s composer will write an original pop song based on their themes, such as <a href="/wiki/James_Horner" title="James Horner">James Horner</a>\'s "<a href="/wiki/My_Heart_Will_Go_On" title="My Heart Will Go On">My Heart Will Go On</a>" from <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i>, written for <a href="/wiki/Celine_Dion" title="Celine Dion">Celine Dion</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Process of creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spotting">Spotting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spotting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The composer usually enters the creative process towards the end of filming, at around the same time as the film is being <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">edited</a>, although on some occasions the composer is on hand during the entire film shoot, especially when actors are required to perform with or be aware of original <a href="/wiki/Diegetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Diegetic">diegetic</a> music. The composer is shown an unpolished "rough cut" of the film, before the editing is completed, and talks to the director or producer about what sort of music is required for the film in terms of style and tone. The director and composer will watch the entire film, taking note of which scenes require original music. During this process, the composer will take precise timing notes so that he or she knows how long each cue needs to last, where it begins, where it ends, and of particular moments during a scene with which the music may need to coincide in a specific way. This process is known as "spotting".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Occasionally, a filmmaker will actually edit their film to fit the flow of music, rather than have the composer edit his score to the final cut. Director <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Reggio" title="Godfrey Reggio">Godfrey Reggio</a> edited his films <i><a href="/wiki/Koyaanisqatsi" title="Koyaanisqatsi">Koyaanisqatsi</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Powaqqatsi" title="Powaqqatsi">Powaqqatsi</a></i> based on composer <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">Philip Glass</a>\'s music.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the relationship between director <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Leone" title="Sergio Leone">Sergio Leone</a> and composer <a href="/wiki/Ennio_Morricone" title="Ennio Morricone">Ennio Morricone</a> was such that the finale of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good,_the_Bad_and_the_Ugly" title="The Good, the Bad and the Ugly">The Good, the Bad and the Ugly</a></i> and the films <i><a href="/wiki/Once_Upon_a_Time_in_the_West" title="Once Upon a Time in the West">Once Upon a Time in the West</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Once_Upon_a_Time_in_America" title="Once Upon a Time in America">Once Upon a Time in America</a></i> were edited to Morricone\'s score as the composer had prepared it months before the film\'s production ended.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In another notable example, the finale of <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial</a></i> was edited to match the music of his long-time collaborator <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a>: as recounted in a companion documentary on the DVD, Spielberg gave Williams complete freedom with the music and asked him to record the cue without picture; Spielberg then re-edited the scene later to match the music.\n</p><p>In some circumstances, a composer will be asked to write music based on his or her impressions of the <a href="/wiki/Script_(recorded_media)" class="mw-redirect" title="Script (recorded media)">script</a> or <a href="/wiki/Storyboards" class="mw-redirect" title="Storyboards">storyboards</a>, without seeing the film itself, and is given more freedom to create music without the need to adhere to specific cue lengths or mirror the emotional arc of a particular scene.  This approach is usually taken by a director who does not wish to have the music comment specifically on a particular scene or nuance of a film, and which can instead be inserted into the film at any point the director wishes during the post-production process. Composer <a href="/wiki/Hans_Zimmer" title="Hans Zimmer">Hans Zimmer</a> was asked to write music in this way in 2010 for director <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Nolan" title="Christopher Nolan">Christopher Nolan</a>\'s film <i><a href="/wiki/Inception" title="Inception">Inception</a></i>;<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> composer <a href="/wiki/Gustavo_Santaolalla" title="Gustavo Santaolalla">Gustavo Santaolalla</a> did the same thing when he wrote his Oscar-winning score for <i><a href="/wiki/Brokeback_Mountain" title="Brokeback Mountain">Brokeback Mountain</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syncing">Syncing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Syncing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When writing music for film, one goal is to sync dramatic events happening on screen with musical events in the score. There are many different methods for syncing music to picture. These include using sequencing software to calculate timings, using mathematic formulas and free timing with reference timings. Composers work using <a href="/wiki/SMPTE_timecode" title="SMPTE timecode">SMPTE timecode</a> for syncing purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When syncing music to picture, generally a leeway of 3-4 frames late or early allows the composer to be extremely accurate. Using a technique called Free Timing, a conductor will use either (<b>a</b>) a stopwatch or studio size stop clock, or (<b>b</b>) watch the film on a screen or video monitor while conducting the musicians to predetermined timings. These are represented visually by vertical lines (streamers) and bursts of light called punches. These are put on the film by the Music Editor at points specified by the composer. In both instances, the timings on the clock or lines scribed on the film have corresponding timings which are also at specific points (beats) in the composer/conductor score.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Written_click_track">Written click track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Written click track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A written click track is a method of writing bars of music in consistent time values (i.e. 4 beats in :02\xe2\x85\x94 seconds) to establish a constant tempo in lieu of a metronome value (e.g. 88 Bpm). A composer would use a written click if they planned to conduct live performers. When using other methods such as a metronome, the conductor has a perfectly spaced click playing in his ear which he conducts to. The methods of writing the score vary from composer to composer; some composers prefer to work with a traditional pencil and paper, writing notes by hand on a <a href="/wiki/Staff_(music)" title="Staff (music)">staff</a> and performing works-in-progress for the director on a piano, while other composers write on computers using sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Scorewriter" title="Scorewriter">music composition software</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Digital_Performer" title="Digital Performer">Digital Performer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic_Pro" title="Logic Pro">Logic Pro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finale_(software)" title="Finale (software)">Finale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubase" class="mw-redirect" title="Cubase">Cubase</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Protools" class="mw-redirect" title="Protools">Protools</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Working with software allows composers to create MIDI-based demos of cues, called <a href="/wiki/MIDI_mockup" title="MIDI mockup">MIDI mockups</a>, for review by the filmmaker prior to the final orchestral recording.\n</p><p>The length of time a composer has to write the score varies from project to project; depending on the post-production schedule, a composer may have as little as two weeks or as much as three months to write the score. In normal circumstances, the actual writing process usually lasts around six weeks from beginning to end. \n</p><p>Maestro <a href="/wiki/Ilaiyaraaja" title="Ilaiyaraaja">Ilaiyaraaja</a> is known to have completed most of his film scores within a week including spotting, syncing, writing and recording. On many occasions he has scored for around 55 movies a year and has written scores for more than a 1000 films in various Indian languages, and one in English.\n</p><p>The actual musical content of a film score is wholly dependent on the type of film being scored, and the emotions the director wishes the music to convey. A film score can encompass literally thousands of different combinations of instruments, ranging from full symphony orchestral ensembles to single solo instruments to rock bands to jazz combos, along with a multitude of ethnic and world music influences, soloists, vocalists, choirs and electronic textures. The style of the music being written also varies massively from project to project, and can be influenced by the time period in which the film is set, the geographic location of the film\'s action, and even the musical tastes of the characters. As part of their preparations for writing the score the composer will often research different musical techniques and genres as appropriate for that specific project; as such, it is not uncommon for established film composers to be proficient at writing music in dozens of different styles.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orchestration">Orchestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Orchestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Once the music has been written, it must then be <a href="/wiki/Orchestration#Film_orchestration" title="Orchestration">arranged or orchestrated</a> in order for the ensemble to be able to perform it. The nature and level of orchestration varies from project to project and composer to composer, but in its basic form the orchestrator\'s job is to take the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Single-line_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Single-line (music) (page does not exist)">single-line music</a> written by the composer and "flesh it out" into instrument-specific sheet music for each member of the orchestra to perform.\n</p><p>Some composers, notably <a href="/wiki/Ennio_Morricone" title="Ennio Morricone">Ennio Morricone</a>, orchestrate their own scores themselves, without using an additional orchestrator. Some composers provide intricate details in how they want this to be accomplished and will provide the orchestrator with copious notes outlining which instruments are being asked to perform which notes, giving the orchestrator no personal creative input whatsoever beyond re-notating the music on different sheets of paper as appropriate. Other composers are less detailed, and will often ask orchestrators to "fill in the blanks", providing their own creative input into the makeup of the ensemble, ensuring that each instrument is capable of performing the music as written, and even allowing them to introduce performance techniques and flourishes to enhance the score. In many cases, time constraints determined by the film\'s post-production schedule dictate whether composers orchestrate their own scores, as it is often impossible for the composer to complete all the required tasks within the timeframe allowed.\n</p><p>Over the years several orchestrators have become linked to the work of one particular composer, often to the point where one will not work without the other. Examples of enduring composer-orchestrator relationships include <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Goldsmith" title="Jerry Goldsmith">Jerry Goldsmith</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Morton_(orchestrator)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arthur Morton (orchestrator) (page does not exist)">Arthur Morton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Courage" title="Alexander Courage">Alexander Courage</a>; <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a> with <a href="/wiki/Herbert_W._Spencer" title="Herbert W. Spencer">Herbert W. Spencer</a>; <a href="/wiki/Alan_Menken" title="Alan Menken">Alan Menken</a> with <a href="/wiki/Danny_Troob" title="Danny Troob">Danny Troob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Starobin" title="Michael Starobin">Michael Starobin</a>; <a href="/wiki/Carter_Burwell" title="Carter Burwell">Carter Burwell</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonny_Kompanek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sonny Kompanek (page does not exist)">Sonny Kompanek</a>; <a href="/wiki/Graeme_Revell" title="Graeme Revell">Graeme Revell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Giacchino" title="Michael Giacchino">Michael Giacchino</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tim_Simonec&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tim Simonec (page does not exist)">Tim Simonec</a>; <a href="/wiki/Alan_Silvestri" title="Alan Silvestri">Alan Silvestri</a> with <a href="/wiki/James_B._Campbell" title="James B. Campbell</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Ross_(composer)" title="William Ross (composer)">William Ross</a>; <a href="/wiki/Mikl%C3%B3s_R%C3%B3zsa" title="Mikl\xc3\xb3s R\xc3\xb3zsa">Mikl\xc3\xb3s R\xc3\xb3zsa</a> with <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Zador" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene Zador">Eugene Zador</a>; <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Newman_(composer)" title="Alfred Newman (composer)">Alfred Newman</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_B._Powell_(orchestrator)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Edward B. Powell (orchestrator) (page does not exist)">Edward Powell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Darby" title="Ken Darby">Ken Darby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Friedhofer" title="Hugo Friedhofer">Hugo Friedhofer</a>; <a href="/wiki/Danny_Elfman" title="Danny Elfman">Danny Elfman</a> with <a href="/wiki/Steve_Bartek" title="Steve Bartek">Steve Bartek</a>; <a href="/wiki/Mark_Isham" title="Mark Isham">Mark Isham</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ken_Kulger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ken Kulger (page does not exist)">Ken Kulger</a>; <a href="/wiki/David_Arnold" title="David Arnold">David Arnold</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Dodd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nicholas Dodd (page does not exist)">Nicholas Dodd</a>; <a href="/wiki/Randy_Edelman" title="Randy Edelman">Randy Edelman</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Ferraro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ralph Ferraro (page does not exist)">Ralph Ferraro</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stuart_Balcomb&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stuart Balcomb (page does not exist)">Stuart Balcomb</a>; <a href="/wiki/Basil_Poledouris" title="Basil Poledouris">Basil Poledouris</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greig_McRitchie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Greig McRitchie (page does not exist)">Greig McRitchie</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Elliot_Goldenthal" title="Elliot Goldenthal">Elliot Goldenthal</a> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Elhai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Elhai (page does not exist)">Robert Elhai</a>. Others have become orchestrators-for-hire, and work with many different composers over the course of their careers; examples of prominent film music orchestrators include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Anthony&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pete Anthony (page does not exist)">Pete Anthony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Atmajian" title="Jeff Atmajian">Jeff Atmajian</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brad_Dechter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brad Dechter (page does not exist)">Brad Dechter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Fowler" title="Bruce Fowler">Bruce Fowler</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Neufeld&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Neufeld (page does not exist)">John Neufeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pasatieri" title="Thomas Pasatieri">Thomas Pasatieri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Pope" title="Conrad Pope">Conrad Pope</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nic_Raine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nic Raine (page does not exist)">Nic Raine</a> and <a href="/wiki/J.A.C._Redford" class="mw-redirect" title="J.A.C. Redford">J.A.C. Redford</a>.\n</p><p>Once the orchestration process has been completed, the sheet music is physically printed onto paper by one or more <a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_copyists&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Music copyists (page does not exist)">music copyists</a> and is ready for performance.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the music has been composed and <a href="/wiki/Orchestration" title="Orchestration">orchestrated</a>, the orchestra or ensemble then performs it, often with the composer conducting. Musicians for these ensembles are often uncredited in the film or on the album and are contracted individually (and if so, the orchestra contractor is credited in the film or the <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack_album" title="Soundtrack album">soundtrack album</a>). However, some films have recently begun crediting the contracted musicians on the albums under the name <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Studio_Symphony" title="Hollywood Studio Symphony">Hollywood Studio Symphony</a> after an agreement with the <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Musicians" title="American Federation of Musicians">American Federation of Musicians</a>. Other performing ensembles that are often employed include the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a> (performing film music since 1935)<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Prague_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="City of Prague Philharmonic Orchestra">City of Prague Philharmonic Orchestra</a> (an orchestra dedicated mostly to recording), the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Philharmonic" title="BBC Philharmonic">BBC Philharmonic</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Sinfonia" title="Northwest Sinfonia">Northwest Sinfonia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The orchestra performs in front of a large screen depicting the film, The conductor and musicians habitually wear headphones that sound a series of clicks called a "click-track" that changes with <a href="/wiki/Meter_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Meter (music)">meter</a> and tempo, assisting to synchronize the music with the film.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>More rarely, the director will talk to the composer before shooting has started, so as to give more time to the composer or because the director needs to shoot scenes (namely song or dance scenes) according to the final score. Sometimes the director will have edited the film using "temp (temporary) music": already published pieces with a character that the director believes to fit specific scenes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements_of_a_film_score">Elements of a film score</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Elements of a film score">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most films have between 40 and 120 minutes of music. However, some films have very little or no music; others may feature a score that plays almost continuously throughout.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temp_tracks">Temp tracks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Temp tracks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In some instances, film composers have been asked by the director to imitate a specific composer or style present in the temp track.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> On other occasions, directors have become so attached to the temp score that they decide to use it and reject the original score written by the film composer. One of the most famous cases is <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i>, where Kubrick opted for existing recordings of classical works, including pieces by composer <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Ligeti" title="Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Ligeti">Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Ligeti</a> rather than the score by <a href="/wiki/Alex_North" title="Alex North">Alex North</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  although Kubrick had also hired <a href="/wiki/Frank_Cordell" title="Frank Cordell">Frank Cordell</a> to do a score. Other examples include <i><a href="/wiki/Torn_Curtain" title="Torn Curtain">Torn Curtain</a></i> (Bernard Herrmann),<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Troy_(film)" title="Troy (film)">Troy</a></i> (Gabriel Yared),<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_The_Curse_of_the_Black_Pearl" title="Pirates of the Caribbean: The Curse of the Black Pearl">Pirates of the Caribbean: The Curse of the Black Pearl</a></i> (Klaus Badelt),<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Peter Jackson\'s <i><a href="/wiki/King_Kong_(2005_film)" title="King Kong (2005 film)">King Kong</a></i> (Howard Shore),<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bourne_Identity_(2002_film)" title="The Bourne Identity (2002 film)">The Bourne Identity</a></i> (Carter Burwell).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Films often have different themes for important characters, events, ideas or objects, an idea often associated with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>\'s use of <a href="/wiki/Leitmotif" title="Leitmotif">leitmotif</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> These may be played in different variations depending on the situation they represent, scattered amongst incidental music.\n</p><p>This common technique may often pass unnoticed by casual moviegoers, but has become well known among genre enthusiasts. One prominent example is <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a>\' score for the <i>Star Wars</i> saga, and the numerous themes in  <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Star Wars music"><i>Star Wars</i> music</a> associated with individual characters such as <a href="/wiki/Darth_Vader" title="Darth Vader">Darth Vader</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luke_Skywalker" title="Luke Skywalker">Luke Skywalker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Leia" title="Princess Leia">Princess Leia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Music_of_The_Lord_of_the_Rings_film_series" title="Music of The Lord of the Rings film series">music of the <i>Lord of the Rings</i> film series</a> featured recurring themes for many main characters and places. Another notable example is <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Goldsmith" title="Jerry Goldsmith">Jerry Goldsmith</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Klingon" title="Klingon">Klingon</a> theme from <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Motion_Picture" title="Star Trek: The Motion Picture">Star Trek: The Motion Picture</a></i> (1979), which later composers in the <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film_series)" title="Star Trek (film series)"><i>Star Trek</i> film series</a> quoted in their Klingon motifs, and which was included on numerous occasions as a theme for <a href="/wiki/Worf" title="Worf">Worf</a>, the franchise\'s most prominent Klingon character.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Giacchino" title="Michael Giacchino">Michael Giacchino</a> employed character themes in the soundtrack for the 2009 animated film <i><a href="/wiki/Up_(2009_film)" title="Up (2009 film)">Up</a></i>, for which he received the Academy Award for Best Score. His orchestral soundtrack for the <a href="/wiki/Television_series" class="mw-redirect" title="Television series">television series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_(TV_series)" title="Lost (TV series)">Lost</a></i> also depended heavily on character and situation-specific themes.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_music">Source music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Source music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>"<a href="/wiki/Source_music" title="Source music">Source music</a>" (or a "source cue") comes from an on screen source that can actually be seen or that can be inferred (in academic <a href="/wiki/Film_theory" title="Film theory">film theory</a> such music is called "<a href="/wiki/Diegesis" title="Diegesis">diegetic</a>" music, as it emanates from the "<a href="/wiki/Diegesis" title="Diegesis">diegesis</a>" or "story world"). An example of "source music" is the use of the <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Valli" title="Frankie Valli">Frankie Valli</a> song "Can\'t Take My Eyes Off You" in <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cimino" title="Michael Cimino">Michael Cimino</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Deer_Hunter" title="The Deer Hunter">The Deer Hunter</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>\'s 1963 thriller <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birds_(film)" title="The Birds (film)">The Birds</a></i> is an example of a Hollywood film with no <a href="/wiki/Diegesis#In_film" title="Diegesis">non-diegetic</a> music whatsoever. <a href="/wiki/Dogme_95" title="Dogme 95">Dogme 95</a> is a filmmaking movement, started in Denmark in 1995, with a manifesto that prohibits any use of non-diegetic music in its films.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistic_merit">Artistic merit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Some critics value it highly, pointing to music such as that written by <a href="/wiki/Erich_Wolfgang_Korngold" title="Erich Wolfgang Korngold">Erich Wolfgang Korngold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">Aaron Copland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Herrmann" title="Bernard Herrmann">Bernard Herrmann</a>, and others. Some consider film music to be a defining genre of classical music in the late 20th century, if only because it is the brand of classical music heard more often than any other. In some cases, film themes have become accepted into the canon of <a href="/wiki/European_classical_music" class="mw-redirect" title="European classical music">classical music</a>. These are mostly works from already noted composers who have done scores; for instance, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">Sergei Prokofiev</a>\'s score to <i><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Nevsky_(film)" title="Alexander Nevsky (film)">Alexander Nevsky</a></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Vaughan Williams</a>\' score to <i><a href="/wiki/Scott_of_the_Antarctic_(1948_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Scott of the Antarctic (1948 film)">Scott of the Antarctic</a></i>. Others see the great bulk of film music as meritless. They consider that much film music is derivative, borrowing heavily from previous works. Composers of film scores typically can produce about three or four per year. The most popular works by composers such as <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danny_Elfman" title="Danny Elfman">Danny Elfman</a> are still far from entering the accepted canon. Even so, considering they are often the most popular modern compositions of classical music known to the general public, major orchestras sometimes perform <a href="/wiki/Film_music_concerts" title="Film music concerts">concerts of such music</a>, as do <a href="/wiki/Pops_orchestra" title="Pops orchestra">pops orchestras</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation_efforts">Preservation efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Preservation efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1983, a non-profit organization, the <a href="/wiki/The_Film_Music_Society" title="The Film Music Society">Society for the Preservation of Film Music</a>, was formed to preserve the "byproducts" of creating a film score, including the music manuscripts (written music) and other documents and studio recordings generated in the process of composing and recording scores which, in some instances, have been discarded by <a href="/wiki/Movie_studio" class="mw-redirect" title="Movie studio">movie studios</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The written music must be kept to perform the music on concert programs and to make new recordings of it. Sometimes only after decades has an archival recording of a film score been released on CD.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to Kurt London, film music "began not as a result of any artistic urge, but from a dire need of something which would drown the noise made by the projector. For in those times there was as yet no sound-absorbent walls between the projection machine and the auditorium. This painful noise disturbed visual enjoyment to no small extent. Instinctively cinema proprietors had recourse to music, and it was the right way, using an agreeable sound to neutralize one less agreeable."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Before the age of recorded sound in motion pictures, efforts were taken to provide suitable music for films, usually through the services of an in-house pianist or organist, and, in some cases, entire orchestras, typically given <a href="/wiki/Sheet_music" title="Sheet music">cue sheets</a> as a guide. A pianist was present to perform at the Lumiere brother\'s first film screening in 1895.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 1914, <a href="/wiki/The_Oz_Film_Manufacturing_Company" title="The Oz Film Manufacturing Company">The Oz Film Manufacturing Company</a> sent full-length scores by <a href="/wiki/Louis_F._Gottschalk" title="Louis F. Gottschalk">Louis F. Gottschalk</a> for their films. Other examples of this include <a href="/wiki/Victor_Herbert" title="Victor Herbert">Victor Herbert</a>\'s score in 1915 to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fall_of_a_Nation" title="The Fall of a Nation">The Fall of a Nation</a></i> (a sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_a_Nation" title="The Birth of a Nation">The Birth of a Nation</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Sa\xc3\xabns">Camille Saint-Sa\xc3\xabns</a>\' music for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Assassination_of_the_Duke_of_Guise" title="The Assassination of the Duke of Guise">The Assassination of the Duke of Guise</a></i> in 1908. It was preceded by <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_D._Mann" title="Nathaniel D. Mann">Nathaniel D. Mann</a>\'s score for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fairylogue_and_Radio-Plays" title="The Fairylogue and Radio-Plays">The Fairylogue and Radio-Plays</a></i> by four months, but that was a mixture of interrelated stage and film performance in the tradition of old <a href="/wiki/Magic_lantern" title="Magic lantern">magic lantern</a> shows.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Most accompaniments at this time, these examples notwithstanding, comprised pieces by famous composers, also including studies. These were often used to form catalogues of <a href="/wiki/Photoplay_music" title="Photoplay music">photoplay music</a>, which had different subsections broken down by \'mood\' and genre: dark, sad, suspense, action, chase, etc.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/German_cinema" class="mw-redirect" title="German cinema">German cinema</a>, which was highly influential in the era of silent movies, provided some original scores such as <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lang" title="Fritz Lang">Fritz Lang</a>\'s movies <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Nibelungen" title="Die Nibelungen">Die Nibelungen</a></i> (1924) and <i><a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(1927_film)" title="Metropolis (1927 film)">Metropolis</a></i> (1927) which were accompanied by original full scale orchestral and leitmotific scores written by <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Huppertz" title="Gottfried Huppertz">Gottfried Huppertz</a>, who also wrote piano-versions of his music, for playing in smaller cinemas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Friedrich W. Murnau\'s movies <i><a href="/wiki/Nosferatu" title="Nosferatu">Nosferatu</a></i> (1922 - music by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Erdmann" title="Hans Erdmann">Hans Erdmann</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Faust_%E2%80%93_Eine_deutsche_Volkssage" class="mw-redirect" title="Faust \xe2\x80\x93 Eine deutsche Volkssage">Faust \xe2\x80\x93 Eine deutsche Volkssage</a></i> (1926&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 music by <a href="/wiki/Werner_Richard_Heymann" class="mw-redirect" title="Werner Richard Heymann">Werner Richard Heymann</a>) also had original scores written for them. Other films like Murnau\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Laugh_(1924_film)" title="The Last Laugh (1924 film)">Der letzte Mann</a></i> contained a mixing of original compositions (in this case by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Becce" title="Giuseppe Becce">Giuseppe Becce</a>) and library music / folk tunes, which were artistically included into the score by the composer.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> before the advent of talkies, <a href="/wiki/Erik_Satie" title="Erik Satie">Erik Satie</a> composed what many consider the first "frame by frame" synchronous film score for director <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Clair" title="Ren\xc3\xa9 Clair">Ren\xc3\xa9 Clair</a>\'s avant-garde short <i><a href="/wiki/Entr%27acte_(film)" title="Entr&#39;acte (film)">Entr\'acte</a></i> (1924).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Anticipating "spotting" techniques and the inconsistencies of projection speeds in screenings of silent films, Satie took precise timings for each sequence and created a flexible, <a href="/wiki/Aleatoric_music" title="Aleatoric music">aleatoric</a> score of brief, evocative motifs which could be repeated and varied in tempo as required.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> American composers <a href="/wiki/Virgil_Thomson" title="Virgil Thomson">Virgil Thomson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">Aaron Copland</a> cited Satie\'s music for <i>Entr\'acte</i> as a major influence on their own forays into film scoring.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When sound came to movies, director Fritz Lang barely used music in his movies anymore. Apart from <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lorre" title="Peter Lorre">Peter Lorre</a> whistling a short piece from Edvard Grieg\'s <a href="/wiki/Peer_Gynt" title="Peer Gynt">Peer Gynt</a>, Lang\'s movie <i><a href="/wiki/M_(1931_film)" title="M (1931 film)">M - Eine Stadt sucht einen M\xc3\xb6rder</a></i> was lacking musical accompaniment completely and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Testament_of_Dr._Mabuse" title="The Testament of Dr. Mabuse">Das Testament des Dr. Mabuse</a></i> only included one original piece written for the movie by Hans Erdmann played at the very beginning and end of the movie. One of the rare occasions on which music occurs in the movie is a song one of the characters sings, that Lang uses to put emphasis on the man\'s insanity, similar to the use of the whistling in <a href="/wiki/M_(1931_film)" title="M (1931 film)"><i>M</i></a>.\n</p><p>A landmark event in music synchronization with the action in film was achieved in the score composed by Max Steiner for David O. Selznick\'s 1933 <i>King Kong.</i> A fine example of this is when the aborigine chief slowly approaches the unwanted visitors to Skull Island who are filming the natives\' sacred rites. As he strides closer and closer, each footfall is reinforced by a background chord.\n</p><p>Though "the scoring of narrative features during the 1940s lagged decades behind technical innovations in the field of concert music,"<sup id="cite_ref-Cooke_2008_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke_2008-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> the 1950s saw the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Modernist" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernist">modernist</a> film score.  Director <a href="/wiki/Elia_Kazan" title="Elia Kazan">Elia Kazan</a> was open to the idea of jazz influences and dissonant scoring and worked with Alex North, whose score for <i><a href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(1951_film)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (1951 film)">A Streetcar Named Desire</a></i> (1951) combined dissonance with elements of blues and jazz.  Kazan also approached <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a> to score <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Waterfront" title="On the Waterfront">On the Waterfront</a></i> (1954) and the result was reminiscent of earlier works by <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">Aaron Copland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Igor Stravinsky</a> with its "jazz-based harmonies and exciting additive rhythms."<sup id="cite_ref-Cooke_2008_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke_2008-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  A year later, <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Rosenman" title="Leonard Rosenman">Leonard Rosenman</a>, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>, experimented with atonality in his scores for <i><a href="/wiki/East_of_Eden_(film)" title="East of Eden (film)">East of Eden</a></i> (1955) and <i><a href="/wiki/Rebel_Without_a_Cause" title="Rebel Without a Cause">Rebel Without a Cause</a></i> (1955).  In his ten-year collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Herrmann" title="Bernard Herrmann">Bernard Herrmann</a> experimented with ideas in <i><a href="/wiki/Vertigo_(film)" title="Vertigo (film)">Vertigo</a></i> (1958) and <i><a href="/wiki/Psycho_(1960_film)" title="Psycho (1960 film)">Psycho</a></i> (1960).  The use of non-diegetic jazz was another modernist innovation, such as jazz star <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington" title="Duke Ellington">Duke Ellington</a>\'s score for <a href="/wiki/Otto_Preminger" title="Otto Preminger">Otto Preminger</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Anatomy_of_a_Murder" title="Anatomy of a Murder">Anatomy of a Murder</a></i> (1959).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composer">Composer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Composer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academy_Award_nominees_and_winners">Academy Award nominees and winners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_score&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Academy Award nominees and winners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The following list includes all composers who have been nominated for an Academy Award by the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Best Original Score</a> category (which, over the years, had gone by a variety of names, included song scores and arrangements, and been split into awards for scoring in dramas and comedies). Sometimes called library music, the music is owned by production music libraries and licensed to customers for use in film, television, radio and other media. Unlike popular and classical music publishers, who typically own less than 50 percent of the copyright in a composition, music production libraries own all of the copyrights of their music, meaning that it can be licensed without seeking the composer\'s permission, as is necessary in licensing music from normal publishers. This is because virtually all music created for music libraries is done on a <a href="/wiki/Work_for_hire" title="Work for hire">work for hire</a> basis.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Production music is therefore a very convenient medium for media producers&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 they can be assured that they will be able to license any piece of music in the library at a reasonable rate.\n</p><p>Production music libraries will typically offer a broad range of musical styles and genres, enabling producers and editors to find much of what they need in the same library. Music libraries vary in size from a few hundred tracks up to many thousands. The first production music library was set up by De Wolfe in 1927 with the advent of sound in film, the company originally scored music for use in silent film.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Another music library was set up by Ralph Hawkes of Boosey &amp; Hawkes Music Publishers in the 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> APM, the largest US library, has over 250,000 tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a
