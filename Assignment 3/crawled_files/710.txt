title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, comprising liquid <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, produced from <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_reservoir" title="Petroleum reservoir">petroleum reservoirs</a> beneath the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a>, the term "North Sea" often includes areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a> and the area known as "West of Shetland", "the Atlantic Frontier" or "the Atlantic Margin" that is not geographically part of the North Sea.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Brent_crude" class="mw-redirect" title="Brent crude">Brent crude</a> is still used today as a standard <a href="/wiki/Benchmark_(crude_oil)" title="Benchmark (crude oil)">benchmark</a> for pricing oil, although the contract now refers to a blend of oils from fields in the northern North Sea.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=North_Sea_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="1851.E2.80.931963"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1851\xe2\x80\x931963">1851\xe2\x80\x931963</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Sea_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1851\xe2\x80\x931963">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Commercial extraction of oil on the shores of the North Sea dates back to 1851, when <a href="/wiki/James_Young_(Scottish_chemist)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Young (Scottish chemist)">James Young</a> retorted oil from <a href="/wiki/Torbanite" title="Torbanite">torbanite</a> (boghead coal, or oil shale) mined in the Midland Valley of Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-Glennie_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glennie-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Across the sea in Germany, oil was found in the Wietze field near Hanover in 1859, leading to the discovery of seventy more fields, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Cretaceous" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower Cretaceous">Lower Cretaceous</a> and Jurassic reservoirs, producing a combined total of around 1340 m\xc2\xb3 (8,400 barrels) per day.<sup id="cite_ref-Glennie_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glennie-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gas was found by chance in a water well near Hamburg in 1910, leading to minor gas discoveries in <a href="/wiki/Zechstein" title="Zechstein">Zechstein</a> dolomites elsewhere in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-Glennie_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glennie-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In England, <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a> discovered gas in similar reservoirs in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskdale_Anticline&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eskdale Anticline (page does not exist)">Eskdale</a> anticline in 1938, and in 1939 they found commercial oil in <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> rocks at <a href="/wiki/Eakring" title="Eakring">Eakring</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Glennie_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glennie-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Discoveries elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East Midlands</a> lifted production to 400 m\xc2\xb3 (2,500 barrels) per day, and a second wave of exploration from 1953 to 1961 found the <a href="/wiki/Gainsborough,_Lincolnshire" title="Gainsborough, Lincolnshire">Gainsborough</a> field and ten smaller fields.<sup id="cite_ref-Glennie_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glennie-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Netherlands\' first oil shows were seen in a drilling demonstration at De Mient during the 1938 <a href="/wiki/World_Petroleum_Council#World_Petroleum_Congress" title="World Petroleum Council">World Petroleum Congress</a> at The Hague.<sup id="cite_ref-Glennie_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glennie-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Subsequent exploration led to the 1943 discovery by Exploratie Nederland, part of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch/Shell" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Dutch/Shell">Royal Dutch/Shell</a> company Bataafsche Petroleum Maatschappij, of oil under the Dutch village of <a href="/wiki/Schoonebeek" title="Schoonebeek">Schoonebeek</a>, near the German border.<sup id="cite_ref-NAM_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> NAM found the Netherlands\' first gas in Zechstein carbonates at Coevorden in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-NAM_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 1952 saw the first exploration well in the province of Groningen, Haren-1, which was the first to penetrate the Lower Permian <a href="/wiki/Rotliegendes" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotliegendes">Rotliegendes</a> sandstone that is the main reservoir for the gas fields of the southern North Sea, although in Haren-1 it contained only water.<sup id="cite_ref-Stauble_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stauble-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Ten Boer well failed to reach target depth for technical reasons, but was completed as a minor gas producer from the Zechstein carbonates.<sup id="cite_ref-Stauble_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stauble-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Slochteren-1 well found gas in the Rotliegendes in 1959,<sup id="cite_ref-Stauble_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stauble-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> although the full extent of what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Groningen_gas_field" title="Groningen gas field">Groningen gas field</a> was not appreciated until 1963\xe2\x80\x94it is currently estimated at \xe2\x89\x8896<span style="margin-left:0.2em">\xc3\x97<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>12</sup>&#160;cu&#160;ft (2,700&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) recoverable gas reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-NAM_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Smaller discoveries to the west of Groningen followed.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1964.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1964\xe2\x80\x93present">1964\xe2\x80\x93present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Sea_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1964\xe2\x80\x93present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The UK Continental Shelf Act came into force in May 1964. Seismic exploration and the first well followed later that year. It and a second well on the Mid North Sea High were dry, as the Rotliegendes was absent, but BP\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Gem" title="Sea Gem">Sea Gem</a></i> rig struck gas in the <b>West Sole Field</b> in September 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The celebrations were shortlived since the Sea Gem sank, with the loss of 13 lives, after part of the rig collapsed as it was moved away from the discovery well.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <b>Viking Gas Field</b> was discovered in December 1965 with the <a href="/wiki/Conoco" title="Conoco">Conoco</a>/National Coal Board well 49/17-1, finding the gas-bearing <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rotliegend" title="Rotliegend">Rotliegend</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">Sandstone</a> at a depth of 2,756 m subsea.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">Helicopters</a> were first used to transport workers.<sup id="cite_ref-vert2015-04_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vert2015-04-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Larger gas finds followed in 1966\xe2\x80\x94<b>Leman Bank</b>, <b>Indefatigable</b> and <b>Hewett</b>, but by 1968 companies had lost interest in further exploration of the British sector, a result of a ban on gas exports and low prices offered by the only buyer, <a href="/wiki/British_Gas" title="British Gas">British Gas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> West Sole came onstream in May 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Licensing regulations for Dutch waters were not finalised until 1967.\n</p><p>The situation was transformed in December 1969, when Phillips Petroleum discovered oil in <a href="/wiki/Chalk_Formation#Chalk_petroleum_reservoirs" class="mw-redirect" title="Chalk Formation">Chalk</a> of <a href="/wiki/Danian" title="Danian">Danian</a> age at <a href="/wiki/Ekofisk" class="mw-redirect" title="Ekofisk">Ekofisk</a>, in Norwegian waters in the central North Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The same month, Amoco discovered the <b>Montrose Field</b> about 217&#160;km (135&#160;mi) east of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> BP had been awarded several licences in the area in the second licensing round late in 1965, but had been reluctant to work on them.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The discovery of Ekofisk prompted them to drill what turned out to be a dry hole in May 1970, followed by the discovery of the giant <a href="/wiki/Forties_Oil_Field" title="Forties Oil Field">Forties Oil Field</a> in October 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-BP_history_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP_history-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The following year, Shell Expro discovered the giant <a href="/wiki/Brent_oilfield" title="Brent oilfield">Brent oilfield</a> in the northern North Sea east of Shetland in Scotland and the Petronord Group discovered the <a href="/wiki/Frigg_gas_field" title="Frigg gas field">Frigg gas field</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Piper_oilfield" title="Piper oilfield">Piper oilfield</a> was discovered in 1973 and the <a href="/wiki/Statfjord_Field" class="mw-redirect" title="Statfjord Field">Statfjord Field</a> and the <b>Ninian Field</b><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> in 1974, with the Ninian reservoir consisting of Middle <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> sandstones at a depth of 3000 m subsea in a "westward tilted <a href="/wiki/Horst_(geology)" title="Horst (geology)">horst</a> block".\n</p><p>Off shore production, like that of the North Sea, became more economical after the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a> caused the world oil price to quadruple, followed by the <a href="/wiki/1979_oil_crisis" title="1979 oil crisis">1979 oil crisis</a>, causing another tripling in the oil price. Oil production started from the <b>Argyll &amp; Duncan Oilfields</b> (now the <b>Ardmore</b>) in June 1975<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> followed by Forties Oil Field in November of that year.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The inner <a href="/wiki/Moray_Firth" title="Moray Firth">Moray Firth</a> <b>Beatrice Field</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>/<a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a> reservoir 1829 m deep in a "<a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">fault-bounded</a> <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">anticlinal</a> trap", was discovered in 1976 with well 11/30-1, drilled by the Mesa Petroleum Group (named after T. Boone Pickens\' wife Bea, "the only oil field in the North Sea named for a woman")<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> in 49 m of water.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oil_platform_Norway.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Oil_platform_Norway.jpg/250px-Oil_platform_Norway.jpg" width="250" height="342" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Oil_platform_Norway.jpg/375px-Oil_platform_Norway.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Oil_platform_Norway.jpg/500px-Oil_platform_Norway.jpg 2x" data-file-width="941" data-file-height="1289" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oil_platform_Norway.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A \'Statfjord\' <a href="/wiki/Gravity_base_structure" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravity base structure">gravity base structure</a> under construction in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. Almost all of the structure was submerged.</div></div></div>\n<p>Volatile weather conditions in Europe\'s North Sea have made drilling particularly hazardous, claiming many lives (see <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">Oil platform</a>). The conditions also make extraction a costly process; by the 1980s, costs for developing new methods and technologies to make the process both efficient and safe, far exceeded <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>\'s budget to land a man on the moon.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The exploration of the North Sea has been a story of continually pushing the edges of the technology of exploitation (in terms of what can be produced) and later the technologies of discovery and evaluation (2-D seismic, followed by <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">3-D</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">4-D seismic</a>; sub-salt seismic; <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">immersive display</a> and analysis suites and supercomputing to handle the flood of computation required).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Gullfaks_oil_field" title="Gullfaks oil field">Gullfaks oil field</a> was discovered in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <b>Snorre Field</b> was discovered in 1979, producing from the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> Lunde Formation and the Triassic-Jurassic Statfjord Formation, both <a href="/wiki/Fluvial" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluvial">fluvial</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandstones" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandstones">sandstones</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Mudstone" title="Mudstone">mudstone</a> matrix.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Oseberg_oil_field" title="Oseberg oil field">Oseberg oil field</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Troll_gas_field" title="Troll gas field">Troll gas field</a> were also discovered in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Miller_oilfield" title="Miller oilfield">Miller oilfield</a> was discovered in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <b>Alba Field</b> produces from sandstones in the middle <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> Alba Formation at 1860 m subsea and was discovered in 1984 in UKCS Block 16/26.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The <b>Smorbukk Field</b> was discovered in 1984 in 250\xe2\x80\x93300 m of water that produces from Lower to Middle <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> sandstone formations within a fault block.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Snohvit" class="mw-redirect" title="Snohvit">Snohvit</a> Gas Field<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Draugen_oil_field" title="Draugen oil field">Draugen oil field</a> were discovered in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Heidrun_oil_field" title="Heidrun oil field">Heidrun oil field</a> was discovered in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The largest UK field discovered in the past 25 years is <b>Buzzard</b>, also located off Scotland, found in June 2001 with producible reserves of almost 64\xc3\x9710<sup>6</sup> m\xc2\xb3 (400m bbl) and an average output of 28,600 m\xc2\xb3 to 30,200 m\xc2\xb3 (180,000-190,000 bbl) per day.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The largest field found in the past five years on the Norwegian part of the North Sea, is the <a href="/wiki/Johan_Sverdrup_oil_field" title="Johan Sverdrup oil field">Johan Sverdrup oil field</a> which was discovered in 2010, with further oil of the same field was discovered the next year. Total reserves of the field are estimated at 1.7 to 3.3 billion barrels of gross recoverable oil and Johan Sverdrup is expected to produce 120,000 to 200,000 barrels of oil per day. Production start is planned to happen in 2018. It is one of the largest discoveries made in the Norwegian Continental Shelf.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of  January&#160;2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_Sea_oil&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the North Sea was the world\'s most active offshore drilling region with 173 active rigs drilling.<sup id="cite_ref-vert2015-04_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vert2015-04-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> By May 2016, the North Sea oil&amp;gas industry was financially stressed by the <a href="/wiki/Price_of_oil#2015-16_Prices:_The_lows_of_January_2016" title="Price of oil">reduced oil prices</a>, and called for government support.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The distances, number of workplaces and fierce weather push the 290,000 square miles (750,000 square kilometers) North Sea area to operate the world\xe2\x80\x99s largest fleet of heavy <a href="/wiki/Instrument_flight_rules" title="Instrument flight rules">instrument flight rules</a> (IFR) helicopters, some specifically developed for the North Sea. They carry about two million passengers per year from 16 onshore bases, of which <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Airport" title="Aberdeen Airport">Aberdeen Airport</a> is the world\xe2\x80\x99s busiest with 500,000 passengers per year.<sup id="cite_ref-vert2015-04_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vert2015-04-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Licensing">Licensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Sea_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Licensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_sea_eez.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/North_sea_eez.PNG/220px-North_sea_eez.PNG" width="220" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/North_sea_eez.PNG/330px-North_sea_eez.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/North_sea_eez.PNG/440px-North_sea_eez.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1034" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_sea_eez.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclusive Economic Zone">Exclusive Economic Zones</a> in the North Sea</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the 1958 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Continental_shelf_convention&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Continental shelf convention (page does not exist)">Continental shelf convention</a> and after some disputes on the rights to natural resource exploitation<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> the national limits of the <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zones</a> were ratified.\nFive countries are involved in oil production in the North Sea. All operate a tax and <a href="/wiki/Royalties" class="mw-redirect" title="Royalties">royalty</a> licensing regime. The respective sectors are divided by median lines agreed in the late 1960s:\n</p>\n<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 Exploration and production licences are regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Oil_and_Gas_Authority" title="Oil and Gas Authority">Oil and Gas Authority</a> following the 2014 Wood Review on maximising UKCS oil and gas recovery. Licences were formerly granted by the  <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Energy_and_Climate_Change" title="Department of Energy and Climate Change">Department of Energy and Climate Change</a> (DECC \xe2\x80\x93 formerly the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Trade_and_Industry_(United_Kingdom)" title="Department of Trade and Industry (United Kingdom)">Department of Trade and Industry</a>). The UKCS (<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Continental_Shelf" title="United Kingdom Continental Shelf">United Kingdom Continental Shelf</a>) is divided into quadrants of 1 degree latitude and one degree longitude. Each quadrant is divided into 30 blocks measuring 10 minutes of latitude and 12 minutes of longitude. Some blocks are divided further into part blocks where some areas are relinquished by previous licensees. For example, block 13/24a is located in quad 13 and is the 24th block and is the \'a\' part block. The UK government has traditionally issued licences via periodic (now annual) licensing rounds. Blocks are awarded on the basis of the work programme bid by the participants. The UK government has actively solicited new entrants to the UKCS via "promote" licensing rounds with less demanding terms and the fallow acreage initiative, where non-active licences have to be relinquished.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 The <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Petroleum_Directorate" title="Norwegian Petroleum Directorate">Norwegian Petroleum Directorate</a> (NPD <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.npd.no/en/">Website in English</a>) grants licences. The NCS is also divided into quads of 1 degree by 1 degree. Norwegian licence blocks are larger than British blocks, being 15 minutes of latitude by 20 minutes of longitude (12 blocks in a quad). Like in Britain, there are numerous part blocks formed by re-licensing relinquished areas.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 The Danish Energy Authority (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ens.dk/en">website in English</a>) administers the Danish sector. The Danes also divide their sector of the North Sea into 1 degree by 1 degree quadrants. Their blocks, however, are 10 minutes latitude by 15 minutes longitude. Part blocks exist where partial relinquishment has taken place.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 Germany and the Netherlands share a quadrant and block grid\xe2\x80\x94quadrants are given letters rather than numbers. The blocks are 10 minutes latitude by 20 minutes longitude. Germany has the smallest sector in the North Sea.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 The Dutch sector is located in the Southern Gas Basin and shares a grid pattern with Germany.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reserves_and_production">Reserves and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Sea_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reserves and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The British and Norwegian sectors hold most of the remainder of the large oil reserves. It is estimated that the Norwegian sector alone contains 54% of the sea\'s oil reserves and 45% of its gas reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\nMore than half of the North Sea oil reserves have been extracted, according to official sources in both Norway and the UK. For Norway, the Norwegian Petroleum Directorate <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> gives a figure of 4,601 million cubic metres of oil (corresponding to 29 billion barrels) for the Norwegian North Sea alone (excluding smaller reserves in Norwegian Sea and Barents Sea) of which 2,778 million cubic metres (60%) has already been produced prior to January 2007. UK sources give a range of estimates of reserves, but even using the most optimistic "maximum" estimate of ultimate recovery, 76% had been recovered as of the end of 2010.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Note the UK figure includes fields which are not in the North Sea (onshore, West of Shetland).\n</p><p>United Kingdom Continental Shelf production was 137 million tonnes of oil and 105 billion m\xc2\xb3 of gas in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Trewin_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trewin-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> (1 tonne of crude oil converts to 7.5 barrels).<sup id="cite_ref-Trewin_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trewin-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-KeyFacts_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KeyFacts-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Morton_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morton-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The Danish explorations of Cenozoic stratigraphy, undertaken in the 1990s, showed petroleum-rich reserves in the northern Danish sector, especially the Central Graben area.<sup id="cite_ref-Konradi_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Konradi-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The Dutch area of the North Sea followed through with onshore and offshore gas exploration, and well creation.<sup id="cite_ref-Ascent_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ascent-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hanze_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hanze-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Exact figures are debatable, because methods of estimating reserves vary and it is often difficult to forecast future discoveries.\n</p><p>When it <a href="/wiki/Oil_peak" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil peak">peaked</a> in 1999, production of North Sea oil was nearly 950,000 m\xc2\xb3 (6 million <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)" title="Barrel (unit)">barrels</a>) per day. <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a> production was nearly 280\xc3\x9710<sup>9</sup> m\xc2\xb3 (10 trillion cubic feet) in 2001; it continues to increase, although British gas production is in sharp decline.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>UK oil production has seen two peaks, in the mid-1980s and the late 1990s,<sup id="cite_ref-vert2015-04_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vert2015-04-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> with a decline to around 300\xc3\x9710<sup>3</sup> m\xc2\xb3 (1.9 million barrels) per day in the early 1990s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Monthly oil production peaked at 13.5\xc3\x9710<sup>6</sup> m\xc2\xb3 (84.9 million barrels) in January 1985<sup id="cite_ref-BERR_monthly_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BERR_monthly-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> although the highest annual production was seen in 1999, with offshore oil production in that year of 407\xc3\x9710<sup>6</sup> m\xc2\xb3 (398 million barrels) and had declined to 231\xc3\x9710<sup>6</sup> m\xc2\xb3 (220 million barrels) in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-BERR_annual_production_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BERR_annual_production-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This was the largest decrease of any oil-exporting nation in the world, and has led to Britain becoming a net importer of crude for the first time in decades, as recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Energy policy of the United Kingdom">energy policy of the United Kingdom</a>. The production is expected to fall to one-third of its peak by 2020.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Norwegian crude oil production as of 2013 is 1.4 mbpd. This is a more than 50% decline since the peak in 2001 of 3.2 mbpd.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_sequestration">Carbon dioxide sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Sea_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, Norway\'s <a href="/wiki/Statoil" class="mw-redirect" title="Statoil">Statoil</a> natural-gas platform <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field" title="Sleipner gas field">Sleipner</a> strips carbon dioxide out of the natural gas with amine solvents and disposes of this carbon dioxide by geological sequestration ("<a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">Carbon sequestration</a>") while keeping up gas production pressure. Sleipner reduces emissions of carbon dioxide by approximately one million tonnes a year; that is about one-9000th of total emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The cost of geological sequestration is minor relative to the overall running costs.
