<b>upper house</b> is one of two <a href="/wiki/Debate_chamber" title="Debate chamber">chambers</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Bicameralism" title="Bicameralism">bicameral legislature</a> (or one of three chambers of a <a href="/wiki/Tricameralism" title="Tricameralism">tricameral legislature</a>), the other chamber being the <a href="/wiki/Lower_house" title="Lower house">lower house</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The house formally designated as the upper house is usually smaller and often has more restricted power than the lower house. Examples of upper houses in countries include  the United Kingdom\'s <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, India\'s <a href="/wiki/Rajya_Sabha" title="Rajya Sabha">Rajya Sabha</a>, Russia\'s <a href="/wiki/Federation_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Federation Council">Federation Council</a>, Ireland\'s <a href="/wiki/Seanad" class="mw-redirect" title="Seanad">Seanad</a>, Malaysia\'s <a href="/wiki/Dewan_Negara" title="Dewan Negara">Dewan Negara</a>, Germany\'s <a href="/wiki/Bundesrat_of_Germany" title="Bundesrat of Germany">Bundesrat</a>, Switzerland\'s <a href="/wiki/Council_of_States_(Switzerland)" title="Council of States (Switzerland)">Council of States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Senate" title="Australian Senate">Australian Senate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>.\n</p><p>A legislature composed of only one house (and which therefore has neither an upper house nor a lower house) is described as <a href="/wiki/Unicameral" class="mw-redirect" title="Unicameral">unicameral</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-headline" id="Possible_specific_characteristics">Possible specific characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upper_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Possible specific characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An upper house is usually different from the lower house in at least one of the following respects:\n</p><p><b>Powers:</b>\n</p>\n<ul><li>In a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary system</a>, it often has much less power than the lower house. Therefore, in certain countries the Upper House\n<ul><li>votes on only limited legislative matters, such as constitutional amendments.</li>\n<li>cannot initiate legislation (or cannot initiate legislation on money).</li>\n<li>cannot vote a <a href="/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence" title="Motion of no confidence">motion of no confidence</a> against the government (or such an act is much less common), while the lower house always can.</li></ul></li>\n<li>In a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_system" title="Presidential system">presidential system</a>:\n<ul><li>It may have equal or nearly equal power with the lower house.</li>\n<li>It may have specific powers not granted to the lower house. For example:\n<ul><li>It may give <a href="/wiki/Advice_and_consent" title="Advice and consent">advice and consent</a> to some executive decisions (e.g. appointments of cabinet ministers, judges or ambassadors).</li>\n<li>It may have the sole power to try <a href="/wiki/Impeachments" class="mw-redirect" title="Impeachments">impeachments</a> against officials of the <a href="/wiki/Executive_branch" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive branch">executive</a>, following enabling resolutions passed by the lower house.</li>\n<li>It may have the sole power to ratify treaties.</li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>\n<p><b>Status:</b>\n</p>\n<ul><li>In some countries, its members are not popularly elected; membership may be indirect, hereditary, ex officio or by appointment.</li>\n<li>Its members may be elected with a different voting system than that used to elect the lower house (for example, upper houses in Australia and its states are usually elected by <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>, whereas lower houses are not).</li>\n<li>Less populated states, provinces, or <a href="/wiki/Administrative_division" title="Administrative division">administrative divisions</a> may be better represented in the upper house than in the lower house; representation is not completely proportional to population (or not at all).</li>\n<li>Members\' terms may be longer than in the lower house and may be for life.</li>\n<li>Members may be elected in portions, for staggered terms, rather than all at one time.</li>\n<li>In some countries, the upper house cannot be dissolved at all, or can be dissolved only in more limited circumstances than the lower house.</li>\n<li>It typically has fewer members or <a href="/wiki/Legislative_seat" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative seat">seats</a> than the lower house (though notably not in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom parliament">United Kingdom parliament</a>).</li>\n<li>It has usually a higher <a href="/wiki/Age_of_candidacy" title="Age of candidacy">age of candidacy</a> than the lower house.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers">Powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span href="/w/index.php?title=Upper_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Parliamentary systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary systems</a> the upper house is frequently seen as an advisory or "revising" chamber; for this reason, its powers of direct action are often reduced in some way. Some or all of the following restrictions are often placed on upper houses:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Lack of control over the <a href="/wiki/Executive_branch" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive branch">executive branch</a>. (On the other hand, in the US and many other presidential systems, the Senate or upper chamber has more control over the composition of the Cabinet and the administration generally, through its prerogative of confirming the president\'s nominations to senior offices.)</li>\n<li>No absolute <a href="/wiki/Veto" title="Veto">veto</a> of proposed legislation, though suspensive vetoes are permitted in some states.</li>\n<li>In countries where it <i>can</i> veto legislation (such as the <a href="/wiki/Senate_(Netherlands)" title="Senate (Netherlands)">Netherlands</a>), it may not be able to amend the proposals.</li>\n<li>A reduced or even absent role in initiating legislation.</li>\n<li>No power to block <i><a href="/wiki/Loss_of_supply" title="Loss of supply">supply</a></i>, or budget measures (a rare example of a Parliamentary upper house that does possess this power is the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Senate" title="Australian Senate">Australian Senate</a>, which notably <a href="/wiki/Australian_constitutional_crisis_of_1975" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian constitutional crisis of 1975">exercised that power in 1975</a>)</li></ul>\n<p>In parliamentary democracies and among European upper houses the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Senate">Italian Senate</a> is a notable exception to these general rules, in that it has the same powers as its lower counterpart: any law can be initiated in either house and must be approved in the same form by both houses. Additionally, a Government must have the consent of both to remain in office, a position which is known as "perfect bicameralism" or "equal bicameralism".\n</p><p>The role of a revising chamber is to scrutinise legislation that may have been drafted over-hastily in the lower house and to suggest amendments that the lower house may nevertheless reject if it wishes to. An example is the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Lords">British House of Lords</a>. Under the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949" title="Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949">Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949</a>, the House of Lords can no longer prevent the passage of most bills, but it must be given an opportunity to debate them and propose amendments, and can thereby delay the passage of a bill with which it disagrees. Bills can only be delayed for up to one year before the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">Commons</a> can use the Parliament Act, although economic bills can only be delayed for one month. It is sometimes seen as having a special role of safeguarding the uncodified <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">Constitution of the United Kingdom</a> and important <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> against ill-considered change. The <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Lords">British House of Lords</a> has a number of ways to block legislation and to reject it, however, the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> can eventually use the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949" title="Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949">Parliament Act</a> to force something through. The <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">Commons</a> will occasionally bargain and negotiate with the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Lords">Lords</a> such as when the Labour Government of 1999 tried to expel all <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_Peer" class="mw-redirect" title="Hereditary Peer">Hereditary Peers</a> from the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Lords">Lords</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Lords">Lords</a> threatened to wreck the Government\'s entire legislative agenda and to block every bill which was sent to the chamber. This led to negotiations between <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gascoyne-Cecil,_7th_Marquess_of_Salisbury" title="Robert Gascoyne-Cecil, 7th Marquess of Salisbury">Viscount Cranborne</a> the then Shadow Leader of the House, and the Labour Government which resulted in the Weatherill Amendment to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Act_1999" title="House of Lords Act 1999">House of Lords Act 1999</a> which preserved 92 Hereditary Peers in the house. The Parliament Act is not valid with all legislation and is a very rarely used backup plan.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:House_of_Lords_Chamber.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/House_of_Lords_Chamber.png/220px-House_of_Lords_Chamber.png" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/House_of_Lords_Chamber.png/330px-House_of_Lords_Chamber.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/House_of_Lords_Chamber.png/440px-House_of_Lords_Chamber.png 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1080" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:House_of_Lords_Chamber.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The chamber of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, the UK\'s Upper House</div></div></div>\n<p>Even without a veto, an upper house may defeat legislation. Its opposition may give the lower chamber a chance to reconsider or even abandon a controversial measure. It can also delay a bill so that it does not fit within the legislative schedule, or until a <a href="/wiki/General_election" title="General election">general election</a> produces a new lower house that no longer wishes to proceed with the bill.\n</p><p>Nevertheless, some states have long retained powerful upper houses. For example, the consent of the upper house to legislation may be necessary (though, as noted above, this seldom extends to budgetary measures). Constitutional arrangements of states with powerful upper houses usually include a means to resolve situations where the two houses are at odds with each other.\n</p><p>In recent times, Parliamentary systems have tended to weaken the powers of upper houses relative to their lower counterparts. Some upper houses have been abolished completely (see below); others have had their powers reduced by constitutional or legislative amendments. Also, <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_custom)" title="Constitutional convention (political custom)">conventions</a> often exist that the upper house ought not to obstruct the business of government for frivolous or merely partisan reasons. These conventions have tended to harden with a passage of time.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_systems">Presidential systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upper_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Presidential systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Presidential_system" title="Presidential system">presidential systems</a>, the upper house is frequently given other powers to compensate for its restrictions:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Executive appointments, to the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinet</a> and other offices, usually require its approval.</li>\n<li>It frequently has the sole authority to give consent to or denounce foreign <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutional_structure">Institutional structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upper_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Institutional structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is a variety of ways an upper house\'s members are assembled: by direct or indirect election, appointment, heredity, or a mixture of these. The <a href="/wiki/German_Bundesrat" class="mw-redirect" title="German Bundesrat">German Bundesrat</a> is composed of members of the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinets</a> of the German <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">states</a>, in most cases the state <a href="/wiki/Premier" title="Premier">premier</a> and several ministers; they are delegated and can be recalled anytime. In a very similar way, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_European_Union" title="Council of the European Union">Council of the European Union</a> is composed of national ministers.\n</p><p>Many upper houses are not directly elected but appointed: either by the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a> or in some other way. This is usually intended to produce a house of experts or otherwise distinguished citizens, who would not necessarily be returned in an election. For example, members of the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Canada" title="Senate of Canada">Senate of Canada</a> are appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General</a> on the advice of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">Prime Minister</a>.\n</p><p>In the past, some upper houses had seats that were hereditary, such as in the British <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> until 1999 and in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_House_of_Peers" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese House of Peers">Japanese House of Peers</a> until it was abolished in 1947.\n</p><p>It is also common that the upper house consists of delegates chosen by state governments or local officials. Members of the <a href="/wiki/Rajya_Sabha" title="Rajya Sabha">Rajya Sabha</a> in India are nominated by various states and union territories, while 12 of them are nominated by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_India" title="President of India">President of India</a>. Similarly, at the state level, one-third of the members of the <a href="/wiki/Vidhan_Parishad" title="Vidhan Parishad">Vidhan Parishad</a> are nominated by local governments, one-third by sitting legislators, and the rest are elected by select members of the electorate. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> was chosen by the State legislatures until the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Seventeenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Seventeenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Seventeenth Amendment</a> in 1913.\n</p><p>The upper house may be directly elected but in different proportions to the lower house - for example, the Senate of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> have a fixed number of elected members from each state, regardless of the population.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abolition">Abolition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upper_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Abolition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_abolished_upper_houses" title="List of abolished upper houses">List of abolished upper houses</a></div>\n<p>Many jurisdictions, such as <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> and many <a href="/wiki/States_and_union_territories_of_India" title="States and union territories of India">Indian states</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian states</a> and <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">Canadian provinces</a>, once possessed upper houses but abolished them to adopt <a href="/wiki/Unicameral" class="mw-redirect" title="Unicameral">unicameral</a> systems. <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> had a <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative Council">Legislative Council</a> prior to joining <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, as did <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> when it was <a href="/wiki/Upper_Canada" title="Upper Canada">Upper Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> from 1791 (as <a href="/wiki/Lower_Canada" title="Lower Canada">Lower Canada</a>) to 1968. <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> is the only state in the United States with a unicameral legislature, having abolished its lower house in 1934.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> also once had an appointed <a href="/wiki/Queensland_Legislative_Council" title="Queensland Legislative Council">Legislative Council</a> before abolishing it in 1922.  All other Australian states continue to have bicameral systems (the two territories have always been unicameral).\n</p><p>Like Queensland, the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> had an appointed upper house, the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate of Bavaria">Senate of Bavaria</a>, from 1946 to 1999.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Philippines" title="Senate of the Philippines">Senate of the Philippines</a> was abolished \xe2\x80\x93 and restored \xe2\x80\x93 twice: from 1935 to 1945 when a unicameral <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_the_Philippines" title="National Assembly of the Philippines">National Assembly</a> convened, and from 1972 to 1987 when Congress was closed, and later a new constitution was approved instituting a unicameral <a href="/wiki/Batasang_Pambansa" title="Batasang Pambansa">Parliament</a>. The Senate was re-instituted with the restoration of a bicameral Congress via a constitutional amendment in 1941, and via adoption of a new constitution in 1987.\n</p><p>A previous government of Ireland (<a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_31st_D%C3%A1il" title="Government of the 31st D\xc3\xa1il">the 31st D\xc3\xa1il</a>) promised a national referendum on the abolition of its upper house, the <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad \xc3\x89ireann">Seanad \xc3\x89ireann</a>, during the 24th Seanad session. By a narrow margin, the Irish public voted to retain it. Conservative-leaning <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a> and Left-leaning <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> both supported the abolition, while the centrist <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna F\xc3\xa1il">Fianna F\xc3\xa1il</a> was alone among major parties in supporting the retention of the Seanad.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_of_upper_houses">Titles of upper houses</span><span
