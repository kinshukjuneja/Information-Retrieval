There is no standard size for this <a href="/wiki/Newspaper_format" title="Newspaper format">newspaper format</a>.\n</p><p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Tabloid_journalism" title="Tabloid journalism">tabloid journalism</a></i> refers to an emphasis on such topics as sensational crime stories, astrology, celebrity gossip and television, and is not a reference to newspapers printed in this format. Some small-format papers with a high standard of journalism refer to themselves as <i><a href="/wiki/Compact_(newspaper)" title="Compact (newspaper)">compact</a></i> newspapers. Larger newspapers, traditionally associated with higher-quality journalism, are called <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheets</a>, even if the newspaper is now printed on smaller pages.  In common usage, <i>tabloid</i> and <i>broadsheet</i> are frequently more descriptive of a newspaper\'s market position than physical format.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" company <a href="/wiki/GlaxoSmithKline" title="GlaxoSmithKline">Burroughs Wellcome &amp; Co.</a> to the compressed <a href="/wiki/Tablet_(pharmacy)" title="Tablet (pharmacy)">tablets</a> they marketed as "Tabloid" pills in the late 1880s.<sup id="cite_ref-pills_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pills-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The connotation of <i>tabloid</i> was soon applied to other small compressed items. A 1902 item in London\'s <i>Westminster Gazette</i> noted, "The proprietor intends to give in tabloid form all the news printed by other journals."  Thus "tabloid journalism" in 1901 originally meant a paper that condensed stories into a simplified, easily absorbed format. The term preceded the 1918 reference to smaller sheet newspapers that contained the condensed stories.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_newspaper_size.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Comparison_newspaper_size.svg/300px-Comparison_newspaper_size.svg.png" width="300" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Comparison_newspaper_size.svg/450px-Comparison_newspaper_size.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Comparison_newspaper_size.svg/600px-Comparison_newspaper_size.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="410" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_newspaper_size.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of some newspaper sizes with metric paper sizes. Approximate nominal dimensions are in millimetres.</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tabloid_(newspaper_format)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Tabloid newspapers, especially in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, vary widely in their target market, political alignment, editorial style, and circulation.  Thus, various terms have been coined to describe the subtypes of this versatile paper format.  There are, broadly, two main types of tabloid newspaper: <i>red top</i> and <i>compact</i>.  The distinction is largely of editorial style; both red top and compact tabloids span the width of the political spectrum from <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> to <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Conservative" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative">conservatism</a>, although red-top tabloids, on account of their historically working-class target market, generally embrace <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">populism</a> to some degree. Red top tabloids are so named due to their tendency, in British and Commonwealth usage, to have their <a href="/wiki/Nameplate_(publishing)" title="Nameplate (publishing)">mastheads</a> printed in red ink; the term <i>compact</i> was coined to avoid the connotation of the word <i>tabloid</i>, which implies a red top tabloid, and has lent its name to <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_journalism" title="Tabloid journalism">tabloid journalism</a>, which is journalism after the fashion of red top reporters.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Red_top_tabloids">Red top tabloids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tabloid_(newspaper_format)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Red top tabloids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Red_tops" class="mw-redirect" title="Red tops">Red tops</a></div>\n<p>Red top tabloids, named after their distinguishing red mastheads, employ a form of writing known as <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_journalism" title="Tabloid journalism">tabloid journalism</a>; this style emphasizes features such as <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensational</a> crime stories, astrology, <a href="/wiki/Gossip_column" class="mw-redirect" title="Gossip column">gossip columns</a> about the personal lives of celebrities and sports stars, and <a href="/wiki/Junk_food_news" title="Junk food news">junk food news</a>. Celebrity gossip columns which appear in red top tabloids and focus on their <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_activity" title="Human sexual activity">sexual practices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">misuse of narcotics</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">private</a> aspects of their lives often border on, and sometimes cross the line of <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamation</a>.\n</p><p>Red tops tend to be written with a simplistic, straightforward vocabulary and grammar; their layout usually gives greater prominence to the picture than to the word.  The writing style of red top tabloids is often accused of <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensationalism</a>; red tops have been accused of deliberately igniting <a href="/wiki/Controversy" title="Controversy">controversy</a> and selectively reporting on attention-grabbing stories, or those with <a href="/wiki/Shock_value" title="Shock value">shock value</a>. In the extreme case, red top tabloids have been accused of lying or misrepresenting the truth to increase circulation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Examples of British red top newspapers include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Star_(United_Kingdom)" title="Daily Star (United Kingdom)">Daily Star</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">Daily Mirror</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compact_tabloids">Compact tabloids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tabloid_(newspaper_format)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Compact tabloids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a list of newspapers publishing in the compact "tabloid" format, see <a href="/wiki/Compact_(newspaper)" title="Compact (newspaper)">Compact (newspaper)</a>.</div>\n<p>In contrast to red top tabloids, compacts use an editorial style more closely associated with <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet</a> newspapers. In fact, most compact tabloids formerly used the broadsheet paper size, but changed to accommodate reading in tight spaces, such as on a crowded <a href="/wiki/Commuter_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Commuter bus">commuter bus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a>. The term <i>compact</i> was coined in the 1970s by the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i>, one of the earlier newspapers to make the change, although it now once again calls itself a tabloid.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The purpose behind this was to avoid the association of the word <i>tabloid</i> with the flamboyant, salacious editorial style of the red top newspaper.\n</p><p>The early converts from broadsheet format made the change in the 1970s; two British papers that took this step at the time were the <i>Daily Mail</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">Daily Express</a></i>. In 2003, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> also made the change for the same reasons, quickly followed by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scotsman" title="The Scotsman">The Scotsman</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>. On the other hand, <i><a href="/wiki/Morning_Star_(British_newspaper)" title="Morning Star (British newspaper)">The Morning Star</a></i> had always used the tabloid size, but stands in contrast to both the red top papers and the former broadsheets; although <i>The Morning Star</i> emphasises <a href="/wiki/Hard_news" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard news">hard news</a>, it embraces <a It is also into independent film making.\n</p><p>In Brazil, many newspapers are tabloids, including sports daily <i><a href="/wiki/Lance!" title="Lance!">Lance!</a></i> (which circulates in cities such as <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="S\xc3\xa3o Paulo">S\xc3\xa3o Paulo</a>), most publications currently and formerly owned by <a href="/wiki/Grupo_RBS" title="Grupo RBS">Grupo RBS</a> (especially the <a href="/wiki/Porto_Alegre" title="Porto Alegre">Porto Alegre</a> daily <i><a href="/wiki/Zero_Hora" title="Zero Hora">Zero Hora</a></i>), and, in March 2009, Rio de Janeiro-based <i><a href="/wiki/O_Dia" title="O Dia">O Dia</a></i> switched to tabloid from broadsheet, though, several years later, it reverted to being a broadsheet. Its sister publication, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meia_Hora&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Meia Hora (page does not exist)">Meia Hora</a></i> has always been a tabloid, but in slightly smaller format than <i>O Dia</i> and <i>Lance!</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_weekly_alternative_newspaper">As a weekly alternative newspaper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tabloid_(newspaper_format)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: As a weekly alternative newspaper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The more recent usage of the term \'tabloid\' refers to weekly or semi-weekly newspapers in tabloid format. Many of these are essentially straightforward newspapers, publishing in tabloid format, because subway and bus commuters prefer to read smaller-size newspapers due to lack of space.\nThese newspapers are distinguished from the major daily newspapers, in that they purport to offer an "alternative" viewpoint, either in the sense that the paper\'s editors are more locally oriented, or that the paper is editorially independent from major media conglomerates.\n</p><p>Other factors that distinguish "alternative" weekly tabloids from the major daily newspapers are their less-frequent publication, and that they are usually free to the user, since they rely on ad revenue. As well, alternative weekly tabloids tend to concentrate on local- or even neighbourhood-level issues, and on local entertainment in the bars and local theatres.\n</p><p>Alternative tabloids can be positioned as <a href="/wiki/Upmarket" class="mw-redirect" title="Upmarket">upmarket</a> (quality) newspapers, to appeal to the better-educated, higher-income sector of the market; as <a href="/wiki/Middle-market_newspaper" title="Middle-market newspaper">middle-market</a> (popular); or as <a href="/wiki/Downmarket" class="mw-redirect" title="Downmarket">downmarket</a> (sensational) newspapers, which emphasize sensational crime stories and celebrity gossip. In each case, the newspapers will draw their advertising revenue from different types of businesses or services. An upmarket weekly\'s advertisers are often organic grocers, boutiques, and theatre companies while a downmarket\'s may have those of trade schools, supermarkets, and the sex industry. Both usually contain ads from local bars, auto dealers, movie theaters, and a classified ads section.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tabloid_(newspaper_format)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Berliner_(format)" title="Berliner (format)">Berliner</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Compact_(newspaper)" title="Compact (newspaper)">Compact</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Newspaper_format" title="Newspaper format">Newspaper format</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Paper_size" title="Paper size">Paper size</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tabloid_(newspaper_format)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 40em; -webkit-column-width: 40em; column-width: 40em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-pills-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-pills_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.wellcome.ac.uk/About-us/History/WTX051935.htm">"Henry Wellcome the Sailesman"</a>. <i>Wellcome</i>. 18 November 2008<span class="reference-accessdate">.
