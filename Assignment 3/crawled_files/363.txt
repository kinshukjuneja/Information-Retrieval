href="http://www.eurid.eu/">www.eurid.eu</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>.eu</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Country_code_top-level_domain" title="Country code top-level domain">country code top-level domain</a> (ccTLD) for the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Launched on 7 December 2005, the domain is available for any person, company or organization based in the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">EU member states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The TLD is administered by <a href="/wiki/EURid" title="EURid">EURid</a>, a consortium originally consisting of the national <a href="/wiki/CcTLD" class="mw-redirect" title="CcTLD">ccTLD</a> registry operators of Belgium, Sweden, and Italy, joined later by the national registry operator of the Czech Republic. Trademark owners were able to submit registrations through a <a href="/wiki/Sunrise_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunrise process">sunrise period</a>, in an effort to prevent <a href="/wiki/Cybersquatting" title="Cybersquatting">cybersquatting</a>. Full registration started on 7 April 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The .eu TLD was approved by <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> on 22 March 2005<sup id="cite_ref-approval_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-approval-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and put in the Internet <a href="/wiki/DNS_root_zone" title="DNS root zone">root zone</a> on 2 May 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Even though the EU is not a country (it is a <a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a> <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_organization" title="Intergovernmental organization">intergovernmental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supranational" class="mw-redirect" title="Supranational">supranational</a> organisation), there are precedents of issuing top-level domains to other entities\xe2\x80\x94e.g. <a href="/wiki/.nato" title=".nato">.nato</a>. See also <a href="/wiki/GeoTLD" title="GeoTLD">GeoTLD</a>.\n</p><p><i>.eu.int</i> was the <a href="/wiki/Subdomain" title="Subdomain">subdomain</a> most used by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/.int" title=".int">.int</a> <a href="/wiki/Generic_top-level_domain" title="Generic top-level domain">generic top-level domain</a> (gTLD) for international bodies, until 9 May 2006. The .eu domain (ccTLD) was launched in December 2005, and because of this most .eu.int domain names changed to .<a href="/wiki/Europa.eu" class="mw-redirect" title="Europa.eu">europa.eu</a> on <a href="/wiki/Europe_day" class="mw-redirect" title="Europe day">Europe day</a>, 9 May 2006.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunrise_period">Sunrise period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sunrise period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sunrise_Period" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunrise Period">Sunrise Period</a> was broken into two phases. The first phase, which began on 7 December 2005 was to facilitate applications by registrants with prior rights based on trademarks and geographic names. The second phase began on 7 February 2006 and covered company, trade and personal names. In the case of all Sunrise applications, the application needed to be accompanied by documents proving the claim to ownership of a certain right. The decision was then made by <a href="/wiki/PricewaterhouseCoopers" title="PricewaterhouseCoopers">PricewaterhouseCoopers</a> Belgium, which had been chosen as the validation agent by <a href="/wiki/EURid" title="EURid">EURid</a>.\n</p><p>On 7 February 2006, the registry was opened for company, trade and personal names. In the first 15&#160;minutes, there were 27,949 total applications, and after one hour, 71,235.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landrush">Landrush</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landrush">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 7 April 2006 at 11&#160;am CET registration became possible for non-trademark holders. Most people requesting domains had asked their registrars to put their requested domains in a queue, ensuring the best chance to register a domain. This way more than 700,000 domains were registered during the first 4 hours of operation. Some large registrars like <a href="/wiki/Go_Daddy" class="mw-redirect" title="Go Daddy">Go Daddy</a> and small registrars like <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dotster (page does not exist)">Dotster</a> suffered from long queues and unresponsiveness, allowing people to \'beat the queue\' by registering through a registrar that had already processed its queue. By August 2006, 2 Million .eu domains had been registered. It is now the fourth-largest ccTLD in Europe, after <a href="/wiki/.de" title=".de">.de</a>, <a href="/wiki/.uk" title=".uk">.uk</a> and <a href="/wiki/.nl" title=".nl">.nl</a>, and is one of the largest internationally.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bob_Parsons" title="Bob Parsons">Bob Parsons</a>, CEO and co-founder of Go Daddy, criticized the landrush process designed by EURid. Particularly, he condemned the use of shell companies by some registrars. In his blog, he stated "These companies, instead of only registering their real active registrars, created hundreds of new "phantom" registrars."<sup id="cite_ref-landrush_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-landrush-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Parsons cited a group of about 400 companies, all with similar address and contact information based in New York, each registered as an <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">LLC</a>; in his opinion, these were phantom registrars "created to hijack the .EU landrush."\n</p><p>These "phantom" registrars effectively had hundreds of opportunities of registering a domain whereas a genuine registrar effectively only had one opportunity to register the same domain. Thus some registrants were crowded out of the .eu landrush process and many generic .eu domain names are now owned by the companies using these "phantom" registrars.\n</p><p>Patrik Lind\xc3\xa9n, spokesman for EURid at the time, denied the allegations by Parsons, stating that "[EURid] verified that each registrar was an individual legal entity. Each had to sign an agreement with us, and prepay \xe2\x82\xac10,000."<sup id="cite_ref-refutation_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refutation-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Parsons didn\'t dispute that each registrar was a separate legal entity, but noted that creating such entities was trivial: "Mr. Linden seemed proud that the EURid registry verified that each applicant was a legal entity before it was accredited. Take a moment and think about what that means. You can form a \xe2\x80\x9clegal entity\xe2\x80\x9d for $50 \xe2\x80\x93 an LLC \xe2\x80\x93 and you are good to go. Is that what we want a registry to do? Don\xe2\x80\x99t we want them instead to make sure that the organization it allows to provide end-users with its domain names \xe2\x80\x93 especially Europe\xe2\x80\x99s very own domain name \xe2\x80\x93 are actually in the domain name registration business?"<sup id="cite_ref-entity_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-entity-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The EURid organisation investigated some allegations of abuse, and in July 2006 announced the suspension of over 74,000 domain names and that they were suing 400 registrars for breach of contract.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The status of the domains was changed from ACTIVE to ON-HOLD. This meant that the domains could not be moved or have their ownership changed. The registrars also lost their access to the EURid registration database meaning that they could no longer register .eu domain names. The legal action relates to the practice of <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_warehousing" title="Domain name warehousing">Domain name warehousing</a>, whereby large numbers of domain names are registered, often by registrars, with the intention of subsequently selling them on to third parties.  EURid rules state that applications for domains can only be made after a legitimate application has been made to a registrar.  The 74,000 applications were made in the name of only three Cyprus registered companies\xe2\x80\x94 Ovidio Ltd., Fausto Ltd. and Gabino Ltd.\n</p><p>The affected registrars, joined in the action by the affected registrants obtained a provisional order from the Court of First Instance in Brussels, Belgium on 27 September 2006. The court ordered EURid to release the blocked domain names or else pay a fine of \xe2\x82\xac25,000 per hour for each affected domain name. EURid complied with the court order and changed the status of the domains from ON HOLD to ACTIVE and restored EURid registration database access to the affected registrars.\n</p><p>The main legal action, that of EURid seeking the registrar agreements between EURid and the registrars in question to be dissolved has still to be heard.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brexit">Brexit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Brexit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 29 March 2018, as a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">the United Kingdom\'s exit from the European Union</a>, it was announced that "as of the withdrawal date, undertakings and organisations that are established in the United Kingdom but not in the EU, and natural persons who reside in the United Kingdom will no longer be eligible to register .eu domain names or, if they are .eu registrants, to renew .eu domain names registered before the withdrawal date".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Commission announced on 27 April 2018 that it It is often used to emphasise the \'European identity\' of a website, as opposed to the website having a strictly national ccTLD or global "dotcom" nature. Alternative (<a href="/wiki/Domain_hack" title="Domain hack">opportunistic</a>) uses include <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque</a> webpages (as the initial letters of <a href="/wiki/Euskadi" class="mw-redirect" title="Euskadi">Euskadi</a> or the language <a href="/wiki/Euskara" class="mw-redirect" title="Euskara">Euskara</a>) and Romanian, Portuguese, or <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galician</a> personal sites, as <i>eu</i> is the equivalent of the English pronoun \'I\' in those languages.\n</p><p>In most countries of the EU, the national ccTLDs have the major share of the market with the remainder spread over <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a>/<a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a>/<a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">.org</a>/<a href="/wiki/.info" title=".info">.info</a>/<a href="/wiki/.biz" title=".biz">.biz</a>. As a result of this, .eu has had an uphill battle to gain a significant share of these national markets. The dominant players tend to be the national ccTLD and .com. The other TLDs such as <a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a>, <a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">.org</a> and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/.info" title=".info">.info</a> and <a href="/wiki/.biz" title=".biz">.biz</a> have progressively smaller shares of these national markets.\n</p><p>Over one year after the launch of .eu (5 July 2007), the number of <a href="/wiki/.de" title=".de">.de</a> domains registered was 11,079,557 according to the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070629120049/http://www.denic.de/en/domains/statistiken/index.html">German .de registry\'s statistics page</a>, while number of German owned .eu domains according to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070710114305/http://status.eurid.eu/registered.html">EURid\'s statistics page</a> was 796,561. The number of <a href="/wiki/.uk" title=".uk">.uk</a> domains registered was 6,038,732 according to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nominet.org.uk/intelligence/statistics/registration/">.uk registry Nominet\'s statistics page</a>. The number of apparently UK owned .eu domains was 344,584.\n</p><p>Some .eu domain names have had some popularity, such as the <a href="/wiki/Leave.EU" title="Leave.EU">Leave.EU</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ref1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The 317.000 British .eu domain names will be subject to <a href="/wiki/Brexit_negotiations" title="Brexit negotiations">Brexit negotiations</a> because the .eu domain is reserved for European use. The .eu brexit could occur on March 30th, 2019, in case of no deal.<sup id="cite_ref-ref1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parking_and_redirects">Parking and redirects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Parking and redirects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. This percentage does not include .eu domains that are pointed to holding pages or not set up in DNS.</li>\n<li>26% of .eu domain names are redirects for existing national ccTLD or .com websites.<sup id="cite_ref-eurid.eu_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurid.eu-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Domain_name_speculation" title="Domain name speculation">Domain name speculation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_warehousing" title="Domain name warehousing">Domain name warehousing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cybersquatting" title="Cybersquatting">cybersquatting</a> are always features of the launch of any new TLD; however, this was more widespread in the case of the .eu launch, as seen below.</li></ul>\n<p>According to page 20 of <a href="/wiki/EURid" title="EURid">EURid</a>\'s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111110022315/http://www.eurid.eu/files/2006_annual_report.pdf">Annual Report for 2006</a>, the breakdown of .eu domain ownership figures on 31 December 2006 was:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Registrants with more than 10,000 domains: 6</li>\n<li>Registrants with 5,000\xe2\x80\x939,999 domains: 18</li>\n<li>Registrants with 1,000\xe2\x80\x934,999 domains: 64</li>\n<li>Registrants with 100-999 domains: 1,257</li>\n<li>Registrants with 10\xe2\x80\x9399 domains: 20,886</li>\n<li>Registrants with 6\xe2\x80\x939 domains: 22,933</li>\n<li>Registrants with 5 domains: 13,200 \xe2\x80\x93 (66,000 domains)</li>\n<li>Registrants with 4 domains: 23,007 \xe2\x80\x93 (92,028 domains)</li>\n<li>Registrants with 3 domains: 42,887 \xe2\x80\x93 (128,661 domains)</li>\n<li>Registrants with 2 domains: 115,543 \xe2\x80\x93 (231,086 domains)</li>\n<li>Registrants with 1 domain: 610,679</li></ul>\n<p>The number of registrants with five domains or fewer registered in .eu ccTLD was, according to these statistics, 805,316. These registrants accounted for 1,128,454 domains out of 2,444,947 .eu domains registered as of 31 December 2006. These registrations, typically those of individuals and companies protecting their brand, only represent 46% of the number of registered .eu domains.\n</p><p>It had been actively targeted during the Sunrise period by speculators using fast track Benelux trademarks to create <i>prior rights</i> on various high value generic terms and during the landrush by speculators using EU front companies in the UK and Cyprus to register large numbers of domains. While speculative activity occurred with the launch of other domains, it was the scale of the activity that called into question the competence of EURid in protecting the integrity of eu ccTLD.\n</p><p>The number of .eu domain registrations during the year after the landrush 7 April 2006 to 6 April 2007 seems to have peaked at approximately 2.6 million .eu domains. The market adjustment that follows a landrush in any domain name extension ensures that the number of registered domains will fall as many speculative domain registrations that failed to be resold will not be renewed. This is sometimes referred to as the <i>Junk Dump</i>. On the morning of 7 April 2007, the number of active .eu domains stood at 2,590,160 with approximately 15,000 domains having been deleted since 5 April 2007.\n</p><p>Approximately 1.5 million .eu domains were up for renewal in April 2007. The EURid registry software is based on the DNS. be software and domains are physically renewed at the end of the month of their anniversary of registration. This process differs from more sophisticated registries like that of <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a> <a href="/wiki/TLD" class="mw-redirect" title="TLD">TLD</a> and other <a href="/wiki/CcTLD" class="mw-redirect" title="CcTLD">ccTLDs</a> that operate on a daily basis. As with any post-landrush phase, an extension shrinks as the <i>Junk Dump</i> takes effect.\n</p><p>The extent of the shrinkage of .eu ccTLD is difficult to estimate because EURid does not publish detailed statistics on the number of new domains registered each day. Instead it provides only a single figure for the number of active domains. The number of new registrations are combined with numbers of domains registered. Approximately 250,000 .eu domains were either deleted or moved into quarantine by 30 April 2007. In the intervening years the renewal rate has stabilised to approximately 80%, which is above the industry average.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyrillic_domain">Cyrillic domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.eu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cyrillic domain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>.\xd0\xb5\xd1\x8e</b>, a top level domain using Cyrillic letters was put into operation on 1 June 2016. A Cyrillic domain was needed because Bulgaria, a member of the EU, uses the Cyrillic alphabet. The EU is called \xd0\x95\xd0\xa1 in Bulgarian Cyrillic, but .\xd0\xb5\xd1\x81 is much too similar to <a href="/wiki/.ec" title=".ec">.ec</a>, an existing domain, so .\xd0\xb5\xd1\x8e was chosen. (While the letters look identical to those used in English, \xd0\x95\xd0\xa1 and .\xd0\xb5\xd1\x81 in Bulgarian Cyrillic do not use the same character set, and are distinct for data-processing purposes.)\n</p><p>EurId has a rule that the second-level domain name must be in the same script as the top-level domain,\nso Cyrillic second-level domains must go under .\xd0\xb5\xd1\x8e instead of .eu. <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Older Cyrillic domains under .eu were cloned into .\xd0\xb5\xd1\x8e at its launch.\n</p>\n<h2><span
