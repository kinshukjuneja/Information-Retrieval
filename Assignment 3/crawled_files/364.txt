Powell</b> (30 September 1905 \xe2\x80\x93 19 February 1990) was an English <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">film director</a>, celebrated for his partnership with <a href="/wiki/Emeric_Pressburger" title="Emeric Pressburger">Emeric Pressburger</a>. Through their production company "<a href="/wiki/Powell_and_Pressburger" title="Powell and Pressburger">The Archers</a>", they together wrote, produced and directed a series of classic British films, notably <i><a href="/wiki/49th_Parallel_(film)" title="49th Parallel (film)">49th Parallel</a></i> (1941), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Death_of_Colonel_Blimp" title="The Life and Death of Colonel Blimp">The Life and Death of Colonel Blimp</a></i> (1943), <i><a href="/wiki/A_Matter_of_Life_and_Death_(film)" title="A Matter of Life and Death (film)">A Matter of Life and Death</a></i> (1946, also called <i>Stairway to Heaven</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Narcissus" title="Black Narcissus">Black Narcissus</a></i> (1947), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Red_Shoes_(1948_film)" title="The Red Shoes (1948 film)">The Red Shoes</a></i> (1948), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tales_of_Hoffmann_(film)" title="The Tales of Hoffmann (film)">The Tales of Hoffmann</a></i> (1951). His later controversial 1960 film <i><a href="/wiki/Peeping_Tom_(film)" title="Peeping Tom (film)">Peeping Tom</a></i>, while today considered a classic, and a contender as the first "<a href="/wiki/Slasher_film" title="Slasher film">slasher</a>", was so vilified on first release that his career was seriously damaged.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crouse2003_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crouse2003-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many film-makers such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Francis Ford Coppola</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_A._Romero" title="George A. Romero">George A. Romero</a> have cited Powell as an influence.<sup id="cite_ref-Crook_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crook-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1981, he received the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Academy_Fellowship_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="BAFTA Academy Fellowship Award">BAFTA Academy Fellowship Award</a> along with his partner Pressburger, the highest honour the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="British Film Academy">British Film Academy</a> can give a filmmaker.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Powell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Powell was the second son and youngest child of Thomas William Powell, a <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hop farmer</a>, and Mabel, daughter of Frederick Corbett, of <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. Powell was born in <a href="/wiki/Bekesbourne" title="Bekesbourne">Bekesbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, and educated at <a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_School,_Canterbury" title="The King&#39;s School, Canterbury">The King\'s School, Canterbury</a> and then at <a href="/wiki/Dulwich_College" title="Dulwich College">Dulwich College</a>. He started work at the <a href="/wiki/National_Provincial_Bank" title="National Provincial Bank">National Provincial Bank</a> in 1922 but quickly realised he was not cut out to be a banker.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_career">Film career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Powell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Film career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Powell entered the film industry in 1925 through working with director <a href="/wiki/Rex_Ingram_(director)" title="Rex Ingram (director)">Rex Ingram</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Victorine_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorine Studios">Victorine Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nice" title="Nice">Nice</a>, France (the contact with Ingram was made through Powell\'s father, who owned a hotel in Nice). He first started out as a general studio hand, the proverbial "<a href="/wiki/Gofer" title="Gofer">gofer</a>": sweeping the floor, making coffee, fetching and carrying. Soon he progressed to other work such as stills photography, writing titles (for the silent films) and many other jobs including a few acting roles, usually as comic characters. Powell made his film d\xc3\xa9but as a "comic English tourist" in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magician_(1926_film)" title="The Magician (1926 film)">The Magician</a></i> (1926).\n</p><p>Returning to England in 1928, Powell worked at a diverse series of jobs for various filmmakers including as a stills photographer on <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>\'s silent film <i><a href="/wiki/Champagne_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Champagne (film)">Champagne</a></i> (1928). He also signed on in a similar role on Hitchcock\'s first "talkie", <i><a href="/wiki/Blackmail_(1929_film)" title="Blackmail (1929 film)">Blackmail</a></i> (1929). In his autobiography, Powell claims he suggested the ending in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> which was the first of Hitchcock\'s "monumental" climaxes to his films.<sup id="cite_ref-ALIM_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALIM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Powell and Hitchcock remained friends for the remainder of Hitchcock\'s life.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;N 1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After scriptwriting on two productions, Powell entered into a partnership with American producer Jerry Jackson in 1931 to make "<a href="/wiki/Cinematograph_Films_Act_1927" title="Cinematograph Films Act 1927">quota quickies</a>", hour-long films needed to satisfy a legal requirement that British cinemas screen a certain quota of British films. During this period, he developed his directing skills, sometimes making up to seven films a year.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_Early_Years_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_Early_Years-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although he had taken on some directing responsibilities in other films, Powell had his first screen credit as a director on <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Crowded_Hours" title="Two Crowded Hours">Two Crowded Hours</a></i> (1931). This thriller was considered a modest success at the box office despite its limited budget.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_Early_Years_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_Early_Years-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> From 1931 to 1936, Powell was the director of 23 films, including the critically received  <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Ensign_(film)" title="Red Ensign (film)">Red Ensign</a></i> (1934) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Phantom_Light" title="The Phantom Light">The Phantom Light</a></i> (1935).<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_Early_Years_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_Early_Years-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1937 Powell completed his first truly personal project, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Edge_of_the_World" title="The Edge of the World">The Edge of the World</a></i>. Powell gathered together a cast and crew who were willing to take part in an expedition to what was then a very isolated part of the UK. They had to stay there for quite a few months and finished up with a film which not only told the story he wanted but also captured the raw natural beauty of the location.\n</p><p>By 1939, Powell had been hired as a contract director by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a> on the strength of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Edge_of_the_World" title="The Edge of the World">The Edge of the World</a></i>. Korda set him to work on some projects such as <i>Burmese Silver</i> that were subsequently cancelled.<sup id="cite_ref-ALIM_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALIM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, Powell was brought in to save a film that was being made as a vehicle for two of Korda\'s star players, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Veidt" title="Conrad Veidt">Conrad Veidt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valerie_Hobson" title="Valerie Hobson">Valerie Hobson</a>. The film was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spy_in_Black" title="The Spy in Black">The Spy in Black</a></i>, where Powell first met <a href="/wiki/Emeric_Pressburger" title="Emeric Pressburger">Emeric Pressburger</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meeting_Emeric_Pressburger">Meeting Emeric Pressburger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Powell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meeting Emeric Pressburger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The original script of <i>The Spy in Black</i> followed the book quite closely, but was too verbose and did not have a good role for either Veidt or Hobson. Korda called a meeting where he introduced a diminutive man, saying, "Well now, I have asked Emeric to read the script, and he has things to say to us."<sup id="cite_ref-ALIM_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALIM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Powell then went on to record (in <i>A Life in Movies</i>) how:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"Emeric produced a very small piece of rolled-up paper, and addressed the meeting. I listened spellbound. Since talkies took over the movies, I had worked with some good writers, but I had never met anything like this. In the silent days, the top [American] screenwriters were technicians rather than dramatists ... the European cinema remained highly literate and each country, conscious of its separate culture and literature, strove to outdo the other. All this was changed by the talkies. America, with its enormous wealth and enthusiasm and it technical resources, waved the big stick. ... The European film no longer existed. ... Only the great German film business was prepared to fight the American monopoly, and Dr. Goebbels soon put a stop to that in 1933. But the day that Emeric walked out of his flat, leaving the key in the door to save the storm-troopers the trouble of breaking it down, was the worst day\'s work that the clever doctor ever did for his country\'s reputation, as he was soon to find out.\nAs I said, I listened spellbound to this small Hungarian wizard, as Emeric unfolded his notes, until they were at least six inches long. He had stood Storer Clouston\'s plot on its head and completely restructured the film."<sup id="cite_ref-ALIM_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALIM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>They both soon recognised that although they were total opposites in background and personality, they had a common attitude to film-making and that they could work very well together. After making two more films together (<i><a href="/wiki/Contraband_(1940_film)" title="Contraband (1940 film)">Contraband</a></i> (1940) and <i><a href="/wiki/49th_Parallel_(film)" title="49th Parallel (film)">49th Parallel</a></i>) with separate credits, the pair decided to form a partnership and to sign their films jointly as "Written, Produced and Directed by Michael Powell and Emeric Pressburger."<sup id="cite_ref-ALIM_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALIM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Archers">The Archers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Powell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Archers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Working together as co-producers, writers and directors in a partnership they dubbed "<a href="/wiki/Powell_and_Pressburger" title="Powell and Pressburger">The Archers</a>", they made 19 feature films, many of which received critical and commercial success. Their best films are still regarded as classics of 20th century British cinema.  The <a href="/wiki/BFI_Top_100_British_films" title="BFI Top 100 British films">BFI 100</a> list of "the favourite British films of the 20th century" contains five of Powell\'s films, four with Pressburger.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although admirers would argue that Powell ought to rank alongside fellow British directors <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Lean" title="David Lean">David Lean</a>, his career suffered a severe reversal after the release of the controversial psychological <a href="/wiki/Thriller_film" title="Thriller film">thriller film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Peeping_Tom_(film)" title="Peeping Tom (film)">Peeping Tom</a></i>, made in 1960 as a solo effort.<sup id="cite_ref-FT.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT.com-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The film was excoriated by mainstream British critics, who were offended by its sexual and violent images; Powell was ostracized by the film industry and found it almost impossible to work thereafter.\n</p><p>The film did however meet with the rapturous approval of the young critics of <a href="/wiki/Positif_(magazine)" title="Positif (magazine)"><i>Positif</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Midi_Minuit_Fantastique" title="Midi Minuit Fantastique"><i>Midi-Minuit Fantastique</i></a> in France, and those of <i>Motion</i> in England, and in 1965 he was subject of a major positive revaluation by <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Durgnat" title="Raymond Durgnat">Raymond Durgnat</a> in the auteurist magazine <i>Movie</i>, later included in Durgnat\'s influential book <i>A Mirror for England</i>.\n</p><p>Powell\'s films came to have a cult reputation, broadened during the 1970s and early 1980s by a series of retrospectives and rediscoveries, as well as further articles and books. By the time of his death, he and Pressburger were recognised as one of the foremost film partnerships of all time \xe2\x80\x93 and cited as a key influence by many noted filmmakers such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_De_Palma" title="Brian De Palma">Brian De Palma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Francis Ford Coppola</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FT.com_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT.com-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Powell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1927 Powell married Gloria Mary Rouger, an American dancer; they were married in France and stayed together for only three weeks. During the 1940s, Powell had love affairs with actresses <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Kerr" title="Deborah Kerr">Deborah Kerr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Byron" title="Kathleen Byron">Kathleen Byron</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ALIM_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALIM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> From 1 July 1943 until her death on 5 July 1983, Powell was married to Frances "Frankie" May Reidy, the daughter of medical practitioner Jerome Reidy; they had two sons: Kevin Michael Powell (b. 1945) and Columba Jerome Reidy Powell (b. 1951). He also lived with actress <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Brown_(actress)" title="Pamela Brown (actress)">Pamela Brown</a> for many years until her death from cancer in 1975.\n</p><p>Subsequently, Powell was married to <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">film editor</a> <a href="/wiki/Thelma_Schoonmaker" title="Thelma Schoonmaker">Thelma Schoonmaker</a> from 19 May 1984 until his own death from cancer at his home in <a href="/wiki/Avening" title="Avening">Avening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FT.com_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT.com-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation">Preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
