title="Non-profit organization">non-profit making</a> organisations that provide low-cost "<a href="/wiki/Public_housing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Public housing in the United Kingdom">social housing</a>" for people in need of a home. Any trading surplus is used to maintain existing housing and to help finance new homes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Although independent, they are <a href="/wiki/Regulated_by_the_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Regulated by the state">regulated by the state</a> and commonly receive <a href="/wiki/Public_funding" class="mw-redirect" title="Public funding">public funding</a>. They are now the United Kingdom\'s major providers of new housing for <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">rent</a>, while many also run <a href="/wiki/Equity_sharing" title="Equity sharing">shared ownership</a> schemes to help those who cannot afford to buy a home outright.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Housing associations provide a wide range of housing, some managing large estates of housing for families, while the smallest may perhaps manage a single scheme of housing for older people. Much of the supported accommodation in the UK is also provided by housing associations, with specialist projects for people with <a href="/wiki/Mental_disorder" title="Mental disorder">mental health</a> issues or <a href="/wiki/Learning_disability" title="Learning disability">learning disabilities</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Substance_misuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Substance misuse">substance misuse</a> problems (<a href="/wiki/Alcohol_abuse" title="Alcohol abuse">alcohol</a> or illegal <a href="/wiki/Drug_abuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug abuse">drugs</a>), the formerly <a href="/wiki/Homeless" class="mw-redirect" title="Homeless">homeless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Young people">young people</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ex-offender" class="mw-redirect" title="Ex-offender">ex-offenders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asylum_seekers" class="mw-redirect" title="Asylum seekers">asylum seekers</a>, and people fleeing <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">domestic violence</a>.\n</p><p>In Australia, the term "housing association" refers to larger, growth oriented \'not-for-dividend\' community housing providers. Smaller community housing providers may include trusts, cooperatives etc. State and Territory owned public housing represents about 80% of social housing in Australia. Over the years these public housing entities have had different names including \'housing commissions\', \'housing trusts\' etc.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Housing associations first appeared in the second half of the nineteenth century as part of the growth in <a href="/wiki/Philanthropy" title="Philanthropy">philanthropic</a> and voluntary organisations brought about by the growth of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle classes</a> in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. Early examples are the <a href="/wiki/Guinness_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinness Trust">Guinness Trust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Trust" title="Peabody Trust">Peabody Trust</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Association_for_Improving_the_Dwellings_of_the_Industrious_Classes" title="Metropolitan Association for Improving the Dwellings of the Industrious Classes">Metropolitan Association for Improving the Dwellings of the Industrious Classes</a>.\n</p><p>They increased in importance over the last decades of the twentieth century due to changes to <a href="/wiki/Council_house" title="Council house">council housing</a> brought in by the <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Thatcher</a> government, when rules were introduced that prevented councils subsidising their housing from local taxes, channelled grants for construction of new social housing to housing associations and allowed council tenants to buy their homes at a large discount. This, combined with cost-cutting initiatives in local government and a housing benefit scheme that was more generous to housing associations than local authorities, led to many <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">councils</a> transferring their housing stock to housing associations. These organisations are often referred to as large-scale voluntary transfer organisations or local housing companies.\n</p><p>The Housing Acts of <a href="/wiki/Housing_Act_1985" title="Housing Act 1985">1985</a> and <a href="/wiki/Housing_Act_1988" title="Housing Act 1988">1988</a> facilitated the transfer of council housing to not-for-profit housing associations. The 1988 Act redefined housing associations as non-public bodies, permitting access to private finance, which was a strong motivation for transfer as public sector borrowing had been severely constrained. These new housing associations were also the providers of most new public-sector housing. By 2003 36.5% of the social rented housing stock was held by housing associations.<sup id="cite_ref-jrf-20030910_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jrf-20030910-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Housing associations may be constituted using various forms of <a href="/wiki/Legal_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal entity">legal entity</a>. Many are <a href="/wiki/Industrial_and_provident_society" title="Industrial and provident society">industrial and provident societies</a>, but there are also <a href="/wiki/Charitable_trusts_in_English_law" title="Charitable trusts in English law">trusts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Housing_cooperative#United_Kingdom" title="Housing cooperative">co-operatives</a> and companies. They may or may not be registered <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization">charities</a>.\n</p><p><b>Registered social landlord</b> (RSL) is the technical name for social landlords that in England were formerly registered with the Housing Corporation, or in Wales with the Welsh Government. From 2010 to 2012, associations were termed registered providers under the <a href="/wiki/Housing_and_Regeneration_Act_2008" title="Housing and Regeneration Act 2008">Housing and Regeneration Act 2008</a>, irrespective of status (private, public, for-profit or not-for-profit). As of  2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Housing_association&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the terms registered social landlord and <b>private registered providers of social housing</b> are both used as alternative names for housing association.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Housing associations are generally considered as private entities in that they are not owned or directly controlled by the state. This status, however, has been challenged by a number of legal rulings. In 2004 the British government accepted an EU ruling that considered housing associations as public bodies for the purposes of procurement. \n</p><p>Subsequently, the <a href="/wiki/English_High_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="English High Court">English High Court</a> in <i>Weaver v. London and Quadrant Housing Trust [2008]</i> ruled that housing associations were public authorities and as a result could be subject to <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a> in certain circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The court stated that the housing association sector was \'permeated by state control and influence with a view to meeting the Government\'s aims for affordable housing, and in which RSLs work side by side with, and can in a very real sense be said to take the place of, local authorities\'.\n</p><p>This issue had wider political significance since housing associations\' borrowing (which stood at approximately \xc2\xa330 billion in 2006)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> does not contribute to the UK\'s public sector borrowing requirement, the control of which is both a stated government objective and part of the EU\'s criteria for membership of the European single currency.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A feature of housing associations is that, although the larger housing associations usually have paid staff, a committee or board of management made up of volunteers, or paid non-executive members, has overall responsibility for the work of the organisation. A board might include residents, representatives from local authorities and community groups, business people and politicians. There are more than 30,000 voluntary board members running housing associations throughout <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_and_regulation">Funding and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Funding and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Housing associations\' day-to-day activities are funded by rent and service charges payments made by, or on behalf of, those living in its properties. In this sense, housing associations are run as commercial entities and the majority do not depend on donations for their general activities. \n</p><p>New housing generally receives economic subsidies, the source of which will depend on where the association is based:\n</p>\n<ul><li>In England, housing associations are funded and regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Homes_and_Communities_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Homes and Communities Agency">Homes and Communities Agency</a> (HCA), the exception being funding in London which from April 2012 is the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The HCA\'s predecessor until 2008 was the <a href="/wiki/Housing_Corporation" title="Housing Corporation">Housing Corporation</a>. The Housing Corporation\'s regulatory role was split out to a separate body from 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Tenant_Services_Authority" title="Tenant Services Authority">Tenant Services Authority</a> (TSA), but merged again into the HCA from April 2012.</li>\n<li>In Northern Ireland, the same role is carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Housing_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Housing Executive">Northern Ireland Housing Executive</a>.</li>\n<li>In Scotland, this function is fulfilled by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>.</li>\n<li>In Wales, the regulation and funding of housing associations is carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>Subsidies for new homes (often termed \'social housing grant\') amount to sizeable public investments. In its 2008\xe2\x80\x9311 prospectus, the Housing Corporation stated that in the three-year period to 2011 investment would be "at least \xc2\xa38 billion".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The majority of this would go to housing associations for use in development projects. Since 2003, in an effort to seek greater value for money, much of the funding by the Housing Corporation for new house building has been channelled to fewer than 80 "developing housing associations" that have achieved "partner status" through Partner Programme Agreements. Long term <a href="/wiki/Lender_option_borrower_option" title="Lender option borrower option">lender option borrower option</a> loans (LOBOs) have been taken out in the past by housing associations.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Housing associations borrow money to pay for new homes and improvements. After the <a href="/wiki/Housing_Act_1988" title="Housing Act 1988">Housing Act 1988</a>, the proportion of the cost of new homes met by capital grant was scaled back by the Government, so borrowing became the primary source of funding for investment. Much of this was simply borrowed from banks and <a href="/wiki/Building_society" title="Building society">building societies</a>, but after the <a href="/wiki/Late-2000s_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-2000s financial crisis">late-2000s financial crisis</a> these institutions ceased to offer long-term loans, so developing associations are increasingly turning to <a href="/wiki/Corporate_bond" title="Corporate bond">corporate bonds</a> to raise funds for expansion.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The HCA implemented a new Government policy of "affordable rents" for its 2011\xe2\x80\x9315 funding round, requiring associations to set rents at up to 80% of market rents, so that less up-front capital subsidy would be required. In September 2013, a group of London Boroughs initiated a <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a> to challenge this policy.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_bodies">Industry bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industry bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are four industry bodies representing housing associations working in the UK, each covering a respective country. They are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>England \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/National_Housing_Federation" title="National Housing Federation">National Housing Federation</a> (NHF)</li>\n<li>Scotland \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Federation_of_Housing_Associations&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Scottish Federation of Housing Associations (page does not exist)">Scottish Federation of Housing Associations</a> (SFHA)</li>\n<li>Wales \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_Housing_Cymru&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Community Housing Cymru (page does not exist)">Community Housing Cymru</a></li>\n<li>Northern Ireland \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Ireland_Federation_of_Housing_Associations&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Northern Ireland Federation of Housing Associations (page does not exist)">Northern Ireland Federation of Housing Associations</a> (NIFHA)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Housing_Federation" title="National Housing Federation">NHF</a> (formerly the National Federation of Housing Associations) claimed that at the start of 2003 they had around 1400 non-profit housing organisations in their membership, owning or managing approximately 1.8 million homes across England.\n</p><p>In the 2000s, some larger associations formed regional clubs for purposes including lobbying government bodies. The <a href="/wiki/G15_(housing_associations)" title="G15 (housing associations)">G15</a> group of London\xe2\x80\x99s largest associations<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> was followed by <b>East Seven</b> in East Anglia.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
