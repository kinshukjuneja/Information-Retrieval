navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the sociopolitical concept encompassing developed nations where English is the primary language. For other nations where English is an official language or widely spoken, see <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a> or <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_where_English_is_an_official_language" class="mw-redirect" title="List of countries where English is an official language">List of countries where English is an official language</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p><p>The <b>Anglosphere</b> is the set of <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking</a> nations that share common cultural and historical roots with the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-definition_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-definition-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ipol2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipol2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and which today maintain close cultural, political, diplomatic and military cooperation. While the nations included in different sources vary, the Anglosphere is usually not considered to include all countries where English is an official language, although the nations that are commonly included were all once part of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Most definitions include the five main countries, namely the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The term can also cover the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a><sup id="cite_ref-Anglosphere_Prison_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anglosphere_Prison-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and countries in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> where <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> is widely spoken, including <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELloyd2000_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELloyd2000-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" (<i>The Anglosphere Challenge</i>)</div></div></div>\n<p>The term <i>Anglosphere</i> was first coined, but not explicitly defined, by the science fiction writer <a href="/wiki/Neal_Stephenson" title="Neal Stephenson">Neal Stephenson</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Diamond_Age" title="The Diamond Age">The Diamond Age</a></i>, published in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-wordspy_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wordspy-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> John Lloyd adopted the term in 2000 and defined it as including the United States and the United Kingdom along with English-speaking Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Ireland, South Africa and the <a href="/wiki/British_West_Indies" title="British West Indies">British West Indies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELloyd2000_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELloyd2000-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Webster%27s_Dictionary" title="Webster&#39;s Dictionary">Merriam-Webster</a> dictionary defines the Anglosphere as "the countries of the world in which the English language and cultural values predominate".<sup and the United Kingdom) maintain a close affinity of cultural, diplomatic and military links with one another. All are aligned under such programs as:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/UKUSA_Agreement" title="UKUSA Agreement">UKUSA Agreement</a> (signals intelligence),</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Five_Eyes" id="Public_relations">Public relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Public relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Public opinion research has found that people in the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia and New Zealand consistently rank each other\'s countries as their country\'s most important allies in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LowyAus_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LowyAus-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Relations have traditionally been warm between Anglosphere countries, with bilateral partnerships such as those between <a href="/wiki/Australia%E2%80%93New_Zealand_relations" title="Australia\xe2\x80\x93New Zealand relations">Australia and New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States%E2%80%93Canada_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="United States\xe2\x80\x93Canada relations">the USA and Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="United States\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom relations">the USA and UK</a> constituting among the most successful partnerships in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Favourability ratings tend to be overwhelmingly positive between countries within a subset of the Anglosphere known as <a href="/wiki/CANZUK" title="CANZUK">CANZUK</a> (consisting of Canada, Australia, New Zealand and the United Kingdom),<sup id="cite_ref-negUS_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-negUS-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BrexitRelations_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrexitRelations-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LowyAus_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LowyAus-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> whose members form part of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> and retain Elizabeth II as head of state. While the <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">United Kingdom\'s decision to leave the European Union</a> <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">in 2016</a> has had little impact on its favourability ratings with other members of the Anglosphere,<sup id="cite_ref-negUS_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-negUS-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BrexitRelations_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrexitRelations-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LowyAus_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LowyAus-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> there has been a marked drop in the United States favourability ratings with other Anglosphere nations since <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2016" title="United States presidential election, 2016">the election</a> of <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_the_United_States" title="List of Presidents of the United States">45th</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-negUS_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-negUS-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LowyAus_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LowyAus-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USindicator_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USindicator-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, the United States had negative favourability ratings with the CANZUK countries.<sup id="cite_ref-negUS_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-negUS-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USindicator_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USindicator-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proponents">Proponents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proponents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The American businessman <a href="/wiki/James_C._Bennett" title="James C. Bennett">James C. Bennett</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> a proponent of the idea that there is something special about the cultural and legal traditions of English-speaking nations, writes in his 2004 book <i>The Anglosphere Challenge</i>:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Anglosphere, as a network civilization without a corresponding political form, has necessarily imprecise boundaries. Geographically, the densest nodes of the Anglosphere are found in the United States and the United Kingdom. English-speaking Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Ireland and English-speaking South Africa (who constitute a very small minority in that country) are also significant populations. The English-speaking Caribbean, English-speaking <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a> and the English-speaking educated populations in Africa and India constitute other important nodes.</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>James C. Bennett.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBennett200480_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBennett200480-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Bennett argues that there are two challenges confronting his concept of the Anglosphere. The first is finding ways to cope with rapid technological advancement and the second is the geopolitical challenges created by what he assumes will be an increasing gap between anglophone prosperity and economic struggles elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>British historian <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Roberts_(historian)" title="Andrew Roberts (historian)">Andrew Roberts</a> claims that the Anglosphere has been central in the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. He goes on to contend that anglophone unity is necessary for the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Islamism" title="Islamism">Islamism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to a 2003 profile in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, historian <a href="/wiki/Robert_Conquest" title="Robert Conquest">Robert Conquest</a> favoured a British withdrawal from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in favour of creating "a much looser association of English-speaking nations, known as the \'Anglosphere\'".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown2003_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown2003-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>New Zealand historian <a href="/wiki/James_Belich_(historian)" title="James Belich (historian)">James Belich</a> connected patterns of growth in the industrialisation of the United States and the United Kingdom with former <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> New Zealand, Australia, Canada and South Africa, and more loosely to growth in former UK constituent country Ireland, as well as British-allied <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, during the 19th and early to mid-20th century, in his book <i>Replenishing the Earth</i>. He used the term "Anglo-World" to refer to the US, UK and former Dominions, arguing that the experience and present reality of former British colonies like <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> differ in substantial and important ways from this core group of countries.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Ignatieff" title="Michael Ignatieff">Michael Ignatieff</a> wrote in an exchange with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Conquest" title="Robert Conquest">Robert Conquest</a>, published by the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Review_of_Books" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Review of Books">New York Review of Books</a></i>, that the term neglects the evolution of fundamental legal and cultural differences between the US and the UK, and the ways in which UK and European norms have been drawn closer together during Britain\'s membership in the EU through regulatory harmonisation. Of Conquest\'s view of the Anglosphere, Ignatieff writes: "He seems to believe that Britain should either <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">withdraw from Europe</a> or refuse all further measures of cooperation, which would jeopardize Europe\'s real achievements. He wants Britain to throw in its lot with a union of English-speaking peoples, and I believe this to be a romantic illusion".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConquestReply_by_Ignatieff2000_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConquestReply_by_Ignatieff2000-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Cohen" title="Nick Cohen">Nick Cohen</a> wrote in an article titled "It\xe2\x80\x99s a Eurosceptic fantasy that the \xe2\x80\x98Anglosphere\xe2\x80\x99 wants Brexit" for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator" title="The Spectator">The Spectator</a></i>\xe2\x80\x99s Coffee House blog: "\xe2\x80\x98Anglosphere\xe2\x80\x99 is just the right\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">PC</a> replacement for what we used to call in blunter times \xe2\x80\x98the white Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> He repeated this criticism in another article for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Similar criticism was presented by other critics such as Canadian academic Sr\xc4\x91an Vu\xc4\x8deti\xc4\x87.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2018, amidst the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Brexit_referendum" class="mw-redirect" title="Brexit referendum">Brexit referendum</a>, two British professors of public policy <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kenny_(political_scientist)" title="Michael Kenny (political scientist)">Michael Kenny</a> and Nick Pearce published a critical scholarly monograph titled <i>Shadows of Empire: The Anglosphere in British Politics</i> (<a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">ISBN</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/978-1509516612" title="Special:BookSources/978-1509516612">978-1509516612</a>). In one of a series of accompanying opinion pieces, they questioned:<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The tragedy of the different national orientations that have emerged in British politics after empire\xe2\x80\x94whether pro-European, Anglo-American, Anglospheric or some combination of these\xe2\x80\x94is that none of them has yet been the compelling, coherent and popular answer to the country\xe2\x80\x99s most important question: How should Britain find its way in the wider, modern world?\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>They stated in another article:<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Meanwhile, the other core English-speaking countries to which the Anglosphere refers, show no serious inclination to join the UK in forging new political and economic alliances.
