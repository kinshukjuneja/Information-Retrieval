\xe2\x86\x92</td></tr></tbody></table></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>M25</b> or <b>London Orbital Motorway</b> is a 117-mile (188&#160;km) <a href="/wiki/Motorway" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorway">motorway</a> that encircles almost all of <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>, England (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/North_Ockendon" title="North Ockendon">North Ockendon</a>), in the United Kingdom. An ambitious concept to build <a href="/wiki/London_Ringways" title="London Ringways">four concentric ring roads</a> around London was first mooted in the 1960s. A few sections of the outer two rings were constructed in the early 1970s, but the plan was abandoned and the sections were later integrated to form a single ring which became the M25, aka London Ring Road, finally completed in 1986.\n</p><p>It is one of the busiest of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_motorways_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of motorways in the United Kingdom">British motorway network</a>: 196,000 vehicles were recorded on a busy day near <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a> in 2003<sup id="cite_ref-Motorway_traffic_up_4%_on_2003_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Motorway_traffic_up_4%_on_2003-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the western half experienced an average daily flow of 147,000 vehicles in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The M25, plus the short non-motorway A282 which joins the two ends of the M25 across the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Crossing" title="Dartford Crossing">Dartford Crossing</a>, is Europe\'s second longest orbital road after the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_10" title="Bundesautobahn 10">Berliner Ring</a>, which is 122 miles (196&#160;km).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Originally built almost wholly as a dual three-lane motorway, much of the motorway has been widened: to dual four lanes for almost half, to a dual five-lanes section between junctions 12 and 14 and a dual six-lane section between junctions 14 and 15. Further widening is in progress of minor sections with plans for <a href="/wiki/Managed_motorways_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Managed motorways in the United Kingdom">managed motorways</a> in many others.<sup id="cite_ref-bp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To the east of London the two ends of the M25 are joined to complete a loop by the non-motorway A282 <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Crossing" title="Dartford Crossing">Dartford Crossing</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">River Thames</a> between <a href="/wiki/Thurrock" title="Thurrock">Thurrock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dartford" title="Dartford">Dartford</a>. This crossing, which consists of twin two-lane tunnels and the four-lane <a href="/wiki/QE2_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="QE2 Bridge">QE2 (Queen Elizabeth II) bridge</a>, is named <i>Canterbury Way</i>. Passage across the bridge or through the tunnels is subject to a <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">toll</a>, its level depending on the kind of vehicle. This stretch being non-motorway, it allows traffic, including that not permitted to use motorways, to cross the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Woolwich_Ferry" title="Woolwich Ferry">Woolwich Ferry</a>; the only crossing further to the east is <a href="/wiki/Gravesend-Tilbury_Ferry" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravesend-Tilbury Ferry">a passenger ferry</a> between <a href="/wiki/Gravesend,_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravesend, Kent">Gravesend</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tilbury" title="Tilbury">Tilbury</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>. However, in 2017 <a href="/wiki/Highways_England" title="Highways England">Highways England</a> published plans to build another motorway-grade Thames tunnel to the east of <a href="/wiki/Gravesend,_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravesend, Kent">Gravesend</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grays,_Essex" class="mw-redirect" title="Grays, Essex">Grays</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Thames_Crossing" title="Lower Thames Crossing">Lower Thames Crossing</a>, in order to relieve congestion on the A282 <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Crossing" title="Dartford Crossing">Dartford Crossing</a> and connect the M25 at North Ockendon in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> with the <a href="/wiki/M2_motorway_(Great_Britain)" title="M2 motorway (Great Britain)">M2</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At Junction 5, the clockwise carriageway of the M25 is routed off the  main north\xe2\x80\x93south <a href="/wiki/Dual_carriageway" title="Dual carriageway">dual carriageway</a> onto the main east\xe2\x80\x93west dual carriageway with the main north\xe2\x80\x93south carriageway becoming the <a href="/wiki/A21_road_(England)" title="A21 road (England)">A21</a>.  In the opposite direction, to the east of the point where the M25 diverges from the main east\xe2\x80\x93west carriageway, that carriageway become the <a href="/wiki/M26_motorway" title="M26 motorway">M26 motorway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-J5_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-J5-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The radial distance from London (taken as <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross" title="Charing Cross">Charing Cross</a>) varies from 12.5 miles (20.1&#160;km) in <a href="/wiki/Potters_Bar" title="Potters Bar">Potters Bar</a> to 19.5 miles (31.4&#160;km) in <a href="/wiki/Byfleet,_Surrey" class="mw-redirect" title="Byfleet, Surrey">Byfleet</a>. Three Greater London boroughs (Enfield, Hillingdon and Havering) <a href="/wiki/List_of_Greater_London_boundary_changes" title="List of Greater London boundary changes">have realigned their boundaries</a> to the M25 for minor stretches; while in others, most notably in Essex and Surrey, the radial gap between Greater London and the motorway reaches 7.8 miles (12.6&#160;km),<sup id="cite_ref-grf_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grf-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> neither of which coincide with the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Green_Belt" title="Metropolitan Green Belt">Metropolitan Green Belt</a>.  Major towns listed as destinations (right), in various counties, adjoin the M25. <a href="/wiki/North_Ockendon" title="North Ockendon">North Ockendon</a> is the only settlement of Greater London situated outside the M25. In 2004, following an opinion poll, the <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a> mooted for consultation alignment of the Greater London boundary with the M25.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> "Inside the M25" and "outside/beyond the M25" are colloquial, looser alternatives to "Greater London" sometimes used in haulage.  The <a href="/wiki/Communications_Act_2003" title="Communications Act 2003">Communications Act 2003</a> explicitly uses the M25 as the boundary in requiring a proportion of television programmes to be made outside the London area.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two <a href="/wiki/Motorway_service_area" title="Motorway service area">motorway service areas</a> are on the M25, and two others are directly accessible from it. Those on the M25 are <a href="/wiki/Clacket_Lane_services" title="Clacket Lane services">Clacket Lane</a> between junctions 5 and 6 (in the south-east) and <a href="/wiki/Cobham_services" title="Cobham services">Cobham</a> between junctions 9 and 10 (in the south-west). Those directly accessible from it are <a href="/wiki/South_Mimms_services" title="South Mimms services">South Mimms</a> off junction 23 (to the north of London) and <a href="/wiki/Thurrock_services" title="Thurrock services">Thurrock</a> off junction 31 (to the east of London). Cobham services opened on 13 September 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-cobhamServices_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cobhamServices-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CobhamOpen_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CobhamOpen-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Originally, the M25 was unlit except for sections around Heathrow, major interchanges and Junctions 23\xe2\x80\x9330. Originally, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_vapor_lamp" class="mw-redirect" title="Sodium vapor lamp">low pressure sodium</a> (SOX) lighting was the most prominent technology used, but widening projects from the 1990s onwards have all used high-pressure sodium (SON) lighting and this has diminished the original installations. By 2014 only one significant stretch was still SOX-lit (Junction 25\xe2\x80\x9326) and the units were removed the same year.\n</p><p>The motorway passes through five counties. Junctions 1A\xe2\x80\x935 are in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, 6\xe2\x80\x9314 are in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, 15\xe2\x80\x9316 are in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>, 17\xe2\x80\x9325 are in <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>, and 26\xe2\x80\x9331 are in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>. Policing of the road is carried out by an integrated policing group made up of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police_Service" title="Metropolitan Police Service">Metropolitan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thames_Valley_Police" title="Thames Valley Police">Thames Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essex_Police" title="Essex Police">Essex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent_Police" title="Kent Police">Kent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire_Constabulary" title="Hertfordshire Constabulary">Hertfordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surrey_Police" title="Surrey Police">Surrey</a> forces.\n</p><p>The M25 is one of Europe\'s busiest motorways. In 2003, a maximum of 196,000 vehicles a day were recorded on the motorway just south of <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow Airport</a> between junctions 13 and 14.<sup id="cite_ref-Motorway_traffic_up_4%_on_2003_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Motorway_traffic_up_4%_on_2003-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M25_motorway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plans">Plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M25_motorway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ringways_3_%26_4.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Ringways_3_%26_4.png/220px-Ringways_3_%26_4.png" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Ringways_3_%26_4.png/330px-Ringways_3_%26_4.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Ringways_3_%26_4.png/440px-Ringways_3_%26_4.png 2x" data-file-width="1871" data-file-height="1871" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ringways_3_%26_4.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Ringways 3 &amp; 4 showing sections combined to form the M25</div></div></div>\n<p>The idea of an orbital road around London was first proposed early in the 20th century. An outer orbital road around London had first been proposed in 1913, and was re-examined as a motorway route in <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bressey" title="Charles Bressey">Sir Charles Bressey\'s</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Lutyens" title="Edwin Lutyens">Sir Edwin Lutyens\'</a> <i>The Highway Development Survey, 1937</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Abercrombie" title="Patrick Abercrombie">Sir Patrick Abercrombie\'s</a> <i><a href="/wiki/County_of_London_Plan" title="County of London Plan">County of London Plan</a>, 1943</i> and <i>Greater London Plan, 1944</i> proposed a series of five roads encircling the capital.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2001148_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2001148-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The northern sections of the M25 follow a similar route to the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> <a href="/wiki/Outer_London_Defence_Ring" title="Outer London Defence Ring">Outer London Defence Ring</a>, a concentric series of tanks and pillboxes designed to slow down a potential German invasion of the capital.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton201519_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton201519-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Little was done to progress these plans until the 1960s when the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a> developed its <a href="/wiki/London_Ringways" title="London Ringways">London Ringways</a> plan<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> consisting of four "rings" around the capital. Sections of the two outer rings&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Ringway_3" title="Ringway 3">Ringway 3</a> (the \'<a href="/wiki/M16_motorway" title="M16 motorway">M16 motorway</a>\') and <a href="/wiki/Ringway_4" title="Ringway 4">Ringway 4</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 were constructed in the early 1970s and were integrated into the single M25 orbital motorway. But the Ringways plan was hugely controversial owing to the destruction required for the inner two ring roads, (<a href="/wiki/Ringway_1" title="Ringway 1">Ringway 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ringway_2" title="Ringway 2">Ringway 2</a>). Parts of Ringway 1 were constructed (including <a href="/wiki/West_Cross_Route" title="West Cross Route">West Cross Route</a>), against stiff opposition, before the overall plan was abandoned in 1973 following pressure from residents in the threatened areas.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span 1973. The first section, between South Mimms and Potters Bar in Hertfordshire (junction 23 to junction 24) opened in September 1975 and was given the temporary general purpose road designation <a href="/wiki/A1178_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A1178 road">A1178</a> (a section of motorway-standard-road, originally the M16, which eventually was incorporated into the M25) was completed and operational before this. A Watford-avoiding route between the M1 and the A40 between north Watford and Denham was locally known as the Croxley Green/Rickmansworth bypass, and was operational about 1973/4; a section south of London (junction 6 to junction 8) opened in 1976. A section of Ringway 3 south of the river between Dartford and Swanley (junction 1 to junction 3) was constructed between 1974 and 1977. In 1975 the plans for Ringway 3 were modified to combine it with Ringway 4, the outermost Ringway. The M25 as a component of ringway 4, was first conceived to be an east-west road south of London to relieve the <a href="/wiki/A25_road" title="A25 road">A25</a>, and running parallel to it, with its eastern end following the route of what is now the <a href="/wiki/M26_motorway" title="M26 motorway">M26</a>. However, it was subsequently routed northwards towards the <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Tunnel" class="mw-redirect" title="Dartford Tunnel">Dartford Tunnel</a> to form, in conjunction with similar roads, including the M16 planned to the north of London, part of the London Orbital.<sup id="cite_ref-J5_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-J5-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The combined motorway was given the designation M25 which had originally been intended for the southern and western part of Ringway 4 and the M16 designation was dropped.  The section of Ringway 3 west of South Mimms anti-clockwise around London to Swanley in Kent was cancelled. The stages were not constructed contiguously but in small sections. As the orbital road developed the sections were linked. Each section was presented to planning authorities in its own right and was individually justified, with almost 40 public inquiries relating to sections of the route. Maps at this time depicting these short sections named the route as the M16 but this changed before completion.\n</p><p>The section from Potters Bar to the <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Tunnel" class="mw-redirect" title="Dartford Tunnel">Dartford Tunnel</a> was constructed between 1979 and 1982. Construction of the M25 continued in stages until its completion in 1986. <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> officially opened the M25 on 29 October 1986, with a ceremony in the section between J22 and J23 (<a href="/wiki/London_Colney" title="London Colney">London Colney</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Mimms" title="South Mimms">South Mimms</a>). The initial tenders for the construction of the M25 totalled \xc2\xa3631.9 million. This did not include compulsory purchase of land and subsequent upgrades and repairs.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span Borough of Enfield">Enfield</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>Soon after the motorway opened in 1986 traffic levels exceeded the maximum design capacity and in 1990 the Secretary of State for Transport announced plans to widen the whole of the M25 to four lanes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> By 1993 the motorway, which was designed for a maximum of 88,000 vehicles per day, was carrying 200,000 vehicles per day.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> 15% of UK motorway traffic volume was on the M25 and there were plans to add six lanes to the section from Junctions 12 to 15 as well as widening the rest of the motorway to four lanes.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In parts, particularly the western third this plan went ahead, due to consistent congestion. Again, however, plans to widen further sections to eight lanes (four each way) were scaled back in 2009 in response to rising costs. The plans were reinstated in the agreed <a href="/wiki/Highways_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Highways Agency">Highways Agency</a> 2013-14 business plan.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1995 a contract was awarded to widen the section between Junctions 8 and 10 from six to eight lanes for a cost of \xc2\xa393.4 million<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Motorway_Incident_Detection_and_Automatic_Signalling" title="Motorway Incident Detection and Automatic Signalling">Motorway Incident Detection and Automatic Signalling</a> (MIDAS) system was introduced to the M25 from Junction 10 to Junction 15 at a cost of \xc2\xa313.5m in 1995. This was then extended to Junction 16 at a cost of \xc2\xa311.7m in 2002. This consists of a distributed network of traffic and weather sensors, <a href="/wiki/Speed_camera" class="mw-redirect" title="Speed camera">speed cameras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speed_limit#Variable_speed_limits" title="Speed limit">variable-speed</a> signs that control traffic speeds with little human supervision, and has improved traffic flow slightly, reducing the amount of start-stop driving.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CongestionM25.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/CongestionM25.jpg/220px-CongestionM25.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/CongestionM25.jpg/330px-CongestionM25.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/CongestionM25.jpg/440px-CongestionM25.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1269" data-file-height="870" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CongestionM25.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The M25 between junctions 7 (<a href="/wiki/M23_motorway" title="M23 motorway">M23</a>) and 6 (<a href="/wiki/A22_road" title="A22 road">A22</a>) near <a href="/wiki/Redhill,_Surrey" title="Redhill, Surrey">Redhill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>. The signs are indicating an advisory reduced speed of 40&#160;mph (64&#160;km/h) due to congestion.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1995 there was a proposal to widen the section close to Heathrow Airport  to fourteen lanes. At the <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Terminal Five</a> public inquiry a Highways Agency official said that the widening was needed to accommodate traffic to the proposed new terminal, however the transport minister said that no such evidence had been given.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Environmental groups objected to the decision to go ahead with a scheme that would create the widest motorways in the UK without holding a <a href="/wiki/Public_inquiry" title="Public inquiry">public inquiry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The decision was again deferred. A decision to go-ahead was given for a ten-lane scheme in 1998<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and the \xc2\xa3148 million \'M25 Jct 12 to 15 Widening\' contract was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Beatty" title="Balfour Beatty">Balfour Beatty</a> in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The scheme was completed in 2005 as dual-five lanes between Junctions 12 and 14 and dual-six lanes from Junctions 14 to 15.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Autoroute_M25.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Autoroute_M25.jpg/220px-Autoroute_M25.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Autoroute_M25.jpg/330px-Autoroute_M25.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Autoroute_M25.jpg/440px-Autoroute_M25.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Autoroute_M25.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The M25 motorway looking south between junctions 14 and 15, near <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a>. It was designed to reduce the amount of traffic queueing on the slip roads at busy periods, particularly traffic from the clockwise M25 joining the northbound A12 where the queue extended onto the inside lane of the Motorway.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Design.2C_Build.2C_Finance_and_Operate_.28DBFO.29_contract"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Design,_Build,_Finance_and_Operate_(DBFO)_contract">Design, Build, Finance and Operate (DBFO) contract</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M25_motorway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design, Build, Finance and Operate (DBFO) contract">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2006 the Highways Agency proposed to widen 63 miles (101&#160;km) of M25 from six to eight lanes, between junctions 5\xe2\x80\x936 and 16\xe2\x80\x9330 as part of a Design, Build, Finance and Operate (DBFO) project.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> A shortlist of contractors was announced in October 2006 for the project which was expected to cost \xc2\xa34.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Contractors were asked to resubmit their bids in January 2008<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and in June 2009 the new transport minister indicated that the cost had risen to \xc2\xa35.5 billion and the benefit to cost ratio had dropped considerably.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In January 2009 the government announced that plans to widen the sections from Junction 5\xe2\x80\x937 and from 23\xe2\x80\x9327 had been \'scrapped\' and that hard shoulder running would be introduced instead.  However widening was reinstated to four lanes in the 2013\xe2\x80\x9314 Highways Agency Business Plan.<sup id="cite_ref-bp_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2009 a \xc2\xa36.2 billion M25 DBFO <a href="/wiki/Private_finance_initiative" title="Private finance initiative">private finance initiative</a> contract<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> was awarded to Connect Plus to widen the sections between junctions 16 and 23 and between junctions 27 and 30 and maintain the M25 and the Dartford Crossing for a 30-year period.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Highways_Agency_Smart_Motorways_control_centre.jpg" frequent traffic jams. These have been the subject of so much comment from such an early stage that even at the official opening ceremony <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> complained about "those who carp and criticise". The jams have inspired jokes (e.g., "the world\'s first circular car park", "the London Orbital Car Park") and songs (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rea" title="Chris Rea">Chris Rea</a>\'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Road_to_Hell_(song)" title="The Road to Hell (song)">The Road to Hell</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The M25 plays a role in the comedy-fantasy novel <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Omens" title="Good Omens">Good Omens</a></i>, as "evidence for the hidden hand of Satan in the affairs of Man".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The demon character, Crowley, had manipulated the design of the M25 to resemble a Satanic <a href="/wiki/Sigil_(magic)" title="Sigil (magic)">sigil</a>, and tried to ensure it would anger as many people as possible to drive them off the path of good.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gaiman_2003_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaiman_2003-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The M25 enjoyed a more positive reputation among <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">ravers</a> in the late 1980s, when this new orbital motorway became a popular route to the parties that took place around the outskirts of London.
