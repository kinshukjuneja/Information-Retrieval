href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">KOTS</span>-wohldz</i></a>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;w&#39; in &#39;wind&#39;">w</span><span title="/\xc9\x99/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">\xc9\x99</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i Cotswold Hills, a range of rolling hills which rise from the meadows of the upper Thames to an <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpment</a>, known as the Cotswold Edge, above the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Valley" title="Severn Valley">Severn Valley</a> and Evesham Vale. The area is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> that creates a type of <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> habitat rare in the UK and that is quarried for the golden coloured Cotswold stone.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It contains unique features derived from the use of this mineral; the predominantly rural landscape contains stone-built villages, historical towns and stately homes and gardens.\n</p><p>Designated as an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) in 1966,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the Cotswolds covers 787&#160;sq&#160;mi (2,040&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) and is the second largest protected landscape in England (second to the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-cotswolds.info_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswolds.info-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and the largest AONB.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Its boundaries are roughly 25 miles (40&#160;km) across and 90 miles (140&#160;km) long, stretching south-west from just south of <a href="/wiki/Stratford-upon-Avon" title="Stratford-upon-Avon">Stratford-upon-Avon</a> to just south of <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>. It lies across the boundaries of several English counties; mainly <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>.  The highest point of the region is <a href="/wiki/Cleeve_Hill" title="Cleeve Hill">Cleeve Hill</a> at 1,083&#160;ft (330&#160;m),<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> just to the east of <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>.\n</p><p>The hills give their name to the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_District" title="Cotswold District">Cotswold</a> local-government district, formed on 1 April 1974, which administers over half of the area. Most of the District is in the county of <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>; some 80% of it is within the Cotswold Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty. The main town is <a href="/wiki/Cirencester" title="Cirencester">Cirencester</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_District_Council" title="Cotswold District Council">Cotswold District Council</a> offices are located in that community.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The population of the 450 square mile District,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> primarily in Gloucestershire, was about 83,000 in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The much larger area referred to as the Cotswolds encompasses nearly 800 square miles,<sup id="cite_ref-cotswolds.com_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswolds.com-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> over five counties: Gloucestershire, Oxfordshire, Warwickshire, Wiltshire, and Worcestershire.<sup id="cite_ref-butterfield.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-butterfield.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The population of this Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty was 139,000 in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-cotswoldsaonb.org.uk_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswoldsaonb.org.uk-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is evidence of <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> settlement from burial chambers on Cotswold Edge, and there are remains of Bronze and Iron Age forts.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Later the Romans built villas, such as at Chedworth,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> settlements such as Gloucester, and paved the Celtic path later known as <a href="/wiki/Fosse_Way" title="Fosse Way">Fosse Way</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, thanks to the breed of sheep known as the Cotswold Lion, the Cotswolds became prosperous from the wool trade with the continent, with much of the money made from wool directed towards the building of churches. The most successful era for the wool trade was 1250-1350; much of the wool at that time was sold to Italian merchants. The area still preserves numerous large, handsome Cotswold Stone "wool churches". The affluent area in the 21st century has attracted wealthy Londoners and others who own second homes there or have chosen to retire to the Cotswolds.<sup id="cite_ref-butterfield.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-butterfield.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The name Cotswold is popularly attributed the meaning "sheep enclosure in rolling hillsides",<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> incorporating the term, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wolds" title="The Wolds">wold</a></i>, meaning hills. Compare also <a href="/wiki/The_Weald" class="mw-redirect" title="The Weald">the Weald</a> from the Saxon/German word <i>Wald</i> meaning \'forest\'. However, the <a href="/wiki/English_Place-Name_Society" title="English Place-Name Society">English Place-Name Society</a> has for many years accepted that the term Cotswold is derived from <i>Codesuualt</i> of the 12th century or other variations on this form, the etymology of which was given, \'Cod\'s-wold\', which is \'Cod\'s high open land\'.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Cod was interpreted as an Old English personal name, which may be recognised in further names: <a href="/wiki/Cutsdean" title="Cutsdean">Cutsdean</a>, Codeswellan, and Codesbyrig, some of which date back to the eighth century AD.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It has subsequently been noticed that "Cod" could derive philologically from a Brittonic female cognate <i>"Cuda"</i>, a hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Mother_goddess" title="Mother goddess">mother goddess</a> in <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic mythology</a> postulated to have been worshipped in the Cotswold region.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg/220px-Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg/330px-Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg/440px-Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3427" data-file-height="2233" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bibury_Cottages_in_the_Cotswolds_-_June_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bibury" title="Bibury">Bibury</a>, a typical Cotswold village</div></div></div>\n<p>The spine of the Cotswolds runs southwest to northeast through six counties, particularly Gloucestershire, west Oxfordshire and south western Warwickshire. The northern and western edges of the Cotswolds are marked by steep <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpments</a> down to the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">Severn valley</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Warwickshire" title="River Avon, Warwickshire">Warwickshire Avon</a>. This feature, known as the Cotswold escarpment, or sometimes the Cotswold Edge, is a result of the uplifting (tilting) of the limestone layer, exposing its broken edge.<sup id="cite_ref-cotsstone_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotsstone-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This is a <a href="/wiki/Cuesta" title="Cuesta">cuesta</a>, in geological terms. The <a href="/wiki/Dip_slope" title="Dip slope">dip slope</a> is to the southeast. On the eastern boundary lies the city of <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> and on the west is <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a>. To the southeast, the upper reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Valley" title="Thames Valley">Thames Valley</a> and towns such as <a href="/wiki/Lechlade" title="Lechlade">Lechlade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tetbury" title="Tetbury">Tetbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fairford" title="Fairford">Fairford</a> are often considered to mark the limit of this region. To the south the Cotswolds, with the characteristic uplift of the Cotswold Edge, reach beyond <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>, and towns such as <a href="/wiki/Chipping_Sodbury" title="Chipping Sodbury">Chipping Sodbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marshfield,_Gloucestershire" title="Marshfield, Gloucestershire">Marshfield</a> share elements of Cotswold character.\n</p><p>The area is characterised by attractive small towns and villages built of the underlying Cotswold stone (a yellow <a href="/wiki/Oolite" title="Oolite">oolitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-cotsstone_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotsstone-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This limestone is rich in <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>, particularly of fossilised <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>. Cotswold towns include <a href="/wiki/Bourton-on-the-Water" title="Bourton-on-the-Water">Bourton-on-the-Water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broadway,_Worcestershire" title="Broadway, Worcestershire">Broadway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burford" title="Burford">Burford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chipping_Norton" title="Chipping Norton">Chipping Norton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricklade" title="Cricklade">Cricklade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dursley" title="Dursley">Dursley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malmesbury" title="Malmesbury">Malmesbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moreton-in-Marsh" title="Moreton-in-Marsh">Moreton-in-Marsh</a>, Nailsworth, <a href="/wiki/Northleach" title="Northleach">Northleach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stow-on-the-Wold" title="Stow-on-the-Wold">Stow-on-the-Wold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winchcombe" title="Winchcombe">Winchcombe</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cirencester" title="Cirencester">Cirencester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucester" title="Gloucester">Gloucester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a> are larger urban centres that border on, or are virtually surrounded by, the Cotswold AONB.\n</p><p>The town of <a href="/wiki/Chipping_Campden" title="Chipping Campden">Chipping Campden</a> is notable for being the home of the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> at the end of the 19th and beginning of the 20th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> William Morris lived occasionally in <a href="/wiki/Broadway_Tower,_Worcestershire" title="Broadway Tower, Worcestershire">Broadway Tower</a>, a folly, now part of a country park.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Chipping Campden also is known for the annual <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_Olimpick_Games" title="Cotswold Olimpick Games">Cotswold Olimpick Games</a>, a celebration of sports and games dating back to the early 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The nearly 800 square miles of the Cotswolds, roughly 80% farmlands,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> contains over 3,000 miles of footpaths and bridleways. There are also 4,000 miles of historic stone walls.<sup id="cite_ref-cotswolds.com_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswolds.com-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg/220px-Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg/330px-Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg/440px-Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1602" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Broadway_row_b_8294.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Row houses of Cotswold stone in Broadway, Worcestershire; the quaint buildings of the village attract numerous tourists</div></div></div>\n<p>Tourism is a significant part of the economy. The Cotswold District area alone gained over \xc2\xa3373 million from visitor spending on accommodation, \xc2\xa3157 million on local attractions and entertainments, and about \xc2\xa3100m on travel in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In the larger Cotswolds Tourism area, including Stroud, Cheltenham, Gloucester and Tewkesbury,<sup id="cite_ref-cotswold.gov.uk_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswold.gov.uk-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> tourism generated about \xc2\xa31 billion in 2016, providing 200,000 jobs. Some 38 million day visits were made to the Cotswold Tourism area that year.\n</p><p>A 2017 report on employment within the Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty, stated that the main sources of income were real estate, renting and business activities, manufacturing\nand wholesale &amp; retail trade repairs. Some 44% of residents were employed in these sectors.<sup id="cite_ref-cotswoldsaonb.org.uk_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswoldsaonb.org.uk-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Agriculture is also important. Some 86% of the land in the AONB is used for this purpose. The primary crops include barley, beans, oil seed rape and wheat, while the raising of sheep is also important; cows and pigs are also reared. The livestock sector has been declining since 2002, however.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the 2011 Census data for the Cotswolds,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> the wholesale\nand retail trade was the largest employer (15.8% of the workforce), followed by education (9.7%) and health and social work (9.3%). The report also indicates that a relatively higher\nproportion of residents were working in agriculture, forestry and fishing, accommodation and food services as well as in and professional, scientific and technical activities.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unemployment in the Cotswold District was among the lowest in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-cotswold.gov.uk_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswold.gov.uk-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A report in August 2017, showed only 315 unemployed persons, a slight decrease of five from a year earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing_development">Housing development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Housing development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In August 2018, the final decision was made for a Local Plan that would lead to the building of nearly 7,000 additional homes by 2031, in addition to over 3,000 already built. Areas for development include Cirencester, Bourton-on-the-Water, Down Ampney, Fairford, Kemble, Lechlade, Northleach, South Cerney, Stow-on-the-Wold, Tetbury and Moreton-in-Marsh. Some of the money received from developers will be earmarked for new infrastructure to support the increasing population.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_tourist_path">The tourist path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The tourist path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many travel guides direct tourists to <a href="/wiki/Chipping_Campden" title="Chipping Campden">Chipping Campden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stow-on-the-Wold" title="Stow-on-the-Wold">Stow-on-the-Wold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bourton-on-the-Water" title="Bourton-on-the-Water">Bourton-on-the-Water</a><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Broadway,_Worcestershire" title="Broadway, Worcestershire">Broadway, Worcestershire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bibury" title="Bibury">Bibury</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stanton,_Gloucestershire" title="Stanton, Gloucestershire">Stanton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Some of these locations can be very crowded at times. Roughly 300,000 people visit Bourton, per year, for example, with about half staying for a day or less.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A report in <i>Cotswold Life</i> magazine however, March 2018, named only two of those villages as among the "prettiest places" for tourists to visit:<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Blockley" title="Blockley">Blockley</a>, "with its peaceful nature and beautiful appearance ... golden Cotswold cottages and limestone walls punctuating the rolling hills..."</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lower_Slaughter" title="Lower Slaughter">Lower Slaughter</a>, "home to honey-coloured stone architecture, quaint little cottages and the most romantic street in Britain, Copse Hill Road".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Kingham" title="Kingham">Kingham</a>, with its "rows of unspoilt limestone cottages and open village greens ... a community you\xe2\x80\x99d expect to find in a story book".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mickleton,_Gloucestershire" title="Mickleton, Gloucestershire">Mickleton, Gloucestershire</a>, with an "abundance of black and white thatched cottages interspersed with limestone architecture - creating a picture perfect Cotswold scene".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bibury" title="Bibury">Bibury</a>, "one of England\xe2\x80\x99s most iconic hamlets and home to the peaceful River Coln and some of the most photographed houses in the country".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bourton-on-the-Water" title="Bourton-on-the-Water">Bourton-on-the-Water</a>, with its "tea rooms, museums, cosy pubs and picture-book houses ... the River Windrush trickles through the village ... [and] golden stone cottages built many centuries ago".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bredon" title="Bredon">Bredon</a>, by the River Avon, a "delightful village ... steeped in history, ... full of medieval barns, thatched cottages and Cotswold stone houses".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ashton_under_Hill" title="Ashton under Hill">Ashton under Hill</a>, with its history going back to the Roman occupation, "scattered with cosy timber-framed cottages and pretty Victorian houses".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Naunton" title="Naunton">Naunton</a>, a "a peaceful and unspoilt Cotswold village ... a relatively tourist-free place".</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cotswold_stone">Cotswold stone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cotswold stone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg/220px-Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg/330px-Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg/440px-Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanton_cottage_8348.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Typical stone cottage and wall in Stanton</div></div></div>\n<p>Cotswold stone is a yellow <a href="/wiki/Oolite" title="Oolite">oolitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. This limestone is rich in <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>, particularly of fossilised <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>. When weathered, the colour of buildings made or faced with this stone is often described as honey or golden.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The stone varies in colour from north to south, being honey-coloured in the north and north east of the region, as shown in Cotswold villages such as <a href="/wiki/Stanton,_Gloucestershire" title="Stanton, Gloucestershire">Stanton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broadway,_Worcestershire" title="Broadway, Worcestershire">Broadway</a>; golden-coloured in the central and southern areas, as shown in <a href="/wiki/Dursley" title="Dursley">Dursley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cirencester" title="Cirencester">Cirencester</a>; and pearly white in <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The rock outcrops at places on the Cotswold Edge; small quarries are common. The exposures are rarely sufficiently compact to be good for <a href="/wiki/Rock-climbing" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock-climbing">rock-climbing</a>, but an exception is Castle Rock, on <a href="/wiki/Cleeve_Hill" title="Cleeve Hill">Cleeve Hill</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>. Due to the rapid expansion of the Cotswolds in order for nearby areas to capitalize on increased house prices, well known ironstone villages, such as <a href="/wiki/Hook_Norton" title="Hook Norton">Hook Norton</a>, have even been claimed by some to be in the Cotswolds despite lacking key features of Cotswolds villages such as Cotswold stone and are instead built using a deep red/orange <a href="/wiki/Ironstone" title="Ironstone">ironstone</a>, known locally as Horton Stone.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; overflow:hidden; width:auto; max-width:788px;">\n<div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg" class="image" title="Rolling hills and farm fields that typify the Cotswolds landscape"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg/780px-Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg" width="780" height="229" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg/1170px-Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg/1560px-Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4188" data-file-height="1228" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="text-align:center"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cotswolds_Panorama_Fields.jpg" title="File:Cotswolds Panorama Fields.jpg"> </a></div>Rolling hills and farm fields that typify the Cotswolds landscape</div>\n</div></div>\n<p>The Cotswolds were designated as an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) in 1966, with an expansion on 21 December 1990 to 1,990 square kilometres (768&#160;sq&#160;mi). In 1991, all AONBs were measured again using modern methods. The official area of the Cotswolds AONB was increased to 2,038 square kilometres (787&#160;sq&#160;mi). In 2000, the government confirmed that AONBs had the same landscape quality and status as <a href="/wiki/National_Parks" class="mw-redirect" title="National Parks">National Parks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aonb_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aonb-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\nThe Cotswolds AONB, which is the largest in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, stretches from the border regions of South Warwickshire and Worcestershire, through West Oxfordshire and Gloucestershire, and takes in parts of Wiltshire, <a href="/wiki/Bath_and_North_East_Somerset" title="Bath and North East Somerset">Bath and North East Somerset</a> in the south.\nGloucestershire County Council is responsible for sixty-three percent of the AONB.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In the summer of 2018, the final draft of the Cotswolds AONB Management Plan 2018-2023 was out for consultation. The final text should be written after  14 August 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Cotswolds Conservation Board has the task of conserving and enhancing the AONB. Established under statute in 2004 as an independent public body, the Board carries out a range of work from securing funding for \'on the ground\' conservation projects, to providing a strategic overview of the area for key decision makers, such as planning officials. The Board is funded by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> and the seventeen local authorities that are covered by the AONB.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The landscape of the AONB is varied, including escarpment outliers, escarpments, rolling hills and valleys, enclosed limestone valleys, settled valleys, ironstone hills and valleys, high wolds and high wold valleys, high wold dip-slopes, dip-slope lowland and valleys, a Low limestone plateau, cornbrash lowlands, farmed slopes, a broad floodplain valley, a large pastoral lowland vale, a aettled unwooded vale and an unwooded vale.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While the beauty of the Cotswolds AONB is intertwined with that of the villages that seem almost to grow out of the landscape, the Cotswolds were primarily designated an <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> for the rare limestone grassland habitats as well as the old growth <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> woodlands that typify the area. These habitat areas are also the last refuge for many other flora and fauna, with some so endangered that they are protected under the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_and_Countryside_Act_1981" title="Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981">Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cleeve_Hill" title="Cleeve Hill">Cleeve Hill</a>, and its associated commons, is a fine example of a limestone grassland and it is one of the few locations where the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy_butterfly" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Burgundy butterfly">Duke of Burgundy butterfly</a> may still be found in abundance.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A June 2018 report stated that the AONB receives "23 million visitors a year, the third largest of any protected landscape".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Earlier that year, Environment secretary <a href="/wiki/Michael_Gove" title="Michael Gove">Michael Gove</a> announced that a panel would be formed to consider making some of the AONBs into National Parks. The review will file its report in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In April 2018, the Cotswolds Conservation Board had written to <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> "requesting that consideration be given to making the Cotswolds a National Park", according to Liz Eyre, Chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> This has led to some concern as stated by one member of the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_District_Council" title="Cotswold District Council">Cotswold District Council</a>, "National Park designation is a significant step further and raises the prospect of key decision making powers being taken away from democratically elected councillors".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> In other words, Cotswold District Council would no longer have the authority to grant and refuse housing applications.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The uniqueness and value of the Cotswolds is shown in the fact that five European <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Areas of Conservation</a>, three <a href="/wiki/National_Nature_Reserves_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="National Nature Reserves in England">National Nature Reserves</a> and more than eighty <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Sites of Special Scientific Interest</a> are within the Cotswolds AONB.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Cotswold Voluntary Wardens Service was established in 1968 to help conserve and enhance the area, and now has more than 300 wardens.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_Way" title="Cotswold Way">Cotswold Way</a> is a long-distance footpath, just over 100 miles (161&#160;km) long, running the length of the AONB, mainly on the edge of the Cotswold escarpment with views over the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Valley" title="Severn Valley">Severn Valley</a> and the Vale of <a href="/wiki/Evesham" title="Evesham">Evesham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_of_interest">Places of The house sits in an estate of 700 acres (300&#160;ha)<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> at the southern end of the Cotswold escarpment.\n</p><p>Of the many Manor houses built in the area <a href="/wiki/Owlpen_Manor" title="Owlpen Manor">Owlpen Manor</a> is a Tudor Grade I listed manor house of the Mander family, situated in the village of Owlpen in the <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>. Moving further north, <a href="/wiki/Broadway_Tower,_Worcestershire" title="Broadway Tower, Worcestershire">Broadway Tower</a> is a folly on Broadway Hill, near the village of Broadway, in the English county of Worcestershire. class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cotswold_roads_1933.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Cotswolds roads from 1933</div></div></div>\n<p>The Cotswolds lie between the <a href="/wiki/M5_motorway" title="M5 motorway">M5</a>, <a href="/wiki/M40_motorway" title="M40 motorway">M40</a> and <a href="/wiki/M4_motorway" title="M4 motorway">M4 motorways</a>. The main A-roads through the area are the <a href="/wiki/A46_road" title="A46 road">A46</a>: <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>; the <a href="/wiki/A419_road" title="A419 road">A419</a>: <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Cirencester" title="Cirencester">Cirencester</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Stroud; the <a href="/wiki/A429_road" title="A429 road">A429</a>: Cirencester&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Stow-on-the-Wold" title="Stow-on-the-Wold">Stow-on-the-Wold</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Moreton-in-Marsh" title="Moreton-in-Marsh">Moreton-in-Marsh</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/A40_road" title="A40 road">A40</a>: <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Burford" title="Burford">Burford</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Cheltenham. These all roughly follow the routes of ancient roads, some laid down by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Romans</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Ermin_Way" title="Ermin Way">Ermin Way</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fosse_Way" title="Fosse Way">Fosse Way</a>.\n</p><p>There are local bus services across the area, but some are infrequent.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> flows from the Cotswolds and is navigable from <a href="/wiki/Inglesham" title="Inglesham">Inglesham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lechlade" title="Lechlade">Lechlade-on-Thames</a> downstream to <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The area is bounded by two major rail routes: in the south by the main <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Main_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Western Main Line">Bristol\xe2\x80\x93Bath\xe2\x80\x93London High Speed line</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_Main_Line" title="South Wales Main Line">South Wales Main Line</a>) and in the west by the <a href="/wiki/Cross_Country_Route" title="Cross Country Route">Bristol\xe2\x80\x93Birmingham main line</a>. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_Line" title="Cotswold Line">Cotswold Line</a> runs through the Cotswolds from <a href="/wiki/Oxford_railway_station" title="Oxford railway station">Oxford</a> to <a href="/wiki/Worcester_Shrub_Hill_railway_station" title="Worcester Shrub Hill railway station">Worcester</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Valley_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Valley Line">Golden Valley Line</a> runs from Swindon via <a href="/wiki/Stroud_railway_station" title="Stroud railway station">Stroud</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_railway_station" title="Gloucester railway station">Gloucester</a>, carrying high speed and local services.\n</p><p>Mainline, high-speed rail services to the big cities run from railway stations such as <a href="/wiki/Bath_Spa_railway_station" title="Bath Spa railway station">Bath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swindon_railway_station" title="Swindon railway station">Swindon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Spa_railway_station" title="Cheltenham Spa railway station">Cheltenham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worcester_Shrub_Hill_railway_station" title="Worcester Shrub Hill railway station">Worcester</a>. Mainline trains run by <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway_(train_operating_company)" title="Great Western Railway (train operating company)">Great Western Railway</a> to <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a> also are available from <a href="/wiki/Kemble_railway_station" title="Kemble railway station">Kemble station</a> near Cirencester, <a href="/wiki/Kingham_railway_station" title="Kingham railway station">Kingham station</a> near Stow-on-the-Wold, <a href="/wiki/Charlbury_railway_station" title="Charlbury railway station">Charlbury station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moreton-in-Marsh_railway_station" title="Moreton-in-Marsh railway station">Moreton-in-Marsh station</a>.\n</p><p>Additionally, there is the <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_Warwickshire_Railway" title="Gloucestershire Warwickshire Railway">Gloucestershire Warwickshire Railway</a>, a steam heritage railway serving the Cotswolds from <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Racecourse_railway_station" title="Cheltenham Racecourse railway station">Cheltenham Racecourse</a> through <a href="/wiki/Gotherington_railway_station" title="Gotherington railway station">Gotherington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winchcombe_railway_station" title="Winchcombe railway station">Winchcombe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hayles_Abbey_Halt_railway_station" title="Hayles Abbey Halt railway station">Hayles Abbey Halt</a> to <a href="/wiki/Toddington_railway_station" title="Toddington railway station">Toddington</a> and Laverton. The preserved line is currently being extended to Broadway, with the aim of reaching <a href="/wiki/Honeybourne_railway_station" title="Honeybourne railway station">Honeybourne</a> and making a reconnection with the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_Line" title="Cotswold Line">Cotswold Line</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cotswolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Cotswold region has inspired several notable English composers. In the early 1900s, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Howells" title="Herbert Howells">Herbert Howells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Gurney" title="Ivor Gurney">Ivor Gurney</a> used to take long walks together over the hills, and Gurney urged Howells to make the landscape, including the nearby <a href="/wiki/Malvern_Hills" title="Malvern Hills">Malvern Hills</a>, the inspiration for his future work. Accepting, in 1916, Howells wrote his first major piece, the <i>Piano Quartet in A minor,</i> inspired by the magnificent view of the <a href="/wiki/Malvern_Hills" title="Malvern Hills">Malverns</a>; he dedicated it to "the hill at Chosen (<a href="/wiki/Churchdown" title="Churchdown">Churchdown</a>) and Ivor Gurney who knows it".<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Another contemporary of theirs, <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Finzi" title="Gerald Finzi">Gerald Finzi</a>, lived in nearby <a href="/wiki/Painswick" title="Painswick">Painswick</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gustav_Holst" title="Gustav Holst">Gustav Holst</a> titled his Symphony in F major, Op. 8 H47 <i>The Cotswolds</i>. The composer <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a> composed his opera <i>"Hugh the Drover"</i> from 1913 to 1924, which depicts life in a Cotswold village and incorporates local folk melodies. In 1988 the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._6_(Bourgeois)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Symphony No. 6 (Bourgeois) (page does not exist)">6th symphony</a> (Op. 109) of composer <a href="/wiki/Derek_Bourgeois" title="Derek Bourgeois">Derek Bourgeois</a> was titled <i>"A Cotswold Symphony"</i>.\n</p><p>The Cotswolds are a popular location for filming scenes for movies and television programmes.<sup id="cite_ref-cotswoldlife.co.uk_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswoldlife.co.uk-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The film <i><a href="/wiki/Better_Things_(film)" title="Better Things (film)">Better Things</a></i> (2008), directed by Duane Hopkins, is set in a small Cotswold village. The fictional detective <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Raisin" title="Agatha Raisin">Agatha Raisin</a> lives in the fictional village of <a href="/wiki/Carsely" class="mw-redirect" title="Carsely">Carsely</a> in the Cotswolds. The <a href="/wiki/Chipping_Norton_set" title="Chipping Norton set">Chipping Norton set</a> are based in the Cotswolds.\n</p><p>Other movies filmed in the Cotswolds or nearby, at least in part, include some of the Harry Potter Series (Gloucester Cathedral), <a href="/wiki/Bridget_Jones%27s_Diary_(film)" title="Bridget Jones&#39;s Diary (film)">Bridget Jones\'s Diary (film)</a> (Snowshill), Pride and Prejudice (Cheltenham Town Hall) and <a href="/wiki/Braveheart" title="Braveheart">Braveheart</a> (Cotswold Farm Park).<sup id="cite_ref-manorcottages.co.uk_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manorcottages.co.uk-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, some scenes of the 2016 movie Alice in Wonderland: Through the Looking Glass were filmed at the Gloucester Docks just outside the Cotswold District; some scenes for the 2006 movie Amazing Grace were also filmed at the Docks.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The television series <i><a href="/wiki/Father_Brown_(2013_TV_series)" title="Father Brown (2013 TV series)">Father Brown</a></i> was almost entirely filmed in the Cotswolds. Scenes and buildings in <a href="/wiki/Sudeley_Castle" title="Sudeley Castle">Sudeley Castle</a> was often featured in the series.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The vicarage in Blockley was used for the main character\'s residence while St. Peter and St. Paul church was the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> St. Mary\xe2\x80\x99s in the series.<sup id="cite_ref-cotswoldlife.co.uk_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cotswoldlife.co.uk-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>  Other filming locations included <a href="/wiki/Guiting_Power" title="Guiting Power">Guiting Power</a>, the former hospital in <a href="/wiki/Moreton-in-Marsh" title="Moreton-in-Marsh">Moreton-in-Marsh</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Winchcombe" title="Winchcombe">Winchcombe</a> railway station, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Slaughter" title="Lower Slaughter">Lower Slaughter</a> and St Peter\'s Church in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Slaughter" title="Upper Slaughter">Upper Slaughter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many exterior shots of village life in the <a href="/wiki/Downton_Abbey" title="Downton Abbey">Downton Abbey</a> TV series were filmed in <a href="/wiki/Bampton,_Oxfordshire" title="Bampton, Oxfordshire">Bampton, Oxfordshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-manorcottages.co.uk_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manorcottages.co.uk-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Other filming locations in that county included Swinbrook, Cogges and Shilton.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The city of Bath hosted crews that filmed parts of the movies Vanity Fair, Persuasion, Dracula and The Duchess.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gloucester" title="Gloucester">Gloucester</a>, and other places in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, some within the Area of Natural Beauty, have been a popular location for filming period films and television programmes over the years. <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_Cathedral" title="Gloucester Cathedral">Gloucester Cathedral</a> has been particularly popular.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bordering_area">Bordering area</span><span
