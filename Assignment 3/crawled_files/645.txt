href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">The Irish state</a> came into being in 1922 as the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, a dominion of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a>, having seceded from the United Kingdom under the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>. It comprises 26 of the <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">island of Ireland</a>\'s 32 counties. The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">1937 constitution</a> renamed the state <i>Ireland</i>. In 1949 it explicitly became a republic under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act_1948" title="Republic of Ireland Act 1948">Republic of Ireland Act 1948</a>, definitively ending its tenuous membership of the British Commonwealth. In 1973 it joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Communities" title="European Communities">European Communities</a>.\n</p><p>From its foundation, the Irish Free State was embroiled in a civil war between nationalists supporting the Treaty and opponents who supported a republic. The pro-Treaty side, organised as <a href="/wiki/Cumann_na_nGaedheal" title="Cumann na nGaedheal">Cumann na nGaedheal</a> emerged victorious from the conflict and won subsequent elections. They formed the government of the state until 1932, when they peacefully handed over power to the anti-Treaty faction in <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna F\xc3\xa1il">Fianna F\xc3\xa1il</a>, who defeated them in an election. The Irish state, despite its violent beginnings, has remained a liberal democracy throughout its existence. Changes in the 1930s removed many of the links with Britain established under the Treaty and Ireland\'s neutrality in the Second World War demonstrated its independence in foreign policy matters from Britain.\n</p><p>In the economic sphere, the Irish state has had a mixed performance. On independence, it was one of the wealthier countries in Europe per head of population.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However it also inherited from British rule the problems of unemployment, emigration, uneven geographical development and lack of a native industrial base. For much of its history, the state struggled to rectify these problems. Particular peaks of emigration were recorded during the 1930s, 1950s and 1980s, when the Irish economy recorded little growth.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1930s, Fianna F\xc3\xa1il governments attempted to create Irish domestic industries using subsidies and protective tariffs. In the late 1950s, these policies were dropped in favour of free trade with selected countries and encouraging of foreign investment with low taxes. This was expanded when Ireland entered the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> in 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the 1990s and 2000s, Ireland experienced an economic boom known as the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Tiger" title="Celtic Tiger">Celtic Tiger</a>, in which the country\'s GDP surpassed many of its European neighbours.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Immigration also surpassed emigration, bringing the state\'s population up to over 4 million.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, since 2008, Ireland has experienced a severe crisis in the banking sector and with sovereign debt. The resultant economic slump has deepened the effect of the world recession on Ireland.\n</p><p>From 1937 to 1998, the Irish constitution included an irredentist claim on <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> as a part of the national territory. However, the state also opposed and used its security forces against those armed groups \xe2\x80\x93 principally the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a> \xe2\x80\x93 who tried to unite Ireland by force. This occurred in the 1950s, throughout the 1970s and 1980s and has continued on a reduced scale.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Irish governments meanwhile tried to broker an agreement to the conflict known as <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a> within Northern Ireland from 1968 to the late 1990s. The British government officially recognised the right of the Irish government to be a party to the Northern negotiations in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a> of 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1998, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>, the Irish constitution was altered by referendum to remove the territorial claim to Northern Ireland and instead extend the right of Irish citizenship to all the people of the island should they wish to have it.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background to independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Separatism.2C_rebellion_and_partition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Separatism,_rebellion_and_partition">Separatism, rebellion and partition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Separatism, rebellion and partition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1800">Union</a> in 1801 until 6 December 1922 the whole of Ireland was part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>. However, from the 1880s there had been long standing nationalist agitation for autonomy or <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Rule">Home Rule</a>. Other, more radical voices such as the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Brotherhood" title="Irish Republican Brotherhood">Irish Republican Brotherhood</a> called for independence, but these were in a minority.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1912\xe2\x80\x931913, the Liberal government in Britain proposed a Bill for Home Rule. Alarmed, unionists in the north organized the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteers" title="Ulster Volunteers">Ulster Volunteers</a>, an armed militia proposing to resist Home Rule by force. Nationalists in response founded the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Volunteers" title="Irish Volunteers">Irish Volunteers</a>. Arising out of this stand off, the partition of Ireland was proposed in three way talks between the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a>, the Unionist Party and the British government. In 1914, the UK Parliament enacted a <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Third Irish Home Rule Bill</a> but suspended its effect until after World War I.\n</p><p>The nationalist leader <a href="/wiki/John_Redmond" title="John Redmond">John Redmond</a> pledged support for the British war effort and many Irishmen served in the British Army (see <a href="/wiki/Ireland_and_World_War_I" title="Ireland and World War I">Ireland and World War I</a>), but the war and the frustration of nationalist ambitions regarding Home Rule led to a radicalisation of Irish nationalism. In 1916, a group of IRB activists within the Irish Volunteers led an insurrection aimed at Irish independence in Dublin, known as the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a>. The rebellion did not have popular support and was put down within a week, but the execution of its leaders, and the subsequent wholesale arrest of radical nationalist activists proved very unpopular with the nationalist public.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Coming directly after the Rising, a further attempt was made at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Convention" title="Irish Convention">Irish Convention</a> to resolve the impasse over Home Rule, but without success. Finally, the British proposal to extend conscription for the war to Ireland provoked widespread resistance, (see <a href="/wiki/Conscription_Crisis_of_1918" title="Conscription Crisis of 1918">Conscription Crisis of 1918</a>) and discredited the Irish Parliamentary Party who had supported the British war effort.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>All of these factors led to a swing towards support for <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the party which was led by veterans of the Easter Rising and which stood for an independent Irish Republic. In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1918" title="Irish general election, 1918">Irish general election, 1918</a>, Sinn F\xc3\xa9in won the vast majority of seats, many of which were uncontested. Sinn F\xc3\xa9in\'s elected candidates refused to attend the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">UK Parliament</a> at <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a> and instead assembled in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> as a new revolutionary parliament called "<a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann_(1919-1922)" class="mw-redirect" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann (1919-1922)">D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann</a>". They declared the existence of a new state called the "<a href="/wiki/Irish_Republic" title="Irish Republic">Irish Republic</a>" and established a system of government to rival the institutions of the United Kingdom.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/First_D%C3%A1il" title="First D\xc3\xa1il">first meeting</a> of the D\xc3\xa1il coincided with an unauthorised shooting of two <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Constabulary" title="Royal Irish Constabulary">RIC</a> men in <a href="/wiki/Soloheadbeg" title="Soloheadbeg">Tipperary</a>, now regarded as the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>. From 1919 to 1921 the Irish Volunteers (now renamed as the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a>, being deemed by the D\xc3\xa1il to be the army of the new Irish Republic) engaged in <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla warfare</a> against the British army, the RIC and paramilitary police units known as the <a href="/wiki/Black_and_Tans" title="Black and Tans">Black and Tans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_Division" title="Auxiliary Division">Auxiliaries</a>. The violence started out slowly, with only 19 deaths in 1919, but escalated sharply from the second half of 1920 and in the first six months of 1921 alone there were 1,000 deaths on all sides.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The principle political leader of the republican movement was <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the President of the Republic. However he spent much of the conflict in the United States, raising money and support for the Irish cause. In his absence, two young men, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(Irish_leader)" title="Michael Collins (Irish leader)">Michael Collins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Mulcahy" title="Richard Mulcahy">Richard Mulcahy</a> rose to prominence as the clandestine leaders of the IRA \xe2\x80\x93 respectively Director of Intelligence and Chief of Staff of the guerrilla organisation.\n</p><p>There were several failed attempts to negotiate an end to the conflict. In the summer of 1920, the British government proposed the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a> (which passed into law on 3 May 1921) that envisaged the partition of the island of Ireland into two autonomous regions <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a></i> (six northeastern counties) and <i><a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a></i> (the rest of the island, including its most northerly county, Donegal).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, this was not acceptable to southern republicans and only the entity of Northern Ireland was established under the Act in 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-Furlong_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furlong-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The political entity of Southern Ireland was superseded in 1922 by the creation of the Irish Free State.\n</p><p>After further failed talks in December 1920, the guerrilla conflict was brought to an end in July 1921, with a truce agreed between the IRA and the British. Talks were then formally begun in pursuit of a peace settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To some extent, the War of Independence exposed political and religious fissures in Irish society. The IRA killed over 200 civilians as alleged informers in the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It has been alleged that groups like Protestants and ex-servicemen were disproportionately represented in this figure \xe2\x80\x93 an argument disputed by other historians.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> However whether due to violence and intimidation or due to their loyalty to the British presence in Ireland, between 1911 and 1926 some 34 percent of the Free State\'s Protestant population \xe2\x80\x93 or about 40,000 people \xe2\x80\x93 left the 26 counties, mostly for Northern Ireland or Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-fn_3_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fn_3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> While there were many reasons for this, secession from the United Kingdom was a factor in Protestant emigration.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Irish_Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anglo-Irish Treaty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png/200px-Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png" width="200" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png/300px-Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png/400px-Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png 2x" data-file-width="1630" data-file-height="2078" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Ireland%27s_capitals.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Political map of the island of Ireland today showing Northern Ireland (part of the UK) and the Republic of Ireland</div></div></div>\n<p>Negotiations between the British and Irish negotiating teams produced the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>, concluded on 6 December 1921. The Irish team was led by Michael Collins, who had organised the IRA intelligence during the War of Independence. The British team led by <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> were prepared to make concessions on Irish independence but would not concede a republic. Towards the end of negotiations, Lloyd George threatened, "immediate and terrible war" if the Irish did not accept the terms offered.\n</p><p>The Treaty envisaged a new system of Irish self-government, known as "<a href="/wiki/Dominion_status" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominion status">dominion status</a>", with a new state, to be called the Irish Free State. The Free State was considerably more independent than a Home Rule Parliament would have been. It had its own police and armed forces and control over its own taxation and fiscal policy, none of which had been envisaged under Home Rule. However, there were some limits to its sovereignty. It remained a dominion of the British Commonwealth and members of its parliament had to swear an oath of loyalty to the British monarch. The British also retained three naval bases \xe2\x80\x93 known as the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_Ports_(Ireland)" title="Treaty Ports (Ireland)">Treaty Ports</a>. In addition, the Irish state was obliged to honour the contracts of the existing civil service -with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Constabulary" title="Royal Irish Constabulary">Royal Irish Constabulary</a>, which was disbanded, albeit with full pensions \xe2\x80\x93 payable by the Irish state.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was also the question of partition, which pre-dated the Treaty but which was copper-fastened by it. In theory, Northern Ireland was included under the terms of the Treaty but under Article 12 was, given the option to opt out within a month. (See <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State_Constitution_Act_1922" title="Irish Free State Constitution Act 1922">Irish Free State Constitution Act 1922</a>) Thus for three days from midnight on 6 December 1922 the newly established Irish Free State, in theory included all of the island of Ireland (including Northern Ireland).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> However, in practice, Northern Ireland was already a functioning autonomous area by this time and it formally left the Irish Free State on 8 December 1922.\n</p><p>As a result of these limits to the Free State\'s sovereignty, and because the Treaty dismantled the Republic declared by nationalists in 1918, the Sinn F\xc3\xa9in movement, the D\xc3\xa1il and the IRA were all deeply split over whether to accept the Treaty. \xc3\x89amon de Valera, the President of the Republic was the most prominent leader of those who rejected the Treaty. Among other things, he objected to the fact that Collins and the negotiating team had signed it without the authorisation of the D\xc3\xa1il Cabinet.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_War">Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a></div>\n<p>On a vote of 64 to 57, the D\xc3\xa1il narrowly passed the Anglo-Irish Treaty on 7 January 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a>, President of the Republic and several other cabinet members resigned in protest.\n</p><p>The pro-Treaty leadership of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(Irish_leader)" title="Michael Collins (Irish leader)">Michael Collins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Griffith" title="Arthur Griffith">Arthur Griffith</a>, organised in a Provisional Government, set about establishing the Irish Free State created by the Treaty. To this end, they began recruiting for a new army, based initially at <a href="/wiki/Beggars_Bush_Barracks" title="Beggars Bush Barracks">Beggars Bush Barracks</a> in Dublin, composed of pro-Treaty IRA units. They also began recruiting for a new police, the Civic Guard, to replace the RIC which was disbanded as of August 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However a majority of the IRA led by <a href="/wiki/Rory_O%27Connor_(Irish_republican)" title="Rory O&#39;Connor (Irish republican)">Rory O\'Connor</a> opposed the Treaty, on the grounds that it disestablished the Irish republic, which they argued they were sworn to defend, and that it imposed a declaration of fidelity to the British monarch on Irish parliamentarians. The IRA held a convention in March 1922, in which they renounced their allegiance to the D\xc3\xa1il and vested it in their own Army Council.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> O\'Connor in April led the occupation by anti-Treaty forces of several public buildings in Dublin, notably the <a href="/wiki/Four_Courts" title="Four Courts">Four Courts</a> \xe2\x80\x93 centre of the Irish legal system. \xc3\x89amon de Valera, while not in command of the anti-Treaty IRA, also led political opposition to the Treaty in a new party named <a href="/wiki/Cumann_na_Poblachta" title="Cumann na Poblachta">Cumann na Poblachta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With two rival Irish armed forces now in the country, civil war looked likely from the spring of 1922. Three events set it off. The first was the election of 18 June 1922, which the pro-Treaty Sinn F\xc3\xa9in party won, giving the Free State a popular mandate. The second was the assassination by Irish republicans in London of a retired British general <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hughes_Wilson" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Hughes Wilson">Henry Hughes Wilson</a>. While it is not clear who ordered the killing, the British government assumed it was the anti-Treaty IRA and ordered Collins to act against them or risk armed British intervention to do it. The third trigger was the kidnapping by the IRA in the Four Courts of Free State general, JJ O\'Connell. This combination of events forced the Collins government to assault and take the anti-Treaty positions in Dublin, which it succeeded in doing after a week\'s fighting in July 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> \xc3\x89amon de Valera declared his support for the anti-Treaty IRA after the outbreak of hostilities.\n</p><p>A further military offensive secured Free State control over the other major towns and cities in its territory by the beginning of August. Despite their defeat in open warfare, the IRA regrouped and took up a guerrilla campaign, as they saw it, to restore the Irish Republic. The war dragged on in a guerrilla form until April 1923. In August 1922, the Free State was rocked by the death of its two main leaders. Michael Collins was killed in an ambush at Beal na mBlath, Cork, on 22 August 1922 and Arthur Griffith died of a stroke a week earlier. <a href="/wiki/W._T._Cosgrave" title="W. T. Cosgrave">W. T. Cosgrave</a> assumed control of both the Irish Republic\'s cabinet and the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Provisional Government of Ireland">Provisional Government</a> and both administrations disappeared simultaneously shortly afterwards, replaced by the institutions of the Irish Free State on 6 December 1922.\n</p><p>The anti-Treaty IRA under <a href="/wiki/Liam_Lynch_(Irish_republican)" title="Liam Lynch (Irish republican)">Liam Lynch</a> tried to use the same guerrilla tactics against the Free State as they had against the British in 1919\xe2\x80\x931921. However, without the same degree of popular support, they were less effective. By late 1922, the <a href="/wiki/Irish_National_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish National Army">Irish National Army</a> had taken all the major towns in the country and reduced the IRA\'s campaign to small scale attacks. A very large number of anti-Treaty fighters, some 12,000 in all, were interned by the Free State. Moreover, as it went on the war produced acts of great cruelty on both sides. The Free State embarked on a policy of selective executions  \xe2\x80\x93 77 prisoners were judicially shot with over 100 more \'unofficially\' killed in the field. The anti-Treaty forces assassinated one pro-Treaty member of Parliament, and several other civilian politicians, wounded more and burned their houses. However the Free State\'s tactics of internment and executions combined to cripple the anti-Treaty forces by April 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The death in action of Liam Lynch in this month led to the anti-Treaty IRA, under the orders of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Aiken" title="Frank Aiken">Frank Aiken</a> and on the urgings of civilian leader de Valera, calling a ceasefire and to "dump arms". There was no negotiated end to the war however.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Civil War between Irish nationalists created a great deal of bitterness and the Civil War cleavage also produced the two main parties of independent Ireland in the 20th century. The number of dead has yet to be accurately counted but is considered to be around 2,000; at least as high as the number killed in the preceding War of Independence.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="The_Irish_state.2C_1922.E2.80.931939"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Irish_state,_1922\xe2\x80\x931939">The Irish state, 1922\xe2\x80\x931939</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Irish state, 1922\xe2\x80\x931939">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Immediately after the Civil War, elections were held in which anti-Treaty Sinn F\xc3\xa9in were allowed to participate. Although many of their candidates, including \xc3\x89amon de Valera, were imprisoned, they won about one third of the vote. However the pro-Treaty side, organised in <a href="/wiki/Cumann_na_nGaedheal" title="Cumann na nGaedheal">Cumann na nGaedheal</a>, won a comfortable majority and went on to form the government of the new state until 1932.\n</p><p>The Cumann na nGaedheal governments, led by WT Cosgrave, were highly conservative \xe2\x80\x93 being more concerned with establishing the state\'s basic institutions after the havoc of the Civil War than with social or political reform. According to Kevin O\'Higgins, the Minister for Justice, "we were the most conservative group of revolutionaries ever to have carried out a successful revolution".\n</p><p>The Irish Civil Service was largely inherited intact from the British and senior civil servants such as C.J. Gregg were \'lent\' to the Irish from London to get the new state\'s bureaucracy off the ground. The new service, and especially its comptroller, Joseph Brennan were initially most concerned with balancing the state\'s budget and avoiding long-term in-debtedness<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The Free State printed its own notes (the <a href="/wiki/Irish_pound" title="Irish pound">punt</a>), and minted its own coins but their value remained tied to British sterling currency until the 1970s.\n</p><p>Whereas the British had devolved much power to local government in the 1890s, one of the Free State\'s first acts was to abolish many of the powers of <a href="/wiki/County_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="County Council">County Councils</a> and replace them with unelected County managers.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> This was partly due to the allegiance of some councils to the anti-Treaty side in the Civil War, but also due to the belief that giving power to local government bred corruption. One of the major successes of the Cumann na nGaedheal governments was to establish the police, the <a href="/wiki/Garda_S%C3%ADoch%C3%A1na" title="Garda S\xc3\xadoch\xc3\xa1na">Garda S\xc3\xadoch\xc3\xa1na</a>, as an unarmed and politically neutral force, relatively untainted by the bitterness of the civil war.\n</p><p>On the economic front, the Cosgrave administration saw its role as supporting the Irish agricultural export sector by consolidating farms and improving the quality of their produce. Ernest Blythe, the first Minister for Finance, in a bid to reduce the public debt, cut public expenditure from \xc2\xa342 million in 1923 to \xc2\xa327 million in 1926. The Cumann na nGeadhael governments did not see providing social services as a priority and instead cut income tax from 5 shillings to 3 shillings.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> One exception to the generally low level of public spending was the <a href="/wiki/Shannon_hydroelectric_scheme" title="Shannon hydroelectric scheme">Shannon hydroelectric scheme</a>, which provided Ireland\'s first autonomous source of electricity.\n</p><p>While the last prisoners of the Civil War were released in 1924, the Free State retained extensive emergency powers to intern and even execute political opponents, under a series of Public Safety Acts (1923, 1926 and 1931). These powers were used after the IRA assassinated Minister <a href="/wiki/Kevin_O%27Higgins" title="Kevin O&#39;Higgins">Kevin O\'Higgins</a> (in revenge for the executions during the Civil War) in 1927 after which several hundred IRA suspects were interned.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Fianna_F.C3.A1il_comes_to_power"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fianna_F\xc3\xa1il_comes_to_power">Fianna F\xc3\xa1il comes to power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fianna F\xc3\xa1il comes to power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The political representatives of the anti-Treaty side had re-grouped in 1926 as Fianna F\xc3\xa1il, leaving only a minority of intransigent republicans in Sinn F\xc3\xa9in and the IRA who refused to recognise the legitimacy of the state. Fianna F\xc3\xa1il initially refused to take their seats after being elected to the D\xc3\xa1il. However, they entered the parliament in 1927, in part to disassociate themselves from the killing of Kevin O\'Higgins.\n</p><p>Initially Cumann na nGaedheal had been popular as the party that had established the state, but by 1932, their economic conservatism and continued repression of anti-Treaty Republicans was becoming unpopular. Fianna F\xc3\xa1il won the 1932 election on a programme of developing Irish industry, creating jobs, providing more social services and cutting the remaining links with the British Empire. In 1932, Fianna F\xc3\xa1il entered government in coalition with the Labour Party, but a year later they won an absolute majority. They would be in government without interruption until 1948 and for much of the rest of the 20th century.\n</p><p>One of Fianna F\xc3\xa1il\'s first actions in government was to legalise the IRA and to release imprisoned republicans. IRA members began attacking Cumann na nGaedhal supporters, who they considered "traitors" at rallies. This greatly antagonised pro-Treaty Civil War veterans, who in response formed the quasi-fascist <a href="/wiki/Blueshirts" title="Blueshirts">Blueshirts</a> (initially the "Army Comrades Association"), led by the former Garda Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Eoin_O%27Duffy" title="Eoin O&#39;Duffy">Eoin O\'Duffy</a> to oppose the IRA. There were frequent riots and occasional shootings between the two factions in the early 1930s. De Valera banned the Blueshirts in 1933, after a threatened march on the D\xc3\xa1il, in imitation of Mussolini\'s <a href="/wiki/March_on_Rome" title="March on Rome">March on Rome</a>. Not long afterwards, in 1936, de Valera made a clean break with political violence when he banned the increasingly left-wing IRA after they murdered a landlord\'s agent, Richard More O\'Farrell, in a land dispute and fired shots at police during a strike of Tramway workers in Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In 1939 it enacted the <a href="/wiki/Offences_against_the_State_Acts_1939%E2%80%931998" title="Offences against the State Acts 1939\xe2\x80\x931998">Offences against the State Act</a>, for the prosecution of illegal armed groups; an act just as draconian as any legislation previous administrations had passed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_nationalism_and_trade_war_with_Britain">Economic nationalism and trade war with Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economic nationalism and trade war with Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Fianna F\xc3\xa1il\'s economic programme marked a sharp break with their predecessors in Cumann na nGaedheal. Instead of Free Trade, which benefited mainly substantial farmers, Fianna F\xc3\xa1il pursued the nationalist aim of establishing Irish domestic industries, which were protected from foreign competitors by tariffs and subsidies. Fianna F\xc3\xa1il made it mandatory for foreign companies to have a quota of Irish members on their boards. They also set up a large number of semi-state companies such as the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_Supply_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Electricity Supply Board">Electricity Supply Board</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Turf_Development_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Turf Development Board">Turf Development Board</a>. While this state-led strategy had some positive results, emigration remained high throughout this period, with up to 75,000 leaving for Britain in the late 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the course of their pursuit of economic independence, Fianna F\xc3\xa1il also provoked what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Trade_War" title="Anglo-Irish Trade War">Anglo-Irish Trade War</a> with Britain in 1933, by refusing to continue paying back "Land Annuities" -money provided under the 1903 <a href="/wiki/Irish_Land_Acts#Wyndham_Land_(Purchase)_Act_1903" title="Irish Land Acts">Wyndham Land Act</a> by the British Government to enable Irish farmers purchase their own land. The British in retaliation raised tariffs on Irish agricultural produces, hurting Ireland\'s export trade. De Valera in turn raised taxes on the importation of British goods. The burden of this standoff fell on the cattle farmers, who could no longer sell their cattle at competitive rates in Britain. Additionally the Fianna F\xc3\xa1il government continued to collect half the land annuities as taxation. Police and sometimes troops were used to seize cattle off farmers who would or could not pay. Farmers aggrieved at these policies were one of the principal support bases of the Blueshirt movement<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The dispute with Britain was finally settled in 1939. Half of the land annuity debt (c. \xc2\xa390 million) was written off and the rest paid as lump sum. The British also returned to Ireland the Treaty ports, which they had retained since the Treaty of 1922. Irish control over these bases made possible Irish neutrality in the looming Second World War.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_status">Constitutional status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Constitutional status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Free State from 1922\xe2\x80\x931937 was a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> over which the British monarch reigned (from 1927 with the title "<a href="/wiki/King_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Ireland">King of Ireland</a>"). The Representative of the Crown was known as the <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="Governor-General of the Irish Free State">Governor-General</a>. The Free State had a <a href="/wiki/Bicameral" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicameral">bicameral</a> parliament and a cabinet, called the "<a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="Executive Council of the Irish Free State">Executive Council</a>" answerable to the lower house of parliament, the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann_(Irish_Free_State)" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann (Irish Free State)">Free State D\xc3\xa1il</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a> was called the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Executive_Council_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="President of the Executive Council of the Irish Free State">President of the Executive Council</a>.\n</p><p>The parliament of the UK passed <a href="/wiki/The_Statute_of_Westminster_1931" class="mw-redirect" title="The Statute of Westminster 1931">The Statute of Westminster 1931</a>, which granted legislative independence to the six Dominions, Australia, Canada, the Irish Free State, Newfoundland, New Zealand, and South Africa.\nIn 1932, after <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna F\xc3\xa1il">Fianna F\xc3\xa1il</a>\'s victory in the general election, the 1922 Irish Free State constitution was amended through a series of legislative changes, was subsequently replaced with a new constitution. This document was drawn up by the de Valera administration. It was approved by the electorate in a plebiscite by a simple majority.\n</p><p>On 29 December 1937 the new "<a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution of Ireland</a>" came into effect, renaming the Irish Free State to simply "\xc3\x89ire" or in the English language "Ireland". The Governor-General was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> and a new more powerful prime minister, called the "<a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>", came into being, while the Executive Council was renamed the "<a href="/wiki/Irish_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Government">Government</a>". Though it had a president, the new state was not a republic. The British monarch continued to <a href="/wiki/Reign" title="Reign">reign</a> theoretically as <i>King of Ireland</i> and was used as an "organ" in international and diplomatic relations, with the President of Ireland relegated to symbolic functions within the state but never outside it.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_of_Northern_Ireland">Status of Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status of Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Anglo-Irish Treaty provided that should Northern Ireland choose not be included in the Free State, a <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission (Ireland)">Boundary Commission</a> would be set up to revise the borders between the two jurisdictions. The Irish perspective was that this was intended to allow largely nationalist areas of Northern Ireland to join the Free State, and shortly after the establishment of the Free State this commission came into being. However <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_(Ireland)#Negotiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission (Ireland)">the commission concentrated on economic and topographic factors</a>, rather than the political aspirations of the people who would be living near the new border. In 1925 the Boundary Commission report, contrary to expectations, proposed ceding some small areas of the Free State to Northern Ireland. For <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_(Ireland)#Imperial_debt" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission (Ireland)">a variety of reasons</a> the governments agreed to accept the original Northern Ireland/Southern Ireland delineation in return for Britain dropping the Irish obligation to share in paying Britain\'s Imperial debts. The D\xc3\xa1il approved the boundary by a large margin of 71 to 20.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="World_War_II.2C_neutrality.2C_and_.22The_Emergency.22_1939.E2.80.931945"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II,_neutrality,_and_&quot;The_Emergency&quot;_1939\xe2\x80\x931945">World War II, neutrality, and "The Emergency" 1939\xe2\x80\x931945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: World War II, neutrality, and &quot;The Emergency&quot; 1939\xe2\x80\x931945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Irish_neutrality_during_World_War_II" title="Irish neutrality during World War II">Irish neutrality during World War II</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(Ireland)" title="The Emergency (Ireland)">The Emergency (Ireland)</a></div>\n<p>The outbreak of the Second World War put the state and the de Valera government in a difficult situation. It came under pressure from Britain and later the USA, to enter the war, or at least to allow the allies to use its ports. However, there remained a minority who felt that national independence had yet to be achieved and who were resolutely opposed to any alliance with Britain. For this reason, de Valera ensured that the state remained neutral throughout the War which was officially known as the "<a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(Ireland)" title="The Emergency (Ireland)">Emergency</a>". The state\'s decision to adopt neutrality was influenced by memories of the Anglo-Irish War and the Civil War, and the state\'s lack of military preparedness for involvement in a war.\n</p><p>The remnants of the IRA, which had split several times into ever smaller groupings since 1922, embarked on a bombing campaign in Britain (see <a href="/wiki/S-Plan" title="S-Plan">Sabotage Campaign (IRA)</a>) and some attacks in Northern Ireland (see <a href="/wiki/Northern_Campaign_(Irish_Republican_Army)" title="Northern Campaign (Irish Republican Army)">Northern Campaign</a>), intended to force a British withdrawal from Northern Ireland. Some of its leadership, notably <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Russell" title="Se\xc3\xa1n Russell">Se\xc3\xa1n Russell</a>, sought help from Nazi Germany for this project. De Valera, considering this activity a threat to Irish neutrality and therefore to the state\'s vital interests, interned all active IRA members and executed several. Another was hanged in Northern Ireland for shooting a policeman.\n</p><p>Behind the scenes the Irish state worked with the Allies; in 1940, the government agreed provisionally with Britain that it would accept the entry of British troops and put its own armed forces under their command should the Germans invade Ireland \xe2\x80\x93 see <a href="/wiki/Plan_W" title="Plan W">Plan W</a>. There was a provisional German plan for an invasion of Ireland, known as <a href="/wiki/Operation_Green_(Ireland)" title="Operation Green (Ireland)">Operation Green</a>, but it was never carried out. Additionally, Irish fire fighters were sent to Northern Ireland to help fight the fires caused by the German bombing of Belfast in 1941 (See <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Blitz" title="Belfast Blitz">Belfast Blitz</a>).\n</p><p>There were a number of further examples of co-operation. German pilots who crashed in Ireland were interned while Allied airmen were returned to Britain. There was also mutual sharing of intelligence. For example, the date of the D-Day Normandy landings was decided on the basis of transatlantic weather reports supplied by the Irish state. It is estimated that between 50,000 and 150,000 men from Ireland took part, with that number roughly evenly divided between Northern Ireland and the southern state.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Conversely, following the suicide of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, de Valera, following diplomatic protocol, controversially offered condolences to the German ambassador.\n</p><p>Economically, the War was a difficult time for the state. Industrial production fell by 25%.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the First World War, when Irish farmers had made substantial profits selling food to Britain, in the Second World War, Britain imposed strict price controls on Irish agricultural imports. Due to the war, imports to Ireland dried up -leading to drive for self-sufficiency in food and strict rationing, which continued until the 1950s. Nevertheless, as a result of neutrality, Ireland emerged from the war having been spared the physical destruction and extreme hardship undergone by combatant nations on the European mainland.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="1949_.E2.80.93_declaring_a_republic"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1949_\xe2\x80\x93_declaring_a_republic">1949 \xe2\x80\x93 declaring a republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1949 \xe2\x80\x93 declaring a republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 18 April 1949 the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act_1948" title="Republic of Ireland Act 1948">Republic of Ireland Act 1948</a>, which had been enacted by the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a>, came into force. That legislation described Ireland as the Republic of Ireland but did not change the <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_Irish_state" title="Names of the Irish state">country\'s name</a>. The international and diplomatic functions previously vested in or exercised by the King were now vested in the President of Ireland who finally became unambiguously the Irish head of state. Under the Commonwealth rules then in force, the declaration of a republic automatically terminated the state\'s membership of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, which became a republic shortly afterwards, Ireland chose not to reapply for admittance to the Commonwealth.\n</p><p>Though a republic since 1949, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Ireland_Act_1542" title="Crown of Ireland Act 1542">Crown of Ireland Act 1542</a> that had established the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> was not finally repealed until 1962, along with many other obsolete <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Parliament of Ireland</a> statutes.<sup id="cite_ref-rev1962_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rev1962-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> However, long before that, the British Government in its <a href="/wiki/Ireland_Act_1949" title="Ireland Act 1949">Ireland Act 1949</a> recognised that "the Republic of Ireland had ceased to be part of His Majesty\'s dominions" (but would not be "a foreign country" for the purposes of any law).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The state joined the United Nations in December 1955, after a lengthy veto by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Turned away by the veto of France in 1961, the state finally succeeded in joining the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> (now known as the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>) in 1973.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Economic.2C_political_and_social_history.2C_1945.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic,_political_and_social_history,_1945\xe2\x80\x93present">Economic, political and social history, 1945\xe2\x80\x93present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economic, political and social history, 1945\xe2\x80\x93present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Ireland emerged from the Second World War in better condition than many European countries, having been spared direct involvement in the war and with an income per capita higher than that of most belligerent countries. Ireland also benefited from a loan under the Marshal Plan; $36 million, at 2% interest. The money was spent on an extensive housing and slum-clearing project and a successful campaign to eradicate <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, whereas most European countries experienced a sustained economic boom in the 1950s, Ireland did not, its economy growing by only 1% a year during the decade. Ireland as a result experienced sharp emigration of around 50,000 per year during the decade and the population of the state fell to an all-time low of 2.81 million.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The policies of protectionism and low public spending which had predominated since the 1930s were widely viewed to be failing.\n</p><p>Fianna F\xc3\xa1il\'s political dominance was broken in 1948\xe2\x80\x9351 and in 1954\xe2\x80\x931957, when coalitions led by Fine Gael (descendants of Cumann na nGaedheal), and including the Labour Party and <a href="/wiki/Clann_na_Poblachta" title="Clann na Poblachta">Clann na Poblachta</a> won elections and formed the government. However, the periods of coalition rule did little to radically alter government policies. An initiative by <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Browne" title="No\xc3\xabl Browne">No\xc3\xabl Browne</a>, the Minister for Health, to introduce the <a href="/wiki/Mother_and_Child_Scheme" title="Mother and Child Scheme">Mother and Child Scheme</a>, providing free medical care to mothers and children, came to nothing when opposed by the Catholic Church and by private medical practitioners.\n</p><p>Poor economic growth and lack of social services led Se\xc3\xa1n Lemass, who succeeded the veteran \xc3\x89amon de Valera as leader of Fianna F\xc3\xa1il and as Taoiseach in 1958, to state that if economic performance did not improve, the very future of the independent Irish state was at risk. "[Something] has got to be done now... If we fail everything else goes with it and all the hopes of the past will have been falsified".<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lemass, along with <a href="/wiki/T._K._Whitaker" title="T. K. Whitaker">T. K. Whitaker</a> as Secretary of the Department of Finance, set specific plans for economic growth, including planned investment in industrial infrastructure and dropping of many protective tariffs and giving tax incentives to foreign manufacturing companies to set up in Ireland. Attracting <a href="/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment" title="Foreign direct investment">foreign direct investment</a> has remained a central part of Irish economic planning since that time. The economic plans of the Lemass era yielded economic growth of 4% a year between 1959\xe2\x80\x931973. A result of having more public revenue was more investment in social infrastructure \xe2\x80\x93 free secondary education, for instance, was instituted in 1968, by the then Minister for Education, Donough O\'Malley. Emigration fell as living standards in Ireland went up by 50% and began to catch up with the European average.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, in the 1970s, the world energy crisis \xe2\x80\x93 where <a href="/wiki/OPEC" title="OPEC">OPEC</a> countries withheld supplies of oil \xe2\x80\x93 resulted in rising inflation and a budget deficit in Ireland. From 1973\xe2\x80\x931977 a coalition government of Fine Gael and Labour tried to keep spending under control by imposing a series of cuts in public spending.\n</p><p>The period of economic crisis of the late 1970s provoked a new economic crisis in Ireland that would endure throughout the 1980s. Fianna F\xc3\xa1il, back in power after the 1977 election, tried to reactivate the economy by increasing public spending, which by 1981 amounted to 65% of Irish GNP. Irish national debt in 1980 was \xc2\xa37 billion or 81% of GNP. By 1986, it was over \xc2\xa323 billion \xe2\x80\x93  142% of Irish GNP.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This massive public debt hindered Irish economic performance throughout the 1980s. The governments of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Haughey" title="Charles Haughey">Charles Haughey</a> (Fianna F\xc3\xa1il) and <a href="/wiki/Garret_FitzGerald" title="Garret FitzGerald">Garret FitzGerald</a> (Fine Gael/Labour) borrowed even more, and income tax rates went up to between 35% and 60% of wage earners\' income. The combination of high taxes and high unemployment caused emigration to pick up again, with up to 40,000 leaving the country each year in that decade.  Power alternated between the Fianna F\xc3\xa1il and Fine Gael, with some governments not even lasting a year, and in one case, three elections in a period of 18 months.\n</p><p>Starting in 1989 there were significant policy changes with economic reform, tax cuts, welfare reform, an increase in competition, and a ban on borrowing to fund current spending. There was also a "Social Partnership Agreement" with the trade unions, whereby unions agreed not to strike in return for gradual, negotiated pay increases. These policies was started by the 1989\xe2\x80\x931992 <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna F\xc3\xa1il">Fianna F\xc3\xa1il</a>/<a href="/wiki/Progressive_Democrats" title="Progressive Democrats">Progressive Democrat</a> government, with the support of the opposition <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a>, and continued by the subsequent Fianna F\xc3\xa1il/Labour government (1992\xe2\x80\x931994) and Fine Gael/Labour Party/Democratic Left governments (1994\xe2\x80\x931997). This was known as the <a href="/wiki/Tallaght_Strategy" title="Tallaght Strategy">Tallaght Strategy</a>, where the opposition promised not to oppose certain necessary economic measures brought in by the government of the day.\n</p><p>The Irish economy returned to growth by the 1990s but unemployment remained high until the second half of that decade.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Celtic_Tiger">Celtic Tiger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Celtic Tiger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Tiger" title="Celtic Tiger">Celtic Tiger</a></div>\n<p>The state had had a disappointing economic performance for much of its existence, but it became one of the fastest growing economies in the world by the 1990s, a phenomenon known as the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Tiger" title="Celtic Tiger">Celtic Tiger</a>.  One factor in this was a policy of attracting foreign investment by offering very low taxes on profits ("corporations taxes", which were set at 12%) and by investing in education \xe2\x80\x93 offering a well-educated work force at relatively low wages and access to the now-open European market. The second factor was getting public spending under control by a series of agreements, termed \'social partnership\' with the trade unions \xe2\x80\x93 where gradual increases in pay were awarded in return for no industrial action. However it was not until the second half of the 1990s that figures for unemployment and emigration were reversed.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the early 2000s, the Republic had become the second richest (in terms of GDP per capita, adjusted for purchasing power parity) member of the European Union, had moved from being a net recipient of EU funds to a net contributor, and from a position of net emigration to one of net immigration. In 2005, its <i>per capita</i> GDP (adjusted for purchasing power parity) became the second highest in the world (behind <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>) with 10 percent of the population born abroad. The population grew to an all-time high for the state of about 4.5 million.\n</p><p>By 2000 Ireland had a substantial budget surplus and the first decade of the new millennium also saw a significant expansion of public spending on infrastructure and social services. As against this, several state-run industries were also privatised \xe2\x80\x93 Eircom for instance. In 2002, Irish national debt was 32% of GNP and fell further until 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Celtic Tiger started in the mid-1990s and boomed until 2001, when it slowed down, only to pick up again in 2003. It slowed again in 2007 and in June 2008 the Irish <a href="/wiki/Economic_and_Social_Research_Institute" title="Economic and Social Research Institute">Economic and Social Research Institute</a> (ESRI) predicted that Ireland would go into recession briefly before growth would resume.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, since 2001, the Irish economy had been heavily dependent of the property market and when this crashed in 2008, the country\'s economy was badly hit.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_downturn">Economic downturn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Economic downturn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Post-2008_Irish_economic_downturn" title="Post-2008 Irish economic downturn">Post-2008 Irish economic downturn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-2008_Irish_banking_crisis" title="Post-2008 Irish banking crisis">Post-2008 Irish banking crisis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Economy of the Republic of Ireland">Economy of the Republic of Ireland</a></div>\n<p>The Irish banks had invested heavily in loans to property developers and were facing ruin as result of the property markets\' collapse and also the international \'<a href="/wiki/Credit_crunch" title="Credit crunch">Credit crunch</a>\' or drying up of loans from abroad. Much of the Irish economy and public finances had also depended on the property market and its collapse at roughly the same time as the banking crisis impacted all parts of the Irish economy. It also meant that revenue collected by the state fell radically.\n</p><p>This situation was compounded by the assumption by the state of the banks\' debts in 2008. The Irish government led by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Cowen" title="Brian Cowen">Brian Cowen</a>, following a late-night meeting with all the senior banking officials in the country on 30 September 2008, agreed to cover all of the banks debts. This debt, now estimated at over \xe2\x82\xac50 billion, (over half of which will be paid to <a href="/wiki/Anglo_Irish_Bank" title="Anglo Irish Bank">Anglo Irish Bank</a>) imposed a heavy burden on the tax-payer and severely damaged Ireland\'s ability to borrow money from the International Bond markets.\n</p><p>The second problem is that public spending, which rose steeply in the 2000s, was now unsustainable. The total Irish budget deficit as of December 2010, stood at 93.4 billion with General Government Debt at 148.6 billion or 94.2% of GDP.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> As it was not clear how much money would be needed to revitalise the banks \xe2\x80\x93 to clear their debts and supply them with enough money to start lending again \xe2\x80\x93 the international markets were unwilling to lend Ireland money at an interest rate it could afford.\n</p><p>Under pressure from the European Union, which feared a \'run\' (selling causing a collapse in value) of the euro, Ireland was forced to accept a 16-year loan of \xe2\x82\xac85 billion at just under 6% interest from IMF and EU itself.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Not only were the interest rates of the loan high, but the deal also involved a humiliating loss of sovereignty, in which Irish budgets had to first be approved by other parliaments of the EU \xe2\x80\x93 notably that of Germany.\n</p><p>The political result of this crisis was the fall of the Cowen government and a shattering defeat for Fianna F\xc3\xa1il in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2011" title="Irish general election, 2011">Irish general election, 2011</a>, in which the party won just 17% of the vote and retained only 20 out of its 71 seats in the D\xc3\xa1il.  Emigration from Ireland has again picked up and many remain anxious about the economic future.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Relationship_with_Northern_Ireland_1945.E2.80.931998"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Northern_Ireland_1945\xe2\x80\x931998">Relationship with Northern Ireland 1945\xe2\x80\x931998</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Relationship with Northern Ireland 1945\xe2\x80\x931998">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The official position of the Irish state, as laid out in the 1937 constitution, was that the territory of the state comprised the whole island of Ireland, but that its laws applied only to the territory of the Free State, as outlined in the 1922 Treaty. Thereafter the policies of Irish governments pursued the peaceful unification of Ireland through the pressure groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Partition_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Partition League">anti-Partition League</a>. However, at the same time, the state recognised that paramilitary groups \xe2\x80\x93 in particular the IRA \xe2\x80\x93 were also a threat to its own security. Furthermore, their attacks on Northern Ireland could drag the Irish state into an unwanted confrontation with Britain.\n</p><p>In the 1950s, the IRA launched a campaign of attacks on Northern security targets along the border (the <a href="/wiki/Border_Campaign_(Irish_Republican_Army)" title="Border Campaign (Irish Republican Army)">Border Campaign</a>). The Irish government first detained the IRA\'s leaders under the Offences Against the State Act and later introduced internment for all IRA activists. This helped to halt the campaign in its tracks, and the IRA called it off in 1962. In the aftermath of this episode, the southern government under <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Lemass" title="Se\xc3\xa1n Lemass">Se\xc3\xa1n Lemass</a>, himself an IRA veteran of the War of Independence and Civil War, tried to forge closer ties with the authorities in Northern Ireland to promote peaceful co-operation on the island. He and Northern premier <a href="/wiki/Terence_O%27Neill" title="Terence O&#39;Neill">Terence O\'Neill</a> exchanged visits, the first of the respective heads of state to do so since the very early days of partition in 1922.\n</p><p>However, in 1969, the Irish government found itself placed in a very difficult position when conflict erupted in Northern Ireland in the form of rioting in <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> and other urban centres. The violence arose out of agitation by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Civil_Rights_Association" title="Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association">Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association</a> for the redress of grievances of Catholics and nationalists in Northern Ireland. Two episodes in particular caused concern \xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bogside" title="Battle of the Bogside">Battle of the Bogside</a> in Derry, in which nationalists fought the police for three days and the <a href="/wiki/1969_Northern_Ireland_riots" title="1969 Northern Ireland riots">rioting in Belfast</a>, in which several Catholic neighbourhoods were attacked and burned by loyalists.\n</p><p>Taoiseach <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lynch" title="Jack Lynch">Jack Lynch</a> in a televised address, said, "we can not stand by and watch innocent people being injured and perhaps worse", comments taken to mean that Irish troops would be sent over the border to assist Northern nationalists. This was not done, but Irish Army field hospitals were set up and some money and arms were covertly supplied to nationalist groups for self-defence. Government ministers, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Haughey" title="Charles Haughey">Charles Haughey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neil_Blaney" title="Neil Blaney">Neil Blaney</a>, were later put on trial for allegedly supplying arms to republican paramilitaries.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_IRA" class="mw-redirect" title="Provisional IRA">Provisional IRA</a>, emerged from the 1969 rioting intending to launch an armed campaign against the Northern state. By 1972, their campaign was of considerable intensity, killing over 100 British soldiers in that year alone. Unlike the IRA campaign of the 1950s, this campaign was viewed as having considerable public support among Northern nationalists and for this reason, Irish governments did not introduce internment as they had previously, in the absence of a political settlement in Northern Ireland. The Irish government also refused to allow British and Northern Ireland security units to pursue Republican paramilitaries over the border into the Republic and arrested those soldiers or police who did enter its territory armed.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Irish governments however, continued to view illegal armed activity by republicans on its territory as a major security risk. The Garda\xc3\xad and the Irish Army were used to try to impede the activities of republican paramilitary groups throughout the conflict in Northern Ireland known as <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> (c. 1968\xe2\x80\x931998). The paramilitaries\' activities in the south included bank robberies, kidnappings and occasional attacks on the Irish security forces (killing 6 garda\xc3\xad and one Irish soldier) as well as attacks on British forces over the border. Representatives of republican paramilitaries were forbidden from appearing on television or radio by Section 31 of the Broadcasting Act, (1971).<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There were also some attacks by loyalist paramilitary groups in southern territory, notably the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_and_Monaghan_bombings" title="Dublin and Monaghan bombings">Dublin and Monaghan bombings</a> of 1974, which killed 33 people.\n</p><p>In 1985, the Irish government was part of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a>, in which the British government recognised that the Irish government had a role to play in a future peace settlement in the North. In 1994, the Irish government was heavily involved in negotiations which brought about an IRA ceasefire.\n</p><p>In 1998, the Irish authorities were again party to a settlement, the Good Friday Agreement, which set up power-sharing institutions within Northern Ireland, North-South instructions and links between the various components of the United Kingdom and the Republic of Ireland. The Irish state also changed Articles 2 and 3 of the constitution to acknowledge both the existence of Northern Ireland and the desire of Irish nationalists for a united Ireland. Even in the wake of the post-Good Friday Agreement incorporation of the Provisional IRA and Sinn F\xc3\xa9in into electoral politics, there remain several republican paramilitary groups who wish to use force to destabilise Northern Ireland \xe2\x80\x93 such as the <a href="/wiki/Real_IRA" class="mw-redirect" title="Real IRA">Real IRA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Continuity_IRA" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuity IRA">Continuity IRA</a>. Irish security forces continue to be used to try to prevent attacks by such groups.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_liberalisation">Social liberalisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Social liberalisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the late twentieth century, Irish society underwent rapid social change. After the introduction of free education in the late 1960s, many more people had access to second and third level qualifications. The relative economic success of the 1960s and 1970s also decreased emigration, meaning that Ireland became a younger and much more urban society than before. The spread of television and other mass media also exposed Irish citizens to a far wider range of influences than previously. All of these factors loosened the power of the traditional political parties and the Catholic Church over society.\n</p><p>By the 1980s, some were calling for liberalisation of the state\'s laws, particularly a review of the bans on divorce, contraception, and homosexuality. However, they were also opposed by well-organised groups who accused the reformers of being irreligious and "anti-family". That decade saw bitter disagreement between socially conservative, principally religious, elements and liberals over a series of referendums.\n</p><p>In 1983, the Pro-Life Amendment Campaign campaigned for and won a referendum, explicitly including a ban on abortion into the constitution \xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">Eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland</a>.\n</p><p>The liberals had a victory in 1985, when it was made legal to buy <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">condoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spermicide" title="Spermicide">spermicides</a> without prescription. Nevertheless, it was not until 1993 that all restrictions on information and sale of contraceptives were abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> In 1986, the Fine Gael/Labour coalition proposed to remove the ban on divorce. This was opposed by Fianna F\xc3\xa1il and the Catholic Church and the <a href="/wiki/Tenth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_Bill_1986" title="Tenth Amendment of the Constitution Bill 1986">Tenth Amendment of the Constitution Bill 1986</a> was defeated in a referendum.\n</p><p>Since 1992 the state has become less socially conservative. Liberalisation has been championed by figures like <a href="/wiki/Mary_Robinson" title="Mary Robinson">Mary Robinson</a>, a radical feminist senator who became <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Norris_(politician)" title="David Norris (politician)">David Norris</a>, who led the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Homosexual_Law_Reform" title="Campaign for Homosexual Law Reform">Campaign for Homosexual Law Reform</a>. Homosexual sex was decriminalised by an act of parliament in 1993.\n</p><p>The constitutional ban on abortion was softened somewhat in 1992. After a referendum in that year, the <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_Bill_1992" title="Twelfth Amendment of the Constitution Bill 1992">Twelfth Amendment of the Constitution Bill 1992</a> was approved, it was made legal to perform an abortion to save the life of a mother, to give information about abortion and to travel to another country for an abortion.\n</p><p>In 1995, after a referendum, the <a href="/wiki/Fifteenth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Fifteenth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">Fifteenth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland</a> legalised divorce.\n</p><p>In the 1980s and early 1990s, these questions were deeply divisive in the Republic of Ireland and exposed deep social cleavages between religious and secular-minded people, urban and rural, middle and working classes. Issues such as divorce, contraception and homosexuality have since become accepted by many and have ceased to be matters of serious political debate. However, legalising abortion remained controversial. Opinion poll evidence on the subject of abortion is mixed.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On May 25, 2018 Ireland voted to repeal the ban on abortion. Legislation is expected by the end of the year to allow abortion on demand up to 12 weeks and under restrictions after that. <sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2015, Ireland became the first country in the world to <a href="/wiki/Recognition_of_same-sex_unions_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Recognition of same-sex unions in the Republic of Ireland">legalise same-sex marriage</a> by means of popular referendum, when the <a href="/wiki/Thirty-fourth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_(Marriage_Equality)_Bill_2015" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty-fourth Amendment of the Constitution (Marriage Equality) Bill 2015">Thirty-fourth Amendment of the Constitution (Marriage Equality) Bill 2015</a> was passed by just over 60% of voters.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_scandals">National scandals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: National scandals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Part of the reason why social liberalisaton was widely accepted by the 1990s was due to a very damaging series of scandals in that decade. The revelation that one senior <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> bishop, <a href="/wiki/Eamon_Casey" class="mw-redirect" title="Eamon Casey">Eamon Casey</a>, fathered a child by a divorc\xc3\xa9e caused a major reaction, as did the discovery of a child abuse ring whereby offenders became clerics in order to use their position in the Roman Catholic Church to obtain access to victims\xe2\x80\x94notably the infamous paedophile <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Smyth_(priest)" class="mw-redirect" title="Brendan Smyth (priest)">Father Brendan Smyth</a>. Another bishop, McGee, subsequently resigned over his mishandling of child abuse cases in his diocese.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\nIt was also revealed, in the 2000s, after an enquiry, the <a href="/wiki/Commission_to_Inquire_into_Child_Abuse" title="Commission to Inquire into Child Abuse">Ryan Commission</a>, that there had been widespread physical and sexual abuse of children in secular and Church-run <a href="/wiki/Industrial_school" title="Industrial school">industrial schools</a> and orphanages from the 1920s until the 1960s. These were institutions which were set up to house children without families or with very poor parents. In some cases, it was claimed, these children had been removed from their parents only to be put into institutions worse than their previous state.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While other factors have also played a role, the scandals in the Catholic Church have contributed to a steep decline in church attendance among Irish Catholics. While in 1991, 92% of the Republic\'s population identified themselves as Roman Catholics, by 2006 this had dropped to 86%. More starkly, whereas in 1990, 85% of Catholics attended mass weekly, by 2008 this had fallen to 43% among Catholics and 40% of the population in general.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> (See also <a href="/wiki/Catholic_sexual_abuse_scandal_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic sexual abuse scandal in Ireland">Catholic sexual abuse scandal in Ireland</a>)\n</p><p>In the 1990s, a series of tribunals began inquiring into major allegations of corruption against senior politicians. <a href="/wiki/Ray_Burke_(Irish_politician)" title="Ray Burke (Irish politician)">Ray Burke</a>, who served as <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs and Trade">Minister for Foreign Affairs</a> in 1997 was <a href="/wiki/Gaol" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaol">gaoled</a> on charges of <a href="/wiki/Tax_evasion" title="Tax evasion">tax evasion</a> in January 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Beef_Tribunal" title="Beef Tribunal">Beef Tribunal</a> in the early 1990s found that major food companies, notably in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> had been given preferential treatment by the Fianna F\xc3\xa1il government in return for donations to that party.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Former Taoisaighs Charles Haughey and <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a> were also brought before Tribunals to explain their acceptance of very large personal donations of money to them by private businessmen.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
