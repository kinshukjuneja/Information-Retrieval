template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Hong Kong English</b> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">\xe6\xb8\xaf\xe5\xbc\x8f\xe8\x8b\xb1\xe6\x96\x87</span>) is the <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> most commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>. The dialect is a result of Hong Kong\'s <a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">British overseas territory history</a> and the influence of native <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> speakers.\n</p><p>Being a former British colony, Hong Kong predominantly uses British spellings.  Pronunciations and words are also predominantly British<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, although influences from American, Canadian and <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a> do exist as a result of <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_movies" class="mw-redirect" title="Hollywood movies">Hollywood movies</a>, TV and Internet culture.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In fact, a lot of Hong Kong Chinese families migrated to (in alphabetical order) Australia, Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Ireland, New Zealand and the United States in the 1990s after <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_protests_of_1989" title="Tiananmen Square protests of 1989">Tiananmen Square protests of 1989</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>, and when they move back they are less likely to use <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>. There is also an influence from the significant non-Chinese demographic (e.g., expats and <a href="/wiki/Foreign_domestic_helpers_in_Hong_Kong" title="Foreign domestic helpers in Hong Kong">maids</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Examinations_and_Assessment_Authority" title="Hong Kong Examinations and Assessment Authority">Hong Kong Examinations and Assessment Authority</a> accepts "all varieties of English" as "[e]xaminers come from many different places."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> According to article 9 of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Basic_Law" title="Hong Kong Basic Law">Hong Kong Basic Law</a>, "English may also be used as an official language" but the law does not specify which <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Varieties of English">type of English</a>.\n</p><p><b>Cantonese English</b>, locally referred to as <a href="/wiki/Chinglish" title="Chinglish">Chinglish</a>, in theory, refers to the <a href="/wiki/Accent_(dialect)" class="mw-redirect" title="Accent (dialect)">accent</a> and characteristics of English spoken by native <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kongers" class="mw-redirect" title="Hong Kongers">Hong Kongers</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_people" title="Cantonese people">Cantonese people</a>. Overall, it is primarily spoken by native Hong Kong language (Cantonese) speakers. Therefore, although it is called <i>Hong Kong English</i>, it is not only spoken in Hong Kong. People who come from <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a>, or whose first language is Cantonese speak it.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_Kong_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>English is one of the <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official languages</a> in Hong Kong, and is used widely in the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Hong Kong Government">Government</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Hong_Kong" class="mw-redirect" title="List of universities in Hong Kong">academic circles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Hong_Kong" title="Economy of Hong Kong">business</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_of_Hong_Kong" title="Judiciary of Hong Kong">courts</a>. All road and government signs are bilingual and English is as equally valid as Chinese on legal and business standings. English is what distinguished most and those who spoke English or were taught English were considered the elite, meaning those able to be taught English were considered upperclassmen. This conceptualized way of thinking arose in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-Eoyang_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eoyang-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This dialect is its own category and is the standard in Hong Kong.<sup id="cite_ref-Eoyang_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eoyang-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>People with higher education, past experience of living in English-speaking countries, or who constantly interact with Hong Kong\'s English-speaking <a href="/wiki/Expatriate" title="Expatriate">expatriate</a> communities, generally speak an acquired form of English. <a href="/wiki/Accent_(sociolinguistics)" title="Accent (sociolinguistics)">Accent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spelling" title="Spelling">spelling</a> preference may vary from person to person, depending on the people they have interacted with and the country they have studied in. For most ordinary local Hong Kongers however, the English spoken is generally typical of <a href="/wiki/Foreign_language" title="Foreign language">foreign language</a> learners: Cantonese-influenced pronunciation with some acquired <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a> characteristics, and with vocabularies and sentence structure generally more formal than those of native speakers. For instance, contractions and slang are not used, and many idioms are alien to Hong Kongers because the terms pertain more to the cultures of English-speaking countries.\nThe falling English proficiency of local English language teachers has come under criticism.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_sovereignty_over_Hong_Kong" class="mw-redirect" title="Transfer of sovereignty over Hong Kong">Handover</a>, English in Hong Kong remains primarily a second language, in contrast to Singapore where English has been shifting toward being a first language.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">Pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span In contrast, big is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/p\xc9\xaak\xcc\x9a/</span>. This is because in Cantonese, the checked tones t\xcc\x9a k\xcc\x9a p\xcc\x9a have only one vowel assigned to them that is approximate to i or \xc9\xaa (it\xcc\x9a, \xc9\xaak\xcc\x9a, and ip\xcc\x9a) .</li>\n<li>Merging <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x8a/</span> with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u\xcb\x90/</span>; "foot" and "food" are pronounced both <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/fut\xcc\x9a/</span>.</li>\n<li>The letter "z" is generally pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ji\xcb\x88s\xc9\x9bt\xcc\x9a/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i\xcb\x88s\xc9\x9bt\xcc\x9a/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i\xcb\x88z\xc9\x9bt\xcc\x9a/</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ji\xcb\x88z\xc9\x9bt\xcc\x9a/</span> derived from a British pronunciation <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xaa\xcb\x88z\xc3\xa6d/</span> now considered archaic elsewhere; the usual pronunciations, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/z\xc9\x9bd/</span> (used in UK and most of the Commonwealth nations) and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/zi\xcb\x90/</span> (used in USA), are not understood by some.</li>\n<li>Due to Cantonese phonology, many Hong Kongers have difficulty pronouncing double consonant endings, except when the second element is fricative. e.g. "think" as "thing", "slamp" as "slem", "white" as "why", "send" and "sent" as "sen". "Sense" is unaffected.</li>\n<li>Finals like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/-kt/</span> are reduced to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/-k\xcc\x9a/</span>.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intonation">Intonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_Kong_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Intonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>Multi-<a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllable</a> words are often differently stressed. In Chinese, 10 thousand is read as one <a href="/wiki/Myriad" title="Myriad">myriad</a> (\xe8\x90\xac), 100 thousand as 10 myriad, one million as 100 myriad. Despite this, most people will not use the English word "myriad" so this type of English is hardly ever seen.</li>\n<li>Fractions: In Chinese, the denominator is read before the numerator, so "three over four" (or three-fourths) may be misunderstood as "four over three". In this example, three-fourths in Chinese is "\xe5\x9b\x9b\xe5\x88\x86\xe4\xb9\x8b\xe4\xb8\x89", literally "out of four portions, three".</li>\n<li>Discounts: the Chinese way of saying 10% off is "90% of the original price". This is often written as 9\xe6\x8a\x98 (or \xe4\xb9\x9d\xe6\x8a\x98) which is understood to mean "discounted to 9/10 of the original price". When two digits are given the divisor is understood to be 100, e.g. 75\xe6\x8a\x98 (or \xe4\xb8\x83\xe4\xba\x94\xe6\x8a\x98) means 25% off.</li></ul>\n<h2><span id="American.2FBritish_spelling_and_word_usage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="American/British_spelling_and_word_usage">American/British spelling and word usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_Kong_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: American/British spelling and word usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg/200px-TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg" width="200" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg/300px-TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg/400px-TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="568" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TSTNewWorldCentre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This is the entrance of the shopping centre "<a href="/wiki/New_World_Centre" title="New World Centre">New World Centre</a>" in Hong Kong. Center">The Center</a> (\xe4\xb8\xad\xe7\x92\xb0\xe4\xb8\xad\xe5\xbf\x83) is a rare example of American spelling in Hong Kong.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_Kong_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>end-word: In informal conversation like instant messengers, <a href="/wiki/Sentence-final_particle" title="Sentence-final particle">sentence-final particles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Interjections" class="mw-redirect" title="Interjections">interjections</a> of Cantonese <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">origin</a> such as <i>ah</i>, <i>lah</i>, <i>law</i>, <i>mah</i> and <i>waw\'</i>\xe2\x80\x94many of these being "<a href="/wiki/Chinese_grammar" title="Chinese grammar">flavouring particles</a>"\xe2\x80\x94are used at the ends of English <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentences</a>.</li>\n<li>"I\'ve eaten dinner law" ("I\'ve had dinner"\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9claw" <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l\xc9\x94\xcb\x90\xcb\xa7/</span> indicates a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">perfect</a> and makes the sentence more informal)</li>\n<li>"I go lah, bye" ("I\'m leaving, bye!"\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9clah" <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/la\xcb\x90\xcb\xa7/</span> indicates intent and makes the sentence more informal)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nullah_Road_HK.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nullah_Road_HK.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nullah Road, Mong Kok</div></div></div>\n<p>Some words and phrases widely understood in Hong Kong are rare or unheard of elsewhere.  These often derive from Chinese, <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Indian" title="Anglo-Indian">Anglo-Indian</a> or Portuguese/<a href="/wiki/Macanese_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Macanese language">Macanese</a>.\n</p>\n<ul><li>A \'<a href="/wiki/Chop_(device)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chop (device)">chop</a>\' is a seal or stamp, e.g. a "Company chop" is the seal or stamp of a corporation (It actually originates from colonial Indian English.) It is now used in some other Commonwealth countries as a non-official term</li>\n<li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Tai-Pan" class="mw-redirect" title="Tai-Pan">Tai-Pan</a></i> (or \'taipan\') is a term used in the early 20th century for a business executive of a large corporation.</li>\n<li>An <i><a href="/wiki/Amah_(occupation)" title="Amah (occupation)">amah</a></i> is a term used in the early 20th century for a live-in servant (from Macanese/Portuguese- <i>ama</i> nurse); now supplanted by "<a href="/wiki/Foreign_domestic_helpers_in_Hong_Kong" title="Foreign domestic helpers in Hong Kong">[domestic] helper</a>"</li>\n<li>A \'shroff\' is a cashier in a hospital, a government office or a car park (parking garage).</li>\n<li>"Godown" is a <a href="/wiki/Warehouse" title="Warehouse">warehouse</a> From the Malay "gudang".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Nullah" title="Nullah">Nullah</a></i> is a concrete-lined canal or an re-enforced creek bed used to contain run-off.  <i>Nullah</i> entered the English language from Hindi.  The word nullah is used almost exclusively in Hong Kong.</li>\n<li><i>Lai see</i> means Lucky money.</li>\n<li>\'FILTH\'/\'Filth\' (<b>F</b>ailed <b>i</b>n <b>L</b>ondon, <b>T</b>ry <b>H</b>ongkong<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>) is a slang expression used to refer to people who look for employment opportunities in Hong Kong after being unsuccessful in looking for a job in the United Kingdom.</li>\n<li>The word \'seldom\' is used instead of \'rarely\' in informal English \xe2\x80\x93 the word seldom is considered formal in English speaking countries.</li>\n<li>A <a href="/wiki/Raffle" title="Raffle">raffle</a> is invariably referred to as a \'lucky draw\'.</li>\n<li><i>Jetso</i> ("\xe8\x91\x97\xe6\x95\xb8") is sometimes used to mean <i>discount</i> or <i>special offer</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (June 2018)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li>\'Hong Kong foot\', a literal translation of the Chinese slang term "\xe9\xa6\x99\xe6\xb8\xaf\xe8\x85\xb3" for <a href="/wiki/Athlete%27s_foot" title="Athlete&#39;s foot">athlete\'s foot</a>. An early record for the Chinese name was found in Chen Jun Bao\'s diary (\xe9\x99\xb3\xe5\x90\x9b\xe8\x91\x86\xe6\x97\xa5\xe8\xa8\x98),<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> where an entry from 1944-08-03 claimed the term was named by Shanghaiese, while Hongkongers called it \'Singapore foot\' (\xe6\x98\x9f\xe5\x8a\xa0\xe5\x9d\xa1\xe8\x85\xb3), and Singaporean called it \'Manila foot\' (\xe6\x9b\xbc\xe5\xb0\xbc\xe5\x89\x8c\xe8\x85\xb3).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> \'Singapore foot\' is still used by Singaporean media as of 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The word \'cheap\' can mean "of good value" in formal English.  In Hong Kong English, the word \'cheap\' tends towards the more derogatory connotation, meaning something is of low quality and distasteful.</li></ul>\n<p>Research is also being done on the generation of new Hong Kong English vocabulary driven by computer mediated communication between bilingual Cantonese and English speakers. Rather than use complicated Chinese character keyboard interfaces, Hong Kong English speakers will text and email English translations to the point that the English word often gains independent usage.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>\'Add Oil\': [add oil!] verb, literally to fuel, is used more as an idiom to encourage a team or person. The term has long been limited to the Cantonese "gaa jau!" (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8A%A0%E6%B2%B9" class="extiw" title="wikt:\xe5\x8a\xa0\xe6\xb2\xb9">\xe5\x8a\xa0\xe6\xb2\xb9</a>), after originating as a cheer at the Macau Grand Prix in the 60s, but Hong Kong English speakers are increasingly using the English transliteration.</li>\n<li>\'Locust\': [lo-cust] noun, referring to tourists from the mainland and meant to connote both masses and a drain on local resources. The word is derogatory and originated during shortages of powdered milk, which <a href="/wiki/Anti-parallel_trading_protests" title="Anti-parallel trading protests">anti-parallel trading protesters</a> blamed on mainland parallel traders.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Light_bulb" class="mw-redirect" title="Light bulb">Light bulb</a></i> (literal translation of "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9B%BB%E7%87%88%E6%B3%A1" class="extiw" title="wikt:\xe9\x9b\xbb\xe7\x87\x88\xe6\xb3\xa1">\xe9\x9b\xbb\xe7\x87\x88\xe8\x86\xbd</a>") means a third person that spoils the ideal combination of a couple.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> "Do not be a light bulb" means "Do not play <a href="/wiki/Gooseberry" title="Gooseberry">gooseberry</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_differences_the_Cantonese-speaking_community">Common differences the Cantonese-speaking community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_Kong_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Common differences the Cantonese-speaking community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>A \'body check\' is used to refer to a medical checkup (medical examination), not a contact with an opponent from the front (due to literal translation from Chinese)</li>\n<li>"Outlook" is often (mis)understood as "appearance".  The noun "look" or "appearance" ("\xe8\xa1\xa8") in Cantonese is commonly preceded by the character for "outer" ("\xe5\xa4\x96")</li>\n<li>Using "open" instead of "turn on" and "close" instead of "turn off" for electronics such as televisions and fans. In spoken Cantonese, closing the door("\xe9\x96\x82" for the action, "\xe9\x96\x80" for the door) and turning off appliances("\xe7\x86\x84" for the action) is denoted differently. Nonetheless, in formal writing, the same character ("\xe9\x97\x9c") is commonly used for both.</li>\n<li>read "late-er" for later.</li>\n<li>read "surface" for service.</li>\n<li>read "pon-t" for
