template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>London boroughs</b> are 32 of the 33 <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">local authority districts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> administrative area (the 33rd is the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>). Each is governed by a <a href="/wiki/London_borough_council" class="mw-redirect" title="London borough council">London borough council</a>. The London boroughs were all created at the same time as Greater London on 1 April 1965 by the <a href="/wiki/London_Government_Act_1963" title="London Government Act 1963">London Government Act 1963</a> and are a type of local government district. Twelve were designated as <a href="/wiki/Inner_London" title="Inner London">Inner London</a> boroughs and twenty as <a href="/wiki/Outer_London" title="Outer London">Outer London</a> boroughs.\n</p><p>The London boroughs have populations of around 150,000 to 300,000. Inner London boroughs tend to be smaller, in both population and area, and more densely populated than Outer London boroughs. The London boroughs were created by combining groups of former local government units. A review undertaken between 1987 and 1992 led to a number of relatively small alterations in borough boundaries.\n</p><p>London borough councils provide the majority of local government services (schools, waste management, social services, libraries, etc.), in contrast to the strategic <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a>, which has limited authority over all of Greater London.\n</p><p>The councils were first <a href="/wiki/London_local_elections,_1964" title="London local elections, 1964">elected in 1964</a> and acted as shadow authorities until 1 April 1965. Each borough is divided into <a href="/wiki/List_of_electoral_wards_in_Greater_London" title="List of electoral wards in Greater London">electoral wards</a>, subject to periodic review, for the purpose of electing councillors. Council elections take place every four years, with the <a href="/wiki/London_local_elections,_2014" title="London local elections, 2014">most recent elections</a> in 2018 and the next elections due in 2022.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Political_make-up_of_London_borough_councils" class="mw-redirect" title="Political make-up of London borough councils">political make-up of London borough councils</a> is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> parties. Twenty-eight councils follow the <a href="/wiki/Leader_and_cabinet" class="mw-redirect" title="Leader and cabinet">leader and cabinet</a> model of executive governance, with directly elected mayors in <a href="/wiki/Directly_elected_mayor_of_Hackney" class="mw-redirect" title="Directly elected mayor of Hackney">Hackney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Directly_elected_mayor_of_Lewisham" class="mw-redirect" title="Directly elected mayor of Lewisham">Lewisham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Directly_elected_mayor_of_Newham" class="mw-redirect" title="Directly elected mayor of Newham">Newham</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Directly_elected_mayor_of_Tower_Hamlets" class="mw-redirect" title="Directly elected mayor of Tower Hamlets">Tower Hamlets</a>. The <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> is instead governed by the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">Creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From the mid-1930s, the Greater London area comprised four types of local government authorities. There were <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal boroughs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban districts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_boroughs_of_the_County_of_London" title="Metropolitan boroughs of the County of London">metropolitan boroughs</a>. The large county boroughs provided all local government services and held the powers usually invested in county councils. The municipal borough and urban district authorities had fewer powers. The situation was made more complex because county councils could delegate functions such as elementary education and library provision to the municipal borough and district councils, and this was implemented piecemeal. Reform of London local government sought to regularise this arrangement.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_Local_Government_in_Greater_London" title="Royal Commission on Local Government in Greater London">Royal Commission on Local Government in Greater London</a> was established in 1957 and the report was published on 19 October 1960. It proposed 52 "Greater London Boroughs" with a population range of 100,000 to 250,000.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This was made up of a mixture of whole existing units, mergers of two or three areas, and two boroughs formed as the result of a split. In December 1961 the government proposed that there would be 34 boroughs rather than 52, and detailed their boundaries. The proposed number was further reduced to 32 in 1962.\n</p><p>On 1 April 1965, the 32 London boroughs and <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> were created by the <a href="/wiki/London_Government_Act_1963" title="London Government Act 1963">London Government Act 1963</a>. Twelve boroughs in the former County of London area were designated <a href="/wiki/Inner_London" title="Inner London">Inner London</a> boroughs and the twenty others were designated <a href="/wiki/Outer_London" title="Outer London">Outer London</a> boroughs. Outer London borough councils were <a href="/wiki/Local_education_authority" title="Local education authority">local education authorities</a>, but Inner London borough councils were so designated primarily to continue the existence of an <a href="/wiki/Inner_London_Education_Authority" title="Inner London Education Authority">Inner London Education Authority</a>, praised by official Opposition and government who further noted that unusually the former County of London\'s many small local authorities had no history of providing education. The <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> continued to be administered by the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Temple" title="Inner Temple">Inner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Temple" title="Middle Temple">Middle</a> Temples.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;notes 1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Elections were held on <a href="/wiki/London_local_elections,_1964" title="London local elections, 1964">7 May 1964</a>, with the new councils acting as shadow authorities before coming into their powers the following year.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_authorities">Former authorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Former authorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The boroughs were created as follows. id="Greater_London_Council">Greater London Council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Greater London Council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Between 1965 and 1986 the boroughs were part of a two-tier system of government and shared power with the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a> (GLC). The split of powers and functions meant that the Greater London Council was responsible for "wide area" services such as fire, ambulance, flood prevention, and refuse disposal; with the London borough councils responsible for "personal" services such as social care, libraries, cemeteries and refuse collection. Several London borough councils and the GLC were involved in the <a href="/wiki/Rate-capping_rebellion" title="Rate-capping rebellion">rate-capping rebellion</a> of 1985. On 1 April 1986 the GLC was abolished and the borough councils gained responsibility for some services that had been provided by the Greater London Council, such as <a href="/wiki/Waste_disposal_authorities_in_London" title="Waste disposal authorities in London">waste disposal</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Inner_London_Education_Authority" title="Inner London Education Authority">Inner London Education Authority</a> continued to exist as an ad hoc authority. In 1990 it was abolished and the Inner London borough councils also became local education authorities.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_boundary_changes">Name and boundary changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Name and boundary changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> provided a mechanism for the name of a London borough and its council to be changed. This was used by the London Borough of Hammersmith (changed to Hammersmith and Fulham) on 1 April 1979 and the London Borough of Barking (changed to Barking and Dagenham) on 1 January 1980. Borough names formed by combining two locality names had been discouraged when the boroughs were created.\n</p><p>The London boroughs were created by combining whole existing units of local government and it was realised that this might provide arbitrary boundaries in some places. The London Government Act 1963 provided a mechanism for communities on the edge of Greater London to petition for transfer from London boroughs to a neighbouring county district.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This was used in 1969 in the transfers of <a href="/wiki/Knockholt" title="Knockholt">Knockholt</a> in Bromley to Kent, and of <a href="/wiki/Farleigh,_Surrey" title="Farleigh, Surrey">Farleigh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hooley" title="Hooley">Hooley</a> in Croydon to Surrey. The Act also provided for transfers between London boroughs and neighbouring counties where there was consensus for the change between all the relevant local authorities. This provision was used to exchange two islands on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> between Richmond upon Thames and Surrey. (See <a href="/wiki/List_of_Greater_London_boundary_changes" title="List of Greater London boundary changes">List of Greater London boundary changes</a>.)\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Boundary_Commission_for_England" title="Local Government Boundary Commission for England">Local Government Boundary Commission for England</a> was established by the Local Government Act 1972 to review periodically the boundaries of Greater London and the London boroughs. The first review of boundaries commenced on 1 April 1987 and reported in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Following the review a series of relatively minor adjustments were made to borough boundaries, for example uniting the whole of the <a href="/wiki/Becontree" title="Becontree">Becontree</a> estate in <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Barking_and_Dagenham" title="London Borough of Barking and Dagenham">Barking and Dagenham</a>. The commission noted that many of its recommendations were strongly opposed and were not implemented. The boundary of the City of London with adjacent boroughs was adjusted to remove some anomalies.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the London boroughs the legal entity is not the council as elsewhere in the country, but the inhabitants incorporated as a legal entity by <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a> (a process abolished elsewhere in England and Wales under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>). Thus, a London authority\'s official legal title is "The Mayor and Burgesses of the London Borough of X" (or "The Lord Mayor and Citizens of the City of Westminster").\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_London_Authority">Greater London Authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Greater London Authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2000 the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a> was created, comprising the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a>. As a strategic authority, it absorbed only limited powers, such as major highways and planning strategy, from the borough councils.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="London_borough_councils">London borough councils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: London borough councils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The London boroughs are administered by London borough councils (sometimes abbreviated LBCs) which are elected every four years. They are the principal <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">local authorities</a> in London and are responsible for running most local services, such as schools, social services, waste collection and roads. Some London-wide services are run by the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a>, and some services and lobbying of government are pooled within <a href="/wiki/London_Councils" title="London Councils">London Councils</a>. Some councils group together for services such as waste collection and disposal (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Waste_disposal_authorities_in_London" title="Waste disposal authorities in London">West London Waste Authority</a>). The boroughs are <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">local government districts</a> and have similar functions to <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a>. Each borough council is a <a href="/wiki/Local_education_authority" title="Local education authority">local education authority</a>.\n</p>\n<table class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Shared services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Shared_services" title="Shared services">Shared services</a> are borough council services shared between two or more boroughs. Shared services were previously resisted due to councils guarding their authority. However, as the need for budget cuts in the late 2000s became apparent some councils have sought service mergers.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Westminster and Hammersmith &amp; Fulham were due to merge their education services, including school admissions and transport, by 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In October 2010, <a href="/wiki/Hammersmith_and_Fulham_London_Borough_Council" title="Hammersmith and Fulham London Borough Council">Hammersmith &amp; Fulham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kensington_and_Chelsea_London_Borough_Council" title="Kensington and Chelsea London Borough Council">Kensington &amp; Chelsea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westminster_City_Council" title="Westminster City Council">Westminster</a> announced <a href="/wiki/Tri-borough_shared_services" title="Tri-borough shared services">plans to merge all their services</a> to create a "super-council". Each would retain its own political identity, leadership and councillors but staff and budgets would be combined for cost savings.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Lambeth and Southwark likewise expressed an interest in sharing services.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Critics_of_shared_services">Critics of shared services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_boroughs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critics of shared services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The management thinker and inventor of <a href="/wiki/The_Vanguard_Method" title="The Vanguard Method">the Vanguard Method</a>, Professor <a href="/wiki/John_Seddon" title="John Seddon">John Seddon</a>, claims that shared service projects based on attempts to achieve <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a> are a mix of a) the plausibly obvious and b) a little hard data<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, brought together to produce two broad assertions, for which there is little hard factual evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He argues that shared service projects fail (and often end up costing more than they hoped to save) because they cause a disruption to the service flow by
