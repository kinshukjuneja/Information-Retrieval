data-file-width="177" data-file-height="174" /></th></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Northern Isles</b> (<a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>: <i lang="sco">Northren Isles</i>; <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <i lang="gd">Na h-Eileanan a Tuath</i>; <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a>: <i lang="non">Nor\xc3\xb0reyjar</i>) are a pair of <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a> off the north coast of mainland <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, comprising <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>. The climate is cool and temperate and much influenced by the surrounding seas. There are a total of 26 inhabited islands with landscapes of the fertile agricultural islands of Orkney contrasting with the more rugged Shetland islands to the north, where the economy is more dependent on fishing and the oil wealth of the surrounding seas.  Both have a developing <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> industry.  They also share a common <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norse_activity_in_the_British_Isles" title="Norse activity in the British Isles">Norse</a> history. Both island groups were absorbed into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a> in the 15th century and remained part of the country following the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> in 1707, and later the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> after 1801.  The islands played a significant naval role during the <a href="/wiki/World_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="World wars">world wars</a> of the 20th century.\n</p><p>Tourism is important to both archipelagos, with their distinctive prehistoric ruins playing a key part in their attraction, and there are regular ferry and air connections with mainland Scotland.  The <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> influence remains strong, especially in relation to local folklore, and both island chains have strong, although distinct, local cultures.  The place names of the islands are dominated by their Norse heritage, although some may retain pre-<a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> elements.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" The main islands lie on a north south axis, with the Norwegian Sea to the north and west and the North Sea to the south and east." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Wfm_shetland_map.png/220px-Wfm_shetland_map.png" width="220" height="289" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Wfm_shetland_map.png/330px-Wfm_shetland_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Wfm_shetland_map.png/440px-Wfm_shetland_map.png 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="2624" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wfm_shetland_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The phrase "Northern Isles" generally refers to the main islands of the Orkney and Shetland archipelagos. <a href="/wiki/Stroma,_Scotland" title="Stroma, Scotland">Stroma</a>, which lies between mainland Scotland and Orkney, is part of <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>, and so falls under <a href="/wiki/Highland_(council_area)" title="Highland (council area)">Highland</a> <a href="/wiki/Council_areas_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Council areas of Scotland">council area</a> for <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">local government</a> purposes, not Orkney. It is, however, clearly one of the "northern isles" of Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fair_Isle" title="Fair Isle">Fair Isle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foula" title="Foula">Foula</a> are outliers of Shetland, but would normally be considered as part of Shetland and thus the Northern Isles. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Sule_Skerry" title="Sule Skerry">Sule Skerry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sule_Stack" title="Sule Stack">Sule Stack</a>, although distant from the main group, are part of Orkney and technically amongst the Northern Isles. However, the other small <a href="/wiki/List_of_outlying_islands_of_Scotland" title="List of outlying islands of Scotland">islands that lie off the north coast</a> of Scotland are in Highland and thus not usually considered to be part of the Northern Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Orkney is situated 16 kilometres (10&#160;mi) north of the coast of mainland Scotland, from which it is separated by the waters of the <a href="/wiki/Pentland_Firth" title="Pentland Firth">Pentland Firth</a>.\nThe largest island, known as the "<a href="/wiki/Mainland,_Orkney" title="Mainland, Orkney">Mainland</a>" has an area of 523.25 square kilometres (202.03&#160;sq&#160;mi), making it the sixth largest <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Scotland" title="List of islands of Scotland">Scottish island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The total population in 2001 was 19,245 and the largest town is <a href="/wiki/Kirkwall" title="Kirkwall">Kirkwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vis_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vis-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Shetland is around 170 kilometres (110&#160;mi) north of mainland Scotland, covers an area of 1,468 square kilometres (567&#160;sq&#160;mi) and has a coastline 2,702 kilometres (1,679&#160;mi) long.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lerwick" title="Lerwick">Lerwick</a>, the capital and largest settlement, has a population of around 7,500 and about half of the archipelago\'s total population of 22,000 people live within 16 kilometres (10&#160;mi) of the town.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Orkney has 20 inhabited islands and Shetland a total of 16.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Orkney" title="Geology of Orkney">Geology of Orkney</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:OrkneyGeologyMap.png" class="image"><img alt="A map of the geology of Orkney. Hoy to the south west is predominantly formed from Hoy/Eday Sandstones. The Mainland at centre is largely Stromness flagstones with Rousay flagstones to the east. The smaller North and South Isles are a mixture of Eday and Rousay sandstones." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OrkneyGeologyMap.png/220px-OrkneyGeologyMap.png" width="220" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OrkneyGeologyMap.png/330px-OrkneyGeologyMap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OrkneyGeologyMap.png/440px-OrkneyGeologyMap.png 2x" data-file-width="709" data-file-height="840" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:OrkneyGeologyMap.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Orkney" title="Geology of Orkney">Geology of Orkney</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The superficial rock of Orkney is almost entirely <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a>, mostly of Middle <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> age.<sup id="cite_ref-Millennium_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millennium-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As in the neighbouring mainland county of Caithness, this sandstone rests upon the <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Moine_Supergroup" title="Moine Supergroup">Moine</a> series, as may be seen on the Orkney Mainland, where a narrow strip of the older rock is exposed between <a href="/wiki/Stromness" title="Stromness">Stromness</a> and Inganess, and again on the small island of <a href="/wiki/Graemsay" title="Graemsay">Graemsay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Middle Devonian <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic rocks</a> are found on western <a href="/wiki/Hoy" title="Hoy">Hoy</a>, on Deerness in eastern Mainland and on <a href="/wiki/Shapinsay" title="Shapinsay">Shapinsay</a>. Correlation between the Hoy volcanics and the other two exposures has been proposed, but differences in chemistry means this remains uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lamprophyre" title="Lamprophyre">Lamprophyre</a> <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dykes</a> of Late <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> age are found throughout Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glacial_striation" title="Glacial striation">Glacial striation</a> and the presence of <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> <a href="/wiki/Glacial_erratic" title="Glacial erratic">erratics</a> that originated from the bed of the North Sea demonstrate the influence of ice action on the <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> of the islands. Boulder <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> is also abundant and <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> cover substantial areas.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The geology of Shetland is quite different. It is extremely complex, with numerous <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faults</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)#Describing_folds" title="Fold (geology)">fold axes</a>. These islands are the northern outpost of the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">Caledonian orogeny</a> and there are outcrops of <a href="/wiki/Lewisian_complex" title="Lewisian complex">Lewisian</a>, Dalriadan and Moine metamorphic rocks with similar histories to their equivalents on the Scottish mainland. There are also small Old Red Sandstone deposits and <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> intrusions. The most distinctive feature is the ultrabasic <a href="/wiki/Ophiolite" title="Ophiolite">ophiolite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a> on <a href="/wiki/Unst" title="Unst">Unst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fetlar" title="Fetlar">Fetlar</a>, which are remnants of the <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Ocean" title="Iapetus Ocean">Iapetus Ocean</a> floor.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Much of Shetland\'s economy depends on the oil-bearing sediments in the surrounding seas.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Geological evidence shows that at around 6100 BC a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Storegga_Slide" title="Storegga Slide">Storegga Slides</a> hit the Northern Isles, (as well as much of the east coast of Scotland), and may have created a wave of up to 25 metres (82&#160;ft) high in the <a href="/wiki/Firth" title="Firth">voes</a> of Shetland where modern populations are highest.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Orkney#Climate" title="Orkney">Orkney \xc2\xa7&#160;Climate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shetland#Climate" title="Shetland">Shetland \xc2\xa7&#160;Climate</a></div>\n<p>The Northern Isles have a cool, temperate climate that is remarkably mild and steady for such a northerly <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, due to the influence of the surrounding seas and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chalm129_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chalm129-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In Shetland average peak temperatures are 5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (41&#160;\xc2\xb0F) in February and 15&#160;\xc2\xb0C (59&#160;\xc2\xb0F) in August and temperatures over 21&#160;\xc2\xb0C (70&#160;\xc2\xb0F) are rare.<sup id="cite_ref-climatetemp_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-climatetemp-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SIC200505_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIC200505-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The frost-free period may be as little as 3 months.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The average annual rainfall is 982 millimetres (38.7&#160;in) in Orkney<sup id="cite_ref-Chalm129_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chalm129-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and 1,168 millimetres (46.0&#160;in) in Shetland.<sup id="cite_ref-SIC200505_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIC200505-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Winds are a key feature of the climate and even in summer there are almost constant breezes. In winter, there are frequent strong winds, with an average of 52 hours of gales being recorded annually in Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Burradale wind farm on Shetland, which operates with five <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> V47 660&#160;kW turbines, achieved a world record of 57.9% <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity</a> over the course of 2005 due to the persistent strong winds.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Snowfall is usually confined to the period November to February and seldom lies on the ground for more than a day. Less rain falls from April to August although no month receives less than an average of 50&#160;mm (2.0&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Chalm129_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chalm129-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-climatetemp_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-climatetemp-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SIC200505_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIC200505-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Annual bright sunshine averages 1082 hours in Shetland and overcast days are common.<sup id="cite_ref-climatetemp_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-climatetemp-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To tourists, one of the fascinations of the islands is their "nightless" summers. On the <a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">longest day</a> in Shetland there are over 19 hours of daylight and complete darkness is unknown. This long twilight is known in the Northern Isles as the "simmer dim". Winter nights are correspondingly long with less than six hours of daylight at <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">midwinter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  At this time of year the <a href="/wiki/Aurora_borealis" class="mw-redirect" title="Aurora borealis">aurora borealis</a> can occasionally be seen on the northern horizon during moderate auroral activity.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Orkney" title="Prehistoric Orkney">Prehistoric Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Shetland" title="Prehistoric Shetland">Prehistoric Shetland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Green fields lie to the right of a body of dark blue water. At the water&#39;s edge there is a tall, round, stone tower, possibly unroofed." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg/220px-Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg/330px-Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg/440px-Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mousa_Broch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_335305.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Mousa" title="Broch of Mousa">Broch of Mousa</a></div></div></div>\n<p>There are numerous important prehistoric remains in Orkney, especially from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period, four of which form the <a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Neolithic_Orkney" title="Heart of Neolithic Orkney">Heart of Neolithic Orkney</a> <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> that was inscribed in 1999: <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">Skara Brae</a>; <a href="/wiki/Maes_Howe" class="mw-redirect" title="Maes Howe">Maes Howe</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Stones_of_Stenness" class="mw-redirect" title="Stones of Stenness">Stones of Stenness</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Brodgar" title="Ring of Brodgar">Ring of Brodgar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Knap_of_Howar" title="Knap of Howar">Knap of Howar</a> Neolithic farmstead situated on the island of <a href="/wiki/Papa_Westray" title="Papa Westray">Papa Westray</a> is probably the oldest preserved house in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a>.  This structure was inhabited for 900 years from 3700 BC but was evidently built on the site of an even older settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Shetland is also extremely rich in physical remains of the prehistoric eras and there are over 5,000 archaeological sites all told.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Funzie_Girt" title="Funzie Girt">Funzie Girt</a> is a remarkable Neolithic dividing wall that ran for 4 kilometres (2.5&#160;mi) across the island of Fetlar,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> although the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> has provided the most outstanding archaeology on Shetland. Numerous <a href="/wiki/Broch" title="Broch">brochs</a> were erected  at that time of which the <a href="/wiki/Broch_of_Mousa" title="Broch of Mousa">Broch of Mousa</a> is the finest preserved example of these round towers.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>  In 2011 the collective site, "<a href="/wiki/The_Crucible_of_Iron_Age_Shetland" class="mw-redirect" title="The Crucible of Iron Age Shetland">The Crucible of Iron Age Shetland</a>" including Broch of Mousa, <a href="/wiki/Old_Scatness" title="Old Scatness">Old Scatness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jarlshof" title="Jarlshof">Jarlshof</a>  joined the UK\'s "Tentative List" of World Heritage Sites.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC311_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC311-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="History.2C_culture_and_politics"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="History,_culture_and_politics">History, culture and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History, culture and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pictish_times">Pictish times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pictish times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The culture that built the brochs is unknown, but by the late Iron Age the Northern Isles were  part of the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a> kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The main archaeological relics from these times are <a href="/wiki/Pictish_symbol_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Pictish symbol stones">symbol stones</a>. One of the best examples is located on the <a href="/wiki/Brough_of_Birsay" title="Brough of Birsay">Brough of Birsay</a>; it shows three warriors with spears and sword scabbards combined with traditional Pictish symbols.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-R39_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R39-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/St_Ninian%27s_Isle" title="St Ninian&#39;s Isle">St Ninian\'s Isle</a> Treasure was discovered in 1958. The silver bowls, jewellery and other pieces are believed to date from approximately 800 AD.  O\'Dell (1959) stated that "the treasure is the best survival of Scottish silver metalwork from the period" and that "the brooches show a variety of typical Pictish forms, with both animal-head and lobed geometrical forms of terminal".<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> probably arrived in Orkney in the 6th century and organised church authority emerged in the 8th century. The <a href="/wiki/Buckquoy_spindle-whorl" title="Buckquoy spindle-whorl">Buckquoy spindle-whorl</a> found at a Pictish site on <a href="/wiki/Birsay" title="Birsay">Birsay</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Ogham" title="Ogham">Ogham</a>\xe2\x80\x93inscribed artefact whose interpretation has caused controversy although it is now generally considered to be of Irish Christian origin.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse_era">Norse era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Norse era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_nicolai_arbo,_olaf_tryggvasson_king.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A group of warriors in medieval garb surround two men whose postures suggest they are about to embrace. The man on the right is taller, has long fair hair and wears a bright red tunic. The man on the left his balding with short grey hair and a white beard. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Orkneyinga_Saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkneyinga Saga">Orkneyinga Saga</a></i>, Vikings then made the islands the headquarters of  <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirate</a> expeditions carried out against Norway and the coasts of mainland Scotland. In response, Norwegian king <a href="/wiki/Harald_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Harald I of Norway">Harald H\xc3\xa5rfagre</a> ("Harald Fair Hair") annexed the Northern Isles in 875  and <a href="/wiki/Rognvald_Eysteinsson" title="Rognvald Eysteinsson">Rognvald Eysteinsson</a> received Orkney and Shetland from Harald as an <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Orkney" title="Earl of Orkney">earldom</a> as reparation for the death of his son in battle in Scotland. (Some scholars believe that this story is apocryphal and based on the later voyages of <a href="/wiki/Magnus_III_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus III of Norway">Magnus Barelegs</a>.)<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The islands were fully Christianised by <a href="/wiki/Olaf_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Olaf I of Norway">Olav Tryggvasson</a> in 995 when he stopped at <a href="/wiki/South_Walls" title="South Walls">South Walls</a> on his way from Ireland to Norway. The King summoned the <i><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">jarl</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_the_Stout" title="Sigurd the Stout">Sigurd the Stout</a> and said, "I order you and all your subjects to be baptised. If you refuse, I\'ll have you killed on the spot and I swear I will ravage every island with fire and steel." Unsurprisingly, Sigurd agreed and the islands became Christian at a stroke,<sup id="cite_ref-T69_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-T69-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> receiving their own <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Orkney" title="Bishop of Orkney">bishop</a> in the early 11th century.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Annexation_by_Scotland">Annexation by Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Annexation by Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A picture on a page in an old book. A man at left wears tights and a tunic with a lion rampant design and holds a sword and scepter. A woman at right wears a dress with an heraldic design bordered with ermine and carries a thistle in one hand and a scepter in the other. //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2a/James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg 2x" data-file-width="346" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_III_and_Margaret_of_Denmark.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/James_III_of_Scotland" title="James III of Scotland">James III</a> and <a Scotland</div></div></div>\n<p>In the 14th century  Orkney and Shetland remained a Norwegian province, but Scottish influence was growing. <a href="/wiki/Jon_Haraldsson" title="Jon Haraldsson">Jon Haraldsson</a>, who was murdered in <a href="/wiki/Thurso" title="Thurso">Thurso</a> in 1231, was the last of an unbroken line of Norse jarls,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> and thereafter the earls were Scots noblemen of the houses of <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Angus" title="Earl of Angus">Angus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_Sinclair,_Earl_of_Orkney" title="Henry I Sinclair, Earl of Orkney">St. Clair</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 1468 Shetland was <a href="/wiki/Pledge_(law)" title="Pledge (law)">pledged</a> by <a href="/wiki/Christian_I_of_Denmark" title="Christian I of Denmark">Christian I</a>, in his capacity as King of Norway, as security against the payment of the <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a> of his daughter <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Denmark,_Queen_of_Scotland" title="Margaret of Denmark, Queen of Scotland">Margaret</a>, betrothed to <a href="/wiki/James_III_of_Scotland" title="James III of Scotland">James III of Scotland</a>. As the money was never paid, the connection with the crown of Scotland became permanent. In 1470 <a href="/wiki/William_Sinclair,_1st_Earl_of_Caithness" title="William Sinclair, 1st Earl of Caithness">William Sinclair, 1st Earl of Caithness</a> ceded his title to James III and the following year the Northern Isles were directly annexed to Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="17th.2C_18th_and_19th_Centuries"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="17th,_18th_and_19th_Centuries">17th, 18th and 19th Centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 17th, 18th and 19th Centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From the early 15th century on the Shetlanders had sold their goods through the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> of German merchantmen.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> This trade with the North German towns lasted until the 1707 <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1707">Act of Union</a> when high salt duties prohibited the German merchants from trading with Shetland. Shetland then went into an economic depression as the Scottish and local traders were not as skilled in trading with salted fish. However, some local merchant-lairds took up where the German merchants had left off, and fitted out their own ships to export fish from Shetland to the Continent. For the independent farmer/fishermen of Shetland this had negative consequences, as they now had to fish for these merchant-lairds.<sup id="cite_ref-Visit.Shetland.org_history_page_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visit.Shetland.org_history_page-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A sepia-tone photograph of a 3-masted 19th century sailing vessel at sea with the shore in the foreground and dark hills beyond. The sun&#39;s rays cast light and shadows in the sky as they break through the clouds." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg/220px-Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg/330px-Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg/440px-Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2850" data-file-height="2055" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wind_Bound_Lerwick_around_1880.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Full-rigged ship <i>Maella</i>, of <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, in Bressay Sound circa 1922</div></div></div>\n<p>British rule came at a price for many ordinary people as well as traders. The Shetlanders\' nautical skills were sought by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>: some 3,000 served during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> from 1800 to 1815 and <a href="/wiki/Impressment" title="Impressment">press gangs</a> were rife. During this period 120 men were taken from <a href="/wiki/Fetlar" title="Fetlar">Fetlar</a> alone and only 20 of them returned home. By the late 19th century 90% of all Shetland was owned by just 32 people, and between 1861 and 1881 more than 8,000 Shetlanders emigrated.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sch16_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sch16-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> With the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Crofters%27_Holdings_(Scotland)_Act_1886" title="Crofters&#39; Holdings (Scotland) Act 1886">Crofters\' Act</a> in 1886 the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> prime minister <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Gladstone</a> emancipated crofters from the rule of the landlords. The Act enabled those who had effectively been landowners\' serfs to become owner-occupiers of their own small farms.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Orcadian experience was somewhat different. An influx of Scottish entrepreneurs helped to create a diverse and independent community that included farmers, fishermen and merchants that called themselves <i>comunitatis Orcadie</i> and who proved themselves increasing able to defend their rights against their feudal overlords.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> In the 17th century, Orcadians formed the overwhelming majority of employees of the <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson&#39;s Bay Company">Hudson\'s Bay Company</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The harsh climate of Orkney and the Orcadian reputation for sobriety and their boat-handling skills made them ideal candidates for the rigours of the Canadian north.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> During this period, burning <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> briefly became a mainstay of the islands\' economy.  For example, on <a href="/wiki/Shapinsay" title="Shapinsay">Shapinsay</a> over 3,048 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> (3,000 <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a>) of burned seaweed were produced per annum to make <a href="/wiki/Soda_ash" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda ash">soda ash</a>, bringing in \xc2\xa320,000 to the local economy.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Agricultural improvements beginning in the 17th century coincided with the <a href="/wiki/Enclosure_of_the_commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclosure of the commons">enclosure of the commons</a> and in the Victorian era the emergence of large and well-managed farms using a five-shift <a href="/wiki/Rotation_system" title="Rotation system">rotation system</a> and producing high quality beef cattle.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> There is little evidence of an Orcadian fishing fleet until the 19th century but it grew rapidly and 700 boats were involved by the 1840s with <a href="/wiki/Stronsay" title="Stronsay">Stronsay</a> and then later <a href="/wiki/Stromness" title="Stromness">Stromness</a> becoming leading centres of development.<sup id="cite_ref-Coull_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coull-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;Note 1&#93;</a></sup> Many Orcadian seamen became involved in <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> waters during the 19th century, although the boats were generally based elsewhere in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Wars">World Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: World Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG" class="image"><img alt="A small chapel sits in green fields under a blue sky. A body of water lies to the left. The front of the building is painted red and white and is decorated with colonnades and a small bell tower. By contrast the main part of the building is painted grey and has a curved exterior reminiscent of a Nissen hut." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG/220px-Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG/330px-Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG/440px-Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Italian_Chapel_orkney.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Italian_Chapel" title="Italian Chapel">Italian Chapel</a> on <a href="/wiki/Lamb_Holm" title="Lamb Holm">Lamb Holm</a>, built by <a href="/wiki/POW" class="mw-redirect" title="POW">POWs</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Orkney was the site of a naval base at <a href="/wiki/Scapa_Flow" title="Scapa Flow">Scapa Flow</a>, which played a major role in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Armistice" title="Armistice">Armistice</a> in 1918, the <a href="/wiki/German_High_Seas_Fleet" class="mw-redirect" title="German High Seas Fleet">German High Seas Fleet</a> was transferred in its entirety to Scapa Flow while a decision was to be made on its future; however, the German sailors opened the sea-cocks and <a href="/wiki/Scuttling_of_the_German_fleet_in_Scapa_Flow" title="Scuttling of the German fleet in Scapa Flow">scuttled all the ships</a>. During World War I the 10th Cruiser Squadron was stationed at Swarbacks Minn in Shetland and during a single year from March 1917 more than 4,500 ships sailed from Lerwick as part of an escorted convoy system. In total, Shetland lost more than 500 men, a higher proportion than any other part of Britain, and there were waves of emigration in the 1920s and 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-Sch16_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sch16-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-N94_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N94-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One month into <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the Royal Navy battleship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_Oak_(08)" title="HMS Royal Oak (08)">HMS <i>Royal Oak</i></a> was sunk by a German <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> in Scapa Flow. As a result <a href="/wiki/Churchill_Barriers" title="Churchill Barriers">barriers</a> were built to close most of the access channels; these had the advantage of creating causeways enabling travellers to go from island to island by road instead of being obliged to rely on ferries. The causeways were constructed by Italian prisoners of war, who also constructed the ornate <a href="/wiki/Italian_Chapel" title="Italian Chapel">Italian Chapel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> The Scapa Flow base was run down after the war, eventually closing in 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-Thom439_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thom439-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During World War II a Norwegian naval unit nicknamed the "<a href="/wiki/Shetland_bus" title="Shetland bus">Shetland Bus</a>" was established by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Operations_Executive" title="Special Operations Executive">Special Operations Executive</a> in the autumn of 1940 with a base first at <a href="/wiki/Lunna_House" title="Lunna House">Lunna</a> and later in <a href="/wiki/Scalloway" title="Scalloway">Scalloway</a> to conduct operations around the coast of Norway. About 30 fishing vessels used by Norwegian refugees were gathered and the Shetland Bus conducted covert operations, carrying intelligence agents, refugees, instructors for the resistance, and military supplies. It made over 200 trips across the sea with <a href="/wiki/Leif_Larsen" title="Leif Larsen">Leif Larsen</a>, the most highly decorated <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">allied</a> naval officer of the war, making 52 of them.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-scotsatwar_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotsatwar-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The problem of a declining population was significant in the post-war years, although in the last decades of the 20th century there was a recovery and life in the islands focused on growing prosperity and the emergence of a relatively classless society.<sup id="cite_ref-Thom439_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thom439-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Due to their history, the islands have a Norse, rather than a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Gaelic</a> flavour, and have historic links with the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. The similarities of both geography and history are matched by some elements of the current political process. Both Orkney and Shetland are represented in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> as constituting the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_and_Shetland_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Orkney and Shetland (UK Parliament constituency)">Orkney and Shetland</a> <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_constituencies" title="United Kingdom constituencies">constituency</a>, which elects one <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP), the current incumbent being <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Carmichael" title="Alistair Carmichael">Alistair Carmichael</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>  Both are also within the <a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_(Scottish_Parliament_region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Highlands and Islands (Scottish Parliament region)">Highlands and Islands</a> <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_constituencies_and_regions" title="Scottish Parliament constituencies and regions">electoral region</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>.\n</p><p>However there are also two separate constituencies that elect one <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Member of the Scottish Parliament">Member of the Scottish Parliament</a> each for Orkney and Shetland by the <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">first past the post</a> system.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Orkney and Shetland also have separate local Councils which are dominated by <a href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent (politician)">independents</a>, that is they are not members of a political party.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Orkney Movement, a political party that supported devolution for Orkney from the rest of Scotland, contested the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1987" title="United Kingdom general election, 1987">1987 general election</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_and_Shetland_Movement" title="Orkney and Shetland Movement">Orkney and Shetland Movement</a> (a coalition of the Orkney movement and its equivalent for Shetland). Their candidate, John Goodlad, came 4th with 3,095 votes, 14.5% of those cast, but the experiment has not been repeated.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a harbour</div></div></div>\n<p>Ferry services link Orkney and Shetland to the rest of Scotland, the main routes being <a href="/wiki/Scrabster" title="Scrabster">Scrabster harbour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thurso" title="Thurso">Thurso</a> to Stromness and <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> to Lerwick, both operated by <a href="/wiki/NorthLink_Ferries" title="NorthLink Ferries">NorthLink Ferries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-visork_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visork-81">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Inter-island ferry services are operated by <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Ferries" title="Orkney Ferries">Orkney Ferries</a> and <a href="/wiki/SIC_Ferries" title="SIC Ferries">SIC Ferries</a>, which are operated by the respective <a href="/wiki/Local_authorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Local authorities">local authorities</a> and Northlink also run a Lerwick to Kirkwall service.<sup id="cite_ref-visork_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visork-81">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> The archipelago is exposed to wind and tide, and there are numerous sites of wrecked ships. <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">Lighthouses</a> are sited as an aid to navigation at various locations.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A small dark-blue twin prop plane sits on tarmac surrounded by grass under blue skies. In the foreground an individual wearing a uniform that is similar in colour to the plane pulls a full baggage trolley towards it." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg/220px-Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg/330px-Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg/440px-Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Papa_Westray_Airport.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Britten-Norman_Islander" class="mw-redirect" title="Britten-Norman Islander">BN2 Islander</a> being loaded with luggage at <a href="/wiki/Papa_Westray_Airport" title="Papa Westray Airport">Papa Westray Airport</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The main airport in Orkney is at <a href="/wiki/Kirkwall_Airport" title="Kirkwall Airport">Kirkwall</a>, operated by <a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_Airports_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Highlands and Islands Airports Limited">Highland and Islands Airports</a>. <a href="/wiki/Loganair" title="Loganair">Loganair</a>, a franchise of <a href="/wiki/Flybe" title="Flybe">Flybe</a>, provides services to the Scottish mainland (<a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Airport" title="Aberdeen Airport">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Airport" title="Edinburgh Airport">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Airport" title="Glasgow Airport">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inverness_Airport" title="Inverness Airport">Inverness</a>), as well as to <a href="/wiki/Sumburgh_Airport" title="Sumburgh Airport">Sumburgh Airport</a> in Shetland. Similar services fly from Sumburgh to the Scottish mainland.<sup id="cite_ref-visork_81-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visork-81">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Inter-Island flights are available from Kirkwall to several Orkney islands and from the Shetland Mainland to most of the inhabited islands including those from <a href="/wiki/Tingwall_Airport" title="Tingwall Airport">Tingwall Airport</a>.  There are frequent charter flights from Aberdeen to <a href="/wiki/Scatsta" class="mw-redirect" title="Scatsta">Scatsta</a> near <a href="/wiki/Sullom_Voe_Terminal" title="Sullom Voe Terminal">Sullom Voe</a>, which are used to transport oilfield workers and this small terminal has the fifth largest number of international passengers in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The scheduled air service between <a href="/wiki/Westray_Airport" title="Westray Airport">Westray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Papa_Westray_Airport" title="Papa Westray Airport">Papa Westray</a> is reputedly the shortest in the world at two minutes\' duration.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The very different geologies of the  two archipelagos have resulted in dissimilar local economies. In Shetland, the main revenue producers are agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Scotland" title="Renewable energy in Scotland">renewable energy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum industry</a> (offshore <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> production), the creative industries and tourism.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Oil and gas was first landed at <a href="/wiki/Sullom_Voe" title="Sullom Voe">Sullom Voe</a> in 1978, and it has subsequently become one of the largest oil terminals in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Taxes from the oil have increased public sector spending in Shetland on social welfare, art, sport, environmental measures and financial development. Three quarters of the islands\' work force is employed in the service sector<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands_Council" title="Shetland Islands Council">Shetland Islands Council</a> alone accounted for 27.9% of output in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Fishing remains central to the islands\' economy today, with the total catch being 75,767 tonnes (74,570 long tons; 83,519 short tons) in 2009, valued at over \xc2\xa373.2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-SIC16_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIC16-93">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg" class="image"><img alt="An oil platform constructed in two main parts sits in a calm blue sea. The larger section at right has a helideck, the smaller at left is flaring gas from two stacks." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg/220px-Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg/330px-Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg/440px-Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="759" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beryl_alpha_from_air.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a>\'s Beryl alpha oil platform in the <a href="/wiki/East_Shetland_Basin" title="East Shetland Basin">East Shetland Basin</a></div></div></div>\n<p>By contrast, fishing has declined in Orkney since the 19th century and the impact of the oil industry has been much less significant.  However, the soil of Orkney is generally very fertile and most of the land is taken up by farms, agriculture being by far the most important sector of the economy and providing employment for a quarter of the workforce.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> More than 90% of agricultural land is used for grazing for sheep and cattle, with cereal production utilising about 4% (4,200 hectares (10,000 acres)), although woodland occupies only 134 hectares (330 acres).<sup id="cite_ref-OER_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OER-95">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Orkney and Shetland have significant <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Scotland" title="Wind power in Scotland">wind</a> and marine energy resources, and <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Scotland" title="Renewable energy in Scotland">renewable energy</a> has recently come into prominence. The <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">European Marine Energy Centre</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>-backed research facility that has installed a wave testing system at Billia Croo on the Orkney Mainland and a tidal power testing station on the island of <a href="/wiki/Eday" title="Eday">Eday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> This has been described as "the first of its kind in the world set up to provide developers of wave and tidal energy devices with a purpose-built performance testing facility."<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> Billia Croo also houses an experimental underwater <a href="/wiki/Data_center" title="Data center">data center</a> run by <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Northern Isles have a rich folklore. For example, there are many Orcadian tales concerning <a href="/wiki/Trow_(folklore)" title="Trow (folklore)">trows</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Troll" title="Troll">troll</a> that draws on the islands\' Scandinavian connections.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Local customs in the past included marriage ceremonies at the Odin Stone that forms part of the Stones of Stenness.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>\nThe best known literary figures from modern Orkney are the poet <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Muir" title="Edwin Muir">Edwin Muir</a>, the poet and novelist <a href="/wiki/George_Mackay_Brown" title="George Mackay Brown">George Mackay Brown</a> and the novelist <a href="/wiki/Eric_Linklater" title="Eric Linklater">Eric Linklater</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Shetland has a strong tradition of local music. <a href="/wiki/The_Forty_Fiddlers" class="mw-redirect" title="The Forty Fiddlers">The Forty Fiddlers</a> was formed in the 1950s to promote the traditional fiddle style, which is a vibrant part of local culture today.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>  Notable exponents of Shetland folk music include <a href="/wiki/Aly_Bain" title="Aly Bain">Aly Bain</a> and the late <a href="/wiki/Tom_Anderson_(fiddler)" title="Tom Anderson (fiddler)">Tom Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peerie_Willie_Johnson" title="Peerie Willie Johnson">Peerie Willie Johnson</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fraser_(singer)" title="Thomas Fraser (singer)">Thomas Fraser</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country musician</a> who never released a commercial recording during his life, but whose work has become popular more than 20 years after his untimely death in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn language</a> formerly spoken in the islands, a descendant of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> of the Vikings, became extinct in the 18th or 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> The local dialects of the <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots language</a>, collectively known as <a href="/wiki/Insular_Scots" title="Insular Scots">Insular Scots</a>, are highly distinctive and retain strong Norn influences.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Island_names">Island names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Island names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_island_names" title="Scottish island names">etymology of the island names</a> is dominated by Norse influence. There follows a listing of the derivation of all the inhabited islands in the Northern Isles.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shetland">Shetland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Shetland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The oldest version of the modern name Shetland is <i>Hetlandensis</i> recorded in 1190 becoming <i>Hetland</i> in 1431 after various intermediate transformations.<sup id="cite_ref-G21_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G21-107">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-S9_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S9-108">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> This then became <i>Hjaltland</i> in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-G22_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G22-109">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> As Shetlandic <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn</a> was gradually replaced by <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> <i>Hjaltland</i> became <i><i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">\xc8\x9cetland</i></i>. When use of the letter <a href="/wiki/Yogh" title="Yogh">yogh</a> was discontinued, it was often replaced by the similar-looking letter <a href="/wiki/Z" title="Z">z</a>, hence <i>Zetland</i>, the mis<a href="/wiki/Pronounce" class="mw-redirect" title="Pronounce">pronounced</a> form used to describe the pre-1975 <a href="/wiki/Traditional_counties_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional counties of Scotland">county council</a>. However the earlier name is <i>Innse Chat</i> \xe2\x80\x93 the island of the cats (or the cat tribe) as referred to in <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">early Irish</a> literature and it is just possible that this forms part of the Norse name.<sup id="cite_ref-G21_107-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G21-107">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-S9_108-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S9-108">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> The Cat tribe also occupied parts of the northern Scottish mainland \xe2\x80\x93 hence the name of <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a> via the Norse <i>Katanes</i> ("headland of the cat"), and the Gaelic name for <a href="/wiki/Sutherland" title="Sutherland">Sutherland</a>, <i>Cataibh</i>, meaning "among the Cats".<sup id="cite_ref-G9_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G9-110">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Watson_1994_p._30_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson_1994_p._30-111">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The location of "<a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">Thule</a>", first mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Massilia" class="mw-redirect" title="Massilia">Massilia</a> when he visited Britain sometime between 322 and 285 BC<sup id="cite_ref-Breeze_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Breeze-112">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> is not known for certain. When <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> mentioned it in AD 98 it is clear he was referring to Shetland.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Isles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Orkney">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Pytheas described <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> as being triangular in shape, with a northern tip called <i>Orcas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Breeze_112-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Breeze-112">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> This may have referred to <a href="/wiki/Dunnet_Head" title="Dunnet Head">Dunnet Head</a>, from which Orkney is visible.<sup id="cite_ref-jarehro_127-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jarehro-127">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup> Writing in the 1st century AD, the Roman geographer <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a> called the Orkney islands <i>Orcades</i>, as did <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> in AD 98<sup id="cite_ref-jarehro_127-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jarehro-127">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup> "Orc" is usually interpreted as a Pictish tribal name meaning "young pig" or "young <a href="/wiki/Boar" class="mw-redirect" title="Boar">boar</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-W116_129-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W116-129">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> The old <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish Gaelic</a> name for the islands was <i>Insi Orc</i> ("island of the pigs").<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jaroo_131-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jaroo-131">&#91;130&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">&#91;Note 2&#93;</a></sup> The ogham script on the Buckquoy spindle-whorl is also cited as evidence for the pre-Norse existence of <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> in Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup> The Pictish association with Orkney is leant weight by the Norse name for the <a href="/wiki/Pentland_Firth" title="Pentland Firth">Pentland Firth</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <i>Pettaland-fj\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0r</i> i.e "Pictland Firth.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_1994_p._30_111-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson_1994_p._30-111">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Norse retained the earlier root but changed the meaning, providing the only definite example of an adaption of a pre-Norse place name in the Northern Isles. The islands became <i>Orkneyar</i> meaning "seal islands".<sup id="cite_ref-G8_135-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G8-135">&#91;133&#93;</a></sup> An alternative name for Orkney is recorded in 1300\xe2\x80\x94Hrossey, meaning "horse isle" and this may also contain a Pictish element of <i>ros</i> meaning "moor" or "plain".<sup id="cite_ref-G21_107-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G21-107">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unlike most of the larger Orkney islands, the derivation of the name "Shapinsay" is not obvious. The final \'ay\' is from the Old Norse for <i>island</i>, but the first two syllables are more difficult to interpret. Haswell-Smith (2004) suggests the root may be <i>hjalpandis-\xc3\xb8y</i> (helpful island) due to the presence of a good harbour, although anchorages are plentiful in the archipelago.<sup id="cite_ref-H364_136-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H364-136">&#91;134&#93;</a></sup> The first written record dates from 1375 in a reference to <i>Scalpandisay</i>, which may suggest a derivation from "judge\'s island". Another suggestion is "Hyalpandi\'s island", although no one of that name is known to have been associated with Shapinsay.<sup id="cite_ref-Orkneyjar_137-0" class="reference"><a The Norse often gave animal names to islands and these have been transferred into English in for example, the <a href="/wiki/Calf_of_Flotta" title="Calf of Flotta">Calf of Flotta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horse_of_Copinsay" title="Horse of Copinsay">Horse of Copinsay</a>. <a href="/wiki/Brother_Isle" title="Brother Isle">Brother Isle</a> is an anglicisation of the Norse <i>brei\xc3\xb0are\xc3\xb8y</i> meaning "broad beach island".<sup id="cite_ref-157" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-157">&#91;155&#93;</a></sup> The Norse <i>holmr</i>, meaning  "a small islet" has become "<a href="/wiki/Holm_(island)" title="Holm (island)">Holm</a>" in English and there are numerous examples of this use including <a href="/wiki/Corn_Holm" title="Corn Holm">Corn Holm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thieves_Holm" title="Thieves Holm">Thieves Holm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_Holm,_Yell_Sound" title="Little Holm, Yell Sound">Little Holm</a>. "Muckle" meaning large or big is one of few <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> words in the island names of the <i>Nordreyar</i> and appears in <a href="/wiki/Muckle_Roe" title="Muckle Roe">Muckle Roe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muckle_Flugga" title="Muckle Flugga">Muckle Flugga</a> in Shetland and <a href="/wiki/Muckle_Green_Holm" title="Muckle Green Holm">Muckle Green Holm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muckle_Skerry" title="Muckle Skerry">Muckle Skerry</a> in Orkney. Many small islets and skerries have Scots or <a href="/wiki/Insular_Scots" title="Insular Scots">Insular Scots</a> names such as Da Skerries o da Rokness and Da Buddle Stane  in Shetland, and Kirk Rocks in Orkney.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
