ball</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Shinty</b> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <i lang="gd">camanachd, iomain</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Team_sport" title="Team sport">team game</a> played with sticks and a ball. Shinty is now played mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>, and amongst Highland migrants to the big cities of Scotland, but it was formerly more widespread in Scotland,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and was even played for a considerable time in England<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and other areas in the world where Scottish Highlanders migrated.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While comparisons are often made with <a href="/wiki/Field_hockey" title="Field hockey">field hockey</a>, the two games have several important differences. In shinty, a player is allowed to play the ball in the air and is allowed to use both sides of the stick, called a <i>caman</i>, which is wooden and slanted on both sides. The stick may also be used to block and to tackle, although a player may not come down on an opponent\'s stick, a practice called hacking. Players may also tackle using the body as long as it is shoulder-to-shoulder.\n</p><p>The game was derived from the same root as the <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> game of <a href="/wiki/Hurling" title="Hurling">hurling</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a> game of <a href="/wiki/Bando_(sport)" title="Bando (sport)">bando</a>, but has developed unique rules and features. These rules are governed by the <a href="/wiki/Camanachd_Association" title="Camanachd Association">Camanachd Association</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Composite_rules_shinty%E2%80%93hurling" title="Composite rules shinty\xe2\x80\x93hurling">composite rules shinty\xe2\x80\x93hurling</a> game has been developed, which allows Scotland and Ireland to play annual international matches.\n</p><p>Another sport with common ancestry is <a href="/wiki/Bandy" title="Bandy">bandy</a>, which is played on ice. In fact, in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> the name for bandy is "ice shinty" (camanachd-deighe) and once upon a time bandy and shinty (and shinney) could be used interchangeably in the English language.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" derived from the historic game common to both peoples. Shinty/Hurling appears prominently in the legend of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%BAchulainn" class="mw-redirect" title="C\xc3\xbachulainn">C\xc3\xbachulainn</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">Celtic mythology</a> hero.<sup id="cite_ref-uscamanachd.org_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uscamanachd.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A similar game was played on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Cammag" title="Cammag">cammag</a>, a name cognate with <a href="/wiki/Camanachd" class="mw-redirect" title="Camanachd">camanachd</a>. The old form of hurling played in the northern half of Ireland, called "commons", resembled shinty more closely than the standardised form of hurling of today. Like shinty, it was commonly known as cam\xc3\xa1nacht and was traditionally played in winter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The origins of the name shinty are uncertain. There is a theory that the name was derived from the cries used in the game; <i>shin ye</i>, <i>shin you</i> and <i>shin t\'ye</i>, other dialect names were <i>shinnins</i>, <i>shinnack</i> and <i>shinnup</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or as <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Dan_MacLennan" title="Hugh Dan MacLennan">Hugh Dan MacLennan</a> proposes from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> <i>s\xc3\xacnteag</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-uscamanachd.org_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uscamanachd.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, there was never one all encompassing name for the game, as it held different names from glen to glen, including cluich-bhall (<i>play-ball</i> in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>) and in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Scottish Lowlands</a>, where it was formerly referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Hailes_(ball_game)" title="Hailes (ball game)">Hailes</a></i>, <i>common</i>/<i>cammon</i> (<i>caman</i>), <i>cammock</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> <i>camag</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Knotty" title="Knotty">knotty</a></i> and various other names, as well as the terms still used to refer to it in modern Gaelic, <i>camanachd</i> or <i>iomain</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Shinty was once a popular game in lowland Scotland, as shown by its name Shintie, a term which took that form around 1700, displacing the earlier Shinnie - of which there is a written record about 100 years earlier.  Shinnie may also derive from "shin" in English, with the affix "ie", a common termination to the name of many games in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Game">Game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_area">Playing area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Playing area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The objective of the game is to play a small ball into a goal, or "hail", erected at the ends of a 140 to 170-yard-long by 70 to 80-yard-wide pitch.<sup id="cite_ref-shinty.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shinty.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The game is traditionally played on grass, although as of 2009 the sport may be played on artificial turf.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The pitch also has marks indicating a 10 yard area around the goals, the penalty and centre spots (along with their associated arcs/circles of 5 yards or 5 metres radius), and corner arcs at the corners of the rectangular pitch of 2 yards or 2 metres radius.<sup id="cite_ref-shinty.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shinty.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The goals, at opposite ends of the field, measure 12 feet wide and 10 feet high and a net is affixed to catch the ball when a goal is scored.<sup id="cite_ref-shinty.com_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shinty.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div With the permitted circumference between seven and a half and eight inches and weight between two and a half and three ounces.<sup id="cite_ref-shinty.com_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shinty.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The ball is usually white, but there is no statutory colour, black being a common colour for Kyles Athletic and fluorescent balls now being available.\n</p><p>Plastic balls or soft balls are often used in youth competitions such as the variant, "First Shinty".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stick">Stick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The ball is played using a <i>caman</i>, which is a stick of about <span class="frac nowrap">3<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;ft (1.1&#160;m) long with two slanted faces and the head, which is wedge shaped (a triangular cross section),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> must be able to pass through a ring two and a half inches in diameter.<sup id="cite_ref-shinty.com_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shinty.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the Irish <i>cam\xc3\xa1n</i>, it has no blade. The <i>caman</i> is traditionally made of wood, traditionally <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">ash</a> but now more commonly <a href="/wiki/Hickory" title="Hickory">hickory</a>, and must not have any plate or metal attached to it. The <i>caman</i> would be made from any piece of wood with a hook in it, hence <i>caman</i>, from the Scottish and Irish Gaelic word, <i>cam</i> meaning bent or crooked. It can also be called a stick or club. The slant of the face will vary according to the position that the stick is used for. The stick may also be used to block and to tackle, although a player may not bring their stick down on an opponent\'s stick, which is defined as hacking. A player may tackle an opponent using the body as long as it is shoulder-to-shoulder as in <a href="/wiki/Association_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="Association Football">Association Football (soccer)</a>.\n</p><p>A player may only stop the ball with the stick, the chest, two feet together or one foot on the ground. Only the goalkeeper may use his hands, but only with an open palm since he is not allowed to catch it. Playing the ball with the head constitutes a foul whether intentional or not, as it is considered dangerous play. Other examples of dangerous play, which will be penalised, are a player, while grounded, playing the ball, or a player recklessly swinging the caman in the air in a way which might endanger another player.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shinty_Style.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Shinty_Style.jpg/200px-Shinty_Style.jpg" width="200" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Shinty_Style.jpg/300px-Shinty_Style.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Shinty_Style.jpg/400px-Shinty_Style.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1252" data-file-height="1350" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shinty_Style.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A player doing keepy-uppy.</div></div></div><p>Fouls are penalised by a free-hit, which is indirect unless the foul is committed in the penalty area, commonly referred to as "The D". This results in a penalty hit from 20 yards. <sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A ball played by a team over the opposing bye line results in a goal hit from the edge of the D, while a ball played by a team over their own line results in a corner. A ball hit over the sideline results in a shy: a shinty shy involves the taker tossing the ball above his head and hitting the ball with the shaft of the caman, and the ball must be directly overhead when struck.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scoring">Scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The winner of a game is the team that scores the most goals. A team scores a goal "when the whole of the ball has passed over the goal-line and under the cross-bar". A goal can only be scored with the caman; there is no goal when the ball "has been kicked, carried or propelled by hand or arm by a player of the attacking side." A goal can not be scored directly from a free-hit.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Team_size">Team size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Team size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Teams consist of 12 players (men) or 10 players (women), including a goalkeeper. A match is played over two halves of 45 minutes. With the exception of the goalkeeper, no player is allowed to play the ball with his hands. There are also variants with smaller sides, with some adjustments in the field size and duration of play.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Substitutions">Substitutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Substitutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As with sports such as <a href="/wiki/Football" title="Football">football</a>, shinty originally did not have substitutes. These were introduced in the 1960s, progressively expanding to allow a maximum of three substitutions per game. As of 2011, a rule change allowed for rolling substitutions to be made at senior level.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In common with many sports, it became formalised in the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Era">Victorian Era</a> and the first organised clubs were established in cities such as Glasgow and London where there were thousands of Gaels resident.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1887, a historic game was played between <a href="/wiki/Glenurquhart_Shinty_Club" title="Glenurquhart Shinty Club">Glenurquhart Shinty Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strathglass_Shinty_Club" title="Strathglass Shinty Club">Strathglass Shinty Club</a> in <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a>. This game was attended by thousands of people and was a major milestone in developing a set of common rules.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This fixture was to be repeated on 12 January 2007 in Inverness as the opening centrepiece of the <a href="/wiki/Highland_2007" title="Highland 2007">Highland 2007</a> celebrations in Scotland, but was postponed due to a waterlogged pitch.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The modern sport is governed by the <a href="/wiki/Camanachd_Association" title="Camanachd Association">Camanachd Association</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scots_Gaelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots Gaelic">Scots Gaelic</a>: Comann na Camanachd). The association came into being in the late <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> in as a means of formulating common rules to unite the various different codes and rules which differed between neighbouring glens. Its first meeting was held in <a href="/wiki/Kingussie" title="Kingussie">Kingussie</a> in 1893.\n</p><p>The Camanachd Association maintained its initial structure for much of its first century. The \'Future of Shinty\' Report published in 1981 led to a compete restructuring of the way in which shinty was organised and managed. That led to the move away from a dependence on volunteers to govern the sport, to the Association\'s first salaried employees.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a Clubs compete in various competitions, both cup and league, on a national and also North/South basis. While the top <a href="/wiki/Premier_Division_(shinty)" title="Premier Division (shinty)">Premier Division</a> has been played on a Scotland-wide basis since 1996, the lower leagues are based on geography. Many clubs run second teams that also compete in these leagues against clubs with only one senior side.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season">Season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Shinty was traditionally played through the <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>, based around the tradition of the "Iomain Challainn", where <a href="/wiki/New_Year" title="New Year">New Year</a> was marked by a game between neighbouring parishes. The <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> was left free for seasonal work and friendly tournaments. The Winter season always ran over, however, and many teams would find themselves finishing the previous season only weeks before the next one would start.\n</p><p>In 2003, shinty clubs voted for a trial period of two years of a summer season from March to October, with a view to moving permanently to summer shinty if the experiment was judged to be a success. Despite opposition from the "Big Two", <a href="/wiki/Kingussie" title="Kingussie">Kingussie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newtonmore_(Shinty)" class="mw-redirect" title="Newtonmore (Shinty)">Newtonmore</a>, and other small groups in the game, an <a href="/wiki/Extraordinary_General_Meeting" class="mw-redirect" title="Extraordinary General Meeting">EGM</a> in November 2005 voted by an overwhelming majority (well over the required two thirds) to make summer shinty the basis upon which the game would proceed.<sup id="cite_ref-timesonline.co.uk_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesonline.co.uk-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There have been teething problems since the move to summer shinty, with a couple of teams being culpable for the season running over into November and December. Season 2010 saw the league season finished by the first weekend in October, almost on schedule.\n</p><p>Shinty does still get played during the winter, in <a href="/wiki/University_Shinty" class="mw-redirect" title="University Shinty">University Shinty</a> which teams compete for the second most valuable trophy in Scottish sport - the Littlejohn Vase - and in New Year fixtures, the most prestigious of which is the <a href="/wiki/Lovat_Cup" title="Lovat Cup">Lovat Cup</a>, played between Beauly and Lovat.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leagues">Leagues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Leagues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Shinty_league_system" title="Shinty league system">Shinty league system</a></i>\n</p><p>League shinty was originally organised on a regional basis, with distinct competitions for the North District and at one time, two separate leagues for Argyll (the Dunn League) and the Southern League, for clubs in Glasgow and Edinburgh and the surrounding areas. Over time, there have been moves to amalgamate leagues and, since the 1980s, a push for national competition at the highest levels. In the modern era of league shinty, <a href="/wiki/Kingussie_(Shinty)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingussie (Shinty)">Kingussie</a> have been unsurpassed in their domination of the sport; according to the <a href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinness Book of Records">Guinness Book of Records</a> 2005, Kingussie is world sport\'s most successful sporting team of all time,<sup id="cite_ref-timesonline.co.uk_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesonline.co.uk-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> winning 20 consecutive league championships and going 4 years without losing a single fixture in the early 1990s. This unmatched run of dominance was ended on 2 September 2006 by rivals <a href="/wiki/Newtonmore_(Shinty)" class="mw-redirect" title="Newtonmore (Shinty)">Newtonmore</a>, who defeated <a href="/wiki/Oban_Camanachd" title="Oban Camanachd">Oban Camanachd</a> 2-0 to ensure that Kingussie could not catch the team at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Premier_Division_(shinty)" title="Premier Division (shinty)">Premier Division</a>. However, Newtonmore were unable to replace their neighbours as champions, as the first post-Kingussie champions were confirmed as <a href="/wiki/Fort_William_Shinty_Club" title="Fort William Shinty Club">Fort William</a>, who sealed the title on 30 September 2006 having won their games in hand over Newtonmore. Kingussie regained the title in 2007. Since 2010, Newtonmore has been the dominant league force.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cup">Cup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cup shinty has always been seen as being more important than league shinty and the premier national competition remains the Scottish Cup or the Camanachd Association Challenge Cup, the <a href="/wiki/Camanachd_Cup" title="Camanachd Cup">Camanachd Cup</a> for short. Until 1983 the competition was designed to ensure the final was between the North and South.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Macaulay_Cup" title="Macaulay Cup">Macaulay Cup</a> still preserves a guaranteed North/South Final. There are national equivalents for the Camanachd Cup for intermediate and junior teams. There are regional cups for both senior and junior teams; the <a href="/wiki/MacTavish_Cup" title="MacTavish Cup">MacTavish Cup</a> is the senior cup for the North and the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Celtic_Society_Cup" title="Glasgow Celtic Society Cup">Glasgow Celtic Society Cup</a> is the one for the South.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shinty_and_hurling_internationals">Shinty and hurling internationals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Shinty and hurling internationals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In recognition of shinty\'s shared roots with hurling, an annual international between the two codes from <a href="/wiki/Scotland_national_shinty_team" title="Scotland national shinty team">Scotland</a> and Ireland is played on a home and away basis using <a href="/wiki/Composite_rules_Shinty/Hurling" class="mw-redirect" title="Composite rules Shinty/Hurling">composite rules</a>. In recent years, the Irish have had the upper hand, but the Scots won the fixture narrowly in 2005 and again in 2006, this time at <a href="/wiki/Croke_Park" title="Croke Park">Croke Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, albeit with the Irish fielding weaker players from the second tier <a href="/wiki/Christy_Ring_Cup" title="Christy Ring Cup">Christy Ring Cup</a>. Scotland made it four in a row when they won in 2008.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_Scotland">Outside Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Outside Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/London_Camanachd" title="London Camanachd">London Camanachd</a> is a shinty club first established in the Victorian era. They do not play league matches but sometimes compete in the Bullough Cup. They have historically been attached to the South District. They went into abeyance in 1992, but were reconstituted in 2005. They played the first officially recognised shinty match outside Scotland in 80 years on 22 July 2006 against <a href="/wiki/The_Highlanders_Shinty_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="The Highlanders Shinty Club">the Highlanders</a>.  They compete annually in the English Shinty Championships against rivals Cornwall as well as playing shinty-hurling matches and organising sporadic friendlies against visiting teams.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On 28 December 2010 Ireland held its first dedicated shinty match in Westmeath, with players who have played the <a href="/wiki/Compromise_rules_Shinty/Hurling" class="mw-redirect" title="Compromise rules Shinty/Hurling">Compromise rules Shinty/Hurling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Shinty_Club" title="Cornwall Shinty Club">Cornwall Shinty Club</a> was established in 2012 playing their first game on 21 April 2012 against London; the match finished a draw. They also entered the St Andrew\'s Sixes tournament in 2012. Following this, in December 2012, two more Cornish clubs were created; the first being the Combined Universities of Cornwall and the second being Mabe. These two teams put forward their best players to play for the Cornwall Shinty Club.\n</p><p>There was a team in <a href="/wiki/Northallerton" title="Northallerton">Northallerton</a> in the 1970s, which competed in six-a-sides; and on 1 August 2012 a re-vamped Northallerton Shinty Club was formed. The club is hoping to draw in a few former players, but wants to focus on raising awareness of the game in Yorkshire and bringing new local players into the game.\n</p><p>Shinty was previously played widely in England in the 19th century and early 20th century, with teams such as London Scots, Bolton Caledonian and Cottonopolis; <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Forest_F.C." title="Nottingham Forest F.C.">Nottingham Forest F.C.</a> was established by shinty players.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2012 London has hosted the annual "London Shinty Festival"  which has been attended by Cornwall, London, Oxford, St Andrews university ladies team, and the Scots.  It is an open tournament held in late September after the Shinty season is finished to allow any traveling teams the opportunity to attend,\n</p><p>Since 2013, a combined English Shinty Association side has entered the Bullough cup, being beaten in 2013 by Tayforth and then in 2014 by Ballachulish.\nShinty is played in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>, with <a href="/wiki/The_Scots_Shinty_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="The Scots Shinty Club">The Scots Shinty Club</a> keeping alive the tradition of the game being played in the Forces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Shinty is also spreading to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>; though originally played in the 18th and 19th century by Scottish immigrants, the sport died out. However, it is enjoying a revival; teams such as <a href="/wiki/Northern_California_Camanachd_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern California Camanachd Club">Northern California Camanachd Club</a> (NCCC), Central California Cammanchd (CCC)), and Oregon Shinty-Camanachd (OSC) play at <a href="/wiki/Highland_Games" class="mw-redirect" title="Highland Games">Highland Games</a> and other venues across the USA.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A small pocket of shinty has also started up in <a href="/wiki/Shinty_in_Russia" title="Shinty in Russia">Russia</a> as of 2014.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_coverage">Media coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shinty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Media coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Local papers, such as the <a href="/wiki/West_Highland_Free_Press" title="West Highland Free Press">West Highland Free Press</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Buteman" title="The Buteman">The Buteman</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Oban_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Oban Times">Oban Times</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dunoon_Observer_and_Argyllshire_Standard" title="Dunoon Observer and Argyllshire Standard">Dunoon Observer and Argyllshire Standard</a>, have in-depth shinty reports. The Inverness-based media reduce shinty coverage to one summary of the whole weekend\'s action as do national newspapers, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Herald" title="Sunday Herald">Sunday Herald</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday Post">Sunday Post</a>. The only significant national press coverage is of the <a href="/wiki/Camanachd_Cup" title="Camanachd Cup">Camanachd Cup</a> final. Regional newspaper <a href="/wiki/The_Press_and_Journal_(Scotland)" title="The Press and Journal (Scotland)">The Press and Journal</a> runs shinty coverage twice a week (Mondays and Fridays).\n</p><p>The first ever shinty match broadcast live on television was the 1964 Celtic Society Cup Final in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Although Camanachd Cup finals and internationals have been shown over the years, 2006 marked the first ever regular TV deal for shinty with matches being shown on the BBC Sports show <b>Sp\xc3\xb2rs</b>. This was then followed by the STV show "An Caman".\n</p><p>2009 saw the Camanachd Association sign a deal with <a href="/wiki/BBC_Alba" title="BBC Alba">BBC Alba</a> to broadcast all national finals as well as the Marine Harvest Festival. The MacAulay Cup and Camanachd Cup final were also shown on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a>. There is also an increasing amount of shinty on the internet, with various clips garnering attention on video sites such as <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>. 2011 was a disappointing year for TV coverage outside of the usual games, but 2012 saw several games filmed live on BBC Alba.\n</p><p>The sport is featured on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_nan_Gaidheal" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC Radio nan Gaidheal">BBC Radio nan Gaidheal</a> by the programme, Sp\xc3\xb2rs na Seachdain, although English-language radio interest is usually restricted to the big events in the year.
