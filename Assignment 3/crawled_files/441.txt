title="Wikisource">Wikisource</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>European Convention on Human Rights</b> (<b>ECHR</b>) (formally the <b>Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms</b>) is an international treaty to protect <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">political freedoms</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.  Drafted in 1950 by the then newly formed <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Council_of_Europe_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Council_of_Europe-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the convention entered into force on 3 September 1953. All <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Member states of the Council of Europe">Council of Europe member states</a> are party to the Convention and new members are expected to ratify the convention at the earliest opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Convention established the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECtHR). Any person who feels his or her rights have been violated under the Convention by a state party can take a case to the Court. Judgments finding violations are binding on the States concerned and they are obliged to execute them. The <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Ministers_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Committee of Ministers of the Council of Europe">Committee of Ministers of the Council of Europe</a> monitors the execution of judgements, particularly to ensure payment of the amounts awarded by the Court to the applicants in compensation for the damage they have sustained.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The compensations imposed under ECHR can be large; in 2014 Russia was ordered to pay in excess of $2 billion in damages to former shareholders of <a href="/wiki/Yukos" title="Yukos">Yukos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Convention has several <a href="/wiki/Protocol_(treaty)" class="mw-redirect" title="Protocol (treaty)">protocols</a>, which amend the convention framework.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_(1).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_%281%29.jpg/220px-Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_%281%29.jpg" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_%281%29.jpg/330px-Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_%281%29.jpg/440px-Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_%281%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="442" data-file-height="308" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stamp_2010_HR_Convention-60_(1).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ukrainian stamp, commemorating 60 years of European Convention on Human Rights</div></div></div> \n<p>The European Convention on Human Rights has played an important role in the development and awareness of Human Rights in Europe.\nThe development of a regional system of human rights protection operating across Europe can be seen as a direct response to twin concerns. First, in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>, the convention, drawing on the inspiration of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> can be seen as part of a wider response of the Allied Powers in delivering a human rights agenda through which it was believed that the most serious human rights violations which had occurred during the Second World War could be avoided in the future. Second, the Convention was a response to the growth of Communism in Central and Eastern Europe and designed to protect the member states of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a> from communist subversion. This, in part, explains the constant references to values and principles that are "necessary in a democratic society" throughout the Convention, despite the fact that such principles are not in any way defined within the convention itself.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 7 to 10 May 1948 with the attendance of politicians (such as <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois Mitterrand">Fran\xc3\xa7ois Mitterrand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>), civil society representatives, academics, business leaders, trade unionist and religious leader was organised gathering-The "<a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Europe" title="Congress of Europe">Congress of Europe</a>" in <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">Hague.</a> At the end of Congress the declaration and following pledge was issued which demonstrated the initial seeds of modern European institutes, including ECHR. The second and third Articles of Pledge stated: We desire  a Charter of Human Rights guaranteeing liberty of thought, assembly and expression as well as right to form a political opposition. We desire a Court of Justice with adequate sanctions for the implementation of this Charter.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Convention was drafted by the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a> after the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> in response to a call issued by Europeans from all walks of life who had gathered at the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Congress_(1948)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hague Congress (1948)">Hague Congress</a>. Over 100 parliamentarians from the twelve member states of the Council of Europe gathered in Strasbourg in the summer of 1949 for the first ever meeting of the Council\'s Consultative Assembly to draft a "charter of human rights" and to establish a court to enforce it. British MP and lawyer Sir <a href="/wiki/David_Maxwell-Fyfe" class="mw-redirect" title="David Maxwell-Fyfe">David Maxwell-Fyfe</a>, the Chair of the Assembly\'s Committee on Legal and Administrative Questions, was one of its leading members and guided the drafting of the Convention. As a prosecutor at the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg Trials">Nuremberg Trials</a>, he had seen first-hand how international justice could be effectively applied. With his help, the French former minister and Resistance fighter <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Henri_Teitgen" title="Pierre-Henri Teitgen">Pierre-Henri Teitgen</a> submitted a report<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> to the Assembly proposing a list of rights to be protected, selecting a number from the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> just agreed to in New York, and defining how the enforcing judicial mechanism might operate. After extensive debates,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the Assembly sent its final proposal<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> to the Council\'s Committee of Ministers, which convened a group of experts to draft the Convention itself.\n</p><p>The Convention was designed to incorporate a traditional <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> approach to securing "effective political democracy", from the strongest traditions in the United Kingdom, France and other member states of the fledgling <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>, as said by <a href="/wiki/Guido_Raimondi" title="Guido Raimondi">Guido Raimondi</a>, President of <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a>:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The European system of protection of human rights with its Court would be inconceivable untied from democracy. In fact we have a bond that is not only regional or geographic: a State cannot be party to the European Convention on Human Rights if it is not a member of the Council of Europe; it cannot be a member State of the Council of Europe if it does not respect pluralist democracy, the rule of law and human rights. So a non-democratic State could not participate in the ECHR system: the protection of democracy goes hand in hand with the protection of rights.</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Guido Raimondi<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The Convention was opened for signature on 4 November 1950 in Rome. It was ratified and entered into force on 3 September 1953. It is overseen and enforced by the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> in Strasbourg, and the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>. Until procedural reforms in the late 1990s, the Convention was also overseen by a European Commission on Human Rights.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drafting">Drafting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drafting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Convention is drafted in broad terms, in a similar (albeit more modern) manner to the <a href="/wiki/English_Bill_of_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="English Bill of Rights">English Bill of Rights</a>, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bill_of_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Bill of Rights">U.S. Bill of Rights</a>, the French <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Declaration of the Rights of Man">Declaration of the Rights of Man</a> or the first part of the German <a href="/wiki/Grundgesetz" class="mw-redirect" title="Grundgesetz">Basic law</a>. Statements of principle are, from a legal point of view, not determinative and require extensive interpretation by courts to bring out meaning in particular factual situations.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Convention_articles">Convention articles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Convention articles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As amended by Protocol 11, the Convention consists of three parts. The main rights and freedoms are contained in Section I, which consists of Articles 2 to 18. Section II (Articles 19 to 51) sets up the Court and its rules of operation. Section III contains various concluding provisions.\n</p><p>Before the entry into force of Protocol 11, Section II (Article 19) set up the Commission and the Court, Sections III (Articles 20 to 37) and IV (Articles 38 to 59) included the high-level machinery for the operation of, respectively, the Commission and the Court, and Section V contained various concluding provisions.\n</p><p>Many of the Articles in Section I are structured in two paragraphs: the first sets out a basic right or freedom (such as Article 2(1) \xe2\x80\x93 the right to life) but the second contains various exclusions, exceptions or limitations on the basic right (such as Article 2(2) \xe2\x80\x93 which excepts certain uses of force leading to death).\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_1_.E2.80.93_respecting_rights"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_1_\xe2\x80\x93_respecting_rights">Article 1 \xe2\x80\x93 respecting rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Article 1 \xe2\x80\x93 respecting rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_1_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 1 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 1 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 1 simply binds the signatory parties to secure the rights under the other Articles of the Convention "within their jurisdiction". In exceptional cases, "jurisdiction" may not be confined to a Contracting State\'s own national territory; the obligation to secure Convention rights then also extends to foreign territories, such as occupied land in which the State exercises effective control.\n</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Loizidou_v_Turkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Loizidou v Turkey">Loizidou v Turkey</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> ruled that jurisdiction of member states to the convention extended to areas under that state\'s effective control as a result of military action.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_2_.E2.80.93_life"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_2_\xe2\x80\x93_life">Article 2 \xe2\x80\x93 life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Article 2 \xe2\x80\x93 life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_2_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 2 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 2 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 2 protects the right of every person to their life. The right to life extends only to human beings, not to non-human animals,<sup id="cite_ref-korff_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-korff-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> or to "legal persons" such as corporations.<sup id="cite_ref-korff_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-korff-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  In <i><a href="/wiki/Evans_v_United_Kingdom" title="Evans v United Kingdom">Evans v United Kingdom</a></i>, the Court ruled that the question of whether the right to life extends to a human embryo fell within a state\'s <a href="/wiki/Margin_of_appreciation" title="Margin of appreciation">margin of appreciation</a>. In <i>Vo v France</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> the Court declined to extend the right to life to an unborn child, while stating that "it is neither desirable, nor even possible as matters stand, to answer in the abstract the question whether the unborn child is a person for the purposes of Article 2 of the Convention".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Court has ruled that states have three main duties under Article 2:\n</p>\n<ol><li>a duty to refrain from unlawful killing,</li>\n<li>a duty to investigate suspicious deaths, and</li>\n<li>in certain circumstances, a positive duty to prevent foreseeable loss of life.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>The first paragraph of the article contains an exception for <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">lawful executions</a>, although this exception has largely been superseded by Protocols 6 and 13. Protocol 6 prohibits the imposition of the death penalty in peacetime, while Protocol 13 extends the prohibition to all circumstances. (For more on Protocols 6 and 13, see <a href="#Protocol_13_\xe2\x80\x93_complete_abolition_of_death_penalty">below</a>).\n</p><p>The second paragraph of Article 2 provides that death resulting from defending oneself or others, arresting a suspect or fugitive, or suppressing riots or insurrections, will not contravene the Article when the use of force involved is "no more than absolutely necessary".\n</p><p>Signatory states to the Convention can only derogate from the rights contained in Article 2 for deaths which result from lawful acts of war.\n</p><p>The European Court of Human Rights did not rule upon the right to life until 1995, when in <i><a href="/wiki/McCann_and_Others_v_United_Kingdom" title="McCann and Others v United Kingdom">McCann and Others v United Kingdom</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> it ruled that the exception contained in the second paragraph does not constitute situations when it is permitted to kill, but situations where it is permitted to use force which might result in the deprivation of life.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_3_.E2.80.93_torture"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_3_\xe2\x80\x93_torture">Article 3 \xe2\x80\x93 torture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Article 3 \xe2\x80\x93 torture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_3_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 3 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 3 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 3 prohibits <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a> and "inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment". There are no exceptions or limitations on this right. This provision usually applies, apart from torture, to cases of severe police violence and poor conditions in detention.\n</p><p>The Court has emphasized the fundamental nature of Article 3 in holding that the prohibition is made in "absolute terms&#160;... irrespective of a victim\'s conduct".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Court has also held that states cannot deport or <a href="/wiki/Extradition" title="Extradition">extradite</a> individuals who might be subjected to torture, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment, in the recipient state.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Initially, the Court took a restrictive view on what consisted of torture, preferring to find that states had inflicted inhuman and degrading treatment. Thus the court held that practices such as sleep deprivation, subjecting individual to intense noise and requiring them to stand against a wall with their limbs outstretched for extended periods of time, did not constitute torture.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In fact the Court only found a state guilty of torture in 1996 in the case of a detainee who was suspended by his arms while his hands were tied behind his back.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Since then the Court has appeared to be more open to finding states guilty of torture and has even ruled that since the Convention is a "living instrument", treatment which it had previously characterized as inhuman or degrading treatment might in future be regarded as torture.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_4_.E2.80.93_servitude"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_4_\xe2\x80\x93_servitude">Article 4 \xe2\x80\x93 servitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Article 4 \xe2\x80\x93 servitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_4_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 4 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 4 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 4 prohibits <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>, servitude and <a href="/wiki/Forced_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labour">forced labour</a> but exempts labour:\n</p>\n<ul><li>done as a normal part of imprisonment,</li>\n<li>in the form of <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">compulsory military service</a> or work done as an alternative by conscientious objectors,</li>\n<li>required to be done during a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a>, and</li>\n<li>considered to be a part of a person\'s normal "civic obligations".</li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Article_5_.E2.80.93_liberty_and_security"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_5_\xe2\x80\x93_liberty_and_security">Article 5 \xe2\x80\x93 liberty and security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Article 5 \xe2\x80\x93 liberty and security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_5_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 5 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 5 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 5 provides that everyone has the right to liberty and security of person. Liberty and security of the person are taken as a "compound" concept \xe2\x80\x93 security of the person has not been subject to separate interpretation by the Court.\n</p><p>Article 5 provides the right to <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">liberty</a>, subject only to lawful arrest or detention under certain other circumstances, such as arrest on reasonable suspicion of a crime or imprisonment in fulfilment of a sentence. The article also provides those arrested with the right to be informed, in a language they understand, of the reasons for the arrest and any charge they face, the right of prompt access to judicial proceedings to determine the legality of the arrest or detention, to trial within a reasonable time or release pending trial, and the right to compensation in the case of arrest or detention in violation of this article.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Assanidze_v._Georgia" title="Assanidze v. Georgia"> Assanidze v. Georgia, App. No. 71503/01 (Eur. Ct. H.R. Apr. 8, 2004)</a></li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Article_6_.E2.80.93_fair_trial"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_6_\xe2\x80\x93_fair_trial">Article 6 \xe2\x80\x93 fair trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Article 6 \xe2\x80\x93 fair trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_6_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 6 provides a detailed <a href="/wiki/Right_to_a_fair_trial" title="Right to a fair trial">right to a fair trial</a>, including the right to a <a href="/wiki/Hearing_(law)" title="Hearing (law)">public hearing</a> before an independent and impartial tribunal within reasonable time, the <a href="/wiki/Presumption_of_innocence" title="Presumption of innocence">presumption of innocence</a>, and other minimum rights for those charged with a criminal offence (adequate time and facilities to prepare their defence, access to legal representation, right to examine witnesses against them or have them examined, right to the free assistance of an interpreter).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The majority of Convention violations that the Court finds today are excessive delays, in violation of the "reasonable time" requirement, in civil and criminal proceedings before national courts, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Under the "independent tribunal" requirement, the Court has ruled that military judges in Turkish state security courts are incompatible with Article 6. In compliance with this Article, Turkey has now adopted a law abolishing these courts.\n</p><p>Another significant set of violations concerns the "confrontation clause" of Article 6 (i.e. the right to examine witnesses or have them examined). In this respect, problems of compliance with Article 6 may arise when national laws allow the use in evidence of the testimonies of absent, anonymous and vulnerable witnesses.\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/McLibel_Case" class="mw-redirect" title="McLibel Case">Steel v. United Kingdom</a></i> (1998) 28 EHRR 603</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Assanidze_v._Georgia" title="Assanidze v. Georgia"> Assanidze v. Georgia, App. No. 71503/01 (Eur. Ct. H.R. Apr. 8, 2004)</a></li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Othman_(Abu_Qatada)_v._United_Kingdom" title="Othman (Abu Qatada) v. United Kingdom">Othman (Abu Qatada) v. United Kingdom</a></i> (2012) \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Abu_Qatada" title="Abu Qatada">Abu Qatada</a> could not be deported to Jordan as that would be a violation of Article 6 "given the real risk of the admission of evidence obtained by torture". This was the first time the court ruled that such an expulsion would be a violation of Article 6.<sup id="cite_ref-Tele_17.01.2012_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tele_17.01.2012-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Article_7_.E2.80.93_retroactivity"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_7_\xe2\x80\x93_retroactivity">Article 7 \xe2\x80\x93 retroactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Article 7 \xe2\x80\x93 retroactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_7_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 7 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 7 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 7 prohibits the retroactive criminalisation of acts and omissions. No person may be punished for an act that was not a criminal offence at the time of its commission. The article states that a criminal offence is one under either national or international law, which would permit a party to prosecute someone for a crime which was not illegal under domestic law at the time, so long as it was prohibited by <a href="/wiki/Public_international_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Public international law">international law</a>. The Article also prohibits a heavier penalty being imposed than was applicable at the time when the criminal act was committed.\n</p><p>Article 7 incorporates the legal principle <i><a href="/wiki/Nullum_crimen,_nulla_poena_sine_lege" class="mw-redirect" title="Nullum crimen, nulla poena sine lege">nullum crimen, nulla poena sine lege</a></i> into the convention.\n</p><p>Relevant cases are: \n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Kokkinakis_v._Greece" title="Kokkinakis v. Greece">Kokkinakis v. Greece</a></i> [1993] ECHR 20</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/S.A.S._v._France" title="S.A.S. v. France">S.A.S. v. France</a></i> [2014] ECHR 69</li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Article_8_.E2.80.93_privacy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_8_\xe2\x80\x93_privacy">Article 8 \xe2\x80\x93 privacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Article 8 \xe2\x80\x93 privacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_8_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 8 provides a right to respect for one\'s "private and family life, his home and his <a href="/wiki/Privacy_of_correspondence" class="mw-redirect" title="Privacy of correspondence">correspondence</a>", subject to certain restrictions that are "in accordance with law" and "necessary in a democratic society". This article clearly provides a right to be free of unlawful searches, but the Court has given the protection for "private and family life" that this article provides a broad interpretation, taking for instance that prohibition of private consensual homosexual acts violates this article. There have been cases discussing consensual familial sexual relationships, and how the criminalisation of this may violate this article. However, the ECHR still deems such familial sexual acts to be criminal.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> This may be compared to the jurisprudence of the United States Supreme Court, which has also adopted a somewhat broad interpretation of the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_privacy" title="Right to privacy">right to privacy</a>. Furthermore, Article&#160;8 sometimes comprises <a href="/wiki/Positive_obligations" title="Positive obligations">positive obligations</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> whereas classical human rights are formulated as prohibiting a State from interfering with rights, and thus <i>not</i> to do something (e.g. not to separate a family under family life protection), the effective enjoyment of such rights may also include an obligation for the State to become active, and to <i>do</i> something (e.g. to enforce access for a divorced parent to his/her child).\n</p><p>Notable cases:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Zakharov_v._Russia" title="Zakharov v. This includes the freedom to change a religion or belief, and to manifest a religion or belief in worship, teaching, practice and observance, subject to certain restrictions that are "in accordance with law" and "necessary in a democratic society"\n</p><p>Relevant cases are: \n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Kokkinakis_v._Greece" title="Kokkinakis v. integrity or public safety</li>\n<li>prevention of disorder or crime</li>\n<li>protection of health or morals</li>\n<li>protection of the reputation or the rights of others</li>\n<li>preventing the disclosure of information received in confidence</li>\n<li>maintaining the authority and impartiality of the judiciary</li></ul>\n<p>Relevant cases are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a id="Article_11_\xe2\x80\x93_association">Article 11 \xe2\x80\x93 association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Article 11 \xe2\x80\x93 association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_11_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 11 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 11 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 11 protects the right to <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_assembly" title="Freedom of assembly">freedom of assembly</a> and association, including the right to form <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a>, subject to certain restrictions that are "in accordance with law" and "necessary in a democratic society".\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Vogt_v_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Vogt v Germany">Vogt v Germany</a></i> (1995)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yazar,_Karatas,_Aksoy_and_Hep_v_Turkey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yazar, Karatas, Aksoy and Hep v Turkey (page does not exist)">Yazar, Karatas, Aksoy and Hep v Turkey</a></i> (2003) 36 EHRR 59</li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Article_12_.E2.80.93_marriage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_12_\xe2\x80\x93_marriage">Article 12 \xe2\x80\x93 marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Article 12 \xe2\x80\x93 marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_12_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 12 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 12 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 12 provides a right for women and men of <a href="/wiki/Marriageable_age" title="Marriageable age">marriageable age</a> to <a href="/wiki/Marry" class="mw-redirect" title="Marry">marry</a> and establish a family.\n</p><p>Despite a number of invitations, the Court has so far refused to apply the protections of this article to <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>. The Court has defended this on the grounds that the article was intended to apply only to different-sex marriage, and that a wide margin of appreciation must be granted to parties in this area.\n</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Goodwin_v_United_Kingdom" title="Goodwin v United Kingdom">Goodwin v United Kingdom</a></i> the Court ruled that a law which still classified post-operative <a href="/wiki/Transsexual" title="Transsexual">transsexual</a> persons under their pre-operative sex, violated article 12 as it meant that transsexual persons were unable to marry individuals of their post-operative opposite sex. This reversed an earlier ruling in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rees_v_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rees v United Kingdom (page does not exist)">Rees v United Kingdom</a></i>. This did not, however, alter the Court\'s understanding that Article 12 protects only different-sex couples.\n</p><p>The European Court of Human Rights ruled in <i><a href="/wiki/Schalk_and_Kopf_v_Austria" title="Schalk and Kopf v Austria">Schalk and Kopf v Austria</a></i> that countries are not required to provide marriage licenses for same-sex couples, however if a country allows same-sex couple marriage it must be done so under the same conditions that opposite-sex couples marriage face: in order to prevent a breach of article 14 \xe2\x80\x93 the prohibition of discrimination. Additionally, the court ruled in the 2015 case of <i><a href="/wiki/Oliari_and_Others_v_Italy" title="Oliari and Others v Italy">Oliari and Others v Italy</a></i>, that states have a positive obligation to ensure there is a specific legal framework for the recognition and protection of same-sex couples.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_13_.E2.80.93_effective_remedy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_13_\xe2\x80\x93_effective_remedy">Article 13 \xe2\x80\x93 effective remedy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Article 13 \xe2\x80\x93 effective remedy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Article 13 provides for the right for an effective <a href="/wiki/Legal_remedy" title="Legal remedy">remedy</a> before national authorities for violations of rights under the Convention. The inability to obtain a remedy before a national court for an infringement of a Convention right is thus a free-standing and separately actionable infringement of the Convention.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_14_.E2.80.93_discrimination"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_14_\xe2\x80\x93_discrimination">Article 14 \xe2\x80\x93 discrimination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Article 14 \xe2\x80\x93 discrimination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Article 14 contains a prohibition of <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a>. This prohibition is broad in some ways and narrow in others. It is broad in that it prohibits discrimination under a potentially unlimited number of grounds. While the article specifically prohibits discrimination based on "sex, race, colour, language, religion, political or other opinions, national or social origin, association with a national minority, property, birth or other status", the last of these allows the court to extend to Article 14 protection to other grounds not specifically mentioned such as has been done regarding discrimination based on a person\'s sexual orientation.\n</p><p>At the same time, the article\'s protection is limited in that it only prohibits discrimination with respect to rights under the Convention. Thus, an applicant must prove discrimination in the enjoyment of a specific right that is guaranteed elsewhere in the Convention (e.g. discrimination based on sex \xe2\x80\x93 Article 14 \xe2\x80\x93 in the enjoyment of the right to freedom of expression \xe2\x80\x93 Article 10). It has been said that laws regarding familial sexual relationships (or incest) are in breach of Article 14 when combined with Article 8.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Protocol 12 extends this prohibition to cover discrimination in any legal right, even when that legal right is not protected under the Convention, so long as it is provided for in national law.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_15_.E2.80.93_derogations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_15_\xe2\x80\x93_derogations">Article 15 \xe2\x80\x93 derogations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Article 15 \xe2\x80\x93 derogations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Article 15 allows contracting states to <a href="/wiki/Derogation" title="Derogation">derogate</a> from certain rights guaranteed by the Convention in a time of "war or other public emergency threatening the life of the nation". Permissible derogations under article 15 must meet three substantive conditions:\n</p>\n<ol><li>there must be a public emergency threatening the life of the nation;</li>\n<li>any measures taken in response must be "strictly required by the exigencies of the situation", and</li>\n<li>the measures taken in response to it, must be in compliance with a state\'s other obligations under international law</li></ol>\n<p>In addition to these substantive requirements, the derogation must be procedurally sound. There must be some formal announcement of the derogation and notice of the derogation, any measures adopted under it, and the ending of the derogation must be communicated to the Secretary-General of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of 2016, eight member states had ever invoked derogations.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The Court is quite permissive in accepting a state\'s derogations from the Convention but applies a higher degree of scrutiny in deciding whether measures taken by states under a derogation are, in the words of Article 15, "strictly required by the exigencies of the situation". Thus in <i>A&#160;v&#160;United Kingdom</i>, the Court dismissed a claim that a derogation lodged by the British government in response to the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> was invalid, but went on to find that measures taken by the United Kingdom under that derogation were disproportionate.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In order for a derogation itself to be valid, the emergency giving rise to it must be:\n</p>\n<ul><li>actual or imminent, although states do not have to wait for disasters to strike before taking preventive measures,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>involve the whole nation, although a threat confined to a particular region may be treated as "threatening the life of the nation" in that particular region,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>threaten the continuance of the organised life of the community,<sup id="cite_ref-greekcase_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greekcase-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>exceptional such that measures and restriction permitted by the Convention would be "plainly inadequate" to deal with the emergency.<sup id="cite_ref-greekcase_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greekcase-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Examples of such derogations include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Operation_Demetrius" title="Operation Demetrius">Operation Demetrius</a>\xe2\x80\x94Internees arrested without trial pursuant to "Operation Demetrius" could not complain to the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission_of_Human_Rights" title="European Commission of Human Rights">European Commission of Human Rights</a> about breaches of Article 5 because on 27 June 1975, the UK lodged a notice with the Council of Europe declaring that there was a "public emergency within the meaning of Article 15(1) of the Convention".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Article_16_.E2.80.93_aliens"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_16_\xe2\x80\x93_aliens">Article 16 \xe2\x80\x93 aliens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Article 16 \xe2\x80\x93 aliens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Article 16 allows states to restrict the political activity of foreigners. The Court has ruled that European Union member states cannot consider the nationals of other member states to be aliens.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_17_.E2.80.93_abuse_of_rights"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_17_\xe2\x80\x93_abuse_of_rights">Article 17 \xe2\x80\x93 abuse of rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Article 17 \xe2\x80\x93 abuse of rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Article 17 provides that no one may use the rights guaranteed by the Convention to seek the abolition or limitation of rights guaranteed in the Convention. This addresses instances where states seek to restrict a human right in the name of another human right, or where individuals rely on a human right to undermine other human rights (for example where an individual issues a death threat).\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Article_18_.E2.80.93_permitted_restrictions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_18_\xe2\x80\x93_permitted_restrictions">Article 18 \xe2\x80\x93 permitted restrictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Article 18 \xe2\x80\x93 permitted restrictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_18_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 18 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 18 of the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Article 18 provides that any limitations on the rights provided for in the Convention may be used only for the purpose for which they are provided. For example, Article 5, which guarantees the right to personal freedom, may be explicitly limited in order to bring a suspect before a judge. To use pre-trial detention as a means of intimidation of a person under a false pretext is, therefore, a limitation of right (to freedom) which does not serve an explicitly provided purpose (to be brought before a judge), and is therefore contrary to Article 18.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Convention_protocols">Convention protocols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Convention protocols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As of  January 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, fifteen protocols to the Convention have been opened for signature. These can be divided into two main groups: those amending the framework of the convention system, and those expanding the rights that can be protected. The former require unanimous ratification by member states before coming into force, while the latter require a certain number of states to sign before coming into force.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_1">Protocol 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Protocol 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This Protocol contains three different rights which the signatories could not agree to place in the Convention itself.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> have signed but never ratified Protocol 1.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Article_1_.E2.80.93_property"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_1_\xe2\x80\x93_property">Article 1 \xe2\x80\x93 property</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Article 1 \xe2\x80\x93 property">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Article 1 provides for the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_property" title="Right to property">right to the peaceful enjoyment of one\'s possessions</a>. The <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> acknowledged a violation of the fair balance between the demands of the general interest of the community and the requirements of the protection of the individual\'s fundamental rights, also, in the uncertainty \xe2\x80\x93 for the owner \xe2\x80\x93 about the future of the property, and in the absence of an allowance.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Article_2_.E2.80.93_education"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_2_\xe2\x80\x93_education">Article 2 \xe2\x80\x93 education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Article 2 \xe2\x80\x93 education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Article 2 provides for the right not to be denied an education and the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_education" title="Freedom of education">right for parents to have their children educated</a> in accordance with their religious and other views. It does not however guarantee any particular level of education of any particular quality.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although phrased in the Protocol as a negative right, in <i><a href="/wiki/Leyla_%C5%9Eahin_v._Turkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Leyla \xc5\x9eahin v. Turkey">\xc5\x9eahin v. Turkey</a></i> the Court ruled that:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>it would be hard to imagine that institutions of higher education existing at a given time do not come within the scope of the first sentence of Article 2 of Protocol No 1. Although that Article does not impose a duty on the Contracting States to set up institutions of higher education, any State doing so will be under an obligation to afford an effective right of access to them. In a democratic society, the right to education, which is indispensable to the furtherance of human rights, plays such a fundamental role that a restrictive interpretation of the first sentence of Article 2 of Protocol No. 1 would not be consistent with the aim or purpose of that provision.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h4><span id="Article_3_.E2.80.93_elections"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_3_\xe2\x80\x93_elections">Article 3 \xe2\x80\x93 elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Article 3 \xe2\x80\x93 elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Article 3 provides for the right to elections performed by secret ballot, that are also free and that occur at regular intervals.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Matthews_v._United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthews v. United Kingdom">Matthews v. United Kingdom</a></i> (1999) 28 EHRR 361</li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Protocol_4_.E2.80.93_civil_imprisonment.2C_free_movement.2C_expulsion"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_4_\xe2\x80\x93_civil_imprisonment,_free_movement,_expulsion">Protocol 4 \xe2\x80\x93 civil imprisonment, free movement, expulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Protocol 4 \xe2\x80\x93 civil imprisonment, free movement, expulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Article 1 prohibits the imprisonment of people for inability to fulfil a contract. Article 2 provides for a <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_movement" title="Freedom of movement">right to freely move</a> within a country once lawfully there and for a right to leave any country. Article 3 prohibits the expulsion of nationals and provides for the right of an individual to enter a country of his or her nationality. Article 4 prohibits the collective expulsion of foreigners.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> have signed but never ratified Protocol 4. <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> have neither signed nor ratified this protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The United Kingdom\'s failure to ratify this protocol is due to concerns over the interaction of Article 2 and Article 3 with <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British nationality law</a>. Specifically, several classes of "British national" (such as <a href="/wiki/British_National_(Overseas)" title="British National (Overseas)">British National (Overseas)</a>) do not have the right of abode in the United Kingdom and are subject to immigration control there. In 2009, the UK government stated that it had no plans to ratify Protocol 4 because of concerns that those articles could be taken as conferring that right.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Protocol_6_.E2.80.93_restriction_of_death_penalty"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_6_\xe2\x80\x93_restriction_of_death_penalty">Protocol 6 \xe2\x80\x93 restriction of death penalty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Protocol 6 \xe2\x80\x93 restriction of death penalty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Requires parties to restrict the application of the <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">death penalty</a> to times of war or "imminent threat of war".\n</p><p>Every Council of Europe member state has signed and ratified Protocol 6, except <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, which has signed but not ratified.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Protocol_7_.E2.80.93_crime_and_family"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_7_\xe2\x80\x93_crime_and_family">Protocol 7 \xe2\x80\x93 crime and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Protocol 7 \xe2\x80\x93 crime and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>Article 1 provides for a <a href="/wiki/Right_to_fair_trial" class="mw-redirect" title="Right to fair trial">right to fair procedures</a> for <a href="/wiki/Permanent_residency" title="Permanent residency">lawfully resident</a> foreigners facing expulsion.</li>\n<li>Article 2 provides for the right to <a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a> in criminal matters.</li>\n<li>Article 3 provides for compensation for the victims of miscarriages of justice.</li>\n<li>Article 4 prohibits the re-trial of anyone who has already been finally acquitted or convicted of a particular offence (<a href="/wiki/Double_jeopardy" title="Double jeopardy">Double jeopardy</a>).</li>\n<li>Article 5 provides for equality between <a href="/wiki/Spouse" title="Spouse">spouses</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>Despite having signed the protocol more than thirty years ago Germany and the Netherlands have never ratified it. Turkey, which signed the protocol in 1985, ratified it in 2016, becoming the latest member state to do so. The United Kingdom has neither signed nor ratified the protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Protocol_12_.E2.80.93_discrimination"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_12_\xe2\x80\x93_discrimination">Protocol 12 \xe2\x80\x93 discrimination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Protocol 12 \xe2\x80\x93 discrimination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Protocol_12_to_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Protocol 12 to the European Convention on Human Rights">Protocol 12 to the European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>Applies the current expansive and indefinite grounds of prohibited discrimination in <a href="#Article_14_\xe2\x80\x93_prohibition_of_discrimination">Article 14</a> to the exercise of any legal right and to the actions (including the obligations) of public authorities.\n</p><p>The Protocol entered into force on 1 April 2005 and has (As of  March 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>) been ratified by 20 member states. Several member states\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>\xe2\x80\x94have not signed the protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The United Kingdom government has declined to sign Protocol 12 on the basis that they believe the wording of protocol is too wide and would result in a flood of new cases testing the extent of the new provision. They believe that the phrase "rights set forth by law" might include international conventions to which the UK is not a party, and would result in incorporation of these instruments by stealth. It has been suggested that the protocol is therefore in a <a href="/wiki/Catch-22_(logic)" title="Catch-22 (logic)">catch-22</a>, since the UK will decline to either sign or ratify the protocol until the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> has addressed the meaning of the provision, while the court is hindered in doing so by the lack of applications to the court concerning the protocol caused by the decisions of Europe\'s most populous states\xe2\x80\x94including the UK\xe2\x80\x94not to ratify the protocol. The UK government, nevertheless, "agrees in principle that the ECHR should contain a provision against discrimination that is free-standing and not parasitic on the other Convention rights".<sup id="cite_ref-UK_position_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UK_position-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The first judgment that found a violation of Protocol No. 12, <i><a href="/wiki/Sejdi%C4%87_and_Finci_v._Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Sejdi\xc4\x87 and Finci v. Bosnia and Herzegovina">Sejdi\xc4\x87 and Finci v. Bosnia and Herzegovina</a></i>, was delivered in 2009.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Protocol_13_.E2.80.93_complete_abolition_of_death_penalty"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_13_\xe2\x80\x93_complete_abolition_of_death_penalty">Protocol 13 \xe2\x80\x93 complete abolition of death penalty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Protocol 13 \xe2\x80\x93 complete abolition of death penalty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Protocol 13 provides for the total abolition of the death penalty.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Currently all Council of Europe member states but three have ratified Protocol 13. <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> has signed but not ratified the protocol. <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> have not signed it.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedural_and_institutional_protocols">Procedural and institutional protocols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Procedural and institutional protocols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Convention\'s provisions affecting institutional and procedural matters have been altered several times by means of protocols. These amendments have, with the exception of Protocol 2, amended the text of the convention. Protocol 2 did not amend the text of the convention as such but stipulated that it was to be treated as an integral part of the text. All of these protocols have required the unanimous ratification of all the member states of the Council of Europe to enter into force.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_11">Protocol 11</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Protocol 11">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Protocols 2, 3, 5, 8, 9 and 10 have now been superseded by Protocol 11 which entered into force on 1 November 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> It established a fundamental change in the machinery of the convention. It abolished the Commission, allowing individuals to apply directly to the Court, which was given compulsory jurisdiction and altered the latter\'s structure. Previously states could ratify the Convention without accepting the jurisdiction of the Court of Human Rights. The protocol also abolished the judicial functions of the Committee of Ministers.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol_14">Protocol 14</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Convention_on_Human_Rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Protocol 14">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Protocol 14 follows on from Protocol 11 in proposing to further improve the efficiency of the Court. It seeks to "filter" out cases that have less chance of succeeding along with those that are broadly similar to cases brought previously against the same member state. Furthermore, a case will not be considered admissible where an applicant has not suffered a "significant disadvantage". This latter ground can only be used when an examination of the application on the merits is not considered necessary and where the subject-matter of the application had already been considered by a national court.\n</p><p>A new mechanism was introduced by Protocol 14 to assist enforcement of judgements by the Committee of Ministers. The Committee can ask the Court for an interpretation of a judgement and can even bring a member state before the Court for non-compliance of a previous judgement against that state. Protocol 14 also allows for <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> <a href="/wiki/Relationship_between_the_European_Court_of_Justice_and_European_Court_of_Human_Rights#EU_accession_to_ECHR" title="Relationship between the European Court of Justice and European Court of Human Rights">accession to the Convention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The protocol has been ratified by every Council of Europe member state, Russia being last in February 2010. It entered into force on 1 June 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>&lt;f.aldi&gt;\n</p><p>A provisional <b>Protocol 14bis</b> had been opened for signature in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Pending the ratification of Protocol 14 itself, 14bis was devised to allow the Court to implement revised procedures in respect of the states which have ratified it. It allowed single judges to reject manifestly inadmissible applications made against the states that have ratified the protocol. It also extended the competence of three-judge chambers to declare applications made against those states admissible and to decide on their merits where there already is a well-established case law of the Court. Now that all Council of Europe member states have ratified Protocol 14, Protocol 14bis has lost its <i>raison d\'\xc3\xaatre</i> and according to its own terms ceased to have any effect when Protocol 14 entered into force on 1 June 2010.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
