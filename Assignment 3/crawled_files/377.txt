Several accounts of how he originally defined his scale exist. The lower defining point, 0&#160;\xc2\xb0F, was established as the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of a solution of <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> made from equal parts of ice, water and salt (ammonium chloride).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Further limits were established as the melting point of ice (32&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and his best estimate of the average <a href="/wiki/Human_body_temperature" title="Human body temperature">human body temperature</a> (96&#160;\xc2\xb0F, about 2.6&#160;\xc2\xb0F less than the modern value due to a later redefinition of the scale).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The scale is now usually defined by two fixed points: the temperature at which <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> freezes into <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> is defined as 32&#160;\xc2\xb0F, and the boiling point of water is defined to be 212&#160;\xc2\xb0F, a 180&#160;\xc2\xb0F separation, as defined at sea level and standard atmospheric pressure.\n</p><p>By the end of the 20th century, Fahrenheit was used as the official temperature scale only in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (including its <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_territories_of_the_United_States" title="Unincorporated territories of the United States">unincorporated territories</a>), its <a href="/wiki/Compact_of_Free_Association" title="Compact of Free Association">freely associated states</a> in the Western Pacific (<a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia" title="Federated States of Micronesia">Federated States of Micronesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a>), the <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda" title="Antigua and Barbuda">Antigua and Barbuda</a> and other islands which use the same meteorological service, such as <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Saint Kitts and Nevis">Saint Kitts and Nevis</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>, use Fahrenheit and <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>. All other countries in the world now use the Celsius scale, named after <a href="/wiki/Swedish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish people">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a>, defined since 1954 by absolute zero being \xe2\x88\x92273.15&#160;\xc2\xb0C and the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of water being at 0.01&#160;\xc2\xb0C.<sup id="cite_ref-nist_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nist-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Before 1954, the Celsius scale was based on 0&#160;\xc2\xb0C for the freezing point of water and 100&#160;\xc2\xb0C for the boiling point of water at 1 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>. Since the conversion between \xc2\xb0C and \xc2\xb0F was kept unchanged<sup id="cite_ref-nist_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nist-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the definition of \xc2\xb0F is now based on the triple point, too; the freezing and boiling points of water are only very good approximations.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" style="text-align:center;">For temperature <i>intervals</i> rather than specific temperatures,<br />1&#160;\xc2\xb0F = 1&#160;\xc2\xb0R = &#x200b;<span class="frac href="/wiki/Temperature_conversion_formulas" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperature conversion formulas">Comparisons among various temperature scales</a></small>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>On the Fahrenheit scale, the <a href="/wiki/Freezing_point" class="mw-redirect" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is 32&#160;degrees&#160;Fahrenheit (\xc2\xb0F) and the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> is 212&#160;\xc2\xb0F (at <a href="/wiki/Standard_atmospheric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard atmospheric pressure">standard atmospheric pressure</a>). This puts the boiling and freezing points of water 180&#160;degrees apart.<sup id="cite_ref-rank_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rank-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, a degree on the Fahrenheit scale is &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>180</sub></span> of the interval between the freezing point and the boiling point. On the Celsius scale, the freezing and boiling points of water are 100&#160;degrees apart. A temperature interval of 1&#160;\xc2\xb0F is equal to an interval of &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>5</sup>&#8260;<sub>9</sub></span>&#160;degrees Celsius. The Fahrenheit and Celsius scales intersect at \xe2\x88\x9240\xc2\xb0 (i.e., \xe2\x88\x9240&#160;\xc2\xb0F = \xe2\x88\x9240&#160;\xc2\xb0C).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">Absolute zero</a> is \xe2\x88\x92273.15&#160;\xc2\xb0C or \xe2\x88\x92459.67&#160;\xc2\xb0F. The <i>Rankine</i> temperature scale uses degree intervals of the same size as those of the Fahrenheit scale, except that absolute zero is 0&#160;\xc2\xb0R \xe2\x80\x94 the same way that the <i><a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvin</a></i> temperature scale matches the Celsius scale, except that absolute zero is 0&#160;K.<sup id="cite_ref-rank_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rank-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Fahrenheit scale uses the symbol <a href="/wiki/Degree_symbol" title="Degree symbol">\xc2\xb0</a> to denote a point on the temperature scale (as does Celsius) and the letter F to indicate the use of the Fahrenheit scale (<i>e.g.</i> "<a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">Gallium</a> melts at 85.5763 \xc2\xb0F"),<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> as well as to denote a difference between temperatures or an uncertainty in temperature (<i>e.g.</i> "The output of the heat exchanger experiences an increase of 72&#160;\xc2\xb0F" and "Our standard uncertainty is \xc2\xb15 \xc2\xb0F").\n</p><p>For an exact conversion, the following formulas can be applied. Here, <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i></span> is the value in Fahrenheit and <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i></span> the value in Celsius:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>f</i> \xc2\xb0Fahrenheit to <i>c</i> \xc2\xb0Celsius&#160;: <span class="texhtml">(<i>f</i> \xe2\x88\x92 32) \xc2\xb0F \xc3\x97 <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">5\xc2\xb0C</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">9\xc2\xb0F</span></span> = <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">(<i>f</i> \xe2\x88\x92 32)</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">1.8</span></span> \xc2\xb0C = <i>c</i> \xc2\xb0C</span></li>\n<li><i>c</i> \xc2\xb0Celsius to <i>f</i> \xc2\xb0Fahrenheit&#160;: <span class="texhtml">(<i>c</i> \xc2\xb0C \xc3\x97 <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">9\xc2\xb0F</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">5\xc2\xb0C</span></span>) + 32 \xc2\xb0F = (<i>c</i> \xc3\x97 1.8) \xc2\xb0F + 32 \xc2\xb0F = <i>f</i> \xc2\xb0F</span></li></ul>\n<p>This is also an exact conversion making use of the identity -40&#160;\xc2\xb0F = -40&#160;\xc2\xb0C. Again, <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i></span> is the value in Fahrenheit and <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i></span> the value in Celsius:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>f</i> \xc2\xb0Fahrenheit to <i>c</i> \xc2\xb0Celsius&#160;: <span class="texhtml">((<i>f</i> + 40) \xc3\xb7 1.8) \xe2\x88\x92 40 = <i>c</i></span>.</li>\n<li><i>c</i> \xc2\xb0Celsius to <i>f</i> \xc2\xb0Fahrenheit&#160;: <span class="texhtml">((<i>c</i> + 40) \xc3\x97 1.8) \xe2\x88\x92 40 = <i>f</i></span>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahrenheit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:168px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fahrenheit_small.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Fahrenheit_small.jpg" width="166" height="227" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="166" data-file-height="227" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fahrenheit_small.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Gabriel_Fahrenheit" title="Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit">Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit</a>, a pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Precision_thermometry" class="mw-redirect" title="Precision thermometry">exact thermometry</a> (<a href="/wiki/Temperature_measurement" title="Temperature measurement">temperature measurement</a>). He invented the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_thermometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury thermometer">mercury thermometer</a> (first practical, accurate <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a>) and Fahrenheit scale (first widely used, standardized <a href="/wiki/Temperature_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperature scale">temperature scale</a>).</div></div></div>\n<p>Fahrenheit proposed his temperature scale in 1724, basing it on two reference points of temperature. In his initial scale (which is not the final Fahrenheit scale), the zero point was determined by placing the <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a> in  a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, water, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> (<i>salis Armoniaci</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This is a <a href="/wiki/Frigorific_mixture" title="Frigorific mixture">frigorific mixture</a> which stabilizes its temperature automatically: that stable temperature was defined as 0&#160;\xc2\xb0F (\xe2\x88\x9217.78&#160;\xc2\xb0C). The second point, 96&#160;degrees, was approximately the human body\'s temperature (<i>sanguine hominis sani</i>, the blood of a healthy man).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to a story in Germany, Fahrenheit actually chose the lowest air temperature measured in his hometown <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gda\xc5\x84sk">Danzig</a> in winter 1708/09 as 0&#160;\xc2\xb0F, and only later had the need to be able to make this value reproducible using brine.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This is one explanation given why 0&#160;\xc2\xb0F is \xe2\x88\x9217.78&#160;\xc2\xb0C, but the <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> cooling temperature actually is \xe2\x88\x923&#160;\xc2\xb0C, whereas that of NaCl is \xe2\x88\x9221.1&#160;\xc2\xb0C; the other explanation is that he did not have a good enough brine solution to obtain the <a href="/wiki/Eutectic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eutectic">eutectic</a> equilibrium exactly (i.e. he might have had a mixture of salts, or it had not fully dissolved). In any case, the definition of the Fahrenheit scale has changed since.\n</p><p>According to a letter Fahrenheit wrote to his friend <a href="/wiki/Herman_Boerhaave" title="Herman Boerhaave">Herman Boerhaave</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> his scale was built on the work of <a href="/wiki/Ole_R%C3%B8mer" title="Ole R\xc3\xb8mer">Ole R\xc3\xb8mer</a>, whom he had met earlier. In <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B8mer_scale" title="R\xc3\xb8mer scale">R\xc3\xb8mer\'s scale</a>, brine freezes at zero, water freezes and melts at 7.5&#160;degrees, body temperature is 22.5, and water boils at 60&#160;degrees. Fahrenheit multiplied each value by four in order to eliminate fractions and increase the <a href="/wiki/Granularity" title="Granularity">granularity</a> of the scale. He then re-calibrated his scale using the melting point of ice and normal human body temperature (which were at 30 and 90 degrees); he adjusted the scale so that the melting point of ice would be 32 degrees and body temperature 96 degrees, so that 64 intervals would separate the two, allowing him to mark degree lines on his instruments by simply bisecting the interval six times (since 64 is 2 to the sixth power).<sup id="cite_ref-TMU_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TMU-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Fahrenheit observed that water boils at about 212 degrees using this scale. The use of the freezing and boiling points of water as thermometer fixed reference points became popular following the work of <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a> and these fixed points were adopted by a committee of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cavendish" title="Henry Cavendish">Henry Cavendish</a> in 1776.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Under this system, the Fahrenheit scale is redefined slightly so that the freezing point of water is exactly 32&#160;\xc2\xb0F, and the boiling point is exactly 212&#160;\xc2\xb0F or 180 degrees higher. It is for this reason that <a href="/wiki/Normal_human_body_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Normal human body temperature">normal human body temperature</a> is approximately 98\xc2\xb0 (oral temperature) on the revised scale (whereas it was 90\xc2\xb0 on Fahrenheit\'s multiplication of R\xc3\xb8mer, and 96\xc2\xb0 on his original scale).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Rankine_scale" title="Rankine scale">Rankine temperature scale</a> was based upon the Fahrenheit temperature scale, with its zero representing <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a> instead.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahrenheit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg/350px-Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg.png" width="350" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg/525px-Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg/700px-Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1314" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #339933; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Countries that use Fahrenheit.</div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #cccccc; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Countries that use Celsius.</div></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thermometer_CF.svg" class="image"><img alt="Thermometer CF.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Thermometer_CF.svg/220px-Thermometer_CF.svg.png" width="220" height="383" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Thermometer_CF.svg/330px-Thermometer_CF.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Thermometer_CF.svg/440px-Thermometer_CF.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1150" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thermometer_CF.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>The Fahrenheit scale was the primary temperature standard for climatic, industrial and medical purposes in English-speaking countries until the 1960s. In the late 1960s and 1970s, the Celsius scale replaced Fahrenheit in almost all of those countries\xe2\x80\x94with the notable exception of the United States\xe2\x80\x94typically during their <a href="/wiki/Metrication" title="Metrication">metrication</a> process.\n</p><p>Fahrenheit is used in the United States, its territories and associated states (all served by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>), as well as the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a> for everyday applications. For example, U.S. weather forecasts, food cooking, and freezing temperatures are typically given in degrees Fahrenheit. Scientists, such as meteorologists, use Celsius or Kelvin in all countries.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Early in the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_A._Halsey" title="Frederick A. Halsey">Halsey</a> and Dale suggested that the resistance to the use of centigrade (now Celsius) system in the U.S. included the larger size of each degree Celsius and the lower zero point in the Fahrenheit system.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Canada <a href="/wiki/Metrication_in_Canada" title="Metrication in Canada">has passed legislation</a> favoring the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a>, while also maintaining legal definitions for traditional Canadian imperial units.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Canadian weather reports are conveyed using degrees Celsius with occasional reference to Fahrenheit especially for cross-border broadcasts. Virtually all Canadian ovens make legal use of the Fahrenheit scale.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Thermometers, both digital and analog, sold in Canada usually employ both the Celsius and Fahrenheit scales.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Canadian-unit-laws_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian-unit-laws-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  Also, in some instances (swimming pool temperature, thermostats, or cooking temperatures for example), temperatures are still expressed in Fahrenheit.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Waschen_40.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Waschen_40.svg/170px-Waschen_40.svg.png" width="170" use kelvins or degrees Celsius when quoting temperature for "economic, public health, public safety and administrative" purposes, though degrees Fahrenheit may be used alongside degrees Celsius as a supplementary unit.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Laundry_symbol" title="Laundry symbol">laundry symbols</a> used in the United Kingdom follow the recommendations of ISO 3758:2005 showing the temperature of the washing machine water in degrees Celsius only.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The equivalent label in North America uses one to six dots to denote temperature with an optional temperature in degrees Celsius.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Within the unregulated sector, such as journalism, the use of Fahrenheit in the United Kingdom follows no fixed pattern with degrees Fahrenheit often appearing alongside degrees Celsius. <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">The Daily Mail</a></i>, on its daily weather page, quotes Celsius first, followed by Fahrenheit in brackets,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> does not mention Fahrenheit on its daily weather page<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> also has an all-metric daily weather page but has a Celsius-to-Fahrenheit conversion table.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> When publishing news stories, much of the UK  press and population have adopted a convention of using degrees Celsius in headlines and discussion relating to low temperatures and Fahrenheit for high temperatures. In February 2006, the writer of an article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> suggested that the rationale was one of emphasis:  "\xe2\x88\x926 \xc2\xb0C" sounds colder than "21 \xc2\xb0F" and "94 \xc2\xb0F" sounds more impressive than "34 \xc2\xb0C".<sup id="cite_ref-T20060223_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-T20060223-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicode_representation_of_symbol">Unicode representation of symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahrenheit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unicode representation of symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> provides the Fahrenheit symbol at code point <span class="nowrap"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r861301850">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced">U+2109</span></span>&#x20;<span style="font-size:125%">&#x2109;</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">degree fahrenheit</span>.
