style="padding:0;background:transparent;text-align:right;">\n2018</td></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:right"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Comhaont\xc3\xba Aoine an Ch\xc3\xa9asta</i> or <i>Comhaont\xc3\xba Bh\xc3\xa9al Feirste</i>; <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_dialects" title="Ulster Scots dialects">Ulster-Scots</a>: <i>Guid Friday Greeance</i> or <i>Bilfawst Greeance</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was a major political development in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a> of the 1990s. <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>\'s present <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolved</a> system of government is based on the agreement. The agreement also created a number of institutions between Northern Ireland and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, and between the Republic of Ireland and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.\n</p><p>The agreement is made up of two inter-related documents, both agreed in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> on <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a>, 10 April 1998:\n</p>\n<ol><li>a multi-party agreement by most of Northern Ireland\'s political parties (the <b>Multi-Party Agreement</b>);</li>\n<li>an international agreement between the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Government">British</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Irish</a> governments (the <b>British-Irish Agreement</b>).</li></ol>\n<p>The agreement set out a complex series of provisions relating to a number of areas including:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The status and system of government of Northern Ireland within the United Kingdom. (Strand 1)</li>\n<li>The relationship between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland. (Strand 2)</li>\n<li>The relationship between the Republic of Ireland and the United Kingdom. (Strand 3)</li></ul>\n<p>Issues relating to <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_rights" title="Cultural rights">cultural rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decommissioning_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Decommissioning in Northern Ireland">decommissioning of weapons</a>, demilitarisation, justice, and policing were central to the agreement.\n</p><p>The agreement was approved by voters across the island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> in two referendums held on 22 May 1998. In Northern Ireland, voters were asked in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Good_Friday_Agreement_referendum,_1998" title="Northern Ireland Good Friday Agreement referendum, 1998">Northern Ireland Good Friday Agreement referendum, 1998</a> whether they supported the multi-party agreement. In the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, voters were asked whether they would allow the state to sign the agreement and allow necessary constitutional changes (<a href="/wiki/Nineteenth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Nineteenth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">Nineteenth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland</a>) to facilitate it. The people of both jurisdictions needed to approve the agreement in order to give effect to it.\n</p><p>The British-Irish Agreement came into force on 2 December 1999 with the help of British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cain.ulst.ac.uk_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cain.ulst.ac.uk-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morgan2000_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morgan2000-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a> (DUP) was the only major political group in Northern Ireland to oppose the Good Friday Agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parties_and_structure">Parties and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Parties and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The agreement was made between the British and Irish governments and eight political parties or groupings from Northern Ireland: the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster Unionist Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Alliance Party of Northern Ireland">Alliance Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Unionist_Party" title="Progressive Unionist Party">Progressive Unionist Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Women%E2%80%99s_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ireland Women\xe2\x80\x99s Coalition">Northern Ireland Women\'s Coalition</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Democratic_Party" title="Ulster Democratic Party">Ulster Democratic Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labour_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour Coalition">Labour</a>.\n</p><p>The agreement comprises two elements:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the legal agreement between the two governments, signed by the leaders of the two governments; and</li>\n<li>a more substantial agreement between the eight political parties and the two governments.</li></ul>\n<p>The former text has just four articles; it is that short text that is the legal agreement, but it incorporates in its schedules the latter agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-morgan2000_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morgan2000-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Technically, this scheduled agreement can be distinguished as the <i>Multi-Party Agreement</i>, as opposed to the Belfast Agreement itself.<sup id="cite_ref-morgan2000_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morgan2000-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The vague wording of some of the provisions, described as "constructive ambiguity",<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> helped ensure acceptance of the agreement and served to postpone debate on some of the more contentious issues. Most notably these included paramilitary decommissioning, police reform and the normalisation of Northern Ireland.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_of_Northern_Ireland">Status of Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Status of Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The agreement acknowledged:\n</p>\n<ul><li>that the majority of the people of Northern Ireland wished to remain a part of the United Kingdom;</li>\n<li>that a substantial section of the people of Northern Ireland, and the majority of the people of the island of Ireland, wished to bring about a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>Both of these views were acknowledged as being legitimate. For the first time, the Irish government accepted in a binding international agreement that Northern Ireland was part of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-Austen_Morgan_pg._7_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Austen_Morgan_pg._7-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Irish Constitution</a> was also amended to implicitly recognise Northern Ireland as part of the United Kingdom\'s sovereign territory,<sup id="cite_ref-morgan2000_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morgan2000-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> conditional upon the consent for a united Ireland from majorities of the people in both jurisdictions on the island. On the other hand, the language of the agreement reflects a switch in the United Kingdom\'s statutory emphasis from one for the union to one for a united Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Austen_Morgan_pg._7_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Austen_Morgan_pg._7-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The agreement thus left the issue of future sovereignty over Northern Ireland open-ended.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The agreement reached was that Northern Ireland was part of the United Kingdom, and would remain so until a majority of the people both of Northern Ireland and of the Republic of Ireland wished otherwise. Should that happen, then the British and Irish governments are under "a binding obligation" to implement that choice.\n</p><p>Irrespective of Northern Ireland\'s constitutional status within the United Kingdom, or part of a united Ireland, the right of "the people of Northern Ireland" to "identify themselves and be accepted as Irish or British, or both" (as well as their right to hold either or both <a href="/wiki/British_citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="British citizen">British</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Irish_citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish citizen">Irish citizenship</a>) was recognised. By the words "people of Northern Ireland" the Agreement meant "all persons born in Northern Ireland and having, at the time of their birth, at least one parent who is a British citizen, an Irish citizen or is otherwise entitled to reside in Northern Ireland without any restriction on their period of residence."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The two governments also agreed, irrespective of the position of Northern Ireland:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>the power of the sovereign government with jurisdiction there shall be exercised with rigorous impartiality on behalf of all the people in the diversity of their identities and traditions and shall be founded on the principles of full respect for, and equality of, civil, political, economic, social and cultural rights, of freedom from discrimination for all citizens, and of parity of esteem and of just and equal treatment for the identity, ethos and aspirations of both communities.</p></blockquote>\n<p>As part of the agreement, the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British parliament</a> repealed the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a> (which had established Northern Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partitioned Ireland</a> and asserted a territorial claim over all of Ireland) and the people of the Republic of Ireland amended <a href="/wiki/Articles_2_and_3_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Articles 2 and 3 of the Constitution of Ireland">Articles 2 and 3 of the Constitution of Ireland</a>, which asserted a territorial claim over Northern Ireland.\n</p><p>In its white paper on <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a>, the United Kingdom government reiterated its commitment to the Belfast Agreement. With regard to Northern Ireland\'s status, it said that the UK Government\'s "clearly-stated preference is to retain Northern Ireland\'s current constitutional position: as part of the UK, but with strong links to Ireland".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_institutions">New institutions</span><span "strands".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strand_1">Strand 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Strand 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Strand 1 dealt with the democratic institutions of Northern Ireland and established two major institutions:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a></li></ul>\n<p>The Northern Ireland Assembly is a devolved legislature for Northern Ireland with mandatory <a href="/wiki/Cross-community_vote" title="Cross-community vote">cross-community voting</a> on certain major decisions. The Northern Ireland Executive is a <a href="/wiki/Consociationalism" title="Consociationalism">power-sharing</a> executive with ministerial portfolios to be allocated between parties by the <a href="/wiki/D%27Hondt_method" title="D&#39;Hondt method">d\'Hondt method</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strand_2">Strand 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Strand 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Strand 2 dealt with "north-south" issues and institutions to be created between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland. These are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/North/South_Inter-Parliamentary_Association" title="North/South Inter-Parliamentary Association">North/South Inter-Parliamentary Association</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/North/South_Consultative_Forum" title="North/South Consultative Forum">North/South Consultative Forum</a></li></ul>\n<p>The North/South Ministerial Council is made up of ministers from the Northern Ireland Executive and the Government of Ireland. It was established "to develop consultation, co-operation and action" in twelve areas of mutual interest. These include six areas where the Northern Ireland Executive and the Government of Ireland form common policies but implement these separately in each jurisdiction, and six areas where they develop common policies that are implemented through shared all-Ireland institutions.\n</p><p>The various "institutional and constitutional arrangements" set out in the Agreement are also stated to be "interlocking and interdependent".\n</p><p>As part of the Agreement, the newly created Northern Ireland Assembly and the national parliament of Ireland (the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a>) agreed to consider creating a joint parliamentary forum made up of equal numbers from both institutions. In October 2012, this forum was created as the North/South Inter-Parliamentary Association.\n</p><p>The Northern Ireland political parties who endorsed the agreement were also asked to consider the establishment of an independent consultative forum representative of <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil society</a> with members with expertise in social, cultural, economic and other issues and appointed by the two administrations. An outline structure for the North/South Consultative Forum was agreed in 2002 and in 2006 the Northern Ireland Executive agreed it would support its establishment.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg/220px-North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg/330px-North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg/440px-North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2193" data-file-height="1404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North-South_Ministerial_Council_Offices.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The offices of the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a> on Upper English Street, <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a>, Northern Ireland</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strand_3">Strand 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Strand 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Strand 3 dealt with "east-west" issues and institutions to be created between Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> (as well as the Crown dependencies). These are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Council" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council</a></li>\n<li>An expanded <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Parliamentary_Assembly" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Parliamentary Assembly">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Interparliamentary Body</a></li></ul>\n<p>The British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Intergovernmental Conference was agreed to replace the Anglo-Irish Intergovernmental Council and the Intergovernmental Conference created under the 1985 <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a>.\n</p><p>The conference takes the form of regular and frequent meetings between the British and Irish ministers to promote co-operation at all levels between both governments. On <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly#Powers_and_functions" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">matters not devolved</a> to Northern Ireland, the Government of Ireland may put forward view and proposals. All decisions of the conference will be by agreement between both governments and the two governments agreed to make determined efforts to resolve disagreements between them.\n</p><p>The British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council is made up of ministerial representatives from the British and Irish governments, the UK\'s devolved administrations (Northern Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Scotland">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Wales">Wales</a>), as well as from the <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>. The purpose of the council is to promote co-operations and pose a forum for the creation of common policies.\n</p><p>Under the agreement, it was proposed that the already-existing British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Interparliamentary Body would be built upon. Prior to the agreement, the body was composed of parliamentarians from the British and Irish parliaments only. In 2001, as suggested by the agreement, it was expanded to incorporate parliamentarians from all of the members of the British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council.\n</p><p>These institutional arrangements created across these three strands are set out in the agreement as being "interlocking and interdependent". In particular, the functioning of the Northern Ireland Assembly and the North/South Ministerial Council are stated to be "so closely inter-related that the success of each depends on that of the other" and participation in the North/South Ministerial Council is "one of the essential responsibilities attaching to relevant posts in [Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland]."\n</p><p>In the opinion of analyst <a href="/wiki/Brendan_O%27Leary" title="Brendan O&#39;Leary">Brendan O\'Leary</a>, the institutions established by the deal "made Northern Ireland bi-national" and reinforced "imaginative elements of co-sovereignty."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decommissioning_and_normalisation">Decommissioning and normalisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decommissioning and normalisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(April 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Against the background of political violence during <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>, the agreement committed the participants to "exclusively democratic and peaceful means of resolving differences on political issues". This took two aspects:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Decommissioning_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Decommissioning in Northern Ireland">decommissioning of weapons</a> held by paramilitary groups;</li>\n<li>the normalisation of security arrangements in Northern Ireland.</li></ul>\n<p>The participants to the agreement comprised two sovereign states (the United Kingdom and the Republic of Ireland) with armed and police forces involved in the Troubles. Two political parties, Sinn F\xc3\xa9in and the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Unionist_Party" title="Progressive Unionist Party">Progressive Unionist Party</a> (PUP), were linked to <a href="/wiki/Paramilitary" title="Paramilitary">paramilitary</a> organisations: the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA) and the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force" title="Ulster Volunteer Force">Ulster Volunteer Force</a> (UVF) respectively. The <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Democratic_Party" title="Ulster Democratic Party">Ulster Democratic Party</a> (UDP), which was linked to the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Defence_Association" title="Ulster Defence Association">Ulster Defence Association</a> (UDA), had withdrawn from the talks three months previously.\n</p><p>The multi-party agreement committed the parties to "use any influence they may have" to bring about the decommissioning of all paramilitary arms within two years of the referendums approving the agreement. The process of normalisation committed the British government to the reduction in the number and role of its <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed Forces of the Crown">armed forces</a> in Northern Ireland "to levels compatible with a normal peaceful society". This included the removal of security installations and the removal of special emergency powers in Northern Ireland. The Irish government committed to a "wide-ranging review" of its <a href="/wiki/Offences_against_the_State_Acts_1939%E2%80%931998" title="Offences against the State Acts 1939\xe2\x80\x931998">Offences against the State</a> legislation.\n</p><p>The agreement called for the establishment of an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Commission_on_Policing_for_Northern_Ireland" title="Independent Commission on Policing for Northern Ireland">independent commission</a> to review policing arrangements in Northern Ireland "including [the] means of encouraging widespread community support" for those arrangements. The British government also committed to a "wide-ranging review" of the criminal justice system in Northern Ireland.\n</p><p>Both the British and Irish governments committed to the early release of prisoners serving sentences in connection with the activities of paramilitary groups, provided that those groups continued to maintain "a complete and unequivocal ceasefire". Cases were reviewed individually.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  There was no amnesty for crimes which had not been prosecuted.\n</p><p>A date of May 2000 was set for total disarming of all paramilitary groups. This was not achieved leading the assembly to be suspended on a number of occasions as a consequence of unionist objections.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A series of rounds of decommissioning by the IRA took place (in October 2001, April 2002 and October 2003) and in July 2005 the IRA announced the formal end of its campaign. Loyalist decommissioning did not follow immediately. In June 2009, the UVF announced it had completed decommissioning and the UDA said it had started to decommission its arsenal.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equality_and_human_rights">Equality and human rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Equality and human rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The agreement affirmed a commitment to "the mutual respect, the civil rights and the religious liberties of everyone in the community". The multi-party agreement recognised "the importance of respect, understanding and tolerance in relation to <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_diversity" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguistic diversity">linguistic diversity</a>", especially in relation to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_dialects" title="Ulster Scots dialects">Ulster Scots</a>, and the languages of Northern Ireland\'s other ethnic minorities, "all of which are part of the cultural wealth of the island of Ireland".\n</p><p>The British government committed to incorporate the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_of_Human_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="European Convention of Human Rights">European Convention of Human Rights</a> (ECHR) into the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of Northern Ireland">law of Northern Ireland</a> and to the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Human_Rights_Commission" title="Northern Ireland Human Rights Commission">Northern Ireland Human Rights Commission</a>. Setting statutory obligations for public authorities in Northern Ireland to carry out their work "with due regard to the need to promote <a href="/wiki/Equality_of_opportunity" class="mw-redirect" title="Equality of opportunity">equality of opportunity</a> was set as a particular priority." The Irish government committed to "[taking] steps to further the protection of human rights in its jurisdiction" and to the establishment of an <a href="/wiki/Irish_Human_Rights_Commission" title="Irish Human Rights Commission">Irish Human Rights Commission</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Referendums">Referendums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span entry into force was to be upon the receipt of the latter of the two notifications.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The British government agreed to participate in a televised ceremony at <a href="/wiki/Iveagh_House" title="Iveagh House">Iveagh House</a> in Dublin, the Irish department of foreign affairs. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Mandelson" title="Peter Mandelson">Peter Mandelson</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Northern_Ireland" title="Secretary of State for Northern Ireland">Secretary of State for Northern Ireland</a>, attended early on 2 December 1999. He exchanged notifications with <a href="/wiki/David_Andrews_(Irish_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Andrews (Irish politician)">David Andrews</a>, the Irish foreign minister. Shortly after the ceremony, at 10:30 am, the <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a>, signed the declaration formally amending <a href="/wiki/Articles_2_and_3_of_the_Irish_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Articles 2 and 3 of the Irish Constitution">Articles 2 and 3 of the Irish Constitution</a>. He then announced to the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il" class="mw-redirect" title="D\xc3\xa1il">D\xc3\xa1il</a> that the British-Irish Agreement had entered into force (including certain supplementary agreements concerning the Belfast Agreement).<sup id="cite_ref-morgan2000_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morgan2000-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nSpeaking at the 1998 commemoration of the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> of 1916, Ahern said:</p><blockquote><p>The British Government are effectively out of the equation and neither the British parliament nor people have any legal right under this agreement to impede the achievement of Irish unity if it had the consent of the people North and South... Our nation is and always will be a 32-county nation. Antrim and Down are, and will remain, as much a part of Ireland as any southern county.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>The assembly and executive were eventually established in December 1999 on the understanding that decommissioning would begin immediately, but were suspended within two months due to lack of progress, before being re-established in May 2000 as Provisional IRA decommissioning eventually began. Aside from the decommissioning issue, however, ongoing paramilitary activity (albeit relatively low-level compared to the past) by the Provisional Irish Republican Army\xe2\x80\x94e.g., arms importations, smuggling, organised crime, "punishment beatings", intelligence-gathering and rioting\xe2\x80\x94was also a stumbling block. The loyalist paramilitaries also continued similar activity although as they were not represented by a significant political party, their position was less central to political change.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source needed for this section (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The overall result of these problems was to damage confidence among unionists in the agreement, which was exploited by the anti-agreement DUP, which eventually overtook the pro-agreement <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster Unionist Party</a> (UUP) in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2003" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2003">2003 Assembly election</a>. The UUP had already resigned from the power-sharing Executive in 2002 following the <a href="/wiki/Stormontgate" title="Stormontgate">Stormontgate</a> scandal, which saw three men charged with intelligence-gathering. These charges were eventually dropped in 2005 on the controversial grounds that pursuit would not be "in the public interest". Immediately afterwards, one of the accused Sinn F\xc3\xa9in members, <a href="/wiki/Denis_Donaldson" title="Denis Donaldson">Denis Donaldson</a>, was exposed as a British agent.\n</p><p>In 2004, negotiations were held between the two governments, the DUP, and Sinn F\xc3\xa9in on an agreement to re-establish the institutions. These talks failed, but a document published by the governments detailing changes to the Belfast Agreement became known as the "<a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Agreement" title="Comprehensive Agreement">Comprehensive Agreement</a>". On 26 September 2005, however, it was announced that the Provisional Irish Republican Army had completely decommissioned its arsenal of weapons and "put them beyond use". Nonetheless, many unionists, most notably the DUP, remained sceptical. Of the loyalist paramilitaries, only the <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_Volunteer_Force" title="Loyalist Volunteer Force">Loyalist Volunteer Force</a> (LVF) had decommissioned any weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Further negotiations took place in October 2006, leading to the <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews_Agreement" title="St Andrews Agreement">St Andrews Agreement</a>.\n</p><p>In May 2007, a power-sharing executive was again established to govern Northern Ireland in devolved matters. The second <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> had <a href="/wiki/Ian_Paisley" title="Ian Paisley">Ian Paisley</a> of the DUP as First Minister and <a href="/wiki/Martin_McGuinness" title="Martin McGuinness">Martin McGuinness</a> of Sinn F\xc3\xa9in as Deputy First Minister. Although Paisley was the official head of the government, he and Martin McGuinness held equal powers.\n</p><p>Paisley retired from the office of First Minister and from the leadership of the DUP on 5 June 2008 and was succeeded in both functions by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Robinson_(Northern_Ireland_politician)" title="Peter Robinson (Northern Ireland politician)">Peter Robinson</a>. In the third Northern Ireland Executive, the same political relationship existed between Robinson and McGuinness as existed formerly between Paisley and McGuinness. After Robinson resigned as First Minister on 11 January 2016, he was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Arlene_Foster" title="Arlene Foster">Arlene Foster</a>. Upon McGuinness\'s resignation on 9 January 2017, the devolved government in Stormont collapsed, as the Agreement demands when no new leader is appointed. An election was called by Secretary of State for Northern Ireland <a href="/wiki/James_Brokenshire" title="James Brokenshire">James Brokenshire</a>, whereby the DUP and Sinn F\xc3\xa9in were returned as the largest parties, and so began a countdown of talks between both leaders before devolved government could be restored. Currently, Stormont is not in session and no government is in power.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_the_Sunningdale_Agreement">Comparison to the Sunningdale Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: href="/wiki/Sunningdale_Agreement" title="Sunningdale Agreement">Sunningdale Agreement</a></div>\n<p>Some commentators have referred to the Agreement as "Sunningdale for slow learners", which suggests that it was nothing more than what was on offer in the Sunningdale Agreement of 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This assertion has been criticised by some political scientists, one of whom stated that "there are... significant differences between them [Sunningdale and Belfast], both in terms of content and the circumstances surrounding their negotiation, implementation, and operation".<sup id="cite_ref-wil_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wil-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The main issues omitted by Sunningdale and addressed by the Belfast Agreement are the principle of <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a>, the recognition of both national identities, British-Irish intergovernmental cooperation and the legal procedures to make power-sharing mandatory, such as the cross-community vote and the d\'Hondt system to appoint ministers to the executive.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Former IRA member and journalist <a href="/wiki/Tommy_McKearney" title="Tommy McKearney">Tommy McKearney</a> says that the main difference is the intention of the British government to broker a comprehensive deal by including the IRA and the most uncompromising unionists.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Regarding the right to self-determination, two qualifications are noted by the legal writer Austen Morgan. Secondly, the people of Northern Ireland can no longer bring about a united Ireland on their own; they need not only the Irish government but the people of their neighbouring state, Ireland, to also endorse unity. Ireland Constitution Act 1973">Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973</a>, devised under Sunningdale, the 1998 agreement and the consequent British legislation did expressly foresee the possibility of a united Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As well as the number of signatories,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a addressed in the Sunningdale Agreement\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_relevance">Constitutional relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Good_Friday_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Constitutional relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Because the Good Friday Agreement binds the British government on several points of law in Northern Ireland, it has de facto become a part of <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">Constitution of the United Kingdom</a>. Legal commentator <a href="/wiki/David_Allen_Green" title="David Allen Green">David Allen Green</a> described it as "a core constitutional text of the UK, and of Ireland [...] of more everyday importance than hallowed instruments such as, say, <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a> of 1215 or the <a href="/wiki/1689_Bill_of_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="1689 Bill of Rights">1689 Bill of Rights</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-DAG_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAG-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because the Agreement commits the government to enshrine the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> in law and allows Northern Irish residents access to the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a>, it required enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a>. Consequently, the Agreement was a significant factor preventing the repeal of that Act and its replacement with the <a href="/wiki/Proposed_British_Bill_of_Rights" title="Proposed British Bill of Rights">proposed British Bill of Rights</a> that Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> had promised.<sup id="cite_ref-DAG_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAG-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Agreement also makes reference to the UK and Ireland as "partners in the European Union", and it was argued in <i><a href="/wiki/R_(Miller)_v_Secretary_of_State_for_Exiting_the_European_Union" title="R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union">R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union</a></i> that the Agreement meant that the consent of Northern Ireland\'s voters was required to <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">leave the European Union</a>. The <a href="/wiki/UK_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Supreme Court">UK Supreme Court</a> unanimously held that this was not the case, but the Agreement has nevertheless strongly shaped the form of Brexit.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Brexit_negotiations" title="Brexit negotiations">negotiations on Britain\'s planned 2019 withdrawal from the European Union</a>, the EU produced a position paper on its concerns regarding support of the Good Friday Agreement by the UK during Brexit. The position paper address topics including the avoidance of a hard border, the North-South cooperation between Ireland and Northern Ireland, the birthright of all of the people of Northern Ireland (as set out in the Agreement), and the <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EU_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Anyone born in Northern Ireland, and thus entitled to an Irish passport by the Good Friday Agreement, will also be able to retain EU citizenship after Brexit.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_negotiating_directives_for_Brexit" title="European Union negotiating directives for Brexit">European Union negotiating directives for Brexit</a>, the UK was asked to satisfy the other EU members that these topics had been addressed in order to progress to the second stage of Brexit negotiations. In order to protect North-South co-operation and avoid controls on the <a href="/wiki/Irish_border_question" title="Irish border question">Irish border</a>, the UK agreed to protect the Agreement in all its parts and "in the absence of agreed solutions, the United Kingdom will maintain full alignment with those rules of the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Market" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal Market">Internal Market</a> and the <a href="/wiki/EU_Customs_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="EU Customs Union">Customs Union</a> which, now or in the future, support North-South cooperation, the all-island economy and the protection of the 1998 Agreement."<sup id="cite_ref-DAG_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAG-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
