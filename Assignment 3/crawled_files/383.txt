id="Irish_home_rule">Irish home rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Irish home rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_movement" title="Irish Home Rule movement">Irish Home Rule movement</a></div>\n<p>The issue of <a href="/wiki/Irish_home_rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish home rule">Irish home rule</a> was the dominant political question of <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> politics at the end of the 19th and beginning of the 20th century.\n</p><p>Earlier in the 19th century, Irish politicians like <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O&#39;Connell">Daniel O\'Connell</a> had demanded a <a href="/wiki/(Irish)_Repeal" class="mw-redirect" title="(Irish) Repeal">repeal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1800">Act of Union 1800</a> and a return to two separate kingdoms and parliaments, united only in the personal union of the monarch of Great Britain and Ireland. In contrast to this, demands for home rule called for autonomy for Ireland within the United Kingdom, with a subsidiary Irish parliament subject to the authority of the parliament at Westminster. This issue was first introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Butt" title="Isaac Butt">Isaac Butt</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shaw_(Irish_politician)" title="William Shaw (Irish politician)">William Shaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Charles Stewart Parnell</a>.\n</p><p>Over the course of four decades, four <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Home Rule Bill">Irish Home Rule Bills</a> were introduced into the British Parliament:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Government_Bill_1886" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Government Bill 1886">First Home Rule Bill</a> was introduced in 1886 by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a>. Following <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionism" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster Unionism">intense opposition in Ulster</a> and the departure of <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Unionist_Party" title="Liberal Unionist Party">Unionists</a> from Gladstone\'s <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>, the bill was defeated in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>.</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Government_Bill_1893" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Government Bill 1893">Second Home Rule Bill</a> was introduced in 1893 by Prime Minister Gladstone and passed the Commons but was rejected in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Third Home Rule Bill</a> was introduced in 1912 by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a> based on an agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a>. After a prolonged parliamentary struggle was passed under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act of 1911</a>, under which the Commons overruled the veto by the Lords. Again, this bill was fiercely opposed by <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionists" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster Unionists">Ulster Unionists</a> who raised the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteers" title="Ulster Volunteers">Ulster Volunteers</a> and signed the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Covenant" title="Ulster Covenant">Ulster Covenant</a> to oppose the bill, thereby raising the spectre of civil war. The act received royal assent (with restrictions in regard to Ulster) shortly after the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> but implementation was suspended until after the war\'s conclusion. Attempts at implementation failed in 1916 and 1917 and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a> (1919\xe2\x80\x9322) resulted in it never coming into force.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Fourth Home Rule Bill</a> was introduced in 1920 by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> and passed both houses of parliament. It divided Ireland into <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> (six counties) and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> (twenty-six counties), which each had their own parliament and judiciary but which also shared some common institutions. The Act was implemented in Northern Ireland, where it served as the basis of government until its suspension in 1972 following the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>. The southern parliament convened only once and in 1922, under the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>, Southern Ireland became the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, a <a href="/wiki/British_dominion" class="mw-redirect" title="British dominion">dominion</a> within the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, and was declared fully sovereign in 1937 (see <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>).</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Northern_Ireland" title="History of Northern Ireland">History of Northern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/British_Isles_Northern_Ireland.svg/240px-British_Isles_Northern_Ireland.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="675" data-file-height="1180" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Northern_Ireland.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing Northern Ireland</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StormontGeneral.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/StormontGeneral.jpg/220px-StormontGeneral.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:StormontGeneral.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Parliament_Buildings_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Parliament Buildings (Northern Ireland)">Stormont Parliament Building</a>, home of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Home Rule came into effect for <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> in 1921 under the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Fourth Home Rule Act</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a> established under that act was prorogued (the session ended) on 30 March 1972 owing to the destabilisation of Northern Ireland upon the onset of <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> in late 1960s. This followed escalating violence by state and paramilitary organisations following the suppression of civil rights demands by Northern Ireland Catholics.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ireland Parliament">Northern Ireland Parliament</a> was abolished by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Constitution_Act_1973" title="Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973">Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973</a>, which received royal assent on 19 July 1973. A <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_(1973)" title="Northern Ireland Assembly (1973)">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> was elected on 28 June 1973 and following the <a href="/wiki/Sunningdale_Agreement" title="Sunningdale Agreement">Sunningdale Agreement</a>, a power-sharing <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive_(1974)" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ireland Executive (1974)">Northern Ireland Executive</a> was formed on 1 January 1974.  This collapsed on 28 May 1974, due to the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Workers%27_Council_strike" title="Ulster Workers&#39; Council strike">Ulster Workers\' Council strike</a>. The Troubles continued.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Constitutional_Convention" title="Northern Ireland Constitutional Convention">Northern Ireland Constitutional Convention</a> (1975\xe2\x80\x931976) and second <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_(1982)" title="Northern Ireland Assembly (1982)">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> (1982\xe2\x80\x931986) were unsuccessful at restoring devolution.  In the absence of devolution and power-sharing, the <a href="/wiki/UK_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Government">UK Government</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Government">Irish Government</a> formally agreed to co-operate on security, justice and political progress in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a>, signed on 15 November 1985. More progress was made after the ceasefires by the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_IRA" class="mw-redirect" title="Provisional IRA">Provisional IRA</a> in 1994 and 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1998 <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Belfast Agreement">Belfast Agreement</a> (also known as the Good Friday Agreement), resulted in the creation of a new <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, intended to bring together the two communities (<a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish nationalist">nationalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_unionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish unionist">unionist</a>) to govern Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson_2003_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson_2003-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, renewed devolution in Northern Ireland was conditional on co-operation between the newly established <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> through a new all-Ireland body, the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a>.  A <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Council">British-Irish Council</a> covering the whole British Isles and a <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Intergovernmental_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Intergovernmental Conference">British-Irish Intergovernmental Conference</a> (between the British and Irish Governments) were also established.\n</p><p>From 15 October 2002, the Northern Ireland Assembly was suspended due to a breakdown in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a> but, on 13 October 2006, the British and Irish governments announced the <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews_Agreement" title="St Andrews Agreement">St Andrews Agreement</a>, a \'road map\' to restore devolution to Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_2006_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_2006-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> On 26 March 2007, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a> (DUP) leader <a href="/wiki/Ian_Paisley" title="Ian Paisley">Ian Paisley</a> met <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Adams" title="Gerry Adams">Gerry Adams</a> for the first time and together announced that a devolved government would be returning to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Executive was restored on 8 May 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Several policing and justice powers were transferred to the Assembly on 12 April 2010.\n</p><p>The 2007\xe2\x80\x932011 Assembly (the third since the 1998 Agreement) was dissolved on 24 March 2011 in preparation for an <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2011" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2011">election</a> to be held on Thursday 5 May 2011, this being the first Assembly since the Good Friday Agreement to complete a full term.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The fifth Assembly convened in May 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  That assembly dissolved on 26 January 2017, and an <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2017" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2017">election</a> for a reduced Assembly was held on 2 March 2017.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Scottish_devolution" title="History of Scottish devolution">History of Scottish devolution</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Scotland.svg" class="image"><img alt="" data-file-height="507" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scottish_Parliament,_from_Salisbury_Crags.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_Building" title="Scottish Parliament Building">Scottish Parliament Building</a>, Edinburgh</div></div></div>\n<p>Ever since the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a> adjourned in 1707 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union</a>, individuals and organisations had advocated the return of a Scottish Parliament. The drive for home rule first took concrete shape in the 19th century, as demands for it in Ireland were met with similar (although not as widespread) demands in Scotland. The <a href="/wiki/National_Association_for_the_Vindication_of_Scottish_Rights" title="National Association for the Vindication of Scottish Rights">National Association for the Vindication of Scottish Rights</a> was established in 1853, a body close to the <a href="/wiki/Unionist_Party_(Scotland)" title="Unionist Party (Scotland)">Tories</a> and motivated by a desire to secure more focus on Scottish problems in response to what they felt was undue attention being focused on Ireland by the then <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> government. In 1871, <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a> stated at a meeting held in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Aberdeen, Scotland">Aberdeen</a> that if Ireland was to be granted home rule, then the same should apply to Scotland. A Scottish home rule bill was presented to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster Parliament</a> in 1913 but the legislative process was interrupted by the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>.\n</p><p>The demands for political change in the way in which Scotland was run changed dramatically in the 1920s when Scottish nationalists started to form various organisations. The <a href="/wiki/Scots_National_League" title="Scots National League">Scots National League</a> was formed in 1920 in favour of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a>, and this movement was superseded in 1928 by the formation of the <a href="/wiki/National_Party_of_Scotland" title="National Party of Scotland">National Party of Scotland</a>, which became the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> (SNP) in 1934. At first the SNP sought only the establishment of a devolved Scottish assembly, but in 1942 they changed this to support all-out independence. This caused the resignation of <a href="/wiki/John_MacCormick" title="John MacCormick">John MacCormick</a> from the SNP and he formed the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Covenant_Association" title="Scottish Covenant Association">Scottish Covenant Association</a>. This body proved to be the biggest mover in favour of the formation of a Scottish assembly, collecting over two million signatures in the late 1940s and early 1950s and attracting support from across the political spectrum. However, without formal links to any of the political parties it withered, devolution and the establishment of an assembly were put on the political back burner.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>\'s Labour government set up a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Royal Commission on the Constitution</a> in 1969, which reported in 1973 to <a href="/wiki/Ted_Heath" class="mw-redirect" title="Ted Heath">Ted Heath</a>\'s Conservative government. The Commission recommended the formation of a devolved Scottish assembly, but was not implemented.\n</p><p>Support for the SNP reached 30% in the October, 1974 general election, with 11 SNP MPs being elected. In 1978 the Labour government passed the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1978" title="Scotland Act 1978">Scotland Act</a> which legislated for the establishment of a Scottish Assembly, provided the Scots voted for such in a <a href="/wiki/Scotland_referendum,_1979" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotland referendum, 1979">referendum</a>. However, the Labour Party was bitterly divided on the subject of devolution. An amendment to the Scotland Act that had been proposed by Labour MP <a href="/wiki/George_Cunningham_(MP)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Cunningham (MP)">George Cunningham</a>, who shortly afterwards defected to the newly formed Social Democratic Party (SDP), required 40% of the total electorate to vote in favour of an assembly. Despite officially favouring it, considerable numbers of Labour members opposed the establishment of an assembly. This division contributed to only a narrow \'Yes\' majority being obtained, and the failure to reach Cunningham\'s 40% threshold. The 18 years of Conservative government, under <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> and then <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a>, saw strong resistance to any proposal for devolution for <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.\n</p><p>In response to Conservative dominance, in 1989 the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Constitutional_Convention" title="Scottish Constitutional Convention">Scottish Constitutional Convention</a> was formed encompassing the Labour Party, <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regions_and_districts_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Regions and districts of Scotland">local authorities</a>, and sections of "civic Scotland" like <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Trades_Union_Congress" title="Scottish Trades Union Congress">Scottish Trades Union Congress</a>, the Small Business Federation and <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> and the other major churches in Scotland. Its purpose was to devise a scheme for the formation of a devolution settlement for Scotland. The SNP decided to withdraw as independence was not a constitutional option countenanced by the convention. The convention produced its final report in 1995.\n</p><p>In May 1997, the <a href="/wiki/Labour_party_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour party (UK)">Labour</a> government of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> was elected with a promise of creating devolved institutions in Scotland. In late 1997, a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_devolution_referendum,_1997" title="Scottish devolution referendum, 1997">referendum</a> was held which resulted in a "yes" vote. The newly created <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> (as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a>) has powers to make primary <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislation</a> in all areas of policy which are not expressly \'reserved\' for the UK Government and parliament such as national defence and international affairs.\n</p><p>Devolution for Scotland was justified on the basis that it would make government more representative of the people of Scotland. It was argued that the population of Scotland felt detached from the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Government">Westminster government</a> (largely because of the policies of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_party_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative party (UK)">Conservative</a> governments led by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a>).\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">referendum on Scottish independence</a> was held on 18 September 2014, with the referendum being defeated 44.7% (Yes) to 55.3% (No). In the 2015 UK General Election the SNP won 56 of the 59 Scottish seats with 50% of all Scottish votes. This saw the SNP replace the Liberal Democrats as the third largest in the UK Parliament.\n</p><p>In the 2016 Scottish Parliament election the SNP fell 2 seats short of an overall majority  with 63 seats but remained in government for a third term. The Conservative Party won 31 seats and became the second largest party for the first time. The Labour Party down to 24 seats from a 38 fell to third place. The Scottish Greens took 6 seat and overtook the Liberal Democrats who remained flat on 5 seats.\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">2016 referendum on EU membership</a>, where Scotland and Northern Ireland voted to Remain and <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> voted to Leave (leading to a <a href="/wiki/Results_of_the_United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="Results of the United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">52% Leave vote nationwide</a>), the Scottish Parliament voted for a <a href="/wiki/Proposed_second_Scottish_independence_referendum" title="Proposed second Scottish independence referendum">second independence referendum</a> to be held once conditions of the UK\'s EU exit are known. Conservative Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a> to date has rejected this request citing a need to focus on <a href="/wiki/Brexit_negotiations" title="Brexit negotiations">EU exit negotiations</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Wales.svg" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Wales.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing Wales</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Welsh_National_Assembly_Senedd.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Welsh_National_Assembly_Senedd.jpg/220px-Welsh_National_Assembly_Senedd.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Welsh_National_Assembly_Senedd.jpg/440px-Welsh_National_Assembly_Senedd.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Welsh_National_Assembly_Senedd.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Senedd" title="Senedd">Senedd</a>, home to the National Assembly for Wales</div></div></div>\n<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542</a>, Wales was treated in legal terms as part of England. However, during the later part of the 19th century and early part of the 20th century the notion of a distinctive Welsh <a href="/wiki/Polity" title="Polity">polity</a> gained credence. In 1881 the <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Closing_(Wales)_Act_1881" title="Sunday Closing (Wales) Act 1881">Sunday Closing (Wales) Act 1881</a> was passed, the first such legislation exclusively concerned with Wales. The Central Welsh Board was established in 1896 to inspect the grammar schools set up under the Welsh Intermediate Education Act 1889, and a separate Welsh Department of the Board of Education was formed in 1907. The Agricultural Council for Wales was set up in 1912, and the Ministry of Agriculture and Fisheries had its own Welsh Office from 1919.\n</p><p>Despite the failure of popular political movements such as <a href="/wiki/Cymru_Fydd" title="Cymru Fydd">Cymru Fydd</a>, a number of institutions, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Eisteddfod" class="mw-redirect" title="National Eisteddfod">National Eisteddfod</a> (1861), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wales" title="University of Wales">University of Wales</a> (Prifysgol Cymru) (1893), the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Wales" title="National Library of Wales">National Library of Wales</a> (Llyfrgell Genedlaethol Cymru) (1911) and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Guards" title="Welsh Guards">Welsh Guards</a> (Gwarchodlu Cymreig) (1915) were created. The campaign for disestablishment of the Anglican Church in Wales, achieved by the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a>, was also significant in the development of Welsh political consciousness. <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> was formed in 1925 with the goal of securing a Welsh-speaking Wales but initially its growth was slow and it gained few votes at parliamentary elections.\n</p><p>An appointed <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Wales_and_Monmouthshire" title="Council for Wales and Monmouthshire">Council for Wales and Monmouthshire</a> was established in 1949 to "ensure the government is adequately informed of the impact of government activities on the general life of the people of Wales". The council had 27 members nominated by local authorities in Wales, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wales" title="University of Wales">University of Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Eisteddfod_of_Wales" title="National Eisteddfod of Wales">National Eisteddfod Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Tourist_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Tourist Board">Welsh Tourist Board</a>. A cross-party Parliament for Wales campaign in the early 1950s was supported by a number of Labour MPs, mainly from the more Welsh-speaking areas, together with the Liberal Party and Plaid Cymru. A post of Minister of Welsh Affairs was created in 1951 and the post of <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> were established in 1964 leading to the abolition of the Council for Wales and Monmouthshire.\n</p><p>Labour\'s incremental embrace of a distinctive Welsh polity was arguably catalysed in 1966 when Plaid Cymru president <a href="/wiki/Gwynfor_Evans" title="Gwynfor Evans">Gwynfor Evans</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen_by-election" title="Carmarthen by-election">Carmarthen by-election</a>. In response to the emergence of Plaid Cymru and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> (SNP) <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>\'s Labour Government set up the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Royal Commission on the Constitution</a> (the Kilbrandon Commission) to investigate the UK\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional arrangements in 1969. The 1974\xe2\x80\x9379 Labour Government proposed a Welsh Assembly in parallel to its proposals for Scotland. These were rejected by voters in the <a href="/wiki/Wales_referendum,_1979" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales referendum, 1979">Wales referendum, 1979</a>, with 956,330 votes against, compared with 243,048 for.\n</p><p>In May 1997, the <a href="/wiki/Labour_party_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour party (UK)">Labour</a> government of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> was elected with a promise of creating a devolved assembly in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Wales_referendum,_1997" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales referendum, 1997">Wales referendum, 1997</a>, resulted in a "yes" vote. The <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>, as a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a>, possesses the power to determine how the <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> budget for Wales is spent and administered. The 1998 Act was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> which created an executive body, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly Government">Welsh Assembly Government</a>, separate from the legislature, the National Assembly for Wales. It also conferred on the National Assembly some limited legislative powers.\n</p><p>In Wales the 1997 referendum on devolution was only narrowly passed, and most voters rejected devolution in all the counties bordering England, as well as <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a>. However, all recent opinion polls indicate an increasing level of support for further devolution, with support for some tax varying powers now commanding a majority, and diminishing support for abolition of the Assembly.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_2011" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 2011">March 2011 referendum</a> in Wales saw a majority of 21 local authority constituencies to 1 voting in favour of more legislative powers being transferred from the UK parliament in Westminster to the Welsh Assembly. The turnout was 35.4% with 517,132 votes (63.49%) in favour and 297,380 (36.51%) against increased legislative power.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Devolution_in_Wales" title="Commission on Devolution in Wales">Commission on Devolution in Wales</a> was set up in October 2011 to consider further devolution of powers from London. The commission issued a report on the devolution of fiscal powers in November 2012 and a report on the devolution of legislative powers in March 2014. The fiscal recommendations formed the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Act_2014" title="Wales Act 2014">Wales Act 2014</a>, while majority of the legislative recommendations were put into law by the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Act_2017" title="Wales Act 2017">Wales Act 2017</a>.\n</p><p>The National Assembly is set to be renamed the Welsh Parliament (Senedd Cymru in Welsh) in 2020.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Devolved_English_parliament" title="Devolved English parliament">Devolved English parliament</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:English_regions_2009.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/English_regions_2009.svg/220px-English_regions_2009.svg.png" width="220" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/English_regions_2009.svg/330px-English_regions_2009.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/English_regions_2009.svg/440px-English_regions_2009.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1108" data-file-height="1345" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:English_regions_2009.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the informal <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">regions of England</a> as defined in 2009</div></div></div>\n<p>England is the only country of the United Kingdom to not have a devolved Parliament or Assembly and English affairs are decided by the Westminster Parliament. Devolution for England was proposed in 1912 by the <a href="/wiki/Dundee_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Dundee (UK Parliament constituency)">Member of Parliament for Dundee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, as part of the debate on <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Home Rule for Ireland</a>. In a speech in <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> on 12 September, Churchill proposed that the government of England should be divided up among regional parliaments, with power devolved to areas such as Lancashire, Yorkshire, the Midlands and London as part of a federal system of government.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-churchill1912_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-churchill1912-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The division of England into provinces or regions was explored by several post-<a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> royal commissions. The <a href="/wiki/Redcliffe-Maud_Report" title="Redcliffe-Maud Report">Redcliffe-Maud Report</a> of 1969 proposed devolving power from central government to eight provinces in England. In 1973 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)</a> proposed the creation of eight English appointed regional assemblies with an advisory role; although the report stopped short of recommending legislative devolution to England, a minority of signatories wrote a memorandum of dissent which put forward proposals for devolving power to elected assemblies for Scotland, Wales and five Regional Assemblies in England.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 1994 the <a href="/wiki/Second_Major_ministry" title="Second Major ministry">Government of John Major</a> created a set of ten <a href="/wiki/Government_Office" title="Government Office">Government Office Regions</a> for England to coordinate central government departments at a provincial level.<sup id="cite_ref-audretsch_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audretsch-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Regional_development_agency" title="Regional development agency">English Regional Development Agencies</a> were set up in 1998 under the <a href="/wiki/Blair_ministry" title="Blair ministry">Government of Tony Blair</a> to foster economic growth around England. These Agencies were supported by a set of eight newly created <a href="/wiki/Regional_assembly_(England)" title="Regional assembly (England)">Regional Assemblies</a>, or Chambers. These bodies were not directly elected but members were appointed by local government and local interest groups.\n</p><p>English Regional Assemblies were abolished between 2008 and 2010, but proposals to replace them were put forward. Following devolution of power to Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland in 1998, the government proposed similar decentralisation of power across England.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Following a <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority_referendum,_1998" title="Greater London Authority referendum, 1998">referendum in 1998</a>, a directly elected administrative body was created for Greater London, the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a>. Proposals to devolve political power to fully elected bodies English Regional Assemblies was put to public vote in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_England_devolution_referendums,_2004" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern England devolution referendums, 2004">Northern England devolution referendums, 2004</a>. Originally three referendums were planned, but following a decisive rejection of the plans by voters in <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>, further referendums were abandoned. Although moves towards English regional devolution were called off, the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">Regions of England</a> continue to be used in certain governmental administrative functions.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There have been proposals for the establishment of a single <a href="/wiki/Devolved_English_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Devolved English Parliament">devolved English Parliament</a> to govern the affairs of England as a whole. This has been supported by groups such as English Commonwealth, the <a href="/wiki/English_Democrats" title="English Democrats">English Democrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_an_English_Parliament" title="Campaign for an English Parliament">Campaign for an English Parliament</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> who have both expressed support for greater autonomy for all four nations while ultimately striving for a dissolution of the Union. Without its own devolved Parliament, England continues to be governed and legislated for by the UK Government and UK Parliament which gives rise to the <a href="/wiki/West_Lothian_question" title="West Lothian question">West Lothian question</a>. The question concerns the fact that, on devolved matters, Scottish MPs continue to help make laws that apply to England alone, although no English MPs can make laws on those same matters for Scotland. Since the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum, 2014</a> there has been a wider debate about the UK adopting a federal system with each of the four <a href="/wiki/Home_nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Home nations">home nations</a> having its own, equal devolved legislatures and law-making powers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the first five years of devolution for Scotland and Wales, support in England for the establishment of an English parliament was low at between 16 and 19 per cent.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> While a 2007 opinion poll found that 61 per cent would support such a parliament being established,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> a report based on the <a href="/wiki/British_Social_Attitudes_Survey" title="British Social Attitudes Survey">British Social Attitudes Survey</a> published in December 2010 suggests that only 29 per cent of people in England support the establishment of an English parliament, though this figure has risen from 17 per cent in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Curtice" title="John Curtice">John Curtice</a> argues that tentative signs of increased support for an English parliament might represent "a form of English nationalism...beginning to emerge among the general public".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Krishan Kumar, however, notes that support for measures to ensure that only English MPs can vote on legislation that applies only to England is generally higher than that for the establishment of an English parliament, although support for both varies depending on the timing of the opinion poll and the wording of the question.<sup id="cite_ref-Kumar_2010_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kumar_2010-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2011 it was announced that the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">British government</a> was to set up a commission to examine the West Lothian question.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In January 2012 it was announced that this six-member commission would be named the <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_the_consequences_of_devolution_for_the_House_of_Commons" title="Commission on the consequences of devolution for the House of Commons">Commission on the consequences of devolution for the House of Commons</a>, would be chaired by former <a href="/wiki/Clerk_of_the_House_of_Commons" title="Clerk of the House of Commons">Clerk of the House of Commons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_McKay" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir William McKay">Sir William McKay</a>, and would have one member from each of the devolved countries.  The McKay Commission reported in March 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcnews250313_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcnews250313-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_votes_for_English_laws">English votes for English laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: English votes for English laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 22 October 2015 The House of Commons voted in favour of implementing a system of "<a href="/wiki/English_votes_for_English_laws" title="English votes for English laws">English votes for English laws</a>" by 312 votes to 270 after four hours of intense debate. Amendments to the proposed standing orders put forward by both Labour and The Liberal Democrats were defeated.  Scottish National Party MPs criticized the measures stating that the bill would render Scottish MPs as "second class citizens".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Under the new procedures, if the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" title="Speaker of the House of Commons (United Kingdom)">Speaker of The House</a> determines if a proposed bill or <a href="/wiki/Statutory_Instrument_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutory Instrument (UK)">statutory instrument</a> exclusively affects England, England and Wales or England, Wales and Northern Ireland, then legislative consent should be obtained via a <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Grand_Committee" title="Legislative Grand Committee">Legislative Grand Committee</a>. This process will be performed at the second reading of a bill or instrument and is currently undergoing a trial period, as an attempt at answering the <a href="/wiki/West_Lothian_question" title="West Lothian question">West Lothian question</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_London">Greater London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Greater London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Within England, <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">regional</a> devolution has only extended to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a> has greater powers than other local authority bodies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornwall">Cornwall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cornwall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Assembly" title="Cornish Assembly">Cornish Assembly</a></div>\n<p>There is a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_self-government_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish self-government movement">movement that supports devolution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. A law-making Cornish Assembly is party policy for the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Greens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ld_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ld-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> A Cornish Constitutional Convention was set up in 2001 with the goal of establishing a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Assembly" title="Cornish Assembly">Cornish Assembly</a>. Several Cornish Liberal Democrat MPs such as <a href="/wiki/Andrew_George_(politician)" title="Andrew George (politician)">Andrew George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rogerson" title="Dan Rogerson">Dan Rogerson</a> and former MP <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Taylor_(Liberal_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew Taylor (Liberal politician)">Matthew Taylor</a> are strong supporters of Cornish devolution.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 12 December 2001, the Cornish Constitutional Convention and Mebyon Kernow submitted a 50,000-strong petition supporting devolution in Cornwall to <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> This was over 10% of the Cornish electorate, the figure that the government had stated was the criteria for calling a referendum on the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In December 2007 Cornwall Council leader David Whalley stated that "There is something inevitable about the journey to a Cornish Assembly".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A poll carried out by <a href="/wiki/Survation" title="Survation">Survation</a> for the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Exeter" title="University of Exeter">University of Exeter</a> in November 2014 found that 60% were in favour of power being devolved from Westminster to Cornwall, with only 19% opposed and 49% were in favour of the creation of a Cornish Assembly, with 31% opposed.<sup id="cite_ref-Western_Morning_News_-_27_November_2014_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Western_Morning_News_-_27_November_2014-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 2015 <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Shadow_Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Shadow Chancellor of the Exchequer">Shadow Chancellor</a> promised the delivery of a Cornish assembly in the next parliament if Labour are elected. <a href="/wiki/Ed_Balls" title="Ed Balls">Ed Balls</a> made the statement whilst on a visit to <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_College" title="Cornwall College">Cornwall College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a> and it signifies a turn around in policy for the Labour party who in government prior to 2010 voted against the Government of Cornwall Bill 2008-09.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Yorkshire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Yorkshire Devolution Movement is an all party and no party campaign group, while the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Party" title="Yorkshire Party">Yorkshire Party</a> is a political party. Both campaign for devolution to <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, which has a population of 5.4 million \xe2\x80\x93 similar to Scotland \xe2\x80\x93 and whose economy is roughly twice as large as that of Wales. Arguments for devolution to Yorkshire focus on the area as a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural region">cultural region</a> or even a <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a> separate from England,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> whose inhabitants share common features.  In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (United Kingdom)">European Parliament election in 2014</a>, Yorkshire First attained 1.47% of the vote (19,017 total votes).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Northern_England_.28as_whole.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_England_(as_whole)">Northern England (as whole)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Northern England (as whole)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Party" title="Northern Party">Northern Party</a> seeks to establish a Regional Government for the <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">North of England</a> covering the six historic counties of the region.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The Campaign aims to create a Northern Government with tax-raising powers and responsibility for policy areas including economic development, education, health, policing and emergency services.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/North_East_England_devolution_referendum,_2004" title="North East England devolution referendum, 2004">2004</a>, a referendum on devolution for <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a> took place, in which devolution was defeated 78% to 22%.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_dependencies">Crown dependencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Crown dependencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg/120px-British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg.png" width="120" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg/180px-British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg/240px-British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="675" data-file-height="1180" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Channel_Islands.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing the Channel Islands</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg/120px-British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg.png" width="120" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg/180px-British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg/240px-British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="675" data-file-height="1180" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Isles_Isle_of_Man.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing the Isle of Man</div></div></div>\n<p>The legislatures of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a> are not devolved as their origins predate the establishment of the United Kingdom and their attachment to the <a href="/wiki/British_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="British Crown">British Crown</a>, and the Crown Dependencies are not part of the United Kingdom. However, the United Kingdom has redefined its formal relationship with the Crown Dependencies since the late 20th century.\n</p><p>Crown dependencies are possessions of the British Crown, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Overseas_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas territories">overseas territories</a> or <a href="/wiki/Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonies">colonies</a> of the United Kingdom. They comprise the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Channel Island">Channel Island</a> <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick" title="Bailiwick">bailiwicks</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> in the Irish Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For several hundred years, each has had its own separate legislature, government and judicial system. However, as possessions of the Crown they are not sovereign nations in their own right and the British Government is responsible for the overall good governance of the islands and represents the islands in international law. Acts of the UK Parliament are normally only extended to the islands only with their specific consent.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Each of the islands is represented on the <a href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="British-Irish Council">British-Irish Council</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a>, a post in the UK Government, is responsible for relations between the government and the Channel Islands. All insular legislation must be approved by the <a href="/wiki/Queen_in_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen in Council">Queen in Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> is responsible for proposing the legislation on the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a>. He can refuse to propose insular legislation or can propose it for the Queen\'s approval.\n</p><p>In 2007\xe2\x80\x932008, each Crown Dependency and the UK signed agreements<sup id="cite_ref-gov.je_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.je-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> that established frameworks for the development of the international identity of each Crown Dependency. Among the points clarified in the agreements were that:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the UK has no democratic accountability in and for the Crown Dependencies which are governed by their own democratically elected assemblies;</li>\n<li>the UK will not act internationally on behalf of the Crown Dependencies without prior consultation;</li>\n<li>each Crown Dependency has an international identity that is different from that of the UK;</li>\n<li>the UK supports the principle of each Crown Dependency further developing its international identity;</li>\n<li>the UK recognises that the interests of each Crown Dependency may differ from those of the UK, and the UK will seek to represent any differing interests when acting in an international capacity; and</li>\n<li>the UK and each Crown Dependency will work together to resolve or clarify any differences that may arise between their respective interests.</li></ul>\n<p>Jersey has moved further than the other two Crown dependencies in asserting its autonomy from the United Kingdom. The preamble to the States of Jersey Law 2005 declares that \'it is recognized that Jersey has autonomous capacity in domestic affairs\' and \'it is further recognized that there is an increasing need for Jersey to participate in matters of international affairs\'.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In July 2005, the Policy and Resources Committee of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Jersey" title="States of Jersey">States of Jersey</a> established the Constitutional Review Group, chaired by Sir <a href="/wiki/Philip_Bailhache" title="Philip Bailhache">Philip Bailhache</a>, with terms of reference \'to conduct a review and evaluation of the potential advantages and disadvantages for Jersey in seeking independence from the United Kingdom or other incremental change in the constitutional relationship, while retaining the Queen as Head of State\'. The Group\'s \'Second Interim Report\' was presented to the States by the Council of Ministers in June 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In January 2011, one of Jersey\'s Council of Ministers was for the first time designated as having responsibility for external relations and is often described as the island\'s \'minister\'.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Proposals for Jersey independence have not, however, gained significant political or popular support.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In October 2012 the Council of Ministers issued a "Common policy for external relations"<sup id="cite_ref-External_policy_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-External_policy-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> that set out a number of principles for the conduct of external relations in accordance with existing undertakings and agreements. This document noted that Jersey "is a self-governing, democratic country with the power of self-determination" and "that it is not Government policy to seek independence from the United Kingdom, but rather to ensure that Jersey is prepared if it were in the best interests of Islanders to do so". On the basis of the established principles the Council of Ministers decided to "ensure that Jersey is prepared for external change that may affect the Island\xe2\x80\x99s formal relationship with the United Kingdom and/or European Union".\n</p><p>There is also public debate in Guernsey about the possibility of independence.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, however, an official group reached the provisional view that becoming a <a href="/wiki/Microstate" title="Microstate">microstate</a> would be undesirable <sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> and it is not supported by Guernsey\'s Chief Minister.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2010, the governments of Jersey and Guernsey jointly created the post of director of European affairs, based in Brussels, to represent the interests of the islands to European Union policy-makers.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2010 the Lieutenant Governors of each Crown dependency have been recommended to the Crown by a panel in each respective Crown dependency; this replaced the previous system of the appointments being made by the Crown on the recommendation of UK ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competences_of_devolved_administrations">Competences of devolved administrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Competences of devolved administrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Northern Ireland, Scotland &amp;
