class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>\n\n<b>Transport in the United Kingdom</b> is facilitated with road, air, rail, and water networks. A radial road network totals 29,145 miles (46,904&#160;km) of main roads, 2,173 miles (3,497&#160;km) of motorways and 213,750 miles (344,000&#160;km) of paved roads. The <a href="/wiki/National_Rail" title="National Rail">National Rail</a> network of 10,072 route miles (16,116&#160;km) in Great Britain and 189 route miles (303 route km) in Northern Ireland carries over 18,000 passenger and 1,000 freight trains daily. <a href="/wiki/Commuter_rail_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Commuter rail in the United Kingdom">Urban rail networks</a> exist in <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Suburban_Rail" class="mw-redirect" title="Belfast Suburban Rail">Belfast</a>, Birmingham, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, Edinburgh, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, Liverpool, London and <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>. There are many regional and international airports, with <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a> in London being one of the busiest in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-geography.about.com_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geography.about.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The UK also has a network of ports which received over 558 million tons of class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Transport_modal_share_from_1952-2014.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Transport_modal_share_from_1952-2014.png/330px-Transport_modal_share_from_1952-2014.png" width="330" height="192" class="thumbimage" <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Transport_modal_share_from_1952-2014.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Transport modal share from 1952 to 2016 showing the initial rise in car use which then peaked and started a gentle decline as rail use increased in the past twenty years<sup id="cite_ref-transportstats_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transportstats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Since 1952 (the earliest date for which comparable figures are available), the United Kingdom saw a growth of car use, which increased its <a href="/wiki/Modal_share" title="Modal share">modal share</a>, while the use of buses declined, and railway use has grown more slowly.<sup id="cite_ref-ec2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ec2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eddington_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddington-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, since the 1990s, rail has started increasing its modal share at the expense of cars, increasing from 5% to 10% of passenger-kilometres travelled.<sup id="cite_ref-transportstats_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transportstats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This coincided with the <a href="/wiki/Privatisation_of_British_Rail" title="Privatisation of British Rail">privatisation of British Rail</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Impact_of_the_privatisation_of_British_Rail" title="Impact of the privatisation of British Rail">its impact is heavily debated</a>.\n</p><p>In 1952, 27% of distance travelled was by car or taxi; with 42% being by bus or coach and 18% by rail. A further 11% was by bicycle and 3% by motorcycle. The distance travelled by air was negligible.\n</p><p>By 2015 83% of distance travelled was by car or taxi; with 5% being by bus and 10% by rail. Air, pedal cycle and motorcycle accounted for roughly 1% each.<sup id="cite_ref-transportstats_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transportstats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In terms of passenger-kilometres, slightly over 662&#160;billion were made by cars, motorcycles vans and taxis, 78&#160;billion by rail, 39&#160;billion by bus, 5&#160;billion by pedal cycle and 9&#160;billion on domestic air flights.<sup id="cite_ref-transportstats_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transportstats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Passenger transport has grown in recent years. Figures from the DfT show that total passenger travel inside the United Kingdom has risen from 403&#160;billion passenger kilometres in 1970 to 793&#160;billion in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-transportstats_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transportstats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Freight transport has undergone similar changes, increasing in volume and shifting from railways onto the road. In 1953 89&#160;billion tonne kilometres of goods were moved, with rail accounting for 42%, road 36% and water 22%. By 2010 the volume of freight moved had more than doubled to 222&#160;billion tonne kilometres, of which 9% was moved by rail, 19% by water, 5% by pipeline and 68% by road.<sup id="cite_ref-dft2011_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dft2011-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Despite the growth in tonne kilometres, the environmental external costs of trucks and lorries in the UK have reportedly decreased. Between 1990 and 2000, there has been a move to heavier goods vehicles due to major changes in the haulage industry including a shift in sales to larger articulated vehicles. A larger than average fleet turnover has ensured a swift introduction of new and cleaner vehicles in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-AEAT_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEAT-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the decline in railway use led to a reduction in the length of the rail network, the length of the road network has not increased in proportion to the increase in road use. Whereas the rail network has halved from 19,471&#160;mi (31,336&#160;km) in 1950 to 10,014&#160;mi (16,116&#160;km) today,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2014)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the major road network only increased from 44,710&#160;mi (71,950&#160;km) in 1951 to 50,893&#160;mi (81,904&#160;km) in 1990, and reduced slightly to 50,265&#160;mi (80,894&#160;km) by 2010.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Department for Transport</a> stated that <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a> is one of the most serious transport problems facing the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-dft_congestion_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dft_congestion-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> According to the government-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Eddington_Transport_Study" title="Eddington Transport Study">Eddington report</a> of 2006, bottleneck roads are in serious danger of becoming so congested that it may damage the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_transport">Air transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a There are also 11 <a href="/wiki/Heliport" title="Heliport">heliports</a>. <i>(2004 <a href="/wiki/CIA_World_Factbook" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA World Factbook">CIA</a> estimates)</i>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a> is the largest airport by traffic volume in the UK, is owned by <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport_Holdings" title="Heathrow Airport Holdings">Heathrow Airport Holdings</a>, and is one of the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_busiest_airport" class="mw-redirect" title="World&#39;s busiest airport">world\'s busiest airports</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">Gatwick Airport</a>, the second largest, and is owned by <a href="/wiki/Global_Infrastructure_Partners" title="Global Infrastructure Partners">Global Infrastructure Partners</a>.  The third largest is <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport" title="Manchester Airport">Manchester Airport</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, which is run by <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Airport Group">Manchester Airport Group</a>, which also owns various other airports.\n</p><p>Other major airports include <a href="/wiki/Stansted_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Stansted Airport">Stansted Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Essex,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Essex, England">Essex</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Luton_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Luton Airport">London Luton Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Bedfordshire, England">Bedfordshire</a>, both about 30 miles (48&#160;km) north of London, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Airport" title="Birmingham Airport">Birmingham Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Solihull" title="Metropolitan Borough of Solihull">Solihull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_Airport" title="Newcastle Airport">Newcastle Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_John_Lennon_Airport" title="Liverpool John Lennon Airport">Liverpool Airport</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Airport" title="Bristol Airport">Bristol Airport</a>.\n</p><p>Outside England, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Airport" title="Cardiff Airport">Cardiff Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Airport" title="Edinburgh Airport">Edinburgh Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belfast_International_Airport" title="Belfast International Airport">Belfast International Airport</a>, are the busiest airports serving Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland respectively.\n</p><p>The largest airline in the United Kingdom by passenger traffic is <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">easyJet</a>, whereas <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> is largest by fleet size and international destinations. Others include <a href="/wiki/Flybe" title="Flybe">Flybe</a>, <a href="/wiki/BMI_Regional" class="mw-redirect" title="BMI Regional">BMI Regional</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet2" class="mw-redirect" title="Jet2">Jet2</a>, <a href="/wiki/TUI_Airways" title="TUI Airways">TUI Airways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Atlantic_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Atlantic Airways">Virgin Atlantic</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_transport">Rail transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transport_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rail transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<dl><dd><i>Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_Great_Britain" title="Rail transport in Great Britain">Rail transport in Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_Ireland" title="Rail transport in Ireland">Rail transport in Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Rapid transit in the United Kingdom">Rapid transit in the United Kingdom</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>The rail network in the United Kingdom consists of two independent parts, that of Northern Ireland and that of Great Britain. Since 1994, the latter has been connected to <a href="/wiki/Mainland_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Mainland Europe">mainland Europe</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Tunnel" title="Channel Tunnel">Channel Tunnel</a>. The network of Northern Ireland is connected to that of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>. The National Rail network of 10,072 miles (16,209&#160;km) in Great Britain and 189&#160;route&#160;miles (303&#160;route&#160;km) in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> carries 1.7&#160;billion passengers and 110 million tonnes of freight annually.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UICRS_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UICRS-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Urban rail networks are also well developed in London and several other cities. There were once over 30,000 miles (48,000&#160;km) of rail network in the UK; however, most of this was reduced over a time period from 1955 to 1975, much of it after a report by a government advisor <a href="/wiki/Richard_Beeching" title="Richard Beeching">Richard Beeching</a> in the mid-1960s (known as the <a href="/wiki/Beeching_Axe" class="mw-redirect" title="Beeching Axe">Beeching Axe</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transport_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png/360px-GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png" width="360" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png/540px-GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png/720px-GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png 2x" data-file-width="1079" data-file-height="649" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GBR_rail_passengers_by_year_1830-2015.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rail Passengers in Great Britain from 1829 to 2016, showing the early era of small companies, the amalgamation into the "Big Four", nationalisation and finally the current era of privatisation</div></div></div>\n<p>The rail network in Great Britain is the oldest such network in the world. The system consists of five high-speed main lines (the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Main_Line" title="West Coast Main Line">West Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midland_Main_Line" title="Midland Main Line">Midland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Main_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Western Main Line">Great Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Eastern_Main_Line" title="Great Eastern Main Line">Great Eastern</a>), which radiate from London to the rest of the country, augmented by regional rail lines and dense commuter networks within the major cities. <a href="/wiki/High_Speed_1" title="High Speed 1">High Speed 1</a> is operationally separate from the rest of the network, and is built to the same standard as the <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a> system in France.\n</p><p>The world\'s first passenger railway running on steam was the <a href="/wiki/Stockton_and_Darlington_Railway" title="Stockton and Darlington Railway">Stockton and Darlington Railway</a>, opened on 27 September 1825. Just under five years later the world\'s first intercity railway was the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway" title="Liverpool and Manchester Railway">Liverpool and Manchester Railway</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a> and opened by the Prime Minister, the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Duke of Wellington</a> on 15 September 1830. The network grew rapidly as a patchwork of literally hundreds of separate companies during the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, which eventually was consolidated into just four by 1922, as the boom in railways ended and they began to lose money. Eventually, the entire system came under state control in 1948, under the <a href="/wiki/British_Transport_Commission" title="British Transport Commission">British Transport Commission</a>\'s Railway Executive. After 1962 it came under the control of the <a href="/wiki/British_Railways_Board" title="British Railways Board">British Railways Board</a>; then British Railways (later <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Rail</a>), and the network was reduced to less than half of its original size by the infamous <a href="/wiki/Beeching_Axe" class="mw-redirect" title="Beeching Axe">Beeching</a> cuts of the 1960s when many unprofitable branch lines were closed. Several stations have been reopened in <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Wales" title="Transport in Wales">Wales</a>.\n</p><p>In 1994 and 1995, British Rail was split into infrastructure, maintenance, rolling stock, passenger and freight companies, which were <a href="/wiki/Privatised" class="mw-redirect" title="Privatised">privatised</a> from 1996 to 1997. <a href="/wiki/Impact_of_the_privatisation_of_British_Rail" title="Impact of the privatisation of British Rail">The privatisation</a> has delivered very mixed results, with healthy passenger growth, mass refurbishment of infrastructure, investment in new rolling stock, and safety improvements being offset by concerns over network capacity and the overall cost to the taxpayer, which has increased due to growth in passenger numbers. It has also led to some confusion as to who looks after different aspects of the rail service among the general public. This is because, for example, different companies run the tracks to those that run the trains and locomotives. Since privatisation, passenger levels have more than doubled and have surpassed the level they had been at in the late 1940s. But while the price of anytime and off-peak tickets has increased, the price of Advance tickets has dramatically decreased in real terms: the average Advance ticket in 1995 cost \xc2\xa39.14 (in 2014 prices) compared to \xc2\xa35.17 in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Subsidies to the rail industry have slightly increased from \xc2\xa33.7bn in 1992\xe2\x80\x9393 to \xc2\xa33.9bn in 2012\xe2\x80\x9313 (in 2013 prices) but more than halved in terms of subsidy per journey from \xc2\xa34.98 to \xc2\xa32.46.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a depots and signalling chiefly) is owned and maintained by <a href="/wiki/Network_Rail" title="Network Rail">Network Rail</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit organization">not-for-profit company</a>. Network Rail replaced <a href="/wiki/Railtrack" title="Railtrack">Railtrack</a>, which became bankrupt in 2002 following the <a href="/wiki/Hatfield_rail_crash" title="Hatfield rail crash">Hatfield rail crash</a> in 2000. Passenger services are operated by <a href="/wiki/Train_operating_company" title="Train operating company">train-operating companies</a> (TOCs), which are franchises awarded by the United Kingdom Government or the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish government">Scottish government</a>. Examples include <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands_Trains" title="East Midlands Trains">East Midlands Trains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Trains" title="Virgin Trains">Virgin Trains</a>. Freight trains are operated by <a href="/wiki/Freight_operating_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Freight operating company">freight operating companies</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/DB_Schenker_Rail_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="DB Schenker Rail (UK)">DB Schenker Rail (UK)</a>, which are commercial operations unsupported by the government. Most train operating companies do not own the locomotives and coaches that they use to operate passenger services. Instead, they are required to lease these from the three <a href="/wiki/Rolling_stock_operating_company" title="Rolling stock operating company">rolling stock operating companies</a> (ROSCOs), with train maintenance carried out by companies such as <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Transportation" title="Bombardier Transportation">Bombardier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a>.\n</p><p>In Great Britain there are 10,274 miles (16,534&#160;km) of <span class="nowrap">1,435&#160;mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">4&#160;ft&#160;<span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in</span>) <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard gauge">gauge</a> track, reduced from a historic peak of over 30,000 miles (48,000&#160;km). Of this, 3,062 miles (4,928&#160;km) is electrified and 7,823 miles (12,590&#160;km) is <a href="/wiki/Double_track" class="mw-redirect" title="Double track">double</a> or multiple tracks. The maximum scheduled speed on the regular network has historically been around 125 miles per hour (201&#160;km/h) on the <a href="/wiki/InterCity_(British_Rail)" title="InterCity (British Rail)">InterCity</a> lines. On <a href="/wiki/High_Speed_1" title="High Speed 1">High Speed 1</a>, trains are now able to reach the speeds of French <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGVs</a>.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/High_Speed_2" title="High Speed 2">High Speed 2</a> line running north from London has been proposed and the legislation needed for the first phase is (as of May 2014) proceeding through parliament. <a href="/wiki/Network_Rail" title="Network Rail">Network Rail</a> are considering reopening a <a href="/wiki/Exeter_to_Plymouth_railway_of_the_LSWR" title="Exeter to Plymouth railway of the LSWR">railway</a> in south-west England connecting Tavistock to Okehampton and Exeter as an alternative to the coastal mainline which was damaged at <a href="/wiki/Dawlish" title="Dawlish">Dawlish</a> by coastal storms in February 2014, causing widespread disruption.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To cope with increasing passenger numbers, there is a large ongoing <a href="/wiki/History_of_rail_transport_in_Great_Britain_1995_to_date#Timeline" title="History of rail transport in Great Britain 1995 to date">programme of upgrades</a> to the network, including <a href="/wiki/Thameslink_Programme" title="Thameslink Programme">Thameslink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crossrail" title="Crossrail">Crossrail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_in_Great_Britain" title="Railway electrification in Great Britain">electrification of lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Rail_Traffic_Management_System_in_Great_Britain" title="European Rail Traffic Management System in Great Britain">in-cab signalling</a>, new <a href="/wiki/Intercity_Express_Programme" title="Intercity Express Programme">inter-city trains</a> and a new <a href="/wiki/HS2" class="mw-redirect" title="HS2">high-speed line</a>.\n</p><p>The Office for Rail and Road (ORR) are the regulator for Network Rail, they are currently overseeing funding for Control Period 6 (CP6) \xe2\x80\x93 from 1 April 2019 to 31 March 2024.\n</p>\n<h3><span cities in the United Kingdom have <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">rapid transit</a> systems. The most well known is the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> (commonly known as the Tube), the oldest rapid transit system in the world (opened 1863). Another system also in London is the separate <a href="/wiki/Docklands_Light_Railway" title="Docklands Light Railway">Docklands Light Railway</a> (opened 1987). (114,656&#160;km) of "B" and "C" roads, and 145,017 miles (233,382&#160;km) of unclassified roads (mainly local streets and access roads) \xe2\x80\x93 totalling 247,523 miles (398,350&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbg_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbg-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Road is the most popular method of transport in the United Kingdom, carrying over 90% of motorised passenger travel and 65% of domestic freight.<sup id="cite_ref-bbg_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbg-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The major motorways and <a href="/wiki/Trunk_roads" class="mw-redirect" title="Trunk roads">trunk roads</a>, many of which are <a href="/wiki/Dual_carriageway" title="Dual carriageway">dual carriageway</a>, form the trunk network which links all cities and major towns, these carry about one third of the nation\'s traffic, and occupy about 0.16% of its land area.<sup id="cite_ref-bbg_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbg-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The motorway system, which was constructed from the 1950s onwards, is stated by the <a href="/wiki/British_Chambers_of_Commerce" title="British Chambers of Commerce">British Chambers of Commerce</a> to be, by virtually every measurement of motorway capacity, well below the capacity of other leading\nEuropean nations.<sup id="cite_ref-bcc_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bcc-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> They give comparative figures for a selection of nations of (units = km/million population): United Kingdom 60, Luxembourg 280, Spain 225, Austria 200, France 185, Belgium 165, Denmark 165, Sweden 165, Netherlands 140, Germany 140, Italy 115, Finland 100, Portugal 80, Greece 45 and Ireland 30.<sup id="cite_ref-bcc_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bcc-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Highways_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Highways Agency">Highways Agency</a> (an <a href="/wiki/Executive_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive Agency">Executive Agency</a> of the Department for Transport) is responsible for maintaining motorways and trunk roads in England. Other English roads are maintained by <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local authorities</a>. In Scotland and Wales roads are the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Transport_Scotland" title="Transport Scotland">Transport Scotland</a>, an Executive Agency of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish government">Scottish government</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly Government">Welsh Assembly Government</a> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-admin_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-admin-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Northern Ireland\'s roads are overseen by the <a href="/wiki/Roads_Service_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Roads Service Northern Ireland">Roads Service Northern Ireland</a>, a section of the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Regional_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Regional Development">Department for Regional Development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-admin_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-admin-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In London, <a href="/wiki/Transport_for_London" title="Transport for London">Transport for London</a> is responsible for all trunk roads and other major roads, which are part of the <a href="/wiki/Transport_for_London_Road_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Transport for London Road Network">Transport for London Road Network</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-admin_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-admin-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">Toll roads</a> are rare in the United Kingdom, though there are many <a href="/wiki/Toll_bridge" title="Toll bridge">toll bridges</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Severn_crossing" title="Severn crossing">Severn crossing</a>. Road traffic congestion has been identified as a key concern for the future prosperity of the United Kingdom, and policies and measures are being investigated and developed by the government to ameliorate its effects.<sup id="cite_ref-dft_congestion_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dft_congestion-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2003, the United Kingdom\'s first toll motorway, the <a href="/wiki/M6_Toll" title="M6 Toll">M6 Toll</a>, opened in the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(region)" title="West Midlands (region)">West Midlands</a> area to relieve the congested <a href="/wiki/M6_motorway" title="M6 motorway">M6 motorway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Rod Eddington, in his 2006 <i>Transport\'s role in sustaining the UK\'s productivity and competitiveness</i> report, recommended that the congestion problem should be tackled with a "sophisticated policy mix" of <a href="/wiki/Congestion_pricing" title="Congestion pricing">congestion-targeted road pricing</a> and improving the capacity and performance of the transport network through infrastructure investment and better use of the existing network.<sup id="cite_ref-eddington_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddington-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Congestion_charging" class="mw-redirect" title="Congestion charging">Congestion charging</a> systems do operate in the cities of <a href="/wiki/London_congestion_charge" title="London congestion charge">London</a><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Durham_city_congestion_charge" class="mw-redirect" title="Durham city congestion charge">Durham</a><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and on the <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Crossing" title="Dartford Crossing">Dartford Crossing</a>.  In 2005, the Government published proposals for a United Kingdom-wide <a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">road pricing</a> scheme. This was designed to be <a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue_neutral&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Revenue neutral (page does not exist)">revenue neutral</a> with other motoring taxes to be reduced to compensate.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The plans were extremely controversial with 1.8&#160;million people signing a petition against them.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Driving is on the left.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 29 April 2015, the <a href="/wiki/UK_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Supreme Court">UK Supreme Court</a> ruled that the government ClientEarth.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_lanes">Cycle lanes</span><span In London the <a href="/wiki/Woolwich_Ferry" title="Woolwich Ferry">Woolwich Ferry</a> links the <a href="/wiki/North_Circular_Road" title="North Circular Road">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Circular_Road,_London" title="South Circular Road, London">South</a> circular roads. <a href="/wiki/Gosport" title="Gosport">Gosport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> are linked by the <a href="/wiki/Gosport_Ferry" title="Gosport Ferry">Gosport Ferry</a>; <a href="/wiki/North_Shields" title="North Shields">North Shields</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Shields" title="South Shields">South Shields</a> on <a href="/wiki/Tyneside" title="Tyneside">Tyneside</a> are linked by the <a href="/wiki/Shields_Ferry" title="Shields Ferry">Shields Ferry</a>; and the Mersey has the <a href="/wiki/Mersey_Ferry" title="Mersey Ferry">Mersey Ferry</a>.\n</p><p>In Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_MacBrayne" title="Caledonian MacBrayne">Caledonian MacBrayne</a> provides passenger and <a href="/wiki/RO-RO" class="mw-redirect" title="RO-RO">RO-RO</a> ferry services in the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Firth of Clyde</a>, to various islands from <a href="/wiki/Oban" title="Oban">Oban</a> and within the inner and outer <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>. <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Ferries" title="Orkney Ferries">Orkney Ferries</a> provides services within the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Isles">Orkney Isles</a>; and <a href="/wiki/NorthLink_Ferries" title="NorthLink Ferries">NorthLink Ferries</a> provides services from the Scottish mainland to Orkney and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, mainly from <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> although other ports are also used. Ferries operate to Northern Ireland from <a href="/wiki/Stranraer_Harbour_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Stranraer Harbour railway station">Stranraer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairnryan" title="Cairnryan">Cairnryan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Larne" title="Larne">Larne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>, in Wales, is the principal ferry port for Wales and England to Dublin and formerly <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAn_Laoghaire" title="D\xc3\xban Laoghaire">D\xc3\xban Laoghaire</a>, in Ireland.\n</p><p>Passenger ferries operate internationally to nearby countries such as France, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and Spain. Ferries usually originate from one of the following:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a> with services to <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a> operated by <a href="/wiki/P%26O_Ferries" title="P&amp;O Ferries">P&amp;O Ferries</a>, <a href="/wiki/My_Ferry_Link" class="mw-redirect" title="My Ferry Link">My Ferry Link</a> and <a href="/wiki/DFDS_Seaways" title="DFDS Seaways">DFDS Seaways</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_International_Port" title="Portsmouth International Port">Portsmouth International Port</a> is the main hub for longer services on the Western Channel to <a href="/wiki/Ouistreham" title="Ouistreham">Ouistreham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Le_Havre" title="Le Havre">Le Havre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherbourg" class="mw-redirect" title="Cherbourg">Cherbourg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Malo" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Malo">Saint Malo</a> in France, and <a href="/wiki/Santander,_Spain" title="Santander, Spain">Santander</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a> in Spain. Most services are operated by French company <a href="/wiki/Brittany_Ferries" title="Brittany Ferries">Brittany Ferries</a> with some to Cherbourg and Le Havre by <a href="/wiki/Condor_Ferries" title="Condor Ferries">Condor Ferries</a> and DFDS Seaways.</li>\n<li>Services from <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> operate to Santander (Spain) and <a href="/wiki/Roscoff" title="Roscoff">Roscoff</a> (France) with Brittany Ferries.</li>\n<li>Services from <a href="/wiki/Weymouth,_Dorset" title="Weymouth, Dorset">Weymouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a> operate to the Channel Islands with <a href="/wiki/Condor_Ferries" title="Condor Ferries">Condor Ferries</a> and to Cherbourg (from Poole) with Brittany Ferries.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a> is the principal ferry port for services to Ireland, operated by <a href="/wiki/Irish_Ferries" title="Irish Ferries">Irish Ferries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stena_Line" title="Stena Line">Stena Line</a> with some services originating from <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_Dock" title="Pembroke Dock">Pembroke</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Port_of_Hull" title="Port of Hull">Hull</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harwich" title="Harwich">Harwich</a> are the main hubs for services to the Netherlands.</li></ul>\n<p>More services from <a href="/wiki/Ramsgate" title="Ramsgate">Ramsgate</a>, Liverpool, <a href="/wiki/Newhaven,_East_Sussex" title="Newhaven, East Sussex">Newhaven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymington" title="Lymington">Lymington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishguard" title="Fishguard">Fishguard</a> operate to Ireland, France, Belgium and the Isle of Wight.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Waterbus" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterbus">Waterbuses</a> operate on rivers in some of the country\'s largest cities such as London (<a href="/wiki/London_River_Services" title="London River Services">London River Services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thames_Clippers" title="Thames Clippers">Thames Clippers</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Waterbus" title="Cardiff Waterbus">Cardiff Waterbus</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bristol_Ferry_Boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Bristol Ferry Boat">Bristol Ferry Boat</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_shipping">Other shipping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span private yachts in the world.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inland_waterways">Inland waterways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transport_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Inland waterways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canals_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Canals of the United Kingdom">canals of the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>Major <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a> building began in the United Kingdom after the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">Industrial revolution</a> in the 18th century. A large canal network was built and it became the primary method of transporting goods throughout the country; however, by the 1830s with the development of the railways, the canal network began to go into decline.\n</p><p>There are currently 1,988 miles (3,199&#160;km) of <a href="/wiki/Waterways_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Waterways in the United Kingdom">waterways in the United Kingdom</a> and the primary use is recreational. 385 miles (620&#160;km) is used for commerce. <i>(2004 CIA estimate)</i>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_professional_development">Education and professional development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transport_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Education and professional development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The United Kingdom also has a well-developed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
