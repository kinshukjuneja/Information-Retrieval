/></a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Central Saint Martins</b>, often abbreviated to <b>CSM</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_education" title="Tertiary education">tertiary</a> <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">art school</a> in London, England. It is a constituent college of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Arts_London" title="University of the Arts London">University of the Arts London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bus_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-times_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It offers full-time courses at foundation, <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Postgraduate">postgraduate</a> levels, and a variety of short and summer courses.\n</p><p>It was formerly known as <b>Central Saint Martins College of Arts and Design</b>, and before that as <b>Central Saint Martins College of Art and Design</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-aim2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aim2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Saint_Martins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Central Saint Martins College of Art and Design was formed in 1989 from the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Central_School_of_Art_and_Design" title="Central School of Art and Design">Central School of Art and Design</a>, founded in 1896, and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Martin%27s_School_of_Art" title="Saint Martin&#39;s School of Art">Saint Martin\'s School of Art</a>, founded in 1854.<sup id="cite_ref-aim2_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aim2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-alex_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alex-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Since 1986 both schools had been part of the <a href="/wiki/London_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="London Institute">London Institute</a>, formed by the <a href="/wiki/Inner_London_Education_Authority" title="Inner London Education Authority">Inner London Education Authority</a> to bring together seven London art, design, fashion and media schools.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The London Institute became a legal entity in 1988, could award taught degrees from 1993, was granted university status in 2003 and was renamed University of the Arts London in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It also includes <a href="/wiki/Camberwell_College_of_Arts" title="Camberwell College of Arts">Camberwell College of Arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_College_of_Arts" title="Chelsea College of Arts">Chelsea College of Arts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/London_College_of_Communication" title="London College of Communication">London College of Communication</a>, the <a href="/wiki/London_College_of_Fashion" title="London College of Fashion">London College of Fashion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wimbledon_College_of_Arts" title="Wimbledon College of Arts">Wimbledon College of Arts</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Drama_Centre_London" title="Drama Centre London">Drama Centre London</a>, founded in 1963, joined Central Saint Martins in 1999 as an integral school, maintaining its name and teaching approaches. The <a href="/wiki/Byam_Shaw_School_of_Art" title="Byam Shaw School of Art">Byam Shaw School of Art</a>, founded in 1910, was merged into Central Saint Martins in 2003.\n</p>\n<h3><span data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="727" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Central_Saint_Martins_College_of_Art_and_Design_-_Blue_Plaque.jpg" href="/wiki/Central_School_of_Art_and_Design" title="Central School of Art and Design">Central School of Art and Design</a></div>\n<p>The Central School of Art and Design was established as the Central School of Arts and Crafts in 1896 by <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London County Council</a>. It grew directly from the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a> of <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>. The first principal, from 1896 until 1911, was <a href="/wiki/William_Richard_Lethaby" class="mw-redirect" title="William Richard Lethaby">William Richard Lethaby</a>; a <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> in his memory was erected in 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-plaque_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plaque-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The school was at first housed in Morley Hall, rented from the <a href="/wiki/Regent_Street_Polytechnic" class="mw-redirect" title="Regent Street Polytechnic">Regent Street Polytechnic</a>. It moved to purpose-built premises in <a href="/wiki/Southampton_Row" title="Southampton Row">Southampton Row</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Camden" title="London Borough of Camden">London Borough of Camden</a>, in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-oxford_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the same year the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Female_School_of_Art" title="Royal Female School of Art">Royal Female School of Art</a>, established in 1842, was merged into the school.<sup id="cite_ref-aim25b_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aim25b-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Central became part of the London Institute in 1986, and merged with Saint Martin\'s in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-frieze_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frieze-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Saint_Martin.27s_School_of_Art"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Martin\'s_School_of_Art">Saint Martin\'s School of Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Saint_Martins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Saint Martin&#039;s School of Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Saint_Martin%27s_School_of_Art" title="Saint Martin&#39;s School of Art">Saint Martin\'s School of Art</a></div>\n<p>Saint Martin\'s School of Art was established in 1854 by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Mackenzie_(bishop)" title="Henry Mackenzie (bishop)">Henry Mackenzie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vicar" title="Vicar">vicar</a> of the church of <a href="/wiki/St_Martin-in-the-Fields" title="St Martin-in-the-Fields">St Martin-in-the-Fields</a>. It became independent from the church in 1859.<sup id="cite_ref-oxford2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Frank Martin became head of the <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a> department in 1952; he brought in young sculptors and recent graduates of the department as teachers. Among these, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Caro" title="Anthony Caro">Anthony Caro</a> was particularly influential. The group around him came to be known as the New Generation of British sculptors and the sculpture department of Saint Martin\'s became, in the words of <a href="/wiki/Tim_Scott_(artist)" title="Tim Scott (artist)">Tim Scott</a>: "the most famous in the art world".<sup id="cite_ref-mclean_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Saint Martin\'s became part of the London Institute in 1986, and merged with Central in 1989.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drama_Centre_London">Drama Centre London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Saint_Martins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Drama Centre London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drama_Centre_London" title="Drama Centre London">Drama Centre London</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Drama_Centre_London" title="Drama Centre London">Drama Centre London</a> was founded in 1963 by a breakaway group of teachers and students from the <a href="/wiki/Central_School_of_Speech_and_Drama" class="mw-redirect" title="Central School of Speech and Drama">Central School of Speech and Drama</a>, led by John Blatchley, <a href="/wiki/Yat_Malmgren" title="Yat Malmgren">Yat Malmgren</a> and Christopher Fettes. The school is a member of <a href="/wiki/Drama_UK" title="Drama UK">Drama UK</a><sup id="cite_ref-dramauk_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dramauk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and its undergraduate <a href="/wiki/Acting" title="Acting">Acting</a> course is accredited by Drama UK.<sup id="cite_ref-dramauk2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dramauk2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Drama Centre London merged with Central Saint Martins in 1999.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Byam_Shaw_School_of_Art">Byam Shaw School of Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Saint_Martins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Byam Shaw School of Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Byam_Shaw_School_of_Art" title="Byam Shaw School of Art">Byam Shaw School of Art</a></div>\n<p>Byam Shaw School of Art was founded by the artists <a href="/wiki/Byam_Shaw" title="Byam Shaw">John Byam Shaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rex_Vicat_Cole" title="Rex Vicat Cole">Rex Vicat Cole</a> in 1910 as a school of <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>. It was originally located in Campden Street, <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a>, and moved to larger premises in <a href="/wiki/Archway,_London" title="Archway, London">Archway</a> in 1990. It was subsumed by Central Saint Martins in 2003.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_assessment">Awards and assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Saint_Martins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards and assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1998 the London Institute received a <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Anniversary_Prize" title="Queen&#39;s Anniversary Prize">Queen\'s Anniversary Prize</a> for the "massive contribution" of Central Saint Martins College of Art &amp; Design to the growth of the fashion industry in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-royal_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royal-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The University of the Arts London received a Queen\'s Anniversary Prize in 2013, for the contribution of CSM industrial and product design graduates to commerce, industry and the design profession.<sup id="cite_ref-rat_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rat-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>CSM does not receive independent assessment in the <i><a href="/wiki/Complete_University_Guide" class="mw-redirect" title="Complete University Guide">Complete University Guide</a></i> league tables, but is ranked as part of the University of the Arts London. In 2014 the university received an overall ranking of 67 out of 124 institutions, with a rank of 102 for graduate prospects and 123 for student satisfaction with teaching.<sup id="cite_ref-indepedent_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indepedent-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cug_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cug-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In 2018 it was placed 83rd out of 129 universities, with a rank of 125 for student satisfaction.<sup id="cite_ref-cug2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cug2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools_and_location">Schools and location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Saint_Martins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Schools and location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Teaching at Central Saint Martins is organised into nine programmes, which include acting, art, design, fashion, graphics and jewellery and textiles, as well as foundation courses.<sup id="cite_ref-programmes_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-programmes-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Central Saint Martins moved to a converted warehouse complex on <a href="/wiki/Granary_Square" title="Granary Square">Granary Square</a> at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Cross,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="King&#39;s Cross, London">King\'s Cross</a> in 2011. Most of the college is housed there, but it also uses the former Byam Shaw building in Elthorne Road, <a href="/wiki/Archway,_London" title="Archway, London">Archway</a>, and premises in Richbell Place, <a href="/wiki/Holborn" title="Holborn">Holborn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-locations_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-locations-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
