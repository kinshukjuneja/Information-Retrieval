represented a very small part of <a href="/wiki/Electricity_sector_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Electricity sector in the United Kingdom">electricity production in United Kingdom</a> until 2011. The installed base has increased rapidly in recent years as a result of reductions in the cost of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> (PV) panels, and the introduction of a <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariff</a> (FIT) subsidy in April 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In 2012, the government announced that 4 million homes across the UK will be powered by the sun within eight years,<sup id="cite_ref-Fiona_Harvey_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fiona_Harvey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> representing 22 GW of installed solar power capacity by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\nThe 72MW<sub>(DC)</sub> Shotwick Solar Farm is the largest in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of May 2017, there is a total installed capacity of 12,318&#160;<a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">megawatt (MW)</a> of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">PV</a> solar power,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DECC-statistics-PV_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DECC-statistics-PV-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> placing the United Kingdom in sixth place internationally in terms of total installed capacity. In 2017 the UK generated 3.4% of its total electricity using solar power.<sup id="cite_ref-DECC-trends2018_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DECC-trends2018-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\nThe all-time peak generation from photovoltaics was 9.42GW on 14 May 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_power_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2006, the United Kingdom had installed about 12 MW of photovoltaic capacity<sup id="cite_ref-ER1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ER1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and represented only 0.3% of total <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy_in_the_European_Union" title="Solar energy in the European Union">European solar PV</a> of 3,400&#160;MW.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> \nIn August 2006 there was widespread news coverage in the United Kingdom of the major high street electrical retailers <a href="/wiki/Currys" title="Currys">Currys</a>\' decision to stock PV modules, manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Sharp_Corporation" title="Sharp Corporation">Sharp</a>, at a cost of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\xc2\xa3</a>1,000 per module. \nThe retailer also provided an installation service.\n</p><p>Solar power use increased very rapidly in subsequent years, as a result of reductions in the cost of PV panels, and the introduction of a FIT subsidy in April 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> \nThe introduction of the feed-in-tariff (FiT) in 2010 saw rapid growth of the UK photovoltaic market, with many thousands of domestic installations along with numerous commercial, community and industrial projects.\n</p><p>The FiT were cut in the fast track review announced by DECC on 9&#160;June 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> \nAs a result, large arrays of solar photovoltaics became a much less attractive investment opportunity for developers, especially for projects greater than 250&#160;kW, so large field arrays such as these were less likely to be built beyond the 1&#160;August 2011 cut off date, at least not until 2012, when PV prices reduced somewhat - a utility scale solar farm is paid 8.9 p/kWhr generated.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> \nThe first solar park in Wales became operational in 2011 at <a href="/wiki/Rhoshill,_Pembrokeshire" title="Rhoshill, Pembrokeshire">Rhosygilwen</a>, north <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  \nAt the end of 2011, there were 230,000 solar power projects in the United Kingdom,<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> with a total installed generating capacity of 750 MW.<sup id="cite_ref-epia2012_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-epia2012-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 13&#160;July 2011, construction of the largest solar park in the United Kingdom was completed in <a href="/wiki/Newark-on-Trent" title="Newark-on-Trent">Newark-on-Trent</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>. \nThe 4.9&#160;MW free-field system was built in just seven weeks after being granted planning permission. \nThe system generates an estimated 4,860&#160;MW\xc2\xb7h of electricity (an average power of 560&#160;kW) into the national grid each year.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\nThere are several other examples of 4\xe2\x80\x935&#160;MW field arrays of photovoltaics in the UK, including the 5&#160;MW Langage Solar Park, the 5&#160;MW Westmill Solar Farm, the 4.51&#160;MW Marsten Solar Farm and Toyota\'s 4.6&#160;MW plant in Burnaston, Derbyshire.<sup id="cite_ref-spp-5478_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spp-5478-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first large solar farm in the United Kingdom, a 32&#160;MW solar farm, began construction in November 2012. \nIt is located in <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>, between the runways of the former military airfield, Wymeswold.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> By February 2012 the installed capacity had reached 1,000&#160;MW.<sup id="cite_ref-Jonathan_Gifford_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jonathan_Gifford-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> \nIn 2012, the government said that 4 million homes across the UK will be powered by the sun within eight years.<sup id="cite_ref-Fiona_Harvey_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fiona_Harvey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The government expects Britain to have 22 <a href="/wiki/Gigawatts" class="mw-redirect" title="Gigawatts">gigawatts</a> of installed solar power capacity by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of September 2013, retailer <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">Ikea</a> announced that solar panel packages for houses would be sold at 17 United Kingdom stores by July 2014. The decision followed a successful pilot project at the Thurrock Ikea store, during which one <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> (PV) system was sold almost every day. The panels are manufactured by the Chinese company <a href="/wiki/Hanergy" title="Hanergy">Hanergy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This partnership did not last and in October 2015 Ikea ended its relationship with Hanergy.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of June 2014 there were 18 schemes generating more than 5&#160;MW and 34 in planning or construction in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\nBy 2016 the total installed capacity was over 10,000 MW.\nIn the summer half-year from April to September 2016, UK solar panels produced more electricity (6,964 GWh) than did coal power (6,342 GWh). Each is about 5% of demand.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_potential">Solar potential</span><span in the UK and on the European continent (different colour scale)</div></div></div>\n<p>The UK\'s annual <a href="/wiki/Insolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Insolation">insolation</a> is in the range of 750\xe2\x80\x931,100 <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hour" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilowatt-hour">kilowatt-hours</a> per square metre (kWh/m\xc2\xb2). \nLondon receives 0.52 and 4.74 kWh/m\xc2\xb2 per day in December and July, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> \nWhile the sunniest parts of the UK receive much less solar radiation than the sunniest parts of Europe, the country\'s insolation in the south is comparable with that of central European countries, including <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Germany" title="Solar power in Germany">Germany</a>, which generates about 7% of its electricity from solar power.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> \nAdditionally, the UK\'s higher wind speeds cool PV modules, leading to higher efficiencies than could be expected at these levels of insolation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Energy_and_Climate_Change" title="Department of Energy and Climate Change">Department of Energy and Climate Change</a> (DECC) assumes an average capacity factor of 9.7% for solar photovoltaics in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Derry Newman, chief executive of <a href="/wiki/Solarcentury" title="Solarcentury">Solarcentury</a>, argues that the UK\'s "famously overcast weather does not make it an unsuitable place for solar power, as solar panels work on daylight, not necessarily direct sunlight."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Some solar cells work better in direct sunlight, others can use more diffuse light. While insolation rates are lower in England than France and Spain, they are still usable.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Many of the solar panels can be <a href="/wiki/List_of_monitored_photovoltaic_power_stations" class="mw-redirect" title="List of monitored photovoltaic power stations">monitored</a> on the internet, such as the <a href="/wiki/Slepe" title="Slepe">Slepe</a> Farm in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, a 492&#160;kW solar field.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_programmes">Government programmes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_power_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Government programmes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Energy_Saving_Trust" title="Energy Saving Trust">Energy Saving Trust</a> that administers government grants for domestic photovoltaic systems, the <a href="/wiki/Low_Carbon_Building_Programme" title="Low Carbon Building Programme">Low Carbon Building Programme</a>, estimates that an installation for an average-sized house would cost between \xc2\xa35,000\xe2\x80\x93\xc2\xa38,000, with most domestic systems usually between 1.5 and 3&#160;kWp, and yield annual savings between \xc2\xa3150 and \xc2\xa3200.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Green Energy for Schools programme will be providing 100 schools across the UK with solar panels. The first school in Wales was the Tavernspite School, near <a href="/wiki/Whitland" title="Whitland">Whitland</a>, which has received panels worth \xc2\xa320,000, sufficient to produce 3,000&#160;kW\xc2\xb7h of electricity each year.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feed-in_tariff">Feed-in tariff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_power_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feed-in tariff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariffs_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Feed-in tariffs in the United Kingdom">Feed-in tariffs in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<p>Discussion on implementation of a feed-in tariff programme concluded on 26&#160;September 2008, and the results were published in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The government in the UK agreed in April 2010 to pay for all grid-connected generated electricity at an initial rate of up to 41.3p (US$0.67) per kWh, whether used locally or exported.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> \nThe rates proved more attractive than necessary, and in August 2011, were drastically reduced for installations over 50&#160;kW,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> \na policy change criticized as marking "the end of the UK\xe2\x80\x99s solar industry as we know it".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Feed In Tariff rates are adjusted annually by the government<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>. \nAs of 8 February 2016, the rate is 4.39 pence per kWh of power generated for domestic systems of 4kWp (p means peak i.e. the maximum power that the system can produce) or less and where homes meet the minimum <a href="/wiki/Energy_Performance_Certificate" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy Performance Certificate">EPC</a> requirement of band D.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  The Export Tariff is 4.85 pence per kWh exported to the grid.  The amount of electricity exported is not usually measured for domestic installations. It is calculated by assuming that 50% of the electricity produced is exported into the grid.\n</p><p>The Department of Business Energy and Industrial Strategy (BEIS) published a consultation on 19 July 2018. In this, they state their intention to close the Feed-in Tariff scheme to new applicants from 1 April 2019<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and will not be replaced by a new subsidy.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Net_metering">Net metering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_power_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Net metering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Net metering is only available from one company, Eastern Energy, where it is referred to as "SolarNet".<sup
