International</b> (<b>ODI</b>) is a form of <a href="/wiki/Limited_overs_cricket" title="Limited overs cricket">limited overs</a> <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(sport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cricket (sport)">cricket</a>, played between two teams with international status, in which each team faces a fixed number of <a href="/wiki/Over_(cricket)" title="Over (cricket)">overs</a>, usually 50. The <a href="/wiki/Cricket_World_Cup" title="Cricket World Cup">Cricket World Cup</a> is played in this format, which is generally held every four years. One Day International matches are also called <b>Limited Overs Internationals</b> (<b>LOI</b>), although this generic term may also refer to <a href="/wiki/Twenty20_International" title="Twenty20 International">Twenty20 International</a> matches. They are major matches and considered the highest standard of <a href="/wiki/List_A_cricket" title="List A cricket">List A</a>, limited overs competition.\n</p><p>The international one-day game is a late-twentieth-century development. The first ODI was played on 5 January 1971 between Australia and England at the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Cricket_Ground" title="Melbourne Cricket Ground">Melbourne Cricket Ground</a>. When the first three days of the third <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test</a> were washed out officials decided to abandon the match and, instead, play a one-off one-day game consisting of 40 eight-ball overs per side. Australia won the game by 5 wickets. ODIs were played in white kits with a red ball.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the late 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Packer" title="Kerry Packer">Kerry Packer</a> established the rival <a href="/wiki/World_Series_Cricket" title="World Series Cricket">World Series Cricket</a> competition, and it introduced many of the features of One Day International cricket that are now commonplace, including coloured uniforms, matches played at night under floodlights with a white ball and dark sight screens, and, for television broadcasts, multiple camera angles, effects microphones to capture sounds from the players on the pitch, and on-screen graphics. The first of the matches with coloured uniforms was the WSC Australians in wattle gold versus WSC West Indians in coral pink, played at <a href="/wiki/VFL_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="VFL Park">VFL Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> on 17 January 1979. This led not only to Packer\'s <a href="/wiki/Nine_Network" title="Nine Network">Channel 9</a> getting the TV rights to cricket in Australia but also led to players worldwide being paid to play, and becoming international professionals, no longer needing jobs outside cricket. Matches played with coloured kits and a white ball became more commonplace over time, and the use of white flannels and a red ball in ODIs ended in 2001.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Australia_vs_India.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Australia_vs_India.jpg/220px-Australia_vs_India.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Australia_vs_India.jpg/330px-Australia_vs_India.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Australia_vs_India.jpg/440px-Australia_vs_India.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Australia_vs_India.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Cricket_Ground" title="Melbourne Cricket Ground">Melbourne Cricket Ground</a> hosts an ODI match between Australia and India. href="/w/index.php?title=One_Day_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the main the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of cricket">Laws of cricket</a> apply. However, in ODIs, each team bats for a fixed number of <a href="/wiki/Over_(cricket)" title="Over (cricket)">overs</a>. In the early days of ODI cricket, the number of overs was generally 60 overs per side, and matches were also played with 40, 45 or 55 overs per side, but now it has been uniformly fixed at 50 overs.\n</p><p>Simply stated, the game works as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-ODIRules_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODIRules-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>An ODI is contested by two teams of 11 players each.</li>\n<li>The Captain of the side winning the <a href="/wiki/Coin_flipping" title="Coin flipping">toss</a> chooses to either bat or <a href="/wiki/Bowling_(cricket)" title="Bowling (cricket)">bowl</a> (field) first.</li>\n<li>The team batting first sets the target score in a single <a href="/wiki/Innings" title="Innings">innings</a>. The innings lasts until the batting side is "all out" (i.e., 10 of the 11 batting players are "out") or all of the first side\'s allotted overs are completed.</li>\n<li>Each bowler is restricted to bowling a maximum of 10 overs (fewer in the case of rain-reduced matches and in any event generally no more than one fifth or 20% of the total overs per innings). Therefore, each team must comprise at least five competent bowlers (either dedicated bowlers or all-rounders).</li>\n<li>The team batting second tries to score more than the target score in order to win the match. Similarly, the side bowling second tries to bowl out the second team or make them exhaust their overs before they reach the target score in order to win.</li>\n<li>If the number of runs scored by both teams is equal when the second team <a href="/wiki/Wicket#Dismissing_a_batsman" title="Wicket">loses all its wickets</a> or exhausts all its overs, then the game is declared a <i>tie</i> (regardless of the number of wickets lost by either team).</li></ul>\n<p>Where a number of overs are lost, for example, due to inclement weather conditions, then the total number of overs may be reduced. In the early days of ODI cricket, the team with the better run rate won (see <a href="/wiki/Average_Run_Rate_method" title="Average Run Rate method">Average Run Rate method</a>), but this favoured the second team.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> For the <a href="/wiki/1992_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1992 Cricket World Cup">1992 World Cup</a>, an alternative method was used of simply omitting the first team\'s worst overs (see <a href="/wiki/Most_Productive_Overs_method" title="Most Productive Overs method">Most Productive Overs method</a>), but that favoured the first team.<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Since the late 1990s, the target or result is usually determined by the <a href="/wiki/Duckworth-Lewis_method" class="mw-redirect" title="Duckworth-Lewis method">Duckworth-Lewis (DL) method</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Methods_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methods-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which is a method with statistical approach. It takes into consideration the fact that the wickets in hand plays a crucial role in pacing the <a href="/wiki/Run_rate" title="Run rate">run-rate</a>.  In other words, a team with more wickets in hand can play way more aggressively than the team with fewer wickets in hand. When insufficient overs are played to apply the Duckworth-Lewis method, a match is declared no result. Important one-day matches, particularly in the latter stages of major tournaments, may have two days set aside, such that a result can be achieved on the "reserve day" if the first day is washed out\xe2\x80\x94either by playing a new game, or by resuming the match which was rain-interrupted. The original DL-method however had a few inherent flaws. For example, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Lewis_(mathematician)" title="Tony Lewis (mathematician)">Tony Lewis</a>, one of the formulators of this method recognized after the match between India and Kenya during the 1999 World Cup held in Bristol, that the original method gave an unfair advantage to the team chasing scores above 350 runs in a 50 overs match (and likewise scores in other formats). Hence, the method was revised and a new version was released in 2004. There was one more such change made which was first implemented on 2009. Off late (as up to 9 August 2018), the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-DLS-method-in-cricket-and-how-does-it-work">Duckworth-Lewis-Stern (DLS) method</a> is widely used, which is a modification of the DL-Method suggested by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Stern" title="Steven Stern">Prof. Steven Stern</a>. It was first implemented during the <a href="/wiki/2015_Cricket_World_Cup" title="2015 Cricket World Cup">2015 World Cup.</a> One of the major changes made to DLS from DL method was based on a historic analysis by Prof. Stern that a team with higher run rate in their initial stages has a greater chance to get to a high score than a team with slow initial run rate, but more wickets in hand.\n</p><p>Because the game uses a white ball instead of the red one used in <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class cricket</a>, the ball can become discoloured and hard to see as the innings progresses, so the ICC has used various rules to help keep the ball playable. Most recently, ICC has made the use of two new balls (one from each end), the same strategy that was used in the 1992 and <a href="/wiki/1996_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1996 Cricket World Cup">1996</a> World Cups so that each ball is used for only 25 overs.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Previously, in October 2007, the ICC sanctioned that after the 34th over, the ball would be replaced with a cleaned previously-used ball.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Before October 2007 (except 1992 and 1996 World Cups), only one ball would be used during an innings of an ODI and it was up to the umpire to decide whether to change the ball.<sup id="cite_ref-ODIRules_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODIRules-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fielding_restrictions_and_powerplays">Fielding restrictions and powerplays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=One_Day_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fielding restrictions and powerplays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Powerplay_(cricket)" title="Powerplay (cricket)">Powerplay (cricket)</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cricket_field_parts.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Cricket_field_parts.svg/220px-Cricket_field_parts.svg.png" width="220" height="242" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Cricket_field_parts.svg/330px-Cricket_field_parts.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Cricket_field_parts.svg/440px-Cricket_field_parts.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="2200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cricket_field_parts.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A limited number of fielders are allowed in the outfield during powerplays.</div></div></div>\n<p>The bowling side is subjected to <a href="/wiki/Fielding_restrictions_(cricket)" title="Fielding restrictions (cricket)">fielding restrictions</a> during an ODI, in order to prevent teams from setting wholly defensive fields. Fielding restrictions dictate the maximum number of fieldsmen allowed to be outside the thirty-yard circle.\n</p><p>Under current ODI rules, there are three levels of fielding restrictions:\n</p>\n<ul><li>In the first 10 overs of an innings (the <i>mandatory <a href="/wiki/Powerplay_(cricket)" title="Powerplay (cricket)">powerplay</a></i>), the fielding team may have at most two fielders outside the 30-yard circle.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Between 11 and 40 overs four fielders will be allowed to field outside the 30-yard circle.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In final 10 overs five fielders will be allowed to field outside the 30-yard circle.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Where a match is shortened by rain, the duration of the powerplays is adjusted to equal 30% of the team\'s overs wherever possible (20% for the first powerplay, 10% for the second).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=One_Day_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Fielding restrictions were first introduced in the Australian 1980\xe2\x80\x9381 season.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By 1992, only two fieldsmen were allowed outside the circle in the first fifteen overs, then five fieldsmen allowed outside the circle for the remaining overs.<sup id="cite_ref-1992newrules_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1992newrules-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This was shortened to ten overs in 2005, and two five-over powerplays were introduced, with the bowling team having discretion over the timing for both. In 2008, the batting team was given discretion for the timing of one of the two powerplays. In 2011, the teams were restricted to completing the discretionary powerplays between the 16th and 40th overs; previously, the powerplays could take place at any time between the 11th and 50th overs. Finally, in 2012, the bowling powerplay was abandoned, and the number of fielders allowed outside the 30-yard circle during non-powerplay overs was reduced from five to four.<sup id="cite_ref-ODIRules_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODIRules-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NEWRULES_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEWRULES-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial_regulations">Trial regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=One_Day_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trial regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The trial regulations also introduced a substitution rule that allowed the introduction of a replacement player at any stage in the match and until he was called up to play he assumed the role of 12th man. Teams nominated their replacement player, called a <i>Supersub</i>, before the toss. The Supersub could bat, bowl, field or keep wicket once a player was replaced; the replaced player took over the role of 12th man. Over the six months it was in operation, it became very clear that the Supersub was of far more benefit to the side that won the toss, unbalancing the game. Several international captains reached "gentleman\'s agreements" to discontinue this rule late in 2005. They continued to name Supersubs, as required, but they did not field them by simply using them as a normal 12th man. This was originally introduced to allow the best associate members to gain regular experience in internationals before making the step up to full membership. First Bangladesh and then Kenya received this status. Bangladesh have since made the step up to Test status and full membership; but as a result of disputes and poor performances, Kenya\'s ODI status was reduced to temporary in 2005, meaning that it had to perform well at World Cup Qualifiers to keep ODI status. Kenya lost ODI status after finishing in fifth place at the <a href="/wiki/2014_Cricket_World_Cup_Qualifier" title="2014 Cricket World Cup Qualifier">2014 Cricket World Cup Qualifier</a> event.<sup It has mainly featured Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh and India.
