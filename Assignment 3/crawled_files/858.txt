class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:20180620_190248_HDR.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Main gate with porter\'s lodge</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Girton College</b> is one of the <a href="/wiki/Colleges_of_the_University_of_Cambridge" title="Colleges of the University of Cambridge">31 constituent colleges</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>. The college was established in 1869 by <a href="/wiki/Emily_Davies" title="Emily Davies">Emily Davies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Bodichon" title="Barbara Bodichon">Barbara Bodichon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Stanley,_Baroness_Stanley_of_Alderley" title="Henrietta Stanley, Baroness Stanley of Alderley">Lady Stanley of Alderley</a> as the first <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_college" title="Women&#39;s college">women\'s college</a> in Cambridge. In 1948, it was granted full college status by the university, marking the official admittance of women to the university. In 1976, it was the first Cambridge women\'s college to become <a href="/wiki/Mixed-sex_education" title="Mixed-sex education">coeducational</a>.\n</p><p>The main College site, situated on the outskirts of the village of <a href="/wiki/Girton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Girton, Cambridgeshire">Girton</a>, about 2.5&#160;miles (4&#160;km) northwest of the university town, comprises 33 acres (13.4&#160;ha) of land. In a typical <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> red brick design, most was built by architect <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Waterhouse" title="Alfred Waterhouse">Alfred Waterhouse</a> between 1872 and 1887. It provides extensive sports facilities, an indoor swimming pool, an award-winning library and a chapel with two organs. There is an accommodation annexe, known as Wolfson Court, situated in Cambridge\'s western suburbs, close to the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Mathematical_Sciences_(Cambridge)" title="Centre for Mathematical Sciences (Cambridge)">Centre for Mathematical Sciences</a>. This annexe was opened in 1961 and provides housing for graduates, and for second-year undergraduates and above.\n</p><p>The College\'s formal governance is led by a <a href="/wiki/Mistress_(college)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mistress (college)">Mistress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_J._Smith" title="Susan J. Smith">Susan J. Smith</a>, who has held the position since 2009.\n</p><p>The College has a tradition of fostering student equality, kept alive with a balanced male-to-female ratio, equal rents for undergraduate rooms irrespective of size (although the rents vary according to matriculation year), and several equal-access admittance schemes. It also has a reputation of encouraging talent in music. Several art collections are held on the main site, including <i>People\'s Portraits</i>, the millennial exhibition of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Portrait_Painters" title="Royal Society of Portrait Painters">Royal Society of Portrait Painters</a>, and an Egyptian collection containing the world\'s most reproduced <a href="/wiki/Fayum_mummy_portraits" title="Fayum mummy portraits">portrait mummy</a>.\n</p><p>Among Girton college\'s notable alumnae are the queen <a href="/wiki/Margrethe_II_of_Denmark" title="Margrethe II of Denmark">Margrethe II of Denmark</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/HuffPost" title="HuffPost">HuffPost</a></i> co-founder <a href="/wiki/Arianna_Huffington" title="Arianna Huffington">Arianna Huffington</a>, the comedian/author <a href="/wiki/Sandi_Toksvig" title="Sandi Toksvig">Sandi Toksvig</a>, the comedian/broadcaster/GP <a href="/wiki/Phil_Hammond_(comedian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Phil Hammond (comedian)">Phil Hammond</a>, the economist <a href="/wiki/Joan_Robinson" title="Joan Robinson">Joan Robinson</a>, and the anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Strathern" title="Marilyn Strathern">Marilyn Strathern</a>, also Mistress from 1998 to 2009.\n</p><p>Its <a href="/wiki/Sister_college" title="Sister college">sister college</a> is <a href="/wiki/Somerville_College,_Oxford" title="Somerville College, Oxford">Somerville College</a>, one of the first two women\'s colleges of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="1869_to_1976:_Pioneering_for_women.27s_education"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1869_to_1976:_Pioneering_for_women\'s_education">1869 to 1976: Pioneering for women\'s education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1869 to 1976: Pioneering for women&#039;s education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The early feminist movement began to argue for the improvement of women\'s education in the 1860s: <a href="/wiki/Emily_Davies" title="Emily Davies">Emily Davies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Bodichon" title="Barbara Bodichon">Barbara Bodichon</a> met through their activism at the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Promoting_the_Employment_of_Women" title="Society for Promoting the Employment of Women">Society for the Employment of Women</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Englishwoman%27s_Review" class="mw-redirect" title="Englishwoman&#39;s Review">Englishwoman\'s Review</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They shared the aim of securing women\'s admission to university.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\nIn particular, they wanted to determine whether girls could be admitted at Oxford or Cambridge to sit the Senior and Junior Local Examinations.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Davies and Bodichon set up a committee to that effect in 1862. In 1865, with the help of Henry Tomkinson, <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College</a> alumnus and owner of an insurance company with good contacts within the University,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 91 female students entered the Cambridge Local Examination.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This first concession to women\'s educational rights met relatively little resistance, as admission to the examination did not imply residence of women at the university site.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg/220px-Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg" width="220" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg/330px-Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg/440px-Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4201" data-file-height="2267" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College_Water_Colour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water colour by the architect of Girton College Alfred Waterhouse</div></div></div>\n<p>At that time, students had the option of doing a Pass degree, which consisted of \'a disorderly collection of fragmented learning\',<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> or an <a href="/wiki/Honours_degree" title="Honours degree">Honours degree</a>, which at that time meant the Mathematics <a href="/wiki/Tripos" title="Tripos">Tripos</a>, classics, natural or moral sciences. An Honours degree was considered more challenging than the Pass degree. In 1869, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sidgwick" title="Henry Sidgwick">Henry Sidgwick</a> helped institute the Examinations for Women, which was designed to be of intermediate difficulty.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This idea was heavily opposed by Emily Davies, as she demanded admittance to the Tripos examinations.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The college was established on 16 October 1869 under the name of the <b>College for Women at Benslow House</b> in <a href="/wiki/Hitchin" title="Hitchin">Hitchin</a>, which was considered to be a convenient distance from Cambridge and London.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It was thought to be less \'risky\' and less controversial to locate the college away from Cambridge in the beginning.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The college was one of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>\'s first residential colleges for women. (<a href="/wiki/Whitelands_College" title="Whitelands College">Whitelands College</a>, now part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Roehampton" title="University of Roehampton">University of Roehampton</a>, was established as a college of higher education for women earlier, in 1841.)\n</p><p>In July and October 1869, entrance examinations were held in London, to which 21 candidates came; 16 passed.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The first term started on 16 October 1869, when five students began their studies.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Adelaide_Manning" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Adelaide Manning">Elizabeth Adelaide Manning</a> was registered as a student and her step-mother <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Manning" title="Charlotte Manning">Charlotte Manning</a> was the first Mistress.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The first three students to unofficially sit the <a href="/wiki/Tripos" title="Tripos">Tripos exams</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lent_term" title="Lent term">Lent term</a> 1873, Rachel Cook and <a href="/wiki/Louisa_Lumsden" title="Louisa Lumsden">Louisa Lumsden</a>, who both took the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tripos" title="Classical Tripos">Classical Tripos</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Woodhead" title="Sarah Woodhead">Sarah Woodhead</a>, who took the <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Tripos" title="Mathematical Tripos">Mathematical Tripos</a>, were known as "The Pioneers".<sup id="cite_ref-College_songs_GC_2010_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_songs_GC_2010-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Through fundraising, \xc2\xa37,000 were collected, which allowed for the purchase of land either at Hitchin or near Cambridge in 1871.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1913,_p.18_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_1913,_p.18-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> By 1872, sixteen acres of land at the present site were acquired near the village of <a href="/wiki/Girton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Girton, Cambridgeshire">Girton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1913,_p.18_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_1913,_p.18-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The college was then renamed Girton College, and opened at the new location in October 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1913,_p.18_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_1913,_p.18-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The buildings had cost \xc2\xa312,000,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and consisted of a single block which comprised the east half of Old Wing.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> At the time, thirteen students were admitted.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1876, Old Wing was completed, and Taylor\'s Knob, the college laboratory and half of Hospital Wing built. In 1884, Hospital Wing was completed, and Orchard Wing, Stanley Library and the Old Kitchens added. At that time, Girton had 80 students. By 1902, Tower Wing, Chapel Wing and Woodlands Wing as well as the Chapel and the Hall were finished, which allowed the college to accommodate 180 students.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College,_Cambridge,_Swimming_Pool.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Girton_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_Swimming_Pool.jpg/220px-Girton_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_Swimming_Pool.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Girton_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_Swimming_Pool.jpg/330px-Girton_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_Swimming_Pool.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Girton_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_Swimming_Pool.jpg/440px-Girton_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_Swimming_Pool.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College,_Cambridge,_Swimming_Pool.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Girton College is the only Cambridge College with an indoor heated swimming pool. It is Grade II listed.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg/220px-Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg/330px-Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg/440px-Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3120" data-file-height="4160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eliza_Baker_Court_Girton_College.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eliza Baker Court</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College_Library.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Girton_College_Library.jpg/220px-Girton_College_Library.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Girton_College_Library.jpg/330px-Girton_College_Library.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Girton_College_Library.jpg/440px-Girton_College_Library.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3120" data-file-height="4160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College_Library.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The College library\'s upper reading room</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1921, a committee was appointed to draft a charter for the college. By summer 1923 the committee had completed the task, and on 21 August 1924 the <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King</a> granted the charter to "the Mistress and Governors of Girton College" as a <a href="/wiki/Body_corporate" class="mw-redirect" title="Body corporate">Body Corporate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Girton was not officially a college yet, nor were its members part of the University. Girton and <a href="/wiki/Newnham_College,_Cambridge" title="Newnham College, Cambridge">Newnham</a> were classed as "recognised institutions for the higher education for women", not colleges of the university. On 27 April 1948, women were admitted to full membership of the University of Cambridge, and Girton College received the status of a college of the university.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1976_to_present:_Pioneering_for_sexual_equality">1976 to present: Pioneering for sexual equality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1976 to present: Pioneering for sexual equality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Social and cultural changes in the <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_of_World_War_II" title="Aftermath of World War II">post-war period</a> led to an increasing number of British universities to become co-educational. In Cambridge, <a href="/wiki/Churchill_College,_Cambridge" title="Churchill College, Cambridge">Churchill College</a>, <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="King&#39;s College, Cambridge">King\'s College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clare_College,_Cambridge" title="Clare College, Cambridge">Clare College</a> were the first men\'s colleges to admit women in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Girton had already amended its statutes in 1971 in such a way as to allow the admission of men should the Governing Body vote in favour at an unspecified date in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The decision to become mixed came in November 1976, when the Governing Body voted to act upon the statute, which made Girton the first women\'s college to admit men.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In January 1977, the first two male <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">Fellows</a>, Frank Wilkinson and John Marks, arrived, followed by male graduate students in 1978, and, finally, undergraduates in October 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> One reason for the change was that the first mixed colleges in Cambridge immediately shot to the top of the Tripos league tables, as they seemed to attract bright students, who preferred to stay in co-educational colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Girton became co-residential as well, which meant that male and female students shared the same facilities. Only one all-female corridor in which rooms were reserved exclusively for women remained. Upon the arrival of male undergraduates, <a href="/wiki/Common_Room_(university)" title="Common Room (university)">JCR and MCR</a> social facilities had to be enlarged. The college bar was opened in 1979 as well as rugby, cricket and soccer pitches provided from 1982 onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On the <a href="/wiki/Tompkins_Table" title="Tompkins Table">Tompkins Table</a>, Girton has averaged at about rank 20 out of 29 colleges in the past 15 years. In 2017, it came 24th, with 21% of all undergraduate students gaining a <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification" title="British undergraduate degree classification">First class</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Trinity_tops_Tompkins_Table_for_seventh_straight_year_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trinity_tops_Tompkins_Table_for_seventh_straight_year-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mistresses">Mistresses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mistresses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mistresses_of_Girton_College,_Cambridge" title="List of Mistresses of Girton College, Cambridge">List of Mistresses of Girton College, Cambridge</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Master_(college)" title="Master (college)">Mistress</a> is the formal head of the college. Her main task is to exercise general superintendence over the college\'s affairs. She presides the College Council and several college committees. The Mistress is elected by the Council, and has to reside at the college precincts for at least two thirds of each term, or 210 days of each academic year.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Ever since the establishment of Girton college, this position has been held by a female, even though male candidates have had equal rights for running for the office since 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_site">Main site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Main site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The initial and defining parts of the college were designed by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Waterhouse" title="Alfred Waterhouse">Alfred Waterhouse</a>: The architect built the main site with the Old Wing, the Hospital Wing, the Orchard Wing, the Stanley library and Old Kitchens between 1873 and 1886,<sup id="cite_ref-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Parapet" title="Parapet">parapetted</a> gatehouse tower in 1886 and 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-EH_1984_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EH_1984-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The red brick design (<a href="/wiki/Brickwork" title="Brickwork">English bond</a>) is typical of <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian architecture</a>, and is enhanced by black mortar courses and terracotta details to the eaves, windows and doorways.<sup id="cite_ref-English_heritage_1981_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_heritage_1981-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The roofs are steeply pitched with crested tiles.<sup id="cite_ref-English_heritage_1981_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_heritage_1981-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In 1913, the site consisted of 33 acres.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg/220px-Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg/330px-Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg/440px-Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3492" data-file-height="4656" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College_Chapel_Window.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Chapel\'s south facing window</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Library">Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Girton\'s first library, the Stanley library, was established in 1884 with a donation from <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Stanley,_Baroness_Stanley_of_Alderley" title="Henrietta Stanley, Baroness Stanley of Alderley">Lady Stanley of Alderley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> It was considered to be luxurious and comfortable, as it contained stained-glass windows, leather furniture and a large chimney. Books were acquired mostly through donations. By 1932 the collection had become so large that a new library was opened. Designed by Michael Waterhouse, descendant of the architects <a href="/wiki/Paul_Waterhouse" title="Paul Waterhouse">Paul Waterhouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Waterhouse" title="Alfred Waterhouse">Alfred Waterhouse</a>, the new library consisted of an upper reading room, crafted in oak, and a ground floor, in which the book collections are held.<sup id="cite_ref-Gandy_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gandy-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> An annexe containing archives was added in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradbrook_1969,_p.161-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The Duke building, a modern library extension offering IT facilities and a reading room, was opened in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Gandy_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gandy-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Named after Alison Duke, a fellow and major donor, the building was designed by <a href="/wiki/Allies_and_Morrison" title="Allies and Morrison">Allies and Morrison</a>. It won a national <a href="/wiki/RIBA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIBA">RIBA</a> award in 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/SCONUL" title="SCONUL">SCONUL</a> Library Design Award in 2007,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Civic_Trust_Awards" title="Civic Trust Awards">Civic Trust Award</a> in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel">Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College,_Cambridge_university_002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Girton_College%2C_Cambridge_university_002.jpg/220px-Girton_College%2C_Cambridge_university_002.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Girton_College%2C_Cambridge_university_002.jpg/330px-Girton_College%2C_Cambridge_university_002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Girton_College%2C_Cambridge_university_002.jpg/440px-Girton_College%2C_Cambridge_university_002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College,_Cambridge_university_002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great Hall</div></div></div>\n<p>Emily Davies first mooted plans for a chapel in Girton college in 1890; however, building only started in 1899,<sup id="cite_ref-Bradbrook_1952_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradbrook_1952-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> four years after the death of <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Stanley,_Baroness_Stanley_of_Alderley" title="Henrietta Stanley, Baroness Stanley of Alderley">Henrietta Stanley, Baroness Stanley of Alderley</a>, who had opposed the idea and instead favoured improving staff salaries and equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-odnb_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The chapel, which was designed by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Waterhouse" title="Alfred Waterhouse">Alfred</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Waterhouse" title="Paul Waterhouse">Paul Waterhouse</a>, was completed in 1901, and inaugurated on 23 May 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradbrook_1952_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradbrook_1952-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> It seats about 200 people and the interior is held very simply with the exception of oak carvings at the <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">Chancel</a> end and on two long desks in front of the choir seats, which were crafted by the mathematician <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Meyer" title="Margaret Meyer">Margaret Meyer</a>, along with students and friends of the college.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\nIn 1910 came a fine <a href="/wiki/Harrison_%26_Harrison" title="Harrison &amp; Harrison">Harrison &amp; Harrison</a> organ, the purchase of which was made possible through donations from students and friends of the college.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The organ was rebuilt in 1974 and can still be found in the college chapel. A second organ was acquired in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-Organ_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Organ-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In 1952, the year of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Jubilee" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Jubilee">Golden Jubilee</a> of the inauguration, a stained glass window was erected.<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_college_1955_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_college_1955-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In the Girton Review, the college\'s official termly newsletter, from <a href="/wiki/Michaelmas_term" title="Michaelmas term">Michaelmas term</a> 1955, a description of the glass window can be found:\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Girton_College.jpg/220px-Girton_College.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Girton_College.jpg/330px-Girton_College.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Girton_College.jpg/440px-Girton_College.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3120" data-file-height="4160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girton_College.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Girton College\'s duck pond with the Great Hall in the background</div></div></div>\n<blockquote><p> The centre light depicts Our Lord in Majesty, as it were the culmination of the Tree of Jesse and in the form described in the book of Revelation. The Lamb who may alone open the book sealed with seven seals is shown at the foot of the light, while the descent of the Holy Spirit in the form of a dove is shown at its apex. The flowers and fruit in the centre light represent the Tree of Jesse. The two lights flanking that in the centre depict scenes from the Passion of Our Lord. On the left are the entry into Jerusalem, the Betrayal of Judas and the Ecce Homo: on the right, the Scourging, Christ bearing His Cross, the Crucifixion. The scenes are linked with a pattern of leaves. Palm is used for the Entry into Jerusalem, and among other plants represented are the Star of Bethlehem, the Passion Flower and the Thorn. The lowest medallion on the right, portraying the crucifixion, is darker than the others, suggesting the darkness that was over the land. The uppermost tracery light depicts the Pelican in her Piety, and the remaining tracery lights contain the symbols of the Passion; the betrayal money, Peter\xe2\x80\x99s lantern, pillar and scourges, dice, ladder and nails, hammer and pincers, crown of thorns and chalice.<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_college_1955_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_college_1955-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>In the original statement of aims and scope for the \'Proposed College for Women\' in 1867, it was announced that religious services and instruction would be in accordance with the principles of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, but where conscientious objections were entertained, attendance would not be necessary. A modified version of this statement appears in the modern college statutes, where it reads that "services in the Chapel shall normally be held in accordance with the practice of the Church of England, but other religious services may also be held there."<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_College_Statutes_2009_p.18_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_College_Statutes_2009_p.18-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\nAt the outset, Chapel was used for morning prayers, usually said by the Mistress, and for Sunday services, taken by clergy of various denominations.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Today, at least two services are held on a weekly basis: <a href="/wiki/Evening_Prayer_(Anglican)" title="Evening Prayer (Anglican)">Evensong</a> on Sunday at 5:30pm, and <a href="/wiki/Compline" title="Compline">Compline</a> on Tuesday at 10pm. They are organised by the college\'s part-time chaplain, who is assisted by student chapel wardens.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The Mistress holds general responsibility in regard to services in the chapel, which she partly delegates to the Chapel Committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_College_Statutes_2009_p.18_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_College_Statutes_2009_p.18-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The current Chaplain is the Reverend Dr <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Guite" title="Malcolm Guite">Malcolm Guite</a>, a poet and singer-songwriter, who is also a bye-fellow at Girton.<sup id="cite_ref-GirtonBio_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GirtonBio-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gardens">Gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the land was bought, trees were planted on bare land.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Today, the gardens of Girton are large compared to those of other Cambridge colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> They became a preoccupation for the college in 1875 when Miss Davies handed over the responsibility for developing the gardens to Miss Bernard.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> A pond, which originated from excavations for the construction of the Stanley library and the Orchard Wing, dates from 1884.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> A 1983 report of the college ornithologists\' society found sixty species of birds, and a moth report from 1986 recorded over 100 species.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The Fellows\' garden was redesigned in 1992 and hosts a green theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Outdoor plays are no longer performed in the Fellows\' garden because of noise from the <a href="/wiki/A14_road_(England)" title="A14 road (England)">A14</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> A rare breed of <a href="/wiki/Black_squirrel" title="Black squirrel">black squirrels</a> can sometimes be seen in Girton.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawrence_Room">Lawrence Room</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lawrence Room">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1934, the Lawrence Room on the college main site was dedicated to be the college museum.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Named after Girton natural scientist Amy Lawrence, it houses an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a> and a Mediterranean collection.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_GC_2010_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_GC_2010-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Before the establishment of the Lawrence room in 1934, antiquities had been stored in and around the college library.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Donations allowed for refurbishments in 1946, 1961, 1991 and 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In 2010/11, Lawrence room is opened once a week to visitors. The exhibitions are free of charge.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_GC_2010_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_GC_2010-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Anglo-Saxon collection stems from excavations on the college main site made during construction work in 1881 and 1886, when an Anglo-Saxon cemetery, presumably from the fifth and sixth century <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">AD</a>, was discovered.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Most findings, such as domestic utensils and personal items, were long held in the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Archaeology_and_Anthropology,_University_of_Cambridge" title="Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology, University of Cambridge">Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology</a> in Cambridge. Some were only returned to the college as late as in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The highlight of the Egyptian collection consists of a portrait mummy bearing the inscription <i>Hermion\xc3\xaa Grammatik\xc3\xaa</i> (translation: \'Hermione the literary lady\' or \'Hermione the language teacher\').<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> It is one of the most widely reproduced and famous <a href="/wiki/Fayum_mummy_portraits" title="Fayum mummy portraits">portrait mummies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Dating from the first century AD, it was discovered in the Roman cemetery of <a href="/wiki/Hawara" title="Hawara">Hawara</a> by the archeologist <a href="/wiki/Flinders_Petrie" title="Flinders Petrie">Flinders Petrie</a> in 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> \'Hermione\' is thought to be an 18- to 25-year-old girl from a wealthy background. Petrie and his wife Hilda wanted the mummy to go to a women\'s college due to its inscription. Funds were gathered, and in 1911 \'Hermione\' moved to Girton college, where she has remained since then.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The Egyptian collection also holds four mummified baby crocodiles, which were thought to bring favour of <a href="/wiki/Sobek" title="Sobek">Sobek</a>, the ancient god of fertility and water. They were presented to the college by Alfred Waterhouse senior, the father of architect <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Waterhouse" title="Alfred Waterhouse">Alfred Waterhouse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008_62-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Room_History_GC_2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Mediterranean collection offers both <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical</a> and pre-Classical material. A collection of Greek <a href="/wiki/Tanagra_figurine" title="Tanagra figurine">Tanagra figurines</a>, which date to the fourth and third century <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">BC</a>, form the most remarkable pieces of this collection.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="People.27s_Portraits"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="People\'s_Portraits">People\'s Portraits</span><span id="cite_ref-Dance_2010_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dance_2010-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-People&#39;s_Portraits_GC_2010_67-0" class="reference"><a Todd">Daphne Todd</a>, June Mendoza and Alastair Adams, the current president of the Society.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_65-2" class="reference"><a An extensive refurbishment of the pitches was completed in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> There are men\'s and women\'s teams for badminton, football, hockey, rowing (<a href="/wiki/Girton_College_Boat_Club" title="Girton College Boat Club">Girton College Boat Club</a>) and rugby. Hockey has a mixed team as well. Cricket, cross country, lacrosse, netball, squash, swimming, table tennis, tennis, volleyball and waterpolo teams are open to both male and female participants. The college has outdoor tennis and indoor squash courts, and a <a href="/wiki/Gym" title="Gym">gym</a>. The indoor-heated swimming pool has been in use since 1900, but is currently out of order due to building work.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> A one-mile running path leads around the college grounds. College alumni are currently raising money in order to build a new sports pavilion.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The college has a strong history of music, which is supported by the university\'s Chairman of the Faculty Board of Music, also Director of Studies in Music at Girton.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> In the last decade, the college has consistently been within the top three colleges for music in the university. In 2005, the highest ever <a href="/wiki/First-class_honours" class="mw-redirect" title="First-class honours">first-class honours</a> in the music Tripos was attained by a Girtonian.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> The student-run and fellow-led Girton College Music Society hosts weekly concerts in term time, as well as termly orchestral concerts.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The college provides four practice grand pianos (including a <a href="/wiki/Steinway_%26_Sons" title="Steinway &amp; Sons">Steinway Model B</a>), a double-manual <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> and two organs. Moreover, all undergraduate music students are provided with a practice piano in their room for the duration of their course.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> The chapel\'s organ is a four-manual, crafted by the Swiss firm St. Martin and acquired in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-Organ_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Organ-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The chapel choir has 28 members and sings Choral Evensong (Sunday) and Compline (Tuesday) in the college chapel every week. The choir has released eight CDs.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Girton grants two undergraduate organ scholarships<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> for \xe2\x82\xa4300 per annum, choral scholarships<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> for \xe2\x82\xa4100 per annum each, and choral exhibitions.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Societies">Societies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Admitting undergraduate students studying all subjects except for the Education Tripos,<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> the college hosts a variety of student-run societies which cater for a wide range of interests. Seven subjects have their own societies: Biology, History, Economics (<a href="/wiki/Joan_Robinson" title="Joan Robinson">Joan Robinson</a> society), Geography, Music, Medicine and Law. There is furthermore the Art society, the Film society and the Girton Amateur Dramatic Society (GADS) which produces around two plays per term. Finally, Girton <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty</a> and the Orchestra on the Hill<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> serve students with specific interest in human rights and music, respectively. Recently, Go and Yes Minister Society (GaYMS) has been founded at Girton College, consisting of students with an interest in both the game <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">Go</a>, and the 80s sitcom <a href="/wiki/Yes_Minister" title="Yes Minister">Yes Minister</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring_Ball">Spring Ball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Spring Ball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20091103055706/http://www.girtonball.co.uk/">Girton Spring Ball</a> is understood to have evolved from a traditional dance, the earliest record of which dates to 1883; it was only in the mid-1900s that the Ball began to resemble its contemporary form, notably in 1969 when the College celebrated its centenary anniversary.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Currently, the Ball is usually held biennially on the Friday after the end of <a href="/wiki/Lent_term" title="Lent term">Lent term</a>, contrasting with most other <a href="/wiki/Colleges_of_the_University_of_Cambridge" title="Colleges of the University of Cambridge">Cambridge Colleges</a> who host <a href="/wiki/May_Ball" title="May Ball">May Balls</a>. It is organised by a committee of around 20 current Girton students. Recent Girton Balls have become much larger and more decadent than their earlier counterparts and the majority of the College site is now included in the Ball area.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 2016 Spring Ball, to feature headliners <a href="/wiki/Tinchy_Stryder" title="Tinchy Stryder">Tinchy Stryder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_Sunshine" title="Bipolar Sunshine">Bipolar Sunshine</a>, was anticipated to be the largest and greatest Girton Ball to date. It ultimately received widespread media attention when it was cancelled by Girton College Council following the death of a student at the College.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> Cancellation was then implemented and overseen almost exclusively by senior officials of the College, with all ticket holders receiving full refunds.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accommodation">Accommodation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span site</div></div></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Undergraduates">Undergraduates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Undergraduates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>It is customary for Cambridge colleges to provide accommodation for the first three-year undergraduate students.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Girton, along with <a href="/wiki/Newnham_College,_Cambridge" title="Newnham College, Cambridge">Newnham College</a>, are the only colleges to charge the same fee for undergraduate accommodation on their premises.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> The main site offers 348 rooms,<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> rented for the entire year (38 or 39 weeks, depending on the term dates).<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> The weekly rent for the undergraduate cohort of 2010/11 will be \xc2\xa3103.25.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> The rooms range in several quality grades.<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_College_JCR_2011_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_College_JCR_2011-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> Every year, a ballot is organised by the <a href="/wiki/Common_Room_(university)" title="Common Room (university)">JCR</a> to determine room distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_College_JCR_2011_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_College_JCR_2011-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> To first years, rooms are allocated randomly.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Rooms in the main site and in Wolfson court are arranged along corridors, which makes it possible to walk from one location in the building to another without going outside.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_71-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Some of the rooms originally designed as sets by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Waterhouse" title="Alfred Waterhouse">Alfred Waterhouse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EH_1984_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EH_1984-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most undergraduate students live in the main site, and second years have the option of living at Wolfson Court, or at one of the college houses: The college owns six houses along Girton Road, another one located opposite the college on <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_Road" title="Huntingdon Road">Huntingdon Road</a> called The Gate and one house located on the college grounds, called The Grange.<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_College_&amp;_Morgan_2009_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_College_&amp;_Morgan_2009-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> These houses are available for second and third year undergraduates.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Graduates_and_fellows">Graduates and fellows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Graduates and fellows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Graduate students have the option of either living in Wolfson Court or in one of the seven graduate houses the college owns. They are located on Huntingdon Road, Chesterton Road, Albert Street, Thornton Road and Park Parade, all in central Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-Girton_College_&amp;_Morgan_2009_100-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Girton_College_&amp;_Morgan_2009-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, there are four flats available at Cockcroft Place in Clarkson Road.<sup class="mw-headline" id="Arms">Arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arms_of_Girton_College,_Cambridge.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Arms_of_Girton_College%2C_Cambridge.svg/220px-Arms_of_Girton_College%2C_Cambridge.svg.png" width="220" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Arms_of_Girton_College%2C_Cambridge.svg/330px-Arms_of_Girton_College%2C_Cambridge.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Arms_of_Girton_College%2C_Cambridge.svg/440px-Arms_of_Girton_College%2C_Cambridge.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="604" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arms_of_Girton_College,_Cambridge.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Girton arms representing the four major benefactors</div></div></div>\n<p>The college applied for a coat-of-arms derived from the arms of its founders and benefactors: Mr H.R. Tomkinson, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Bodichon" title="Barbara Bodichon">Madame Bodichon (n\xc3\xa9e Leigh Smith)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Stanley,_Baroness_Stanley_of_Alderley" title="Henrietta Stanley, Baroness Stanley of Alderley">Henrietta Stanley, Baroness Stanley of Alderley</a> (daughter of the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Dillon,_13th_Viscount_Dillon" title="Henry Dillon, 13th Viscount Dillon">13th Viscount Dillon</a>), and Miss <a href="/wiki/Emily_Davies" title="Emily Davies">Emily Davies</a> who did not have arms and was instead represented by the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Wales" title="Flag of Wales">Welsh colours</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vert_(heraldry)" title="Vert (heraldry)">vert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argent" title="Argent">argent</a>. The Rev. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Earle_Dorling" title="Edward Earle Dorling">Edward Earle Dorling</a> submitted a great variety of designs to the council, however the task was not easy. "A patch-work of elaborate charges and many colours was to be avoided. Mr Tomkinson\'s fascinating martlets and Lady Stanley\'s lion had to be abandoned with regret, as was also a design of green and silver chequers which would have given more prominence to Miss <a href="/wiki/Emily_Davies" title="Emily Davies">Davies</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Finally in 1928 the design was accepted by all and the College was granted the following:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>We \xe2\x80\xa6 grant and assign unto The <a href="/wiki/Mistress_(college)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mistress (college)">Mistress</a> and Governors of Girton College the Arms following that is to say: Quarterly <a href="/wiki/Vert_(heraldry)" title="Vert (heraldry)">Vert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argent" title="Argent">Argent</a> a <a href="/wiki/Cross_flory" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross flory">cross flory</a> countercharged a <a href="/wiki/Roundel" title="Roundel">Roundel</a> <a href="/wiki/Ermine_(heraldry)" title="Ermine (heraldry)">Ermine</a> and in the second and third quarters a <a href="/wiki/Crescent" title="Crescent">Crescent</a> <a href="/wiki/Gules" title="Gules">Gules</a>, \xe2\x80\xa6 to be borne and used for ever, hereafter by the Mistress and Governors of Girton College and by their Successors upon Seals Shields or otherwise according to the Laws of Arms.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gown">Gown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Gown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The college gown has the standard pattern of an <a href="/wiki/Academic_dress_of_the_University_of_Cambridge" title="Academic dress of the University of Cambridge">undergraduate gown at the University of Cambridge</a>, with the sleeves sewn up for a length of eight inches from the shoulder.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> The proper dress of the gown and cap was observed at the first honorary degree to a woman, given to the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Bowes-Lyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon">Queen</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctor of Laws">LL.D</a>. on 21 October 1948. As <a href="/wiki/Academic_dress_of_the_University_of_Cambridge" title="Academic dress of the University of Cambridge">academic dress</a>, gowns were adopted with little changes (the sleeves had to be closed so that even in the summer, when women wear short-sleeved dresses their bare shoulders do not show), and square caps were chosen as head-dress. However, to remember the time when women were not allowed to obtain degrees of the University of Cambridge, no gowns are worn during the college feast, when students in their final year are celebrated.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Graces">Graces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Graces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Girton College has a traditional two-word grace and a more recent full grace, both in Latin.  On regular formal occasions, such as <a href="/wiki/Formal_(university)" title="Formal (university)">Formal Halls</a>, the two-word graces are spoken, <i>Benedictus benedicat</i> (May the blessed one give blessing) at the start of the meal, and <i>Benedictus benedicatur</i> (May praise be given to the blessed one) at the end of the meal. There is evidence that the two-word grace was used in 1926, and it is thought the two-word grace  was used from the foundation of the college onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The words and the music of the full grace were composed in 1950 by Alison Duke and Jill Vlasto respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Wheeler_2002_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wheeler_2002-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> The grace came after the admission of women to full membership of the university so as to bring Girton in line with the other colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-Wheeler_2002_105-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wheeler_2002-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> It is used on the most formal occasions, such as the Foundation Dinner, and it is sung once a year at the College Feast, which all final year students attend.\n</p><p>Full grace (in Latin):\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p><i>Benedic Domine, nobis et omnibus huius collegii alumnis,<br /></i>\ndonisque tuis quae de munificentia tua sumus iam sumpturi;<br />\net illis salubriter nutriti debitas tibi gratias pie reddamus.<br />\nCustodi, quaesumus, Domine, filios et filias<br />\net consule necessitatibus animarum et corporum,<br />\nhoc ipso momento et in aeternum.<i></i>\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p>Full grace (English translation):\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>Bless us, O Lord, and all members of this college,<br />\nand also thy gifts, which of thy bounty we are about to receive;<br />\nand having been wholesomely nourished by the same let us dutifully render to thee the thanks that are owed.<br />\nProtect, we beseech thee, O Lord, thy sons and daughters and provide for the needs both of our souls and bodies,<br />\nat this present time and for evermore.\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Songs">Songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Girton_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg/220px-The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg/330px-The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg/440px-The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="665" data-file-height="434" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Girton_Pioneers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Girton Pioneers</i> (tune of <a href="/wiki/The_British_Grenadiers" title="The British Grenadiers">The British Grenadiers</a>) honours the first three women to sit the <a href="/wiki/Tripos" title="Tripos">Tripos exams</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The oldest college song, <i>The Girton Pioneers</i>, was composed by several students in Hitchin in 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-College_songs_GC_2010_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_songs_GC_2010-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Its purpose was to celebrate the first three students who sat the Tripos examinations in 1871.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1913_p.28f_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_1913_p.28f-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> It is sung with the tune of <i><a href="/wiki/The_British_Grenadiers" title="The British Grenadiers">The British Grenadiers</a></i>. This is the first stanza:\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>Some talk of Senior Wranglers,<br />\nAnd some of Double Firsts,<br />\nAnd truly of their species<br />\nThese are not the worst;<br />\nBut of all the Cambridge heroes<br />\nThere\xe2\x80\x99s none that can compare<br />\nWith Woodhead, Cook and Lumsden,<br />\nThe Girton Pioneers.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1913_p.28f_106-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_1913_p.28f-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p>More recently, Girtonians have become known for their chant of <i>We are Girton \xe2\x80\x93 super Girton! No one likes us, but we don\'t care</i>!, in imitation of the <a href="/wiki/Millwall_F.C." title="Millwall F.C.">Millwall</a> fans\' famous song: <i><a href="/wiki/No_one_likes_us,_we_don%27t_care" title="No one likes us, we don&#39;t care">No one likes us, we don\'t care</a></i>. The reference to <i>no one likes us</i> is supposedly due to the relative distance of Girton in comparison to many of the other colleges. In 2016 the Casual Choir performed <i>Long Way to Girton College</i>, an adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Long_Way_to_Tipperary" title="It&#39;s a Long Way to Tipperary">It\'s a Long Way to Tipperary</a></i>, written and arranged by two choir members. \n</p><p>The college song <i>I love to go a-bicycling</i>, sung with the tune of<i> I love to go a-wandering</i> also alludes to Girton\'s 2.5 miles distance from the town centre. It has been sung since the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-College_songs_GC_2010_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_songs_GC_2010-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> For example, the second stanza reads:\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>But when I climb up Castle Hill<br />\nIn the wind and squall<br />\nA strong desire does then me fill<br />\nTo be-e at <a href="/wiki/Murray_Edwards_College,_Cambridge" title="Murray Edwards College, Cambridge">New Hall</a>.<br />\n<br />\n<i>Chorus:</i><br />\nFol-de-re, Fol-de-ra,<br />\nFol-de-re, Fol-de-ra-a-a-a-<br />\n.... To be-e at New Hall.<sup id="cite_ref-College_songs_GC_2010_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_songs_GC_2010-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p>Another song, composed in 1972 by <a href="/wiki/Common_Room_(university)" title="Common Room (university)">JCR</a> president Anna Cocconi,<sup id="cite_ref-College_songs_GC_2010_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_songs_GC_2010-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> is called <i>Song to Wolfson Court</i>. The annexe of the college was opened in that year. Students often found fault with the modern architecture of the building, which was less spacious and elegant than the main site. The first two stanzas go as follows:\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>&#160;In a wheat field, west of Cambridge,<br />\nOff a lane called Clarkson Road,<br />\nWolfson Court is standing proudly;<br />\nLet its story now be told<br />\n<br />\nHundred rooms with running water,<br />\nCould you ever ask for more?<br />\nNot unless you choose a boy-friend,<br />\nMeas\xe2\x80\x99ring over five foot four!<sup id="cite_ref-College_songs_GC_2010_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_songs_GC_2010-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<h3><span
