Spaniel</a> in a typical English shooting scene</div></div></div>\n<p>In the United Kingdom <b>hunting</b> without qualification generally refers to hunting with hounds-normally <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">fox hunting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beagling" title="Beagling">beagling</a>, stag (deer) hunting or <a href="/wiki/Minkhunting" class="mw-redirect" title="Minkhunting">minkhunting</a>-whereas <b>shooting</b> is the shooting of <a href="/wiki/Game_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Game bird">game birds</a>. What is called <a href="/wiki/Deer_hunting" title="Deer hunting">deer hunting</a> elsewhere is <b>deer stalking</b>.\n</p><p>As of  2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hunting_and_shooting_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (food)">game</a> shooting and <a href="/wiki/Deer_stalking" title="Deer stalking">deer stalking</a> are carried on as <a href="/wiki/Field_sports" title="Field sports">field sports</a> in Great Britain and Northern Ireland. Hunting with hounds in the traditional manner became unlawful in Scotland in 2002 and in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> in 2005, but continues in certain excepted forms. Traditional foxhunting continues in Northern Ireland. Following a trail (similar to <a href="/wiki/Drag_hunting" title="Drag hunting">drag hunting</a>) rather than a live quarry has subsequently grown in importance in Great Britain, as has hunting foxes to a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>. \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Association_for_Shooting_and_Conservation" title="British Association for Shooting and Conservation">British Association for Shooting and Conservation</a> (BASC) says that over a million people a year participate in shooting, including game shooting, clay shooting and target shooting.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 2005 it became unlawful in England and Wales to shoot flights or game birds while they are not in flight, an action which has long been considered unsporting in any event.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Hunting_and_shooting_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_(detail).jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_%28detail%29.jpeg/350px-The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_%28detail%29.jpeg" width="350" height="263" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_%28detail%29.jpeg/525px-The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_%28detail%29.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_%28detail%29.jpeg/700px-The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_%28detail%29.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Setting_Dogg_%26_Partridgs_(detail).jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"The Setting Dogg &amp; Partridgs" (detail)\' by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Blome" title="Richard Blome">Richard Blome</a>, from <i>The Gentlemans Recreation</i> (1686)</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">Hunting</a> has been practised in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> since prehistoric times; it was a crucial activity of <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> societies before the domestication of animals and the dawn of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>, humans and <a href="/wiki/Neanderthals" class="mw-redirect" title="Neanderthals">neanderthals</a> hunted <a href="/wiki/Mammoths" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammoths">mammoths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woolly_rhinoceros" title="Woolly rhinoceros">woolly rhinoceroses</a> by driving them over cliffs; evidence has been found at <a href="/wiki/La_Cotte_de_St_Brelade" title="La Cotte de St Brelade">La Cotte de St Brelade</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>.\n</p><p>In Britain, hunting with hounds was popular in <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic Britain">Celtic Britain</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> arrived, using the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agassaei&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Agassaei (page does not exist)">Agassaei</a> breed.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Romans brought their Castorian and Fulpine hound breeds to England, along with importing the <a href="/wiki/European_Hare" class="mw-redirect" title="European Hare">brown hare</a> (the mountain hare is native) and <a href="/wiki/Fallow_deer" title="Fallow deer">fallow deer</a> as quarry. <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">Wild boar</a> was also hunted.\n</p><p>The earliest known attempt to specifically <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">hunt a fox</a> with hounds was in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East of England</a>, in 1534, where farmers began chasing down foxes with their dogs as a form of <a href="/wiki/Pest_control" title="Pest control">pest control</a>. Packs of <a href="/wiki/Hound" title="Hound">hounds</a> were first trained specifically to <a href="/wiki/Fox_hound" class="mw-redirect" title="Fox hound">hunt</a> foxes in the late 17th century, with the oldest such fox hunt likely to be the Bilsdale in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By the end of the 17th century, many organised packs were hunting both hare and fox.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Shotgun" title="Shotgun">Shotguns</a> were improved during the 18th and 19th centuries and game shooting became more popular. To protect the <a href="/wiki/Common_pheasant" title="Common pheasant">pheasants</a> for the shooters, <a href="/wiki/Gamekeeper" title="Gamekeeper">gamekeepers</a> culled vermin such as foxes, magpies and birds of prey almost to extirpation in popular areas, and landowners improved their coverts and other habitats for game. <a href="/wiki/Game_Act_1831" title="Game Act 1831">Game Laws</a> were relaxed in 1831 which meant anyone could obtain a permit to shoot rabbits, hares, and gamebirds, although shooting and taking away any birds or animals on someone else\'s land without their permission continued to be the crime of <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>, as it still is. \n</p><p>Hunting was formerly a royal sport, and to an extent shooting still is, with many Kings and Queens being involved in hunting and shooting, including <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">King Edward VII</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King George V</a> (who on 18 December 1913 shot over a thousand pheasants out of a total <a href="/wiki/Bag_(fishing_%26_hunting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bag (fishing &amp; hunting)">bag</a> of 3937),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_VI_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George VI of the United Kingdom">King George VI</a> and the present day <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Philip">Prince Philip</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Queen Elizabeth II</a> does not shoot. Shooting on the large estates of Scotland has always been a fashionable country sport. This trend is generally attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Victorians" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorians">Victorians</a>, who were inspired by the romantic imagery of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_hunting_and_shooting">Forms of hunting and shooting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hunting_and_shooting_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forms of hunting and shooting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shooting">Shooting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hunting_and_shooting_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shooting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Snowden_Slights,_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Snowden_Slights%2C_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg/220px-Snowden_Slights%2C_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Snowden_Slights%2C_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg/330px-Snowden_Slights%2C_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Snowden_Slights%2C_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg/440px-Snowden_Slights%2C_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1404" data-file-height="1754" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Snowden_Slights,_front_view_YORYM-S13.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Snowden Slights with retriever and shotgun around 1910 \'the last of Yorkshire\'s Wildfowlers\'<sup id="cite_ref-Blast_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blast-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The shooting of <a href="/wiki/Game_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Game birds">game birds</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Pheasant_Shooting" class="mw-redirect" title="Pheasant Shooting">pheasant</a>, is found in the UK, on large, traditional driven shoots on estates and on small-scale rough shoots. Shooting of game birds is carried out using a <a href="/wiki/Shotgun" title="Shotgun">shotgun</a>, most often <a href="/wiki/Gauge_(bore_diameter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gauge (bore diameter)">12 and 20 bore</a> or a <a href="/wiki/.410_bore" title=".410 bore">.410</a>, often on land managed by a <a href="/wiki/Gamekeeper" title="Gamekeeper">gamekeeper</a>.\n</p><p>Game birds are shot in different ways. In <a href="/wiki/Driven_grouse_shooting" title="Driven grouse shooting">driven game shooting</a>, where beaters are employed to walk through woods and over moors or fields, dependent on the quarry and time of year and drive game towards a line of 8\xe2\x80\x9310 standing guns standing about 50 or 60 metres apart. The guns will be members of a syndicate sharing costs, or have paid in the region of \xc2\xa325+ per bird for pheasants and much more for <a href="/wiki/Red_Grouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Grouse">grouse</a>. The total bag (number of birds shot) will be anywhere between 10 and 400, again dependent on the budget and quarry. The day may be very formal, and the head gamekeeper or a shoot captain will oversee proceedings. Great emphasis is placed on safety. Pickers-up with dogs are also employed to make sure all shot or wounded game is collected. On such estates, large numbers of pheasants, partridge and duck, but not grouse, are reared and released to provide sufficient numbers of game. Grouse cannot be reared intensively but the heather moorland where they live is intensively <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_management" title="Wildlife management">managed</a> to maximise numbers.\n</p><p>Rough shooting, where several guns walk through a woodland, moor or field and shoot the birds their dogs put up, is increasingly popular. It is less formal and may be funded by several people grouping together to form a "syndicate", paying a certain amount each year towards pheasants, habitat maintenance, etc.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wildfowling" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildfowling">Wildfowling</a> is often a lonely and uncomfortable sport. A single gun sits in pursuit of wildfowl by a body of water, or on the coastal foreshore, often at dawn or dusk, and waits for birds to "flight" in. This is sometimes undertaken in total darkness or by the light of the moon. Duck are also shot by the two methods described above.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rook_shooting" title="Rook shooting">Rook shooting</a> was once popular in rural Britain for both pest control and gaining food, wherein juvenile rooks living in rookeries, known as "branchers", were shot before they were able to fly. These events were both very social and a source of food (the rook becomes inedible once mature) as the rook and rabbit pie was considered a great delicacy.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deer_stalking">Deer stalking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hunting_and_shooting_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deer stalking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deer_stalking" title="Deer stalking">Deer stalking</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Deer_hunting" title="Deer hunting">Deer hunting</a></div>\n<p>High-powered rifles are used for <a href="/wiki/Deer_stalking" title="Deer stalking">deer stalking</a>.  This may take place high on moors, or from a "high seat" in woodland.  Venison is also a highly popular meat with sales quadrupling in the UK in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_animals">Game animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hunting_and_shooting_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Game animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the UK "game" is defined in law by the <a href="/wiki/Game_Act_1831" title="Game Act 1831">Game Act 1831</a>. Other (non-game) birds that are hunted for food in the UK are specified under the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_and_Countryside_Act_1981" title="Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981">Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981</a>. in the UK, including woodlands that exhibit denser undergrowth and higher abundances of native birds.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However,
