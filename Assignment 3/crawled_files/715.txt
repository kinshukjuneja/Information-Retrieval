On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, gravity gives <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> to <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">physical objects</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Gravitation_of_the_Moon" title="Gravitation of the Moon">gravity</a> causes the ocean <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>. The gravitational attraction of the original gaseous matter present in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a> caused it to begin <a href="/wiki/Coalescence_(physics)" title="Coalescence (physics)">coalescing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">forming stars</a>&#160;&#8211;&#32;and for the stars to group together into galaxies&#160;&#8211;&#32;so gravity is responsible for many of the large-scale structures in the Universe. Gravity has an infinite range, although its effects become increasingly weaker on farther objects.\n</p><p>Gravity is most accurately described by the <a href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" class="mw-redirect" title="General theory of relativity">general theory of relativity</a> (proposed by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> in 1915) which describes gravity not as a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a>, but as a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Curvature_of_spacetime" class="mw-redirect" title="Curvature of spacetime">curvature of spacetime</a> caused by the uneven distribution of mass. The most extreme example of this curvature of spacetime is a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>, from which nothing\xe2\x80\x94not even light\xe2\x80\x94can escape once past the black hole\'s event horizon.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  However, for most applications, gravity is well approximated by <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton&#39;s law of universal gravitation">Newton\'s law of universal gravitation</a>, which describes gravity as a force which causes any two bodies to be attracted to each other, with the force <a href="/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics)" title="Proportionality (mathematics)">proportional</a> to the product of their masses and <a href="/wiki/Inversely_proportional" class="mw-redirect" title="Inversely proportional">inversely proportional</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Square_(algebra)" title="Square (algebra)">square</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a> between them.\n</p><p>Gravity is the weakest of the four <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Fundamental force">fundamental forces</a> of physics, approximately 10<sup>38</sup> times weaker than the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong force</a>, 10<sup>36</sup> times weaker than the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Electromagnetic force">electromagnetic force</a> and 10<sup>29</sup> times weaker than the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak force</a>. As a consequence, it has no significant influence at the level of subatomic particles.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, it is the dominant force at the <a href="/wiki/Macroscopic_scale" title="Macroscopic scale">macroscopic scale</a>, and is the cause of the formation, shape and <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">trajectory</a> (<a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Astronomical bodies">astronomical bodies</a>.  For example, gravity causes the Earth and the other planets to orbit the Sun, it also causes the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> to orbit the Earth, and causes the formation of <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">formation and evolution of the Solar System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution" title="Galaxy formation and evolution">galaxies</a>.\n</p><p>The earliest instance of gravity in the Universe, possibly in the form of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">quantum gravity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supergravity" title="Supergravity">supergravity</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_singularity" title="Gravitational singularity">gravitational singularity</a>, along with ordinary <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, developed during the <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_formation_of_the_Universe#Planck_epoch" title="Timeline of the formation of the Universe">Planck epoch</a> (up to 10<sup>\xe2\x88\x9243</sup> seconds after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">birth</a> of the Universe), possibly from a primeval state, such as a <a href="/wiki/False_vacuum" title="False vacuum">false vacuum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_vacuum" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantum vacuum">quantum vacuum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Virtual_particle" title="Virtual particle">virtual particle</a>, in a currently unknown manner.<sup id="cite_ref-Planck-UOregon_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planck-UOregon-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Attempts to develop a theory of gravity consistent with <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">quantum gravity</a> theory, which would allow gravity to be united in a common mathematical framework (a <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_everything" title="Theory of everything">theory of everything</a>) with the other three forces of physics, are a current area of research.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" id="History_of_gravitational_theory">History of gravitational theory</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_gravitational_theory" title="History of gravitational theory">History of gravitational theory</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_revolution">Scientific revolution</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific revolution">Scientific revolution</a></div>\n<p>Modern work on gravitational theory began with the work of <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> in the late 16th and early 17th centuries. In his famous (though possibly <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal</a><sup id="cite_ref-Ball_Piza_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball_Piza-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>) experiment dropping balls from the <a href="/wiki/Leaning_Tower_of_Pisa" title="Leaning Tower of Pisa">Tower of Pisa</a>, and later with careful measurements of balls rolling down <a href="/wiki/Inclined_plane" title="Inclined plane">inclines</a>, Galileo showed that gravitational acceleration is the same for all objects. This was a major departure from <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>\'s belief that heavier objects have a higher gravitational acceleration.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Galileo postulated <a href="/wiki/Air_resistance" class="mw-redirect" title="Air resistance">air resistance</a> as the reason that objects with less mass fall more slowly in an atmosphere. Galileo\'s work set the stage for the formulation of Newton\'s theory of gravity.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Newton.27s_theory_of_gravitation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Newton\'s_theory_of_gravitation">Newton\'s theory of gravitation</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton&#39;s law of universal gravitation">Newton\'s law of universal gravitation</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Isaac_Newton_(1643-1727).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sir_Isaac_Newton_%281643-1727%29.jpg/200px-Sir_Isaac_Newton_%281643-1727%29.jpg" width="200" height="244" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sir_Isaac_Newton_%281643-1727%29.jpg/300px-Sir_Isaac_Newton_%281643-1727%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Sir_Isaac_Newton_%281643-1727%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="396" data-file-height="484" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Isaac_Newton_(1643-1727).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Sir Isaac Newton</a>, an English physicist who lived from 1642 to 1727</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1687, English mathematician Sir <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> published <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophi\xc3\xa6 Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Principia</a></i>, which hypothesizes the <a href="/wiki/Inverse-square_law" title="Inverse-square law">inverse-square law</a> of universal gravitation. In his own words, "I deduced that the forces which keep the planets in their orbs must [be] reciprocally as the squares of their distances from the centers about which they revolve: and thereby compared the force requisite to keep the Moon in her Orb with the force of gravity at the surface of the Earth; and found them answer pretty nearly."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The equation is the following:\n</p><p><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\\displaystyle F=G{\\frac {m_{1}m_{2}}{r^{2}}}\\ }">\n  <semantics>\n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n        <mi>F</mi>\n        <mo>=</mo>\n        <mi>G</mi>\n        <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n          <mfrac>\n            <mrow>\n              <msub>\n                <mi>m</mi>\n                <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n                  <mn>1</mn>\n                </mrow>\n              </msub>\n              <msub>\n                <mi>m</mi>\n                <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n                  <mn>2</mn>\n                </mrow>\n              </msub>\n            </mrow>\n            <msup>\n              <mi>r</mi>\n              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n                <mn>2</mn>\n              </mrow>\n            </msup>\n          </mfrac>\n        </mrow>\n        <mtext>&#xA0;</mtext>\n      </mstyle>\n    </mrow>\n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle F=G{\\frac {m_{1}m_{2}}{r^{2}}}\\ }</annotation>\n  </semantics>\n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c6ee5510ba3c7d6664775c0e76b53e72468303a" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -2.171ex; width:14.272ex; height:5.009ex;" alt="F=G{\\frac {m_{1}m_{2}}{r^{2}}}\\ "/></span>\n</p><p>Where <i>F</i> is the force, <i>m<sub>1</sub></i> and <i>m<sub>2</sub></i> are the masses of the objects interacting, <i>r</i> is the distance between the centers of the masses and <i>G</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">gravitational constant</a>.\n</p><p>Newton\'s theory enjoyed its greatest success when it was used to predict the existence of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> based on motions of <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> that could not be accounted for by the actions of the other planets. Calculations by both <a href="/wiki/John_Couch_Adams" title="John Couch Adams">John Couch Adams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urbain_Le_Verrier" title="Urbain Le Verrier">Urbain Le Verrier</a> predicted the general position of the planet, and Le Verrier\'s calculations are what led <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Galle" title="Johann Gottfried Galle">Johann Gottfried Galle</a> to the discovery of Neptune.\n</p><p>A discrepancy in <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>\'s orbit pointed out flaws in Newton\'s theory. By the end of the 19th century, it was known that its orbit showed slight perturbations that could not be accounted for entirely under Newton\'s theory, but all searches for another perturbing body (such as a planet orbiting the Sun even closer than Mercury) had been fruitless. The issue was resolved in 1915 by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>\'s new theory of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, which accounted for the small discrepancy in Mercury\'s orbit. This discrepancy was the advance in the perihelion of Mercury of 42.98 arcseconds per century.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although Newton\'s theory has been superseded by <a href="/wiki/Einstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Einstein">Einstein</a>\'s general relativity, most modern <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">non-relativistic</a> gravitational calculations are still made using Newton\'s theory because it is simpler to work with and it gives sufficiently accurate results for most applications involving sufficiently small masses, speeds and energies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equivalence_principle">Equivalence principle</span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_principle" title="Equivalence principle">equivalence principle</a>, explored by a succession of researchers including Galileo, <a href="/wiki/Lor%C3%A1nd_E%C3%B6tv%C3%B6s" title="Lor\xc3\xa1nd E\xc3\xb6tv\xc3\xb6s">Lor\xc3\xa1nd E\xc3\xb6tv\xc3\xb6s</a>, and Einstein, expresses the idea that all objects fall in the same way, and that the effects of gravity are indistinguishable from certain aspects of acceleration and deceleration.  The simplest way to test the weak equivalence principle is to drop two objects of different masses or compositions in a vacuum and see whether they hit the ground at the same time. Such experiments demonstrate that all objects fall at the same rate when other forces (such as air resistance and electromagnetic effects) are negligible. More sophisticated tests use a torsion balance of a type invented by E\xc3\xb6tv\xc3\xb6s. Satellite experiments, for example <a href="/wiki/STEP_(satellite)" title="STEP (satellite)">STEP</a>, are planned for more accurate experiments in space.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Formulations of the equivalence principle include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The weak equivalence principle: <i>The trajectory of a point mass in a <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">gravitational field</a> depends only on its initial position and velocity, and is independent of its composition.</i><sup id="cite_ref-Wesson_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wesson-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The Einsteinian equivalence principle: <i>The outcome of any local non-gravitational experiment in a freely falling laboratory is independent of the velocity of the laboratory and its location in spacetime.</i><sup id="cite_ref-L\xc4\x81mmerzahl_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L\xc4\x81mmerzahl-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The strong equivalence principle requiring both of the above.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" The starting point for general relativity is the <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_principle" title="Equivalence principle">equivalence principle</a>, which equates free fall with inertial motion and describes free-falling inertial objects as being accelerated relative to non-inertial observers on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Newtonian_physics" class="mw-redirect" title="Newtonian physics">Newtonian physics</a>, however, no such acceleration can occur unless at least one of the objects is being operated on by a force.\n</p><p>Einstein proposed that spacetime is curved by matter, and that free-falling objects are moving along locally straight paths in curved spacetime. These straight paths are called <a href="/wiki/Geodesic_(general_relativity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geodesic (general relativity)">geodesics</a>. Like Newton\'s first law of motion, Einstein\'s theory states that if a force is applied on an object, it would deviate from a geodesic.  For instance, we are no longer following geodesics while standing because the mechanical resistance of the Earth exerts an upward force on us, and we are non-inertial on the ground as a result. This explains why moving along the geodesics in spacetime is considered inertial.\n</p><p>Einstein discovered the <a href="/wiki/Field_equation" title="Field equation">field equations</a> of general relativity, which relate the presence of matter and the curvature of spacetime and are named after him. The <a href="/wiki/Einstein_field_equations" title="Einstein field equations">Einstein field equations</a> are a set of 10 <a href="/wiki/Simultaneous_equations" class="mw-redirect" title="Simultaneous equations">simultaneous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_system" title="Nonlinear system">non-linear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a>. The solutions of the field equations are the components of the <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity)" title="Metric tensor (general relativity)">metric tensor</a> of spacetime. A metric tensor describes a geometry of spacetime. The geodesic paths for a spacetime are calculated from the metric tensor.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span></h4>\n<p>Notable solutions of the Einstein field equations include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Schwarzschild_solution" class="mw-redirect" title="Schwarzschild solution">Schwarzschild solution</a>, which describes spacetime surrounding a <a href="/wiki/Circular_symmetry" title="Circular symmetry">spherically symmetric</a> non-<a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotating</a> uncharged massive object. For compact enough objects, this solution generated a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a> with a central <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_singularity" title="Gravitational singularity">singularity</a>. For radial distances from the center which are much greater than the <a href="/wiki/Schwarzschild_radius" title="Schwarzschild radius">Schwarzschild radius</a>, the accelerations predicted by the Schwarzschild solution are practically identical to those predicted by Newton\'s theory of gravity.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Reissner%E2%80%93Nordstr%C3%B6m_metric" title="Reissner\xe2\x80\x93Nordstr\xc3\xb6m metric">Reissner-Nordstr\xc3\xb6m solution</a>, in which the central object has an electrical charge. For charges with a <a href="/wiki/Geometrized" class="mw-redirect" title="Geometrized">geometrized</a> length which are less than the geometrized length of the mass of the object, this solution produces black holes with double <a href="/wiki/Event_horizon" title="Event horizon">event horizons</a>.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kerr_metric" title="Kerr metric">Kerr solution</a> for rotating massive objects. This solution also produces black holes with multiple event horizons.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kerr%E2%80%93Newman_metric" title="Kerr\xe2\x80\x93Newman metric">Kerr-Newman solution</a> for charged, rotating massive objects. This solution also produces black holes with multiple event horizons.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological</a> <a href="/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric" title="Friedmann\xe2\x80\x93Lema\xc3\xaetre\xe2\x80\x93Robertson\xe2\x80\x93Walker metric">Friedmann-Lema\xc3\xaetre-Robertson-Walker solution</a>, which predicts the expansion of the Universe.</li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tests">Tests</span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity" title="Tests of general relativity">tests of general relativity</a> included the following:<sup id="cite_ref-Pauli1958_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauli1958-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>General relativity accounts for the anomalous <a href="/wiki/Perihelion_precession_of_Mercury" class="mw-redirect" title="Perihelion precession of Mercury">perihelion precession of Mercury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The prediction that time runs slower at lower potentials (<a href="/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation" title="Gravitational time dilation">gravitational time dilation</a>) has been confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Pound%E2%80%93Rebka_experiment" title="Pound\xe2\x80\x93Rebka experiment">Pound\xe2\x80\x93Rebka experiment</a> (1959), the <a href="/wiki/Hafele%E2%80%93Keating_experiment" title="Hafele\xe2\x80\x93Keating experiment">Hafele\xe2\x80\x93Keating experiment</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/GPS#Predecessors" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a>.</li>\n<li>The prediction of the deflection of light was first confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Stanley_Eddington" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Stanley Eddington">Arthur Stanley Eddington</a> from his observations during the <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_29_May_1919" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar eclipse of 29 May 1919">Solar eclipse of 29 May 1919</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Eddington measured starlight deflections twice those predicted by Newtonian corpuscular theory, in accordance with the predictions of general relativity. However, his interpretation of the results was later disputed.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> More recent tests using radio interferometric measurements of <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> passing behind the Sun have more accurately and consistently confirmed the deflection of light to the degree predicted by general relativity.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_lens" title="Gravitational lens">gravitational lens</a>.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Time_delay_of_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Time delay of light">time delay of light</a> passing close to a massive object was first identified by <a href="/wiki/Irwin_I._Shapiro" title="Irwin I. Shapiro">Irwin I. Shapiro</a> in 1964 in interplanetary spacecraft signals.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gravitational_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravitational radiation">Gravitational radiation</a> has been indirectly confirmed through studies of binary <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>. On 11 February 2016, the <a href="/wiki/LIGO" title="LIGO">LIGO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgo_interferometer" title="Virgo interferometer">Virgo</a> collaborations announced the first observation of a gravitational wave.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann" title="Alexander Friedmann">Alexander Friedmann</a> in 1922 found that Einstein equations have non-stationary solutions (even in the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>). In 1927 <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lema\xc3\xaetre">Georges Lema\xc3\xaetre</a> showed that static solutions of the Einstein equations, which are possible in the presence of the cosmological constant, are unstable, and therefore the static Universe envisioned by Einstein could not exist. Later, in 1931, Einstein himself agreed with the results of Friedmann and Lema\xc3\xaetre. Thus general relativity predicted that the Universe had to be non-static\xe2\x80\x94it had to either expand or contract. The expansion of the Universe discovered by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> in 1929 confirmed this prediction.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauli1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauli1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The theory\'s prediction of <a href="/wiki/Frame_dragging" class="mw-redirect" title="Frame dragging">frame dragging</a> was consistent with the recent <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Probe_B" title="Gravity Probe B">Gravity Probe B</a> results.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>General relativity predicts that light should lose <a href="/wiki/Photon_energy" title="Photon energy">its energy</a> when traveling away from massive bodies through <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_redshift" title="Gravitational redshift">gravitational redshift</a>. This was verified on earth and in the solar system around 1960.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gravity_and_quantum_mechanics">Gravity and quantum mechanics</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">Graviton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">Quantum gravity</a></div>\n<p>In the decades after the discovery of general relativity, it was realized that general relativity is incompatible with <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Randall,_Lisa_2005_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Randall,_Lisa_2005-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It is possible to describe gravity in the framework of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a> like the other <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Fundamental forces">fundamental forces</a>, such that the attractive force of gravity arises due to exchange of <a href="/wiki/Virtual_particle" title="Virtual particle">virtual</a> gravitons, in the same way as the electromagnetic force arises from exchange of virtual <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This reproduces general relativity in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_limit" title="Classical limit">classical limit</a>. However, this approach fails at short distances of the order of the <a href="/wiki/Planck_length" title="Planck length">Planck length</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Randall,_Lisa_2005_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Randall,_Lisa_2005-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> where a more complete theory of quantum gravity (or a new approach to quantum mechanics) is required.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifics">Specifics</span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Earth.27s_gravity"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth\'s_gravity">Earth\'s gravity</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Falling_ball.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Falling_ball.jpg/100px-Falling_ball.jpg" width="100" height="336" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Falling_ball.jpg/150px-Falling_ball.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Falling_ball.jpg/200px-Falling_ball.jpg 2x" data-file-width="819" data-file-height="2751" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Falling_ball.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An initially-stationary object which is allowed to fall freely under gravity drops a distance which is proportional to the square of the elapsed time. This image spans half a second and was captured at 20 flashes per second.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_gravity" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s gravity">Earth\'s gravity</a></div>\n<p>Every planetary body (including the Earth) is surrounded by its own gravitational field, which can be conceptualized with Newtonian physics as exerting an attractive force on all objects. Assuming a spherically symmetrical planet, the strength of this field at any given point above the surface is proportional to the planetary body\'s mass and inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the center of the body.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gravity_action-reaction.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Gravity_action-reaction.gif/150px-Gravity_action-reaction.gif" width="150" height="43" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Gravity_action-reaction.gif/225px-Gravity_action-reaction.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Gravity_action-reaction.gif/300px-Gravity_action-reaction.gif 2x" data-file-width="647" data-file-height="185" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gravity_action-reaction.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>If an object with comparable mass to that of the Earth were to fall towards it, then the corresponding acceleration of the Earth would be observable.</div></div></div>\n<p>The strength of the gravitational field is numerically equal to the acceleration of objects under its influence.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The rate of acceleration of falling objects near the Earth\'s surface varies very slightly depending on latitude, surface features such as mountains and ridges, and perhaps unusually high or low sub-surface densities.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> For purposes of weights and measures, a <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">standard gravity</a> value is defined by the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a>, under the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI).\n</p><p>That value, denoted <i>g</i>, is <i>g</i> = 9.80665&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup> (32.1740&#160;ft/s<sup>2</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The standard value of 9.80665&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup> is the one originally adopted by the International Committee on Weights and Measures in 1901 for 45\xc2\xb0 latitude, even though it has been shown to be too high by about five parts in ten thousand.<sup id="cite_ref-List_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> This value has persisted in meteorology and in some standard atmospheres as the value for 45\xc2\xb0 latitude even though it applies more precisely to latitude of 45\xc2\xb032\'33".<sup id="cite_ref-USSA1976_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USSA1976-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Assuming the standardized value for g and ignoring air resistance, this means that an object falling freely near the Earth\'s surface increases its velocity by 9.80665&#160;m/s (32.1740&#160;ft/s or 22&#160;mph) for each second of its descent. Thus, an object starting from rest will attain a velocity of 9.80665&#160;m/s (32.1740&#160;ft/s) after one second, approximately 19.62&#160;m/s (64.4&#160;ft/s) after two seconds, and so on, adding 9.80665&#160;m/s (32.1740&#160;ft/s) to each resulting velocity. Also, again ignoring air resistance, any and all objects, when dropped from the same height, will hit the ground at the same time.\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_3rd_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Newton&#39;s 3rd law">Newton\'s 3rd Law</a>, the Earth itself experiences a <a href="/wiki/Newtons" class="mw-redirect" title="Newtons">force</a> equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to that which it exerts on a falling object.  This means that the Earth also accelerates towards the object until they collide.  Because the mass of the Earth is huge, however, the acceleration imparted to the Earth by this opposite force is negligible in comparison to the object\'s.  If the object doesn\'t bounce after it has collided with the Earth, each of them then exerts a repulsive <a href="/wiki/Contact_force" title="Contact force">contact force</a> on the other which effectively balances the attractive force of gravity and prevents further acceleration.\n</p><p>The force of gravity on Earth is the resultant (vector sum) of two forces:<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  (a) The gravitational attraction in accordance with Newton\'s universal law of gravitation, and (b) the centrifugal force, which results from the choice of an earthbound, rotating frame of reference.  The force of gravity is the weakest at the equator because of the centrifugal force caused by the Earth\'s rotation and because points on the equator are furthest from the center of the Earth.  The force of gravity varies with latitude and increases from about 9.780&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup> at the Equator to about 9.832&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup> at the poles.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equations_for_a_falling_body_near_the_surface_of_the_Earth">Equations for a falling body near the surface of the Earth</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Equations_for_a_falling_body" title="Equations for a falling body">Equations for a falling body</a></div>\n<p>Under an assumption of constant gravitational attraction, <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton&#39;s law of universal gravitation">Newton\'s law of universal gravitation</a> simplifies to <i>F</i> = <i>mg</i>, where <i>m</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of the body and <i>g</i> is a constant vector with an average magnitude of 9.81&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup> on Earth. This resulting force is the object\'s weight. The acceleration due to gravity is equal to this <i>g</i>. An initially stationary object which is allowed to fall freely under gravity drops a distance which is proportional to the square of the elapsed time. even the existence of <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> is inferred using Newton\'s law of gravity. Although we have not traveled to all the planets nor to the Sun, we know their masses. These masses are obtained by applying the laws of gravity to the measured characteristics of the orbit. In space an object maintains its <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> because of the force of gravity acting upon it. Planets orbit stars, stars orbit <a href="/wiki/Bulge_(astronomy)" title="Bulge (astronomy)">galactic centers</a>, galaxies orbit a center of mass in clusters, and clusters orbit in <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">superclusters</a>. The force of gravity exerted on one object by another is directly proportional to the product of those objects\' masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.\n</p><p>The earliest gravity (possibly in the form of quantum gravity, <a href="/wiki/Supergravity" title="Supergravity">supergravity</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_singularity" title="Gravitational singularity">gravitational singularity</a>), along with ordinary space and time, developed during the <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_formation_of_the_Universe#Planck_Epoch" title="Timeline of the formation of the Universe">Planck epoch</a> (up to 10<sup>\xe2\x88\x9243</sup> seconds after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">birth</a> of the Universe), possibly from a primeval state (such as a <a href="/wiki/False_vacuum" title="False vacuum">false vacuum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_vacuum" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantum vacuum">quantum vacuum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Virtual_particle" title="Virtual particle">virtual particle</a>), in a currently unknown manner.<sup id="cite_ref-Planck-UOregon_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planck-UOregon-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gravitational_radiation">Gravitational radiation</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg" class="image"><img alt="LIGO Hanford Observatory" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg/220px-LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg/330px-LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg/440px-LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LIGO_Hanford_aerial_05.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/LIGO" title="LIGO">LIGO</a> Hanford Observatory located in Washington, US where gravitational waves were first observed in September 2015.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">Gravitational wave</a></div>\n<p>According to general relativity, gravitational radiation is generated in situations where the curvature of <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> is oscillating, such as is the case with co-orbiting objects. The gravitational radiation emitted by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> is far too small to measure. However, gravitational radiation has been indirectly observed as an energy loss over time in binary pulsar systems such as <a href="/wiki/PSR_B1913%2B16" class="mw-redirect" title="PSR B1913+16">PSR B1913+16</a>. It is believed that <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron star</a> mergers and <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a> formation may create detectable amounts of gravitational radiation. Gravitational radiation observatories such as the Laser Interferometer Gravitational Wave Observatory (<a href="/wiki/LIGO" title="LIGO">LIGO</a>) have been created to study the problem. In February 2016, the Advanced LIGO team announced that they had detected gravitational waves from a black hole collision. On 14 September 2015, LIGO registered gravitational waves for the first time, as a result of the collision of two black holes 1.3 billion light-years from Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark_2016_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark_2016-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Discovery_2016_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery_2016-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This observation confirms the theoretical predictions of Einstein and others that such waves exist. The event confirms that <a href="/wiki/Binary_black_hole" title="Binary black hole">binary black holes</a> exist. It also opens the way for practical observation and understanding of the nature of gravity and events in the Universe including the Big Bang and what happened after it.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speed_of_gravity">Speed of gravity</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_gravity" title="Speed of gravity">Speed of gravity</a></div>\n<p>In December 2012, a research team in China announced that it had produced measurements of the phase lag of <a href="/wiki/Earth_tide" title="Earth tide">Earth tides</a> during full and new moons which seem to prove that the speed of gravity is equal to the speed of light.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This means that if the Sun suddenly disappeared, the Earth would keep orbiting it normally for 8 minutes, which is the time light takes to travel that distance. The team\'s findings were released in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Science_Bulletin" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Science Bulletin">Chinese Science Bulletin</a> in February 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2017, the <a href="/wiki/LIGO" title="LIGO">LIGO</a> and Virgo detectors received gravitational wave signals within 2 seconds of gamma ray satellites and optical telescopes seeing signals from the same direction. This confirmed that the speed of gravitational waves was the same as the speed of light.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anomalies_and_discrepancies">Anomalies and discrepancies</span></h2>\n<p>There are some observations that are not adequately accounted for, which may point to the need for better theories of gravity or perhaps be explained in other ways.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GalacticRotation2.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/GalacticRotation2.svg/250px-GalacticRotation2.svg.png" width="250" height="150" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="150" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">Rotation curve of a typical spiral galaxy: predicted (<b>A</b>) and observed (<b>B</b>). The discrepancy between the curves is attributed to <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>.</div></div></div>\n<ul><li><b>Extra-fast stars</b>: Stars in galaxies follow a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_rotation_curve" title="Galaxy rotation curve">distribution of velocities</a> where stars on the outskirts are moving faster than they should according to the observed distributions of normal matter. Galaxies within <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_groups_and_clusters" title="Galaxy groups and clusters">galaxy clusters</a> show a similar pattern. <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">Dark matter</a>, which would interact through gravitation but not electromagnetically, would account for the discrepancy. The accelerating expansion of the Universe should stop the photons returning all the energy, but even taking this into account photons from the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> gain twice as much energy as expected. This may indicate that gravity falls off <i>faster</i> than inverse-squared at certain distance scales.<sup id="cite_ref-newsci2699_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsci2699-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Extra massive hydrogen clouds</b>: The spectral lines of the <a href="/wiki/Lyman-alpha_forest" title="Lyman-alpha forest">Lyman-alpha forest</a> suggest that hydrogen clouds are more clumped together at certain scales than expected and, like <a href="/wiki/Dark_flow" title="Dark flow">dark flow</a>, may indicate that gravity falls off <i>slower</i> than inverse-squared at certain distance scales.<sup id="cite_ref-newsci2699_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsci2699-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_theories">Alternative theories</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alternatives_to_general_relativity" title="Alternatives to general relativity">Alternatives to general relativity</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_alternative_theories">Historical alternative theories</span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aristotelian_theory_of_gravity" class="mw-redirect" title="Aristotelian theory of gravity">Aristotelian theory of gravity</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Le_Sage%27s_theory_of_gravitation" title="Le Sage&#39;s theory of gravitation">Le Sage\'s theory of gravitation</a> (1784) also called LeSage gravity, proposed by <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Le_Sage" title="Georges-Louis Le Sage">Georges-Louis Le Sage</a>, based on a fluid-based explanation where a light gas fills the entire Universe.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ritz%27s_Equation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ritz&#39;s Equation">Ritz\'s theory of gravitation</a>, <i>Ann.
