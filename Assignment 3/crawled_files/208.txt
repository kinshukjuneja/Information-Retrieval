It appears that colonisation took place by sea sometime during the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> era (about 6500 BC).<sup id="cite_ref-Brad8_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brad8-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The island has been visited by various <a href="/wiki/Raid_(military)" title="Raid (military)">raiders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trading peoples</a> over the years. After being settled by people from Ireland in the first millennium<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">AD or BC?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, the Isle of Man was converted to Christianity and then suffered raids by <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Vikings</a> from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. After becoming subject to Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Mann and the Isles">Kingdom of Mann and the Isles</a>, the Isle of Man later became a possession of the Scottish and then the English crowns.\n</p><p>Since 1866, the Isle of Man has been a <a href="/wiki/Crown_Dependency" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Dependency">Crown Dependency</a> and has <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Self-government" Text reads BIVAIDONAS MAQI MUCOI CUNAVA[LI]; in English: Of Bivaidonas, son of the tribe Cunava[li].</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mesolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Isle of Man effectively became an island around 8,500 years ago at around the time when rising sea levels caused by the melting glaciers cut <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> Britain off from continental Europe for the last time. A land bridge had earlier existed between the Isle of Man and Cumbria, but the location and opening of the land bridge remain poorly understood.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The earliest traces of people on the Isle of Man date back to the Mesolithic Period, also known as the Middle Stone Age. The first residents lived in small natural shelters, <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunting, gathering and fishing</a> for their food. They used small tools made of <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> or bone, examples of which have been found near the coast. Representatives of these artifacts are kept at the <a href="/wiki/Manx_National_Heritage" title="Manx National Heritage">Manx National Heritage</a> museum.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic_to_Bronze_Age">Neolithic to Bronze Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neolithic to Bronze Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> Period marked the coming of <a href="/wiki/Farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Farming">farming</a>, improved <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>. During this period <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic monuments</a> began to appear around the island. Examples are found at Cashtal yn Ard near <a href="/wiki/Maughold" title="Maughold">Maughold</a>, King Orry\'s Grave in <a href="/wiki/Laxey" title="Laxey">Laxey</a>, Meayll Circle near <a href="/wiki/Cregneash" title="Cregneash">Cregneash</a>, and Ballaharra Stones in <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s,_Isle_of_Man" title="St John&#39;s, Isle of Man">St John\'s</a>. The builders of the megaliths were not the only culture during this time; there are also remains of the local <a href="/wiki/Ronaldsway_culture" title="Ronaldsway culture">Ronaldsway culture</a> (lasting from the late Neolithic into the Bronze Age)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age">Iron Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Iron Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> marked the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> cultural influence. Large <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a> appeared on hill summits and smaller promontory forts along the coastal cliffs, whilst large timber-framed <a href="/wiki/Roundhouse_(dwelling)" title="Roundhouse (dwelling)">roundhouses</a> were built.\n</p><p>It is likely that the first Celts to inhabit the Island were <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Brythonic</a> tribes from mainland Britain. The secular history of the Isle of Man during the Brythonic period remains mysterious. It is not known if the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Romans</a> ever made a landing on the island; if they did, little evidence has been discovered; however there is evidence for contact with Roman Britain as an <a href="/wiki/Amphora" title="Amphora">amphora</a> was discovered at the settlement on the <a href="/wiki/South_Barrule" title="South Barrule">South Barrule</a>; it is hypothesised this may have been trade goods or plunder. It has been speculated that the island may have become a haven for <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">Druids</a> and other refugees from <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> after the sacking of <a href="/wiki/Anglesey#History" title="Anglesey">Mona</a> in AD 60.\n</p><p>It is generally assumed that Irish invasion or immigration formed the basis of the modern Manx language; Irish migration to the island probably began in the 5th century AD. This is evident in the change in language used in <a href="/wiki/Ogham" title="Ogham">Ogham</a> inscriptions. The transition between <i>Manx Brythonic</i> (a <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic language</a> like modern <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Manx_Gaelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Manx Gaelic">Manx Gaelic</a></i> (a <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">Goidelic language</a> like modern <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish Gaelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>) may have been gradual. One question is whether the present-day Manx language survives from pre-Norse days or reflects a linguistic reintroduction after the Norse invasion. The island lends its name to <i><a href="/wiki/Manann%C3%A1n_mac_Lir" title="Manann\xc3\xa1n mac Lir">Manann\xc3\xa1n</a></i>, the Brythonic and Gaelic sea god who is said in myth to have once ruled the island.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Middle_Ages">Early Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tradition attributes the island\'s conversion to Christianity to <a href="/wiki/St_Maughold" class="mw-redirect" title="St Maughold">St Maughold</a> (Maccul), an Irish missionary who gives his name to a parish. There are the remains of around 200 tiny early chapels called <a href="/wiki/Keeill" title="Keeill">keeils</a> scattered across the island. Evidence such as <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_drift&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Magnetic drift (page does not exist)">magnetic drift</a> points to many of these being built around AD 550-600.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Brythonic</a> culture of <i>Manaw</i> appears throughout early British tradition and later <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Welsh_literature" title="Medieval Welsh literature">Welsh writings</a>. The family origins of <a href="/wiki/Gwriad_ap_Elidyr" title="Gwriad ap Elidyr">Gwriad ap Elidyr</a> (father of <a href="/wiki/Merfyn_Frych" title="Merfyn Frych">Merfyn Frych</a> and grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_the_Great" title="Rhodri the Great">Rhodri the Great</a>) are attributed to a <i>Manaw</i> and he is sometimes named as  <i>Gwriad Manaw</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lloyd324_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lloyd324-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The 1896 discovery of a cross inscribed <i>Crux Guriat</i> (Cross of Gwriad) and dated to the 8th or 9th century greatly supports this theory.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The best record of any event before the incursions of the Northmen is attributed to <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1et%C3%A1n_mac_Cairill" title="B\xc3\xa1et\xc3\xa1n mac Cairill">B\xc3\xa1et\xc3\xa1n mac Cairill</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>, who (according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Ulster" title="Annals of Ulster">Annals of Ulster</a></i>) led an expedition to Man in 577-578, imposing his authority on the island (though some have thought this event may refer to <a href="/wiki/Manaw_Gododdin" title="Manaw Gododdin">Manau Gododdin</a> between the Firths of <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Clyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Forth</a>, rather than the Isle of Man). After B\xc3\xa1et\xc3\xa1n\'s death in 581, his rival <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ed%C3%A1n_mac_Gabr%C3%A1in" title="\xc3\x81ed\xc3\xa1n mac Gabr\xc3\xa1in">\xc3\x81ed\xc3\xa1n mac Gabr\xc3\xa1in</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Riata" title="D\xc3\xa1l Riata">D\xc3\xa1l Riata</a>, is said to have taken the island in 582.\n</p><p>Even if the supposed conquest of the Menavian islands \xe2\x80\x93 Mann and <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> \xe2\x80\x93 by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_of_Northumbria" title="Edwin of Northumbria">Edwin of Northumbria</a>, in 616, did take place, it could not have led to any permanent results, for when the English were driven from the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> soon afterwards, they could not well have retained their hold on the island to the west of these coasts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> One can speculate, however, that when <a href="/wiki/Ecgfrith_of_Northumbria" title="Ecgfrith of Northumbria">Ecgfri\xc3\xb0</a>\'s Northumbrians laid Ireland waste from Dublin to <a href="/wiki/Drogheda" title="Drogheda">Drogheda</a> in 684, they temporarily occupied Mann.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viking_Age_and_Norse_kingdom">Viking Age and Norse kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Viking Age and Norse kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Isles" title="Kingdom of the Isles">Kingdom of the Isles</a></div>\n<p>The period of Scandinavian domination is divided into two main epochs \xe2\x80\x93 before and after the conquest of Mann by <a href="/wiki/Godred_Crovan" title="Godred Crovan">Godred Crovan</a> in 1079. Warfare and unsettled rule characterise the earlier epoch; the later saw comparatively more peace.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg/220px-Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg.png" width="220" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg/330px-Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg/440px-Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1297" data-file-height="1362" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Mann and the Isles">Kingdom of Mann and the Isles</a> about the year 1100. Sodor and Mann in red.</div></div></div>\n<p>Between about AD 800 and 815 the Vikings came to Mann chiefly for plunder; between about 850 and 990, when they settled there, the island fell under the rule of the Scandinavian <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Dublin" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Dublin">Kings of Dublin</a>; and between 990 and 1079, it became subject to the powerful <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Orkney" title="Earl of Orkney">Earls of Orkney</a>.\n</p><p>There was a mint producing coins on Mann between c. 1025 and c. 1065. These Manx coins were minted from an imported type 2 <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Norse" class="mw-redirect" title="Hiberno-Norse">Hiberno-Norse</a> penny die from <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. Hiberno-Norse coins were first minted under Sihtric, <a href="/wiki/King_of_Dublin" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Dublin">King of Dublin</a>. This illustrates that Mann may have been under the thumb of Dublin at this time.\n</p><p>The conqueror <a href="/wiki/Godred_Crovan" title="Godred Crovan">Godred Crovan</a> was evidently a remarkable man, though little is known about him. According to the <i>Chronicon Manniae</i> he subdued Dublin, and a great part of <a href="/wiki/Leinster" title="Leinster">Leinster</a>, and held the Scots in such subjection that supposedly no one who set out to build a vessel dared to insert more than three bolts. The memory of such a ruler would be likely to survive in tradition, and it seems probable therefore that he is the person commemorated in Manx legend under the name of King Gorse or Orry. He created the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mann_and_the_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Mann and the Isles">Kingdom of Mann and the Isles</a> in around 1079; it included the south-western islands of Scotland until 1164, when two separate kingdoms were formed from it. In 1154, what was later to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Sodor_and_Man" title="Diocese of Sodor and Man">Diocese of Sodor and Man</a> was formed by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>.\n</p><p>The islands which were under his rule were called the <i>Su\xc3\xb0r-eyjar</i> (south isles, in contradistinction to the <i>Nor\xc3\xb0r-eyjar</i>, or the "north isles", i.e. Orkney and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>), and they consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>, and of all the smaller western islands of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and Mann. At a later date his successors took the title of <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Rex Manniae et Insularum</i> (<a href="/wiki/King_of_Mann_and_the_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Mann and the Isles">King of Mann and of the Isles</a>). The kingdom\'s capital was on <a href="/wiki/St_Patrick%27s_Isle" title="St Patrick&#39;s Isle">St Patrick\'s Isle</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Peel_Castle" title="Peel Castle">Peel Castle</a> was built on the site of a Celtic monastery.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Olaf_I_Godredsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Olaf I Godredsson">Olaf</a>, Godred\'s son, exercised considerable power, and according to the Chronicle, maintained such close alliance with the kings of Ireland and Scotland that no one ventured to disturb the Isles during his time (1113\xe2\x80\x931152). In 1156, his son, <a href="/wiki/Godred_II_Olafsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Godred II Olafsson">Godred</a> (reigned 1153\xe2\x80\x931158), who for a short period ruled over Dublin also, lost the smaller islands off the coast of Argyll as a result of a quarrel with <a href="/wiki/Somerled" title="Somerled">Somerled</a> (the ruler of <a href="/wiki/Argyll" title="Argyll">Argyll</a>). An independent sovereignty thus appeared between<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the two divisions of his kingdom.\n</p><p>In the 1130s the Catholic Church sent a small mission to establish the first <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">bishopric</a> on the Isle of Man, and appointed <a href="/wiki/Wimund" title="Wimund">Wimund</a> as the first bishop. He soon afterwards embarked with a band of followers on a career of murder and looting throughout Scotland and the surrounding islands.\n</p><p>During the whole of the Scandinavian period, the Isles remained nominally under the suzerainty of the <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Norway">Kings of Norway</a>, but the Norwegians only occasionally asserted it with any vigour. The first such king to assert control over the region was likely <a href="/wiki/Magnus_III_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus III of Norway">Magnus Barelegs</a>, at the turn of the 12th century. It was not until Hakon Hakonarson\'s 1263 expedition that another king returned to the Isles.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_Norse_rule">Decline of Norse rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Decline of Norse rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/13/NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg/300px-NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg" width="300" height="252" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/13/NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg/450px-NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/13/NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg/600px-NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg 2x" data-file-width="879" data-file-height="738" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NW_Britain_11th_cent.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>11th century (north is to the right)</div></div></div>\n<p>From the middle of the 12th century until 1217 the suzerainty had remained of a very shadowy character; Norway had become a prey to civil dissensions. But after that date it became a reality, and Norway consequently came into collision with the growing power of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">kingdom of Scotland</a>.\n</p><p>Early in the 13th century, when <a href="/wiki/Ragnald_IV_of_the_Isle_of_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Ragnald IV of the Isle of Man">Ragnald</a> (reigned 1187\xe2\x80\x931229) paid homage to King <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">John of England</a> (reigned 1199\xe2\x80\x931216), we hear for the first time of English intervention in the affairs of Mann. But a period of Scots domination would precede the establishment of full English control.\n</p><p>Finally, in 1261, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_III_of_Scotland" title="Alexander III of Scotland">Alexander III of Scotland</a> sent envoys to Norway to negotiate for the cession of the isles, but their efforts led to no result. He therefore initiated a war, which ended in the indecisive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Largs" title="Battle of Largs">Battle of Largs</a> against the Norwegian fleet in 1263. However, the Norwegian king <a href="/wiki/Haakon_IV_of_Norway" title="Haakon IV of Norway">Haakon Haakonsson</a> died the following winter, and this allowed King Alexander to bring the war to a successful conclusion. <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Olafsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus Olafsson">Magnus Olafsson, King of Mann and the Isles</a> (reigned 1252\xe2\x80\x931265), who had campaigned on the Norwegian side, had to surrender all the islands over which he had ruled, except Mann, for which he did <a href="/wiki/Homage_(feudal)" title="Homage (feudal)">homage</a>. Two years later Magnus died and in 1266 King <a href="/wiki/Magnus_VI_of_Norway" title="Magnus VI of Norway">Magnus VI of Norway</a> ceded the islands, including Mann, to Scotland in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Perth" title="Treaty of Perth">Treaty of Perth</a> in consideration of the sum of 4,000 <a href="/wiki/Mark_(money)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark (money)">marks</a> (known as <i lang="sco" title="Scots language text">merks</i> in Scotland) and an <a href="/wiki/Annuity" title="Annuity">annuity</a> of 100 marks. But Scotland\'s rule over Mann did not become firmly established till 1275, when the Manx suffered defeat in the decisive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ronaldsway" title="Battle of Ronaldsway">Battle of Ronaldsway</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Castletown,_Isle_of_Man" title="Castletown, Isle of Man">Castletown</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_dominance">English dominance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: English dominance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1290 King <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a> sent <a href="/wiki/Walter_de_Huntercombe,_1st_Baron_Huntercombe" title="Walter de Huntercombe, 1st Baron Huntercombe">Walter de Huntercombe</a> to seize possession of Mann, and it remained in English hands until 1313, when <a href="/wiki/Robert_I_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert I of Scotland">Robert Bruce</a> took it after besieging <a href="/wiki/Castle_Rushen" title="Castle Rushen">Castle Rushen</a> for five weeks. In about 1333 King <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III of England</a> granted Mann to <a href="/wiki/William_Montacute,_1st_Earl_of_Salisbury" class="mw-redirect" title="William Montacute, 1st Earl of Salisbury">William de Montacute</a>, 3rd <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a> Montacute (later the 1st <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Salisbury" title="Earl of Salisbury">Earl of Salisbury</a>), as his absolute possession, without reserving any service to be rendered to him. \n</p><p>Then, in 1346, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Neville%27s_Cross" title="Battle of Neville&#39;s Cross">Battle of Neville\'s Cross</a> decided the long struggle between England and Scotland in England\'s favour. King <a href="/wiki/David_II_of_Scotland" title="David II of Scotland">David II of Scotland</a>, Robert Bruce\'s last male heir, had been captured in the Battle of Neville\'s cross and ransomed; however, when Scotland was unable to raise one of the ransom installments, David made a secret agreement with King <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III of England</a> to cancel it, in return for transferring the Scottish kingdom to an English prince.\n</p><p>Following the secret agreement, there followed a confused period when Mann sometimes suffered English rule and sometimes Scottish. In 1388 the island was "ravaged" by <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Douglas_of_Nithsdale" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir William Douglas of Nithsdale">Sir William Douglas of Nithsdale</a> on his way home from the destruction of the town of <a href="/wiki/Carlingford,_County_Louth" title="Carlingford, County Louth">Carlingford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In 1392 William de Montacute\'s son sold the island, including sovereignty, to Sir <a href="/wiki/William_le_Scrope" class="mw-redirect" title="William le Scrope">William le Scrope</a>. In 1399 <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">Henry Bolinbroke</a> brought about the beheading of Le Scrope, who had taken the side of <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II</a> when Bolinbroke usurped the throne and appointed himself <i>Henry IV</i>. The island then came into the de facto possession of Henry, who granted it to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Percy,_1st_Earl_of_Northumberland" title="Henry Percy, 1st Earl of Northumberland">Henry Percy, 1st Earl of Northumberland</a>; but following the latter\'s later attainder, Henry IV, in 1405, made a lifetime grant of it, with the patronage of the bishopric, to Sir <a href="/wiki/John_I_Stanley_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="John I Stanley of the Isle of Man">John Stanley</a>. In 1406 this grant was extended \xe2\x80\x93 on a <a href="/wiki/Feudatory" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudatory">feudatory</a> basis under the English Crown \xe2\x80\x93 to Sir John\'s heirs and assigns, the feudal fee being the service of rendering <a href="/wiki/Homage_(medieval)" class="mw-redirect" title="Homage (medieval)">homage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tribute" title="Tribute">two falcons</a> to all future Kings of England on their <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">coronations</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_period">Early Modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Early Modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With the accession of the Stanleys to the throne there begins a more settled epoch in Manx history. Though the island\'s new rulers rarely visited its shores, they placed it under governors, who, in the main, seem to have treated it with the justice of the time. Of the thirteen members of the family who ruled in Mann, the second <a href="/wiki/John_II_Stanley_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="John II Stanley of the Isle of Man">Sir John Stanley</a> (1414\xe2\x80\x931432), <a href="/wiki/James_Stanley,_7th_Earl_of_Derby" title="James Stanley, 7th Earl of Derby">James, the 7th Earl</a> (1627\xe2\x80\x931651), and the <a href="/wiki/James_Stanley,_10th_Earl_of_Derby" title="James Stanley, 10th Earl of Derby">10th Earl of the same name</a> (1702\xe2\x80\x931736) had the most important influence on it. They first curbed the power of the spiritual barons<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, introduced trial by jury, which superseded <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_battle" class="mw-redirect" title="Trial by battle">trial by battle</a>, and ordered the laws to be written. The second, known as the Great Stanley, and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Stanley,_Countess_of_Derby" title="Charlotte Stanley, Countess of Derby">Charlotte de la Tremoille</a> (or Tremouille), are probably the most striking figures in Manx history.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Civil_War_and_Interregnum">English Civil War and Interregnum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: English Civil War and Interregnum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1643 <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> ordered <a href="/wiki/James_Stanley,_7th_Earl_of_Derby" title="James Stanley, 7th Earl of Derby">James Stanley, 7th Earl of Derby</a> to go to Mann, where the people, no doubt influenced by events in England, threatened to revolt.\n</p><p>Stanley\'s arrival, with English soldiers, soon put a stop to anything of this kind. He conciliated the people by his affability, brought in Englishmen to teach various handicrafts and tried to help the farmers by improving the breed of Manx horses, and, at the same time, he restricted the exactions of the Church. But the Manx also lost much of their liberty under his rule: they were heavily taxed; troops were quartered upon them; and they also had the more lasting grievance of being compelled to accept leases for three lifetimes instead of holding their land by the straw tenure, which they considered to be equivalent to a customary inheritance.\n</p><p>Six months after the death of Charles I (on 30 January 1649), Stanley received a summons from <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ireton" title="Henry Ireton">General Ireton</a> to surrender the island, but he declined to do so. In August 1651 Stanley went to England with some of his troops, among whom were 300 Manxmen, to join <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">King Charles II</a>. Charles was decisively defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Worcester" title="Battle of Worcester">Battle of Worcester</a> and Stanley was captured, imprisoned in <a href="/wiki/Chester_Castle" title="Chester Castle">Chester Castle</a> and then tried by <a href="/wiki/Court-martial" title="Court-martial">court-martial</a> and executed at <a href="/wiki/Bolton" title="Bolton">Bolton</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebellion">Rebellion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rebellion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Soon after Stanley\'s death, the Manx Militia, under the command of William Christian (known by his <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a> name of <a href="/wiki/Illiam_Dhone" title="Illiam Dhone">Illiam Dhone</a>), rose against the Countess and captured all the insular forts except Rushen and Peel. They were then joined by a Parliamentary force under <a href="/wiki/Robert_Duckenfield" title="Robert Duckenfield">Colonel Duckenfield</a>, to whom the Countess surrendered after a brief resistance.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> had appointed <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fairfax,_3rd_Lord_Fairfax_of_Cameron" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Fairfax, 3rd Lord Fairfax of Cameron">Thomas Fairfax</a> "Lord of Mann and the Isles" in September 1651, so that Mann continued under a monarchical government and remained in the same relation to England as before.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration_of_the_Stanleys">Restoration of the Stanleys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Restoration of the Stanleys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The restoration of Stanley government in 1660 therefore caused as little friction and alteration as its temporary cessation had. One of the first acts of the new Lord, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stanley,_8th_Earl_of_Derby" title="Charles Stanley, 8th Earl of Derby">Charles Stanley, 8th Earl of Derby</a>, was to order Christian to be tried. He was found guilty and executed. Of the other persons implicated in the rebellion only three were excepted from the general amnesty. But by <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a>, Charles II pardoned them, and the judges responsible for the sentence on Christian were punished.\n</p><p>Charles Stanley\'s next act was to dispute the permanency of the tenants\' holdings, which they had not at first regarded as being affected by the acceptance of leases, a proceeding which led to an almost open rebellion against his authority and to the neglect of agriculture, in lieu of which the people devoted themselves to the fisheries and to contraband trade.\n</p><p>Charles Stanley, who died in 1672, was succeeded first by his son William Richard George Stanley, 9th Earl of Derby until his death in 1702.\n</p><p>The agrarian question subsided only in 1704, when <a href="/wiki/James_Stanley,_10th_Earl_of_Derby" title="James Stanley, 10th Earl of Derby">James Stanley, 10th Earl of Derby</a>, William\'s brother and successor, largely through the influence of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wilson_(bishop)" title="Thomas Wilson (bishop)">Bishop Wilson</a>, entered into a compact with his tenants, which became embodied in an Act, called the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1703" title="Act of Settlement 1703">Act of Settlement</a>. Their compact secured the tenants in the possession of their estates in perpetuity subject only to a fixed rent, and a small fine on succession or <a href="/wiki/Alienation_(property_law)" title="Alienation (property law)">alienation</a>. From the great importance of this act to the Manx people it has been called their <i><a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a></i>. As time went on, and the value of the estates increased, the rent payable to the Lord became so small in proportion as to be almost nominal, being extinguished by purchase in 1916.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revestment">Revestment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Revestment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>James died in 1736, and the <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a> of the isle passed to <a href="/wiki/James_Murray,_2nd_Duke_of_Atholl" title="James Murray, 2nd Duke of Atholl">James Murray, 2nd Duke of Atholl</a>, his first cousin and heir-male. In 1764 he was succeeded by his only surviving child <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Murray,_Duchess_of_Atholl" title="Charlotte Murray, Duchess of Atholl">Charlotte, Baroness Strange</a>, and her husband, <a href="/wiki/John_Murray,_3rd_Duke_of_Atholl" title="John Murray, 3rd Duke of Atholl">John Murray</a>, who (in right of his wife) became <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Mann" title="Lord of Mann">Lord of Mann</a>. In about 1720 the contraband trade had greatly increased. In 1726 <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a> had checked it somewhat for a time, but during the last ten years of the Atholl regime (1756\xe2\x80\x931765) it assumed such proportions that, in the interests of the Imperial revenue, it became necessary to suppress it. With a view to so doing, Parliament passed the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Purchase_Act_1765" title="Isle of Man Purchase Act 1765">Isle of Man Purchase Act 1765</a> (commonly called the <i>Revestment Act</i> by the Manx), under which it purchased the rights of the Atholls as <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Mann" title="Lord of Mann">Lords of Mann</a>, including the customs revenues of the island, for the sum of \xc2\xa370,000 <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">sterling</a>, and granted an <a href="/wiki/Annuity_(financial_contracts)" class="mw-redirect" title="Annuity (financial contracts)">annuity</a> to the Duke and Duchess. The Atholls still retained their <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorial</a> rights, the patronage of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Sodor_and_Man" title="Bishop of Sodor and Man">bishopric</a>, and certain other <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/perquisites" class="extiw" title="wikt:perquisites">perquisites</a>, until they sold them for the sum of \xc2\xa3417,144 in 1828.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg/220px-Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg/330px-Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg/440px-Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="546" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Iom_mnh_1954_7451_large.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The scene in Ramsey Bay after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bishops_Court" title="Battle of Bishops Court">Battle of Bishops Court</a> between the English and French squadrons, enacted off the Manx coast in 1760</div></div></div>\n<p>Up to the time of the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/revestment" class="extiw" title="wikt:revestment">revestment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a> had passed laws concerning the government of the island in all respects and had control over its finances, subject to the approval of the Lord of Mann. After the revestment, or rather after the passage of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smuggling_Act_1765&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Smuggling Act 1765 (page does not exist)">Smuggling Act 1765</a> (commonly called the Mischief Act by the Manx), the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a> at Westminster legislated with respect to customs, harbours and merchant shipping, and, in measures of a general character, it occasionally inserted clauses permitting the enforcement in the island of penalties in contravention of the Acts of which they formed part. It also assumed the control of the insular customs duties. Such changes, rather than the transference of the full <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a> to the King of Great Britain and Ireland, modified the (unwritten) constitution of the Isle of Man. Its ancient laws and tenures remained untouched, but in many ways the revestment affected it adversely. The hereditary <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Mann" title="Lord of Mann">Lords of Mann</a> had seldom, if ever, functioned as model rulers, but most of them had taken some personal share in its government, and had interested themselves in the well-being of the inhabitants. But now the whole direction of its affairs became the work of officials who regarded the island as a pestilent nest of smugglers, from which it seemed their duty to extract as much revenue as possible.\n</p><p>There was some alleviation of this state of things between 1793 and 1826, when <a href="/wiki/John_Murray,_4th_Duke_of_Atholl" title="John Murray, 4th Duke of Atholl">John Murray, 4th Duke of Atholl</a> served as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Governor of the Isle of Man">Governor</a>, since, though he quarrelled with the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Keys" title="House of Keys">House of Keys</a> and unduly cared for his own pecuniary interests, he did occasionally exert himself to promote the welfare of the island. After his departure the English officials resumed their sway, but they showed more consideration than before. Moreover, since smuggling, which the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Purchase_Act_1765" title="Isle of Man Purchase Act 1765">Isle of Man Purchase Act</a> had only checked \xe2\x80\x93 not suppressed \xe2\x80\x93 had by that time almost disappeared, and since the Manx revenue had started to produce a large and increasing surplus, the authorities looked more favourably on the Isle of Man, and, thanks to this fact and to the representations of the Manx people to British ministers in 1837, 1844 and 1853, it obtained a somewhat less stringent customs <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariff</a> and an occasional <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dole" class="extiw" title="wikt:dole">dole</a> towards erecting its much neglected public works.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_period">Modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since 1866, when the Isle of Man obtained a nominal measure of <a href="/wiki/Home_rule" title="Home rule">Home Rule</a>, the Manx people have made remarkable progress, and currently form a prosperous community, with a thriving <a href="/wiki/Offshore_financial_centre" title="Offshore financial centre">offshore financial centre</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourist</a> industry (albeit smaller than in the past) and a variety of other industries.\n</p><p>The Isle of Man was a base for alien civilian <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">internment</a> camps in both the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> (1914\xe2\x80\x9318) and the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> (1939\xe2\x80\x9345). During the First World War there were two camps: one a requisitioned holiday camp in <a href="/wiki/Douglas,_Isle_of_Man" title="Douglas, Isle of Man">Douglas</a> and the other the purpose-built <a href="/wiki/Knockaloe_internment_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Knockaloe internment camp">Knockaloe camp</a> near <a href="/wiki/Peel,_Isle_of_Man" title="Peel, Isle of Man">Peel</a> in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Patrick_(parish)" title="Patrick (parish)">Patrick</a>. During the Second World War there were a number of smaller camps in Douglas, Peel, <a href="/wiki/Port_Erin" title="Port Erin">Port Erin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramsey,_Isle_of_Man" title="Ramsey, Isle of Man">Ramsey</a>. The (now disbanded) <a href="/wiki/Manx_Regiment" title="Manx Regiment">Manx Regiment</a> was raised in 1938 and saw action during the Second World War.\n</p><p>On 2 August 1973, a <a href="/wiki/Summerland_disaster" title="Summerland disaster">flash fire killed between 50 and 53 people</a> at the Summerland amusement centre in <a href="/wiki/Douglas,_Isle_of_Man" title="Douglas, Isle of Man">Douglas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_autonomy">Greater autonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Isle_of_Man&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Greater autonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The early 20th century saw a revival of music, dance, and a limited revival of the <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx language</a>, although the last "native" speaker of Manx Gaelic died in the 1970s. In the middle of the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a>, visited, and was so dissatisfied with the lack of support for Manx that he immediately had two recording vans sent over. During the 20th century the Manx tourist economy declined, as the English and Irish started flying to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> for <a href="/wiki/Package_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Package holiday">package holidays</a>. The Manx Government responded to this by successfully promoting the island, with its low tax rates, as an offshore financial centre, although it has avoided being placed on a recent UK black list of tax havens.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The financial centre has had its detractors who have pointed to the potential for <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">money laundering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1949 an <a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Executive Council of the Isle of Man">Executive Council</a>, chaired by the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Lieutenant Governor of the Isle of Man">Lieutenant-Governor</a> and including members of <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a>, was created. This was the start of a transfer of executive power from the unelected Lieutenant Governor to democratically elected Manx politicians. Finance and the police passed to Manx control between 1958 and 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1980 the Lieutenant Governor was replaced as Chairman of the Executive Council by a chairman elected by Tynwald.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Following legislation in 1984, the Executive Council was reconstituted in 1985 to include the chairmen of the eight principal Boards;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> in 1986 they were given the title of Minister and the chairman was retitled Chief Minister.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1986 <a href="/wiki/Miles_Walker" title="Miles Walker">Sir Miles Walker CBE</a> became the first <a href="/wiki/Chief_Minister_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Chief Minister of the Isle of Man">Chief Minister of the Isle of Man</a>. In 1990 the Executive Council was renamed the Council of Ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1960s also saw a rise in Manx nationalism, spawning the parties <a href="/wiki/Mec_Vannin" title="Mec Vannin">Mec Vannin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Manx_National_Party" title="Manx National Party">Manx National Party</a>, as well as the now defunct <i lang="gv" title="Manx language text">Fo Halloo</i> (literally "Underground"), which mounted a direct-action campaign of spray-painting and attempted house-burning.\n</p><p>On 5 July 1973, control of the postal service passed from the UK <a href="/wiki/General_Post_Office" title="General Post Office">General Post Office</a> to the new <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Man Post">Isle of Man Post</a>, which began to issue its own <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a>.\n</p><p>The 1990s and early 21st century have seen a greater recognition of indigenous Manx culture, including the opening of the first Manx language primary school, as well as a general re-evaluation of the island\'s economy.\n</p><p>From 1983 on, the Isle of Man government has designated more than 250 historic structures as <a href="/wiki/Registered_Buildings_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Registered Buildings of the Isle of Man">Registered Buildings of the Isle of Man</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
