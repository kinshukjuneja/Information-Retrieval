href="http://neilgunn.org.uk">neilgunn<wbr />.org<wbr />.uk</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Neil Miller Gunn</b> (8 November 1891 \xe2\x80\x93 15 January 1973) was a prolific novelist, critic, and dramatist who emerged as one of the leading lights of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Renaissance" title="Scottish Renaissance">Scottish Renaissance</a> of the 1920s and 1930s.  With over twenty novels to his credit, Gunn was arguably the most influential <a href="/wiki/Scottish_literature" title="Scottish literature">Scottish</a> fiction writer of the first half of the 20th century (with the possible exception of <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Grassic_Gibbon" title="Lewis Grassic Gibbon">Lewis Grassic Gibbon</a>, the pen name of <a href="/wiki/James_Leslie_Mitchell" class="mw-redirect" title="James Leslie Mitchell">James Leslie Mitchell</a>).\n</p><p>Like his contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a>, Gunn was politically committed to the ideals of both Scottish nationalism and socialism (a difficult balance to maintain for a writer of his time). His fiction deals primarily with the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highland</a> communities and landscapes of his youth, though the author chose (<i>contra</i> MacDiarmid and his followers) to write almost exclusively in English rather than <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Gaelic</a> but was heavily influenced in his writing style by the language.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-10"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">10</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_M._Gunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Neil Miller Gunn was born in the village of <a href="/wiki/Dunbeath" title="Dunbeath">Dunbeath</a>, Caithness. His father was the captain of a <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a> boat, and Gunn\'s fascination with the sea and the courage of fishermen can be traced directly back to his childhood memories of his father\'s work.  His mother would also provide Gunn with a crucial model for the types of steadfast, earthy, and tradition-bearing women that would populate many of his works.\n</p><p>Gunn had eight siblings, and when his primary schooling was completed in 1904, he moved south to live with his older sister Mary and her husband Dr. Keiller, the local GP at Kenbank in <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_Town_of_Dalry" title="St John&#39;s Town of Dalry">St John\'s Town of Dalry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kirkcudbrightshire" title="Kirkcudbrightshire">Kirkcudbrightshire</a>. He continued his education there with tutors including the local schoolmaster and the writer and poet J.G.Carter "<i>Theodore</i> <i>Mayne</i>". He sat the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Service_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Service (United Kingdom)">Civil Service</a> exam in 1907.  This led to a move to London, where the adolescent Gunn was exposed to both the exciting world of new political and philosophical ideas as well as to the seamier side of modern urban life. In 1910 Gunn became a <a href="/wiki/HM_Customs_and_Excise" title="HM Customs and Excise">Customs and Excise</a> Officer and was posted back to the Highlands. He would remain a customs officer throughout the First World War and until he was well established as a writer in 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_M._Gunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Gunn married <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jessie_Dallas_Frew&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jessie Dallas Frew (page does not exist)">Jessie Dallas Frew</a> in 1921 and they settled in <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a>, near his permanent excise post at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glen_Mhor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Glen Mhor (page does not exist)">Glen Mhor</a> <a href="/wiki/Distillery" class="mw-redirect" title="Distillery">distillery</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings_as_a_writer">Beginnings as a writer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_M._Gunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beginnings as a writer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 1920s Gunn began to publish short stories, as well as poems and short essays, in various literary magazines.  His writing brought him into contact with other writers associated with the budding <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Renaissance" title="Scottish Renaissance">Scottish Renaissance</a>, such as Hugh MacDiarmid, <a href="/wiki/James_Bridie" title="James Bridie">James Bridie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Mitchison" title="Naomi Mitchison">Naomi Mitchison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Linklater" title="Eric Linklater">Eric Linklater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Muir" title="Edwin Muir">Edwin Muir</a>, Lewis Grassic Gibbon, and George Blake.\n</p><p>Blake and <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Malcolm_Thomson_(1899_-_1996)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="George Malcolm Thomson (1899 - 1996) (page does not exist)">George Malcolm Thomson</a> were the founders of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Porpoise_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Porpoise Press (page does not exist)">Porpoise Press</a>, whose mission was to reestablish a national publishing industry for Scotland, and they became Gunn\'s publisher until their absorption by <a href="/wiki/Faber_and_Faber" title="Faber and Faber">Faber and Faber</a> in the mid-1930s. The first novels Gunn published were <i>The Grey Coast</i> in 1926 and <i>The Lost Glen</i> in 1928. During this period, Gunn was active in the <a href="/wiki/National_Party_of_Scotland" title="National Party of Scotland">National Party of Scotland</a>, which formed part of what became the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_professional_writer">The professional writer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_M._Gunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The professional writer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG/220px-Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG/330px-Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG/440px-Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Neil_Gunn_memorial.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Part of the Neil Gunn memorial above <a href="/wiki/Strathpeffer" title="Strathpeffer">Strathpeffer</a>, erected by the Neil M. Gunn Memorial Trust.</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the publishing success of <i>Highland River</i> (for which he was awarded the 1937 <a href="/wiki/James_Tait_Black_Memorial_Prize" title="James Tait Black Memorial Prize">James Tait Black Memorial Prize</a> for fiction), Gunn was able to resign from the Customs and Excise in 1937 and become a full-time writer. He rented a farmhouse near <a href="/wiki/Strathpeffer" title="Strathpeffer">Strathpeffer</a> and embarked on his most productive period as a novelist and essayist. <i>Butcher\'s Broom</i> and <i>The Silver Darlings</i> are historical novels dealing with the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">Highland Clearances</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Art_and_Old_Hector" title="Young Art and Old Hector">Young Art and Old Hector</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Isle_of_the_Great_Deep" title="The Green Isle of the Great Deep">The Green Isle of the Great Deep</a></i> are both fantasies based on Scottish folklore.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Gunn\'s later works in the 1940s and into the 1950s became concerned with issues of <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarianism</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Highland_Zen_master">The Highland Zen master</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_M._Gunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Highland Zen master">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Gunn\'s final full-length work was a discursive autobiography entitled <i>The Atom of Delight</i>. This text showed the influence which a reading of <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Herrigel" title="Eugen Herrigel">Eugen Herrigel</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Zen_in_the_Art_of_Archery" title="Zen in the Art of Archery">Zen in the Art of Archery</a></i> had upon Gunn. His utilisation of these ideas was not so much mystical as providing a view of the individual in a <i>"small self-contained community, with a long-established way of life, with actions and responses known and defined"</i>. He took the playing of <a href="/wiki/Fiddle" title="Fiddle">fiddle</a> <a href="/wiki/Reel_(dance)" title="Reel (dance)">reels</a> as an example: <i>"how a human hand could perform, on its own, truly astonishing feats \xe2\x80\x93 astonishing in the sense that if thought interfered for a moment the feat was destroyed"</i>. This thought-free state could be a source of delight.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://ncolloff.blogspot.co.uk/2014/11/zen-in-art-of-neil-gunn.html">[2]</a>\n</p><p>In his later years, Gunn was involved in broadcasting and also published in diverse journals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Anarchy_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Anarchy Magazine">Anarchy Magazine</a></i> in London, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glasgow_Herald" class="mw-redirect" title="The Glasgow Herald">The Glasgow Herald</a></i>, <i>Holiday</i> (USA), <i>Saltire Review</i>, <i>Scotland\'s Magazine</i>, <i>Scots Review</i>, and <i>Point</i> magazine in <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>.\n</p><p>In his later years Gunn lived on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Isle" title="Black Isle">Black Isle</a>. He died in Raigmore Hospital in Inverness on 15 January 1973, aged 81.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_M._Gunn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Gunn is commemorated in <a href="/wiki/Makars%27_Court" title="Makars&#39; Court">Makars\' Court</a>, outside the <a href="/wiki/Writers%27_Museum" title="Writers&#39; Museum">Writers\' Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lawnmarket" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawnmarket">Lawnmarket</a>, Edinburgh. Selections for Makars\' Court are made by the Writers\' Museum; the <a href="/wiki/Saltire_Society" title="Saltire Society">Saltire Society</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Poetry_Library" title="Scottish Poetry Library">Scottish Poetry Library</a>. The Neil Gunn Trust was established in 1986, and in October 1987 a monument to the writer was unveiled on the Heights of Brae, Strathpeffer.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Neil Gunn Writing Competition was established in 1988 by Ross &amp; Cromarty District Council (later becoming the Highland Council) and the Trust.
