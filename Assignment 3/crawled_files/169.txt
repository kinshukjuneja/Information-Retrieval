href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Lulyn</i>: Lu \'fleet\', Lynn/Lydn \'pool\') is a seaside town and fishing port in south-west <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, UK.<sup id="cite_ref-OS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Newlyn lies on the shore of <a href="/wiki/Mount%27s_Bay" title="Mount&#39;s Bay">Mount\'s Bay</a> and forms a small <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbation</a> with the neighbouring town of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>. It is part of the Penzance <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a>. The principal industry is <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, although there are also a wide variety of yachts and pleasure boats, in the harbour, as Newlyn is becoming an increasingly popular holiday destination, with many pubs and restaurants. Although the parish is now listed under Penzance there is an electoral ward in separate existence called <b>Newlyn</b> and <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a>. id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newlyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The settlement is recorded as <i>Nulyn</i> in 1279 and as <i>Lulyn</i> in 1290, and the name is thought to be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> for <i>"pool for a fleet of boats"</i> which is thought to refer to the shallows offshore known as <i>Gwavas Lake</i>, traditionally the principal mooring for the fishing fleet in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newlyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before the rise of Newlyn as an important settlement the landing rights and most property within the Newlyn area were owned by the <a href="/wiki/Manor_of_Alverton" title="Manor of Alverton">Manor of Alverton</a>. Newlyn\'s history has been strongly linked to its role as a major fishing port. The natural protection afforded by the <a href="/wiki/Gwavas" title="Gwavas">Gwavas</a> Lake (an area of seawater in <a href="/wiki/Mount%27s_Bay" title="Mount&#39;s Bay">Mount\'s Bay</a>) led to many local fishermen using this area as a preferred landing site.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Cornwall">Spanish Raid of 1595</a> destroyed Penzance, <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a> and Paul as well as Newlyn.\n</p><p>In 1620 the <a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a> stopped off at Newlyn old quay to take on water.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A plaque on the quay reads:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>To the memory of Bill Best Harris 1914 \xe2\x80\x93 1987 Historian and son of Plymouth whose researches indicated that the MAYFLOWER 16 \xe2\x80\x93 8 \xe2\x80\x93 1620 docked at the Old Quay Newlyn for water and supplies making it the last port of call in England<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span> The water supply at Plymouth being the cause of fever and cholera in the city<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span> Let debate begin<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In 1755, the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_earthquake" class="mw-redirect" title="Lisbon earthquake">Lisbon earthquake</a> caused a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> to strike the Cornish coast more than 600 miles (970&#160;km) away from the epicentre. The sea rose ten feet in ten minutes at Newlyn, and ebbed at the same rate. The 19th century French writer, Arnold Boscowitz, claimed that "great loss of life and property occurred upon the coasts of Cornwall".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Before the 19th century, "Newlyn" referred only to the area near the old quay. The part of the village that now contains the fish market was known as "Streetanowan", this was separated at high tide from "Newlyn Town" the site of the lower part of the modern harbour being reclaimed land and formerly a beach. In fact Newlyn comprises three discrete hamlets all previously separated by bodies of water, being Tolcarne (Tal Carn: Brow of the Rocks), Street-an-Nowan (Steet-an-Awan: River Street) and Trewarveneth (Farm/Manor on the Hill).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Newlyn (like Mousehole) was part of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient parish">ancient parish</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paul,_Cornwall" title="Paul, Cornwall">Paul</a>. It was common for villagers to climb the relatively steep route from "Newlyn Cliff" to Paul via the area which is now known as Gwavas to worship at Paul Church. Until the mid-20th century an ancient stone cross was present on this route at "Park an Grouse" (The Field of the Cross), this cross was one site of veneration of the Cornish sea deity <a href="/wiki/Bucca_(mythological_creature)" title="Bucca (mythological creature)">Bucca</a>, (others were the beaches of Newlyn and Mousehole) the name \'Bucca\' has often been used as a nickname for people who reside in Newlyn: the location of the cross is now unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Hardie1995_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hardie1995-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Peter%27s_Church,_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/St_Peter%27s_Church%2C_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg/220px-St_Peter%27s_Church%2C_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/St_Peter%27s_Church%2C_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg/330px-St_Peter%27s_Church%2C_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/St_Peter%27s_Church%2C_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg/440px-St_Peter%27s_Church%2C_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Peter%27s_Church,_Newlyn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_501812.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St Peter\'s Church</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1851 Newlyn became the separate ecclesiastical parish of Newlyn St Peter.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The church of St Peter was built in the Early English style in 1859\xe2\x80\x9366. The interior is embellished with various works of art including the altarpiece and a statue of the Madonna and Child. "The ensemble is an outstanding example of Anglo-Catholic embellishment of the period [1936\xe2\x80\x9355]" (Peter Beacham).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> There is a Cornish cross by the road near the churchyard; it was found at Trereiffe about 1870 and much later placed near the church by the Rev. W. L. Lach-Szyrma.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1880s a number of artists moved to the town and formed an artists\' colony.<sup id="cite_ref-Cherry1993_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cherry1993-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The painters of Newlyn came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Newlyn_School" title="Newlyn School">Newlyn School</a>.\n</p><p>In 1896 Newlyn was the scene of the <a href="/wiki/Newlyn_riots" title="Newlyn riots">Newlyn riots</a> following protests over the landing of fish on a Sunday by fishermen from the <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">North of England</a>, the local Cornish fishermen being members of the <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> church and as such strong supporters of <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Sabbatarianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday Sabbatarianism">sabbatarianism</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newlyn_Tidal_observatory_Map_1946.png" class="image"><img alt="" measurements of tidal height were taken every 15 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1937, the fishing vessel <a href="/wiki/PZ_87_The_Rosebud" title="PZ 87 The Rosebud">Rosebud</a> sailed to London to deliver a petition to the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health,_UK" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Health, UK">Minister of Health</a> on behalf of those villagers whose homes were threatened under the government\'s <a href="/wiki/Slum_clearance" title="Slum clearance">slum clearance</a> scheme.\n</p><p>During the Second World War Newlyn was a base for the Air Sea Rescue craft covering the <a href="/wiki/Western_Approaches" title="Western Approaches">Western Approaches</a>. The harbour was bombed during the war, hitting the collier <i>Greenhithe</i>, which was beached in the harbour at the time and supplied coal to the east coast drifters, which travelled to Newlyn during the mackerel fishing season between the wars.<sup id="cite_ref-Pool1974_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pool1974-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Reporting the event on the <i>"<a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> had sunk a British cruiser in Newlyn Harbour.\n</p><p>In 2014 indie band the <i>Hit Parade</i> released <i>Cornish Pop Songs</i>, an album containing several songs about Newlyn including "Rainy Day in Newlyn" and "The Ghost of the Fishing Fleet".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newlyn_and_the_Cornish_language">Newlyn and the Cornish language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newlyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Newlyn and the Cornish language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Newlyn, along with nearby <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a> and Paul, was the last stronghold of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a>, presumably due to the strength of its fishing fleet. <a href="/wiki/William_Gwavas" title="William Gwavas">William Gwavas</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Jenkins_(Alverton)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Jenkins (Alverton)">James Jenkins</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Boson" title="Nicholas Boson">Nicholas Boson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Boson" title="Thomas Boson">Thomas Boson</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Boson_(writer)" title="John Boson (writer)">John Boson</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Keigwin" title="John Keigwin">John Keigwin</a>, and John Kelynack Jnr had roots in or strong links with the district. Subsequently, several antiquarians including Prince <a href="/wiki/Louis_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Louis Lucien Bonaparte">Louis Lucien Bonaparte</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Daines_Barrington" title="Daines Barrington">Daines Barrington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Sauerwein" title="Georg Sauerwein">Georg Sauerwein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jenner" title="Henry Jenner">Henry Jenner</a> who all collected Cornish writings or sayings, and the latter two became proficient in its use.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newlyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1894 Newlyn became part of Paul <a href="/wiki/Urban_District" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban District">Urban District</a>. The urban district was abolished in 1934 and Newlyn was absorbed into the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a> of Penzance.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Penzance Municipal Borough was itself abolished in 1974 under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, and Newlyn became part of the new <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a> District. The former borough was <a href="/wiki/Unparished_area" title="Unparished area">unparished</a> until 1980. The unparished area was formed into a civil parish in 1980,<sup id="cite_ref-cornwall_list_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornwall_list-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and the new Penzance parish council elected to call itself a town council. Newlyn returns five councillors to Penzance Town Council.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Penwith District was abolished in 2009, and Newlyn now falls under the unitary <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newlyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Newlyn is located in western Cornwall, just south of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>. It lies along the B3315 road which connects it to <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land&#39;s End">Land\'s End</a>. <a href="/wiki/Paul,_Cornwall" title="Paul, Cornwall">Paul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mousehole" title="Mousehole">Mousehole</a> lie to the south.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a>, the United Kingdom\'s <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">mapping</a> agency, bases all <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevations</a> including mapped <a href="/wiki/Contour_lines" class="mw-redirect" title="Contour lines">contour lines</a> and spot heights on the mean <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> at Newlyn (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Datum" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordnance Datum">Ordnance Datum</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-LeeClark2002_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeeClark2002-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The mean sea level data was calculated from hourly readings of the sea level between 1 May 1915 and 30 April 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-LeeClark2002_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeeClark2002-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newlyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Home_by_sunrise_-_geograph.org.uk_-_549297.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Home by sunrise - Today, a few vessels have resumed pilchard fishing and use a modern version of the ring net. The largest vessels are beam trawlers owned by W Stevenson and Sons Ltd, one of Cornwall\'s largest fish producers;<sup id="cite_ref-Bradstreet1994_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradstreet1994-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> most of the other vessels are owned by their skippers. The company based in the Old Pilchard Works today are major supplies of Cornish sardines and mixed species fish.<sup id="cite_ref-PW_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newlyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Newlyn_RFC" title="Newlyn RFC">Newlyn RFC</a> was formed in 1894 (or 1895) by the curate of St Peter\'s Church, the Rev Fred Peel Yates.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The club amalgamated with <a href="/wiki/Penzance_RFC" title="Penzance RFC">Penzance RFC</a> in 1944 to form Penzance and Newly RFC (The Pirates), currently known as the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Pirates" title="Cornish Pirates">Cornish Pirates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Newlyn Non Athletic <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">AFC</a> was formed in 1990 by a group of friends playing on Sunday mornings.
