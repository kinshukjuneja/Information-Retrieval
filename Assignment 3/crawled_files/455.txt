class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liberalism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_liberalism#Glorious_Revolution" title="History of liberalism">History of liberalism \xc2\xa7&#160;Glorious Revolution</a></div>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the word <i>liberalism</i> can have any of several meanings. Scholars use the term to refer to <i><a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classical liberalism</a></i>; the term also can mean <i><a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberalism</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Political_liberalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Political liberalism">political liberalism</a></i>; it can simply refer to the politics of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> party; it can occasionally have <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">the imported American meaning</a>, including the derogatory usage by (American) conservatives. However, the derogatory connotation is much weaker in the UK than in the US, and social liberals from both the left and right wing continue to use <i>liberal</i> and <i>illiberal</i> to describe themselves and their opponents, respectively.\n</p><p>Historically, the term referred to the broad liberal political alliance of the nineteenth century, formed by <a href="/wiki/British_Whig_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="British Whig Party">Whigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peelites" class="mw-redirect" title="Peelites">Peelites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radicals_(UK)" title="Radicals (UK)">radicals</a>. This alliance, which developed into the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>, dominated politics for much of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a> and during the years before the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.\n</p><p>British liberalism is now organised between two schools;\n</p>\n<ol><li>the <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberalism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> (member <a href="/wiki/Liberal_International" title="Liberal International">LI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Liberals_and_Democrats_for_Europe_Party" title="Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe Party">ELDR</a>) and their counterpart the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Alliance Party of Northern Ireland">Alliance Party of Northern Ireland</a> (member <a href="/wiki/Liberal_International" title="Liberal International">LI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Liberals_and_Democrats_for_Europe_Party" title="Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe Party">ELDR</a>),</li>\n<li>and the <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> which was adopted in the late 1970s by the late former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> whose fundamental changes to party policy aligned it to <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classical liberalism</a> with its commitment to low taxation and economic deregulation. In his speech to the <a href="/wiki/Party_conference" title="Party conference">party conference</a> in 2006, <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> described the party as a "<a href="/wiki/Liberal_conservatism" title="Liberal conservatism">liberal conservative</a>" party, and in a speech in Bath on Thursday 22 March 2007, he described himself as a "liberal Conservative".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1900s:_New_Liberalism">1900s: New Liberalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liberalism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1900s: New Liberalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">Social liberalism</a></div>\n<p>When the Liberals lost the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1895" title="United Kingdom general election, 1895">1895 general election</a>, a political crisis shook the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>. Until that, the Liberal Party adhered to the <a href="/wiki/Gladstonian_liberalism" title="Gladstonian liberalism">Gladstonian liberalism</a>, of free markets, low taxation, self-help and freedom of choice, but after the 1895 many Liberals claimed for a political reform. The reformers\' leaders were <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hill_Green" title="Thomas Hill Green">Thomas Hill Green</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Samuel,_1st_Viscount_Samuel" title="Herbert Samuel, 1st Viscount Samuel">Herbert Samuel</a>, that in the <i>Progressive Review</i> of December 1896, said that the classical liberalism was "sapped and raddled", claiming for more state\'s powers.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Samuel\'s "New Liberalism" called for old-age pensions, <a href="/wiki/Labour_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour exchange">labour exchanges</a> (job-placement organizations), and workers\' compensation, all prefiguring modern <a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">welfare</a>. Many Liberals, including future Prime Ministers <a href="/wiki/Henry_Campbell-Bannerman" title="Henry Campbell-Bannerman">Henry Campbell-Bannerman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_H._Asquith" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbert H. Asquith">Herbert H. Asquith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_George" class="mw-redirect" title="Lloyd George">Lloyd George</a>, sceptics of <a href="/wiki/Non-interventionism" title="Non-interventionism">non-interventionism</a> on economy and free market, embraced the New Liberalism. \nDuring the Liberal Governments of 1905\xe2\x80\x931916, the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> was introduced to provide provision for lower incomes. Later, in 1908 a pension system was created with old-age pensions for people older than age 70; an <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a> was introduced and in 1911 the <a href="/wiki/National_Insurance_Act_1911" title="National Insurance Act 1911">National Insurance Act</a> was approved.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, the Great War of 1914 reduced the Liberal support from population, and the Liberals themselves split in two factions in 1918: Asquith\'s supporters and George\'s <a href="/wiki/Coalition_Coupon" title="Coalition Coupon">coupons</a>. While Asquith became Leader of the Opposition, George forged a coalition with the Conservative leader <a href="/wiki/Bonar_Law" title="Bonar Law">Bonar Law</a>, continuing to be Prime Minister. However, the Liberal internal conflict caused many reformer and radical voters to join in the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, while more conservative liberals merged to the Conservatives led by <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1924" title="United Kingdom general election, 1924">1924 general election</a> signalled the end of the Liberal Party as government force. However, the New Liberalism continued to be the preferred ideology by the Liberal Party, until its dissolution in 1988 when formed the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s:_Neoliberalism">1980s: Neoliberalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liberalism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980s: Neoliberalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Thatcherism" title="Thatcherism">Thatcherism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a></div>\n<p>With the rise of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> as Conservative Party leader in the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)_leadership_election,_1975" title="Conservative Party (UK) leadership election, 1975">1975 leadership election</a> ushered in a resurgence of the old 19th-century Gladstone <i><a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">laissez-faire</a></i> economic liberal principles. The UK in the 1970s had seen sustained high <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> rates, which were above 20% at the time of the leadership election, high unemployment, and over the winter of 1978\xe2\x80\x9379 there was a series of strikes known as the "<a href="/wiki/Winter_of_Discontent" title="Winter of Discontent">Winter of Discontent</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Thatcher led her party to victory at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">1979 general election</a> with a manifesto which concentrated on the party\'s philosophy rather than presenting a "shopping list" of policies.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This philosophy became known as <a href="/wiki/Thatcherism" title="Thatcherism">Thatcherism</a> and it focused on rejecting the <a href="/wiki/Post-war_consensus" title="Post-war consensus">post-war consensus</a> that tolerated or encouraged nationalisation, strong labour unions, heavy regulation, high taxes, and a generous welfare state.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Thatcherism was based on social and economic ideas from British and American intellectuals such as <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hayek" title="Friedrich Hayek">Friedrich Hayek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>. Thatcher believed that too much socially democratic-oriented government policy was leading to a long-term decline in the British economy. As a result, her government pursued a programme of <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a>, adopting a free-market approach to public services based on the sale of publicly owned industries and utilities, as well as a reduction in trade union power. She held the belief that the existing trend of unions was bringing economic progress to a standstill by enforcing "wildcat" strikes, keeping wages artificially high and forcing unprofitable industries to stay open.\n</p><p>Thatcherism promoted low inflation, the <a href="/wiki/Small_government" title="Small government">small state</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free markets</a> through <a href="/wiki/Monetarism" title="Monetarism">tight control of the money supply</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatisation</a> and constraints on the labour movement. It is a key part of the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberal</a> movement and as such is often compared with <a href="/wiki/Reaganomics" title="Reaganomics">Reaganomics</a> in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Economic_Rationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic Rationalism">Economic Rationalism</a> in Australia and <a href="/wiki/Rogernomics" title="Rogernomics">Rogernomics</a> in New Zealand. Thatcherism is also often compared to <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classical liberalism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a> said that "Margaret Thatcher is not in terms of belief a Tory. She is a nineteenth-century Liberal."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Thatcher herself stated in 1983: "I would not mind betting that if Mr Gladstone were alive today he would apply to join the Conservative Party".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the 1996 Keith Joseph memorial lecture Thatcher argued that "The kind of Conservatism which he and I&#160;... favoured would be best described as \'liberal\', in the old-fashioned sense. And I mean the liberalism of Mr Gladstone, not of the latter day collectivists".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/File:Palmerston.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Viscount Palmerston</div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>1859</dt>\n<dd>The <a href="/wiki/Whigs_(British_political_party)" The Liberal Party win an overall majority in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1859" title="United Kingdom general election, 1859">1859 general election</a>, winning 356 seats. <a href="/wiki/Henry_John_Temple,_3rd_Viscount_Palmerston" title="Henry John Temple, 3rd Viscount Palmerston">Henry John Temple, 3rd Viscount Palmerston</a> becomes Prime Minister for the second time.</dd>\n<dt>1865</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win an overall majority in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1865" title="United Kingdom general election, 1865">1865 general election</a>, winning 369 seats. Lord Palmerston dies in office and is succeeded by <a href="/wiki/John_Russell,_1st_Earl_Russell" title="John Russell, 1st Earl Russell">John Russell, 1st Earl Russell</a>.</dd></dl>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acgladstone2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Acgladstone2.jpg/170px-Acgladstone2.jpg" width="170" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Acgladstone2.jpg/255px-Acgladstone2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Acgladstone2.jpg/340px-Acgladstone2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="590" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acgladstone2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>William Gladstone</div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>1868</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win an overall majority in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1868" title="United Kingdom general election, 1868">1868 general election</a>, winning 387 seats. <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a> becomes Prime Minister.</dd>\n<dt>1874</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party lose the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1874" title="United Kingdom general election, 1874">1874 general election</a>, winning 242 seats. A Conservative Government is formed.</dd>\n<dt>1880</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win an overall majority in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1880" title="United Kingdom general election, 1880">1880 general election</a>, winning 352 seats. Gladstone becomes Prime Minister for the second time.</dd>\n<dt>1885</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party gain the most seats (319 seats) in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1880" title="United Kingdom general election, 1880">1880 general election</a> but fail to win an overall majority with the Irish Nationalists holding the balance of power.</dd>\n<dt>1886</dt>\n<dd>Opponents of <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Home Rule">Irish Home Rule</a> in the Liberal Party, led by <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Cavendish,_8th_Duke_of_Devonshire" title="Spencer Cavendish, 8th Duke of Devonshire">Lord Hartington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">Joseph Chamberlain</a>, secede to form the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Unionist_Party" title="Liberal Unionist Party">Liberal Unionist Party</a>. The Liberal Party lose the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1886" title="United Kingdom general election, 1886">1886 general election</a> winning 191 seats. The Liberal Unionists win 77 seats and informally co-operate with the newly formed Conservative Government.</dd>\n<dt>1892</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 272 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1892" title="United Kingdom general election, 1892">1892 general election</a> and Gladstone becomes Prime Minister for the fourth time forming a minority government dependent on Irish Nationalist support. The Liberal Unionists win 45 seats.</dd>\n<dt>1894</dt>\n<dd>Gladstone resigns as Prime Minister and his successor is <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Primrose,_5th_Earl_of_Rosebery" title="Archibald Primrose, 5th Earl of Rosebery">Archibald Primrose, 5th Earl of Rosebery</a>.</dd>\n<dt>1895</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party lose the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1895" title="United Kingdom general election, 1895">1895 general election</a>, winning 177 seats. The Liberal Unionists win 71 seats and with the Conservatives form an Unionist Government.</dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1900_to_1944">1900 to 1944</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liberalism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1900 to 1944">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg/170px-Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg" width="170" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg/255px-Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg/340px-Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1208" data-file-height="1691" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir-Henry-Campbell-Bannerman.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Campbell-Bannerman</div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>1900</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 184 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1900" title="United Kingdom general election, 1900">1900 general election</a>. The Liberal Unionists win 68 seats and with the Conservatives form an Unionist Government.</dd>\n<dt>1906</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win an overall majority in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1906" title="United Kingdom general election, 1906">1906 general election</a>, winning 396 seats. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Campbell-Bannerman" title="Henry Campbell-Bannerman">Henry Campbell-Bannerman</a> becomes Prime Minister. This would prove to be the greatest victory for the Liberals and also the last time the Liberal Party won a majority in their own right. The Liberal Unionists win 25 seats.</dd></dl>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Asquith_Q_42036.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Asquith_Q_42036.jpg/170px-Asquith_Q_42036.jpg" width="170" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Asquith_Q_42036.jpg/255px-Asquith_Q_42036.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Asquith_Q_42036.jpg/340px-Asquith_Q_42036.jpg 2x" data-file-width="632" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Asquith_Q_42036.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>H. H. Asquith</div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>1908</dt>\n<dd>Henry Campbell-Bannerman resigns as Prime Minister and is succeeded by <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a>.</dd>\n<dt>1910</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 274 seats and the Liberal Unionists win 32 seats in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_January_1910" title="United Kingdom general election, January 1910">January 1910 general election</a>. Asquith forms a government with the support of the Irish Nationalists. Another <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_December_1910" title="United Kingdom general election, December 1910">election is held in December</a>, with the Liberal Party winning 272 seats and the Liberal Unionists winning 36 seats. This would prove the last time the Liberal Party won the highest number of seats in the House of Commons.</dd>\n<dt>1912</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Unionists merge with the Conservatives to form the present-day <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative and Unionist Party</a> (known as the Conservative Party).</dd>\n<dt>1915</dt>\n<dd>After several British set backs in the First World War, H. H. Asquith invites the Conservatives to form a war-time coalition government. This marked the end of the last all Liberal government.</dd></dl>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Lloyd_George.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/David_Lloyd_George.jpg/170px-David_Lloyd_George.jpg" width="170" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/David_Lloyd_George.jpg/255px-David_Lloyd_George.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/David_Lloyd_George.jpg/340px-David_Lloyd_George.jpg 2x" data-file-width="907" data-file-height="1182" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Lloyd_George.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>David Lloyd George</div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>1916</dt>\n<dd>H. H. Asquith loses support of the Conservative Party and <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> becomes Prime Minister. The Liberal Party is now split into two factions: one camp supporting Lloyd George and the other following Asquith.</dd>\n<dt>1918</dt>\n<dd>At the end of the war, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1918" title="United Kingdom general election, 1918">a general election was held</a>. The Liberal Party remained split with Lloyd George\'s <a href="/wiki/Coalition_Liberals" class="mw-redirect" title="Coalition Liberals">Coalition Liberals</a> winning 127 seats and the Asquith led <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberals</a> winning 36 seats. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1922" title="United Kingdom general election, 1922">1922 general election</a>, the Lloyd George led <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(UK,_1922)" title="National Liberal Party (UK, 1922)">National Liberals</a> win 53 seats, whilst the Asquith led Liberals win 62 seats.</dd>\n<dt>1923</dt>\n<dd>The National Liberals and the Asquith Liberal factions are re-united as one in support of free trade and the Liberal Party win 158 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1923" title="United Kingdom general election, 1923">1923 general election</a>. It was the last election in which the Liberals won more than 100 seats.</dd>\n<dt>1924</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party are nearly wiped out and win only 40 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1924" title="United Kingdom general election, 1924">1924 general election</a>.</dd>\n<dt>1929</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 59 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1929" title="United Kingdom general election, 1929">1929 general election</a>.</dd>\n<dt>1931</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party agrees to join the <a href="/wiki/National_Government_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Government (United Kingdom)">National Government</a>. After the National Government proposed to fight the 1931 election for a mandate of tariffs, the Liberal Party was split into three groups. A faction, led by <a href="/wiki/John_Simon,_1st_Viscount_Simon" title="John Simon, 1st Viscount Simon">John Simon</a>, supported the protectionist government policy and formed the <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(UK,_1931)" title="National Liberal Party (UK, 1931)">Liberal National Party</a>. Another faction, led by Lloyd George, became the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Liberals_(UK,_1931)" title="Independent Liberals (UK, 1931)">Independent Liberals</a>. The third grouping, the \'official\' Liberal Party, was led by <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Samuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbert Samuel">Herbert Samuel</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1931" title="United Kingdom general election, 1931">1931 general election</a>, the Liberal Nationals won 35 seats, the \'official\' Liberals won 33 seats, the Independent Liberals won 4 seats.</dd>\n<dt>1933</dt>\n<dd>The \'official\' Liberal Party leave the National Government.</dd>\n<dt>1935</dt>\n<dd>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1935" title="United Kingdom general election, 1935">1935 general election</a>, the Liberal Nationals won 33 seats, the \'official\' Liberals won 21 seats, the Independent Liberals won 4 seats. Lloyd George\'s Independent Liberals rejoined with the rest of the \'official\' Liberal Party after the general election. The Liberal Nationals remain in the National Government.</dd>\n<dt>1940</dt>\n<dd>Both the Liberal Party and the Liberal National Party join the Churchill Wartime Government.</dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1945_to_present_day">1945 to present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liberalism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1945 to present day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dt>1945</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 12 seats, and the Liberal Nationals win 11 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1945" title="United Kingdom general election, 1945">1945 general election</a>.</dd>\n<dt>1947</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal National Party is renamed <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(UK,_1931)" title="National Liberal Party (UK, 1931)">National Liberal Party</a> and formally merges with the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> at constituency level; however some MPs and candidates continue to use the National Liberal label (and variants thereof) for the next twenty years.</dd>\n<dt>1950</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 9 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1950" title="United Kingdom general election, 1950">1950 general election</a>. Candidates under the National Liberals banner win 17 seats.</dd>\n<dt>1951</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 6 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1951" title="United Kingdom general election, 1951">1951 general election</a>. National Liberals win 19 seats and with the Conservatives form a Conservative Government.</dd>\n<dt>1955</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 6 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1955" title="United Kingdom general election, 1955">1955 general election</a>. National Liberals win 21 seats and with the Conservatives form a Conservative Government.</dd>\n<dt>1959</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 6 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1951" title="United Kingdom general election, 1951">1951 general election</a>. National Liberals win 19 seats and with the Conservatives form a Conservative Government.</dd>\n<dt>1964</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 9 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1964" title="United Kingdom general election, 1964">1964 general election</a>. National Liberals win 6 seats.</dd>\n<dt>1966</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 12 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1966" title="United Kingdom general election, 1966">1966 general election</a>. National Liberals win 3 seats.</dd>\n<dt>1968</dt>\n<dd>The National Liberals merge completely with the Conservative Party.</dd>\n<dt>1970</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 6 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1970" title="United Kingdom general election, 1970">1970 general election</a>.</dd>\n<dt>1974</dt>\n<dd>The Liberal Party win 14 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_February_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, February 1974">February 1974 general election</a> and hold the balance of power. class="mw-redirect" title="Social Democratic Party (UK, 1990-present)">SDP</a> are formed.</dd>\n<dt>1992</dt>\n<dd>The Conservatives win another victory with 336 seats and new Liberal Democrats win
