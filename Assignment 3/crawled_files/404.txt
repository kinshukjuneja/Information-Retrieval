href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Joseph Mallord William Turner</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Royal_Academician" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academician">RA</a></span> (23 April 1775&#160;&#8211;&#32;19 December 1851), known as <b>J. M. W. Turner</b> and contemporarily as <b>William Turner</b>,<sup class="reference" id="ref_Anone"><a href="#endnote_Anone">[a]</a></sup> was an English <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> painter, <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watercolor_painting" title="Watercolor painting">watercolourist</a>, known for his expressive colourisation, imaginative <a href="/wiki/Landscape_painting" title="Landscape painting">landscapes</a> and turbulent, often violent <a href="/wiki/Marine_art" title="Marine art">marine</a> paintings. Turner was born in <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Lane,_Covent_Garden" title="Maiden Lane, Covent Garden">Maiden Lane, Covent Garden</a>, London, to a modest lower middle-class family. He lived in London all his life, retaining his <a href="/wiki/Cockney_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Cockney accent">Cockney accent</a> and assiduously avoiding the trappings of success and fame. A child prodigy, Turner studied at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a> from 1789, enrolling when he was 14, and exhibited his first work there at 21. During this period, he also served as an architectural draftsman. He earned a steady income from commissions and sales, which due to his troubled, contrary nature, were often begrudgingly accepted. He opened his own gallery in 1804 and became professor of <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">perspective</a> at the Academy in 1807, where he lectured until 1828, although he was viewed as profoundly inarticulate. He traveled to Europe from 1802, typically returning with voluminous sketchbooks.\n</p><p>Intensely private, <a href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(behavior)" title="Eccentricity (behavior)">eccentric</a> and reclusive, Turner was a controversial figure throughout his career. He did not marry, but fathered two daughters, Eveline (1801\xe2\x80\x931874) and Georgiana (1811\xe2\x80\x931843), by his housekeeper Sarah Danby. He became more pessimistic and morose as he got older, especially after the death of his father, after which his outlook deteriorated, and his gallery fell into disrepair and neglect, and his art intensified. He lived in near poverty circumstances and in poor health from 1845, and died in London in 1851 aged seventy-six. Turner is buried in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul%27s_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Paul&#39;s Cathedral">Saint Paul\'s Cathedral</a>, London.\n</p><p>He left behind over 2,000 paintings and 19,000 drawings and sketches. He had been championed by the leading English art critic <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> from 1840, and is today regarded as having elevated landscape painting to an eminence rivaling <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history painting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div data-file-width="714" data-file-height="1387" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DV307_no.70_House_where_Turner_was_born,_from_a_print.png" class="internal" William Turner was born on 23 April 1775 and baptised on 14 May.<sup class="reference" id="ref_Cnone"><a href="#endnote_Cnone">[c]</a></sup> He was born in <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Lane,_Covent_Garden" title="Maiden Lane, Covent Garden">Maiden Lane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a>, in London, England.<sup id="cite_ref-shanes2008_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shanes2008-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His father, William Turner (1745\xe2\x80\x9321 September 1829), was a barber and wig maker.<sup id="cite_ref-dnb_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His mother, Mary Marshall, came from a family of butchers.<sup id="cite_ref-hamilton2007_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton2007-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A younger sister, Mary Ann, was born in September 1778 but died in August 1783.<sup id="cite_ref-bailey_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bailey-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turner\'s mother showed signs of mental disturbance from 1785 and was admitted to <a href="/wiki/St_Luke%27s_Hospital_for_Lunatics" title="St Luke&#39;s Hospital for Lunatics">St Luke\'s Hospital for Lunatics</a> in Old Street in 1799 and was moved in 1800 to <a href="/wiki/Bethlem_Hospital" class="mw-redirect" title="Bethlem Hospital">Bethlem Hospital</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> where she died in 1804. Turner was sent to his maternal uncle, Joseph Mallord William Marshall, in <a href="/wiki/Brentford" title="Brentford">Brentford</a>, then a small town on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> west of London. The earliest known artistic exercise by Turner is from this period\xe2\x80\x94a series of simple colourings of engraved plates from Henry Boswell\'s <i>Picturesque View of the Antiquities of England and Wales</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilton14_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilton14-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\nAround 1786, Turner was sent to <a href="/wiki/Margate" title="Margate">Margate</a> on the north-east <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> coast. There he produced a series of drawings of the town and surrounding area that foreshadowed his later work.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> By this time, Turner\'s drawings were being exhibited in his father\'s shop window and sold for a few shillings.<sup id="cite_ref-hamilton2007_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton2007-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His father boasted to the artist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stothard" title="Thomas Stothard">Thomas Stothard</a> that: "My son, sir, is going to be a painter".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1789, Turner again stayed with his uncle who had retired to <a href="/wiki/Sunningwell" title="Sunningwell">Sunningwell</a> in Berkshire (now part of Oxfordshire). A whole <a href="/wiki/Sketchbook" title="Sketchbook">sketchbook</a> of work from this time in Berkshire survives as well as a watercolour of Oxford. The use of pencil sketches on location, as the foundation for later finished paintings, formed the basis of Turner\'s essential working style for his whole career.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilton14_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilton14-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many early sketches by Turner were architectural studies or exercises in perspective, and it is known that, as a young man, he worked for several architects including <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardwick" title="Thomas Hardwick">Thomas Hardwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Wyatt" title="James Wyatt">James Wyatt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bonomi_the_Elder" title="Joseph Bonomi the Elder">Joseph Bonomi the Elder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> By the end of 1789, he had also begun to study under the topographical draughtsman <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malton" title="Thomas Malton">Thomas Malton</a>, specialised in London views. Turner learned from him the basic tricks of the trade, copying and colouring outline prints of British castles and abbeys. He would later call Malton  "My real master".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Topography was a thriving industry by which a young artist could pay for his studies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Academy">Royal Academy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a> of Art in 1789, aged 14,<sup id="cite_ref-finberg_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finberg-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and was accepted into the academy a year later by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Joshua Reynolds">Sir Joshua Reynolds</a>. Turner showed an early interest in architecture, but was advised by Thomas Hardwick to focus on painting. His first watercolour, <i>A View of the Archbishop\'s Palace, Lambeth</i> was accepted for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_summer_exhibition" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy summer exhibition">Royal Academy summer exhibition</a> of 1790 when Turner was 15.\n</p><p>As an academy probationer, Turner was taught drawing from plaster casts of antique sculptures. From July 1790 to October 1793, his name appears in the registry of the academy over a hundred times.<sup id="cite_ref-hamilton1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In June 1792, he was admitted to the life class to learn to draw the human body from nude models.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Turner exhibited watercolours each year at the academy while painting in the winter and travelling in the summer widely throughout Britain, particularly to <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, where he produced a wide range of sketches for working up into studies and watercolours. These particularly focused on architectural work, which used his skills as a draughtsman.<sup id="cite_ref-hamilton1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 1793, he showed the watercolour titled <i>The Rising Squall&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Hot Wells from St Vincent\'s Rock Bristol</i> (now lost), which foreshadowed his later climatic effects.<sup id="cite_ref-wilton20_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilton20-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Cunningham in his obituary of Turner wrote that it was: "recognised by the wiser few as a noble attempt at lifting landscape art out of the tame insipidities...[and] evinced for the first time that mastery of effect for which he is now justly celebrated".<sup id="cite_ref-16" the Royal Academy.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1796, Turner exhibited <i><a href="/wiki/Fishermen_at_Sea" title="Fishermen at Sea">Fishermen at Sea</a></i>, his first oil painting for the academy, of a nocturnal moonlit scene of <a href="/wiki/The_Needles" title="The Needles">the Needles</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>, an image of boats in peril.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Wilton said that the image: "Is a summary of all that had been said about the sea by the artists of the 18th century".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and shows strong influence by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Claude_Joseph_Vernet" title="Claude Joseph Vernet">Claude Joseph Vernet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg" title="Philip James de Loutherbourg">Philip James de Loutherbourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Monamy" title="Peter Monamy">Peter Monamy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Swaine" title="Francis Swaine">Francis Swaine</a>, who was admired for his moonlight marine paintings. This particular painting cannot be said to show any influence of <a href="/wiki/Willem_van_de_Velde_the_Younger" title="Willem van de Velde the Younger">Willem van de Velde the Younger</a>, as not a single nocturnal scene is known by that painter. Some later work, however, was created to rival or complement the manner of the Dutch artist. The image was praised by contemporary critics and founded Turner\'s reputation, as both an oil painter and a painter of maritime scenes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Turner travelled widely in Europe, starting with France and Switzerland in 1802 and studying in <a href="/wiki/The_Louvre" class="mw-redirect" title="The Louvre">the Louvre</a> in Paris in the same year. He made many visits to Venice. Important support for his work came from <a href="/wiki/Walter_Fawkes" title="Walter Fawkes">Walter Ramsden Fawkes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Farnley_Hall_(North_Yorkshire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Farnley Hall (North Yorkshire)">Farnley Hall</a>, near Otley in Yorkshire, who became a close friend of the artist. Turner first visited Otley in 1797, aged&#160;22, when commissioned to paint watercolours of the area. He was so attracted to Otley and the surrounding area that he returned to it throughout his career. The stormy backdrop of <i><a href="/wiki/Snow_Storm:_Hannibal_and_his_Army_Crossing_the_Alps" title="Snow Storm: Hannibal and his Army Crossing the Alps">Hannibal Crossing The Alps</a></i> is reputed to have been inspired by a storm over <a href="/wiki/The_Chevin" title="The Chevin">the Chevin</a> in Otley while he was staying at Farnley Hall.\n</p><p>Turner was a frequent guest of <a href="/wiki/George_O%27Brien_Wyndham,_3rd_Earl_of_Egremont" class="mw-redirect" title="George O&#39;Brien Wyndham, 3rd Earl of Egremont">George O\'Brien Wyndham, 3rd&#160;Earl of Egremont</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Petworth_House" title="Petworth House">Petworth House</a> in West Sussex and painted scenes that Egremont funded taken from the grounds of the house and of the Sussex countryside, including a view of the Chichester Canal. Petworth House still displays a number of paintings.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As Turner grew older, he became more eccentric. He had few close friends except for his father, who lived with him for 30 years and worked as his studio assistant. His father\'s death in 1829 had a profound effect on him, and thereafter he was subject to bouts of depression. He never married but had a relationship with an older widow, Sarah Danby. He is believed to have been the father of her two daughters born in 1801 and 1811.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turner formed a relationship with Sophia Caroline Booth after her second husband died, and he lived for about 18 years as "Mr Booth" in her house in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turner was a habitual user of <a href="/wiki/Snuff_(tobacco)" title="Snuff (tobacco)">snuff</a>; in 1838, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Philippe_I" title="Louis Philippe I">Louis Philippe I</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">King of the French</a> presented a gold <a href="/wiki/Snuff_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Snuff box">snuff box</a> to him.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Of two other snuffboxes, an <a href="/wiki/Agate" title="Agate">agate</a> and silver example bears Turner\'s name,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and another, made of wood, was collected along with his spectacles, magnifying glass and card case by an associate housekeeper.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Turner died of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> at the home of Sophia Caroline Booth, in <a href="/wiki/Cheyne_Walk" title="Cheyne Walk">Cheyne Walk</a> in Chelsea, on 19 December 1851. He is buried in <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul&#39;s Cathedral">St Paul\'s Cathedral</a>, where he lies near to Sir <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Apparently his last words were "The Sun is God",<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> though this may be apocryphal.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turner\'s friend, the architect <a href="/wiki/Philip_Hardwick" title="Philip Hardwick">Philip Hardwick</a> (1792\xe2\x80\x931870), son of his tutor, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardwick" title="Thomas Hardwick">Thomas Hardwick</a>, was in charge of making the funeral arrangements and wrote to those who knew Turner to tell them at the time of his death that, "I must inform you, we have lost him."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Other executors were his cousin and chief mourner at the funeral, Henry Harpur&#160;IV (benefactor of <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Hospital" title="Westminster Hospital">Westminster&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 now Chelsea &amp; Westminster&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Hospital</a>), Revd. Henry Scott Trimmer, <a href="/wiki/George_Jones_(painter)" title="George Jones (painter)">George Jones&#160;RA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Turner_(engraver)" title="Charles Turner (engraver)">Charles Turner&#160;ARA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Thornbury1862_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thornbury1862-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" Financial independence allowed Turner to innovate freely; his mature work is characterised by a chromatic palette and broadly applied atmospheric washes of paint. According to David Piper\'s <i>The Illustrated History of Art</i>, his later pictures were called "fantastic puzzles". Turner was recognised as an artistic genius: the influential English art critic <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> described him as the artist who could most "stirringly and truthfully measure the moods of Nature".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Turner\'s work drew criticism from contemporaries, in particular from <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Beaumont,_7th_Baronet" title="Sir George Beaumont, 7th Baronet">Sir George Beaumont</a>, a landscape painter and fellow member of the Royal Academy, who described his paintings as \'blots\'.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turner\'s imagination was sparked by shipwrecks, fires (including the <a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Parliament" title="Burning of Parliament">burning of Parliament</a> in 1834, an event which Turner witnessed first-hand, and transcribed in a series of watercolour sketches), and natural phenomena such as sunlight, storm, rain, and fog. venture into printmaking was the <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Studiorum" title="Liber Studiorum">Liber Studiorum</a></i> (Book of Studies), seventy prints that he worked on from 1806 to 1819. The <i>Liber Studiorum</i> was an expression of his intentions for landscape art. The idea was loosely based on <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lorrain" title="Claude Lorrain">Claude Lorrain</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Veritatis" title="Liber Veritatis">Liber Veritatis</a></i> (Book of Truth), where Lorrain had recorded his completed paintings; a series of print copies of these drawings, by then at <a href="/wiki/Devonshire_House" title="Devonshire House">Devonshire House</a>, had been a huge publishing success.  Turner\'s plates were meant to be widely disseminated, and categorised the genre into six types: Marine, Mountainous, Pastoral, Historical, Architectural, and Elevated or Epic Pastoral.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> His printmaking was a major part of his output, and a museum is devoted to it, the Turner Museum in <a href="/wiki/Sarasota,_Florida" title="Sarasota, Florida">Sarasota, Florida</a>, founded in 1974 by Douglass Montrose-Graem to house his collection of Turner prints.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turner\'s early works, such as <i><a href="/wiki/File:Turner_Tintern1.jpg" title="File:Turner Tintern1.jpg">Tintern Abbey</a></i> (1795), stayed true to the traditions of English landscape. However, in <i><a href="/wiki/Snow_Storm:_Hannibal_and_His_Army_Crossing_the_Alps" class="mw-redirect" title="Snow Storm: Hannibal and His Army Crossing the Alps">Hannibal Crossing the Alps</a></i> (1812), an emphasis on the destructive power of nature had already come into play. His distinctive style of painting, in which he used watercolour technique with oil paints, created lightness, fluency, and ephemeral atmospheric effects.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Turner\'s later years he used oils ever more transparently and turned to an evocation of almost pure light by use of shimmering colour. A prime example of his mature style can be seen in <i><a href="/wiki/Rain,_Steam_and_Speed_%E2%80%93_The_Great_Western_Railway" title="Rain, Steam and Speed \xe2\x80\x93 The Great Western Railway">Rain, Steam and Speed \xe2\x80\x93 The Great Western Railway</a></i>, where the objects are barely recognisable. The intensity of hue and interest in evanescent light not only placed Turner\'s work in the vanguard of English painting but exerted an influence on art in France; the <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionists</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a>, carefully studied his techniques.\n</p><p>High levels of volcanic ash (from the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mt._Tambora" class="mw-redirect" title="Mt. Tambora">Mt. Tambora</a>) in the atmosphere during 1816, the "<a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">Year Without a Summer</a>", led to unusually spectacular sunsets during this period, and were an inspiration for some of Turner\'s work.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> said that an early patron, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Monro" title="Thomas Monro">Thomas Monro</a>, Principal Physician of <a href="/wiki/Bethlem_Royal_Hospital" title="Bethlem Royal Hospital">Bedlam</a>, and a collector and amateur artist, was a significant influence on Turner\'s style:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>His true master was Dr Monro; to the practical teaching of that first patron and the wise simplicity of method of watercolour study, in which he was disciplined by him and companioned by his friend <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Girtin" title="Thomas Girtin">Girtin</a>, the healthy and constant development of the greater power is primarily to be attributed; the greatness of the power itself, it is impossible to over-estimate.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Together with a number of young artists, Turner was able, in the London house of Dr. Monro, to copy works of the major topographical draughtsmen of his time and perfect his skills in drawing. But the curious atmospherical effects and illusions of the watercolours of <a href="/wiki/John_Robert_Cozens" title="John Robert Cozens">John Robert Cozens</a>, some of which were present in Monro\'s house, went far further than the neat renderings of topography. The solemn grandeur of his Alpine views were an early revelation to the young artist and showed him the true potential of the watercolour medium, conveying mood instead of information.\n</p>\n<ul class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Turner experimented with a wide variety of pigment.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> He used pigments like <a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">carmine</a>, despite knowing that they were not long-lasting, and against the advice of contemporary experts to use more durable pigments. As a result, many of his colours have now faded. John Ruskin complained at how quickly his work decayed; Turner was indifferent to posterity and chose materials that looked good when freshly applied.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> By 1930, there was concern that both his oils and his watercolours were fading.<sup id="cite_ref-perth_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perth-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Turner left a small fortune which he hoped would be used to support what he called "decayed artists". He planned an almshouse at <a href="/wiki/Twickenham" title="Twickenham">Twickenham</a> with a gallery for some of his works. His will was contested and in 1856, after a court battle, his first cousins, including <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Price_Turner" title="Thomas Price Turner">Thomas Price Turner</a>, received part of his fortune.<sup id="cite_ref-Monkhouse_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monkhouse-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Another portion went to the Royal Academy of Arts, which occasionally awards students the Turner Medal. His finished paintings were bequeathed to the British nation, and he intended that a special gallery would be built to house them. This did not happen due to disagreement over the final site. Twenty-two years after his death, the British Parliament passed an act allowing his paintings to be lent to museums outside London, and so began the process of scattering the pictures which Turner had wanted to be kept together.\n</p><p>In 1910, the main part of the Turner Bequest, which includes unfinished paintings and drawings, was rehoused in the Duveen Turner Wing at the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">National Gallery of British Art</a> (now Tate Britain). In 1987, a new wing at the Tate, the <a href="/wiki/Clore_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Clore Gallery">Clore Gallery</a>, was opened to house the Turner bequest, though some of the most important paintings remain in the National Gallery in contravention of Turner\'s condition that they be kept and shown together. Increasingly paintings are lent abroad, ignoring Turner\'s provision that they remain constantly and permanently in Turner\'s Gallery.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Church,_Battersea" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Mary&#39;s Church, Battersea">St.&#160;Mary\'s Church, Battersea</a> added a commemorative stained glass window for Turner, between 1976 and 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> St Paul\'s Cathedral, Royal Academy of Arts and Victoria &amp; Albert Museum all hold statues representing him. A portrait by Cornelius Varley with his patent graphic telescope (Sheffield Museums &amp; Galleries) was compared with his death mask (National Portrait Gallery, London) by Kelly Freeman at Dundee University 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 to ascertain whether it really depicts Turner. The city of Westminster unveiled a memorial plaque at the site of his birthplace at 21 Maiden Lane, <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a> 2 June 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Selby Whittingham founded The Turner Society at London and Manchester in 1975. After the society endorsed the Tate Gallery\'s Clore Gallery wing (on the lines of the Duveen wing of 1910), as the solution to the controversy of what should be done with the Turner Bequest, Selby Whittingham resigned and founded the Independent Turner Society. The Tate created the prestigious annual <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> art award in 1984, named in Turner\'s honour, and 20 years later the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_Painters_in_Water_Colours" title="Royal Institute of Painters in Water Colours">Royal Institute of Painters in Water Colours</a> founded the Winsor &amp; Newton Turner Watercolour Award. A major exhibition, "Turner\'s Britain", with material (including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fighting_Temeraire" title="The Fighting Temeraire">The Fighting Temeraire</a></i>) on loan from around the globe, was held at <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Museum_and_Art_Gallery" title="Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery">Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery</a> from 7 November 2003 to 8 February 2004. In 2005, Turner\'s <i>The Fighting Temeraire</i> was voted Britain\'s "greatest painting" in a public poll organised by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-greatest_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greatest-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Portrayal_in_theatre.2C_television_and_film"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Portrayal_in_theatre,_television_and_film">Portrayal in theatre, television and film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portrayal in theatre, television and film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Leo_McKern" title="Leo McKern">Leo McKern</a> played Turner in <i>The Sun is God</i>, a 1974 <a href="/wiki/Thames_Television" title="Thames Television">Thames Television</a> production directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Darlow" title="Michael Darlow">Michael Darlow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The programme was shown on 17 December 1974, during the Turner Bicentenary Exhibition in London.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 2011 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Painter_(play)" title="The Painter (play)">The Painter</a></i>, a biographical play on his life by <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Lenkiewicz" title="Rebecca Lenkiewicz">Rebecca Lenkiewicz</a>, premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Arcola_Theatre" title="Arcola Theatre">Arcola Theatre</a> in London.\n</p><p>British filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Mike_Leigh" title="Mike Leigh">Mike Leigh</a> wrote and directed <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Turner" title="Mr. Turner">Mr. Turner</a></i>, a biopic of Turner\'s later years, released in 2014. The film starred <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Spall" title="Timothy Spall">Timothy Spall</a> as Turner, Dorothy Atkinson, Marion Bailey and Paul Jesson, and premiered in competition for the <i><a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d&#39;Or">Palme d\'Or</a></i> at the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>, with Spall taking the award for Best Actor.<sup id="cite_ref-child_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-child-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ch4_46-0" class="reference"><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>a.</b> <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_Anone"><a href="#ref_Anone"><b><sup>^</sup></b></a></span>  Although Turner was known by his middle name, William, he is now generally referred to by his initials, in order to avoid confusion with another artist named <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(artist)" title="William Turner (artist)">William Turner</a>.\n</p><p><b>b.</b> <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_Bnone"><a href="#ref_Bnone"><b><sup>^</sup></b></a></span>  Turner claimed to have been born on 23 April 1775, which is both <a href="/wiki/Saint_George%27s_Day" title="Saint George&#39;s Day">Saint George\'s Day</a> and the supposed birthday of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, but this claim has never been verified.<sup id="cite_ref-shanes2008_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shanes2008-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The first verifiable date is that Turner was baptised on 14 May, and some authors doubt the 23 April date, on the grounds that high infant mortality rates would have meant that parents would usually baptise their children shortly after birth.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>c.</b><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_Cnone"><a href="#ref_Cnone"><b><sup>^</sup></b></a></span>   Mary Marshall died in 1804, after having been committed in 1799 to St Luke\'s Hospital and then to the <a href="/wiki/Bethlem_Royal_Hospital" title="Bethlem Royal Hospital">Bethlem Royal Hospital</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mental_asylum" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental asylum">mental asylum</a>. Her illness possibly due in part to the early death of Turner\'s younger sister. Hamilton suggests that this "fit of illness" may have been an early sign of her madness.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._W._Turner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation journal">Lacayo, Richard (11 October 2007). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.time.com/time/magazine/article/0,9171,1670528,00.html">"The Sunshine Boy"</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i>. <q>At the turn of the 18th century, history painting was the highest purpose art could serve, and Turner would attempt those heights all his life.
