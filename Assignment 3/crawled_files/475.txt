href="/wiki/Courts_of_England_and_Wales#Senior_Courts_of_England_and_Wales" title="Courts of England and Wales">Senior Courts of England and Wales</a>, and second in the legal system of <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> only to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The COA was created in 1875,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and today comprises 39 Lord Justices of Appeal and Lady Justices of Appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The court has two divisions, Criminal and Civil, led by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Rolls_and_Records_of_the_Chancery_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Master of the Rolls and Records of the Chancery of England">Master of the Rolls</a> respectively. Criminal appeals are heard in the Criminal Division, and civil appeals in the Civil Division. The Criminal Division also hears <a href="/wiki/Appeals_from_the_Crown_Court" title="Appeals from the Crown Court">appeals from the Crown Court</a>, while the Civil Division hears appeals from the <a href="/wiki/County_court" title="County court">County Court</a>, <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Family_Court_(England_and_Wales)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family Court (England and Wales)">Family Court</a>. Permission to appeal is normally required from either the lower court or the Court of Appeal itself; and with permission, <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom#jurisdiction" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">further appeal</a> may lie to the Supreme Court.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_early_history">Formation and early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The appeal system before 1875 was "chaotic". The superior courts system consisted of 12 different courts, with appeal on common law matters to the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Exchequer_Chamber" title="Court of Exchequer Chamber">Court of Exchequer Chamber</a>, chancery matters to the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_in_Chancery" title="Court of Appeal in Chancery">Court of Appeal in Chancery</a> and other matters to the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a>. This was the subject of a review by the Judicature Commission, established in 1867 to consider the creation of a "Supreme Court" (a High Court and Court of Appeal). The result was published in 1869. The recommendation was that there should be a common system of appeal from all of the High Court divisions, with a limited set of appeals allowed to the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This reform was implemented by the <a href="/wiki/Judicature_Acts" title="Judicature Acts">Judicature Acts</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Appellate_Jurisdiction_Act_1876" title="Appellate Jurisdiction Act 1876">Appellate Jurisdiction Act 1876</a> giving an almost limitless right of appeal to the Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The new legal structure provided a single Court of Appeal, which heard appeals from all the various divisions of the new unified <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a>. It only heard civil cases: opportunities for appealing in criminal cases remained limited until the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-judiciary_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-judiciary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In its early days, the Court of Appeal divided its sittings between <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Westminster Hall">Westminster Hall</a> for appeals from the Common Law divisions, and <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Inn" title="Lincoln&#39;s Inn">Lincoln\'s Inn</a> for Chancery, Probate, Divorce and Admiralty appeals, with five Lords Justices. After the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Courts_of_Justice" title="Royal Courts of Justice">Royal Courts of Justice</a> in 1882 the Court of Appeal transferred there, where it remains. As well as the Lords Justices, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a>, any previous Lords Chancellor, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" title="Lords of Appeal in Ordinary">Lords of Appeal in Ordinary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vice-Chancellor_of_the_Chancery_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice-Chancellor of the Chancery Division">Vice-Chancellor of the Chancery Division</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Rolls" title="Master of the Rolls">Master of the Rolls</a> could also hear cases, although in practice only the Master of the Rolls did so.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_appellate_jurisdiction_and_procedure">Changes in appellate jurisdiction and procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Changes in appellate jurisdiction and procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The absence of limits on appeals to the House of Lords was the cause of much concern: it led to an additional set of expensive and time-consuming appeals from the Court of Appeal, which thus could not take decisions in the knowledge that they were final. The appeals from the <a href="/wiki/County_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="County Court">County Courts</a> were seen similarly, involving an appeal to the High Court of Justice and the bypassing of the Court of Appeal for a second set of appeals to the Lords. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Administration_of_Justice_(Appeals)_Act_1934&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Administration of Justice (Appeals) Act 1934 (page does not exist)">Administration of Justice (Appeals) Act 1934</a>, a short statute, solved both problems neatly by abolishing the appeal of County decisions to the High Court and instead sending them automatically to the Court of Appeal, and by establishing that appeals to the Lords could only take place with the consent of the Court of Appeal or of the Lords themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A second set of reforms to the appeals system followed the report of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Evershed_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Evershed Committee (page does not exist)">Evershed Committee on High Court Procedure</a> in 1953, which recognised the high cost to the litigants of an additional set of appeals, particularly since the loser in a civil case paid the victor\'s legal bills. Among the few changes that were made, the practice ceased of counsel reading out the judgment, cross-examinations, documents and evidence given in the lower court; this saved time and costs. The process of "leapfrogging" (appealing from the High Court to the House of Lords without needing to go through the Court of Appeal), which the Committee had recommended, was eventually brought into force with the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Administration_of_Justice_Act_1969&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Administration of Justice Act 1969 (page does not exist)">Administration of Justice Act 1969</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A separate <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Criminal_Appeal" title="Court of Criminal Appeal">Court of Criminal Appeal</a> had been established in 1908. In 1966 this was merged with its older namesake, establishing the present-day structure of a single Court of Appeal with two Divisions: Civil and Criminal.<sup id="cite_ref-judiciary_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-judiciary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the early 1960s there was discussion between judges and academics in the United Kingdom and the United States comparing the processes of appeal used in each nation. Although the British judges found the emphasis on written arguments unattractive, they did like the idea of pre-reading: that the court should read the pleadings of counsel, the case being appealed and the judgment from the lower court before delivering its judgment. But the idea was quietly scrapped, despite a successful tryout in the Court of Appeal. The court over which <a href="/wiki/Lord_Denning" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Denning">Lord Denning</a> presided from 1962 to 1982 was under no pressure and had no inclination to modernise, with liaisons and management<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> handled by clerks with little knowledge.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This changed in 1981 with the appointment of a Registrar, John Adams, an academic and lawyer, who significantly reformed the internal workings of the Court.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Woolf_and_Bowman_reforms">The Woolf and Bowman reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Woolf and Bowman reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In July 1996, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Woolf,_Baron_Woolf" title="Harry Woolf, Baron Woolf">Lord Woolf</a> published <i>Access to Justice</i>, a report on the accessibility of the courts to the public. Woolf identified civil litigation as being characterised by excessive cost, delay and complexity, and succeeded in replacing the diverse rules with a single set of <a href="/wiki/Civil_Procedure_Rules_1998" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Procedure Rules 1998">Civil Procedure Rules</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Before Woolf had even published his final report, Sir Jeffery Bowman, the recently retired senior partner of <a href="/wiki/PriceWaterhouseCoopers" class="mw-redirect" title="PriceWaterhouseCoopers">PriceWaterhouse</a>, was commissioned to write a report on the Civil Division of the Court of Appeal. Bowman noted a growing workload and delays, with 14 months between setting down and disposing of a case in 70% of cases, the rest taking even longer than that \xe2\x80\x93 some had taken five years.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He recommended extending the requirement to ask leave to appeal to almost all appeal cases; allowing certain appeals to be heard at a lower level; focusing of procedure; imposition of time limits on oral arguments; and the use of judicial time more towards reading and less towards sitting in court.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bowman\'s recommendations were mainly enacted through statutory provisions, such as Part IV of the <a href="/wiki/Access_to_Justice_Act_1999" title="Access to Justice Act 1999">Access to Justice Act 1999</a>. In <i>Tanfern Ltd v Cameron-MacDonald</i> [2000] 1 WLR 1311, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Brooke_(judge)" title="Henry Brooke (judge)">Brooke LJ</a> laid down the procedural methods of the Court of Appeal post-Woolf and Bowman. With a few exceptions, such as cases where "the liberty of the subject" is an issue, permission is required to appeal, and may be granted either by the lower court or by the Court of Appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> As a general rule, appeals are now limited to a review of the decision of the lower court, only allowing a full appeal where there was a serious procedural irregularity or the decision was wrong through "blatant error".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_Division">Civil Division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Civil Division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Civil Division deals with all non-criminal cases, and has been part of the Court since its establishment in 1875. The Civil Division is bound by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> when making decisions, and is normally bound by its own previous decisions, with four exceptions; where the previous decision was made without the judges knowing of a particular law; where there are two previous conflicting decisions; where there is a later conflicting Supreme Court or <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">House of Lords</a> decision, and; where a law was assumed to exist in a previous case but did not. The first three were established by the case of <i>Young v Bristol Aeroplane Co Ltd</i> in 1946, the fourth by <i>R (on the application of Kadhim) v Brent London Borough Housing Benefit Review Board</i> in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The Civil Division is led by the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Rolls" title="Master of the Rolls">Master of the Rolls</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Terence_Etherton" title="Terence Etherton">Sir Terence Etherton</a> (who is entitled to the post-nominal MR), assisted by the <a href="/wiki/Vice-President_of_the_Civil_Division" title="Vice-President of the Civil Division">Vice-President of the Civil Division</a>, Sir <a href="/wiki/Martin_Moore-Bick" title="Martin Moore-Bick">Martin Moore-Bick</a>. The division hears cases from the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="County Court">County Courts</a> and several tribunals.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the Lord Chief Justice is senior to the Master of the Rolls, the Civil Division is much broader in scope than the Criminal Division. With only three judges on the bench (rather than five or more in the Supreme Court), this allows the Master of the Rolls huge opportunity for shaping the common law and, most notably, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Denning" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Denning">Lord Denning</a> made the most of this potential.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_Division">Criminal Division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criminal Division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Criminal Division was established in 1966 with the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Criminal_Appeal" title="Court of Criminal Appeal">Court of Criminal Appeal</a> into the Court of Appeal. It hears all appeals from the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a> which are in connection with a trial on indictment (i.e. with a jury) and where the Crown Court has sentenced a defendant committed from the Magistrates\' Court. It also exercises the jurisdiction to order the issue of writs of <i><a href="/wiki/Venire_de_novo" class="mw-redirect" title="Venire de novo">venire de novo</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Criminal Division, while bound by the Supreme Court, is more flexible with binding itself, due to the heightened stakes in a case where a possible penalty is a prison sentence.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Division is led by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Ian_Burnett,_Baron_Burnett_of_Maldon" title="Ian Burnett, Baron Burnett of Maldon">Lord Burnett of Maldon</a>, assisted by the <a href="/wiki/Vice-President_of_the_Criminal_Division" title="Vice-President of the Criminal Division">Vice-President of the Criminal Division</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Heather_Hallett" title="Heather Hallett">Lady Justice Hallett</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure_for_appeal">Procedure for appeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Procedure for appeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Sections 54 to 59 of the <a href="/wiki/Access_to_Justice_Act_1999" title="Access to Justice Act 1999">Access to Justice Act 1999</a> and Part 52 of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Procedure_Rules_1998" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Procedure Rules 1998">Civil Procedure Rules 1998</a> came into force on 2 May 2000, and created one universal appeals system; not all of these are to the Court of Appeal, with the principle used that an appeal should go to the next highest court in the hierarchy.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Appeals are allowed if the decision in the court below was incorrect, or suffered from a serious procedural error or irregularity.<sup id="cite_ref-lp_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lp-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Almost all appeals require permission, a major innovation from the previous system, where appeals were, on the request of counsel, almost all automatically put through. The application for permission should be made to the lower court, although this is not mandatory; it may be asked of the appellate court itself. In <i>Re T (A Child)</i> [2002] EWCA Civ 1736, the Civil Division strongly advised that counsel apply at the lower courts, since the judge, fully aware of the facts, will take less time to process, there is no harm if the application fails or if it is approved but counsel decides not to proceed with the case and there are no additional costs involved. The only problem here is that judgments may occasionally be reserved, and only given later by post \xe2\x80\x93 there may not be an opportunity to ask for permission to appeal at the lower court.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Court of Appeal, when considering an application for appeal, may decide based on the paper documents or refer the case to an oral hearing, something often done when it is apparent that a refusal of the written case will lead the applicant to send a second, oral application. If a written application is refused, the applicant may ask for an oral hearing to discuss the refusal. Under the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Procedure_Rules_1998" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Procedure Rules 1998">Civil Procedure Rules 1998</a>, the appeal must have "a real prospect of success", or there must be "some other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard" for it to be accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under certain, limited, circumstances, second appeals are allowed. This is when an appeal goes to the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> or <a href="/wiki/County_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="County Court">County Court</a> and a party to the case wishes to appeal it further, to the Court of Appeal. Section 55(1) of the Access to Justice Act 1999 says that, when an appeal is made to a County Court or the High Court and that court makes a decision, no further appeal is allowed to the Court of Appeal unless the Court considers that the case raises "an important point of principle or practice" or "there is some other compelling reason for the Court of Appeal to hear it".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In <i>Tanfern Ltd v Cameron-MacDonald</i> [2000] 1 WLR 1311 the Court commented on this limitation of second appeals, pointing out that the Lords Justices of Appeal were a valuable and scarce resource \xe2\x80\x93 it was necessary to impose limitations on appeals to prevent the Court and its judges becoming overburdened.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are two sorts of hearings that the Court of Appeal can hold; reviews, and full rehearings. Section 52.11(1) of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Procedure_Rules_1998" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Procedure Rules 1998">Civil Procedure Rules 1998</a> establishes that appeals should always be reviews, unless there are individual circumstances that, "in the interest of justice", make a rehearing necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In its case law, the Court has emphasised that it is up to the individual panel of judges to decide whether to hold a review or rehearing, with the circumstances of the case playing a large part.<sup id="cite_ref-lp_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lp-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 2004 the Court heard 1,059 appeals, of which 295 were allowed and 413 directly dismissed.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judges">Judges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Judges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Lords_Justices_of_Appeal" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Lords Justices of Appeal">List of Lords Justices of Appeal</a></div>\n<p>The Court of Appeal\'s main judges are the Lords Justices of Appeal and Lady Justices of Appeal. The Senior Courts Act 1981 provides that the Court of Appeal comprise 39 ordinary sitting Lords and Lady Justices and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Rolls" title="Master of the Rolls">Master of the Rolls</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Queen%27s_Bench_Division" title="President of the Queen&#39;s Bench Division">President of the Queen\'s Bench Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Family_Division" title="President of the Family Division">President of the Family Division</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_High_Court" title="Chancellor of the High Court">Chancellor of the High Court</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Retired Lords and Lady Justices sometimes sit in cases, as have retired <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">Law Lords</a>, and High Court judges are allowed to sit on occasion and, in the Criminal Division, there are a number of Senior Circuit Judges authorised to sit as judges of the Court of Appeal (Criminal Division).\n</p><p>Lords and Lady Justices have, since 1946, been drawn exclusively from the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a>; prior to this, Lords Justices were (rarely) recruited directly from the <a href="/wiki/Bar_(law)" title="Bar (law)">Bar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Butler-Sloss" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Butler-Sloss">Dame Elizabeth Butler-Sloss</a> was the first woman appointed as a Lord Justice of Appeal in 1988; she was known officially as "Lord Justice" until a <a href="/wiki/Practice_direction" title="Practice direction">practice direction</a> was issued in 1994 to refer to her informally as "Lady Justice", and the official title in section 3 of the Senior Courts Act 1981 was amended by the <a href="/wiki/Courts_Act_2003" title="Courts Act 2003">Courts Act 2003</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Thirlwall" title="Kathryn Thirlwall">Dame Kathryn Thirlwall</a> was the twelfth Lady Justice, appointed in 2017, bringing the number of active Lady Justices to 9 out of 39.\n</p><p>The division of work in the Court of Appeal is demonstrated by the 2005 statistics, in which Lords and Lady Justices sat 66% of the time, High Court Judges 26% of the time and Circuit and Deputy High Court Judges 8 per cent of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Lord and Lady Justices are currently paid \xc2\xa3188,900, with the Master of the Rolls paid \xc2\xa3205,700 and the Lord Chief Justice \xc2\xa3230,400.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Civil Division is led by the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Rolls" title="Master of the Rolls">Master of the Rolls</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Terence_Etherton" title="Terence Etherton">Sir Terence Etherton</a>, who uses the postnominal MR; the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_High_Court" title="Chancellor of the High Court">Chancellor of the High Court</a> and <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Family_Division" title="President of the Family Division">President of the Family Division</a> regularly, for a period of weeks, lead the Civil Division. Several Civil Division Lords Justices are seconded to the Criminal Division, which is currently led by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Burnett" class="mw-redirect" title="Ian Burnett">Sir Ian Burnett</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasting">Broadcasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Court_of_Appeal_(England_and_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Broadcasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 31 October 2013 the Court of Appeal allowed cameras in the court for a (70-second <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_delay" title="Broadcast delay">broadcast delay</a>) "live" broadcast feed for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Cameras were banned in all courts in 1925 (although they were allowed in the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> from its 2009 inception). Cameras have now been allowed in some courts due to changes made by the <a href="/wiki/Crime_and_Courts_Act_2013" title="Crime and Courts Act 2013">Crime and Courts Act 2013</a>. Currently, only one court can be broadcast per day.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The Ministry of Justice announced the move via <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
