href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a> use in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> stood at 2,249 <a href="/wiki/TWh" class="mw-redirect" title="TWh">TWh</a> (193.4 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne_of_oil_equivalent" title="Tonne of oil equivalent">tonnes of oil equivalent</a>) in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a electricity in 2014 was 34.42<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">GW</a> on average<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (301.7TWh over the year) coming from a total electricity generation of 335.0<a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">TWh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Successive UK governments have outlined numerous commitments to reduce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions.  One such announcement was the <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_economy" title="Low-carbon economy">Low Carbon</a> Transition Plan launched by the <a href="/wiki/Brown_ministry" title="Brown ministry">Brown ministry</a> in July 2009, which aimed to generate 30% electricity from <a href="/wiki/Renewable" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable">renewable</a> sources, and 40% from low carbon content fuels by 2020. Notably, the UK is <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Wind power in the United Kingdom">one of the best sites in Europe for wind energy</a>, and wind power production is its fastest growing supply.<sup id="cite_ref-Roadmap_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roadmap-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Wind power contributed 15% of UK electricity generation in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-renewableuk.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-renewableuk.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Government commitments to reduce emissions are occurring against a backdrop of <a href="/wiki/Economic_crisis_of_2008" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic crisis of 2008">economic crisis</a> across Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  During the <a href="/wiki/European_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="European financial crisis">European financial crisis</a>, Europe\xe2\x80\x99s consumption of electricity shrank by 5%, with primary production also facing a noticeable decline. Britain\'s trade deficit was reduced by 8% due to substantial cuts in energy imports.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Between 2007 and 2015, the UK\'s peak electrical demand fell from 61.5 GW to 52.7.GW.<sup id="cite_ref-NSE_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NSE-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Energy policy of the United Kingdom">UK government energy policy</a> aims to play a key role in limiting <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a>, whilst meeting energy demand. Shifting availabilities of resources and development of technologies also change the country\'s <a href="/wiki/Energy_mix" title="Energy mix">energy mix</a> through changes in costs. In 2016, the United Kingdom was ranked 12th in the World on the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index" title="Environmental Performance Index">Environmental Performance Index</a>,<sup Scotland also aims to repair 250,000 hectares of degraded <a href="/wiki/Peatland" class="mw-redirect" title="Peatland">peatlands</a>, which store a total of 1.7 gigatonnes of CO2. <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuels">Fossil fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fossil fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_active_coal_fired_power_stations_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of active coal fired power stations in the United Kingdom">List of active coal fired power stations in the United Kingdom</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg/220px-Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg.png" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg/330px-Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg/440px-Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="461" data-file-height="319" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fossil_fuel_consumption_in_the_United_Kingdom.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fossil fuel consumption in the UK. In five years the United Kingdom moved from almost gas self-sufficient (see <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_Gas" class="mw-redirect" title="North Sea Gas">North Sea Gas</a>) to 40% gas import in 2010. Gas was almost 40% of total primary energy supply (TPES) and electricity more than 45% in 2010. Underground storage was about 5% of annual demand and more than 10% of net imports. There is an alternative fuel obligation in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> (see <a id="UK_.27energy_gap.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="UK_\'energy_gap\'">UK \'energy gap\'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: UK &#039;energy gap&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG/220px-UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG/330px-UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG/440px-UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG 2x" data-file-width="824" data-file-height="414" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Electricity_Capacity_Margin.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>UK Electricity Capacity Margin<sup id="cite_ref-DECC2013_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DECC2013-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>In the early years of the 2000s, concerns grew over the prospect of an \'energy gap\' in United Kingdom generating capacity. This was forecast to arise because it was expected that a number of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuel power plant">coal fired power stations</a> would close due to being unable to meet the clean air requirements of the <a href="/wiki/Large_Combustion_Plant_Directive" title="Large Combustion Plant Directive">European Large Combustion Plant Directive</a> (directive 2001/80/EC).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the United Kingdom\'s remaining <a href="/wiki/Magnox" title="Magnox">Magnox</a> nuclear stations were to have closed by 2015. The oldest <a href="/wiki/Advanced_gas-cooled_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced gas-cooled reactor">AGR</a> nuclear power station has had its life extended by ten years,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and it was likely many of the others could be life-extended, reducing the potential gap suggested by the current accounting closure dates of between 2014 and 2023 for the AGR power stations.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A report from the industry in 2005 forecast that, without action to fill the gap, there would be a 20% shortfall in electricity generation capacity by 2015. Similar concerns were raised by a report published in 2000 by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_Environmental_Pollution" title="Royal Commission on Environmental Pollution">Royal Commission on Environmental Pollution</a> <i>(<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070220162203/http://www.rcep.org.uk/newenergy.htm">Energy - The Changing Climate</a></i>). The <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_United_Kingdom#2006_Energy_Review" title="Energy policy of the United Kingdom">2006 Energy Review</a> attracted considerable press coverage - in particular in relation to the prospect of constructing a new generation of nuclear power stations, in order to prevent the rise in carbon dioxide emissions that would arise if other conventional power stations were to be built.\n</p><p>Among the public, according to a November 2005 poll conducted by <a href="/wiki/YouGov" title="YouGov">YouGov</a> for <a href="/wiki/Deloitte" title="Deloitte">Deloitte</a>, 35% of the population expect that by 2020 the majority of electricity generation will come from renewable energy (more than double the government\'s target, and far larger than the 5.5% generated as of 2008),<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> 23% expect that the majority will come from nuclear power, and only 18% that the majority will come from fossil fuels. 92% thought the Government should do more to explore alternative power generation technologies to reduce carbon emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2013, the industry regulator <a href="/wiki/Ofgem" class="mw-redirect" title="Ofgem">Ofgem</a> warned that the UK\'s energy sector faces "unprecedented challenges" and that "spare electricity power production capacity could fall to 2% by 2015, increasing the risk of blackouts". Proposed solutions "could include negotiating with major power users for them to <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_Reserve_Service" title="National Grid Reserve Service">reduce demand during peak times</a> in return for payment".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plugging_the_energy_gap">Plugging the energy gap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Plugging the energy gap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first move to plug the United Kingdom\'s projected energy gap was the construction of the conventionally gas-fired <a href="/wiki/Langage_Power_Station" title="Langage Power Station">Langage Power Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marchwood_Power_Station" title="Marchwood Power Station">Marchwood Power Station</a> which became operational in 2010.\n</p><p>In 2007, proposals for the construction of two new coal-fired power stations were announced, in <a href="/wiki/Tilbury" title="Tilbury">Tilbury</a>, Essex and in <a href="/wiki/Kingsnorth_(Medway)" title="Kingsnorth (Medway)">Kingsnorth</a>, Kent. They were to be the first coal-fired stations to be built in the United Kingdom in 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> No power station at Tilbury was built and the plans for a new Kingsnorth coal powered station were later shelved.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Beyond these new plants, there were a number of options that might have been used to provide the new generating capacity, while minimising <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emission" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emission">carbon emissions</a> and producing less residues and contamination. <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuel power plant">Fossil fuel power plants</a> might provide a solution if there was a satisfactory and economical way of reducing their carbon emissions. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture</a> might provide a way of doing this; however the technology is relatively untried and costs are relatively high. As of 2006 there were no power plants in operation with a full carbon capture and storage system, and as of 2018 the situation is little better.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_gap_disappears">Energy gap disappears</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Energy gap disappears">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>However, due to reducing demand in the <a href="/wiki/Late-2000s_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-2000s recession">late-2000s recession</a> removing any medium term gap, and high gas prices, in 2011 and 2012 over 2 GW of older, less efficient, gas generation plant was mothballed.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-argus-20120312_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-argus-20120312-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In 2011 electricity demand dropped 4%, and about 6.5 GW of additional gas-fired capacity was added over 2011 and 2012. Early in 2012 the reserve margin stood at the high level of 32%.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An important factor in reduced electrical demand in recent years has come from the <a href="/wiki/Phasing_out_of_incandescent_light_bulbs" class="mw-redirect" title="Phasing out of incandescent light bulbs">phasing out of incandescent light bulbs</a> and a switch to <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent" class="mw-redirect" title="Compact fluorescent">compact fluorescent</a> and <a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">LED lighting</a>. Research by the University of Oxford<sup id="cite_ref-NSE_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NSE-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> has shown that the average annual electrical consumption for lighting in a UK home fell from 720&#160;kWh in 1997 to 508&#160;kWh in 2012. Between 2007 and 2015, the UK\'s peak electrical demand fell from 61.5 GW to 52.7.GW.<sup id="cite_ref-NSE_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NSE-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_differences">Regional differences</span><span title="Scotland">Scotland</a> currently has a target of 80% of electricity from renewables by 2020, which was increased from an original ambition of 50% by 2020 after it exceeded its interim target of 31 per cent by 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> It has a quarter of the EU\'s estimated offshore wind potential,<sup id="cite_ref-53" href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">Combined heat and power plants</a>, where \'waste\' hot water from generating is used for <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>, are also a well tried technology in other parts of Europe. While it heats about 50% of all houses in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, it currently only plays a small role in the United Kingdom. It has, however, been rising, with total generation standing at 27.9 TWh by 2008. This consisted of 1,439 predominantly gas-fired schemes with a total CHP electrical generating capacity of 5.47 GW, and contributing 7% of the UK\'s electricity supply.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.uk_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.uk-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Heat generation utilisation has fallen however from a peak of 65 TWh in 1991 to 49 TWh in 2012.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_research">Energy research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Energy research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Historically, public sector support for energy research and development in the United Kingdom has been provided by a variety of bodies with little co-ordination between them. Problems experienced have included poor continuity of funding, and the availability of funding for certain parts of the research\xe2\x80\x94development\xe2\x80\x94commercialisation process but not others. Levels of public funding have also been low by international standards, and funding by the private sector has also been limited.\n</p><p>Research in the area of energy is carried out by a number of public and private sector bodies:\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Engineering_and_Physical_Sciences_Research_Council" title="Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council">Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council</a> funds an energy programme<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>  spanning energy and climate change research. It aims to "develop, embrace and exploit sustainable, low carbon and/or energy efficient technologies and systems" to enable the United Kingdom "to meet the Government\xe2\x80\x99s energy and environmental targets by 2020". Its research includes renewable, conventional, nuclear and <a href="/wiki/Fusion_power" title="Fusion power">fusion</a> electricity supply as well as energy efficiency, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_poverty" title="Fuel poverty">fuel poverty</a> and other topics.\n</p><p>Since being established in 2004, the UK Energy Research Centre<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> carries out research into demand reduction, future sources of energy, infrastructure and supply, energy systems, sustainability and materials for advanced energy systems.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Energy_Technologies_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Energy Technologies Institute">Energy Technologies Institute</a>, expected to begin operating in 2008, is to \'accelerate the development of secure, reliable and cost-effective low-carbon energy technologies towards commercial deployment\'.\n</p><p>In relation to buildings, the Building Research Establishment<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> carries out some research into energy conservation.\n</p><p>There is currently international research being conducted into <a href="/wiki/Fusion_power" title="Fusion power">fusion power</a>. The <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a> reactor is currently being constructed at <a href="/wiki/Cadarache" title="Cadarache">Cadarache</a> in France. The United Kingdom contributes towards this project through membership of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. Prior to this, an experimental fusion reactor (the <a href="/wiki/Joint_European_Torus" title="Joint European Torus">Joint European Torus</a>) had been built at <a href="/wiki/Culham" title="Culham">Culham</a> in Oxfordshire.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency">Energy efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The United Kingdom government has instituted several policies intended to promote an increase in efficient energy use. These include the roll out of <a href="/wiki/Smart_meters" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart meters">smart meters</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Deal" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Deal">Green Deal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/CRC_Energy_Efficiency_Scheme" title="CRC Energy Efficiency Scheme">CRC Energy Efficiency Scheme</a>, the Energy Savings Opportunity Scheme<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Agreement" title="Climate Change Agreement">Climate Change Agreements</a>.\n</p><p>In tackling the energy trilemma, <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">saving energy</a> is the cheapest of all measures. The Guardian newspaper reported that in 2012 that by 2050, Germany projects a 25% drop in electricity demand: the UK projects a rise of up to 66%. <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Energy_and_Climate_Change" title="Secretary of State for Energy and Climate Change">Secretary of State for Energy and Climate Change</a> <a href="/wiki/Ed_Davey" title="Ed Davey">Ed Davey</a> pointed out in November 2012 that a modest 10% reduction in 2030 means five fewer power stations and \xc2\xa34bn cut from bills.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The UK government has implemented measures aimed at cutting the UK\'s energy use by 11% (around 196TWh) by 2020. If achieved, this improvement would be sufficient to replace 22 UK power stations, while possibly providing a boost to the economy and living standards.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Climate_Change" title="Committee on Climate Change">Committee on Climate Change</a> publishes an annual progress report in respect to control the <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Climate change in the United Kingdom">climate change in the United Kingdom</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
