Section 2(2) of the Parliament Act 1949 provides that the two Acts are to be <a href="/wiki/Statutory_interpretation" title="Statutory interpretation">construed</a> as one.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act 1911</a> (1 &amp; 2 Geo. 5. c. 13) asserted the supremacy of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom House of Commons">House of Commons</a> by limiting the legislation-blocking powers of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> (the <i>suspensory veto</i>). Provided the provisions of the Act are met, legislation can be passed without the approval of the House of Lords. Additionally, the 1911 Act amended the <a href="/wiki/Septennial_Act_1716" title="Septennial Act 1716">Septennial Act 1716</a> to reduce the maximum life of a Parliament from seven years to five years. The Parliament Act 1911 was amended by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1949" title="Parliament Act 1949">Parliament Act 1949</a> (12, 13 &amp; 14 Geo. 6. c. 103), which further limited the power of the Lords by reducing the time that they could delay bills, from two years to one.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Parliament Acts have been used to pass legislation against the wishes of the House of Lords on seven occasions since 1911, including the passing of the Parliament Act 1949. Some <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_law" title="Constitutional law">constitutional lawyers</a> had questioned the validity of the 1949 Act. These doubts were rejected in 2005 when members of the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_Alliance" title="Countryside Alliance">Countryside Alliance</a> unsuccessfully challenged the validity of the <a href="/wiki/Hunting_Act_2004" title="Hunting Act 2004">Hunting Act 2004</a>, which had been passed under the auspices of the Act. In October 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">Appellate Committee of the House of Lords</a> dismissed the Alliance\'s appeal against this decision, with an unusually large panel of nine <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" title="Lords of Appeal in Ordinary">Law Lords</a> (out of then-existing twelve) holding that the 1949 Act was a valid Act of Parliament.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act 1911</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg/220px-David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg" width="220" height="298" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg/330px-David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg/440px-David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg 2x" data-file-width="460" data-file-height="624" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Lloyd_George_c1911.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The 1911 Act was a reaction to the clash between the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> government and the House of Lords, culminating in the so-called "<a href="/wiki/People%27s_Budget" title="People&#39;s Budget">People\'s Budget</a>" of 1909. In this <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_budget" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom budget">Budget</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> proposed the introduction of a <a href="/wiki/Land_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Land tax">land tax</a> based on the ideas of the American <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">tax</a> reformer <a href="/wiki/Henry_George" title="Henry George">Henry George</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newstatesman_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newstatesman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This new tax would have had a major effect on large landowners, and was opposed by the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> opposition, many of whom were large landowners themselves. The Conservatives believed that money should be raised through the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> on <a href="/wiki/Imports" class="mw-redirect" title="Imports">imports</a>, which they claimed would help British industry. Contrary to British constitutional convention, the Conservatives used their large majority in the Lords to vote down the Budget. The Liberals made reducing the power of the Lords an important issue of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_January_1910" title="United Kingdom general election, January 1910">January 1910 general election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-liberalhistory_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liberalhistory-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Liberals returned in a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a> after the election:<sup id="cite_ref-oup_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oup-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> their call for action against the Lords had energised believers in hereditary principle to vote for the Conservatives, but had failed to generate much interest with the rest of the voting public. The Liberals formed a <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority government</a> with the support of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> and Irish nationalist MPs. The Lords subsequently accepted the Budget when the land tax proposal was dropped. However, as a result of the dispute over the Budget, the new government introduced resolutions (that would later form the Parliament Bill) to limit the power of the Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-reform_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reform-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a>, asked <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">King Edward VII</a> to create sufficient new Liberal <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">peers</a> to pass the Bill if the Lords rejected it. The King said he would not be willing to do so unless Asquith obtained a clear mandate for such sweeping change by winning a second general election.\n</p><p>The Lords voted this 1910 Bill down. Edward VII had died in May 1910, but his son <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> agreed to grant Asquith a second general election in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_December_1910" title="United Kingdom general election, December 1910">December 1910</a> (this also resulted in a minority government), and at the time he agreed that, if necessary, he would create  hundreds of new Liberal peers to neutralise the Conservative majority in the Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-noten_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noten-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Conservative Lords then backed down, and on 10 August 1911, the House of Lords passed the Parliament Act by a narrow 131\xe2\x80\x93114 vote,<sup id="cite_ref-reformbackground_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformbackground-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> with the support of some two dozen Conservative peers and eleven of thirteen <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Lords Spiritual</a>.\n</p><p>The Parliament Act was intended as a temporary measure. The preamble states:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>whereas it is intended to substitute for the House of Lords as it at present exists a Second Chamber constituted on a popular instead of hereditary basis, but such substitution cannot be immediately brought into operation.<sup id="cite_ref-1911text_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1911text-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>One of the reasons for the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a> MPs\' support for the Parliament Act, and the bitterness of the <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionist</a> resistance, was that the loss of the Lords\' veto would make possible <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Home Rule Movement">Irish Home Rule</a> (i.e. a <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> legislature).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The previous Liberal government\'s attempt to initiate Irish Home Rule had been vetoed by the House of Lords in 1893: at the time of his retirement in 1894, <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a> had not attracted sufficient support from his colleagues for a battle with the House of Lords. The Parliament Act resulted in the eventual enactment of the Irish Home Rule <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Government of Ireland Act 1914</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill,_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill%2C_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg/350px-Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill%2C_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg" width="350" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill%2C_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg/525px-Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill%2C_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill%2C_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg/700px-Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill%2C_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1554" data-file-height="932" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Passing_of_the_Parliament_Bill,_1911_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19609.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Passing of the Parliament Bill, 1911, from the drawing by S. Begg</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisions">Provisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Provisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Act abolished any power of the House of Lords to <a href="/wiki/Veto" title="Veto">veto</a> any <a href="/wiki/Public_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Bill">public Bill</a> introduced in the House of Commons<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> other than a Bill containing any provision to extend the maximum duration of Parliament beyond five years<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> or a Bill for confirming a <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Provisional Order">Provisional Order</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Act does not affect Bills introduced in the House of Lords,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Private_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Private Bill">private Bills</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Delegated_legislation_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Delegated legislation in the United Kingdom">delegated legislation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The effect of the Act is that the House of Lords can delay those Bills that it could formerly veto. If they have been sent up to the House of Lords at least one month before the end of the Session, <a href="/wiki/Money_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Money Bill">Money Bills</a> can be delayed for up to one month after being sent up, and other Bills can be delayed for up to one year after being sent up. The period for which Bills other than Money Bills could be delayed was originally two years.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" title="Speaker of the House of Commons (United Kingdom)">Speaker</a> was given the power to certify which bills are classified as money bills.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Section_1:_Powers_of_House_of_Lords_as_to_Money_Bills">Section 1: Powers of House of Lords as to Money Bills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Section 1: Powers of House of Lords as to Money Bills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Section 1(1) provides:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>If a Money Bill, having been passed by the House of Commons, and sent up to the House of Lords at least one month before the end of the session, is not passed by the House of Lords without amendment within one month after it is so sent up to that House, the Bill shall, unless the House of Commons direct to the contrary, be presented to His Majesty and become an Act of Parliament on the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> being signified, notwithstanding that the House of Lords have not consented to the Bill.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>The word "month" means calendar month.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Section 1(2) defines the expression "<a href="/wiki/Money_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Money Bill">Money Bill</a>".\n</p><p>Section 1(3) provides:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There shall be endorsed on every Money Bill when it is sent up to the House of Lords and when it is presented to His Majesty for assent the certificate of the Speaker of the House of Commons signed by him that it is a Money Bill. Before giving his certificate the Speaker shall consult, if practicable, two members to be appointed from the <a href="/wiki/Panel_of_Chairs" title="Panel of Chairs">Chairmen\'s Panel</a> at the beginning of each Session by the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Selection_(House_of_Commons)" title="Committee of Selection (House of Commons)">Committee of Selection</a>.\n</p></blockquote>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Section_2:_Restriction_of_the_powers_of_the_House_of_Lords_as_to_Bills_other_than_Money_Bills">Section 2: Restriction of the powers of the House of Lords as to Bills other than Money Bills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Section 2: Restriction of the powers of the House of Lords as to Bills other than Money Bills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>This section originally provided that a Bill, to which this section applied, which was rejected by the House of Lords, would be presented for Royal Assent if it was passed by the Commons in three successive sessions, provided that two years had elapsed between <a href="/wiki/Second_Reading" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Reading">Second Reading</a> of the Bill and its final passing in the Commons, notwithstanding that the Lords had not consented to the Bill.\n</p><p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo6/12-13-14/103/section/1">Section 1</a> of the Parliament Act 1949 provides that the Parliament Act 1911 has effect, and is deemed to have had effect from the beginning of the session in which the Bill for the Parliament Act 1949 originated (save as regards that Bill itself), as though sections 2(1) and (4), of Parliament Act 1911, read as they are printed in the following revised text of section 2 of that Act:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>(1) If any Public Bill (other than a Money Bill or a Bill containing any provision to extend the maximum duration of Parliament beyond five years) is passed by the House of Commons [in two successive sessions] (whether of the same Parliament or not), and, having been sent up to the House of Lords at least one month before the end of the session, is rejected by the House of Lords in each of those sessions, that Bill shall, on its rejection [for the second time] by the House of Lords, unless the House of Commons direct to the contrary, be presented to His Majesty and become an Act of Parliament on the Royal Assent being signified thereto, notwithstanding that the House of Lords have not consented to the Bill:\n</p><p>Provided that this provision shall not take effect unless [one year has elapsed] between the date of the second reading in the first of those sessions of the Bill in the House of Commons and the date on which it passes the House of Commons [in the second of these sessions.]\n</p><p>(2) When a Bill is presented to His Majesty for assent in pursuance of the provisions of this section, there shall be endorsed on the Bill the certificate of the Speaker of the House of Commons signed by him that the provisions of this section have been duly complied with.\n</p><p>(3) A Bill shall be deemed to be rejected by the House of Lords if it is not passed by the House of Lords either without amendment or with such amendments only as may be agreed to by both Houses.\n</p><p>(4) A Bill shall be deemed to be the same Bill as a former Bill sent up to the House of Lords in the preceding session if, when it is sent up to the House of Lords, it is identical with the former Bill or contains only such alterations as are certified by the Speaker of the House of Commons to be necessary owing to the time which has elapsed since the date of the former Bill, or to represent any amendments which have been made by the House of Lords in the former Bill in the preceding session, and any amendments which are certified by the Speaker to have been made by the House of Lords [in the second session] and agreed to by the House of Commons shall be inserted in the Bill as presented for Royal Assent in pursuance of this section:\n</p><p>Provided that the House of Commons may, if they think fit, on the passage of such a Bill through the House [in the second session,] suggest any further amendments without inserting the amendments in the Bill, and any such suggested amendments shall be considered by the House of Lords, and, if agreed to by that House, shall be treated as amendments made by the House of Lords and agreed to by the House of Commons; but the exercise of this power by the House of Commons shall not affect the operation of this section in the event of the Bill being rejected by the House of Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The words in square brackets are those substituted by section 1 of the Parliament Act 1949.\n</p><p>Before it was repealed in 1986, the proviso to section 1 of the Parliament Act 1949 read:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Provided that, if a Bill has been rejected for the second time by the House of Lords before the signification of the Royal Assent to the Bill for this Act, whether such rejection was in the same session as that in which the Royal Assent to the Bill for this Act was signified or in an earlier session, the requirement of the said section two that a Bill is to be presented to His Majesty on its rejection for the second time by the House of Lords shall have effect in relation to the Bill rejected as a requirement that it is to be presented to His Majesty as soon as the Royal Assent to the Bill for this Act has been signified, and, notwithstanding that such rejection was in an earlier session, the Royal Assent to the Bill rejected may be signified in the session in which the Royal Assent to the Bill for this Act was signified.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>This proviso provided for the application of the Parliament Act 1911 to any Bill rejected for the second time by the House of Lords before the Royal Assent was given to the Parliament Act 1949 on 16 December 1949. In a report dated 27 September 1985, the <a href="/wiki/Law_Commission_(England_and_Wales)" title="Law Commission (England and Wales)">Law Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Law_Commission" title="Scottish Law Commission">Scottish Law Commission</a> said that this proviso had never been invoked and was, by that date, incapable of being invoked.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> They recommended that it be repealed.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Section_6:_Saving_for_existing_rights_and_privileges_of_the_House_of_Commons">Section 6: Saving for existing rights and privileges of the House of Commons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Section 6: Saving for existing rights and privileges of the House of Commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>This section provides:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Nothing in this Act shall diminish or qualify the existing rights and privileges of the House of Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>The Prime Minister, H. H. Asquith, said of the clause that became this section:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>That is to enable us still, when the occasion arises, to approve of particular Amendments made by the House of Lords in regard to which this House may waive its privilege.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Section_7:_Duration_of_Parliament">Section 7: Duration of Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Section 7: Duration of Parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>This section amended the <a href="/wiki/Septennial_Act_1715" class="mw-redirect" title="Septennial Act 1715">Septennial Act 1715</a>, reducing the maximum duration of any Parliament from seven years to five.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The President of the Board of Education, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Runciman,_1st_Viscount_Runciman_of_Doxford" title="Walter Runciman, 1st Viscount Runciman of Doxford">Walter Runciman</a>, said:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The period of five years is chosen with the deliberate object of striking a compromise between, the old triennial Parliament advocated in <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartist</a> days and the seven years\' Parliament. Our object in limiting the period to five years is that there may be no risk run of the perils which have been enunciated with great vigour by right hon. and hon. Gentlemen opposite of a Government taking advantage of the powers granted to it under the second Resolution, outliving its welcome, getting completely out of touch with the country, and using its extended period of life, say of six years, for carrying through legislation of which the country does not approve. The five years named in the Resolution will in almost every case mean a four years\' Parliament. It means therefore that if a Parliament divided its time in the manner described by the Leader of the Opposition, in the first two years doing the work for which it was returned, and in the second two years looking forward to the election about to come upon it, it would have filled up the whole of the four years\' period for which this Resolution provides. Our only object in limiting the period of the duration of Parliament is that the House of Commons shall not get out of touch with the opinion of the electorate.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>This section was repealed by the <a href="/wiki/Fixed-term_Parliaments_Act_2011" title="Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011">Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> for the United Kingdom<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> on 15 September 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> when parliament was given a <a href="/wiki/Fixed-term_election" title="Fixed-term election">fixed</a> five-year term.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repeal_in_Ireland">Repeal in Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Repeal in Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This Act was repealed for the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> on 16 May 1983 by section 1 of, and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.irishstatutebook.ie/1983/en/act/pub/0011/sched.html#sched-part4">Part IV</a> of the Schedule to, the <a 1949</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Attlee_BW_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" href="/wiki/File:Attlee_BW_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Immediately after the Second World War, the Labour government of <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a> decided to amend the 1911 Act to reduce further the power of the Lords, as a result of their fears that their radical programme of <a href="/wiki/Nationalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalisation">nationalisation</a> would be delayed by the Lords and hence would not be completed within the life of the parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-AB2R_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AB2R-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The House of Lords did not interfere with nationalisations in 1945 or 1946, but it was feared that the proposed nationalisation of the iron and steel industry would be a bridge too far,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> so a bill was introduced in 1947 to reduce the time that the Lords could delay bills, from three sessions over two years to two sessions over one year.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Lords attempted to block this change. The Bill was reintroduced in 1948 and again in 1949, before the 1911 Act was finally used to force it through.<sup id="cite_ref-jackson1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  Since the 1911 Act required a delay over three "sessions", a special short "session" of parliament was introduced in 1948, with a <a href="/wiki/Speech_from_the_Throne" class="mw-redirect" title="Speech from the Throne">King\'s Speech</a> on 14 September 1948, and <a href="/wiki/Legislative_session#Procedure_in_Commonwealth_realms" title="Legislative session">prorogation</a> on 25 October.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The amended Parliament Act was never used in the 1940s or 1950s, possibly because the mere threat of it was enough.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Salisbury convention">Salisbury convention</a> that the Lords would not block government bills that were mentioned in the government\'s <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a> dates from this time. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gascoyne-Cecil,_5th_Marquess_of_Salisbury" title="Robert Gascoyne-Cecil, 5th Marquess of Salisbury">Salisbury</a> believed that since, in being returned to power, the Government was given a clear mandate for the policies proposed in its manifesto, it would be improper for the Lords to frustrate such legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-Salisbury_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salisbury-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In every Bill presented to the Sovereign under sections 1 to 3 of the Parliament Act 1911 (as amended) the <a href="/wiki/Words_of_enactment" class="mw-redirect" title="Words of enactment">words of enactment</a> are:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>BE IT ENACTED by The Queen\'s most Excellent Majesty, by and with the advice and consent of the Commons in this present Parliament assembled, in accordance with the provisions of the Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949, and by the authority of the same, as follows<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>The usual enacting formula, used on other Acts, also refers to the advice and consent of the Lords <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Spiritual</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lords_Temporal" title="Lords Temporal">Temporal</a>, and omits the reference to the Parliament Acts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_Parliament_Acts">Use of the Parliament Acts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Use of the Parliament Acts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The original form of the 1911 Act was used three times.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These were:\n</p>\n<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Government of Ireland Act 1914</a>, which would have established a <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Rule">Home Rule</a> government in Ireland; its implementation was blocked due to the First World War.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a>, under which the Welsh part of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> was <a href="/wiki/Disestablishment" class="mw-redirect" title="Disestablishment">disestablished</a> in 1920, becoming the <a href="/wiki/Church_in_Wales" title="Church in Wales">Church in Wales</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1949" title="Parliament Act 1949">Parliament Act 1949</a>, which amended the Parliament Act 1911 (discussed above).</li></ol>\n<p>The amended form of the 1911 Act has been used four times.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These were:\n</p>\n<ol><li><a href="/wiki/War_Crimes_Act_1991" title="War Crimes Act 1991">War Crimes Act 1991</a>, which extended jurisdiction of UK courts to acts committed on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> during the Second World War (the only time \xe2\x80\x93 to date \xe2\x80\x93 that the Parliament Acts have been used by a <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> government).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/European_Parliamentary_Elections_Act_1999" title="European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999">European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999</a>, which changed the system of elections to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> from <a href="/wiki/First_past_the_post" class="mw-redirect" title="First past the post">first past the post</a> to a form of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Sexual_Offences_(Amendment)_Act_2000" title="Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act 2000">Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act 2000</a>, which equalised the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_consent" title="Age of consent">age of consent</a> for male <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexual</a> sexual activities with that for <a href="/wiki/Heterosexual" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterosexual">heterosexual</a> and female homosexual sexual activities at 16.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hunting_Act_2004" title="Hunting Act 2004">Hunting Act 2004</a>, which prohibited <a href="/wiki/Hare_coursing" title="Hare coursing">hare coursing</a> and (subject to some exceptions) all <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> of wild <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">foxes</a>) with dogs after early 2005.</li></ol>\n<p>The Welsh Church Act and the Government of Ireland Act were both given Royal Assent<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> on the same day as the <a href="/wiki/Suspensory_Act_1914" title="Suspensory Act 1914">Suspensory Act 1914</a>, which meant that neither would come into force until after World War I.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the Labour government came to power in 1997, there was repeated speculation that it would rely on the Parliament Acts to reverse a check from the Lords, but it did not prove necessary. The Parliament Acts were not required to enact, for example, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Criminal_Justice_(Mode_of_Trial)_(No_2)_Bill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Criminal Justice (Mode of Trial) (No 2) Bill (page does not exist)">Criminal Justice (Mode of Trial) (No 2) Bill</a> in 2000<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (which originally proposed to give <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates</a>, not defendants, the choice of where an "<a href="/wiki/Either_way" class="mw-redirect" title="Either way">either way</a>" offence would be tried) because the government abandoned the bill after a <a href="/wiki/Wrecking_amendment" title="Wrecking amendment">wrecking amendment</a> in the House of Lords.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Parliament Act was threatened to be used to get the <a href="/wiki/Identity_Cards_Act_2006" title="Identity Cards Act 2006">Identity Cards Act 2006</a> passed through the Lords. This was backed up by a threat of an immediate introduction of a compulsory ID Card scheme.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Lords had no option but to accept a compromise of a delay in the introduction of the scheme. The Parliament Acts cannot be used to force through legislation that originated in the House of Lords, so they could not have been used to enact the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Partnerships_Act_2004" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Partnerships Act 2004">Civil Partnerships Act 2004</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a>.\n</p><p>The first three measures for which the Act has been used since 1949 were not mentioned in manifestos, and hence in trying to veto them the Lords were not breaking the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Salisbury convention">Salisbury convention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The Hunting Bill was mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> manifesto for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001 general election</a>, so, depending upon how the convention is interpreted, the attempt to block it could be taken as a breach. However, as conventions are merely convention and not law, the House of Lords would not be taking illegal action if they were to act otherwise.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Government of Ireland Act 1914</a> was repealed in entirety by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliamentary_Elections_Act_1999" title="European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999">European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999</a> was repealed in entirety by the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliamentary_Elections_Act_2002" title="European Parliamentary Elections Act 2002">European Parliamentary Elections Act 2002</a> and most provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_Offences_(Amendment)_Act_2000" title="Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act 2000">Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act 2000</a> were repealed by the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_Offences_Act_2003" title="Sexual Offences Act 2003">Sexual Offences Act 2003</a>. While the <a href="/wiki/War_Crimes_Act_1991" title="War Crimes Act 1991">War Crimes Act 1991</a> remains in force, to date only <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Sawoniuk" title="Anthony Sawoniuk">Anthony Sawoniuk</a> has been convicted under it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The threat of the Parliament Acts has been employed by several British governments to force the Lords to accept its legislation. In at least three cases, the procedure authorised by the Parliament Act 1911, or by the Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949, was started, but the legislation was approved by the House of Lords as a result of the government making concessions.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These were:\n</p>\n<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Temperance_(Scotland)_Act_1913" title="Temperance (Scotland) Act 1913">Temperance (Scotland) Act 1913</a>, which allowed the voters in a district to hold a poll to vote on whether their district went "<a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">dry</a>" or remained "wet".</li>\n<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trade_Union_and_Labour_Relations_(Amendment)_Act_1976&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trade Union and Labour Relations (Amendment) Act 1976 (page does not exist)">Trade Union and Labour Relations (Amendment) Act 1976</a>, which amended the <a href="/wiki/Trade_Union_and_Labour_Relations_Act_1974" title="Trade Union and Labour Relations Act 1974">Trade Union and Labour Relations Act 1974</a> to reverse changes made to that Act as it passed through Parliament.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_and_Shipbuilding_Industries_Act_1977" title="Aircraft and Shipbuilding Industries Act 1977">Aircraft and Shipbuilding Industries Act 1977</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Nationalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalisation">nationalised</a> large parts of the UK aerospace and shipbuilding industries and established two corporations, <a href="/wiki/British_Aerospace" title="British Aerospace">British Aerospace</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Shipbuilders" title="British Shipbuilders">British Shipbuilders</a>.</li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Validity_of_the_1949_Act">Validity of the 1949 Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Validity of the 1949 Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since the 1949 Act became law, doubts were raised by some legal <a href="/wiki/Academia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academia">academics</a> as to whether the use of the 1911 Act to pass the 1949 Act, which amended the 1911 Act itself, was valid.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AB2R_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AB2R-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FB_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FB-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Three main concerns were raised:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The continued ability of the House of Lords to veto a bill to prolong the life of Parliament would not be entrenched if the 1911 Act could be used to amend itself first, removing this restriction.</li>\n<li>The 1949 Act could be considered to be <a href="/wiki/Secondary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary legislation">secondary legislation</a>, since it depended for its validity on another Act, the 1911 Act; and the principle that courts will respect an Act of Parliament without enquiring into its origins (an emanation of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">parliamentary sovereignty</a>) would not apply.</li>\n<li>Under the 1911 Act, Parliament (that is, the Commons and the Lords acting together) delegated its ability to pass legislation to another body (the Commons alone). Following legal principles established when the United Kingdom granted legislative powers to <a href="/wiki/Colonial_government_in_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial government in America">assemblies in its colonies</a> in the late 18th century, a subordinate legislative body cannot use the Act under which legislative power was delegated to it to expand its competence without an express power to do so in the enabling Act (see <a href="/wiki/Declaratory_Act" title="Declaratory Act">Declaratory Act</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>To address these concerns, a <a href="/wiki/Law_Lord" class="mw-redirect" title="Law Lord">Law Lord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Donaldson_of_Lymington" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Donaldson of Lymington">Lord Donaldson of Lymington</a>, presented a <a href="/wiki/Private_Member%27s_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Private Member&#39;s Bill">Private Member\'s Bill</a> in <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> in the 2000\xe2\x80\x932001 session of Parliament (the Parliament Acts (Amendment) Bill), which would have had the effect of confirming the legitimacy of the 1949 Act, but prohibiting any further such uses of the Parliament Act to amend itself, or use of it to further modify or curtail the powers of the House of Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AB2R_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AB2R-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  Another Parliament Acts (Amendment) Bill was introduced independently by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Renton_of_Mount_Harry" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Renton of Mount Harry">Lord Renton of Mount Harry</a> in the next session,<sup id="cite_ref-AB2R_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AB2R-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  but neither of these Bills proceeded to a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_procedure" title="Parliamentary procedure">Third Reading</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first legal challenge to the 1949 Act is believed to have been made during the first prosecution for <a href="/wiki/War_crimes" class="mw-redirect" title="War crimes">war crimes</a> under the War Crimes Act 1991, <i>R. v. Serafinowicz</i>, but no record of the legal arguments remains.<sup id="cite_ref-RCJ_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCJ-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Because a second defendant was prosecuted under the War Crimes Act, and was sentenced to life imprisonment and since the War Crimes Act was later amended by both two further acts (the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Justice_and_Public_Order_Act_1994" title="Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994">Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Procedure_and_Investigations_Act_1996" title="Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996">Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996</a>), which were passed by both Houses and received royal assent, the validity of the War Crimes Act is not under question.<sup id="cite_ref-RCJ_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCJ-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1949 Act, and the validity of Acts made under it, were not questioned in court again until the Parliament Acts were used to pass the Hunting Act 2004. Early in 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_Alliance" title="Countryside Alliance">Countryside Alliance</a> took a case to court to challenge the validity of the 1949 Act.<sup id="cite_ref-hansard_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hansard-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="High Court of England and Wales">High Court</a>, the wording of the 1911 Act was held not to imply any entrenchment.<sup id="cite_ref-jackson1_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  Support for this conclusion can be drawn from the parliamentary debates on the 1911 Act, in which an <a href="/wiki/Entrenchment_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Entrenchment clause">entrenchment clause</a> was considered but rejected, the Government clearly displaying the intention to be able to make such amendments if necessary. However, the 2005 decision was made on other grounds, so the question of whether the Courts could refer to the 1949 Act\'s Parliamentary debates under the principle established in <i><a href="/wiki/Pepper_v_Hart" class="mw-redirect" title="Pepper v Hart">Pepper v Hart</a></i> was not decided.<sup id="cite_ref-jackson1_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The High Court held that the 1949 Act was <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>, despite being unusual in that the Courts can rule on whether the provisions of the 1911 Act are complied with.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It was held that the 1911 Act clearly permits the procedures specified in the Parliament Acts to be used for "any Public Bill", and this was sufficient to dispose of the argument that the 1911 Act could not be used to amend itself. The Court took the view that the 1911 Act was a \'remodelling\' of the constitution rather than a delegation of power.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a> ruling agreed that the 1949 Act itself was valid, but left open the question of whether the Commons could use the Parliament Act to make significant changes to the constitution (for example, repealing the Parliament Act\'s provision prohibiting the Act from being used to extend the lifespan of Parliament).<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  The Court of Appeal refused to give the Countryside Alliance permission to appeal their decision to the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">House of Lords</a>; however, a petition for permission to appeal was submitted directly to the Law Lords and granted in July 2005. Argument in the case was heard on 13 and 14 July  2005 by a large committee of nine Law Lords, rather than the normal five. In a unanimous decision, the Law Lords upheld the validity of 1949 Act.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_developments">Future developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Future developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After the "first stage" of <a href="/wiki/Reform_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Reform of the House of Lords">reform of the House of Lords</a> was implemented in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Act_1999" title="House of Lords Act 1999">House of Lords Act 1999</a>, the Wakeham Royal Commission on the proposal of a "second stage" of reform reported in January 2000. Subsequently, the government decided to take no action to change the legislative relationship between the House of Commons and the House of Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-pnote_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnote-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2006, it was reported that the then-Labour Government was considering removing the ability of the Lords to delay legislation that arises as a result of manifesto commitments (while the Lords still acted in accordance with a self-imposed restriction, the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Convention" title="Salisbury Convention">Salisbury Convention</a>, which this legislation would have merely formalised), and reducing their ability to delay other legislation to a period of 60 days<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> (although a compromise of 6 months has also been suggested).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Labour Government made no attempt to enact such changes before the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a>, which Labour lost.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In May 2011, <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Deputy Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Deputy Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Nick_Clegg" title="Nick Clegg">Nick Clegg</a> announced the <a href="/wiki/Coalition_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Coalition Government">Coalition Government</a>\'s plans to legislate for a mainly elected House of Lords. In the face of fierce opposition from the overwhelming majority of the Lords, he indicated that he would consider use of the Parliament Act.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately this did not happen.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; is authorised by section 2(2) of the Parliament Act 1949</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-pnote-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-pnote_2-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a
