title="HOTOL">HOTOL</a> (Horizontal Take-Off and Landing) project\n</td></tr>\n\n\n</tbody></table>\n<p><b>Skylon</b> is a series of designs for a <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">single-stage-to-orbit</a> <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplane</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> company <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_Limited" title="Reaction Engines Limited">Reaction Engines Limited</a> (REL), using <a href="/wiki/SABRE_(rocket_engine)" title="SABRE (rocket engine)">SABRE</a>, a combined-cycle, <a href="/wiki/Air_breathing_engines" class="mw-redirect" title="Air breathing engines">air-breathing</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propulsion" class="mw-redirect" title="Rocket propulsion">rocket propulsion</a> system.  The vehicle design is for a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen-fuelled" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen-fuelled">hydrogen-fuelled</a> aircraft that would take off from a purpose-built <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runway</a>, and accelerate to <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a>&#160;5.4 at 26 kilometres (16&#160;mi; 85,000&#160;ft) altitude (compared to typical airliners\' 9\xe2\x80\x9313 kilometres (6\xe2\x80\x938&#160;mi; 30,000\xe2\x80\x9340,000&#160;ft)) using the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#Oxygen" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere\'s oxygen</a> before switching the engines to use the internal <a href="/wiki/LOX" class="mw-redirect" title="LOX">liquid oxygen</a> (LOX) supply to take it into orbit.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup> It could carry 17 tonnes (17,000&#160;kg; 37,000&#160;lb) of cargo to an equatorial <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO); up to 11 tonnes (11,000&#160;kg; 24,000&#160;lb) to the International Space Station, almost 45% more than the capacity of the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle" title="Automated Transfer Vehicle">Automated Transfer Vehicle</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or 7.3 tonnes; 7,300 kilograms (16,000&#160;lb) to <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_transfer_orbit" title="Geostationary transfer orbit">Geosynchronous Transfer Orbit</a> (GTO), over 24% more than <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">SpaceX F9</a> <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle#Return_to_launch_site" title="Launch vehicle">RTLS</a> (As of  2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-SpaceXcapabilities2016_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceXcapabilities2016-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>) The relatively light vehicle would then <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">re-enter</a> the atmosphere and land on a runway, being protected from the conditions of re-entry by a <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_matrix_composite" title="Ceramic matrix composite">ceramic composite</a> skin.  When on the ground, it would undergo inspection and necessary maintenance, with a turnaround time of approximately two days, and be able to complete at least 200 orbital flights per vehicle.\n</p><p>As work on the project has progressed, information has been published on a number of design versions, including A4,<sup id="cite_ref-spaceflight1993_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spaceflight1993-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>Fig 1 p165</span></sup> C1,<sup id="cite_ref-JBIS2004_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JBIS2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> C2,<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and D1.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv21_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv21-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Testing of the key technologies was successfully completed in November 2012, allowing Skylon\'s design to advance from its research phase to a development phase.<sup id="cite_ref-November2012Test_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-November2012Test-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, an engine test facility was being built at <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Propulsion_Establishment" title="Rocket Propulsion Establishment">Westcott</a> and if all goes to plan, the first ground-based engine tests could happen in 2020, and SABRE engines could be performing unmanned test flights by 2025.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_BAe_investment_2015_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_BAe_investment_2015-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In paper studies, the cost per kilogram of <a href="/wiki/Payload" title="Payload">payload</a> carried to LEO in this way is hoped to be reduced from the current \xc2\xa31,108/kg (as of  December&#160;2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>, to around \xc2\xa3650/kg, with costs expected to fall much more over time after initial expenditures have <a href="/wiki/Amortization" title="Amortization">amortised</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sf20110125_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sf20110125-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, the developer estimated the total lifetime cost of the Skylon C1 programme to be about $12&#160;billion.<sup id="cite_ref-sf20110125_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sf20110125-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, only a small portion of the funding required to develop and build Skylon had been secured. For the first couple of decades the work was privately funded, with public funding beginning in 2009 through a <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> (ESA) contract. The British government pledged \xc2\xa360 million to the project on 16 July 2013 to allow a prototype of the SABRE engine to be built,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> contracts for this funding were signed in 2015.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background and early work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Skylon has its origins within a previous space development programme for an envisioned <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">single-stage-to-orbit</a> <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplane</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/HOTOL" title="HOTOL">HOTOL</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sh20100925_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sh20100925-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1982, when work commenced on the HOTOL by several British companies, there was significant international interest to develop and produce viable <a href="/wiki/Reusable_launch_system" title="Reusable launch system">reusable launch systems</a>, perhaps the most high-profile of these being the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>-operated <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>. In conjunction with <a href="/wiki/British_Aerospace" title="British Aerospace">British Aerospace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolls-Royce plc">Rolls-Royce</a>, a promising design emerged to which the British government contributed \xc2\xa32&#160;million towards its refinement; British engineer <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bond_(engineer)" title="Alan Bond (engineer)">Alan Bond</a> was amongst the engineers who worked on HOTOL.<sup id="cite_ref-fi_nxsp_11_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fi_nxsp_11-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, during 1988, the British government decided to withdraw further funding from the programme, resulting in development work being terminated. Aerospace publication <a href="/wiki/Flight_International" title="Flight International">Flight International</a> observed that HOTOL and other competing spaceplane programmes were "over-ambitious" and that development on such launch systems would involve more research and slower progress than previously envisioned.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HOTOL.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/HOTOL.JPG/220px-HOTOL.JPG" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/HOTOL.JPG/330px-HOTOL.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/HOTOL.JPG/440px-HOTOL.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1436" data-file-height="828" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HOTOL.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Skylon was developed from the British <a href="/wiki/HOTOL" title="HOTOL">HOTOL</a> project.</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the setback of HOTOL\'s cancellation, in 1989 Alan Bond, along with John Scott-Scott and Richard Varvill decided to establish their own company, <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_Limited" title="Reaction Engines Limited">Reaction Engines Limited</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-REL_background_2017_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REL_background_2017-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> to pursue the development of a viable spaceplane and associated technology using private funding.<sup id="cite_ref-sh20100925_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sh20100925-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1993, REL publicly revealed its spaceplane proposal,<sup id="cite_ref-spaceflight1993_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spaceflight1993-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> which it named <i>Skylon</i> after the <a href="/wiki/Skylon_(Festival_of_Britain)" title="Skylon (Festival of Britain)">Skylon</a> structure that had inspired Alan Bond at the <a href="/wiki/Festival_of_Britain" title="Festival of Britain">Festival of Britain exhibition</a>. Skylon was a clean sheet redesign based on lessons learned during development of HOTOL, the new concept again utilised dual-mode propulsion system, using engines that could combust hydrogen with the external air during atmospheric flight; but again SABRE was a clean sheet redesign based on lessons learned during development of <a href="/wiki/HOTOL#Engine" title="HOTOL">HOTOL\'s RB545 engine</a><sup id="cite_ref-ast_98_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ast_98-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Early on, Skylon was promoted by the company to the ESA for its <a href="/w/index.php?title=Future_European_Space_Transportation_Investigations_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Future European Space Transportation Investigations Programme (page does not exist)">Future European Space Transportation Investigations Programme</a> (FESTIP) initiative, as well as seeking out both government or commercial investment in order to finance the vehicle\'s development. REL has also sought to form ties with other companies with the aim of producing an international consortium of interested firms to participate in the Skylon programme.<sup id="cite_ref-ast_98_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ast_98-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After securing additional funding in the 1990s, Reaction Engine\'s initial design underwent radical revision.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_brief">Project brief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Project brief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Skylon design features several distinct differences when compared with the earlier HOTOL programme.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> Whereas HOTOL would have launched from a <a href="/wiki/Rocket_sled" title="Rocket sled">rocket sled</a> as a weight-saving measure, Skylon is to be equipped with conventional <a href="/wiki/Retractable_undercarriage" class="mw-redirect" title="Retractable undercarriage">retractable undercarriage</a>. The revised engine design, using the SABRE engine, is expected to offer higher performance than its predecessor.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> The rear mounted engine of HOTOL meant that the vehicle possessed intrinsically poor in-flight stability; early attempts to resolve this problem had ended up sacrificing much of HOTOL\'s payload potential, which in turn contributed to the failure of the overall project. Skylon\'s solution to the issue was to position its engines at the end of its wings, which located them further forward and much closer to the vehicle\'s longitudinal <a href="/wiki/Center_of_gravity_of_an_aircraft" title="Center of gravity of an aircraft">centre of mass</a>, thereby resolving the instability problem.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11</span></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylon_climbing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cc/Skylon_climbing.jpg/220px-Skylon_climbing.jpg" width="220" height="102" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cc/Skylon_climbing.jpg/330px-Skylon_climbing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cc/Skylon_climbing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="186" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylon_climbing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A computer-generated image of the Skylon spaceplane climbing to orbit.</div></div></div>\n<p>REL intends ultimately to operate as a <a href="/wiki/For-profit_corporation" title="For-profit corporation">for-profit commercial enterprise</a> which, upon the completion of development, shall manufacture Skylon vehicles for multiple international customers who shall operate their fleets directly, while being provided with support from REL.<sup id="cite_ref-fi_nxsp_11_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fi_nxsp_11-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Skylon has been designed with the target of achieving no less than 200 flights per vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-wang_futu_NBF_2009_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wang_futu_NBF_2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> According to the company, its business plan is to sell vehicles for $1 billion each, for which it has forecast a market for at least 30 Skylons, while recurring costs of just $10 million per flight are predicted to be incurred by operators.<sup id="cite_ref-fi_nxsp_11_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fi_nxsp_11-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> While the REL intends to manufacture some components directly, such as the engine precooler, other components have been designed by partner companies and a consortium of various aerospace firms is expected to handle full production of Skylon.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In service, Skylon could potentially lower the cost of launching <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> which, according to evidence submitted to the <a href="/wiki/UK_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="UK parliament">UK parliament</a> by REL, is forecast to be around \xc2\xa3650/kg; as of 2011, the average launch cost using conventional methods was estimated to be roughly \xc2\xa315,000/kg.<sup id="cite_ref-uk20110127_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk20110127-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Amongst other prospective operations, Skylon would be capable of transporting payloads of up to 10-tonnes to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fi_nxsp_11_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fi_nxsp_11-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> REL has also completed internal studies into the use of Skylon as a launch platform for a network of <a href="/wiki/Space-based_solar_power" title="Space-based solar power">space-based solar power satellites</a>, which have been historically unfeasible due to high launch costs.<sup id="cite_ref-wang_futu_NBF_2009_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wang_futu_NBF_2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> According to business publication <a href="/wiki/Management_Today" class="mw-redirect" title="Management Today">Management Today</a>, Skylon has been discussed as a possible replacement for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> programme.<sup id="cite_ref-mt_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mt-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>\nSpeaking in June 2011, REL estimated that it would require ultimately $12 billion to achieve an operational configuration, which was then estimated to be achieved around 2020, dependant on funding. The obtaining of additional financing for the Skylon programme from the British government has often been difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHouse_of_Commons:_Science_and_Technology_Committee2007262_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouse_of_Commons:_Science_and_Technology_Committee2007262-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> During 2000, REL issued an ultimately unsuccessful request for funding from the British government; according to the government, REL\'s proposal had involved an offer of a potentially large return on its investment.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> However, several officials have emerged as proponents and advocated for the official backing of the Skylon programme. Speaking in 2009, the former UK Minister for Science and Innovation, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Drayson,_Baron_Drayson" title="Paul Drayson, Baron Drayson">Lord Drayson</a>, stated of REL: </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"This is an example of a British company developing world-beating technology with exciting consequences for the future of space."<sup id="cite_ref-uk20110127_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk20110127-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>During February 2009, following on from a series of extended discussions with the <a href="/wiki/British_National_Space_Centre" title="British National Space Centre">British National Space Centre</a> (which later became the <a href="/wiki/UK_Space_Agency" title="UK Space Agency">UK Space Agency</a>), it was announced that a major funding agreement had been established between the British National Space Centre, ESA and REL, committing <a href="/wiki/%E2%82%AC" class="mw-redirect" title="\xe2\x82\xac">\xe2\x82\xac</a>1&#160;million ($1.28&#160;million) for the purpose of producing a demonstration engine for the Skylon programme by 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-flightglobal2009_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flightglobal2009-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The initiative, known as the <i>Technology Demonstration Programme</i>, was scheduled to last approximately 2.5&#160;years, during which further financing in the form of <a href="/wiki/%E2%82%AC" class="mw-redirect" title="\xe2\x82\xac">\xe2\x82\xac</a>1&#160;million was made available by the ESA.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The 2009 agreement allowed REL to involve several external companies, including <a href="/wiki/EADS" class="mw-redirect" title="EADS">EADS</a>-owned <a href="/wiki/Astrium" title="Astrium">Astrium</a>, the University of Bristol and the <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">German Aerospace Center</a> (DLR), in further development work.<sup id="cite_ref-flightglobal2009_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flightglobal2009-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> As a consequence of the enactment of the Technology Demonstration Programme, REL was able to transition from a Technology Readiness Level (TRL) of 2/3 up to 4/5 within a matter of months.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 2012, according to the UK Space Agency, the funding required to develop and construct the entire craft has not yet been secured; as such, research and development work was at that point mainly focused on the engines alone, which was supported by an ESA grant of <a href="/wiki/%E2%82%AC" class="mw-redirect" title="\xe2\x82\xac">\xe2\x82\xac</a>1&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-uksa_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uksa-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In January 2011, REL submitted a proposal to the British Government requesting additional funding for the Skylon project.<sup id="cite_ref-uk20110127_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk20110127-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> On 13 April 2011, REL announced that the design of the Skylon had passed several rigorous independent reviews. On 24 May 2011, the ESA publicly declared the design to be feasible, having found "no impediments or critical items" to be present in the proposal.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Speaking on the topic of Skylon in 2011, <a href="/wiki/David_Willetts" title="David Willetts">David Willetts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a> <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Business,_Innovation_and_Skills" title="Department for Business, Innovation and Skills">Minister of State for Universities and Science</a>, stated:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The European Space Agency is funding proof of concept work for Skylon from UK contributions. This work is focusing on demonstrating the viability of the advanced British engine technology that would underpin the project. Initial work will be completed in mid 2011 and if the trial is successful, we will work with industry to consider next steps.<sup id="cite_ref-uk20110127_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk20110127-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In June 2013, <a href="/wiki/George_Osborne" title="George Osborne">George Osborne</a>, the-then <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>, stated that the British government would be giving \xc2\xa360 million towards the further development of the SABRE engine.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The grant was contingent upon REL having an industrial partner. The first grant of \xc2\xa350 million was approved by the European Commission in August 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2015, British defence conglomerate <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a> entered into an agreement with Reaction Engines, under which it would invest \xc2\xa320.6 million in REL to acquire 20% of its share capital, as well as to provide assistance in the development of the SABRE engine.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During July 2016, the second grant of \xc2\xa310 million was approved by the ESA.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 25 September 2017 it was announced that the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) had awarded a contract to Reaction Engines for an undisclosed amount to conduct high-temperature airflow testing at a Colorado, United States site of a Reaction Engines precooler called HTX.  Testing work is scheduled to start in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-reactionengines.co.uk_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reactionengines.co.uk-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On April 2018 Reaction Engines announced Boeing and Rolls-Royce would joining BAE Systems as investors in SABRE engine development.  A total of $37.5 million of new funding is to be provided including contributions from Baillie Gifford Asset Management and Woodford Investment Management.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Precooler_Rig.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" data-file-height="236" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Precooler_Rig.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The precooler rig that tested the heat exchange system of the <a href="/wiki/SABRE_(rocket_engine)" title="SABRE (rocket engine)">SABRE</a> engine.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 2000, the firm completed work with <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bristol" title="University of Bristol">University of Bristol</a> testing the precooler<sup id="cite_ref-wang_futu_NBF_2009_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wang_futu_NBF_2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 2007 to 2009 REL worked with University of Bristol and Airborne Engineering on Project STERN (Static Test Expansion/Deflection Rocket Nozzle), which tested REL\'s engine ignition system, a REL designed air breathing hydrogen rocket engine, and investigated the flow stability and behaviour of with Dr Neil Taylor\'s expansion deflection <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine_nozzle" title="Rocket engine nozzle">nozzle</a> design via multiple test-firings by Airborne Engineering. An <a href="/wiki/Expansion_deflection_nozzle" title="Expansion deflection nozzle">expansion deflection nozzle</a> is capable of compensating for the changing ambient pressure encountered while gaining altitude during atmospheric flight, thus generating greater thrust and thereby efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-stern_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stern-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wang_futu_NBF_2009_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wang_futu_NBF_2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Work on STERN was continued in project STRICT (Static Test Rocket Incorporating Cooled Thrust-chamber), which investigated the stability of the engine\'s <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust</a> flow and the dissipation of the generated heat into the engine walls.<sup id="cite_ref-wang_futu_NBF_2009_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wang_futu_NBF_2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The results and designs delivered by the both the STRICT and STERN projects were subsequently declared by REL to have been "a great success".<sup id="cite_ref-s20110125_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-s20110125-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Static testing of the engine precooler began in June 2011, marking the start of Phase 3 in the Skylon development programme,<sup id="cite_ref-uk20110127_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk20110127-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dan_Thisdell_2011_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dan_Thisdell_2011-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-space.com_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space.com-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In April 2012, REL announced that the first series of the precooler test programme had been successfully completed.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_tests_2012_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_tests_2012-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> On 10 July 2012, REL announced that the second of three series of tests has been completed successfully, and the final series of tests would begin the following month after the testing facilities had been upgraded to allow testing of \xe2\x88\x92150&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x92238&#160;\xc2\xb0F) temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-PR_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PR-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> ESA\'s propulsion division <a href="/wiki/Audit" title="Audit">audited</a> the precooler tests during mid-2012 and found the results satisfactory.<sup id="cite_ref-November2012Test_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-November2012Test-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Register2010_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Register2010-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 9 May 2011, REL stated that a preproduction prototype of the Skylon could be flying by 2016, and the proposed route would be a suborbital flight between the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">Guiana Space Centre</a> near <a href="/wiki/Kourou" title="Kourou">Kourou</a> in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_European_Aerospace_Test_range" title="North European Aerospace Test range">North European Aerospace Test Range</a>, located in northern Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Pre-orders are expected in the 2011\xe2\x80\x932013 time frame coinciding with the formation of the manufacturing consortium.<sup id="cite_ref-uk20110127_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk20110127-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> On 8 December 2011, Alan Bond stated that Skylon would enter into service by 2021\xe2\x80\x932022 instead of 2020 as previously envisaged.<sup id="cite_ref-Progress2011_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Progress2011-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> On 13 July 2012, ESA Director-General <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Dordain" title="Jean-Jacques Dordain">Jean-Jacques Dordain</a> publicly stated that the ESA would be holding a series of talks with REL with the aim of developing a further "technical understanding".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2012, REL announced that it would commence work upon a three-and-a-half-year project to develop and build a test rig of the Sabre engine to prove its performance across both the air-breathing and rocket modes.<sup id="cite_ref-November2012Test_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-November2012Test-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_and_design">Technology and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technology and design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">Single-stage-to-orbit</a></div>\n<p>The Skylon is a fully reusable <a href="/wiki/Single_stage_to_orbit" class="mw-redirect" title="Single stage to orbit">single stage to orbit</a> (SSTO) vehicle, able to achieve <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> without <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">staging</a>, which is intended to be used principally as a <a href="/wiki/Reusable_launch_system" title="Reusable launch system">reusable launch system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond2003108_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond2003108-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Proponents of the SSTO approach have often claimed that staging involves a number of inherent complications and problems due to complexity, such as being difficult or typically impossible to recover and reuse most elements, thus unavoidably incurring great expense to produce entirely new launch vehicles instead; therefore, they believe that SSTO designs hold the promise of providing a reduction to the high cost of space flights.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond2003108_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond2003108-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Operationally, it is envisioned for the non-crewed Skylon to take off from a specially strengthened <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runway</a>, gain altitude in a fashion akin to a conventional <a href="/wiki/Aeroplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Aeroplane">aeroplane</a> and perform an ascent at very high speeds, in excess of five times the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> (3,800 miles per hour), to attain a peak altitude of roughly 92,000 feet, on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Low_earth_orbit" class="mw-redirect" title="Low earth orbit">low earth orbit</a> (LEO), where payloads would typically be launched prior to the vehicle\'s <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric reentry">re-entry into the atmosphere</a>, upon which it will conduct a relatively gentle descent before performing a traditional landing upon a runway.<sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylon_colour.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Skylon_colour.svg/350px-Skylon_colour.svg.png" width="350" height="71" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Skylon_colour.svg/525px-Skylon_colour.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Skylon_colour.svg/700px-Skylon_colour.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1144" data-file-height="231" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylon_colour.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Skylon <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplane</a> is designed as a two-engine, "tailless" aircraft, which is fitted with a steerable <a href="/wiki/Canard_(aeronautics)" title="Canard (aeronautics)">canard.</a></div></div></div><p>\nThe design of the Skylon D1 features a large cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Payload" title="Payload">payload bay</a>, 13&#160;m (42&#160;ft 8&#160;in) long and 4.8&#160;m (15&#160;ft 9&#160;in) in diameter.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv21_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv21-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>13</span></sup> It is designed to be comparable with current payload dimensions, and able to support the <a href="/wiki/Containerisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Containerisation">containerisation</a> of payloads that Reaction Engines envisions being produced in the future. To an <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_orbit" class="mw-redirect" title="Equatorial orbit">equatorial orbit</a>, Skylon could deliver 15&#160;t (33,000&#160;lb) to a 300&#160;km (190&#160;mi) altitude or 11&#160;t (24,000&#160;lb) to a 600&#160;km (370&#160;mi) altitude.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv21_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv21-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>9</span></sup> Using interchangeable payload containers, Skylon could be fitted to carry satellites or fluid cargo into orbit, or, in a specialised habitation module, the latter being capable of housing a maximum of 30 <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> during a single launch.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-passenger_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-passenger-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Richard Varvill, technical director at REL, stated of REL\'s market: </p><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"...we\xe2\x80\x99re competing with expendable rockets, a machine that is only used once".<sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Because the SABRE engine uses the atmosphere as <a href="/wiki/Reaction_mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Reaction mass">reaction mass</a> at low altitude, it will have a high <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> (around 4,100\xe2\x80\x939,200 seconds for SABRE 4,<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv21_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv21-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup> or 3,600 seconds for SABRE 3<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>), and burn about one fifth of the propellant that would have been required by a conventional rocket.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, Skylon would be able to take off with much less total propellant than conventional systems.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The weight reduction enabled by the lower quantity of propellant needed meant that the vehicle would not require as much <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> or <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a>, which in turn permits the use of smaller engines and allows for the use of a <a href="/wiki/Aerofoil" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerofoil">conventional wing</a> configuration.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> While flying within the atmosphere, the use of wings to counteract <a href="/wiki/Gravity_drag" title="Gravity drag">gravity drag</a> is more fuel-efficient than simply expelling propellant (as in a rocket), which again serves to reduce the total amount of propellant needed.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Payload_fraction" title="Payload fraction">payload fraction</a> would be significantly greater than normal rockets and the vehicle should be fully reusable, capable of performing in excess of 200 launches.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond200422_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond200422-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SABRE_engines">SABRE engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: SABRE engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/SABRE_(rocket_engine)" title="SABRE (rocket engine)">SABRE (rocket engine)</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane,_1990s._(9660572897).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane%2C_1990s._%289660572897%29.jpg/220px-SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane%2C_1990s._%289660572897%29.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane%2C_1990s._%289660572897%29.jpg/330px-SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane%2C_1990s._%289660572897%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane%2C_1990s._%289660572897%29.jpg/440px-SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane%2C_1990s._%289660572897%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1250" data-file-height="997" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SABRE_engine_designed_for_Skylon_spaceplane,_1990s._(9660572897).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cross section of a model of an early SABRE engine design</div></div></div>\n<p>One of the most significant features of the Skylon\'s design is its powerplant, known as <i>Synergetic Air-Breathing Rocket Engine</i> (SABRE).<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The design of the SABRE engine has drawn heavily upon the STRICT/STERN experimental engines, sharing many features such as the propellant and the adoption of the trialled Expansion Deflection Nozzle, as well as building upon the wider field of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air_cycle_engine" title="Liquid air cycle engine">liquid air cycle engines</a> (LACE).<sup id="cite_ref-wang_futu_NBF_2009_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wang_futu_NBF_2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> The engines are designed to operate much like a conventional <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a> to around <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a>&#160;5.5 (1,700&#160;<a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> 26 kilometres (16&#160;mi) altitude, beyond which the air inlet closes and the engine operates as a highly efficient <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> to <a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">orbital speed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The proposed SABRE engine is not a <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a>, but a jet engine running <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycles</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Precooled_jet_engine" title="Precooled jet engine">precooled jet engine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sf20110125_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sf20110125-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Originally the key technology for this type of precooled jet engine did not exist, as it required a heat exchanger that was ten times lighter than the state of the art.<sup id="cite_ref-s20110125_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-s20110125-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Research conducted since then has achieved the necessary performance.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Operating an air-breathing jet engine at velocities of up to Mach&#160;5.5 poses numerous engineering problems; several previous engines proposed by other designers have worked well as jet engines, but performed poorly as rockets.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> This engine design aims to be a good jet engine within the atmosphere, as well as being an excellent rocket engine outside; however, the conventional problem posed by operating at Mach&#160;5.5 has been that the air coming into the engine rapidly heats up as it is compressed into the engine; due to certain thermodynamic effects, this greatly reduces the thrust that can be produced by burning fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Attempts to avoid these issues has typically resulted in the engine being much heavier (<a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjets</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjets</a>) or has greatly reduced the thrust generated (conventional turbojets/ramjets); in either of these scenarios, the end result would be an engine that possesses a poor <a href="/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio" title="Thrust-to-weight ratio">thrust to weight ratio</a> at high speeds, which in turn would be too heavy to assist much in reaching orbit.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The SABRE engine design aims to avoid the historic weight-performance issue by using some of the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> fuel to cool <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Precooled_jet_engine" title="Precooled jet engine">closed-cycle precooler</a>, which quickly reduces the temperature of the air at the inlet.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The air is then used for combustion in a similar manner to a conventional jet engine; once the helium has left the pre-cooler, it is further heated by the products of the pre-burner, giving it enough energy to drive the turbine and the liquid hydrogen pump.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> As a consequence of the air being cooled at all speeds, the jet can be built of light <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> and the weight is roughly halved.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, more fuel can be burnt at high speeds. Beyond Mach&#160;5.5, the air would normally become unusably hot despite the cooling; accordingly, the air inlet is closed upon attaining this speed and the engine instead is solely fed via on-board <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> and hydrogen fuel, as in a traditional rocket.<sup id="cite_ref-sab20110125_55-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sab20110125-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2017 it was announced United States Defense Advanced Research projects (DARPA) had contracted with Reaction Engines Inc. to build a high-temperature airflow test facility at Front Range Airport near Watkins, Colorado. The DARPA contract is to test the Sabre engine\xe2\x80\x99s pre-cooler heat exchanger or HTX.\n</p><p>The test work which is to be started in 2018 will focus on running the HTX at temperatures simulating Mach 5 or around 980&#160;\xc2\xb0C (1,800&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-reactionengines.co.uk_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reactionengines.co.uk-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuselage_and_structure">Fuselage and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fuselage and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png/220px-Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png" width="220" height="71" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png/330px-Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png/440px-Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png 2x" data-file-width="1714" data-file-height="556" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylon-r%C3%A9servoirs.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of the vehicle\'s internal sections, highlighting the areas allocated to storing hydrogen, oxygen and the payload</div></div></div>\n<p>The currently proposed Skylon model D1 is a large vehicle, possessing a length of 83.13 metres (272&#160;ft 9&#160;in) and a diameter of 6.30 metres (20&#160;ft 8&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> of the Skylon is expected to be a silicon carbide reinforced titanium <a href="/wiki/Space_frame" title="Space frame">space frame</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Progress2015_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Progress2015-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>8</span></sup> a light and strong structure that supports the weight of the aluminium <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">fuel tanks</a> and to which the ceramic <a href="/wiki/Skin_(aeronautics)" title="Skin (aeronautics)">skin</a> is attached.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11</span></sup>  Multiple layers of titanium foil <a href="/wiki/Multi-layer_insulation" title="Multi-layer insulation">thermal insulation</a> are sandwiched between the skin and frame<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> to protect the inside of Skylon from the heat of hypersonic flight and the intense heat of re-entry.\n</p><p>Due to the vehicle\'s use of a low-density fuel in the form of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>, a great volume is required to contain enough energy to reach orbit. The propellant is intended to be kept at low pressure to minimise stress; a vehicle that is both large and light has an advantage during <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric reentry">atmospheric reentry</a> compared to other vehicles due to a low <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_coefficient" title="Ballistic coefficient">ballistic coefficient</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>7</span></sup> Because of the low ballistic coefficient, Skylon would be slowed at higher altitudes where the air is thinner; as a consequence, the skin of the vehicle would reach only 1,100&#160;K (830&#160;\xc2\xb0C).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond200425_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarvillBond200425-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, the smaller <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> was heated to 2,000&#160;K on its <a href="/wiki/Leading_edge" title="Leading edge">leading edge</a>, and so employed an extremely heat-resistant but fragile silica <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_thermal_protection_system" title="Space Shuttle thermal protection system">thermal protection system</a>. The Skylon design does not require such an approach, instead opting for using a far thinner yet durable reinforced ceramic skin;<sup id="cite_ref-sf20110125_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sf20110125-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> however, due to <a href="/wiki/Turbulent_flow" class="mw-redirect" title="Turbulent flow">turbulent flow</a> around the wings during re-entry, some sections of the vehicle shall need to be provided with active cooling systems.<sup id="cite_ref-verge_2016_33-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_2016-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup>\n</p><p>The Skylon shall possess a retractable <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">undercarriage</a>, equipped with high pressure tyres and water-cooled brakes; if any difficulties were to occur just before a take-off, the brakes would be applied to stop the vehicle, the water boiling away to dissipate the heat.<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>21</span></sup> During a normal landing, the empty vehicle would be far lighter, and hence the water would not be required,<sup id="cite_ref-SUMv11_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUMv11-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>21</span></sup> so upon a successful take-off, the 1,410 kilograms (3,110&#160;lb) of water<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> would be <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/throw" class="extiw" title="wikt:throw">jettisoned</a>. When this feature was introduced in the C1 model of the design the weight of the brakes was reduced from around 3,000 to 415 kilograms (6,600 to 915&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-JBIS2004_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JBIS2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Support_facilities">Support facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skylon_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Support facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A special runway will be required for launch: it needs to be reinforced to tolerate the high equivalent single wheel load;<sup id="cite_ref-RE_2014_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RE_2014-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> necessitated by the Skylon\'s 325 tonnes takeoff weight; it will need to have heat resistant sections<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> at the start of the take-off run and at the <a href="/wiki/Rotation_(aeronautics)" title="Rotation (aeronautics)">rotation</a> zone;<sup id="cite_ref-SELSOtechnical2014_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SELSOtechnical2014-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> and it will have to be 5.9 kilometres (3.7&#160;mi) long<sup id="cite_ref-SELSOtechnical2014_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SELSOtechnical2014-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> to allow the Skylon to accelerate to its 155 metres per second (300&#160;kn) rotation speed,<sup id="cite_ref-SELSOtechnical2014_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SELSOtechnical2014-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup> yet still have 1,500 metres (4,900&#160;ft) to abort the launch and brake to a standstill if required. The Skylon would be able to land on a 3.2 kilometres (2.0&#160;mi) Code F civil runway.<sup id="cite_ref-SELSOtechnical2014_66-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SELSOtechnical2014-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup>\n</p><p>When on the ground, it would undergo inspection and
