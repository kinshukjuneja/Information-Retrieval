title="London">London</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Crown Court of England and Wales</b> is, together with the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="High Court of Justice of England and Wales">High Court of Justice</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a>, one of the constituent parts of the <a href="/wiki/Senior_Courts_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior Courts of England and Wales">Senior Courts of England and Wales</a>. It is the highest <a href="/wiki/Court_of_first_instance" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of first instance">court of first instance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal cases</a>; however, for some purposes the Crown Court is hierarchically subordinate to the High Court and its <a href="/wiki/Divisional_court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Divisional court (England and Wales)">Divisional Courts</a>.\n</p><p>The Crown Court sits in around 92 locations in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>. The administration of the Crown Court is conducted through <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Courts_and_Tribunals_Service" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Courts and Tribunals Service">HM Courts and Tribunals Service</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutHMCTS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutHMCTS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Previously conducted across six circuits (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Midland_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Midland Circuit (page does not exist)">Midland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Circuit" title="Northern Circuit">Northern</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Eastern_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="North Eastern Circuit (page does not exist)">North Eastern</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Eastern_Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="South Eastern Circuit">South Eastern</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_and_Chester_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wales and Chester Circuit (page does not exist)">Wales &amp; Chester</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Western Circuit (page does not exist)">Western</a>), HM Courts and Tribunals Service is now divided into seven regions: Midlands, North East, North West, South East, South West, London and Wales. The Wales region was identified separately, having regard to the devolved <a href="/wiki/Legislative" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative">legislative</a> powers of the Welsh Government.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> When the Crown Court sits in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> it is known as the <b>Central Criminal Court</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Central_Criminal_Court_Act_1856" title="Central Criminal Court Act 1856">"Central Criminal Court"</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">Old Bailey</a>, originally established by its own <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a>, is a Crown Court centre, and is the venue at which many of the most serious criminal cases are heard.\n</p><p>The Crown Court carries out four principal types of activity: appeals from decisions of magistrates; sentencing of defendants committed from magistrates\xe2\x80\x99 courts, jury trials, and the sentencing of those who are convicted in the Crown Court, either after trial or on pleading guilty. The average  time  from  receipt  by  the  Crown  Court  to completion was 177 days by the start of 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-Court_Stats_Q1_2016_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Court_Stats_Q1_2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Rather than speaking of a location at which <i>the</i> Crown Court sits, it is common practice to refer to any venue as <i>a</i> Crown Court, e.g., Teesside Crown Court.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div magistrates\' courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Appeals from magistrates&#039; courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Challenges_to_decisions_of_England_and_Wales_magistrates%27_courts" title="Challenges to decisions of England and Wales magistrates&#39; courts">Challenges to decisions of England and Wales magistrates\' courts</a></div>\n<p>In 2015 the Crown Court heard 11,348 appeals against conviction, sentence or both, from those convicted in the <a href="/wiki/Magistrates%27_Court_(England_and_Wales)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magistrates&#39; Court (England and Wales)">magistrates\' courts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Court_Stats_Q1_2016_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Court_Stats_Q1_2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> At the conclusion of the hearing the Crown Court has the power to confirm, reverse or vary any part of the decision under appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> If the appeal is decided against the accused, the Crown Court has the power to impose any sentence which the magistrates could have imposed, including one which is harsher than the one originally imposed. The average waiting time for appeals was 8.8 weeks in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-Court_Stats_Q1_2016_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Court_Stats_Q1_2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Defendants_committed_from_magistrates.27_for_sentencing"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Defendants_committed_from_magistrates\'_for_sentencing">Defendants committed from magistrates\' for sentencing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Defendants committed from magistrates&#039; for sentencing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2015, the Crown Court dealt with 30,802 cases for sentencing from the magistrates\' courts.<sup id="cite_ref-Court_Stats_Q1_2016_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Court_Stats_Q1_2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As the magistrates\' courts only have the power to impose a six-month custodial sentence or a \xc2\xa35,000 fine, the court has the power to commit defendants to the Crown Court for sentencing \xe2\x80\x94 this can be done when they are of the opinion that either the offence, or the combination of the offence and one or more offences associated with it, was so serious that greater punishment should be inflicted than the magistrates\' court has power to impose, or, in the case of a violent or sexual offence, that a custodial sentence longer than the court has power to impose is necessary to protect the public from serious harm.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Committals may also arise from breaches of the terms of a <i>Community Rehabilitation Order</i> or a suspended sentence of imprisonment. The court performance target is that cases committed for sentence should be heard within 10 weeks.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appeals_from_the_Crown_Court">Appeals from the Crown Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appeals from the Crown Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Appeals_from_the_Crown_Court" title="Appeals from the Crown Court">Appeals from the Crown Court</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_England_and_Wales" title="Courts of England and Wales">Courts of England and Wales</a></div>\n<p>When the Crown Court is dealing with a matter connected with a trial on <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictment</a> (i.e., a jury trial), appeal lies to the criminal division of the Court of Appeal and thence to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In all other cases, appeal from the Crown Court lies by way of <a href="/wiki/Case_stated" title="Case stated">case stated</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Divisional_court_(England_and_Wales)" title="Divisional court (England and Wales)">Divisional Court</a> of the High Court.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judges">Judges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Judges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The judges who normally sit in the Crown Court are <a href="/wiki/High_Court_judge_(England_and_Wales)" title="High Court judge (England and Wales)">High Court judges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circuit_judge_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Circuit judge (UK)">Circuit judges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(judge)" title="Recorder (judge)">Recorders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Circuit judges also sit in the <a href="/wiki/County_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="County Court">County Court</a>. Recorders are <a href="/wiki/Barristers" class="mw-redirect" title="Barristers">barristers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solicitors" class="mw-redirect" title="Solicitors">solicitors</a> in private practice, who sit part-time as judges. The most serious cases (treason, murder, rape etc.) are allocated to High Court judges and Senior Circuit judges. The remainder are dealt with by Circuit judges and Recorders, although Recorders will normally handle less serious work than Circuit judges. The allocation is conducted according to directions given by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Criminal_Justice_Administration_Act_1956&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Criminal Justice Administration Act 1956 (page does not exist)">Criminal Justice Administration Act 1956</a> set up two additional courts of <a href="/wiki/Assizes" title="Assizes">assize</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quarter_session" title="Quarter session">quarter sessions</a>, the Crown Court at Liverpool and the Crown Court at Manchester,<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> to improve the handling of criminal cases in South Lancashire.<sup id="cite_ref-NatArch_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatArch-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  A <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Commission">Royal Commission</a> (Cmnd 4135), headed by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Beeching" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Beeching">Lord Beeching</a>, was established to review the English criminal justice system, and recommended the replacement of the assizes and quarter sessions with a new system of courts, following the examples of Liverpool and Manchester.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Crown Court was established on 1 January 1972 by the <a href="/wiki/Courts_Act_1971" title="Courts Act 1971">Courts Act 1971</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>, acting on the recommendations of the commission.<sup id="cite_ref-NatArch_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatArch-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Crown Court is a permanent unitary court across England and Wales, whereas the assizes were periodic local courts heard before judges of the Queen\'s Bench Division of the High Court, who travelled across the seven circuits into which England and Wales were divided, assembling juries in the assize towns and hearing cases. The quarter sessions were local courts assembled four times a year to dispose of criminal cases which were not serious enough to go before a High Court judge.\n</p><p>The Crown Court and a <a href="/wiki/County_court" title="County court">county court</a> may sit in the same building and use the same Judges. Since the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Courts_Service" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Courts Service">Her Majesty\'s Courts Service</a> in April 2005, there is an increased sharing of facilities between the Crown Court, county courts and magistrates\' courts.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_layout">Physical layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Physical layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crown_Court.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Crown_Court.jpg/220px-Crown_Court.jpg" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Crown_Court.jpg/330px-Crown_Court.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Crown_Court.jpg/440px-Crown_Court.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1080" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crown_Court.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Simplified Layout of a typical Crown Court</div></div></div>\n<p>At the front of the court, on a raised platform, is a large bench. This is where the judge sits.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His/her rank can be distinguished by the colour of gown worn, and different forms of address are appropriate for different <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_of_England_and_Wales" title="Judiciary of England and Wales">ranks of judge</a>, with "Your Honour" used for circuit judges and recorders at most locations, and "My Lord" or "My Lady" being used for High Court Judges, as well as for all judges at the Central Criminal Court. The judge enters and exits the court from a door typically at the side of the platform, preceded by a cry of "All Rise" from the usher or clerk of the court who sits below and in front of the judge\'s bench. Everyone present is required to show their subjection to the court by standing as the judge enters (or exits) until he/she sits down.\n</p><p>Note that there is no national flag in a Crown Court, nor does the judge have a gavel.  There is however a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Coat of Arms of the United Kingdom">Royal Coat of Arms</a> on the wall above the judge\'s bench.\n</p><p>The clerk of the court, who sits facing the court (that is, the same way as the judge) has a dedicated desk with computer and telephone, used when communication is necessary with other parts of the court building (for example the jury assembly area or the cell complex).\n</p><p>Also in the area just in front of the judge\'s bench is a stenographer who records proceedings on a <a href="/wiki/Stenotype" title="Stenotype">stenograph</a>, by typing keys as the witnesses speak, using special shorthand. Alternatively, if there is no stenographer, a tapelogger or shorthand writer will be there to operate the tapes and ensure that a log of the proceedings is kept.\n</p><p>Another member of court staff is the usher. If papers or other objects need to be passed around the court, for example notes from members of the jury, or evidence being shown to the jury, normally the usher will do this and will be the only person in the court to walk around while the court is in session.\n</p><p>Behind the usher, wearing black gowns and white wigs and facing the judge, will be the prosecuting and defending <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barristers</a>. The defending barrister will usually be nearer the jury. The barristers may well have laptop computers in addition to files of papers relating to the case which will be on the desk in front of them. Unlike the judge, who speaks sitting down, the barristers always stand to address the court.\n</p><p>Behind the barristers will sit the instructing solicitors, who in the case of the prosecution will usually be a representative of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prosecution_Service" title="Crown Prosecution Service">Crown Prosecution Service</a>.\n</p><p>At the back of the courtroom, behind the barristers, is a semi-partitioned area known as the "dock". This is where the defendant or defendants are placed. A custody officer will be sitting with them in the dock.\n</p><p>Also at the back of the court, often adjacent to the dock, is an area from where members of the public can observe the proceedings. In some courts, notably the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">Old Bailey</a>, this area is positioned above the defendant.\n</p><p>Members of the press may sit in the press bench, which is usually positioned alongside the prosecuting barrister. Etiquette usually requires reporters to identify themselves to the usher before taking position here and starting to write.\n</p><p>Alongside the defending barrister is the jury box. This is where the jury watch the case from. They will be called to it from the jury waiting area (benches next to it) to be sworn in. Once sworn they always sit in the same seat throughout the trial. If proceedings (such as legal argument about the admissibility of evidence) take place which they are not supposed to see occur, the usher will escort them into a room just outside the courtroom (probably behind the dock). Only jurors and ushers ever enter this room.\n</p><p>Opposite the jury box is the <a href="/wiki/Witness_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Witness box">witness box</a>. Witnesses stand facing the jury and give their evidence so the jury can watch their demeanor while giving it, which might help them decide if the witness is being truthful.\n</p><p>When the judge sends the jury to consider their <a href="/wiki/Verdict" title="Verdict">verdict</a>, the usher escorts them to a small suite consisting of a large table, 12 chairs, lavatory facilities, paper and pencils, a button with which to call the usher, and prominent notices about not revealing deliberations to anyone else. The usher withdraws, and when the jury have arrived at a verdict, they push the button.\n</p><p>During deliberations, only limited contact is permitted with the outside world, always via the usher. The jury will be permitted only (a) to call for refreshments, (b) to pass a note to the judge, perhaps asking for further guidance, or (c) to announce that they have reached a verdict. The judge may decide to recall them to the court to address them again at any time.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circuits">Circuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
