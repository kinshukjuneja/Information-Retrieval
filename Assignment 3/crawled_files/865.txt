class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anselm of Canterbury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm of Canterbury</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anselm_of_Canterbury.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Anselm_of_Canterbury.jpg/200px-Anselm_of_Canterbury.jpg" width="200" height="221" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="391" data-file-height="432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anselm_of_Canterbury.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A colourised 16th-century portrait of Anselm, Archbishop of Canterbury</div></div></div>\n<p>Saint Anselm of Canterbury (c.\xe2\x80\x891033 \xe2\x80\x93 1109) was an important philosopher and theologian of the Catholic Church, who held the office of <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a> from 1093 to 1109. Anselm is famed as the originator of the ontological argument for the existence of God and of the satisfaction theory of atonement. Anselm\'s works are considered philosophical as well as theological since they endeavour to render Christian tenets of faith, traditionally taken as a revealed truth, as a rational system.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies_&amp;_al.2004&#91;httpsbooksgooglecombooksidZ8zGpbpwRhICpgPA2_2&#93;_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies_&amp;_al.2004[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidZ8zGpbpwRhICpgPA2_2]-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roger_Bacon">Roger Bacon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roger Bacon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a></div>\n<p>Roger Bacon (c. 1214\xe2\x80\x931294), also known as Doctor Mirabilis (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: "Wondrous Doctor"), was an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> friar who placed considerable emphasis on empirical methods. He is sometimes credited as one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> advocates of the modern <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> inspired by the works of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> via early <a href="/wiki/Islamic_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic science">Islamic scientists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duns_Scotus">Duns Scotus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Duns Scotus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Duns_Scotus" title="Duns Scotus">Duns Scotus</a></div>\n<p>John Duns Scotus (c. 1265 \xe2\x80\x93 8 November 1308) was an important philosopher and theologian of the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>. Scotus was born around 1265,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Duns" title="Duns">Duns</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Berwickshire" title="Berwickshire">Berwickshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. In 1291 he was ordained as a priest in <a href="/wiki/Northampton,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Northampton, England">Northampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. A note in Codex 66 of <a href="/wiki/Merton_College,_Oxford" title="Merton College, Oxford">Merton College, Oxford</a>, records that Scotus "flourished at <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge, England">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxford,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford, England">Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>". He died in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> in 1308. He is buried in the "Minoritenkirche", the Church of the <a href="/wiki/Franciscans" title="Franciscans">Franciscans</a> (or Minor Friars) in Cologne. He was <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">beatified</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> on 20 March 1993.\n</p><p>Nicknamed <i>Doctor Subtilis</i> (<i>the subtle doctor</i>), he is well known for the "univocity of being," the formal distinction, and the idea of <a href="/wiki/Haecceity" title="Haecceity">haecceity</a>.  The univocity of being holds that existence is the most abstract concept we have and is applicable to everything that exists. The formal distinction is a way of distinguishing between different aspects of the same thing such that the distinction is intermediate between what is merely conceptual, and what is fully real or mind-independent. Haecceity (from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>haecceitas</i>) is the idea of "thisness," a concept which denotes the discrete qualities, properties or characteristics of a thing which make it a particular thing.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_of_Ockham">William of Ockham</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: William of Ockham">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/William_of_Ockham" title="William of Ockham">William of Ockham</a></div>\n<p>William of Ockham (c. 1288 \xe2\x80\x93 c. 1348) was an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">friar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">scholastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>. He is perhaps most well known for his principle of parsimony, famously known as <a href="/wiki/Occam%27s_razor" title="Occam&#39;s razor">Occam\'s razor</a>.  This actual term is claimed not to appear in his writings,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> but rather summarizes the principle he expressed in passages such as <i><a href="https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/William_of_Occam" class="extiw" title="q:William of Occam">Numquam ponenda est pluralitas sine necessitate</a> [Plurality must never be posited without necessity]</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Frustra fit per plura quod potest fieri per pauciora (It is futile to do with more things that which can be done with fewer)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Generally it refers to distinguishing between two hypotheses either by "shaving away" unnecessary assumptions or cutting apart two similar conclusions.\n</p><p>The words often attributed to Occam: <i>entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem ("entities must not be multiplied beyond necessity")</i> are absent in his extant works;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  This particular phrasing comes from <a href="/wiki/John_Punch_(theologian)" title="John Punch (theologian)">John Punch</a> who used it in describing a "common axiom" (<i>axioma vulgare</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">Scholastics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Francis Bacon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg/200px-Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg" width="200" height="246" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg/300px-Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg/400px-Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1629" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Somer_Francis_Bacon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Francis Bacon</div></div></div>\n<p>Francis Bacon (1561\xe2\x80\x931626) was an Englishman who was a <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">statesman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">jurist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a> in addition to being a philosopher. He famously died of pneumonia contracted while studying the effects of freezing on the preservation of meat.  He served both as <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_for_England_and_Wales" title="Attorney General for England and Wales">Attorney General</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> of England. Although his political career ended in disgrace, he remained extremely influential through his works, especially as philosophical advocate and practitioner of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> and pioneer in the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific revolution">scientific revolution</a>.\n</p><p>Bacon has been called the father of empiricism.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> His works established and popularized deductive methodologies for <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> inquiry, often called the <i><a href="/wiki/Baconian_method" title="Baconian method">Baconian method</a></i> or simply, the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>. His demand for a planned procedure of investigating all things natural marked a new turn in the rhetorical and theoretical framework for science, much of which still surrounds conceptions of proper <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodology</a> today. His dedication probably led to his death, so bringing him into a rare historical group of scientists who were killed by their own experiments.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thomas_Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Thomas Hobbes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a></div>\n<p>Thomas Hobbes (1588\xe2\x80\x931679) was an English philosopher, remembered today for his work on <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>. His 1651 book <i><a href="/wiki/Leviathan_(Hobbes_book)" title="Leviathan (Hobbes book)">Leviathan</a></i> established the foundation for most of Western political philosophy from the perspective of <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a> theory.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hobbes was a  champion of absolutism for the sovereign but he also developed some of the fundamentals  of European liberal thought: the right of the individual; the natural equality of all men; the artificial character of the political order (which led to the later distinction between <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil society</a> and the state); the view that all legitimate political power must be "representative" and based on the consent of the people; and a liberal interpretation of  law which  leaves people free to do whatever the law does not explicitly forbid.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hobbes also contributed to a diverse array of fields, including <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, physics of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>, general philosophy, and <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>. His account of human nature as self-interested cooperation has proved to be an enduring theory in the field of <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_anthropology" title="Philosophical anthropology">philosophical anthropology</a>. He was one of the key founders of philosophical <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialism</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_classic_trio_of_British_empiricists">The classic trio of British empiricists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The classic trio of British empiricists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The three \'classic\' British <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricists</a> in the early modern era were John Locke, George Berkeley, and David Hume. The term "British empiricism" refers to the philosophical tradition in Britain that was epitomised by these thinkers (though this tradition did have precursors in Britain stretching back to <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a>). Berkeley, despite being Irish, was referred to as British as <a href="/wiki/County_Kilkenny" title="County Kilkenny">County Kilkenny</a>, where he lived in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, was a part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a> at the time.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Locke">John Locke</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: John Locke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Locke-John-LOC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Locke-John-LOC.jpg/200px-Locke-John-LOC.jpg" width="200" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Locke-John-LOC.jpg/300px-Locke-John-LOC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Locke-John-LOC.jpg/400px-Locke-John-LOC.jpg 2x" data-file-width="875" data-file-height="1140" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Locke-John-LOC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Locke</div></div></div>\n<p>John Locke (1632\xe2\x80\x931704) was an empiricist at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">Modern period</a> of philosophy.  As such (and in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="Ren\xc3\xa9 Descartes">Ren\xc3\xa9 Descartes</a>), he held that all of the objects of the understanding are ideas, where ideas exist in the mind.  One of his goals in his work <i><a href="/wiki/An_Essay_Concerning_Human_Understanding" title="An Essay Concerning Human Understanding">An Essay Concerning Human Understanding</a></i> is to trace the origin of ideas.  There are no innate ideas \xe2\x80\x9cstamped upon the mind\xe2\x80\x9d from birth, and all knowledge is rooted in experience.  Further, there are also <i>simple ideas</i> and <i>complex ideas</i>.  Simple ideas enter by the senses, and they are simple and unmixed.  Complex ideas are simple ideas that have been combined and related together using the abstracting activity of the mind.\n</p><p>Locke is also responsible for an early theory of <a href="/wiki/Personal_identity" title="Personal identity">personal identity</a>. He thought that our being the same person from one time to another consists, not in our having the same soul or the same body, but rather the same series of psychological connections. For Locke, to be a person is to be an intelligent thinking being that can know itself as itself, the same thinking thing in different times and places.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="George_Berkeley">George Berkeley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: George Berkeley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/George_Berkeley" title="George Berkeley">George Berkeley</a></div>\n<p>George Berkeley (1685\xe2\x80\x931753) was an <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> philosopher who served as <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Cloyne" title="Bishop of Cloyne">Bishop of Cloyne</a> from 1734 until his death.  He was a British <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricist</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> an <a href="/wiki/Immaterialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Immaterialism">immaterialist</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealist</a>. Many of his most important ideas were first put forth in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_Concerning_the_Principles_of_Human_Knowledge" title="A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge">A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge</a></i>, a work which was critical of John Locke\'s philosophy.  Berkeley agreed with Locke that there was an outside world which caused the ideas within the mind, but Berkeley sought to prove that the outside world was also composed solely of ideas. Berkeley thought that the ideas that we possessed could only resemble other ideas (not physical objects) and thus the external world consisted not of physical form, but rather of ideas. This world was given logic and regularity by some other force, which Berkeley concluded was God.\n</p><p>Berkeley is famous for his motto <i>"esse est percipi aut percipere"</i>, or otherwise, <i>"to exist is to be perceived, or to perceive"</i>.  This means that there are no things other than ideas and the minds that house them.  There is no such thing as a mind-independent entity.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="David_Hume">David Hume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: David Hume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a></div>\n<p>David Hume (1711\xe2\x80\x931776) was a Scottish <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a>.  His major works, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_of_Human_Nature" title="A Treatise of Human Nature">A Treatise of Human Nature</a></i> (1739\xe2\x80\x931740), the <i><a href="/wiki/An_Enquiry_concerning_Human_Understanding" class="mw-redirect" title="An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding">An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding</a></i> (1748), <i><a href="/wiki/An_Enquiry_Concerning_the_Principles_of_Morals" title="An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals">An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals</a></i> (1751), and <i><a href="/wiki/Dialogues_Concerning_Natural_Religion" title="Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion">Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion</a></i> (1779) remain widely influential.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> His ideas regarding free will and determinism, causation, personal identity, induction, and morality still inspire discussion.\n</p><p>Hume famously described the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_induction" title="Problem of induction">problem of induction</a>. He argues that <a href="/wiki/Inductive_reasoning" title="Inductive reasoning">inductive reasoning</a> cannot be rationally employed, since, in order to justify induction, one would either have to provide a sound deductive argument or an inductively strong argument.  But there is no sound deductive argument for induction, and to ask for an inductive argument to justify induction would be to <a href="/wiki/Begging_the_question" title="Begging the question">beg the question</a>.\n</p><p>Hume\'s problem of causation is related to his problem of induction. He held that there is no empirical access to the supposed necessary connection between cause and effect. In seeking to justify the belief that A causes B, one would point out that, in the past, B has always closely followed A in both space and time. But the special necessary connection that is supposed to be causation is never given to us in experience.  We only observe a constant conjunction of events, with no necessity whatsoever.\n</p><p>In personal identity, Hume was a <a href="/wiki/Bundle_theory" title="Bundle theory">bundle theorist</a>.  He said that there is no robust self to which properties adhere.  Experience only shows us that there is only a bundle of perceptions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adam_Smith">Adam Smith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Adam Smith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a></div>\n<p>Adam Smith (1723\xe2\x80\x931790) was a <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">moral philosopher</a> and a pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economics</a>. Smith wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Theory_of_Moral_Sentiments" title="The Theory of Moral Sentiments">The Theory of Moral Sentiments</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations</a></i>. The latter, usually abbreviated as <i>The Wealth of Nations</i>, is considered his <i><a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a></i> and the first modern work of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. Smith is widely cited as the father of modern economics.\n</p><p>Smith studied <a href="/wiki/Moral_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Moral philosophy">moral philosophy</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>. After graduating, he delivered a successful series of public lectures at <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, leading him to collaborate with <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a> during the Scottish Enlightenment. Smith obtained a professorship at <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> teaching moral philosophy, and during this time he wrote and published <i>The Theory of Moral Sentiments</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jeremy_Bentham">Jeremy Bentham</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Jeremy Bentham">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Bentham" title="Jeremy Bentham">Jeremy Bentham</a></div>\n<p>Jeremy Bentham (1748\xe2\x80\x931832) is well known for beginning the tradition of classical utilitarianism in Britain.  <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">Utilitarianism</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Consequentialism" title="Consequentialism">consequentialist</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Normative_ethics" title="Normative ethics">normative ethics</a> which holds that an act is morally right if and only if that act maximizes happiness or pleasure.  Classical utilitarianism is said to be hedonistic because it regards pleasure as the only intrinsic good and pain as the only intrinsic evil.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Utilitarianism was described by Bentham as "the <i>greatest happiness</i> or <i>greatest felicity</i> principle".<sup id="cite_ref-BenthamIntroductiontothePrinciples_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BenthamIntroductiontothePrinciples-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Bentham\'s utilitarianism is known for arguing that the <a href="/wiki/Felicific_calculus" title="Felicific calculus">felicific calculus</a> should be used to determine the rightness and wrongness of acts.  It does this by measuring the amount of pain and pleasure for various acts.  Bentham thought that pleasure and pain be broke down in distinct units called hedons and dolors.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Stuart_Mill">John Stuart Mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: John Stuart Mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company,_c1870.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company%2C_c1870.jpg/200px-John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company%2C_c1870.jpg" width="200" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company%2C_c1870.jpg/300px-John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company%2C_c1870.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company%2C_c1870.jpg/400px-John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company%2C_c1870.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1274" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Stuart_Mill_by_London_Stereoscopic_Company,_c1870.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Stuart Mill</div></div></div>\n<p>John Stuart Mill (1806\xe2\x80\x931873) was an influential contributor to <a href="/wiki/Social_theory" title="Social theory">social theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Political theory">political theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a>. His conception of  <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">liberty</a> justified the freedom of the individual in opposition to unlimited state control.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mill also continued Bentham\'s tradition of advancing and defending utilitarianism.  Mill\'s book <i>Utilitarianism</i> is a philosophical defense of utilitarianism. The essay first appeared as a series of three articles published in <i><a href="/wiki/Fraser%27s_Magazine" title="Fraser&#39;s Magazine">Fraser\'s Magazine</a></i> in 1861; the articles were collected and reprinted as a single book in 1863.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Henry_Sidgwick">Henry Sidgwick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Henry Sidgwick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sidgwick" title="Henry Sidgwick">Henry Sidgwick</a></div>\n<p>Henry Sidgwick (1838\xe2\x80\x931900) also focused on utilitarian ethics and was one of the founders and first president of the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Psychical_Research" title="Society for Psychical Research">Society for Psychical Research</a>, was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Metaphysical_Society" title="Metaphysical Society">Metaphysical Society</a>, and promoted the <a href="/wiki/Female_education" title="Female education">higher education of women</a>.  <i>The Methods of Ethics</i> is a book on <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a> written by Sidgwick that was first published in 1874.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  The <i><a href="/wiki/Stanford_Encyclopedia_of_Philosophy" title="Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy">Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy</a></i> indicates that <i>The Methods of Ethics</i> "in many ways marked the culmination of the classical utilitarian tradition."  Well-known contemporary utilitarian philosopher <a href="/wiki/Peter_Singer" title="Peter Singer">Peter Singer</a> has said that the <i>Methods</i> "is simply the best book on ethics ever written."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_idealism">British idealism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: British idealism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_idealism" title="British idealism">British idealism</a></div>\n<p>As an area of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_idealism" title="Absolute idealism">absolute idealism</a>, British idealism was a philosophical movement that was influential in Britain from the mid-nineteenth century to the early twentieth century. Important representatives included <a href="/wiki/T._H._Green" class="mw-redirect" title="T. H. Green">T. H. Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/F._H._Bradley" title="F. H. Bradley">F. H. Bradley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Bosanquet_(philosopher)" title="Bernard Bosanquet (philosopher)">Bernard Bosanquet</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._M._E._McTaggart" title="J. M. E. McTaggart">J. M. E. McTaggart</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._H._Joachim" class="mw-redirect" title="H. H. Joachim">H. H. Joachim</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._H._Muirhead" class="mw-redirect" title="J. H. Muirhead">J. H. Muirhead</a>, and <a href="/wiki/G._R._G._Mure" class="mw-redirect" title="G. R. G. Mure">G. R. G. Mure</a>. Two British philosophers, <a href="/wiki/G._E._Moore" title="G. E. Moore">G. E. Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a>, were brought up in this tradition and then reacted against it by pioneering <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_and_beyond">20th century and beyond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 20th century and beyond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytic_philosophy">Analytic philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Analytic philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Analytic philosophy was based on traditional British empiricism, updated to accommodate the new developments in logic pioneered by German mathematician <a href="/wiki/Gottlob_Frege" title="Gottlob Frege">Gottlob Frege</a>. It has dominated philosophy in the English-speaking world since the early 20th century.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="G._E._Moore">G. E. Moore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: G. E. Moore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/G._E._Moore" title="G. E. Moore">G. E. Moore</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Edward_Moore.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/George_Edward_Moore.jpg/200px-George_Edward_Moore.jpg" width="200" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/George_Edward_Moore.jpg/300px-George_Edward_Moore.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/George_Edward_Moore.jpg 2x" data-file-width="340" data-file-height="265" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Edward_Moore.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>G. E. Moore</div></div></div>\n<p>George Edward Moore (1873\xe2\x80\x931958) was an English philosopher. One of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Analytic_tradition" class="mw-redirect" title="Analytic tradition">analytic tradition</a>, he led the British \'revolt against idealism\' at the turn of the twentieth century, along with Bertrand Russell - while Russell is better known, he stated that it was in fact Moore who "led the way".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Moore is best known today for his defence of <a href="/wiki/Ethical_non-naturalism" title="Ethical non-naturalism">ethical non-naturalism</a>, his emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Common_sense" title="Common sense">common sense</a> in philosophical method, and the <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_paradox" title="Moore&#39;s paradox">paradox that bears his name</a>. He was admired by and influential among other philosophers, and also by the <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury_Group" title="Bloomsbury Group">Bloomsbury Group</a>, but is (unlike his  colleague Russell) mostly unknown today outside of academic philosophy. Moore\'s essays are known for his clear, circumspect writing style, and for his methodical and patient approach to philosophical problems. He was critical of philosophy for its lack of <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_progress" class="mw-redirect" title="Philosophical progress">progress</a>, which he believed was in stark contrast to the dramatic advances in the <a href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural sciences">natural sciences</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>.  He often praised the analytic reasoning of <a href="/wiki/Thales_of_Miletus" title="Thales of Miletus">Thales of Miletus</a>, an early Greek philosopher, for his analysis of the meaning of the term "<a href="/wiki/Landscaping" title="Landscaping">landscaping</a>".  Moore thought Thales\' <a href="/wiki/Reasoning" class="mw-redirect" title="Reasoning">reasoning</a> was one of the few historical examples of philosophical inquiry resulting in practical advances.  Among his most famous works are his book <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://fair-use.org/g-e-moore/principia-ethica">Principia Ethica</a></i>, and his essays, "The Refutation of Idealism", "<a href="/wiki/A_Defence_of_Common_Sense" title="A Defence of Common Sense">A Defence of Common Sense</a>", and "A Proof of the External World".\n</p><p>He was president of the <a href="/wiki/Aristotelian_Society" title="Aristotelian Society">Aristotelian Society</a> from 1918 to 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bertrand_Russell">Bertrand Russell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Bertrand Russell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a></div>\n<p>Bertrand Russell (1872\xe2\x80\x931970) led the British "revolt against <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a>" in the early 1900s, along with G. E. Moore. He was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Gottlob_Frege" title="Gottlob Frege">Gottlob Frege</a>, and was the mentor of <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Ludwig Wittgenstein</a>. He is widely held to be one of the 20th century\'s premier logicians.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He co-authored, with <a href="/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead" title="Alfred North Whitehead">Alfred North Whitehead</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Principia_Mathematica" title="Principia Mathematica">Principia Mathematica</a></i>, an attempt to derive all mathematical truths from a set of <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axioms</a> using <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_inference" title="Rule of inference">rules of inference</a> in <a href="/wiki/Symbolic_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbolic logic">symbolic logic</a>. His philosophical essay "<a href="/wiki/On_Denoting" title="On Denoting">On Denoting</a>" has been considered a "paradigm of philosophy."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Both works have had a considerable influence on <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, and philosophy.\n</p><p>Russell\'s <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_descriptions" title="Theory of descriptions">theory of descriptions</a> has been profoundly influential in the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_language" title="Philosophy of language">philosophy of language</a> and the analysis of <a href="/wiki/Definite_description" title="Definite description">definite descriptions</a>.  His theory was first developed in his 1905 paper "On Denoting".\n</p><p>Russell was a prominent <a href="/wiki/Anti-war" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-war">anti-war</a> <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activist</a>; he championed <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-imperialism" title="Anti-imperialism">anti-imperialism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Russell went to prison for his pacifist activism during World War I. Later, he campaigned against <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, then criticised <a href="/wiki/Stalinist" class="mw-redirect" title="Stalinist">Stalinist</a> <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarianism</a>, attacked the United States of America\'s involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, and finally became an outspoken proponent of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_disarmament" title="Nuclear disarmament">nuclear disarmament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gallery_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gallery-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1950, Russell was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>, "in recognition of his varied and significant writings in which he champions <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Humanitarian">humanitarian</a> ideals and <a href="/wiki/Freethought" title="Freethought">freedom of thought</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-nobel_prize_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nobel_prize-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="A._J._Ayer">A. J. Ayer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: A. J. Ayer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/A._J._Ayer" title="A. J. Ayer">A. J. Ayer</a></div>\n<p>Sir Alfred Jules Ayer (29 October 1910, London \xe2\x80\x93 27 June 1989, London), better known as A. J. Ayer or "Freddie" to friends, was a British analytic philosopher known for his promotion of <a href="/wiki/Logical_positivism" title="Logical positivism">logical positivism</a>, particularly in his books <i><a href="/wiki/Language,_Truth_and_Logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Language, Truth and Logic">Language, Truth and Logic</a></i> (1936) and <i>The Problem of Knowledge</i> (1956).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordinary_language_philosophy">Ordinary language philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Ordinary language philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_language_philosophy" title="Ordinary language philosophy">Ordinary language philosophy</a></div>\n<p>Ordinary language philosophy is a philosophical school that approaches traditional philosophical problems as rooted in <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Misunderstanding" class="extiw" title="wikt:Misunderstanding">misunderstandings</a> that philosophers develop by forgetting what words mean in their everyday use.\n</p><p>This approach typically involves eschewing philosophical theories in favour of close attention to the detail of everyday language. Sometimes also called "Oxford philosophy", it is generally associated with the work of a number of mid-century <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a> professors: mainly <a href="/wiki/J._L._Austin" title="J. L. Austin">J. L. Austin</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Ryle" title="Gilbert Ryle">Gilbert Ryle</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._L._A._Hart" title="H. L. A. Hart">H. L. A. Hart</a>, and <a href="/wiki/P.F._Strawson" class="mw-redirect" title="P.F. Strawson">P.F. Strawson</a>.\n</p><p>It was a major philosophic school between 1930 and 1970.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_times">Contemporary times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_philosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Contemporary times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Recent British philosophers particularly active in the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_religion" title="Philosophy of religion">philosophy of religion</a> have included <a href="/wiki/Antony_Flew" title="Antony Flew">Antony Flew</a>, <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">C. S. Lewis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Hick" title="John Hick">John Hick</a>.\n</p><p>Important moral and political philosophers have included <a href="/wiki/R._M._Hare" title="R. M. Hare">R. M. Hare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alasdair_MacIntyre" title="Alasdair MacIntyre">Alasdair MacIntyre</a>.\n</p><p>Other recent figures in the British analytic tradition include <a href="/wiki/David_Wiggins" title="David Wiggins">David Wiggins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Parfit" title="Derek Parfit">Derek Parfit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/P._F._Strawson" title="P. F. Strawson">P. F. Strawson</a>, who have focused on fields such as <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind" title="Philosophy of mind">philosophy of mind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_language" title="Philosophy of language">philosophy of language</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
