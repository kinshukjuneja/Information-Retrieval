title="Established church">established</a> <a href="/wiki/State_religion" title="State religion">state church</a> in England, whose <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Governor_of_the_Church_of_England" title="Supreme Governor of the Church of England">Supreme Governor</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Monarch_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Monarch of England">Monarch</a>. Other Christian traditions in England include <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in England">Roman Catholicism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Church_of_Great_Britain" title="Methodist Church of Great Britain">Methodism</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Baptist_Union_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist Union of Great Britain">Baptists</a>. After Christianity, the religions with the most adherents are <a href="#Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="#Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="#Neopaganism">Neopaganism</a>, <a href="#Sikhism">Sikhism</a>, <a href="#Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="#Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and the <a href="#Bah\xc3\xa1&#39;\xc3\xad_Faith">Bah\xc3\xa1\'\xc3\xad Faith</a>. There are also organisations promoting <a href="/wiki/Irreligion" title="Irreligion">irreligion</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a>.\n</p><p>Many of England\'s most notable buildings and monuments are religious in nature: <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Angel_of_the_North" title="Angel of the North">Angel of the North</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul&#39;s Cathedral">St Paul\'s Cathedral</a>. class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox Christians">Eastern Orthodox Christians</a>. 4.8% were Muslim, 3.4% were members of other religions, 5.3% were Agnostics, 6.8% were Atheists and 15.0% were not sure about their religious affiliation or refused to answer to the question.<sup id="cite_ref-2009_ipsos_3-0" class="reference"><a Prior to <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward&#160;III</a>, the patron saint was <a href="/wiki/Edmund_the_Martyr" title="Edmund the Martyr">St&#160;Edmund</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Alban" title="Saint Alban">St&#160;Alban</a> is also honoured as England\'s <a href="/wiki/Protomartyr" class="mw-redirect" title="Protomartyr">first martyr</a>.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglican">Anglican</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anglican">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Established_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Established church">established religion</a> of the realm is the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Governor_of_the_Church_of_England" title="Supreme Governor of the Church of England">Supreme Governor</a> is <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth&#160;II</a> although the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a> is governed by the <a href="/wiki/General_Synod" title="General Synod">General Synod</a> of its bishops under the authority of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>. 26 of the church\'s 42 bishops are <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Lords Spiritual</a>, representing the church in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. The dioceses of England are divided between the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Church_of_England_dioceses" title="List of Church of England dioceses">two provinces</a> of <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Canterbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">York</a>, both of whose archbishops are considered <a href="/wiki/Primate_(bishop)" title="Primate (bishop)">primates</a>. The church regards itself as the continuation of the Catholic church introduced by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine_of_Canterbury" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Augustine of Canterbury">St&#160;Augustine</a>\'s 6th-century <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_mission" title="Gregorian mission">mission</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kent" title="Kingdom of Kent">Kent</a>, although this is disputed owing to procedural and doctrinal changes introduced by the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Thirty-Nine_Articles_of_Religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty-Nine Articles of Religion">Thirty-Nine Articles of Religion</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>. In 2010, the Church of England counted 25&#160;million baptised members out of the 41&#160;million Christians in Great Britain\'s population of about 60&#160;million;<sup id="cite_ref-londontimes_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londontimes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-beeboop_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeboop-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> around the same time, it also claimed to baptise one in eight newborn children.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Generally, anyone in England may marry or be buried at their local <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">parish church</a>, whether or not they have been baptised in the church.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Actual attendance has declined steadily since 1890,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> with around one million, or 10% of the baptised population attending Sunday services on a regular basis (defined as once a month or more) and three million- roughly 15%- joining Christmas Eve and Christmas services.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It has around <span class="nowrap">18&#8201;000</span> active and ordained clergy.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Free_Church_of_England" title="Free Church of England">Free Church of England</a> is another Anglican denomination which separated from the Church of England in the 19th century in opposition to shifts in doctrine and ceremony that brought the established church closer to Roman Catholicism. The Free Church is in communion with the <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Episcopal_Church" title="Reformed Episcopal Church">Reformed Episcopal Church</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.\n</p><p><span id="Catholicism"></span><span id="Roman_Catholicism"></span>\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" It is divided among <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_England_and_Wales#Hierarchy" title="Catholic Church in England and Wales">five provinces</a> headed by the archbishops of Westminster, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Liverpool" title="Archbishop of Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Birmingham" title="Archbishop of Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Southwark" title="Archbishop of Southwark">Southwark</a> in England and <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Cardiff" title="Archbishop of Cardiff">Cardiff</a> in Wales. The Catholic Church considers itself a continuation of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">Celtic Christian</a> communities, although its formal hierarchy needed to be refounded by the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_mission" title="Gregorian mission">Gregorian mission</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Saxon kingdoms</a> in the 6th and 7th centuries and again following the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_XIII" title="Pope Clement XIII">Papal</a> recognition of <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="George III of England">George&#160;III</a> as the legitimate ruler of Great Britain in 1766 opened the way for the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Emancipation" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic Emancipation">Catholic Emancipation</a>, easing and ultimately eliminating the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Catholicism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Anti-Catholicism in the United Kingdom">anti-Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/English_Penal_Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="English Penal Laws">Penal Laws</a> and <a href="/wiki/Test_Act" title="Test Act">Test Acts</a>. This process sometimes faced great popular opposition, as during the 1780 <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Riots" title="Gordon Riots">Gordon Riots</a> in London. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O&#39;Connell">Daniel O\'Connell</a> was the first Catholic <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">member of Parliament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The influx of large numbers of Irish Catholics during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Irish_Famine" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Irish Famine">Great Famine</a> of the 1840s and \'50s permitted the 1850 <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">papal</a> <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">bull</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Universalis_Ecclesiae" title="Universalis Ecclesiae">Universalis Ecclesiae</a></i> to formally reconstitute the dormant dioceses of the Catholic church in Britain. More recently, the <a href="/wiki/British_Royal_Family" class="mw-redirect" title="British Royal Family">royal family</a> has been permitted to marry Roman Catholics without fear of being disqualified from succession to the throne.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Recent immigration from Catholic countries, particularly <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, has increased the church\'s numbers still more.<sup id="cite_ref-telly_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telly-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Polling in 2009 suggested there were about 5.2&#160;million Catholics in England and Wales, about 9.6% of the population,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> concentrated in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Northwest England">northwest</a>. Some studies show that weekly attendance at Catholic masses now exceeds that of the Anglican services.<sup id="cite_ref-telly_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telly-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Created in 1932, it is the diocese of the <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople">Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople</a> that covers England, Wales, Scotland and Ireland as well as Malta. A Greek Orthodox community already existed at the time the UK was formed, worshipping in the Imperial Russian Embassy in London. However, it was another 130 years until an autonomous community was set up in Finsbury Park in London, in 1837. The first new church was built in 1850, on London Street in the City. In 1882, St Sophia Cathedral was constructed in London, in order to cope with the growing influx of Orthodox immigrants. By the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, there were large Orthodox communities in London, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and Liverpool, each focused on its own church. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and its aftermath also saw a large expansion among the Orthodox Communities. Today, there are <a href="/wiki/List_of_cathedrals_in_the_United_Kingdom#Greek_Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="List of cathedrals in the United Kingdom">seven churches bearing the title of Cathedral in London</a> as well as in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Orthodox_Cathedral" title="Birmingham Orthodox Cathedral">Dormition of the Mother of God and St Andrew</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>. Ibn Sina\'s canon of medicine was a standard text for medical students well into the 17th century.\n</p><p>Today Islam is the second largest religion in England.  About 38% of English Muslims live in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, where they make up 12.4% of the population. There are also large numbers of Muslims in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bradford" Elements of the native Brythonic <a href="/wiki/Celtic_polytheism" title="Celtic polytheism">Celtic religion</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">druids</a>, the Celtic priestly caste who were believed to originate in Britain,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> were outlawed by Claudius,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and in 61 they vainly defended their sacred groves from destruction by the Romans on the island of Mona (<a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> However, under Roman rule the Britons continued to worship native Celtic deities, such as <a href="/wiki/Ancasta" title="Ancasta">Ancasta</a>, but often conflated with their Roman equivalents, like Mars Rigonemetos at <a href="/wiki/Nettleham" title="Nettleham">Nettleham</a>. The founding of a temple to Claudius at Camulodunum was one of the impositions that led to the revolt of Boudica.\n</p><p>Eastern cults such as <a href="/wiki/Mithraism" title="Mithraism">Mithraism</a> also grew in popularity towards the end of the occupation. The <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Mithras,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Temple of Mithras, London">Temple of Mithras</a> is one example of the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Western_esotericism" title="Western esotericism">mystery religions</a> among the rich urban classes.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic_paganism">Germanic paganism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Germanic paganism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>, immigrants from the European continent arrived, bringing <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_paganism" title="Anglo-Saxon paganism">Anglo-Saxon paganism</a>, a subset of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_paganism" title="Germanic paganism">Germanic paganism</a>, with them. Later, after most of the Anglo-Saxon peoples had converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> arrived, bringing with them <a href="/wiki/Norse_paganism" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse paganism">Norse paganism</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_places_of_worship">Notable places of worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Notable places of worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The varied religious and ethnic history of England has left a wide range of religious buildings\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">chapels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapels of ease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temples</a>. Besides its spiritual importance, the religious architecture includes buildings of importance to the tourism industry and local pride. As a result of the Reformation, the ancient cathedrals remained in the possession of the then-established churches, while most Roman Catholic churches date from Victorian times or are of more recent construction (in Liverpool the ultra-modern <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Metropolitan_Cathedral" title="Liverpool Metropolitan Cathedral">Roman Catholic cathedral</a> was actually completed before the more traditional <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Cathedral" title="Liverpool Cathedral">Anglican cathedral</a>, whose construction took most of the twentieth century).
