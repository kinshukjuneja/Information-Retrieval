title="Mary, the mother of Jesus">Saint Mary</a> and Saint Rumon</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Ruin" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruin">ruined</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tavistock,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Tavistock, Devon">Tavistock, Devon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Nothing remains of the abbey except the <a href="/wiki/Refectory" title="Refectory">refectory</a>, two gateways and a porch. The abbey church, dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Our Lady</a> and St Rumon, was destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> raiders in 997<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and rebuilt under <a href="/wiki/Lyfing_of_Winchester" title="Lyfing of Winchester">Lyfing</a>, the second abbot. The church was further rebuilt in 1285 and the greater part of the abbey between 1457 and 1458.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-2" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tavistock_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The abbey was founded in 961 by <a href="/wiki/Ordgar,_Ealdorman_of_Devon" title="Ordgar, Ealdorman of Devon">Ordgar, Ealdorman of Devon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and completed by his son <a href="/wiki/Ordwulf" title="Ordwulf">Ordwulf</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> in 981, in which year the charter of confirmation was granted by King <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_the_Unready" title="\xc3\x86thelred the Unready">\xc3\x86thelred II</a> (c.968-1016) <i>The Unready</i>, nephew of Ordwulf. It was endowed with lands in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, and became one of the richest abbeys in the west of England.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Account_by_Dugdale">Account by Dugdale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tavistock_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Account by Dugdale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/William_Dugdale" title="William Dugdale">William Dugdale</a>\'s <i>Monasticon Anglicanum</i> (1718 edition in English) states as follows concerning the foundation:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"In the reign of King Edgar an Earl call\'d Ordulphus had a vision commanding him to build an oratory in a certain place mark\'d out to him, which he did, making it large enough to contain 1,000 persons, and erecting habitations for several monks, whom he also plac\'d there under an abbat. The lands he and his kindred and friends gave to this monastery were: Tavistock, Midleton, Hatherlege (<a href="/wiki/Hatherleigh" title="Hatherleigh">Hatherleigh</a>), Berliton, Leghe, Dunethem, Chuvelin, Lankinghorn, Home, Werelgete, Orlege (<a href="/wiki/Manor_of_Orleigh" title="Manor of Orleigh">Orleigh</a>), Auri (<a href="/wiki/Annery,_Monkleigh" title="Annery, Monkleigh">Annery</a>), Rame, Savyock, Pannastan, Tomebiry, Colbrok, Lege, Wulsitheton, and Clymesland. These never to be alienated and to be free from all impositions, except an expedition undertaken and the repairing of forts and bridges. This was confirmed in the year 981 and in the reign of King Ethelred...The Bull of Pope Celestin dated 1193 confirms all donations made to these monks..."</dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_abbots">Later abbots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tavistock_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later abbots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Abbot Lyfing and his successor Abbot <a href="/wiki/Ealdred_(archbishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ealdred (archbishop)">Ealdred</a> both became <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishops of Worcester</a>, and the latter is said to have crowned King <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William the Conqueror</a>. The thirty-sixth abbot, John Dynynton, was granted leave in 1458 to use various <a href="/wiki/Pontifical" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontifical">pontificalia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mitre" title="Mitre">mitre</a>, which latter gave him a seat in <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">Parliament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The thirty-ninth abbot, Richard Banham or Baynham,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> was made a <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Parliament" title="Lord of Parliament">lord of Parliament</a> by King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> in 1513.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution">Dissolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tavistock_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dissolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1538 the last abbot, John Peryn, together with twenty monks, surrendered the abbey to the king, receiving a pension of a hundred pounds. The abbey was the second wealthiest in Devon  at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> (1536\xe2\x80\x931541), estimated at \xc2\xa3902, only very slightly behind the wealth of <a href="/wiki/Plympton_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Plympton Abbey">Plympton Abbey</a>. Both were far wealthier than the next wealthiest, <a href="/wiki/Buckfast_Abbey" title="Buckfast Abbey">Buckfast Abbey</a>, with an annual income of about \xc2\xa3460.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1540 the Abbey and its lands were granted by King Henry VIII to <a href="/wiki/John_Russell,_1st_Earl_of_Bedford" title="John Russell, 1st Earl of Bedford">John Russell, 1st Baron Russell</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chenies_Manor_House" title="Chenies Manor House">Chenies</a> in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a> (created in 1550 1st <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bedford" title="Duke of Bedford">Earl of Bedford</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Russell family long maintained a close connection with Tavistock, and in 1694 the family received the additional titles of <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Tavistock" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquess of Tavistock">Marquess of Tavistock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bedford" title="Duke of Bedford">Duke of Bedford</a>. They erected many 19th-century public buildings and statues in the town at their own expense. Before 1810 the family had only one formal residence in Devonshire, namely the <a href="/wiki/Townhouse_(Great_Britain)" title="Townhouse (Great Britain)">townhouse</a> of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bedford_House,_Exeter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bedford House, Exeter (page does not exist)">Bedford House</a> in the City of <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>. In 1810 <a href="/wiki/John_Russell,_6th_Duke_of_Bedford" title="John Russell, 6th Duke of Bedford">John Russell, 6th Duke of Bedford</a> built to the designs of Sir <a href="/wiki/Jeffry_Wyattville" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeffry Wyattville">Jeffry Wyattville</a> a new residence named <a href="/wiki/Endsleigh_Cottage" title="Endsleigh Cottage">Endsleigh Cottage</a> at <a href="/wiki/Milton_Abbot" title="Milton Abbot">Milton Abbot</a>, near Tavistock, a former manor belonging to the Abbey. It was a large <a href="/wiki/Cottage_orn%C3%A9" title="Cottage orn\xc3\xa9">cottage orn\xc3\xa9</a> used as a  summer holiday home, still standing but sold in the 20th century by the Russell family.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is now the Hotel Endsleigh, managed by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Polizzi" title="Alex Polizzi">Alex Polizzi</a> and owned by her mother.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Rumon">Saint Rumon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tavistock_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saint Rumon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rumon_of_Tavistock" title="Rumon of Tavistock">Rumon of Tavistock</a></div>\n<p>St Rumon was probably a brother of St <a href="/wiki/Tudwal" title="Tudwal">Tudwal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A9guier" title="Tr\xc3\xa9guier">Tr\xc3\xa9guier</a>, but little else is known of him beyond that he was probably an Irish missionary and many churches in Devon and Cornwall in England were named after him. Some authorities have identified him with <a href="/wiki/St_Ronan" class="mw-redirect" title="St Ronan">St Ronan</a> (1 June) who is venerated in Brittany, but others believe that he and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kea" title="Saint Kea">St Kea</a> were British monks who founded a monastery at <a href="/wiki/Street,_Somerset" title="Street, Somerset">Street, Somerset</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> At Tavistock, his feast day is kept on 28 or 30 August. The translation of St Rumon is celebrated on 5 January.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornwall">Cornwall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tavistock_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cornwall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The abbey had considerable land holdings in Cornwall and three churches there are dedicated to St Rumon: <a href="/wiki/Ruan_Lanihorne" title="Ruan Lanihorne">Ruan Lanihorne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grade-Ruan" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade-Ruan">Ruan Major and Ruan Minor</a>. In the Domesday Book, the abbey held the manors of Sheviock, Antony, Rame, Tregrenna, Penharget and Tolcarne, while four other manors formerly theirs had been taken by <a href="/wiki/Robert_of_Mortain" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert of Mortain">Robert of Mortain</a>. Only Sheviock was in the hands of the abbey while Ermenhald held five manors from it. Sheviock was worth 60s per annum and the holdings of Ermenhald were worth in all \xc2\xa38-15s. These were the same values as formerly suggesting the monks were good at administering their estate.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These lands in the extreme south-east of Cornwall were the reason for that district being part of Devon from the medieval period until 1844.\n</p><p>In early times the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> were in the possession of a confederacy of hermits. King Henry I gave the hermits\' territory to the abbey of Tavistock, which established a <a href="/wiki/Tresco_Priory" title="Tresco Priory">priory on Tresco</a> that was abolished at the Reformation.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Burials">Burials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
