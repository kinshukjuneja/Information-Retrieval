<b>London and Paris Conferences</b> were two related conferences in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in September\xe2\x80\x93October 1954 to determine the status of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>.  The talks concluded with the signing of the <b>Paris Agreements</b> (<b>Paris Pacts</b>, or <b>Paris Accords</b><sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>), which granted West Germany full sovereignty, ended the <a href="/wiki/Allied-occupied_Germany" title="Allied-occupied Germany">occupation</a>, and allowed its admittance to <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, both West Germany and Italy joined the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Brussels Treaty">Brussels Treaty</a><sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> on 23 October 1954.<sup id="cite_ref-Haftendorn_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haftendorn-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Agreements went into force on 5 May 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-Haftendorn_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haftendorn-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The participating powers included <a href="/wiki/France" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelude">Prelude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_and_Paris_Conferences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prelude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> had been occupied by <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a> and lacked its own means of defense. On 23 July 1952, the <a href="/wiki/European_Coal_and_Steel_Community" title="European Coal and Steel Community">European Coal and Steel Community</a> came into existence, bonding the member states economically. By 1951, fear of possible <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> aggression in Europe led to preparation of an ill-fated <a href="/wiki/European_Defense_Community" class="mw-redirect" title="European Defense Community">European Defense Community</a> (EDC). EDC was a proposed joint Western European military force, at the time favored over admitting Germany to NATO.  The <a href="/wiki/General_Treaty" title="General Treaty">General Treaty</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Deutschlandvertrag</i>) of 1952 formally named the EDC as a prerequisite of the end of Allied occupation of Germany. EDC was, however, rejected by the <a href="/wiki/French_National_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="French National Assembly">French National Assembly</a> on August 30, 1954, and a new solution became necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-Haftendorn_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haftendorn-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="London">London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_and_Paris_Conferences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At the London Conference, often called the <b>Nine-Power Conference</b> (not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Power_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine Power Treaty">Nine Power Treaty</a>), it was agreed that the occupying powers would make every effort to end the occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-jhc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jhc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The limits of German re-armament were also very important especially to France, which was still concerned with a powerful Germany.\n</p><p>Belgium was represented by <a href="/wiki/Paul-Henri_Spaak" title="Paul-Henri Spaak">Paul-Henri Spaak</a>, Canada by <a href="/wiki/Lester_B._Pearson" title="Lester B. Pearson">Lester B. Pearson</a>, France by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Mend%C3%A8s-France" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Mend\xc3\xa8s-France">Pierre Mend\xc3\xa8s-France</a>, Germany by <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>, Italy by <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Martino" title="Gaetano Martino">Gaetano Martino</a>, Luxembourg by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bech" title="Joseph Bech">Joseph Bech</a>, the Netherlands by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Willem_Beyen" class="mw-redirect" title="Jan Willem Beyen">Jan Willem Beyen</a>, the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Eden" title="Anthony Eden">Anthony Eden</a>, and the United States by <a href="/wiki/John_Foster_Dulles" title="John Foster Dulles">John Foster Dulles</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paris">Paris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_and_Paris_Conferences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The powers met again in Paris on October 20\xe2\x80\x9323, in an intergovernmental conference followed by a NATO Council meeting, to put the decisions reached in London into formal declarations and protocols to existing treaties.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>   "<a class="external text" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Paris_Agreements">Protocol No. I Modifying and Completing the Brussels Treaty</a>" formally added West Germany and Italy to the Brussels Treaty, creating the <a href="/wiki/Western_European_Union" title="Western European Union">Western European Union</a> (WEU), which, while not as broad or powerful as the previously proposed EDC, nevertheless was sufficient for the <i>Deutschlandvertrag</i> to come into force and therefore to end the occupation of West Germany and admit it as an ally in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.\n</p><p>Altogether there were as many as twelve international agreements signed in Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-Haftendorn_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haftendorn-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Bonn%E2%80%93Paris_conventions" title="Bonn\xe2\x80\x93Paris conventions">Bonn\xe2\x80\x93Paris conventions</a> ended the occupation of West Germany and West Germany obtained "the full authority of a sovereign state" on 5 May 1955 (although "full sovereignty" was not obtained until the <a href="/wiki/Two_Plus_Four_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Two Plus Four Agreement">Two Plus Four Agreement</a> in 1990).<sup id="cite_ref-Sovereignty_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sovereignty-5">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The treaty allowed Allied troops to remain in the country.\n</p><p>An agreement expanded the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Brussels Treaty">Brussels Treaty</a> of 1948 to include West Germany and Italy, creating the <a href="/wiki/Western_European_Union" title="Western European Union">Western European Union</a>.  This agreement allowed West Germany to start a limited rearmament program though it banned development of certain weapons, such as large warships.  It was signed by the Brussels Treaty countries (Belgium, France, Great Britain, Luxembourg, and the Netherlands) and by West Germany and Italy.\n</p><p>Another accord accepted West Germany into the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic Treaty Organization">North Atlantic Treaty Organization</a> (NATO).<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The negotiations on <a href="/wiki/Saar_(protectorate)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar (protectorate)">Saar</a> status, only between France and West Germany, were held on the night before the conference, on 19 October.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The territory had been essentially annexed by France and a referendum was set up to determine the will of its people (the outcome was that Saarland rejoined West Germany in 1956-1957).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On 27 October 1956<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the <a href="/wiki/Saar_Treaty" title="Saar Treaty">Saar Treaty</a> officially made Saarland a state of the Federal Republic of Germany.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
