title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a> (31 October 1828&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 27 May 1914) was an English physicist, chemist, and inventor. He is known as an independent early developer of a successful <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light bulb</a>, and is the person responsible for developing and supplying the first incandescent lights used illuminate homes and public buildings, including the <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Theatre" title="Savoy Theatre">Savoy Theatre</a>, London, in <a href="/wiki/1881_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1881 in the United Kingdom">1881</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CassellsChronology_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassellsChronology-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1904_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1904 in the United Kingdom">1904</a>, Swan was <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighted</a> by <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">King Edward VII</a>, awarded the Royal Society\'s <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Medal" title="Hughes Medal">Hughes Medal</a>, and was made an honorary member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Pharmaceutical_Society_of_Great_Britain" title="Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain">Pharmaceutical Society</a>. He had received the highest decoration in France, the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Legion of Honour</a>, when he visited the <a href="/wiki/International_Exposition_of_Electricity" title="International Exposition of Electricity">1881 International Exposition of Electricity, Paris</a>. The exhibition included displays of his inventions, and the city was lit with his electric lighting.<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGB.org.uk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGB.org.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Swan was the maternal grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing#Christopher_Morcom" title="Alan Turing">Christopher Morcom</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Alan_Turing:_The_Enigma_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alan_Turing:_The_Enigma-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a>\'s close friend and first love during their studies at the <a href="/wiki/Sherborne_School" title="Sherborne School">Sherborne boarding school</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Joseph Wilson Swan was born in <a href="/wiki/1828_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1828 in the United Kingdom">1828</a> at Pallion Hall in <a href="/wiki/Pallion" title="Pallion">Pallion</a>, in the Parish of <a href="/wiki/Bishopwearmouth" title="Bishopwearmouth">Bishopwearmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Sunderland" title="City of Sunderland">Sunderland</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>. His parents were John Swan and Isabella Cameron.<sup id="cite_ref-fsu_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fsu-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Swan was apprenticed for six years to a <a href="/wiki/Sunderland" title="Sunderland">Sunderland</a> firm of <a href="/wiki/Pharmacist" title="Pharmacist">druggists</a>, <i>Hudson and Osbaldiston</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGB.org.uk_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGB.org.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, it is not known if Swan completed his six-year apprenticeship, as both partners subsequently died.<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGB.org.uk_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGB.org.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He was said to have had an enquiring mind,<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGB.org.uk_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGB.org.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> even as a child. He augmented his education with a fascination of his surroundings, the industry of the area, and reading at Sunderland Library.<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGB.org.uk_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGB.org.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He attended lectures at the Sunderland Atheneum.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Swan subsequently joined Mawson\'s, a firm of manufacturing chemists in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, started in the year of Swan\'s birth by John Mawson<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGB.org.uk_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGB.org.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (9 September 1819&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 17 December 1867), the husband of his sister, Elizabeth Swan (22 November 1822&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 2 August 1905). In <a href="/wiki/1846_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1846 in the United Kingdom">1846</a>, Swan was offered a partnership at Mawson\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGB.org.uk_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGB.org.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This company subsequently existed as <i>Mawson, Swan and Morgan</i> until <a href="/wiki/1973_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1973 in the United Kingdom">1973</a>, formerly located on Grey Street in Newcastle upon Tyne, near <a href="/wiki/Grey%27s_Monument" title="Grey&#39;s Monument">Grey\'s Monument</a>. The premises, now occupied by burger chain restaurant <a href="/wiki/Byron_Hamburgers" title="Byron Hamburgers">Byron</a>, can be identified by a line of <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a>-style electric <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">street lamps</a> in front of the store on Grey Street.\n</p><p>Swan lived at <a href="/wiki/Underhill,_Low_Fell,_Gateshead" title="Underhill, Low Fell, Gateshead">Underhill, Low Fell, Gateshead</a>, a large house on Kells Lane North, where he conducted most of his experiments in the large <a href="/wiki/Conservatory_(greenhouse)" title="Conservatory (greenhouse)">conservatory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The house was later converted into a private fee paying, grant aided co-educational grammar school named Beaconsfield School.<sup id="cite_ref-BEAC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEAC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Here, students could still find examples of Swan\'s original electrical fittings.<sup id="cite_ref-BEAC_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEAC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_light">Electric light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electric light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbonfilament.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Carbonfilament.jpg/190px-Carbonfilament.jpg" width="190" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Carbonfilament.jpg/285px-Carbonfilament.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Carbonfilament.jpg/380px-Carbonfilament.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1061" data-file-height="914" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbonfilament.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carbon filament lamp (E27 socket, 220 <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volts</a>, approx. 30 watts, left side: running with 100 volts)</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/1850_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1850 in the United Kingdom">1850</a>, Swan began working on a light bulb using <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbonised</a> paper filaments in an evacuated glass bulb. By <a href="/wiki/1860_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1860 in the United Kingdom">1860</a>, he was able to demonstrate a working device, and obtained a British patent covering a partial <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, carbon filament incandescent lamp.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, the lack of a good vacuum and an adequate electric source resulted in an inefficient light bulb with a short lifetime.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1875_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1875 in the United Kingdom">1875</a>, Swan returned to consider the problem of the light bulb with the aid of a better vacuum and a carbonised thread as a filament. The most significant feature of Swan\'s improved lamp was that there was little residual <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> tube to ignite the filament, thus allowing the filament to glow almost white-hot without catching fire. However, his filament had low resistance, thus needing heavy copper wires to supply it.<sup id="cite_ref-si.edu_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-si.edu-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Swan first publicly demonstrated his incandescent carbon lamp at a lecture for the Newcastle upon Tyne Chemical Society on 18 December <a href="/wiki/1878_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1878 in the United Kingdom">1878</a>. However, after burning with a bright light for some minutes in his laboratory, the lamp broke down due to excessive current. On 17 January <a href="/wiki/1879_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1879 in the United Kingdom">1879</a>, this lecture was successfully repeated with the lamp shown in actual operation; Swan had solved the problem of incandescent electric lighting by means of a vacuum lamp. On 3 February 1879, he publicly demonstrated a working lamp to an audience of over seven hundred people in the lecture theatre of the <a href="/wiki/Literary_and_Philosophical_Society_of_Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Literary and Philosophical Society of Newcastle upon Tyne">Literary and Philosophical Society of Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">Sir William Armstrong</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">Cragside</a> presiding. Swan turned his attention to producing a better carbon filament, and the means of attaching its ends. He devised a method of treating cotton to produce "parchmentised thread", and obtained British Patent 4933 on 27 November <a href="/wiki/1880_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1880 in the United Kingdom">1880</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> From that time he began installing light bulbs in homes and landmarks in England.\n</p>\n<div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Joseph_Swan_blue_plaque.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">Blue plaque</a> commemorates Swan\'s invention of the electric light bulb and Underhill as the first house in the world to have electric lighting installed</div></div></div>\n<p>His house, <a href="/wiki/Underhill,_Low_Fell,_Gateshead" title="Underhill, Low Fell, Gateshead">Underhill, Low Fell, Gateshead</a>, was the world\'s first to have working light bulbs installed.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Lit &amp; Phil Library in Westgate Road, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>, was the first public room lit by electric light during a lecture by Swan on 20 October <a href="/wiki/1880_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1880 in the United Kingdom">1880</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2litandPhil20110208_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2litandPhil20110208-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1881_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1881 in the United Kingdom">1881</a>, he founded his own company, The Swan Electric Light Company,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and started commercial production.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Theatre" title="Savoy Theatre">Savoy</a>, a state-of-the-art theatre in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>, London, was the first public building in the world lit entirely by electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-CassellsChronology_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassellsChronology-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Burgess_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Swan supplied about 1,200 incandescent lamps, powered by an 88.3-<a href="/wiki/Kilowatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilowatt">kilowatt</a> (118.4-<a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a>) generator on open land near the theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-Times_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Times-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The builder of the Savoy, <a href="/wiki/Richard_D%27Oyly_Carte" title="Richard D&#39;Oyly Carte">Richard D\'Oyly Carte</a>, explained why he had introduced Swan\'s electric light: "The greatest drawbacks to the enjoyment of the theatrical performances are, undoubtedly, the foul air and heat which pervade all theatres. As everyone knows, each <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas-burner</a> consumes as much oxygen as many people, and causes great heat beside. The incandescent lamps consume no oxygen, and cause no perceptible heat."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The first generator proved too small to power the whole building, and though the entire front-of-house was electrically lit, the stage was lit by gas until 28 December 1881. At that performance, Carte stepped onstage and broke a glowing lightbulb before the audience to demonstrate the safety of Swan\'s new technology. On 29 December 1881, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> described the electric lighting as superior, visually, to gaslight.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first private residence, other than the inventor\'s, lit by the new incandescent lamp was that of his friend, <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">Sir William Armstrong</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">Cragside</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Rothbury" title="Rothbury">Rothbury</a>, Northumberland. Swan personally supervised the installation there in December 1880. Swan had formed "The Swan Electric Light Company Ltd" with a factory at Benwell, Newcastle, and had established the first commercial manufacture of incandescent lightbulbs by the beginning of 1881.\n</p><p>Swan\'s carbon rod lamp and carbon filament lamp, while functional, were still relatively impractical due to low resistance (needing very expensive thick copper wiring) and short running life.<sup id="cite_ref-si.edu_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-si.edu-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> While searching for a better filament for his light bulb, Swan inadvertently made another advance. In 1881, he developed and patented a process for squeezing <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a> through holes to form conducting fibres. His newly established company (which by merger eventually became the Edison and Swan United Company) used Swan\'s <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> filaments in their bulbs. The textile industry has also used this process.<sup id="cite_ref-fsu_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fsu-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first ship to use Swan\'s invention was <i>The City of Richmond</i>, owned by the Inman Line. She was fitted with incandescent lamps in June 1881. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> also introduced them to their ships soon after; with <a href="/wiki/HMS_Inflexible_(1876)" title="HMS Inflexible (1876)">HMS <i>Inflexible</i></a> having the new lamps installed in the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-kenneth_swan_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kenneth_swan-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> An early employment in engineering was during the digging of the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Tunnel" title="Severn Tunnel">Severn Tunnel</a>, where contractor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Walker" title="Thomas A. Walker">Thomas Walker</a> installed "20-candlepower lamps" in the temporary pilot tunnels.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Swan was one of the early developers of the electric <a href="/wiki/Safety_lamp" title="Safety lamp">safety lamp</a> for miners, exhibiting his first in Newcastle upon Tyne at the <a href="/wiki/North_of_England_Institute_of_Mining_and_Mechanical_Engineers" title="North of England Institute of Mining and Mechanical Engineers">North of England Institute of Mining and Mechanical Engineers</a> on 14 May 1881.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This required a wired supply, so the following year, he presented one with a battery<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and other improved versions followed.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> By <a href="/wiki/1886_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1886 in the United Kingdom">1886</a>, a lamp with better light output than a flame safety lamp was in production by the Edison-Swan Company.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> However, it suffered from problems of reliability and was not a success. It took development by others over the next 20 years or so before effective electric lamps were in common use.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjunction_with_Edison">Conjunction with Edison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conjunction with Edison">edit</a><span title="Enlarge"></a></div>Edison &amp; Swan United Electric Light Company, otherwise known as "Ediswan"</div></div></div>\n<p>In what is considered to be independent lines of inquiry, Swan\'s incandescent electric lamp was developed at the same time <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> was working on his incandescent lamp<sup id="cite_ref-auto_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> with Swan\'s first successful lamp and Edison\'s lamp both patented in <a href="/wiki/1879_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1879 in the United Kingdom">1879</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Edison\'s goal in developing his lamp was for it to be used as one part of a much larger system: a long-life high-resistance lamp that could be connected in <a href="/wiki/Series_and_parallel_circuits#Parallel_circuits" title="Series and parallel circuits">parallel</a> to work economically with the large-scale electric-lighting utility he was creating.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Swan\'s original lamp design, with its low resistance (the lamp could only be used in <a href="/wiki/Series_and_parallel_circuits#Series_circuits" title="Series and parallel circuits">series</a>) and short life span, was not suited for such an application.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ediswan">Ediswan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ediswan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Swan\'s strong patents in Great Britain led, in <a href="/wiki/1883_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1883 in the United Kingdom">1883</a>, to the two competing companies merging to exploit both Swan\'s and Edison\'s inventions,<sup id="cite_ref-kenneth_swan_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kenneth_swan-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Edison_and_Swan_Electric_Light_Company" title="Edison and Swan Electric Light Company">Edison &amp; Swan United Electric Light Company</a>. Known commonly as "Ediswan", the company sold lamps made with a cellulose filament that Swan had invented in <a href="/wiki/1881_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1881 in the United Kingdom">1881</a>, while the Edison Company continued using <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> filaments outside of Britain. In <a href="/wiki/1892_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1892 in the United Kingdom">1892</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> (GE) began exploiting Swan\'s patents to produce cellulose filaments, until they were replaced in <a href="/wiki/1904_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1904 in the United Kingdom">1904</a> by a GE developed "General Electric Metallized" (GEM) baked cellulose filaments.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1886_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1886 in the United Kingdom">1886</a>, Ediswan moved production to a former <a href="/wiki/Jute" title="Jute">jute</a> mill at <a href="/wiki/Ponders_End" title="Ponders End">Ponders End</a>, North London.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1916_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1916 in the United Kingdom">1916</a>, Ediswan set up the UK\'s first radio <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">thermionic valve</a> factory at Ponders End. This area, with nearby <a href="/wiki/Brimsdown_Industrial_Estate" title="Brimsdown Industrial Estate">Brimsdown</a> subsequently developed as a centre for the manufacture of thermionic valves, <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tubes</a>, etc., and nearby parts of <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Enfield" title="London Borough of Enfield">Enfield</a> became an important centre of the <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> industry for much of the 20th century. Ediswan became part of <a href="/wiki/British_Thomson-Houston" title="British Thomson-Houston">British Thomson-Houston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Associated_Electrical_Industries" title="Associated Electrical Industries">Associated Electrical Industries</a> (AEI) in the late 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">Photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg/220px-Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg/330px-Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg/440px-Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Josepth_Swan_Plaque_Newcastle_upon_Tyne.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Stone tablet of Sir Joseph Wilson Swan in Pilgrim Street, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, on former Electricity Board building</div></div></div>\n<p>When working with wet photographic plates, Swan noticed that heat increased the sensitivity of the silver bromide emulsion. By <a href="/wiki/1871_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1871 in the United Kingdom">1871</a>, he had devised a method of using dry plates, and substituting <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a> plastic for glass plates, thus initiating the age of convenience in photography. Eight years later, he patented <a href="/wiki/Photographic_paper#Bromide_papers" title="Photographic paper">bromide paper</a>, developments of which are still used for black-and-white photographic prints.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1864_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1864 in the United Kingdom">1864</a>, Swan patented the transfer process for making <a href="/wiki/Carbon_print" title="Carbon print">carbon prints</a>, a permanent photographic process. By adding the transfer step, Swan was able to easily make photographs with a full tonal range.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/1894_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1894 in the United Kingdom">1894</a>, Swan was elected a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">Fellow of the Royal Society</a> (FRS),<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and in September <a href="/wiki/1901_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1901 in the United Kingdom">1901</a>, he was awarded the honorary degree of <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">Doctor of Science</a> (D.Sc.) from <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1904_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1904 in the United Kingdom">1904</a>, he was <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knighted</a>, awarded the Royal Society\'s <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Medal" title="Hughes Medal">Hughes Medal</a>, and made an honorary member of the Pharmaceutical Society. Swan died in <a href="/wiki/1914_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1914 in the United Kingdom">1914</a> at <a href="/wiki/Warlingham" title="Warlingham">Warlingham</a> in Surrey.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/1945_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="1945 in the United Kingdom">1945</a>, the London Power Company commemorated Swan by naming a new 1,554 <a href="/wiki/Gross_register_tonnage" title="Gross register tonnage">GRT</a> <a href="/wiki/Coastal_trading_vessel" title="Coastal trading vessel">coastal</a> <a href="/wiki/Collier_(ship)" title="Collier (ship)">collier</a> SS <i>Sir Joseph Swan</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-kenneth_swan_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kenneth_swan-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Burntisland_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burntisland-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Kenneth E.
