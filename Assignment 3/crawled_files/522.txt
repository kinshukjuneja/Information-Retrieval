Since the late-1990s, a system of devolution has emerged in the UK, under which Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> have each been granted some measure of self-government within the UK, which is often referred to as "devolved powers".\n</p><p>Scotland entered into a <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">political union</a> with <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 1707, and since then has sent representatives to the Palace of Westminster, which became the British parliament. Currently, <a href="/wiki/List_of_MPs_for_constituencies_in_Scotland_2010%E2%80%93" class="mw-redirect" title="List of MPs for constituencies in Scotland 2010\xe2\x80\x93">59 Members</a> of Parliament (MPs) represent Scottish constituencies at Westminster, and issues such as the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">constitution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Foreign relations of the United Kingdom">foreign affairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">defence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pensions" class="mw-redirect" title="Pensions">pensions</a>, issues of medical ethics, and fiscal, economic and monetary policy are decided on a nationwide UK level. In 1999, a 129-member <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> was established in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>; it has full power to make law in Scotland, except in <a href="/wiki/Reserved_matters_(Westminster)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reserved matters (Westminster)">reserved matters</a>. In the UK government, Scottish affairs are represented by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/David_Mundell" title="David Mundell">David Mundell</a> MP. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> is headed by a <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">First Minister</a>, who is the leader of the political party with the most support in the Scottish Parliament, currently <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Sturgeon" title="Nicola Sturgeon">Nicola Sturgeon</a> MSP. The <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> in Scotland is the <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">British monarch</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> (since 1952). There are six Members of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> elected by Scotland, as the UK is a member state of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.\n</p><p>Scotland can best be described as having a multi-party system. In the Scottish Parliament, the <a href="/wiki/Centre-left" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-left">centre-left</a> pro-independence <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> is the party which forms the devolved government; it currently holds a plurality of seats in the parliament (62 out of 129). Opposition parties include: the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservative_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Conservative Party">Scottish Conservative Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Centre-right" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-right">centre-right</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative</a>), <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Labour_Party" title="Scottish Labour Party">Scottish Labour Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Centre-left" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-left">centre-left</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democratic</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Scottish Liberal Democrats</a> (<a href="/wiki/Centrist" class="mw-redirect" title="Centrist">centrist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberal</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Centre-left" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-left">centre-left</a> to <a href="/wiki/Left-wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a>). Elections are held once every five years, with 73 Members being elected to represent constituencies, and the remaining 56 elected via a system of proportional representation. At Westminster, Scotland is represented by 35 MPs from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>, 13 from the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>, 7 MPs from the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> and 4 from the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>.\n</p><p>Today, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">the creation of an independent Scottish state outside the United Kingdom</a> remains a prominent political issue. On Thursday 18 September 2014, the Scottish electorate voted in a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">referendum on whether or not to become independent</a>, and opted to stay as part of the United Kingdom, with 55.3% voting against independence and 44.7% voting in favour of independence.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input Party">Scottish National Party</a> (SNP), which campaigns for <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a>. The current <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">First Minister of Scotland</a> is SNP leader <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Sturgeon" title="Nicola Sturgeon">Nicola Sturgeon</a>, who has led a government since November 2014. The previous First Minister, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Salmond" title="Alex Salmond">Alex Salmond</a>, led the SNP to an overall majority victory in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_general_election,_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Parliament general election, 2011">May 2011 general election</a>, which was then lost in 2016 and now forms a minority government. Other parties represented in the parliament are the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Labour_Party" title="Scottish Labour Party">Labour Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservative_and_Unionist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party">Conservative Party</a> which form the official opposition, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrats</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Next_Scottish_Parliament_election" title="Next Scottish Parliament election">next Scottish Parliament election</a> is due to be held in May 2021.\n</p><p>Under devolution, Scotland is represented by <a href="/wiki/Scottish_MPs" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish MPs">59 MPs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons of the United Kingdom</a> elected from territory-based <a href="/wiki/List_of_UK_Parliamentary_constituencies_in_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of UK Parliamentary constituencies in Scotland">Scottish constituencies</a>, out of a total of 650 MPs in the House of Commons. A <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a>, who prior to devolution headed the system of government in Scotland, sits in the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cabinet of the United Kingdom">Cabinet of the United Kingdom</a> and is responsible for the limited number of powers the office retains since devolution, as well as relations with other <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a> Ministers who have power over <a href="/wiki/Reserved_matters" class="mw-redirect" title="Reserved matters">reserved matters</a>. The Scottish Parliament can refer devolved matters back to Westminster to be considered as part of United Kingdom-wide legislation by passing a <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Consent_Motion" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative Consent Motion">Legislative Consent Motion</a> \xe2\x80\x94 usually referred to as a Sewel Motion. This has been done on a number of occasions where it has been seen as either more efficient, or more politically expedient to have the legislation considered by Westminster. The <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotland Office">Scotland Office</a> is a department of the Government of the United Kingdom, responsible for reserved Scottish affairs. The current Secretary of State for Scotland is <a href="/wiki/David_Mundell" title="David Mundell">David Mundell</a> MP, a <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a>. Until 1999, <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Scotland" title="Peerage of Scotland">Scottish peers</a> were entitled to sit in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.\n</p><p>The main political debate in Scotland tends to revolve around attitudes to the constitutional question. Under the pressure of growing support for Scottish independence, a policy of <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a> had been advocated by all three <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">GB</a>-wide parties to some degree during their history (although Labour and the Conservatives have also at times opposed it). This question dominated the Scottish political scene in the latter half of the twentieth century with <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> leader <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(Labour_Party_leader)" title="John Smith (Labour Party leader)">John Smith</a> describing the revival of a Scottish parliament as the "settled will of the Scottish people".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Now that devolution has occurred, the main argument about Scotland\'s constitutional status is over whether the Scottish Parliament should accrue additional powers (for example over <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_policy" title="Fiscal policy">fiscal policy</a>), or seek to obtain full <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a>. Ultimately the long term question is: should the Scottish parliament continue to be a subsidiary assembly created and potentially abolished by the constitutionally dominant and sovereign parliament of the United Kingdom (as in devolution) or should it have an independent existence as of right, with full sovereign powers (either through independence, a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federal</a> United Kingdom or a <a href="/wiki/Confederal" class="mw-redirect" title="Confederal">confederal</a> arrangement)? To clarify these issues, the SNP-led Scottish Executive published <i><a href="/wiki/Choosing_Scotland%27s_Future" title="Choosing Scotland&#39;s Future">Choosing Scotland\'s Future</a></i>, a consultation document directed to the electorate under the <a href="/wiki/National_Conversation" title="National Conversation">National Conversation</a> exercise.\n</p><p>The programmes of legislation enacted by the Scottish Parliament have seen the divergence in the provision of <a href="/wiki/Public_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Public services">public services</a> compared to the rest of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> While the costs of a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish universities">university</a> education, and <a href="/wiki/Care_services_for_the_elderly" class="mw-redirect" title="Care services for the elderly">care services for the elderly</a> are free at point of use in Scotland, fees are paid in the rest of the UK. Scotland was the first country in the UK to <a href="/wiki/Ban_smoking" class="mw-redirect" title="Ban smoking">ban smoking</a> in public places, with the ban effective from 26 March 2006. Also, on 19 October 2017, the Scottish government announced that smacking children as punishment was to be banned in Scotland, the first country of the UK to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Scottish_Parliament">The Scottish Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span government in 1997 was followed by a <a href="/wiki/Scotland_referendum,_1997" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotland referendum, 1997">referendum in Scotland</a> on establishing a devolved <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>. 74.3% of voters agreed with the establishment of the parliament and 63.5% agreed it should have tax-varying powers, which meant that it could adjust <a href="/wiki/Income_taxes" class="mw-redirect" title="Income taxes">income taxes</a> by up to 3%.<sup id="cite_ref-NY_Times_1997_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times_1997-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ScotlandReferendum_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScotlandReferendum-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Parliament was then created by the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Parliament">Parliament of the United Kingdom (Westminster Parliament)</a>. This Act sets out the subjects still dealt with at Westminster, referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Reserved_matters_(Westminster)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reserved matters (Westminster)">reserved matters</a></i>, including Defence, International Relations, Fiscal and Economic Policy, Drugs Law and Broadcasting. Anything <i>not</i> mentioned as a specific reserved matter is automatically devolved to Scotland, including <a href="/wiki/NHS_Scotland" title="NHS Scotland">health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Scotland" title="Education in Scotland">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_local_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish local government">local government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a> and all other issues. This is one of the key differences between the successful Scotland Act 1998 and the failed <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1978" title="Scotland Act 1978">Scotland Act 1978</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:148px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Donald_Dewar_First_Minister.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ee/Donald_Dewar_First_Minister.jpg" width="146" height="199" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="146" data-file-height="199" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Donald_Dewar_First_Minister.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Donald_Dewar" title="Donald Dewar">Donald Dewar</a> became the first <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">First Minister of Scotland</a> and first leader of a Scottish Government in 1999 since the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union" title="Treaty of Union">Treaty of Union</a> in 1707</div></div></div>\n<p>The Parliament is elected by a mixture of the <a href="/wiki/First_past_the_post" class="mw-redirect" title="First past the post">first past the post</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> electoral systems, namely, the <a href="/wiki/Additional_Members_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Additional Members System">additional members system</a>. Thus the Parliament is unlike the Westminster Parliament, which is elected solely by the <a href="/wiki/First_past_the_post" class="mw-redirect" title="First past the post">first past the post</a> method. The Scottish Parliament is elected every four years and has 129 members, referred to as <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Member of the Scottish Parliament">Members of the Scottish Parliament (MSPs)</a>. Of the 129 MSPs, 73 are elected to represent first past the post constituencies, whilst the remaining 56 are elected by the additional member system.\n</p><p>The proportional representation system has resulted in the election of a number of candidates from parties that would not have been expected to get representation through the first past the post system.\n</p><p>A devolved government called the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Executive">Scottish Executive</a> (renamed Scottish Government in 2007) was established along with the Scottish Parliament in 1999, with the <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">First Minister of Scotland</a> at its head. The secretariat of the Executive is part of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Civil Service">UK Civil Service</a> and the head of the Executive, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Permanent_Secretary_(Scotland)" title="Office of the Permanent Secretary (Scotland)">Permanent Secretary</a> (presently <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Evans" title="Leslie Evans">Leslie Evans</a>), is the equivalent of the Permanent Secretary of a <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a> department.\n</p>\n<div>\n\n&#32;\n<table Commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scotselectionreshuffle.svg" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scotselectionreshuffle.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The effect of the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_for_Scotland" general election, 2005">general election</a> upon Scottish seats</div></div></div>\n<p>Until the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 general election</a>, Scotland elected 72 <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MPs</a> from 72 single-member constituencies to serve in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>. As this over-represented Scotland in comparison to the other parts of the UK, Clause 81 of the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a> equalised the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> and Scottish electoral quota. As a result, the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_for_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission for Scotland">Boundary Commission for Scotland</a>\'s recommendations were adopted, reducing Scottish representation in the House of Commons to 59 MPs with effect from the 2005 general election. The necessary amendment to the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a>, was passed by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_(Constituencies)_Act_2004" title="Scottish Parliament (Constituencies) Act 2004">Scottish Parliament (Constituencies) Act 2004</a>. The previous over-representation was widely accepted before to allow for a greater Scottish voice in the Commons, but since the establishment of a Scottish Parliament it has been felt that this is not necessary.\n</p><p>Scottish MPs are elected at the same time as the rest of the UK\'s MPs.\n</p><p>Scotland was historically represented in the <a href="/wiki/UK_government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK government">UK government</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a>. This post was established in the 1880s but recently it has been the topic of much speculation. Many believe that since devolution there is no need for such a role. The Secretary of State\'s department, the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotland Office">Scotland Office</a>, created in 1999, liaises with other <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a> departments about devolution matters.\n</p><p>Current Scottish representation in the Commons is&#160;:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>: 35</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservative_and_Unionist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party">Conservative and Unionist Party</a>: 13</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Labour_Party" title="Scottish Labour Party">Labour Party</a>: 7</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrats</a>: 4</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_House_of_Lords">The House of Lords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The House of Lords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At one stage, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> peers were entitled to elect sixteen <a href="/wiki/Scottish_representative_peers" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish representative peers">representative peers</a> to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. In 1963, the <a href="/wiki/Peerage_Act_1963" title="Peerage Act 1963">Peerage Act</a> was passed, allowing every Scottish peer to sit in the House of Lords.  However, since the previous Labour government\'s reforms of that house this is no longer the case and hereditary Scottish peers have to stand for election from amongst all eligible peers to sit in the house as part of a group of 92 entitled to do so.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland_in_Europe">Scotland in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scotland in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Scotland constitutes a single <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> <a href="/wiki/Constituency" class="mw-redirect" title="Constituency">constituency</a>. See <a href="/wiki/Scotland_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="Scotland (European Parliament constituency)">Scotland (European Parliament constituency)</a>.\n</p><p>It is also represented in the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_the_Regions" class="mw-redirect" title="Committee of the Regions">Committee of the Regions</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">Local government in Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Scotland" title="Subdivisions of Scotland">Subdivisions of Scotland</a></div>\n<p>Local government in Scotland is organised into 32 <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authorities</a>.  Each <a href="/wiki/Local_authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Local authority">local authority</a> is <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governed</a> by a <i>council</i> consisting of elected <a href="/wiki/Councillor" title="Councillor">councillors</a>, who are elected every four years by registered voters in each of the <a href="/wiki/Council_Areas_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Council Areas of Scotland">council areas</a>.\n</p><p>Scottish councils co-operate through, and are represented collectively by, the <a href="/wiki/Convention_of_Scottish_Local_Authorities" title="Convention of Scottish Local Authorities">Convention of Scottish Local Authorities</a> (COSLA).\n</p><p>There are currently 1,222 councillors in total, each paid a part-time salary for the undertaking of their duties.  Each authority elects a <a href="/wiki/Convener" class="mw-redirect" title="Convener">Convener</a> or <a href="/wiki/Provost_(civil)" title="Provost (civil)">Provost</a> to chair meetings of the authority\'s council and act as a figurehead for the area. The four main cities of Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Aberdeen, Scotland">Aberdeen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dundee,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Dundee, Scotland">Dundee</a> have a <a href="/wiki/Lord_Provost" title="Lord Provost">Lord Provost</a> who is also, <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_officio" class="mw-redirect" title="Ex officio">ex officio</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lieutenant">Lord Lieutenant</a> for that city.\n</p><p>There are in total 32 councils, the largest being the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Glasgow" class="mw-redirect" title="City of Glasgow">City of Glasgow</a> with more than 600,000 inhabitants, the smallest, <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, with fewer than 20,000 people.  See <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Scotland" title="Subdivisions of Scotland">Subdivisions of Scotland</a> for a list of the council areas.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_councils">Community councils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Community councils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Community_council" title="Community council">Community council</a></div>\n<p>Community councils represent the interests of local people.  Local authorities have a statutory duty to consult community councils on planning, development and other issues directly affecting that local community.  However, the community council has no direct say in the delivery of services.  In many areas they do not function at all, but some work very effectively at improving their local area.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Elections for Community Councils are determined by the local authority and the law states that candidates cannot stand on a party-political ticket<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Scotland" title="History of Scotland">History of Scotland</a>; <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union,_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Union, 1707">Treaty of Union, 1707</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NationalistGraffiti.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/NationalistGraffiti.jpg/200px-NationalistGraffiti.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/NationalistGraffiti.jpg/300px-NationalistGraffiti.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/NationalistGraffiti.jpg/400px-NationalistGraffiti.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NationalistGraffiti.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pro-independence graffiti in Edinburgh</div></div></div>\n<p>Until 1832 Scottish politics remained very much in the control of landowners in the country, and of small cliques of merchants in the <a href="/wiki/Burgh" title="Burgh">burghs</a>. Agitation against this position through the <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_People_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Friends of the People Society">Friends of the People Society</a> in the 1790s met with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Macqueen,_Lord_Braxfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Macqueen, Lord Braxfield">Lord Braxfield</a>\'s explicit repression on behalf of the landed interests.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reform_Act_1832" title="Scottish Reform Act 1832">Scottish Reform Act 1832</a> rearranged the constituencies and increased the electorate from under 5,000 to 65,000.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Representation_of_the_People_(Scotland)_Act_1868" title="Representation of the People (Scotland) Act 1868">Representation of the People (Scotland) Act 1868</a> extended the electorate to 232,000 but with "residential qualifications peculiar to Scotland".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, by 1885 around 50% of the male population had the vote, the <a href="/wiki/Secret_ballot" title="Secret ballot">secret ballot</a> had become established, and the modern political era had started.\n</p><p>From 1885 to 1918 the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_party_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> almost totally dominated Scottish politics. Only in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1955" title="United Kingdom general election, 1955">general election</a> of 1955 did the <a href="/wiki/Unionist_Party_(Scotland)" title="Unionist Party (Scotland)">Unionist Party</a>, together with their <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(UK,_1931)" title="National Liberal Party (UK, 1931)">National Liberal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Unionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal Unionist">Liberal Unionist</a> allies, win a majority of votes.\n</p><p>In general, the Unionists achieved their best results in the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> area, due to the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Orange Institution">Orange</a> vote.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1918" title="United Kingdom general election, 1918">coupon election</a> of 1918, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1922" title="United Kingdom general election, 1922">1922</a> saw the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> as a major force. <a href="/wiki/Red_Clydeside" title="Red Clydeside">Red Clydeside</a> elected a number of Labour MPs. A <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Communist Party of Great Britain">communist</a> was elected for <a href="/wiki/Motherwell_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Motherwell (UK Parliament constituency)">Motherwell</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1924" title="United Kingdom general election, 1924">1924</a>, but in essence the 1920s saw a 3-way fight between Labour, the Liberals and the Unionists. The <a href="/wiki/National_Party_of_Scotland" title="National Party of Scotland">National Party of Scotland</a> first contested a seat in 1929. It merged with the centre-right <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Party" title="Scottish Party">Scottish Party</a> in 1934 to form the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>, but the SNP remained a peripheral force until the watershed <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_by-election,_1967" title="Hamilton by-election, 1967">Hamilton by-election</a> of 1967.\n</p><p>The Communists won <a href="/wiki/West_Fife_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="West Fife (UK Parliament constituency)">West Fife</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1935" title="United Kingdom general election, 1935">1935</a> and again in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1945" title="United Kingdom general election, 1945">1945</a> (<a href="/wiki/Willie_Gallacher_(politician)" title="Willie Gallacher (politician)">Willie Gallacher</a>) and several <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> Labour MPs joined the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Labour_Party" title="Independent Labour Party">Independent Labour Party</a> in the 1930s, often heavily defeating the official Labour candidates.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/UK_National_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK National Government">National Government</a> won the vast majority of Scottish seats in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1931" title="United Kingdom general election, 1931">1931</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1935" title="United Kingdom general election, 1935">1935</a>: the Liberal Party, banished to the <a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands" title="Highlands and Islands">Highlands and Islands</a>, no longer functioned as a significant force in <a href="/wiki/Central_Belt" title="Central Belt">central Scotland</a>.\n</p><p>In 1945, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">SNP</a> saw its first MP (<a href="/wiki/Robert_McIntyre" title="Robert McIntyre">Robert McIntyre</a>) elected at the <a href="/wiki/Motherwell_by-election,_1945" title="Motherwell by-election, 1945">Motherwell by-election</a>, but had little success during the following decade. The <a href="/wiki/Independent_Labour_Party" title="Independent Labour Party">ILP</a> members rejoined the Labour Party, and Scotland now had in effect a two-party system.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1950" title="United Kingdom general election, 1950">1950</a>: The Liberals won two seats - <a href="/wiki/Jo_Grimond" title="Jo Grimond">Jo Grimond</a> winning <a href="/wiki/Orkney_and_Shetland_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Orkney and Shetland (UK Parliament constituency)">Orkney and Shetland</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1951" title="United Kingdom general election, 1951">1951</a>: Labour and the Unionists won 35 seats each, the Liberals losing one seat.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1955" title="United Kingdom general election, 1955">1955</a>: The Unionists won a majority of both seats and votes. The SNP came second in <a href="/wiki/Perth_and_Kinross_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Perth and Kinross (UK Parliament constituency)">Perth and Kinross</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1959" title="United Kingdom general election, 1959">1959</a>: In contrast to England, Scotland swung to Labour, which scored four gains at the expense of the Unionists. This marked the start of a trend which in less than 40 years saw the Unionists\' Scottish representation at Westminster reduced to zero. This was the last occasion when the Unionists won in Scotland: their merger with the Conservative Party of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> in 1965, to become the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservative_and_Unionist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party">Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party</a>, began a long, steady decline in their support.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1964" title="United Kingdom general election, 1964">1964</a>: There was a substantial swing to Labour, giving them 44 of Scotland\'s 71 seats. The Liberals won four seats, all in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a>.</li>\n<li>1965: <a href="/wiki/David_Steel" title="David Steel">David Steel</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Roxburgh,_Selkirk_and_Peebles_by-election,_1965" title="Roxburgh, Selkirk and Peebles by-election, 1965">Roxburgh, Selkirk and Peebles by-election</a> for the Liberals.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1966" title="United Kingdom general election, 1966">1966</a>: Labour gained 2 more seats and the Liberals made a net gain of 1. The SNP garnered over 100,000 votes and finished second in 3 seats.</li>\n<li>1967: The SNP did well in the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Pollok_by-election,_1967" title="Glasgow Pollok by-election, 1967">Glasgow Pollok by-election</a>, but this allowed the Conservative and Unionist candidate to win. However, in the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_by-election,_1967" title="Hamilton by-election, 1967">Hamilton by-election</a> <a href="/wiki/Winnie_Ewing" title="Winnie Ewing">Winnie Ewing</a> won a sensational victory.</li>\n<li>1968: The SNP made substantial gains in local elections.</li>\n<li>1970: The SNP performed poorly in local elections and in the <a href="/wiki/South_Ayrshire_by-election,_1970" title="South Ayrshire by-election, 1970">Ayrshire South by-election</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1970" title="United Kingdom general election, 1970">general election</a> saw a small swing to the Conservatives &amp; Unionists, but Labour won a majority of seats in Scotland. The SNP made little progress in central Scotland, but took votes from the Liberals in the Highlands and in north east Scotland, and won the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>.</li>\n<li>1971-1973: The SNP did well in by-elections, <a href="/wiki/Margo_MacDonald" title="Margo MacDonald">Margo MacDonald</a> winning <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Govan_by-election,_1973" title="Glasgow Govan by-election, 1973">Glasgow Govan</a>.</li>\n<li>1974: In the two general elections of 1974 (in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_February_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, February 1974">February</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_October_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, October 1974">October</a>) the SNP won 7 and then 11 seats, their share of the vote rising from 11% in 1970 to 22% and then 30%. With the Labour Party winning the October election by a narrow margin, the SNP appeared in a strong position.</li>\n<li>1974-1979: Devolution dominated this period: the Labour government attempted to steer through devolution legislation, based on the recommendations of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Kilbrandon Commission</a>, against strong opposition, not least from its own backbenchers. Finally a <a href="/wiki/Scotland_referendum,_1979" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotland referendum, 1979">referendum</a>, whilst producing a small majority in favour of an elected <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Assembly" title="Scottish Assembly">Scottish Assembly</a>, failed to achieve a turnout of 40% of the total electorate, a condition set in the legislation. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">1979 general election</a> the SNP fared poorly, falling to 17% of the vote and 2 seats. Labour did well in Scotland, but in the United Kingdom as a whole <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> led the Conservatives to a decisive victory.</li>\n<li>1979-1983: The SNP suffered severe splits as the result of the 1979 drop in support. Labour also was riven by internal strife as the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Social Democratic Party</a> split away. Despite this, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1983" title="United Kingdom general election, 1983">1983 election</a> still saw Labour remain the majority party in Scotland, with a smaller swing to the Conservatives than in England. The SNP\'s vote declined further, to 11%, although it won two seats.</li>\n<li>1987: The Labour Party did well in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1987" title="United Kingdom general election, 1987">1987 election</a>, mainly at the expense of the Conservatives &amp; Unionists, who were reduced to their smallest number of Scottish seats since before <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The SNP made a small but significant advance.</li>\n<li>1988: <a href="/wiki/Jim_Sillars" title="Jim Sillars">Jim Sillars</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Govan_by-election,_1988" title="Glasgow Govan by-election, 1988">Glasgow Govan by-election</a> for the SNP.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1992" title="United Kingdom general election, 1992">1992</a>: This election proved a disappointment for Labour and the SNP in Scotland. The SNP went from 14% to 21% of the vote but won only 3 seats. The Conservative and Unionist vote did not collapse, as had been widely predicted, leading to claims that their resolutely anti-devolution stance had paid dividends.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997</a>: In common with England, there was a Labour landslide in Scotland. The SNP doubled their number of MPs to 6, but the Conservatives &amp; Unionists failed to win a single seat. Unlike 1979, Scottish voters delivered a decisive "Yes" vote in the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_referendum,_1997" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotland referendum, 1997">referendum</a> on establishing a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>.</li>\n<li>1999: The Scottish Parliament was established. A coalition of Labour and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrats</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Donald_Dewar" title="Donald Dewar">Donald Dewar</a> took power.</li>\n<li>2007: The SNP became Scotland\'s largest party in the <a href="/wiki/2007_Scottish_Parliamentary_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2007 Scottish Parliamentary election">2007 Scottish Parliamentary election</a> and formed a minority government.</li>\n<li>2008: <a href="/wiki/John_Mason_(Scottish_politician)" title="John Mason (Scottish politician)">John Mason</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_East_by-election" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow East by-election">Glasgow East by-election</a> for the SNP.</li>\n<li>2008: <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Roy" title="Lindsay Roy">Lindsay Roy</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Glenrothes_by-election" class="mw-redirect" title="Glenrothes by-election">Glenrothes by-election</a> for Labour with an increased share of the vote and a 6,737 majority over the SNP.</li>\n<li>2009: <a href="/wiki/Willie_Bain" title="Willie Bain">Willie Bain</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_North_East_by-election" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow North East by-election">Glasgow North East by-election</a> for Labour with 59.4% of the vote and an 8,111 majority over the SNP.</li>\n<li>2010: <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">United Kingdom general election, 2010</a>: Labour won 41 out of 59 Scottish seats including <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_East" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow East">Glasgow East</a> from the SNP and received over 1 million votes across Scotland, despite losing 91 seats across Britain as a whole.</li>\n<li>2011: The SNP become the first party to win an overall majority in the Scottish Parliament.</li>\n<li>2015: The SNP won 56 out of 59 Scottish seats, winning 50% of the popular vote. Labour, the Conservatives and the Liberal Democrats won 1 seat each.</li>\n<li>2016: The SNP lost their parliamentary majority, winning 63 seats. The Conservatives became the second-largest party with 31 seats, pushing Labour into third place.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_parties">Political parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Scotland" title="List of political parties in Scotland">List of political parties in Scotland</a></div>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. The SNP was formed in 1934 with the aim of achieving <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a>. They are broadly <a href="/wiki/Centre-left" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-left">centre-left</a> and are in the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social-democratic</a> mould. They are the largest party in the Scottish Parliament and have formed the Scottish Government since the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_election,_2007" title="Scottish Parliament election, 2007">2007 Scottish Parliament election</a>.\n</p><p>In the course of the twentieth century, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Labour">Scottish Labour</a> rose to prominence as Scotland\'s main political force.  The party was established to represent the interests of workers and trade unionists. From 1999 to 2007, they operated as the senior partners in a coalition Scottish Executive. They lost power in 2007 when the SNP won a plurality of seats and entered a period of dramatic decline, losing all but one of their seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015 UK election</a> and falling to third place in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_election,_2016" title="Scottish Parliament election, 2016">2016 Scottish election</a>. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=2017_UK_election&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="2017 UK election (page does not exist)">2017 UK election</a> produced a mixed result for the party as it gained six seat and increased its vote by 2.8% but the party came in third behind the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">SNP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservatives" title="Scottish Conservatives">Scottish Conservatives</a>. \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Scottish Liberal Democrats</a> were the junior partners in the 1999 to 2007 coalition Scottish Executive. The party has lost much of its electoral presence in Scotland since the UK Liberal Democrats entered into a coalition government with the UK Conservative Party in 2010. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015 UK election</a> they were reduced from 12 seats to one seat, and since the 2016 Scottish Parliament election they have had the fifth highest number of MSPs (five, unchanged on <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_election,_2011" title="Scottish Parliament election, 2011">2011</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Unionist_Party_(Scotland)" title="Unionist Party (Scotland)">Unionist Party</a> was the only party ever to have achieved an outright majority of Scottish votes at any general election, in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1951" title="United Kingdom general election, 1951">1951</a> (they only won a majority if the votes if their <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(UK,_1931)" title="National Liberal Party (UK, 1931)">National Liberal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Unionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal Unionist">Liberal Unionist</a> allies are included). The Unionist Party was allied with the UK Conservative Party until 1965, when the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservative_and_Unionist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party">Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party</a> was formed. The Conservatives then entered a long-term decline in Scotland, culminating in their failure to win any Scottish seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997 UK election</a>. At the four subsequent UK elections (<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015</a>) the Conservatives won only one Scottish seat. The party enjoyed a revival of fortunes in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_election,_2016" title="Scottish Parliament election, 2016">2016 Scottish Parliament election</a>, winning 31 seats and finishing in second place.  The Conservatives are a <a href="/wiki/Centre-right" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-right">centre-right</a> party.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a> have won <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_regions" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Parliament regions">regional</a> <a href="/wiki/Additional_member_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Additional member system">additional member</a> seats in every Scottish Parliament election, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> electoral system. They won one MSP in 1999, increased their total to seven at the 2003 election but saw this drop back to 2 at the 2007 election. They retained two seats at the 2011 election, then increased this total to six in the 2016 election. The Greens support <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Socialist_Party" title="Scottish Socialist Party">Scottish Socialist Party</a> (SSP) were formed in 1998<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Socialist_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Socialist Alliance">Scottish Socialist Alliance</a> which had aimed to unite the <a href="/wiki/Left-wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  They won one MSP in 1999 and increased their total to six at the 2003 election. The SSP split in 2006 when two MSPs and a large number of activists left to form <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Scotland)" title="Solidarity (Scotland)">Solidarity</a>. Both parties lost all their seats at the 2007 election, but the SSP continues to have local representation.  Both the SSP and <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Scotland)" title="Solidarity (Scotland)">Solidarity</a> support <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Senior_Citizens_Unity_Party" title="Scottish Senior Citizens Unity Party">Scottish Senior Citizens Unity Party</a> (SSCUP) were formed just in time to contest the <a href="/wiki/2003_elections_to_the_Scottish_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="2003 elections to the Scottish Parliament">2003 elections to the Scottish Parliament</a>. They were formed to work for the rights of Scotland\'s senior citizens. Thanks to the Scottish Parliament\'s proportional electoral system, they managed to get one MSP elected in 2003, <a href="/wiki/John_Swinburne_(Member_of_Scottish_Parliament)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Swinburne (Member of Scottish Parliament)">John Swinburne</a>, their party founder and leader.  In the 2007 election they lost their only seat.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
