For a guide to IPA symbols, see <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">Help:IPA</a>.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Middle English</b> (<b>ME</b>) is a period when the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>, spoken after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> (1066) until the late 15th century, underwent distinct variations and developments following the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> period. Scholarly opinion varies but the Oxford English Dictionary specifies the period of 1150 to 1500.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This stage of the development of the English language roughly followed the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>.\n</p><p>Middle English developed from Late <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, and saw significant changes to its grammar, pronunciation, and <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>. Writing conventions during the Middle English period varied widely. Examples of writing from this period that have survived show extensive regional variation. The more standardized Old English language became fragmented, localized, and was for the most part, being improvised.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> By the end of the period (about 1470) and aided by the <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">invention of the printing press</a> by Johannes Gensfleisch zur Laden zum Gutenberg in 1439, a standard based on the London dialect (Chancery Standard) had become established. This largely formed the basis for Modern English spelling, although pronunciation has changed considerably since that time. Middle English was succeeded in England by the era of <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a>, which lasted until about 1650. <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Scots_language" title="History of the Scots language">Scots language</a> developed concurrently from a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbrian dialect">Northumbrian dialect</a> (prevalent in northern England and spoken in southeast <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>).\n</p><p>During the Middle English period, many Old English grammatical features either became simplified or disappeared altogether. Noun, adjective and verb <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflections</a> were simplified by the reduction (and eventual elimination) of most <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">grammatical case</a> distinctions. Middle English also saw considerable adoption of <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Norman French</a> vocabulary, especially in the areas of politics, law, the arts and religion. Conventional English vocabulary retained its mostly Germanic <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">etiology</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> influences becoming more apparent. Significant changes in pronunciation took place, particularly involving long vowels and diphthongs which in the later Middle English period, began to undergo the <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a>.\n</p><p>Little survives of early <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_literature" title="Middle English literature">Middle English literature</a>, due in part to Norman domination and the prestige that came with writing in French rather than English. During the 14th century, a new style of literature emerged with the works of writers including <a href="/wiki/John_Wycliffe" title="John Wycliffe">John Wycliffe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>, whose <i><a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Tales" class="mw-redirect" title="Canterbury Tales">Canterbury Tales</a></i> remains the one of most studied and read works of the period.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Transition from Late <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> to Early Middle English occurred in the latter part of the 11th century.\n</p><p>The influence of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> aided the development of English from a <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic language</a> with relatively free word order, to a more <a href="/wiki/Analytic_language" title="Analytic language">analytic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Isolating_language" title="Isolating language">isolating</a> language with a more strict word order.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Both Old English and Old Norse (as well as the contemporary antecedents of the latter, <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>) were synthetic languages with complicated inflections. The eagerness of <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a> to communicate with their southern Anglo-Saxon neighbors resulted in the erosion of inflection in both languages.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Old Norse may have had a more profound impact on Middle and Modern English development than any other language.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Simeon Potter notes: "No less far-reaching was the influence of Scandinavian upon the inflexional endings of English in hastening that wearing away and leveling of grammatical forms which gradually spread from north to south. It was, after all, a salutary influence. The gain was greater than the loss. There was a gain in directness, in clarity, and in strength".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Viking influence on <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> is most apparent in the more indispensable elements of the language. Pronouns, modals, comparatives, pronominal adverbs (like "hence" and "together"), conjunctions and prepositions, show the most marked Danish influence. The best evidence of Scandinavian influence appears in extensive word borrowings, yet no texts exist in either Scandinavia or in Northern England from this period to give certain evidence of an influence on syntax. The change to Old English from Old Norse was substantive, pervasive, and of a democratic character.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Like close cousins, Old Norse and Old English resembled each other, and with some words in common, they roughly understood each other;<sup id="cite_ref-:2_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> in time the inflections melted away and the analytic pattern emerged.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It is most "important to recognise that in many words the English and Scandinavian language differed chiefly in their inflectional elements. The body of the word was so nearly the same in the two languages that only the endings would put obstacles in the way of mutual understanding. In the mixed population which existed in the Danelaw these endings must have led to much confusion, tending gradually to become obscured and finally lost." This blending of peoples and languages happily resulted in "simplifying English grammar."<sup id="cite_ref-:3_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While the influence of Scandinavian languages was strongest in the dialects of the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a> region and Scotland, words in the spoken language emerge in the tenth and eleventh centuries near the transition from the Old to Middle English. Influence on the written language only appeared at the beginning of the thirteenth century, likely because of a scarcity of literary texts from an earlier date.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a> in 1066 saw the replacement of the top levels of the English-speaking political and ecclesiastical hierarchies by <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> rulers who spoke a dialect of <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Old_Norman" title="Old Norman">Old Norman</a>, which developed in England into <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a>. The use of Norman as the preferred language of literature and polite discourse fundamentally  altered the role of Old English in education and administration, even though many Normans of this period were illiterate and depended on the clergy for written communication and record-keeping. A significant number of words of French origin began to appear in the English language alongside native English words of similar meaning, giving rise to such Modern English synonyms as <i><a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pig</a>/<a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a>/<a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calf_(animal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Calf (animal)">calf</a>/<a href="/wiki/Veal" title="Veal">veal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cow" class="mw-redirect" title="Cow">cow</a>/<a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mutton" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutton">mutton</a>, wood/<a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>, house/<a href="/wiki/Mansion" title="Mansion">mansion</a>, worthy/valuable, bold/courageous, freedom/<a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">liberty</a>, sight/vision, eat/dine</i>.\n</p><p>The role of Anglo-Norman as the language of government and law can be seen in the abundance of Modern English words for the mechanisms of government that are derived from Anglo-Norman: <i><a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">court</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judge</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Jury" title="Jury">jury</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a></i>. There are also many Norman-derived terms relating to the <a href="/wiki/Chivalric" class="mw-redirect" title="Chivalric">chivalric</a> cultures that arose in the 12th century; an era of <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crusading" class="mw-redirect" title="Crusading">crusading</a>.\n</p><p>Words were often taken from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, usually through French transmission. This gave rise to various synonyms including <i>kingly</i> (inherited from Old English), <i>royal</i> (from French, which inherited it from Vulgar Latin), and <i>regal</i> (from French, which borrowed it from classical Latin). Later French appropriations were derived from standard, rather than Norman French. Examples of resultant cognate pairs include the words <i>warden</i> (from Norman), and <i>guardian</i> (from later French; both share a common Germanic ancestor).\n</p><p>The end of Anglo-Saxon rule did not result in immediate changes to the language. The general population would have spoken the same <a href="/wiki/Old_English_dialects" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English dialects">dialects</a> as they had before the Conquest. Once the writing of Old English came to an end, Middle English had no standard language, only dialects that derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_dialects" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English dialects">dialects of the same regions</a> in the Anglo-Saxon period.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Middle_English">Early Middle English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early Middle English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Early Middle English (1150\xe2\x80\x931300)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> has a largely Anglo-Saxon vocabulary (with <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_influence_in_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Scandinavian influence in English">many Norse borrowings</a> in the northern parts of the country), but a greatly simplified <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflectional</a> system. The grammatical relations that were expressed in Old English by the <a href="/wiki/Dative" class="mw-redirect" title="Dative">dative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">instrumental cases</a> are replaced in Early Middle English with <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">prepositional</a> constructions. The Old English <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a> <i>-es</i> survives in the <i>-\'s</i> of the modern <a href="/wiki/English_possessive" title="English possessive">English possessive</a>, but most of the other case endings disappeared in the Early Middle English period, including most of the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_declension#Definite_articles_and_demonstratives" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English declension">roughly one dozen forms</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Definite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Definite article">definite article</a> ("the"). The <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a> personal pronouns (denoting exactly two) also disappeared from English during this period.\n</p><p>Gradually, the wealthy and the government <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">Anglicised</a> again, although Norman (and subsequently <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">French</a>) remained the dominant language of literature and law until the 14th century, even after the loss of the majority of the continental possessions of the <a href="/wiki/English_monarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="English monarchy">English monarchy</a>. The loss of case endings was part of a general trend from inflections to fixed <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> that also occurred in other Germanic languages, and therefore cannot be attributed simply to the influence of French-speaking sections of the population: English did, after all, remain the <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a>. It is also argued<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> that Norse immigrants to England had a great impact on the loss of inflectional endings in Middle English. One argument is that, although Norse- and English-speakers were somewhat comprehensible to each other due to similar morphology, the Norse-speakers\' inability to reproduce the ending sounds of English words influenced Middle English\'s loss of inflectional endings.\n</p><p>Important texts for the reconstruction of the evolution of Middle English out of Old English are the <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Chronicle" title="Peterborough Chronicle">Peterborough Chronicle</a>, which continued to be compiled up to 1154; the <i><a href="/wiki/Ormulum" title="Ormulum">Ormulum</a></i>, a biblical commentary probably composed in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> in the second half of the 12th century, incorporating a unique phonetic spelling system; and the <i><a href="/wiki/Ancrene_Wisse" title="Ancrene Wisse">Ancrene Wisse</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Group" title="Katherine Group">Katherine Group</a>, religious texts written for <a href="/wiki/Anchoress" class="mw-redirect" title="Anchoress">anchoresses</a>, apparently in the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(region)" title="West Midlands (region)">West Midlands</a> in the early 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The language found in the last two works is sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/AB_language" title="AB language">AB language</a>.\n</p><p>More literary sources of the twelfth and thirteenth centuries include <i><a href="/wiki/Layamon%27s_Brut" title="Layamon&#39;s Brut">Lawman\'s Brut</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Owl_and_the_Nightingale" title="The Owl and the Nightingale">The Owl and the Nightingale</a></i>.\n</p><p>Some scholars<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> have defined "Early Middle English" as encompassing English texts up to 1350. This longer time frame would extend the corpus to include many Middle English Romances (especially those of the <i><a href="/wiki/Auchinleck_manuscript" title="Auchinleck manuscript">Auchinleck manuscript</a></i> ca. 1330).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="14th_century">14th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 14th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From around the early 14th century there was significant migration into <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, particularly from the counties of the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East Midlands</a>, and a new <a href="/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" title="Prestige (sociolinguistics)">prestige</a> London dialect began to develop, based chiefly on the speech of the East Midlands, but also influenced by that of other regions.<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The writing of this period, however, continues to reflect a variety of regional forms of English. The <i><a href="/wiki/Ayenbite_of_Inwyt" title="Ayenbite of Inwyt">Ayenbite of Inwyt</a></i>, a translation of a French confessional prose work, completed in 1340, is written in a <a href="/wiki/Kentish_dialect" title="Kentish dialect">Kentish dialect</a>. The best known writer of Middle English, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>, wrote in the second half of the 14th century in the emerging London dialect, although he also portrays some of his characters as speaking in northern dialects, as in the "<a href="/wiki/Reeve%27s_Tale" class="mw-redirect" title="Reeve&#39;s Tale">Reeve\'s Tale</a>".\n</p><p>In the English-speaking areas of lowland <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, an independent standard was developing, based on the <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbrian dialect">Northumbrian dialect</a>. This would develop into what came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots language</a>.\n</p><p><span id="Chancery_Standard"></span>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Middle_English">Late Middle English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Late Middle English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <b>Chancery Standard</b> of written English emerged <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1430 in official documents that, since the Norman Conquest, had normally been written in French.<sup><a href="/wiki/Middle_English#cite_note-Wright-16" title="Middle English">[16]</a></sup> Like Chaucer\'s work, this new standard was based on the East-Midlands-influenced speech of London. Clerks using this standard were usually familiar with <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, influencing the forms they chose. The Chancery Standard, which was adopted slowly, was used in England by bureaucrats for most official purposes, excluding those of the Church and legalities, which used Latin and <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">Law French</a> (and some Latin), respectively.<sup><a href="/wiki/Middle_English#cite_note-Nevalainen2008-17" title="Middle English">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Chancery Standard\'s influence on later forms of written English is disputed, but it did undoubtedly provide the core around which <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a> formed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Early Modern English emerged with the help of <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">William Caxton</a>\'s printing press, developed during the 1470s. The press stabilized English through a push towards standardization, led by Chancery Standard enthusiast and writer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pynson" title="Richard Pynson">Richard Pynson</a>.<sup><a href="/wiki/Middle_English#cite_note-18" title="Middle English">[18]</a></sup> Early Modern English officially<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2018)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> began in the 1540s after the printing and wide distribution of the <a href="/wiki/English_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="English Bible">English Bible</a> and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Prayer Book</a>, which made the new standard of English publicly recognizable, and lasted until about 1650.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_phonology" title="Middle English phonology">Middle English phonology</a></div>\n<p>The main changes between the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_phonology" title="Old English phonology">Old English sound system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_phonology" title="Middle English phonology">that of Middle English</a> include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Emergence of the voiced <a href="/wiki/Fricative" class="mw-redirect" title="Fricative">fricatives</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/, /\xc3\xb0/, /z/</span> as separate <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemes</a>, rather than mere <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophones</a> of the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">voiceless</a> fricatives.</li>\n<li>Reduction of the Old English <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongs</a> to monophthongs, and the emergence of new diphthongs due to vowel breaking in certain positions, change of Old English post-vocalic <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/j/, /w/</span> (sometimes resulting from the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xa3]</span> allophone of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xa1/</span>) to offglides, and borrowing from French.</li>\n<li>Merging of Old English <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc3\xa6/ and /\xc9\x91/</span> into a single vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/a/</span>.</li>\n<li>Raising of the long vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc3\xa6\xcb\x90/</span> to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\x9b\xcb\x90/</span>, and (in the south) raising and rounding of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\x91\xcb\x90/</span> to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\x94\xcb\x90/</span>.</li>\n<li>Unrounding of the front <a href="/wiki/Rounded_vowel" class="mw-redirect" title="Rounded vowel">rounded vowels</a> in most dialects.</li>\n<li>Lengthening of vowels in <a href="/wiki/Open_syllable" class="mw-redirect" title="Open syllable">open syllables</a> (and in certain other positions). The resultant long vowels (and other pre-existing long vowels) subsequently underwent changes of quality in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a>, which began during the later Middle English period.</li>\n<li>Loss of <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">gemination</a> (double consonants came to be pronounced as single ones).</li>\n<li>Loss of weak final vowels (<a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a>, written &#x27e8;e&#x27e9;). By <a href="/wiki/Chaucer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaucer">Chaucer</a>\'s time this vowel was silent in normal speech, although it was normally pronounced in verse as the <a href="/wiki/Meter_(verse)" class="mw-redirect" title="Meter (verse)">meter</a> required (much as occurs in modern <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>). Also, non-final unstressed &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; was dropped when adjacent to only a single consonant on either side if there was another short &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; in an adjoining syllable. Thus, <i>every</i> began to be pronounced as "evry", and <i>palmeres</i> as "palmers".</li></ul>\n<p>The combination of the last three processes listed above led to the spelling conventions associated with <a href="/wiki/Silent_e" title="Silent e">silent &#x27e8;e&#x27e9;</a> and <a href="/wiki/Double_letter" class="mw-redirect" title="Double letter">doubled consonants</a> (see under <a href="#Orthography">Orthography</a>, below).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nouns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Middle English retains only two distinct noun-ending patterns from the more complex system of <a href="/wiki/Old_English_grammar#Nouns" title="Old English grammar">inflection in Old English</a>. The Early Middle English nouns <i>engel</i> ("angel") and <i>name</i> ("name") demonstrate the two patterns:\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Strong (engel)\n</th>\n<th>Singular\n</th>\n<th>Plural\n</th>\n<th>Weak (name)\n</th>\n<th>Singular\n</th>\n<th>Plural\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>Nominative\n</th>\n<td rowspan="3">engel\n</td>\n<td rowspan="2">engles\n</td>\n<th>Nominative\n</th>\n<td rowspan="2">name\n</td>\n<td rowspan="2">namen\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>Accusative\n</th>\n<th>Accusative\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>Dative\n</th>\n<td>englen/englem\n</td>\n<th>Dative\n</th>\n<td rowspan="2">namen\n</td>\n<td>namen/namem\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>Genitive\n</th>\n<td>engles\n</td>\n<td>engle\n</td>\n<th>Genitive\n</th>\n<td>namene\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Some nouns of the <i>engel</i> type have an <i>-e</i> in the nominative/accusative singular, like the weak declension, but otherwise strong endings. Often these are the same nouns that had an <i>-e</i> in the nominative/accusative singular of Old English (they, in turn, were inherited from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> <i>ja</i>-stem and <i>i</i>-stem nouns.)\n</p><p>The distinct dative case was lost in early Middle English. The genitive survived, however, but by the end of the Middle English period, only the strong <i>-\'s</i> ending (variously spelt) was in use.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The strong <i>-(e)s</i> plural form has survived into Modern English. The weak <i>-(e)n</i> form is now rare and used only in <i>oxen</i> and, as part of a <a href="/wiki/Double_plural" title="Double plural">double plural</a>, in <i>children</i> and <i>brethren</i>. Some dialects still have forms such as <i>eyen</i> (for <i>eyes</i>), <i>shoon</i> (for <i>shoes</i>), <i>hosen</i> (for <i>hose(s)</i>), <i>kine</i> (for <i>cows</i>), and <i>been</i> (for <i>bees</i>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">Pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pronouns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Middle English <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">personal pronouns</a> were mostly developed from <a href="/wiki/Old_English_pronouns" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English pronouns">those of Old English</a>, with the exception of the third-person plural, a borrowing from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> (the original Old English form clashed with the third person singular and was eventually dropped). Also, the nominative form of the feminine third-person singular was replaced by a form of the <a href="/wiki/Demonstrative" title="Demonstrative">demonstrative</a> that developed into <i>sche</i> (modern <i>she</i>), but the alternative <i>heyr</i> remained in some areas for a long time.\n</p><p>As with nouns, there was some inflectional simplification (the distinct Old English <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a> forms were lost), but pronouns, unlike nouns, retained distinct nominative and accusative forms. Third-person pronouns also retained a distinction between accusative and dative forms, but that was gradually lost: the masculine <i>hine</i> was replaced by <i>him</i> south of the Thames by the early 14th century, and the neuter dative <i>him</i> was ousted by <i>it</i> in most dialects by the 15th.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The following table shows some of the various Middle English pronouns, together with their modern (in quotation marks) and (sometimes) Old English equivalents. (<i><a href="/wiki/Thorn_(letter)" title="Thorn (letter)">\xc3\xbe</a></i> (the letter \'thorn\') is pronounced like the unvoiced <i>th</i> in "think", but, under certain circumstances, it may be like the voiced <i>th</i> in "that"). about 1200 and Northern forms using <i>-es</i> in the third person singular as well as the plural.<sup id="cite_ref-cambridge_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The past tense of weak verbs is formed by adding an <i>-ed(e)</i>, <i>-d(e)</i> or <i>-t(e)</i> ending. The past-tense forms, without their personal endings, also serve as past participles with past-participle prefixes derived from Old English: <i>i-</i>, <i>y-</i> and sometimes <i>bi-</i>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Germanic_strong_verb" title="Germanic strong verb">Strong verbs</a>, by contrast, form their past tense by changing their stem vowel (<i>binden</i> becomes <i>bound</i>, a process called <a href="/wiki/Apophony" title="Apophony">apophony</a>), as in Modern English.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Orthography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With the discontinuation of the <a href="/wiki/Late_West_Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Late West Saxon">Late West Saxon</a> standard used for the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_orthography" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English orthography">writing of Old English</a> in the period prior to the Norman Conquest, Middle English came to be written in a wide variety of scribal forms, reflecting different regional dialects and orthographic conventions. Later in the Middle English period, however, and particularly with the development of the <a href="#Chancery_Standard">Chancery Standard</a> in the 15th century, orthography became relatively standardised in a form based on the East Midlands-influenced speech of London. Spelling at the time was mostly quite <a href="/wiki/Regular_spelling" class="mw-redirect" title="Regular spelling">regular</a> (there was a fairly consistent correspondence between letters and sounds). The irregularity of <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">present-day English orthography</a> is largely due to <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_English" title="Phonological history of English">pronunciation changes</a> that have taken place over the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a> eras.\n</p><p>Middle English generally did not have <a href="/wiki/Silent_letter" title="Silent letter">silent letters</a>. For example, <i>knight</i> was pronounced <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[\xcb\x88kni\xc3\xa7t]</a></span> (with both the &#x27e8;k&#x27e9; and the &#x27e8;gh&#x27e9; pronounced, the latter sounding as the &#x27e8;ch&#x27e9; in German <i>Knecht</i>). The major exception was the <a href="/wiki/Silent_e" title="Silent e">silent &#x27e8;e&#x27e9;</a> \xe2\x80\x93 originally pronounced, but lost in normal speech by Chaucer\'s time. This letter, however, came to indicate a lengthened \xe2\x80\x93 and later also modified \xe2\x80\x93 pronunciation of a preceding vowel. For example, in <i>name</i>, originally pronounced as two syllables, the /a/ in the first syllable (originally an open syllable) lengthened, the final weak vowel was later dropped, and the remaining long vowel was modified in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a> (for these sound changes, see under <a href="#Phonology">Phonology</a>, above). The final &#x27e8;e&#x27e9;, now silent, thus became the indicator of the longer and changed pronunciation of &#x27e8;a&#x27e9;. In fact vowels could have this lengthened and modified pronunciation in various positions, particularly before a single consonant letter and another vowel, or before certain pairs of consonants.\n</p><p>A related convention involved the doubling of consonant letters to show that the preceding vowel was not to be lengthened. In some cases the double consonant represented a sound that was (or had previously been) <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">geminated</a>, i.e. had genuinely been "doubled" (and would thus have regularly blocked the lengthening of the preceding vowel). In other cases, by analogy, the consonant was written double merely to indicate the lack of lengthening.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabet">Alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alphabet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The basic <a href="/wiki/Old_English_Latin_alphabet" title="Old English Latin alphabet">Old English Latin alphabet</a> had consisted of 20 standard letters (there was not yet a distinct <i>j</i>, <i>v</i> or <i>w</i>, and Old English scribes did not generally use <i>k</i>, <i>q</i> or <i>z</i>) plus four additional letters: <a href="/wiki/Ash_(letter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ash (letter)">ash</a> &#x27e8;\xc3\xa6&#x27e9;, <a href="/wiki/Eth" title="Eth">eth</a> &#x27e8;\xc3\xb0&#x27e9;, <a href="/wiki/Thorn_(letter)" title="Thorn (letter)">thorn</a> &#x27e8;\xc3\xbe&#x27e9; and <a href="/wiki/Wynn" title="Wynn">wynn</a> &#x27e8;\xc6\xbf&#x27e9;.\n</p><p>Ash was no longer required in Middle English, as the Old English vowel /\xc3\xa6/ that it represented had <a href="#Phonology">merged into /a/</a>. The symbol nonetheless came to be used as a <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">ligature</a> for the digraph &#x27e8;ae&#x27e9; in many words of Greek or Latin origin, as did <a href="/wiki/%C5%92" title="\xc5\x92">\xc5\x93</a> for &#x27e8;oe&#x27e9;.\n</p><p>Eth and thorn both represented <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb8/</span> in Old English. Eth fell out of use during the 13th century and was replaced by thorn. Thorn mostly fell out of use during the 14th century, and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Th_(digraph)" title="Th (digraph)">&#x27e8;th&#x27e9;</a>. (Anachronistic usage of the scribal abbreviation <a href="/wiki/File:EME_ye.svg" class="image"><img alt="EME ye.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/EME_ye.svg/10px-EME_ye.svg.png" width="10" height="23" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/EME_ye.svg/15px-EME_ye.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/EME_ye.svg/20px-EME_ye.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="9" data-file-height="21" /></a> ("\xc3\xbee", i.e. "the") has led to the modern mispronunciation of <i>thorn</i> as &#x27e8;<a href="/wiki/Y" title="Y">y</a>&#x27e9; in this context; see <i><a href="/wiki/Ye_olde" title="Ye olde">ye olde</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>)\n</p><p>Wynn, which represented the phoneme <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>, was replaced by &#x27e8;<a href="/wiki/W" title="W">w</a>&#x27e9; during the 13th century. Due to its similarity to the letter &#x27e8;p&#x27e9;, it is mostly represented by &#x27e8;w&#x27e9; in modern editions of Old and Middle English texts even when the manuscript has wynn.\n</p><p>Under Norman influence, the continental <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule" title="Carolingian minuscule">Carolingian minuscule</a> replaced the <a href="/wiki/Insular_script" title="Insular script">insular script</a> that had been used for Old English. However, because of the significant difference in appearance between the old <a href="/wiki/Insular_G" title="Insular G">insular <i>g</i></a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_G" title="Carolingian G">Carolingian <i>g</i></a>, the former continued in use as a separate letter, known as <a href="/wiki/Yogh" title="Yogh">yogh</a>, written &#x27e8;\xc8\x9d&#x27e9;. This was adopted for use to represent a variety of sounds: <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xa3], [j], [d\xca\x92], [x], [\xc3\xa7]</span>, while the Carolingian <i>g</i> was normally used for [g]. Instances of yogh were eventually replaced by &#x27e8;j&#x27e9; or &#x27e8;y&#x27e9;, and by &#x27e8;gh&#x27e9; in words like <i>night</i> and <i>laugh</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Middle_Scots" title="Middle Scots">Middle Scots</a> yogh became indistinguishable from cursive <i>z</i>, and printers tended to use &#x27e8;z&#x27e9; when <i>yogh</i> was not available in their fonts; this led to new spellings (often giving rise to new pronunciations), as in <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Mackenzie (disambiguation)"><i>McKenzie</i></a>, where the &#x27e8;z&#x27e9; replaced a yogh which had the pronunciation <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/j/</span>.\n</p><p>Under continental influence, the letters &#x27e8;k&#x27e9;, &#x27e8;q&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;z&#x27e9;, which had not normally been used by Old English scribes, came to be commonly used in the writing of Middle English. Also the newer Latin letter &#x27e8;w&#x27e9; was introduced (replacing wynn). The distinct letter forms <a href="/wiki/V" title="V">&#x27e8;v&#x27e9;</a> and <a href="/wiki/U" title="U">&#x27e8;u&#x27e9;</a> came into use, but were still used interchangeably; the same applies to <a href="/wiki/J" title="J">&#x27e8;j&#x27e9;</a> and <a href="/wiki/I" title="I">&#x27e8;i&#x27e9;</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Salmon_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salmon-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> (For example, spellings such as <i>wijf</i> and <i>paradijs</i> for <i>wife</i> and <i>paradise</i> can be found in Middle English.)\n</p><p>The consonantal &#x27e8;j&#x27e9;/&#x27e8;i&#x27e9; was sometimes used to transliterate the Hebrew letter <a href="/wiki/Yodh" title="Yodh">yodh</a>, representing the <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">palatal approximant</a> sound /j/ (and transliterated in Greek by <a href="/wiki/Iota" title="Iota">iota</a> and in Latin by &#x27e8;i&#x27e9;); words like <i>Jerusalem</i>, <i>Joseph</i>, etc. would have originally followed the Latin pronunciation beginning with /j/, that is, the sound of &#x27e8;y&#x27e9; in <i>yes</i>. In some words, however, notably from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a>, &#x27e8;j&#x27e9;/&#x27e8;i&#x27e9; was used for the <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">affricate consonant</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/d\xca\x92/</span>, as in <i>joie</i> (modern "joy"), used in <a href="/wiki/Wycliffe%27s_Bible" title="Wycliffe&#39;s Bible">Wycliffe\'s Bible</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-j-oed_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-j-oed-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This was similar to the <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">geminate sound</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[dd\xca\x92]</span>, which had been represented as &#x27e8;cg&#x27e9; in Old English. By the time of Modern English, the sound came to be written as &#x27e8;j&#x27e9;/&#x27e8;i&#x27e9; at the start of words (like <i>joy</i>), and usually as &#x27e8;dg&#x27e9; elsewhere (as in <i>bridge</i>). It could also be written, mainly in French loanwords, as &#x27e8;g&#x27e9;, with the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_G" title="Hard and soft G">soft G</a> convention (<i>age</i>, <i>page</i>, etc.)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_symbols">Other symbols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other symbols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Scribal_abbreviation" title="Scribal abbreviation">scribal abbreviations</a> were also used. It was common for the <a href="/wiki/Lollardy" title="Lollardy">Lollards</a> to abbreviate the name of Jesus (as in Latin manuscripts) to <i><a href="/wiki/Christogram" title="Christogram">ihc</a></i>. A thorn with a superscript &#x27e8;t&#x27e9; or &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; could be used for <i>that</i> and <i>the</i>; the thorn here resembled a &#x27e8;Y&#x27e9;, giving rise to the <i>ye</i> of "<a href="/wiki/Ye_Olde" class="mw-redirect" title="Ye Olde">Ye Olde</a>". before us survive, and therefore we are taught about what was written then. For this reason it is good that we also in our time, here among us, write some material from scratch, inspired by the example of these old customs; so that it might, when we are dead and elsewhere, be left to the world\'s ear in the time coming after this. But because men say, and it\'s true, that when someone writes entirely about wisdom, it often dulls a man\'s wit who reads it every day.
