style="text-align:right;">sub-<a href="/wiki/Hewitt_(hill)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hewitt (hill)">Hewitt</a>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Snowdon</b> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy"><b>Yr Wyddfa</b></i>, <small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Welsh" title="Help:IPA/Welsh">[\xc9\x99r \xcb\x88w\xc9\xaa\xc3\xb0va]</a></span>) is the highest mountain in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, at an elevation of 1,085 metres (3,560&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above sea level">above sea level</a>, and the highest point in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> outside the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>. It is located in <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowdonia National Park">Snowdonia National Park</a> (<i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Parc Cenedlaethol Eryri</i></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>. It is the busiest mountain in the United Kingdom and the third most visited attraction in Wales, with 582,000 people visiting annually.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is designated as a <a href="/wiki/National_nature_reserve_(United_Kingdom)" title="National nature reserve (United Kingdom)">national nature reserve</a> for its rare flora and fauna.\n</p><p>The rocks that form Snowdon were produced by <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> period, and the massif has been extensively sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a>, forming the <a href="/wiki/Pyramidal_peak" title="Pyramidal peak">pyramidal peak</a> of Snowdon and the <a href="/wiki/Ar%C3%AAte" title="Ar\xc3\xaate">ar\xc3\xaates</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crib_Goch" title="Crib Goch">Crib Goch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Y_Lliwedd" title="Y Lliwedd">Y Lliwedd</a>. The cliff faces on Snowdon, including <a href="/wiki/Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu" title="Clogwyn Du&#39;r Arddu">Clogwyn Du\'r Arddu</a>, are significant for <a href="/wiki/Rock_climbing" title="Rock climbing">rock climbing</a>, and the mountain was used by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Hillary" title="Edmund Hillary">Edmund Hillary</a> in training for the 1953 ascent of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>.\n</p><p>The summit can be reached by a number of paths, and by the <a href="/wiki/Snowdon_Mountain_Railway" title="Snowdon Mountain Railway">Snowdon Mountain Railway</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Rack_railway" title="Rack railway">rack railway</a> opened in 1896 which carries passengers the 4.7 miles (7.6&#160;km) from <a href="/wiki/Llanberis" title="Llanberis">Llanberis</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Summit_(SMR)_railway_station" title="Summit (SMR) railway station">summit station</a>. The summit also houses a cafe called <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Hafod Eryri</i></i>, open only when the railway is operating; it opened in 2009 to replace one built in the 1930s. The railway generally operates to the summit station from <a href="/wiki/Whitsun" title="Whitsun">Whitsun</a> to October. The daily running schedule depends on weather and customer demand.\n</p><p>The name <i>Snowdon</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> for "snow hill", while the Welsh name \xe2\x80\x93 <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Yr Wyddfa</i></i> \xe2\x80\x93 means "the <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">tumulus</a>" or "the barrow", which may refer to the cairn thrown over the legendary giant <a href="/wiki/Rhitta_Gawr" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhitta Gawr">Rhitta Gawr</a> after his defeat by <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>. As well as other figures from Arthurian legend, the mountain is linked to a legendary <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text"><a href="/wiki/Afanc" title="Afanc">afanc</a></i></i> (water monster) and the <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text"><a href="/wiki/Tylwyth_Teg" title="Tylwyth Teg">Tylwyth Teg</a></i> (fairies).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Height">Height</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Height">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A 1682 survey estimated that the summit of Snowdon was at a height of 3,720 feet (1,130&#160;m); in 1773, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Thomas Pennant</a> quoted a later estimate of 3,568&#160;ft (1,088&#160;m) above sea level at <a href="/wiki/Caernarfon" title="Caernarfon">Caernarfon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Recent surveys give the height of the summit as 1,085&#160;m (3,560&#160;ft), making Snowdon the highest mountain in Wales, and the highest point in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnes_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Snowdon is one of three mountains climbed as part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Three_Peaks_Challenge" title="National Three Peaks Challenge">National Three Peaks Challenge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div serotina</a></i> (the "Snowdon lily") grows on the cliffs of Snowdon</div></div></div>\n<p>The environment of Snowdon, particularly its rare plants, has led to its designation as a <a href="/wiki/National_nature_reserve_(United_Kingdom)" title="National nature reserve (United Kingdom)">national nature reserve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Watkin_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Watkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In addition to plants that are widespread in Snowdonia, Snowdon is home to some plants rarely found elsewhere in Britain. These include the "Snowdon lily", <i><a href="/wiki/Gagea_serotina" title="Gagea serotina">Gagea serotina</a></i>, which is also found in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> and in North America; it was first discovered in Wales by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lhuyd" title="Edward Lhuyd">Edward Lhuyd</a>, and the genus <i>Lloydia</i> (now included in <i><a href="/wiki/Gagea" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Snowdon lies in the northern part of <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowdonia National Park">Snowdonia National Park</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> which has also provided some legal protection since the park\'s establishment in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crib_Goch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_353320.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Crib_Goch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_353320.jpg/220px-Crib_Goch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_353320.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Crib_Goch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_353320.jpg/330px-Crib_Goch_-_geograph.org.uk_-_353320.jpg 1.5x, href="/wiki/Crib_Goch" title="Crib Goch">Crib Goch</a> (foreground) and the <a href="/wiki/Pyramidal_peak" title="Pyramidal peak">pyramidal peak</a> of Snowdon (background) are both the result of <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The rocks which today make up Snowdon and its neighbouring mountains were formed in the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> Period. At that time, most of modern-day Wales was near the edge of <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a>, submerged beneath the ancient <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Ocean" title="Iapetus Ocean">Iapetus Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In the Soudleyan stage (<span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=458\xe2\x80\x93457">458&#160;to&#160;457</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>) of the <a href="/wiki/Caradoc_(age)" class="mw-redirect" title="Caradoc (age)">Caradoc age</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Caldera" href="/wiki/Tuff" title="Tuff">tuff</a>, which formed deposits up to 500 metres (1,600&#160;ft) thick.<sup id="cite_ref-Cope_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cope-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The current summit is near the northern edge of the ancient caldera; the caldera\'s full extent is unclear, but it extended as far as the summit of <a href="/wiki/Moel_Hebog" title="Moel Hebog">Moel Hebog</a> in the south-west.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Snowdon and its surrounding peaks have been described as "true examples of <a href="/wiki/Orography" title="Orography">Alpine topography</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The summits of Snowdon and Garnedd Ugain are surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Cirque" title="Cirque">cwms</a>, rounded valleys scooped out by <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Erosion by glaciers in adjacent cwms caused the characteristic <a href="/wiki/Ar%C3%AAte" title="Ar\xc3\xaate">ar\xc3\xaates</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crib_Goch" title="Crib Goch">Crib Goch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crib_y_Ddysgl" class="mw-redirect" title="Crib y Ddysgl">Crib y Ddysgl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Y_Lliwedd" title="Y Lliwedd">Y Lliwedd</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pyramidal_peak" title="Pyramidal peak">pyramidal peak</a> of Snowdon itself.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Glacial_landform" title="Glacial landform">glacial landforms</a> that can be seen around Snowdon include <a href="/wiki/Roche_moutonn%C3%A9e" class="mw-redirect" title="Roche moutonn\xc3\xa9e">roches moutonn\xc3\xa9es</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glacial_erratic" title="Glacial erratic">glacial erratics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The English name "Snowdon" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i><i lang="ang" title="Old English language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/snaw" class="extiw" title="wikt:snaw">snaw</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dun" class="extiw" title="wikt:dun">dun</a></i></i>, meaning "snow hill", as Snowdon often has a covering of snow.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Although the amount of snow on Snowdon in winter varies significantly, 55% less snow fell in 2004 than in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The slopes of Snowdon have one of the wettest climates in Great Britain, receiving an annual average of more than 200 inches (5,100&#160;mm) of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lakes">Lakes</span><span class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Llyn_Llydaw_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1236149.jpg/220px-Llyn_Llydaw_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1236149.jpg" width="220" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Llyn_Llydaw_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1236149.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Llyn_Llydaw" title="Llyn Llydaw">Llyn Llydaw</a>, the largest and deepest lake on Snowdon\'s flanks, is crossed by a causeway at its eastern end</div></div></div>\n<p>A number of lakes are found in the various <a href="/wiki/Cirque" title="Cirque">cwms</a> of the Snowdon range.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Llyn_Llydaw" title="Llyn Llydaw">Llyn Llydaw</a> \xe2\x80\x93 1,430 feet (440&#160;m) high, 110 acres (45&#160;ha) \xe2\x80\x93 lies in <a href="/wiki/Cwm_Dyli" title="Cwm Dyli">Cwm Dyli</a>, Snowdon\'s eastern cwm, and is one of Snowdonia\'s deepest lakes, at up to 190&#160;ft (58&#160;m) deep. Various explanations of its name have been put forward, including <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">lludw</i></i> ("ash"), from ashen deposits along the shore, to <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Llydaw</i></i> ("<a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Llydaw_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Llydaw-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It contains evidence of a <a href="/wiki/Crannog" title="Crannog">crannog</a> settlement, and was the location of a 10-by-2-foot (3&#160;m \xc3\x97&#160;0.6&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Dugout_canoe" title="Dugout canoe">dugout canoe</a> described in the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambrian_Journal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cambrian Journal (page does not exist)">Cambrian Journal</a></i> in 1862.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Llydaw_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Llydaw-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The lake is significantly coloured by washings from the copper mines nearby, and is used by the Cwm Dyli <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> station, which opened in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Llydaw_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Llydaw-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The lake is crossed by a <a href="/wiki/Causeway" title="Causeway">causeway</a>, built in 1853 and raised in the 20th century to prevent the causeway from flooding frequently.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Glaslyn" title="Glaslyn">Glaslyn</a> \xe2\x80\x93 1,970 feet (600&#160;m) high, 18 acres (7.3&#160;ha) \xe2\x80\x93 lies higher up Cwm Dyli than Llyn Llydaw.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Glaslyn_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Glaslyn-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It was originally called <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Llyn y Ffynnon Glas</i></i>, and has a depth of 127 feet (39&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Glaslyn_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Glaslyn-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For a long time, it was believed to be bottomless, and is also the location for various myths.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Glaslyn_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Glaslyn-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Llyn_Ffynnon-y-gwas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Llyn Ffynnon-y-gwas (page does not exist)">Llyn Ffynnon-y-gwas</a> \xe2\x80\x93 1,430 feet (440&#160;m) high, 10 acres (4.0&#160;ha) \xe2\x80\x93 lies in Cwm Treweunydd, Snowdon\'s north-western cwm, and is passed by the Snowdon Ranger path.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Gwas_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Gwas-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It was enlarged by damming for use as a reservoir for use by slate quarries, but the level has since been lowered, and the lake\'s volume reduced to 24,000 cubic metres (850,000&#160;cu&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Gwas_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Gwas-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Other lakes include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llyn_Du%27r_Arddu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Llyn Du&#39;r Arddu (page does not exist)">Llyn Du\'r Arddu</a> below Clogwyn Du\'r Arddu \xe2\x80\x93 1,901 feet (579&#160;m) high, 5 acres (2.0&#160;ha), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llyn_Teyrn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Llyn Teyrn (page does not exist)">Llyn Teyrn</a> near Pen-y-pass \xe2\x80\x93 1,237 feet (377&#160;m) high, 5 acres (2.0&#160;ha), and several smaller pools.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rock_climbing">Rock climbing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rock climbing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg/220px-Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg/330px-Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg/440px-Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu_-_geograph.org.uk_-_837339.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu" title="Clogwyn Du&#39;r Arddu">Clogwyn Du\'r Arddu</a><br />A rock climbing site</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Snowdon_Massif" title="Snowdon Massif">Snowdon Massif</a> includes a number of cliffs, and holds an important place in the history of <a href="/wiki/Rock_climbing" title="Rock climbing">rock climbing</a> in the United Kingdom. <a href="/wiki/Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu" title="Clogwyn Du&#39;r Arddu">Clogwyn Du\'r Arddu</a> is often colloquially known as \'Cloggy\' among climbers, and was the site of the first recorded climb in Britain, in 1798.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It was carried out by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bailey_Williams" title="Peter Bailey Williams">Peter Bailey Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Bingley" title="William Bingley">William Bingley</a>, while searching for rare plants.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It is now considered to be one of the best cliffs in Britain for rock climbing.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Rhyd_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Rhyd-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Y_Lliwedd" title="Y Lliwedd">Y Lliwedd</a> was also explored by early climbers, and was the subject of a 1909 climbing guide, <i>The Climbs on Lliwedd</i> by J. M. A. Thompson and A. W. Andrews, one of the first in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Snowdon was used by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Hillary" title="Edmund Hillary">Edmund Hillary</a> and his group during preparations for their successful 1953 expedition to climb <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascents">Ascents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ascents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SnowdonMap.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/SnowdonMap.svg/350px-SnowdonMap.svg.png" width="350" height="385" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/SnowdonMap.svg/525px-SnowdonMap.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/SnowdonMap.svg/700px-SnowdonMap.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1100" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SnowdonMap.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sketch map of the <a href="/wiki/Snowdon_Massif" title="Snowdon Massif">Snowdon Massif</a><br /><b>Legend</b><br />\xc2\xb7 grey: ridges<br />\xc2\xb7 red lines: paths<br />\xc2\xb7 orange lines: roads<br />\xc2\xb7 dotted grey line: Snowdon Mountain Railway</div></div></div>\n<p>The first recorded ascent of Snowdon was by the botanist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Johnson_(botanist)" title="Thomas Johnson (botanist)">Thomas Johnson</a> in 1639.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> However, the 18th-century Welsh historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Thomas Pennant</a> mentions a "triumphal fair upon this our chief of mountains" following <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>\'s conquest of Wales in 1284, which could indicate the possibility of earlier ascents.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Snowdon offers some of the most extensive views in the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> On exceptionally clear days, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> are all visible, as well as 24 counties, 29 lakes and 17 islands. The view between Snowdon and <a href="/wiki/Merrick,_Galloway" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrick, Galloway">Merrick</a> (southern Scotland) is the longest theoretical line of sight in the British Isles at 144 miles (232&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-Jonathan_de_Ferranti_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jonathan_de_Ferranti-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Snowdon has been described as "probably the busiest mountain in Britain",<sup id="cite_ref-Llanberis_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Llanberis-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> with approximately 444,000 people having walked up the mountain in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A number of well-established and engineered footpaths lead to Snowdon\'s summit from all sides,<sup id="cite_ref-Barnes_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and can be combined in various ways.<sup id="cite_ref-Rowland_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The circular walk starting and ending at <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text"><a href="/wiki/Pen-y-Pass" title="Pen-y-Pass">Pen-y-Pass</a></i> and using the <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Crib Goch</i> route and the route over <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Y Lliwedd</i> is called the Snowdon Horseshoe, and is considered "one of the finest ridge walks in Britain".<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Horseshoe_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Horseshoe-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The routes are arranged here anticlockwise, starting with the path leading from Llanberis. In winter conditions, all these routes become significantly more dangerous and the Llanberis Mountain Rescue Team state that "additional skills, equipment and knowledge are required".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Many inexperienced walkers have been killed over the years attempting to climb the mountain via the main paths.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>These six main paths were mapped by the Google Trekker in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Llanberis_Path">Llanberis Path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Llanberis Path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 9 miles (14&#160;km) long Llanberis Path has the shallowest gradient and is the longest route to the summit. It follows the line of the railway and being the easiest and least interesting,<sup id="cite_ref-Barnes_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> it is the route used by the annual <a href="/wiki/Snowdon_Race" title="Snowdon Race">Snowdon Race</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> which has a record time of less than 40 minutes recorded from the start to the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The section of the Llanberis Path beside the railway near the summit has been called the "Killer Convex"; in icy conditions, this convex slope can send unwary walkers over the cliffs of Clogwyn Du\'r Arddu. Four people died there in February 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snowdon_Ranger_Path">Snowdon Ranger Path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Llyn_Ffynnon-y-gwas_from_Moel_Gynghorion_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1780520.jpg/440px-Llyn_Ffynnon-y-gwas_from_Moel_Gynghorion_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1780520.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="790" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Llyn_Ffynnon-y-gwas_from_Moel_Gynghorion_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1780520.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Snowdon Ranger Path crosses a boggy area before ascending past Llyn Ffynnon-y-gwas</div></div></div>\n<p>The Snowdon Ranger Path (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Llwybr Cwellyn</i>) begins at the <a href="/wiki/Youth_hostel" class="mw-redirect" title="Youth hostel">youth hostel</a> beside <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Cwellyn" title="Llyn Cwellyn">Llyn Cwellyn</a>, to the west of the mountain, served by the <a href="/wiki/A4085_road" title="A4085 road">A4085</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snowdon_Ranger_railway_station" title="Snowdon Ranger railway station">Snowdon Ranger railway station</a>. This was formerly the Saracen\'s Head Inn, but was renamed under the ownership of the mountain guide John Morton.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is thought to be the oldest path to the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnes_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The route begins with <a href="/wiki/Zigzag" title="Zigzag">zigzags</a> through turf,<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> before reaching a flatter <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">boggy</a> area in front of Llyn Ffynnon-y-gwas. The path then climbs to Bwlch Cwm Brwynog, and then snakes along the ridge above Clogwyn Du\'r Arddu towards the summit. This path meets the railway, the Llanberis Path, the Crib Goch path, and the combined Pyg Track and Miners\' Track all within a short distance, just below the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhyd_Ddu_Path">Rhyd Ddu Path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rhyd Ddu Path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Rhyd Ddu path, also called the Beddgelert Path, leads from the village of <a href="/wiki/Rhyd_Ddu" title="Rhyd Ddu">Rhyd Ddu</a>, west of Snowdon, gently up on to Llechog, a broad ridge dropping west from the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It is considered one of the easier routes to the summit,<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> with the advantage that the summit is visible from the start,<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> but is one of the least used routes.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Rhyd_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Rhyd-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> It climbs at a shallow gradient to Bwlch Main, shortly southwest of the summit, from where it climbs more steeply, meeting up with the Watkin Path at a site marked with a large standing stone a few hundred metres from the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> An alternative start begins at <a href="/wiki/Pitt%27s_Head" title="Pitt&#39;s Head">Pitt\'s Head</a> on the <a href="/wiki/A4085_road" title="A4085 road">A4085 road</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Rhyd_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Rhyd-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Watkin_Path">Watkin Path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Watkin Path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg/220px-Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg/330px-Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg/440px-Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plascwmllan_and_the_Gladstone_Rock_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1286451.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plas Cwmllan (right) and Gladstone Rock (left) in Cwm Llan, looking along the Watkin Path</div></div></div>\n<p>The Watkin Path is "the most demanding route direct to the summit of Snowdon",<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Watkin_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Watkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> since it starts at the lowest elevation of any of the main routes.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It was first conceived by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Watkin" title="Edward Watkin">Edward Watkin</a>, a railway owner who had attempted to build a railway tunnel under the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, and had a <a href="/wiki/Summer_home" class="mw-redirect" title="Summer home">summer home</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nant_Gwynant" title="Nant Gwynant">Nant Gwynant</a> near the start of the path.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnes_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It was originally designed as a donkey track and opened in 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The start of the Watkin Path has been described as "the prettiest beginning" of the routes up Snowdon.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It begins at Bethania on the <a href="/wiki/A498_road" title="A498 road">A498</a> and climbs initially through old <a href="/wiki/Celtic_broadleaf_forests" title="Celtic broadleaf forests">broadleaved woodland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> After leaving the woods, the path climbs past the waterfalls of the Afon Llan to the glacial <a href="/wiki/Cirque" title="Cirque">cirque</a> of Cwm Llan, crossing a disused <a href="/wiki/Inclined_plane" title="Inclined plane">incline</a> from an abandoned slate quarry.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Watkin_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Watkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It then reaches Plas Cwmllan, formerly the home of the quarry manager for the South Snowdon Slate Works beyond, and later used for <a href="/wiki/Target_practice" title="Target practice">target practice</a> by <a href="/wiki/Commando" title="Commando">commandos</a> during the Second World War.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Watkin_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Watkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Near Plas Cwmllan is the large boulder known as Gladstone Rock, which bears a plaque commemorating a speech given in 1892 by <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a>, the then 83-year-old <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>, on the subject of <i>Justice for Wales</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The slate From here, it is also possible to see the mountains of the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Pennines" title="South Pennines">South Pennines</a> that surround Manchester. The view between Snowdon and <a href="/wiki/Merrick,_Galloway" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrick, Galloway">Merrick</a> (southern Scotland) is the longest theoretical line of sight in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> at 144 miles (232&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-Jonathan_de_Ferranti_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jonathan_de_Ferranti-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In practice, atmospheric conditions make such sightings extremely rare and indeed there are no reported sightings. The mountain itself may also be viewed on take off and approach to both <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport" title="Manchester Airport">Manchester Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_John_Lennon_Airport" title="Liverpool John Lennon Airport">Liverpool John Lennon Airport</a> on very clear days, and even from <a href="/wiki/Howth_Head" title="Howth Head">Howth Head</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Snowdon_Mountain_Railway">Snowdon Mountain title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Rheilffordd yr Wyddfa</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Narrow_gauge_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrow gauge railway">narrow gauge</a> <a href="/wiki/Rack_railway" title="Rack railway">rack and pinion</a> <a href="/wiki/Mountain_railway" title="Mountain railway">mountain railway</a> that travels for 4.75 miles (7.6&#160;km) from <a href="/wiki/Llanberis" title="Llanberis">Llanberis</a> to the summit of Snowdon.<sup id="cite_ref-Nabarro_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nabarro-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> It is the only public rack and pinion railway in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Nabarro_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nabarro-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and after more than 100&#160;years of operation it remains a popular <a href="/wiki/Tourist_attraction" title="Tourist attraction">tourist attraction</a>, carrying more than 130,000 passengers annually.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Single carriage trains are pushed up the mountain by either <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diesel_locomotive" title="Diesel locomotive">diesel locomotives</a>. It has also previously used <a href="/wiki/Railcar" title="Railcar">diesel railcars</a> as <a href="/wiki/Multiple_unit" title="Multiple unit">multiple units</a>. The railway was constructed between December 1894, when the first sod was cut by Enid Assheton-Smith (after whom locomotive No.2 was named), and February 1896, at a total cost of \xc2\xa363,800 (equivalent to \xc2\xa36,775,000 as of 2016).<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" refreshments, and a <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_licensing_laws_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Alcohol licensing laws of the United Kingdom">licence to sell intoxicating liquor</a> was granted in 1845.<sup class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/Snowdon_Mountain_Railway" title="Snowdon Mountain Railway">Snowdon Mountain Railway</a> was opened in 1896, it added its own accommodation and sales outlet near the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1930s, many complaints were received about the state of the facilities at the summit and in 1934/5 a new station building was erected in two phases. It was designed by Sir <a href="/wiki/Clough_Williams-Ellis" title="Clough Williams-Ellis">Clough Williams-Ellis</a> and included rooms for visitors and a <a href="/wiki/Cafe_(British)" title="Cafe (British)">cafe</a>. The other operators were bought out and the ramshackle collection of buildings on the summit was cleared. The flat roof was intended to be used as a viewing platform and some photographs show it being used in this way. However, other photographs taken of the cafe show that the roof leaked, which probably explains why the practice was stopped. The Summit was taken over by government agencies during the war and the accommodation was restricted to staff use afterwards.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Having become increasingly dilapidated, this building was described by <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a> as "the highest slum in Wales".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Its state led to a campaign to replace the building. In April 2006, Snowdonia National Park Authority with the support of the <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia_Society" title="Snowdonia Society">Snowdonia Society</a> agreed a deal to start work on a new cafe and visitor centre complex.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> By mid-October 2006 the old building had been largely demolished.\n</p><p>The new <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_British_Architects" title="Royal Institute of British Architects">RIBA</a> Award-winning<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> \xc2\xa38.4&#160;million visitor centre, <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Hafod Eryri</i></i>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Hole_Architects" title="Ray Hole Architects">Ray Hole Architects</a> in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Arup_Group_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Arup Group Limited">Arup</a> and built by <a href="/wiki/Carillion" title="Carillion">Carillion</a>, was officially opened on 12 June 2009<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Wales" title="First Minister of Wales">First Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_Morgan" title="Rhodri Morgan">Rhodri Morgan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The Welsh National Poet, <a href="/wiki/Gwyn_Thomas_(poet)" title="Gwyn Thomas (poet)">Gwyn Thomas</a>, composed a new couplet for the new building, displayed at its entrance and on the windows, which reads "<i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Copa\'r Wyddfa: yr ydych chwi, yma, Yn nes at y nefoedd</i> / The summit of Snowdon: You are, here, nearer to Heaven</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>Hafod Eryri</i> was chosen from several hundred put forward after a competition was held by the BBC.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Hafod</i></i> is <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> for an <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Wales#History" title="Agriculture in Wales">upland summer residence</a>, while <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Eryri</i></i> is the Welsh name for <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia" title="Snowdonia">Snowdonia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bedivere.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Bedivere.jpg/170px-Bedivere.jpg" width="170" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Bedivere.jpg/255px-Bedivere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/01/Bedivere.jpg 2x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="367" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bedivere.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In Arthurian legend, <a href="/wiki/Bedivere" title="Bedivere">Bedivere</a> threw <i><a href="/wiki/Excalibur" title="Excalibur">Excalibur</a></i> into a lake identified by some as <a href="/wiki/Glaslyn" title="Glaslyn">Glaslyn</a> on the slopes of Snowdon.</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Welsh_folklore" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh folklore">Welsh folklore</a>, the summit of Snowdon is said to be the <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tomb</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhitta_Gawr" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhitta Gawr">Rhitta Gawr</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Giants_(Welsh_folklore)" title="Giants (Welsh folklore)">giant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This is claimed to be the reason for the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> name <i>Yr Wyddfa</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hermon_22-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hermon-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> literally meaning "the <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">tumulus</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Rhitta Gawr wore a cloak made of men\'s beards, and was slain by <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> after claiming Arthur\'s beard.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Glaslyn_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Glaslyn-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Other sites with <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_legend" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian legend">Arthurian</a> connections include Bwlch y Saethau, on the ridge between Snowdon and Y Lliwedd, where Arthur himself is said to have died. A cairn, <i>Carnedd Arthur</i>, was erected at the site and was still standing as late as 1850,<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Glaslyn_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Glaslyn-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> but no longer exists.<sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> According to the folklore, Arthur had <a href="/wiki/Bedivere" title="Bedivere">Bedivere</a> throw his sword <i><a href="/wiki/Excalibur" title="Excalibur">Excalibur</a></i> into <a href="/wiki/Glaslyn" title="Glaslyn">Glaslyn</a>, where Arthur\'s body was later placed in a boat to be carried away to <a href="/wiki/Afallon" class="mw-redirect" title="Afallon">Afallon</a>. Arthur\'s men then retreated to a cave on the slopes of Y Lliwedd, where they are said to sleep until such time as they are needed.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Glaslyn_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Glaslyn-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_Horseshoe_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh_Horseshoe-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Merlin" title="Merlin">Merlin</a> is supposed to have hidden the golden throne of Britain among the cliffs north of Crib y Ddysgl when the Saxons invaded.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\nGlaslyn was also the final resting place of a water monster, known as an <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text"><a href="/wiki/Afanc" title="Afanc">afanc</a></i></i> (also the Welsh word for <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beaver</a>), which had plagued the people of the <a href="/wiki/Conwy_valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Conwy valley">Conwy valley</a>. They tempted the monster out of the water with a young girl, before securing it with chains and dragging it to Glaslyn.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_Glaslyn_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_Glaslyn-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marsh_26-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A large stone known as Maen Du\'r Arddu, below Clogwyn Du\'r Arddu, is supposed to have <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magic (paranormal)">magical powers</a>. Like several other sites in Wales, it is said that if two people spend the night there, one will become a great poet while the other will become insane.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Llyn Coch in Cwm Clogwyn has been associated with the <a href="/wiki/Tylwyth_Teg" title="Tylwyth Teg">Tylwyth Teg</a> (fairies), including a version of the <a href="/wiki/Fairy" title="Fairy">fairy</a> bride legend.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Scenes from <i><a href="/wiki/Carry_On..._Up_the_Khyber" class="mw-redirect" title="Carry On... Up the Khyber">Carry On... Up the Khyber</a></i> were filmed on the lower part of the Watkin Path in 1968, with the Watkin Path representing the <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pass" title="Khyber Pass">Khyber Pass</a> in the film. In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Angela_Douglas" title="Angela Douglas">Angela Douglas</a>, one of the stars of the film, unveiled a plaque at the precise location where filming took place, to commemorate the location filming, and it forms part of the North Wales Film and Television Trail run by the Wales Screen Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a
