becoming increasingly inadequate to deal with a <a href="/wiki/British_Commonwealth_of_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="British Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> with independent member states. In 1948, the Commonwealth <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">Heads of Government</a> agreed that each member would adopt a national citizenship (Canada had already done so), but that the existing status of the British subject would continue as a common status held by all <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_citizen" title="Commonwealth citizen">Commonwealth citizens</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Nationality_Act_1948" title="British Nationality Act 1948">British Nationality Act 1948</a> established the status of <i>Citizen of the United Kingdom and Colonies</i> (CUKC), the national citizenship of the United Kingdom and <a href="/wiki/Crown_colony" title="Crown colony">colonies</a> on 1 January 1949. Until the early 1960s there was little difference, if any, in UK law between the rights of CUKCs and other British subjects, all of whom had the right at any time to enter and live in the UK.\n</p><p>Independence Acts, passed when the remaining colonies were granted independence, contained nationality provisions. In general, these provisions withdrew the status of CUKC from anyone who became citizens of the newly independent country, unless one had a connection with the UK or a remaining colony (e.g. through birth in the UK).  Exceptions were sometimes made in cases where the colonies did not become independent. (Notable cases include <a href="/wiki/Crown_Colony_of_Penang" title="Crown Colony of Penang">Crown Colony of Penang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_Colony_of_Malacca" title="Crown Colony of Malacca">Crown Colony of Malacca</a>, which were made part of the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Malaya" title="Federation of Malaya">Federation of Malaya</a> in 1957; CUKC status was not withdrawn from CUKCs from Penang and Malacca even when they would automatically acquire Malayan citizenship at the time of independence.)\n</p><p>Between 1962 and 1971, as a result of fears about increasing immigration by Commonwealth citizens, the UK gradually tightened controls on immigration by British subjects from other parts of the Commonwealth, which included CUKCs without familial or residential ties to the UK. The <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_1971" title="Immigration Act 1971">Immigration Act 1971</a> introduced the concept of <i>patriality</i>, by which only British subjects (i.e., CUKCs and Commonwealth citizens) with sufficiently strong links to the <a href="/wiki/British_Islands" title="British Islands">British Islands</a> (e.g., being born in the islands or had a parent or a grandparent born there) had <i><a href="/wiki/Right_of_abode_(United_Kingdom)" title="Right of abode (United Kingdom)">right of abode</a></i>, meaning they were free from immigration control and had the right to enter, live and work in the islands. The act, therefore, had <i>de facto</i> created two types of CUKCs: those with right of abode in the UK, and those without right of abode in the UK (who might or might not have right of abode in a Crown colony or another country). Despite differences in immigration status was formally created, there existed no <i>de jure</i> difference between the two under the nationality context, as the 1948 law still specified one tier of citizenship throughout the UK and its colonies. This changed in 1983 when the 1948 law was replaced by a multi-tier nationality system.\n</p><p>The current principal British nationality law in force, since 1 January 1983, is the <a href="/wiki/British_Nationality_Act_1981" title="British Nationality Act 1981">British Nationality Act 1981</a>, which established the system of multiple categories of British nationality. To date, six tiers were created, viz. <i>British citizens</i>, <i>British Overseas Territories citizens</i>, <i>British Overseas citizens</i>, <i>British Nationals (Overseas)</i>, <i>British subjects</i>, and <i>British protected persons</i>. Only British citizens and certain Commonwealth citizens have the automatic right of abode in the UK, with the latter holding residual rights they had prior to 1983.\n</p><p>Aside from different categories of a nationality, the 1981 Act also ceased to recognise Commonwealth citizens as British subjects. There remain only two categories of people who are still British subjects: those (formerly known as British subjects without citizenship) who acquired British nationality through a connection with former British India, and those connected with the Republic of Ireland before 1949 who have made a declaration to retain British nationality. British subjects connected with former British India lose British nationality if they acquire any other.\n</p><p>In spite of the fact that the 1981 act repealed most of the provisions of 1948 act and the nationality clauses in subsequent independence acts, the acquisition of new categories of British nationality created by the 1981 act was often dependent on nationality status prior to 1 January 1983, the date the 1981 act came into effect, so many of the provisions of the 1948 act and subsequent independence acts are still relevant.  Not taking this into account might lead one to the erroneous conclusion, for example, that the 1981 act\'s repeal of the nationality clauses in the Kenya Independence Act of 1963 restored British nationality to those who lost their CUKC status as a result of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>\'s independence in 1963.  This is one of the reasons for the complexity of British nationality law; in complicated cases, determining British nationality status requires an examination of several nationality acts in their class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Active categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The following two classes of British nationality are "active", meaning that they can be acquired at birth, or by naturalization or registration for any eligible person.\n</p><p><span id="British_citizenship"></span>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>British citizen</dt>\n<dd>Persons who are British citizens usually hold this status through a connection with the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> ("United Kingdom and Islands"), or, since 2002, a connection with one of the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a> (BOT) except <a href="/wiki/Akrotiri_and_Dhekelia" title="Akrotiri and Dhekelia">Akrotiri and Dhekelia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Citizen_of_the_United_Kingdom_and_Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizen of the United Kingdom and Colonies">Citizens of the United Kingdom and Colonies</a> (CUKCs) who possessed <a href="/wiki/Right_of_abode_(United_Kingdom)" title="Right of abode (United Kingdom)">right of abode</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_1971" title="Immigration Act 1971">Immigration Act 1971</a> through a connection with the UK and Islands generally became British citizens on 1 January 1983, and British Dependent Territories citizens who were not solely connected with Akrotiri and Dhekelia acquired this status on 21 May 2002.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>British citizenship is the most common type of British nationality, and the only one that automatically carries a right of abode in the UK.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>Other rights can vary according to how the British citizenship was acquired. In particular there are restrictions for \'<a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law#British_citizenship_by_descent" title="British nationality law">British citizens by descent</a>\' transmitting British citizenship to children born outside the UK. These restrictions do not apply to \'British citizens otherwise than by descent\'.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories_citizen" title="British Overseas Territories citizen">British Overseas Territories citizen</a> (formerly British Dependent Territories citizenship) (BOTC)</dt></dl>\n<dl><dd>BOTC (formerly BDTC) is the form of British nationality held by connection with a <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territory" class="mw-redirect" title="British Overseas Territory">British Overseas Territory</a> (BOT). It is possible to hold BOTC and British citizenship simultaneously. Nearly all are now also British citizens as a result of the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories_Act_2002" title="British Overseas Territories Act 2002">British Overseas Territories Act 2002</a>.</dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Residual_categories">Residual categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Residual categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The four residual categories are expected to become extinct with the passage of time. They can be passed to children only in exceptional circumstances, e.g., if the child would otherwise be <a href="/wiki/Stateless_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Stateless person">stateless</a>. There is consequently little provision for the acquisition of these classes of nationality by people who do not already have them. To reduce <i>de facto</i> statelessness, most are allowed to be registered as British citizens provided holding no other citizenship or nationality.\n</p>\n<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_citizen" title="British Overseas citizen">British Overseas citizen</a> (BOC)</dt></dl>\n<dl><dd>In general, most BOCs are CUKCs who did not qualify for British citizenship or British Dependent Territories citizenship. Most derived their status as CUKCs from former colonies, such as <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, because of quirks and exceptions in the law that resulted in their retaining CUKC status in spite of the independence of their colonies.  This is fairly uncommon: most CUKCs (including those from Malaysia and Kenya) lost their CUKC status upon independence.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>In 1997, BDTCs with a connection to <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> became BOCs after they did not register as British Nationals (Overseas) and would become stateless after the withdrawal of BDTC status from Hong Kong residents.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/British_subject" title="British subject">British subject</a></dt></dl>\n<dl><dd>British subjects (as defined in the 1981 Act) are British subjects who were not CUKCs or citizens of any other Commonwealth country. Most derived their status as British subjects from <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> as they existed before 1949.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/British_National_(Overseas)" title="British National (Overseas)">British National (Overseas)</a> (BN(O))</dt></dl>\n<dl><dd>The status of BN(O) was created by the Hong Kong Act 1985 and the British Nationality (Hong Kong) Order 1986. BN(O)s are BDTCs with a connection to Hong Kong who applied for registration as BN(O)s before the <a href="/wiki/Handover_of_Hong_Kong" title="Handover of Hong Kong">handover of Hong Kong</a> to the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People\'s Republic of China</a>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/British_protected_person" title="British protected person">British protected person</a> (BPP)</dt></dl>\n<dl><dd>BPPs derive from parts of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> that were <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorates</a> or <a href="/wiki/Protected_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Protected state">protected states</a> with nominally independent rulers under the "protection" of the British Crown, not officially part of the Crown\'s dominions. The status of BPP is <i><a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a></i>&#160;&#8211;&#32;BPPs are not Commonwealth citizens (\'British subjects\', in the old sense) and were not traditionally considered British nationals, but are not aliens either.</dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_right_of_abode">Relationship with right of abode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationship with right of abode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Only the status of British citizen carries with it the <a href="/wiki/Right_of_abode_(United_Kingdom)" title="Right of abode (United Kingdom)">right of abode</a> in a certain country or territory (in this case the UK).\n</p><p>In practice, BOTCs (except those associated with the <a href="/wiki/Akrotiri_and_Dhekelia" title="Akrotiri and Dhekelia">Sovereign bases in Cyprus</a>) were granted full British citizenship in 2002; BN(O)s have right of abode or right to land in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> (note: not conferred by the status itself, but the Immigration Ordinance of Hong Kong) and are eligible for registration as British citizens if holding no other nationality under the <a href="/wiki/Borders,_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Act_2009" title="Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009">Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009</a>; BSs and BPPs lose their statuses upon acquisition of another nationality (except BSs connected with the Republic of Ireland) and so should be eligible for registration as British citizens under the <a href="/wiki/Nationality,_Immigration_and_Asylum_Act_2002" title="Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002">Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002</a>.\n</p><p>British Overseas citizens are unique in that their nationality status is not associated with a right of residence, and only certain types of BOCs are eligible to be registered as British citizens under Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_British_citizenship">Acquisition of British citizenship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acquisition of British citizenship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>British Citizenship can be acquired in the following ways:\n</p>\n<ol><li><i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Lex_soli" class="mw-redirect" title="Lex soli">lex soli</a></i></i>: By birth in the UK or a qualified British Overseas Territory to a parent who is a British citizen at the time of the birth, or to a parent who is settled in the UK or that Overseas Territory</li>\n<li><i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Lex_sanguinis" class="mw-redirect" title="Lex sanguinis">lex sanguinis</a></i></i>: By birth abroad, which constitutes "by descent" if one of the parents is a British citizen otherwise than by descent (for example by birth, adoption, registration or naturalisation in the UK). British citizenship by descent is only transferable to one generation down from the parent who is a British citizen otherwise than by descent, if the child is born abroad.</li>\n<li>By <a href="#British_citizenship_by_naturalisation">naturalisation</a></li>\n<li>By <a href="#Registration_as_a_British_citizen">registration</a></li>\n<li>By <a href="#British_citizenship_by_adoption">adoption</a></li></ol>\n<p>For nationality purposes, the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> are generally treated as if they were part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Information about British citizenship and other kinds of British nationality is available from Her Majesty\'s Government.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Information is also available on provisions for reducing statelessness.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Persons acquiring citizenship by method (2) are called British citizens <i>by descent</i>; those acquiring citizenship by methods (1), (3) or (5) are called British citizens <i>otherwise than by descent</i>. British citizens by registration, method (4), may be either, depending on the circumstances. Only citizens <i>otherwise than by descent</i> can pass on their citizenship to their children born outside the UK or a British Overseas Territory automatically; British citizens <i>by descent</i> can pass on citizenship to their non-UK born children only by meeting certain UK residence requirements and registering them before the age of 18.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_citizenship_by_birth_in_the_United_Kingdom_or_a_qualified_British_Overseas_Territory">British citizenship by birth in the United Kingdom or a qualified British Overseas Territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: British citizenship by birth in the United Kingdom or a qualified British Overseas Territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From 1 January 1983, a child born in the UK to a parent who is a British citizen or \'settled\' in the UK is automatically a British citizen by birth. This provision is extended to children born to such parents in a <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territory</a> other than <a href="/wiki/Akrotiri_and_Dhekelia" title="Akrotiri and Dhekelia">Akrotiri and Dhekelia</a> after 21 May 2002. Since 13 January 2010, a child born to a parent who is a member of the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a> at the time of birth also automatically acquires British citizenship if he or she was born in the UK or a qualified British Overseas Territory.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Only one parent needs to meet this requirement.</li>\n<li>\'Settled\' status usually means the parent is resident in the UK or a <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territory</a> and has the <a href="/wiki/Right_of_abode_(United_Kingdom)" title="Right of abode (United Kingdom)">right of abode</a> (or similar status), or holds <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_Leave_to_Remain" class="mw-redirect" title="Indefinite Leave to Remain">Indefinite Leave to Remain</a> (ILR), or is the citizen of an EU/EEA country and has permanent residence, or otherwise unrestricted by immigration laws to remain in the UK or that Overseas Territory.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Irish citizens in the UK are deemed settled for this purpose.</li>\n<li>To qualify under the armed forces provision, the parent must be a member of the armed forces at the time of the child\'s birth.</li>\n<li>Special rules exist for cases where a parent of a child is a citizen of a <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> or <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a> member state, or <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. The law in this respect was changed on 2 October 2000 and 30 April 2006. See <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law#Citizens_of_EEA_States_and_Switzerland" title="British nationality law">below</a> for details.</li>\n<li>For children born before 1 July 2006, if only the father meets this requirement the parents must be married. Marriage subsequent to the birth is normally enough to confer British citizenship from that point.</li>\n<li>Where the father is not married to the mother, the Home Office usually registers the child as British provided an application is made and the child would have been British otherwise. The child must be under 18 on the date of application.</li>\n<li>Where, after the child\'s birth, a parent subsequently acquires British citizenship or "settled" status, the child can be registered as a British citizen using Form MN1 provided he/she is aged under 18.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA1981s1(3)_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA1981s1(3)-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RegisterBornAfter1983_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RegisterBornAfter1983-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>If the child lives in the UK until the age of 10, there is a lifetime entitlement to register as a British citizen using Form T. The immigration status of the child and his/her parents is irrelevant. During each of the first 10 years of the child\'s life, he/she must not have spent over 90 days outside the UK (unless there were \'special circumstances\'). The applicant must be of good character at the time the application is made.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA1981s1(4)_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA1981s1(4)-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RegisterBornAfter1983_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RegisterBornAfter1983-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>A child born in the United Kingdom who is and has always been stateless may also qualify on the basis of a period of 5, rather than 10, years residence, using Form S3.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Special provisions may apply for the child to acquire British citizenship if a parent is a British Overseas citizen or British subject, or if the child is stateless.</li></ul>\n<p>Even if a child born in the UK on or after 1 January 1983 but does not acquire British citizenship at birth, the child is considered a lawful resident in the UK and is not required to apply for leave to remain.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The child, however, is subject to immigration control and the child\'s parent(s) can choose to apply for regularisation of the child\'s immigration status by the grant of leave to remain (for the same period as that held by the parent(s)). If the child leaves the UK, he/she must hold leave to enter or remain in order to return to the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1983">Before 1983</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Before 1983">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Between 1949 and 1982, birth in the UK or a <a href="/wiki/Crown_Colony" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Colony">Crown Colony</a> was sufficient in itself to confer the status of Citizen of United Kingdom and Colonies (CUKC) irrespective of the status of parents, although only CUKCs with a connection to the UK (i.e., birth in the UK or has a UK-born parent or grandparent) would have right of abode in the UK after 1971 and would eventually become British citizens in 1983. CUKCs without a connection to the UK would become either British Overseas Territories citizens or British Overseas citizens in 1983 depending on whether they have a connection with another BOT.\n</p><p>The only exception to this rule were children of diplomats and enemy aliens. This exception did not apply to most visiting forces, so, in general, children born in the UK before 1983 to visiting military personnel (e.g. US forces stationed in the UK) were CUKCs connected with UK and would become British citizens in 1983, albeit as a second nationality.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_citizenship_by_descent">British citizenship by descent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: British citizenship by descent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><span id="British_citizenship_by_descent"></span>\n"British citizenship by descent" is the category for the children born outside the UK or an Overseas Territory to a British citizen. Rules for acquiring British citizenship by descent depend on when the person was born.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_1983">From 1983</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: From 1983">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A child born outside the UK on or after 1 January 1983 (or outside a British Overseas Territory on or after 21 May 2002) automatically acquires British citizenship <i>by descent</i> if either parent is a British citizen<i> otherwise than by descent</i> at the time of the child\'s birth. \n</p>\n<ul><li>At least one parent must be a British citizen <i>otherwise than by descent</i>.</li>\n<li>As a general rule, an unmarried father cannot pass on British citizenship automatically in the case of a child born before 1 July 2006. If the parents marry subsequent to the birth the child normally becomes a British citizen at that point if <i>legitimated</i> by the marriage and the father was eligible to pass on British citizenship. If the unmarried British father was domiciled in a country that treated (at the date of birth of the child born before 1 July 2006) a child born to unmarried parents in the same way as a child born to married parents, then the father passed on British citizenship automatically to his child, even though the child was born before 1 July 2006 to unmarried parents.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Such countries are listed in the UK Home Office Immigration and Passport Services publication "Legitimation and Domicile".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Where the parent is a British citizen <i>by descent</i> additional requirements apply. In the most common scenario, the parent is normally expected to have lived in the UK for three consecutive years and apply to register the child as a British citizen while the child is a minor (clause 43, <a href="/wiki/Borders,_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Act_2009" title="Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009">Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009</a>, effective from 13 January 2010). Prior to this date, the age limit was 12 months.</li>\n<li>Before 21 May 2002, British Overseas Territories were treated as \'overseas\' for nationality purposes. The exceptions were <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>,  British citizens under the <a href="/wiki/British_Nationality_Act_1981" title="British Nationality Act 1981">British Nationality Act 1981</a>; and the Falkland Islands, granted British citizenship following the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> under the <a href="/wiki/British_Nationality_(Falkland_Islands)_Act_1983" title="British Nationality (Falkland Islands) Act 1983">British Nationality (Falkland Islands) Act 1983</a>. Hence, children born to such parents on a British Overseas Territory other than those listed above acquired British citizenship <i>by descent</i> if they were born prior to 21 May 2002, while children born on or after that day on a British Overseas Territory (other than <a href="/wiki/Akrotiri_and_Dhekelia" title="Akrotiri and Dhekelia">Akrotiri and Dhekelia</a>) acquired British citizenship <i>otherwise by descent</i> as UK-born children.</li>\n<li>Children born overseas to parents on <i>Crown Service</i> are normally granted British citizenship <i>otherwise than by descent</i>, so their status is the same as it would have been had they been born in the UK.</li>\n<li>In exceptional cases, the Home Secretary may register a child of parents who are British <i>by descent</i> as a British citizen under discretionary provisions, for example if the child is stateless, or a second or subsequent generation born abroad into a British citizen family with strong links with the UK, or in \'compassionate circumstances\'.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1983_2">Before 1983</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Before 1983">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Before 1983, as a general rule CUKC status was transmitted automatically only for one generation, with registration in infancy possible for subsequent generations. Transmission was from the father only, and only if the parents were married. (See <a href="/wiki/History_of_British_nationality_law" title="History of British nationality law">History of British nationality law</a>.)\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Children_ineligible_for_British_citizenship_at_birth">Children ineligible for British citizenship at birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Children ineligible for British citizenship at birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Children born outside the UK before 1 January 1983 to a CUKC mother who became a British citizen on 1 January 1983 and a foreign father are not British citizens by birth, and neither are children born between 1 January 1983 to 1 July 2006 to a British citizen father and a foreign mother out of wedlock.\n</p><p>From 20 July 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Borders,_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Act_2009" title="Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009">Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009</a> provides that a person born outside the UK to a British mother may be entitled to register as a British citizen <i>by descent</i> if that person was born before 1 January 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Before the 2009 changes, only persons born <i>after</i> 7 February 1961 and before 1 January 1983 were eligible for this provision that came into force in April 2003. Requirements for successful registration with form UKM are that the applicant be a child of a British mother born before 1983 and be of good character and attend a <a href="#British_citizenship_ceremonies">citizenship ceremony</a>. Since 2010, there is no longer an application fee (of \xc2\xa3540). Applicants do however still have to pay \xc2\xa380 for the citizenship ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 6 April 2015, a child born out of wedlock before 1 July 2006 to a British father is entitled to register as a British citizen <i>by descent</i> under the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_2014" title="Immigration Act 2014">Immigration Act 2014</a> using form UKF.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Such child must also meet character requirements, pay relevant processing fees and attend a citizenship ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> However, if the applicant has a claim to register as a British citizen under other clauses of the British Nationality Act 1981, or has already acquired British citizenship after being legitimised, the application will be refused.\n</p><p>Alternatively, if already resident in the UK, these children may seek <i>naturalisation</i> as a British citizen, which gives transmissible British citizenship <i>otherwise than by descent</i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_citizenship_by_adoption">British citizenship by adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: British citizenship by adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A child adopted by a British citizen acquires British citizenship <i>automatically</i> only if:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the adoption order is made by a court in the UK, Channel Islands, Isle of Man or Falkland Islands on or after 1 January 1983, or in another British Overseas Territory on or after 21 May 2002; or</li>\n<li>it is a Convention adoption under the <i>1993 <a href="/wiki/Hague_Adoption_Convention" title="Hague Adoption Convention">Hague Convention</a> on <a href="/wiki/International_adoption" title="International adoption">Intercountry Adoption</a></i> effected on or after 1 June 2003 and the adopters are habitually resident in the UK on that date.</li></ul>\n<p>In both cases, at least one adoptive parent must be a British citizen on the date of the adoption.\n</p><p>The requirements are different for persons adopted before 1983.\n</p><p>In all other cases, an application for registration of the child as a British citizen must be made before the child is 18. Usually this is granted provided the Secretary of State accepts the adoption is <i>bona fide</i> and the child would have been a British citizen if the natural child of the adopters. Usually the adoption must have taken place under the law of a \'designated country\' (most developed nations along with some others are \'designated\' for this purpose) and be recognised in the UK. This is the standard method for children adopted by British citizens permanently resident overseas to acquire British citizenship.\n</p><p>The cancellation or annulment of an adoption order does not cause loss of British citizenship acquired by that adoption.\n</p><p>British children adopted by non-British nationals do not lose British nationality, even if they acquire a foreign nationality as a The remaining few became British Overseas citizens.\n</p><p>Before the handover in 1997, former BDTCs from Hong Kong had been able to acquire British citizenship under legislation passed in 1990, 1996 and 1997. In other cases, certain persons may already hold British citizenship as a matter of entitlement or through registration under normal procedures.\n</p><p>Although it is no longer possible to acquire British National (Overseas) status after 31 December 1997, stateless children born to such parents are entitled to British Overseas citizenship and can subsequently apply to register as British citizens under the Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002. Since 2009, BN(O)s without other nationalities or citizenship are able to register as British citizens under the <a href="/wiki/Borders,_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Act_2009" title="Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009">Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009</a> as well.\n</p><p>British citizens and BN(O)s who are of full or partial Chinese descent are also Chinese nationals under Chinese law unless they have renounced their Chinese nationality with the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_SAR_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Hong Kong SAR Government">Hong Kong SAR Government</a>. As China does not recognise multiple nationality, those persons are considered by China as solely Chinese nationals before and after the handover of Hong Kong and hence are not eligible for <a href="/wiki/Consular_protection" class="mw-redirect" title="Consular protection">consular protection</a> when on Chinese soil. Although holding the same nationality under the Chinese nationality law, Chinese nationals with a connection to Hong Kong or Macau have been categorised differently from Chinese nationals domiciled in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a>.\n</p><p>In February 2006, in response to extensive representations made by Lord Avebury and Tameem Ebrahim,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> British authorities announced that 600 British citizenship applications of ethnic minority children of Indian descent from Hong Kong were wrongly refused.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The applications dated from the period July 1997 onwards. Where applicants in such cases confirm that they still wish to receive British citizenship, the decision is reconsidered on request. No additional fee is required in such cases. A template to request reconsideration is available for those who want a prior application reconsidered.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persons_born_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland">Persons born in the Republic of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Persons born in the Republic of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_and_the_Republic_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="British nationality and the Republic of Ireland">British nationality and the Republic of Ireland</a></div>\n<p>Approximately 800,000 persons born before 1949 and connected with the Republic of Ireland remain entitled to claim British subject status under section 31 of the 1981 Act.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descendants_of_the_Electress_Sophia_of_Hanover">Descendants of the Electress Sophia of Hanover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Descendants of the Electress Sophia of Hanover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Eligible descendants from the <a href="/wiki/Electress_Sophia_of_Hanover" class="mw-redirect" title="Electress Sophia of Hanover">Electress Sophia of Hanover</a> may hold British Overseas citizenship based on their status as British subjects before 1949. Where such a person acquired a right of abode in the UK before 1983, it is possible for British citizenship to have been acquired. See also <a href="/wiki/History_of_British_nationality_law" title="History of British nationality law">History of British nationality law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sophia_Naturalization_Act_1705" title="Sophia Naturalization Act 1705">Sophia Naturalization Act 1705</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_British_nationality">Loss of British nationality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Loss of British nationality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renunciation_and_resumption_of_British_nationality">Renunciation and resumption of British nationality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Renunciation and resumption of British nationality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>All categories of British nationality can be renounced by a declaration made to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Home_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for the Home Department">Home Secretary</a>. A person ceases to be a British national on the date the Home Secretary registers the declaration of renunciation. If a declaration is registered in the expectation of acquiring another citizenship but one is not acquired within six months of the registration, it does not take effect and the person remains a British national.\n</p><p>Renunciations made to other authorities (such as the <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Allegiance_(United_States)" title="Oath of Allegiance (United States)">general renunciation made as part of the US naturalisation ceremony</a>) are not recognised by the UK. The forms must be sent through the UK Border Agency\'s citizenship renunciation process.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\nThere are provisions for the resumption of British citizenship or British overseas territories citizenship renounced for the purpose of gaining or retaining another citizenship. This can generally only be done once as a matter of entitlement. Further opportunities to resume British citizenship are discretionary.\n</p><p>British subjects, British Overseas citizens and British Nationals (Overseas) cannot resume their British nationality after renunciation.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automatic_loss_of_British_nationality">Automatic loss of British nationality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Automatic loss of British nationality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>British subjects (other than British subjects by virtue of a connection with the Republic of Ireland) and British protected persons lose British nationality upon acquiring any other form of nationality.\n</p>\n<ul><li>These provisions do not apply to British citizens.</li>\n<li>British Overseas Territories citizens (BOTCs) who acquire another nationality do not lose their BOTC status but they may be liable to lose <a href="/wiki/Belonger_status" title="Belonger status">belonger status</a> in their home territory under its immigration laws. Such persons are advised to contact the governor of that territory for information.</li>\n<li>British Overseas citizens (BOCs) do not lose their BOC status upon acquisition of another citizenship but any entitlement to registration as a British citizen on the grounds of having no other nationality no longer exist after acquiring another citizenship.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revocation_of_British_nationality">Revocation of British nationality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Revocation of British nationality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Immigration,_Asylum_and_Nationality_Act_2006" title="Immigration, Asylum and Nationality Act 2006">Immigration, Asylum and Nationality Act 2006</a>, people with dual nationality who are British nationals can be deprived of their British citizenship if the Secretary of State is satisfied that "deprivation is conducive to the public good"; there is a right of appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> This provision has been in force since 16 June 2006 when the Immigration, Nationality and Asylum Act 2006 (Commencement No 1) Order 2006 came into force.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Loss of British nationality in this way applies to people born in the UK as British citizens and who also hold another nationality.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> As the provision applies only to dual nationals, its application cannot render a person stateless.\n</p><p>The Home Office does not issue information on these cases and is resistant to answering questions,<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> for example under the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Information_Act_2000" title="Freedom of Information Act 2000">Freedom of Information Act 2000</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> As of  15&#160;July&#160;2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> at least 17 people had been deprived of their British citizenship, in most cases on the recommendation of <a href="/wiki/MI5" title="MI5">MI5</a>. It appears that the government usually waits until the person has left Britain, then sends a warning notice to their British home and signs a deprivation order a day or two later.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Appeals are heard at the highly secretive <a href="/wiki/Special_Immigration_Appeals_Commission" title="Special Immigration Appeals Commission">Special Immigration Appeals Commission</a> (SIAC), where the government can submit evidence that cannot be seen or challenged by the appellant.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_43-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Previously since 2003 under the <a href="/wiki/Nationality,_Immigration_and_Asylum_Act_2002" title="Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002">Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002</a>, British nationals could be deprived of their citizenship only if the Secretary of State was satisfied they were responsible for acts seriously prejudicial to the vital interests of the United Kingdom or an Overseas Territory.\n</p><p>British nationals who are <i>naturalised</i> or <i>registered</i> may lose British nationality if it was obtained by fraud or concealment of a <i>material fact</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_nationality_and_multiple_citizenship">Multiple nationality and multiple citizenship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Multiple nationality and multiple citizenship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As of  August 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=British_nationality_law&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, there is no restriction in UK law on a British national holding in addition citizenship of other countries; indeed the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> explicitly recognises the right of qualified residents of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> to be British or Irish &#160;&#8211;&#32; or both.\n</p><p>Different rules apply to British protected persons and certain British subjects (that do not apply to British citizens). A person who is a British subject other than by connection with the Republic of Ireland loses that status on acquiring any other nationality or citizenship,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and a British protected person ceases to be such on acquiring any other nationality or citizenship.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Although British Overseas citizens are not subject to loss of citizenship, British Overseas citizens may lose an entitlement to register as a British citizen under <i>s4B</i> of the 1981 Act if they acquire any other citizenship.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A number of other countries do not allow <a href="/wiki/Multiple_citizenship" title="Multiple citizenship">multiple citizenship</a>. If a person has British nationality and is also a national of a country that does not allow dual nationality, the authorities of that country may regard the person as having lost that nationality or may refuse to recognise the British nationality. British nationals who acquire the nationality of a country that does not allow dual nationality may be required by the other country to renounce British nationality to retain the other citizenship. None of this affects a person\'s national status under UK law.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Under the international <a href="/wiki/Master_Nationality_Rule" title="Master Nationality Rule">Master Nationality Rule</a> a state may not give <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_protection" title="Diplomatic protection">diplomatic protection</a> to one of its nationals with dual nationality in a country where the person also holds citizenship.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A British subject who acquired foreign citizenship by naturalisation before 1949 were deemed to have lost his or her British subject status at the time<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. No specific provisions were made in the 1948 legislation for such former British subjects to acquire or otherwise resume British nationality, and hence such a person would not be a British citizen today. However, women who lost British nationality on marriage to a foreign man before 1949 were deemed to have reacquired British subject status immediately before the coming into force of the 1948 act.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The UK is a signatory to the Convention on the Reduction of Cases of Multiple Nationality and on Military Obligations in Cases of Multiple Nationality (1963 Strasbourg Convention). Chapter 1 requires that persons naturalised by another European member country automatically forfeit their original nationality<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> but the UK ratified only Chapter 2, so the convention does not limit the ability of British citizens to become dual citizens of other European countries.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_citizenship_ceremonies">British citizenship width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #A8ACAB; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Visa required prior to arrival</div></div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Visa_requirements_for_British_citizens" title="Visa requirements for British citizens">Visa requirements for British citizens</a></div>\n<p>Visa requirements for <a href="/wiki/British_citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="British citizen">British citizens</a> are administrative entry restrictions by the authorities of other states placed on citizens of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. In 2017, British citizens had visa-free or visa on arrival access to 173 countries and territories, ranking the <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passport</a> 4th in terms of travel freedom (tied with Austrian, Belgian, Dutch, French, Luxembourgish, Norwegian and Singaporean passports) according to the <a href="/wiki/Visa_(document)#Visa_restrictions" class="mw-redirect" title="Visa (document)">Henley visa restrictions index</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/World_Tourism_Organization" title="World Tourism Organization">World Tourism Organization</a> also published a report on 15 January 2016 ranking the British passport 1st in the world (tied with Denmark, Finland, Germany, Italy, Luxembourg and Singapore) in terms of travel freedom, with a mobility index of 160 (out of 215 with no visa weighted by 1, visa on arrival weighted by 0.7, eVisa by 0.5, and traditional visa weighted by 0).<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Visa requirements for other classes of <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_national" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom national">British nationals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/British_Nationals_(Overseas)" class="mw-redirect" title="British Nationals (Overseas)">British Nationals (Overseas)</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="British Overseas Citizen">British Overseas Citizens</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories_Citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="British Overseas Territories Citizen">British Overseas Territories Citizens</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Protected_Person" class="mw-redirect" title="British Protected Person">British Protected Persons</a> or <a href="/wiki/British_Subjects" class="mw-redirect" title="British Subjects">British Subjects</a> are different.\n</p><p>The British nationality is ranked thirteenth in <a href="/wiki/The_Quality_of_Nationality_Index" title="The Quality of Nationality Index">Nationality Index</a> (QNI). This index differs from the <a href="/wiki/Henley_Passport_Index" title="Henley Passport Index">Visa Restrictions Index</a>, which focuses on external factors including travel freedom. The QNI considers, in addition, to travel freedom on internal factors such as peace &amp; stability, economic strength, and human development as well. <sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a
