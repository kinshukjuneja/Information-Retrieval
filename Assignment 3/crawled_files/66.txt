For a guide to IPA symbols, see <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">Help:IPA</a>.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b><i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">Sercquiais</i></b> also known as <b>Sarkese</b> or <b>Sark-French</b> (<b><i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">L\xc3\xa9 S\xc3\xa8rtchais</i></b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman</a> dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bailiwick_of_Guernsey" title="Bailiwick of Guernsey">Bailiwick of Guernsey</a>). In the island it is sometimes known, slightly disparagingly, as the "<a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">patois</a>", a French term meaning "<a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">regional language</a>".\n</p><p>Sarkese is in fact a descendant of the 16th century <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais" title="J\xc3\xa8rriais">J\xc3\xa8rriais</a> used by the original colonists, 40 families mostly from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ouen,_Jersey" title="Saint Ouen, Jersey">Saint Ouen, Jersey</a><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_colonists"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sercquiais#endnote_colonists">[1]</a></sup> who settled the then uninhabited island, although influenced in the interim by <a href="/wiki/Guern%C3%A9siais" title="Guern\xc3\xa9siais">Guern\xc3\xa9siais</a> (the dialect of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>). It is still spoken by older inhabitants of the island. Although the <a href="/wiki/Lexis_(linguistics)" title="Lexis (linguistics)">lexis</a> is heavily anglicised, the <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> retains features lost in J\xc3\xa8rriais since the 16th century. Most of the local placenames are in Sarkese. In former times, there may have been two subdialects of Sercquiais.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is also closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Auregnais" title="Auregnais">Auregnais</a> (<a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a>) dialect, as well as Continental Norman.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input id="Written_Sercquiais">Written Sercquiais</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sercquiais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Written Sercquiais">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Relatively little Sercquiais has been transcribed, and as there is no widely accepted form, it has received a certain amount of stigma as a result. A ruler of Sark, <a href="/wiki/Sibyl_Hathaway" title="Sibyl Hathaway">Sibyl Hathaway</a>, who was a speaker herself, claimed that it could "never be written down", and this myth has continued in the years since then.\n</p><p>The earliest published text in Sercquiais so far identified is the <i><a href="/wiki/Parable_of_the_Sower" title="Parable of the Sower">Parable of the Sower</a></i> (<i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">Parabol du smeaux</i>) from the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a>. Prince <a href="/wiki/Louis_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Louis Lucien Bonaparte">Louis Lucien Bonaparte</a>, linguist, visited the Channel Islands in September 1862 in order to transcribe samples of the insular language varieties, which he subsequently published in 1863:\n&#x5b;\n</p>\n<dl><dd>(3) [...] <i>L\'chen qui s\xc3\xa8me s\'n allit s\'mai</i>;</dd>\n<dd>(4) <i>Et t\xc3\xa0ndis qu\' i s\'maitt une partie d\' la s\'menche quitt le long du ch\'m\xc3\xacnn et l\'s oesiaux du ciel v\xc3\xacndrint et i la m\xc3\xa0ndgirent.</i></dd>\n<dd>(5) <i>Une a\xc3\xbbtre quitt dans d\'s endr\xc3\xa9ts roquieurs, o\xc3\xb9 alle n\'avait pas fort de terre; et ou l\'vist ossivite, par\xc3\xa7e que la terre o\xc3\xb9 al\' \'tait n\'\xc3\xa9tait pas ben avant.</i></dd>\n<dd>(6) <i>Mais l\'sol\xc3\xa9 se l\'vitt et ou fut brulaie; et coumme ou n\'avait pas d\'rachinnes, ou s\'quitt.</i></dd>\n<dd>(7) <i>Une a\xc3\xbbtre quitt dans d\'s \xc3\xa9pinnes, et l\'s \xc3\xa9pinnes v\xc3\xacndrent \xc3\xa0 craitre, et l\'etoupidrent.</i></dd>\n<dd>(8) <i>Une a\xc3\xbbtre enfin quitt dans d\'bouanne terre, et ou portit du fritt; quiq\' gr\xc3\xa2ins r\xc3\xa8ndirent chent pour un, d\'a\xc3\xbbtres sess\xc3\xa0nte, et d\'a\xc3\xbbtres trente.</i></dd>\n<dd>(9) <i>L\'chen qu\'a d\'s oureilles pour ouit qu\' il ou\xc3\xaat.</i> (S. Makyu. Chap. title="J\xc3\xa8rriais">J\xc3\xa8rriais</a>. Palatalisation in J\xc3\xa8rriais of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/k/</span> to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t\xca\x83]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xa1/</span> to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d\xca\x92]</span> has the equivalent in Sercquiais of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/kj/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xa1j/</span>. For example, <i>hiccup</i> is <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">hitchet</i> in J\xc3\xa8rriais and <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">hekyet</i> in Sercquiais; <i>war</i> is respectively <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">dg\xc3\xa8rre</i> and <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">gyer</i>.\n</p><p>Palatalisation of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/tj/</span> in J\xc3\xa8rriais leads to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t\xca\x83]</span>, but in Sercquiais <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> is generally retained: <i>profession, trade</i> in Sercquiais is <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">meeti</i>, whereas J\xc3\xa8rriais has palatalised to <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">m\xc3\xaatchi</i>.\n</p><p><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d\xca\x92]</span> is retained in Sercquiais where J\xc3\xa8rriais has reduced to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x92]</span>, as in <i>to eat</i>: <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">m\xc3\xa3dji</i> (Sercquiais) \xe2\x80\x93 <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">mangi</i> (J\xc3\xa8rriais).\n</p><p>Final consonants of masculine nouns in the singular are in free variation with null in all positions except in <a href="/wiki/Liaison_(French)" title="Liaison (French)">liaison</a>. Final consonants are usually pronounced at ends of phrases. Final consonants are always lost in plural forms of masculine nouns. A <i>cat</i> may therefore be <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">kat</i> or <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">ka</i> in Sercquiais, but <i>cats</i> are <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">kaa</i>. For comparison, J\xc3\xa8rriais <i>cat</i> is usually pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ka/</span>, and the plural has the long vowel as in Sercquiais. It can also therefore be seen that length is <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemic</a> and may denote plurality.\n</p><p>Sercquiais has also retained final consonants that have been entirely lost in J\xc3\xa8rriais, such as final <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> in <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">pret</i> (<i>meadow</i> \xe2\x80\x93 <i lang="roa" title="Romance languages collective text">pr\xc3\xa9</i> in J\xc3\xa8rriais as in French).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Metathesis_(linguistics)" title="Metathesis (linguistics)">Metathesis</a> of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> is uncommon in Sercquiais, and in J\xc3\xa8rriais, by comparison with Guern\xc3\xa9siais.\n</p>\n<table>\n\n<tbody><tr>\n<th style="width:100px;">Sercquiais\n</th>\n<th style="width:100px;">J\xc3\xa8rriais\n</th>\n<th
