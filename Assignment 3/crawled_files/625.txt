Her first Commanding Officer is Commodore <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Kyd" title="Jerry Kyd">Jerry Kyd</a>, who had previously commanded the carriers <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ark_Royal_(R07)" title="HMS Ark Royal (R07)">HMS&#160;<i>Ark Royal</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Illustrious_(R06)" title="HMS Illustrious (R06)">HMS&#160;<i>Illustrious</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> As Captain of HMS <i>Queen Elizabeth</i>, Kyd will wear the Royal Navy rank of Captain while retaining the substantive rank of Commodore.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Queen Elizabeth</i> has no catapults or arrestor wires and is instead designed to operate <a href="/wiki/V/STOL" title="V/STOL">V/STOL</a> aircraft; the air wing will typically consist of <a href="/wiki/F-35B_Lightning_II" class="mw-redirect" title="F-35B Lightning II">F-35B Lightning II</a> <a href="/wiki/Multirole_combat_aircraft" title="Multirole combat aircraft">multirole fighters</a> and <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">Merlin</a> helicopters for <a href="/wiki/Airborne_early_warning" class="mw-redirect" title="Airborne early warning">airborne early warning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine warfare</a>. The design emphasises flexibility, with accommodation for 250 <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a> and the ability to support them with attack helicopters and large troop transports such as <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Chinook_(UK_variants)" title="Boeing Chinook (UK variants)">Chinooks</a>. She is the second <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> vessel to bear the name <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> and is based at <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Portsmouth" title="HMNB Portsmouth">HMNB Portsmouth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Portsmouth_Naval_Base_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Portsmouth_Naval_Base-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 25 July 2007, the then Defence Secretary <a href="/wiki/Des_Browne" title="Des Browne">Des Browne</a>, announced the order for two new carriers.<sup id="cite_ref-MOD_confirms_carrier_order_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MOD_confirms_carrier_order-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> At the time of approval the first carrier was expected to enter service in July 2015 and the budget was \xc2\xa34.085b for two ships.<sup id="cite_ref-MPR2010_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPR2010-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The financial crisis led to a political decision in December 2008 to slow production, delaying <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> until May 2016. This added \xc2\xa31.560b to the cost.<sup id="cite_ref-MPR2010_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPR2010-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> By March 2010 the budget was estimated at \xc2\xa35.900b<sup id="cite_ref-MPR2010_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPR2010-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and in November 2013 the contract was renegotiated with a budget of \xc2\xa36.200b.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The in-service date was further extended to 2020 in the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defence_and_Security_Review_2010" title="Strategic Defence and Security Review 2010">Strategic Defence and Security Review</a> in October 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-sdsr2010_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sdsr2010-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Construction of <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> began in 2009. The assembly took place in the Firth of Forth at <a href="/wiki/Rosyth_Dockyard" title="Rosyth Dockyard">Rosyth Dockyard</a> from nine blocks built in six UK shipyards: <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Surface_Ships" class="mw-redirect" title="BAE Systems Surface Ships">BAE Systems Surface Ships</a> in Glasgow, Babcock at Appledore, Babcock at Rosyth, <a href="/wiki/A%26P_Tyne" class="mw-redirect" title="A&amp;P Tyne">A&amp;P Tyne</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hebburn" title="Hebburn">Hebburn</a>, BAE at <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Portsmouth" title="HMNB Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cammell_Laird" title="Cammell Laird">Cammell Laird</a> (flight decks) at <a href="/wiki/Birkenhead" title="Birkenhead">Birkenhead</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Two of the lower main blocks, together weighing more than 6,000 tonnes and forming part of the base of the ship, were assembled and joined into one piece on 30 June 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> On 16 August 2011, the 8,000-tonne Lower Block 03 of <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> left BAE Systems Surface Ships\' Govan shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> on a large ocean-going barge. Travelling 600 miles (970&#160;km) around the northern coast of Scotland, the block arrived at Rosyth on the evening of 20 August 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> On 28 October 2012, an 11,000-tonne section of the carrier began a lengthy journey around the south coast of England, avoiding bad weather from the shipbuilding hall at Govan to the Rosyth dockyard; it arrived on 21 November.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The forward island was constructed at BAE Portsmouth and attached on 14 March 2013; the aft island was attached in June 2013. The <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_ski-jump" class="mw-redirect" title="Aircraft ski-jump">ski jump</a> was added in November 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-Hargreaves201312_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hargreaves201312-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> leaving just the elevators and radar to be lifted into place.<sup id="cite_ref-Hargreaves201312_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hargreaves201312-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> By September 2013 <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> was 80% complete internally.<sup id="cite_ref-AW20130911_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AW20130911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>She is two and a half times the size of the <i>Invincible</i>-class, and has the ability to carry approximately three times as many aircraft. Despite this, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> has marginally fewer crew than the <i>Invincible</i>-class. She is approximately three times as large as <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ocean_(L12)" title="HMS Ocean (L12)">HMS&#160;<i>Ocean</i></a>. The ship has two superstructures, or islands, one for navigation and ship\'s operations and the other for flight control and aerial operations. The islands can take on each other\'s function in an emergency.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_ceremony">Naming ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Queen Elizabeth</i> was named at Rosyth on 4 July 2014, by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>, who said that the warship "marks a new phase in our naval history". Instead of smashing the traditional bottle of champagne on the hull, she used a bottle of whisky from the <a href="/wiki/Bowmore_distillery" title="Bowmore distillery">Bowmore distillery</a> on <a href="/wiki/Islay" title="Islay">Islay</a>.\n</p><p>The ceremony was attended by the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Duke of Edinburgh</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Admiral_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord High Admiral (United Kingdom)">Lord High Admiral</a>), Admiral <a href="/wiki/George_Zambellas" title="George Zambellas">George Zambellas</a> (<a href="/wiki/First_Sea_Lord" title="First Sea Lord">First Sea Lord</a>), senior naval officers from the United States and France, and by politicians including <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> and his immediate predecessor) and <a href="/wiki/Alex_Salmond" title="Alex Salmond">Alex Salmond</a> (the then <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">First Minister of Scotland</a>). The official piece of music <a href="/wiki/HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_March" title="HMS Queen Elizabeth March">HMS Queen Elizabeth March</a>, composed by WO2 Bandmaster John Morrish, was performed at the naming ceremony by HM Royal Marines Band, Scotland. This piece of music is a competition winning march chosen by the Carrier Alliance Group, performed and recorded by the Royal Marines Massed Bands.\n</p><p>The ceremony also featured a fly-past by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Arrows" title="Red Arrows">Red Arrows</a> and a second comprising Royal Navy, Royal Air Force and British Army helicopters. <a href="/wiki/HMS_Illustrious_(R06)" title="HMS Illustrious (R06)">HMS&#160;<i>Illustrious</i></a> was berthed adjacent to <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> during the ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The ship was floated out of dry dock on the morning of 17 July 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-AW20140717_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AW20140717-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Fitting out was completed at the end of 2015 and the crew moved aboard in May 2016. On 24 May 2016, Commodore Jeremy Kyd assumed command of the ship from Captain Simon Petitt who had been the Senior Naval Officer since October 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Kyd_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kyd-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_trials">Sea trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a This was necessary as the most recent data available were 60 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Queen Elizabeth</i> sailed on 26 June 2017 to undergo sea trials.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc-20170627_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-20170627-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The first stage of the operation was to move the ship from inside the fitting out basin, via one of the access gates, into the Firth of Forth itself, before taking her under the three Forth bridge crossings.<sup id="cite_ref-seatrialsguardian_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seatrialsguardian-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Once this was accomplished, the ship took to the open sea off the east coast of Scotland to undertake the first set of trials, including handling and speed tests. During this period, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> was accompanied by a pair of <a href="/wiki/Type_23_frigate" title="Type 23 frigate">Type 23 frigates</a>, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Sutherland_(F81)" title="HMS Sutherland (F81)">HMS&#160;<i>Sutherland</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Iron_Duke_(F234)" title="HMS Iron Duke (F234)">HMS&#160;<i>Iron Duke</i></a>, acting as escorts.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The first aircraft to land on the ship was a <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">Merlin HM.2</a> of <a href="/wiki/820_Naval_Air_Squadron" title="820 Naval Air Squadron">820 Naval Air Squadron</a> on 3 July.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> arrived at her first stopover at <a href="/wiki/Invergordon" title="Invergordon">Invergordon</a>, where the ship was fuelled and provisioned. At this point inspections of the hull were carried out.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> This opportunity allowed for defect rectification to be carried out prior to the ship returning to sea. During the stopover at Invergordon, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> encountered <a href="/wiki/MS_Queen_Elizabeth" title="MS Queen Elizabeth">the cruise liner of the same name</a>, which was on a cruise around the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 8 August 2017, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> diverted from her sea trials to rendezvous with the ships engaged in Exercise \'Saxon Warrior\'; this allowed for a photo exercise in company with the American <a href="/wiki/Nimitz-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Nimitz-class aircraft carrier"><i>Nimitz</i>-class</a> aircraft carrier <a href="/wiki/USS_George_H.W._Bush" title="USS George H.W. Bush">USS&#160;<i>George H.W. Bush</i></a> and her battle group, comprising <a href="/wiki/USS_Donald_Cook" title="USS Donald Cook">USS&#160;<i>Donald Cook</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Philippine_Sea_(CG-58)" title="USS Philippine id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 30 October 2017, the ship departed Portsmouth for the first time for the second phase of her sea trials off the south and south-west coasts of England.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> For part of this second period of sea trials, she was accompanied by the <a href="/wiki/Type_45_destroyer" title="Type 45 destroyer">Type 45 destroyer</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dragon_(D35)" title="HMS Dragon (D35)">HMS&#160;<i>Dragon</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> returned to Portsmouth on 21 November 2017 to prepare for her official commissioning ceremony, which was held on 7 December 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Once the initial stage 1 and 2 sea trials were complete, the ship was commissioned into the Royal Navy on 7 December 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Following the ship\'s commissioning, she underwent a period of defect correction in Portsmouth; one instance was a leak through a seal in one of the propeller shafts, which the Royal Navy stated was not serious enough to keep <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> from her planned programme.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 2 February 2018, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> sailed from Portsmouth for a second time, for a six-week deployment to undergo the first phases of operational training; initially, the ship was scheduled to head into the <a href="/wiki/Western_Approaches" title="Western Approaches">Western Approaches</a> to undertake her initial <a href="/wiki/Flag_Officer_Sea_Training" title="Flag Officer Sea Training">Operational Sea Training (OST)</a> programme. Once complete, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> was then taken into the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> for both heavy weather testing and operations to begin helicopter certification, including with <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">Merlin Mk2 and Mk3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Chinook_(UK_variants)" title="Boeing Chinook (UK variants)">Chinook</a> helicopters. During this deployment, the ship made her first overseas port visit, stopping over in <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> from 9 to 12 February.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> During this, the ship also began initial amphibious assault trials, with <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a> from <a href="/wiki/42_Commando" title="42 Commando">42 Commando</a> embarked to simulate an air assault scenario.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> was also scheduled to undergo a first <a href="/wiki/Replenishment_at_sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Replenishment at sea">replenishment at sea</a> from <a href="/wiki/RFA_Tidespring_(A136)" title="RFA Tidespring (A136)">RFA&#160;<i>Tidespring</i></a>; although this had to be abandoned due to bad weather, this allowed both ships the opportunity to simulate the scenario in detail.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> returned to Portsmouth on 27 February 2018, berthing at Princess Royal Jetty.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> On 2 March 2018 in Portsmouth Harbour, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> successfully tested her port side Marine Evacuation System (MES), a series of bright orange inflatable escape chutes and rafts.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p><i>Queen Elizabeth</i> departed Portsmouth for a third time on 10 June 2018<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>, heading to the coasts of Cornwall for a second phase of helicopter flight trials. After the trials were completed, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> headed for home and performed her first replenishment at sea (RAS) refuelling operation with <i>Tidespring</i> on 21 June 2018 in the English Channel,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> then returned to Portsmouth on 23 June 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> \nOn return from the initial OST and helicopter certification work, further work was undertaken to prepare the ship for operation of fixed-wing aircraft, in conjunction with regular maintenance and installation of her final weapons fit. \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a 2017</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Westlant_18">Westlant 18</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Westlant 18">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Queen Elizabeth\'</i>s first significant deployment was planned to take place during the Summer of 2018, when the ship was to be taken across the Atlantic to begin flying trials with her fixed-wing aircraft. Although the planned programme for this cruise covers a range of areas, including the continued work-up of the <a href="/wiki/Commander_United_Kingdom_Carrier_Strike_Group" title="Commander United Kingdom Carrier Strike Group">Commander UK Carrier Strike Group</a> and his staff, embarked for a deployment aboard for the first time, and small-scale exercises to test the ship\'s ability to land troops in the secondary amphibious role, the primary purpose is the first phase of fixed-wing flying trials involving the F-35B. These will cover two separate periods of approximately 3-4 weeks each starting in mid-September, and will utilize a pair of the instrumented development aircraft from <a href="/wiki/VX-23" title="VX-23">VX-23</a>, the US Navy\'s air testing unit at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Patuxent_River" title="Naval Air Station Patuxent River">NAS Patuxent River</a>. The testing programme will see the first launch of an F-35 from a <a href="/wiki/Ski-jump_(aviation)" title="Ski-jump (aviation)">ski-jump</a> at sea (the F-35B has operated from the US Navy\'s assault ships, but these are not fitted with ski-jumps), and the first demonstration of the <a href="/wiki/SRVL" title="SRVL">Shipborne Rolling Vertical Landing</a> technique. In addition to the F-35 trials, it is planned that <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> will also begin qualifications for types of aircraft operated by the US armed forces, including the <a href="/wiki/Bell-Boeing_V-22_Osprey" class="mw-redirect" title="Bell-Boeing V-22 Osprey">V-22 Osprey</a>. As part of the deployment, the ship will make her first overseas port visit to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> in October, in between layovers at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">Naval Station Norfolk</a>, while there may also be some operations in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a>, to test the ship\'s hot weather performance, as well as being on hand if needed for any disaster relief operations during the hurricane season. <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> is scheduled to return to the UK in December.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AW20140717_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AW20140717-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>On 18 August 2018, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i>, sailed from Portsmouth on what was termed as "Westlant 18", carrying three Merlin Mk2 helicopters of 820 NAS. Later, on 20 August 2018, three additional Merlin Mk4 helicopters from 845 NAS were embarked to the ship.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> was then met by <a href="/wiki/HMS_Monmouth_(F235)" title="HMS Monmouth (F235)">HMS&#160;<i>Monmouth</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/RFA_Tiderace_(A137)" title="RFA Tiderace (A137)">RFA&#160;<i>Tiderace</i></a>, forming a carrier group.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> On 3 September 2018, <i>Monmouth\'</i>s Wildcat HMA2 helicopter became the first of its type to land aboard <i>Queen Elizabeth</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> On 5 September 2018, the two ships arrived at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Mayport" title="Naval Station Mayport">Naval Station Mayport</a> in north-east Florida, for a period of resupplying.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>. The ships remained in Florida for eight days before departing for the planned trip northwards to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">Naval Station Norfolk</a>. During this transit, it was planned to undertake an amphibious assault exercise on the coast of <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> using the embarked Merlin Mk4 helicopters, and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a> of <a href="/wiki/42_Commando" title="42 Commando">42 Commando</a>. However, due to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Florence" title="Hurricane Florence">Hurricane Florence</a>, this exercise was cancelled and the group instead sailed south; <i>Monmouth</i> headed to waters off <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a>, which acted as a windbreak, while <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> skirted around the south of the hurricane zone. The group eventually arrived at Norfolk on 17 September. During the period alongside at Norfolk, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> embarked a further 200 personnel of the F-35B Integrated Test Force, to undertake the testing and analysis of the two F-35B aircraft to be used during the initial set of flying trials.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>On Tuesday 25 September 2018, two US-owned F-35B test aircraft, based at Naval Air Station Patuxent River flew out to meet HMS <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> off the New Jersey coast.  The first F-35B landing was by Royal Navy Commander Nathan Gray in test aircraft BF-05. The second aircraft, BF-04, was landed by RAF Squadron Leader Andy Edgell.  After refuelling, Commander Gray then achieved the first F-35B takeoff from <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> using the ski-jump ramp.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> During the initial F-35B trials, <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> also began trials for the UK Carrier Group staff when the ship, along with HMS <i>Monmouth</i>, formed a task group with the US Navy destroyer <a href="/wiki/USS_Lassen_(DDG-82)" title="USS Lassen (DDG-82)">USS&#160;<i>Lassen</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The aircraft carrier is expected to reach "Full operational capability" by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-Hansard20120510_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hansard20120510-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft">Aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The two ships of the <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> class are each expected to be capable of carrying forty aircraft, a maximum of thirty-six F-35s and four helicopters.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> The 2010 <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defence_and_Security_Review_2010" title="Strategic Defence and Security Review 2010">SDSR</a> anticipated the routine peacetime deployment of twelve F-35Bs, but a typical warload will be 24 F-35Bs and some helicopters.<sup id="cite_ref-AW20130911_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AW20130911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> These could be a Maritime Force Protection package of nine anti-submarine Merlin HM2 and five Merlin Crowsnest for airborne early warning; alternatively a Littoral Manoeuvre package could include a mix of Royal Navy <a href="/wiki/Commando_Helicopter_Force" title="Commando Helicopter Force">Commando Helicopter Force</a> <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">Merlin HC4</a>, <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW159_Wildcat" title="AgustaWestland AW159 Wildcat">Wildcat AH1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Chinook_(UK_variants)" title="Boeing Chinook (UK variants)">Chinooks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Army_Air_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="Army Air Corps (United Kingdom)">Army Air Corps</a> <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_Apache" title="AgustaWestland Apache">Apaches</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AW20130911_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AW20130911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> As of  September 2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> six landing spots are planned, but the deck could be marked out for the operation of ten medium helicopters at once, allowing the lift of a <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">company</a> of 250 troops.<sup id="cite_ref-AW20130911_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AW20130911-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The hangars are designed for CH-47 Chinook operations without blade folding and for the <a href="/wiki/Bell_Boeing_V-22_Osprey" title="Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey">V-22 Osprey</a> tiltrotor, whilst the aircraft lifts can accommodate two Chinooks with unfolded blades.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Passenger.2Fcrew_transfer_boats"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Passenger/crew_transfer_boats">Passenger/crew transfer boats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Passenger/crew transfer boats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The two ships of the <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> class will each carry four passenger transfer boats (PTBs) made by Blyth-based company Alnmaritec.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Each PTB carries 36 passengers and two crew to operate the vessel. The first boat named is named <i>Swordfish</i>, after the World War II-era <a href="/wiki/Fairey_Swordfish" title="Fairey Swordfish">aircraft of that name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> The boat is 13.1&#160;m long and <a href="/wiki/Davit" title="Davit">davit</a>-launched. To enable the craft to fit into the docking area the navigation and radar masts are fitted with Linak actuators so that they can be lowered automatically from the command console. The enclosed cabin is heated and there is a set of <a href="/wiki/Head_(watercraft)" title="Head (watercraft)">heads</a> forward.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The second of the four PTBs is named <i>Buccaneer</i> after the <a href="/wiki/Blackburn_Buccaneer" title="Blackburn Buccaneer">Blackburn Buccaneer</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> and the third is <i>Sea Vixen,</i> named after the <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Sea_Vixen" title="De Havilland Sea Vixen">De Havilland Sea Vixen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons_systems">Weapons systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Weapons systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Defensive weapons include the <a href="/wiki/Phalanx_CIWS" title="Phalanx CIWS">Phalanx Close-In Weapons System</a> for <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-aircraft">anti-aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-missile">anti-missile</a> defence, and <a href="/wiki/30mm_DS30M_Mark_2_Automated_Small_Calibre_Gun" title="30mm DS30M Mark 2 Automated Small Calibre Gun">30mm Automated Small Calibre Guns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minigun" title="Minigun">Miniguns</a> for use against <a href="/wiki/Fast_attack_craft" title="Fast attack craft">fast attack craft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-facts_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-facts-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> She would be escorted into high risk areas by the Type 45 Destroyer, which was made specially to fulfil this role. In lower risk situations, frigates or even patrol vessels may be used instead.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Highly_Mechanised_Weapon_Handling_System_.28HMWHS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Highly_Mechanised_Weapon_Handling_System_(HMWHS)">Highly Mechanised Weapon Handling System (HMWHS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Highly Mechanised Weapon Handling System (HMWHS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Incorporated into the first two blocks is a sophisticated handling and deployment system for air weapons, with the aim of achieving a <a href="/wiki/Sortie#Military_aviation" title="Sortie">sortie</a> generation rate which is about six times faster than any previous Royal Navy aircraft carrier. The system requires only 50 people and could be operated with as few as 12 in an emergency; it is estimated that 160 would be needed to produce the same efficiency with conventional equipment. The system moves munitions on <a href="/wiki/Pallet" title="Pallet">pallets</a> by means of remotely controlled electric vehicles and lifts.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliations">Affiliations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Affiliations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_affiliations">Official affiliations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Queen_Elizabeth_(R08)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Official affiliations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy_and_Royal_Marines_Charity" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Navy and Royal Marines Charity">Royal Navy and Royal Marines Charity</a><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Midlothian_F.C." title="Heart of Midlothian F.C.">Heart of Midlothian F.C.</a><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Enthusiasts%27_Club" title="Rolls-Royce Enthusiasts&#39; Club">Rolls-Royce Enthusiasts\' Club</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
