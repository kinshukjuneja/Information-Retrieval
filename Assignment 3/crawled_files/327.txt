href="/wiki/Mining_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mining engineer">mining engineer</a></td></tr><tr style="display:none"><td colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Richard Trevithick</b> (13 April 1771 \xe2\x80\x93 22 April 1833) was a British inventor and <a href="/wiki/Mining_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mining engineer">mining engineer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-hathitrust1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hathitrust1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The son of a mining captain, and born in the mining heartland of Cornwall, Trevithick was immersed in mining and engineering from an early age. He performed poorly in school, but went on to be an early pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Steam_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Steam power">steam-powered</a> <a href="/wiki/History_of_steam_road_vehicles#Trevithick&#39;s_steam_carriage" title="History of steam road vehicles">road</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_rail_transport#Steam_power_introduced" title="History of rail transport">rail</a> transport. His most significant contribution was the development of the first high-pressure <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>. He also built the first full-scale working railway <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The world\'s first locomotive-hauled railway journey took place on 21 February 1804, when Trevithick\'s unnamed steam locomotive hauled a train along the <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">tramway</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Penydarren_Ironworks" title="Penydarren Ironworks">Penydarren Ironworks</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a>, Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turning his interests abroad, Trevithick also worked as a mining consultant in Peru and later explored parts of Costa Rica. Throughout his professional career, he went through many ups and downs, and at one point faced financial ruin, also suffering from the strong rivalry of many mining and steam engineers of the day. During the prime of his career, he was a well-respected and known figure in mining and engineering, but near the end of his life he fell out of the public eye.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood_and_early_life">Childhood and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Richard Trevithick was born at <a href="/wiki/Tregajorran" title="Tregajorran">Tregajorran</a> (in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Illogan" title="Illogan">Illogan</a>), between <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">Redruth</a>, in the heart of one of the rich <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a>-mining areas of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. He was the youngest-but-one child and the only boy in a family of six children. He was very tall for the era at 6&#160;ft 2in, as well as athletic and concentrated more on sport than schoolwork. Sent to the <a href="/wiki/Village_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Village school">village school</a> at Camborne, he did not take much advantage of the education provided; one of his school masters described him as "a disobedient, slow, obstinate, spoiled boy, frequently absent and very inattentive". An exception was <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a>, for which he had an aptitude, though arriving at the correct answers by unconventional means.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Trevithick was the son of mine "captain" Richard Trevithick (1735\xe2\x80\x931797) and of miner\'s daughter Ann Teague (died 1810). As a child he would watch <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> pump water from the deep <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a> in Cornwall. For a time he was a neighbour of <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch" title="William Murdoch">William Murdoch</a>, the steam carriage pioneer, and would have been influenced by his experiments with steam-powered road locomotion.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Trevithick first went to work at the age of 19 at the East Stray Park Mine. He was enthusiastic and quickly gained the status of a <a href="/wiki/Consultant" title="Consultant">consultant</a>, unusual for such a young person. He was popular with the miners because of the respect they had for his father.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_family">Marriage and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1797, Trevithick married Jane Harvey of <a href="/wiki/Hayle" title="Hayle">Hayle</a>. They raised six children:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-SteamTrainsBR_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SteamTrainsBR-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Richard Trevithick (1798\xe2\x80\x931872)</li>\n<li>Anne Ellis (1800\xe2\x80\x931877)</li>\n<li>Elizabeth Banfield (1803\xe2\x80\x931870)</li>\n<li>John Harvey Trevithick (1807\xe2\x80\x931877)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Francis_Trevithick" title="Francis Trevithick">Francis Trevithick</a> (1812\xe2\x80\x931877)</li>\n<li>Frederick Henry Trevithick (1816\xe2\x80\x931883)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jane\'s father, <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_(ironfounder)" title="John Harvey (ironfounder)">John Harvey</a>, formerly a blacksmith from <a href="/wiki/Carnhell_Green" title="Carnhell Green">Carnhell Green</a>, formed the local <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harveys_of_Hayle" class="mw-redirect" title="Harveys of Hayle">Harveys of Hayle</a>. His company became famous worldwide for building huge stationary <a href="/wiki/Beam_engine" title="Beam engine">"beam" engines</a> for pumping water, usually from mines. Up to this time such steam engines were of the condensing or atmospheric type, originally invented by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Newcomen" title="Thomas Newcomen">Thomas Newcomen</a> in 1712, which also became known as low-pressure engines. <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a>, on behalf of his partnership with <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Boulton" title="Matthew Boulton">Matthew Boulton</a>, held a number of <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> for improving the efficiency of Newcomen\'s engine\xe2\x80\x94including the "separate <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive_condensing_apparatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Steam locomotive condensing apparatus">condenser</a> patent", which proved the most contentious.\n</p><p>Trevithick became engineer at the <a href="/wiki/Ding_Dong_Mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Ding Dong Mine">Ding Dong Mine</a> in 1797, and there (in conjunction with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Bull&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Edward Bull (page does not exist)">Edward Bull</a>) he pioneered the use of high-pressure steam. He worked on building and modifying steam engines to avoid the royalties due to Watt on the separate condenser patent. <a href="/wiki/Boulton_and_Watt" title="Boulton and Watt">Boulton &amp; Watt</a> served an <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">injunction</a> on him at Ding Dong, and posted it "on the minestuffs" and "most likely on the door" of the Count (Account) House which, although now a ruin, is the only surviving building from Trevithick\'s time there.\n</p><p>He also experimented with the plunger-pole pump, a type of pump\xe2\x80\x94with a beam engine\xe2\x80\x94used widely in Cornwall\'s tin mines, in which he reversed the plunger to change it into a water-power engine.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High-pressure_engine">High-pressure engine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High-pressure engine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="778" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithick_High_Pressure_Steam_Engine_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_14041.png" This engine is on view at the <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Science Museum (London)">Science Museum (London)</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>As his experience grew, he realised that improvements in <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a> technology now permitted the safe production of high-pressure steam, which could move a <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">piston</a> in a steam engine on its own account, instead of using pressure near to atmospheric, in a condensing engine.\n</p><p>He was not the first to think of so-called "strong steam" or steam of about 30 psi.  <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch" title="William Murdoch">William Murdoch</a> had developed and demonstrated a model steam carriage, initially in 1784, and <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch#Steam_powered_locomotion" title="William Murdoch">demonstrated it to Trevithick</a> at his request in 1794. In fact, Trevithick lived next door to Murdoch in Redruth in 1797 and 1798. <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Evans" title="Oliver Evans">Oliver Evans</a> in the U.S. had also concerned himself with the concept, but there is no indication that his ideas had ever come to Trevithick\'s attention.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Independently of this, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Woolf" title="Arthur Woolf">Arthur Woolf</a> was experimenting with higher pressures whilst working as the Chief Engineer of the Griffin Brewery (proprietors Meux and Reid). This was an Engine designed by Hornblower and Maberly, and the proprietors were keen to have the best steam engine in London. Around 1796, Woolf believed he could save substantial amounts of coal consumption.\n</p><p>According to his son Francis, Trevithick was the first to make high-pressure steam work in England in 1799,<sup id="cite_ref-hathitrust1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hathitrust1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> although other sources say he had invented his first high-pressure engine by 1797.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Not only would a high-pressure steam engine eliminate the condenser, but it would allow the use of a smaller cylinder, saving space and weight. He reasoned that his engine could now be more compact, lighter, and small enough to carry its own weight even with a carriage attached. (Note this did not use the <i>expansion</i> of the steam, so-called "expansive working" came later)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_experiments">Early experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Trevithick started building his first models of high-pressure (meaning a few <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheres</a>) steam engines, initially a stationary one and subsequently one attached to a road carriage. A <a href="/wiki/Double-acting_cylinder" class="mw-redirect" title="Double-acting cylinder">double-acting cylinder</a> was used, with steam distribution by means of a <a href="/wiki/Four-way_valve" title="Four-way valve">four-way valve</a>. Exhaust steam was vented via a vertical pipe or <a href="/wiki/Chimney" title="Chimney">chimney</a> straight into the atmosphere, thus avoiding a condenser and any possible infringements of Watt\'s patent. The <a href="/wiki/Linear" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear">linear</a> motion was directly converted into circular motion via a <a href="/wiki/Crank_(mechanism)" title="Crank (mechanism)">crank</a> instead of using a more cumbersome beam.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puffing_Devil"><i>Puffing Devil</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> (A steam wagon built in 1770 by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas-Joseph_Cugnot" title="Nicolas-Joseph Cugnot">Nicolas-Joseph Cugnot</a> may have an earlier claim.) Trevithick named his carriage <i>Puffing Devil</i> and on <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a> that year, he demonstrated it by successfully carrying six passengers up Fore Street and then continuing on up Camborne Hill, from Camborne Cross, to the nearby village of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beacon,_Kerrier,_Cornwall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Beacon, Kerrier, Cornwall (page does not exist)">Beacon</a>. His cousin and associate, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Vivian" title="Andrew Vivian">Andrew Vivian</a>, steered the machine. This is widely recognised as the first demonstration of <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transportation</a> powered by steam. It inspired the popular Cornish folk song "<a href="/wiki/Camborne_Hill" title="Camborne Hill">Camborne Hill</a>".\n</p><p>During further tests, Trevithick\'s locomotive broke down three days later after passing over a gully in the road. The vehicle was left under some shelter with the fire still burning whilst the operators retired to a nearby <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">public house</a> for a meal of roast <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a> and drinks. Meanwhile, the water boiled off, the engine overheated and the machine burned, destroying it. Trevithick did not consider this a serious setback, but rather operator error.\n</p><p>In 1802, Trevithick took out a patent for his high-pressure steam engine.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rogers,_Iron_Road,_pp_40-44_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rogers,_Iron_Road,_pp_40-44-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> To prove his ideas, he built a stationary engine at the <a href="/wiki/Coalbrookdale" title="Coalbrookdale">Coalbrookdale</a> Company\'s works in <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a> in 1802, forcing water to a measured height to measure the <a href="/wiki/Work_done" class="mw-redirect" title="Work done">work done</a>. The engine ran at forty piston strokes a minute, with an unprecedented boiler pressure of 145 <a href="/wiki/Pound-force_per_square_inch" class="mw-redirect" title="Pound-force per square inch">psi</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coalbrookdale_Locomotive">Coalbrookdale Locomotive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coalbrookdale Locomotive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg/220px-Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg/330px-Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg/440px-Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2144" data-file-height="1391" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coalbrookdale_loco.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A drawing of the Coalbrookdale locomotive from the Science Museum</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1802, the Coalbrookdale company built a rail locomotive for him<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>, but little is known about it, including whether or not it actually ran. To date, the only known information about it comes from a drawing preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum,_London" title="Science Museum, London">Science Museum, London</a>, together with a letter written by Trevithick to his friend <a href="/wiki/Davies_Gilbert" title="Davies Gilbert">Davies Giddy</a>. The design incorporated a single horizontal <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(locomotive)" title="Cylinder (locomotive)">cylinder</a> enclosed in a return-flue <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a> drove the wheels on one side through <a href="/wiki/Gear#Spur_Gears" title="Gear">spur gears</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Axles" class="mw-redirect" title="Axles">axles</a> were mounted directly on the boiler, with no frame.<sup id="cite_ref-Westcott,_British_railway_locomotive,_p_3_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Westcott,_British_railway_locomotive,_p_3-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> On the drawing, the piston-rod, guide-bars and cross-head are located directly above the firebox door, thus making the engine extremely dangerous to fire while moving.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  Furthermore, the drawing indicates that the locomotive ran on a <a href="/wiki/Plateway" title="Plateway">plateway</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Track_gauge" title="Track gauge">track gauge</a> of <a href="/wiki/3_ft_gauge_railways" title="3 ft gauge railways"><span class="nowrap">3&#160;ft</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">914&#160;mm</span>).\n</p><p>This is the drawing used as the basis of all images and replicas of the later "Pen-y-darren" locomotive, as no plans for that locomotive have survived.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: London Steam Carriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithicks_Dampfwagen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Trevithicks_Dampfwagen.jpg/220px-Trevithicks_Dampfwagen.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Trevithicks_Dampfwagen.jpg/330px-Trevithicks_Dampfwagen.jpg 1.5x, been of little practical use. He built another steam-powered road vehicle in 1803, called the <a href="/wiki/London_Steam_Carriage" title="London Steam Carriage">London Steam Carriage</a>, which attracted much attention from the public and press when he drove it that year in London from <a href="/wiki/Holborn" title="Holborn">Holborn</a> to <a href="/wiki/Paddington" title="Paddington">Paddington</a> and back. It was uncomfortable for passengers and proved more expensive to run than a horse-drawn carriage, and was abandoned.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tragedy_at_Greenwich">Tragedy at Greenwich</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tragedy at Greenwich">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Also in 1803, one of Trevithick\'s stationary pumping engines in use at <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a> exploded, killing four men. Although Trevithick considered the explosion was caused by another case of careless operation rather than design error, the incident was exploited relentlessly by Watt and Boulton (<a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competitors</a> and promoters of the low-pressure engine) who highlighted the perceived risks of using high-pressure steam.\n</p><p>Trevithick\'s response was to incorporate two <a href="/wiki/Safety_valve" title="Safety valve">safety valves</a> into future designs, only one of which could be adjusted by the operator.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby,_Engineering_in_History,_pp_175-177_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby,_Engineering_in_History,_pp_175-177-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The adjustable valve comprised a disc covering a small hole at the top of the boiler above the water level in the steam chest. The <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> exerted by the steam <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> was equalised by an opposite force created by a weight attached to a pivoted lever. The position of the weight on the lever was adjustable thus allowing the operator to set the maximum steam pressure. Trevithick also added a <a href="/wiki/Fusible_plug" title="Fusible plug">fusible plug</a> of lead, positioned in the boiler just below the minimum safe water level. Under normal operation the water temperature could not exceed that of <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling</a> water and kept the lead below its melting point. If the water ran low, it exposed the lead plug, and the cooling effect of the water was lost. The temperature would then rise sufficiently to melt the lead, releasing steam into the fire, reducing the boiler pressure and providing an audible alarm in sufficient time for the operator to damp the fire, and let the boiler cool before damage could occur. He also introduced the hydraulic testing of boilers, and the use of a <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> <a href="/wiki/Manometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Manometer">manometer</a> to indicate the pressure.\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".22Pen-y-Darren.22_locomotive"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Pen-y-Darren&quot;_locomotive">"Pen-y-Darren" locomotive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: &quot;Pen-y-Darren&quot; locomotive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TrevithicksEngine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/TrevithicksEngine.jpg/220px-TrevithicksEngine.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/TrevithicksEngine.jpg/330px-TrevithicksEngine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/TrevithicksEngine.jpg/440px-TrevithicksEngine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1179" data-file-height="786" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TrevithicksEngine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trevithick\'s 1802 locomotive. This full-scale reconstruction is in the <a href="/wiki/National_Waterfront_Museum" title="National Waterfront Museum">National Waterfront Museum</a>, Swansea.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1802, Trevithick built one of his high-pressure steam engines to drive a <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">hammer</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Penydarren_Ironworks" title="Penydarren Ironworks">Pen-y-Darren Ironworks</a> in <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mid_Glamorgan" title="Mid Glamorgan">Mid Glamorgan</a> . With the assistance of Rees Jones, an employee of the iron works and under the supervision of Samuel Homfray, the proprietor, he mounted the engine on wheels and turned it into a locomotive. In 1803, Trevithick sold the patents for his locomotives to Samuel Homfray.\n</p><p>Homfray was so impressed with Trevithick\'s locomotive that he made a <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">bet</a> with another ironmaster, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Crawshay" title="Richard Crawshay">Richard Crawshay</a>, for 500 <a href="/wiki/Guinea_(British_coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinea (British coin)">guineas</a> that Trevithick\'s steam locomotive could haul ten <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of iron along the <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a> <a href="/wiki/Tramway_(mineral)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tramway (mineral)">Tramroad</a> from <a href="/wiki/Penydarren" title="Penydarren">Penydarren</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Richard_Trevithick&amp;params=51.750825_N_3.375761_W_type:landmark"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">51\xc2\xb045\xe2\x80\xb203\xe2\x80\xb3N</span> <span class="longitude">3\xc2\xb022\xe2\x80\xb233\xe2\x80\xb3W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">51.750825\xc2\xb0N 3.375761\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">51.750825; -3.375761</span></span></span></a></span>) to <a href="/wiki/Abercynon" title="Abercynon">Abercynon</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Richard_Trevithick&amp;params=51.645567_N_3.324233_W_type:landmark"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">51\xc2\xb038\xe2\x80\xb244\xe2\x80\xb3N</span> <span class="longitude">3\xc2\xb019\xe2\x80\xb227\xe2\x80\xb3W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">51.645567\xc2\xb0N 3.324233\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">51.645567; -3.324233</span></span></span></a></span>), a distance of 9.75 miles (15.69&#160;km). Amid great interest from the public, on 21 February 1804 it successfully carried 10 tons of iron, 5 wagons and 70 men the full distance in 4 hours and 5 minutes, an average speed of approximately 2.4&#160;mph (3.9&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> As well as Homfray, Crawshay and the passengers, other witnesses included <a href="/wiki/Davies_Gilbert" title="Davies Gilbert">Mr. Giddy</a>, a respected patron of Trevithick and an \'engineer from the Government\'.<sup id="cite_ref-Rogers,_Iron_Road,_p_40_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rogers,_Iron_Road,_p_40-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The engineer from the government was probably a safety inspector and particularly interested in the boiler\'s ability to withstand high steam pressures.\n</p><p>The configuration of the Pen-y-Darren engine differed from the Coalbrookdale engine. The cylinder was moved to the other end of the boiler so that the fire door was out of the way of the moving parts. This obviously also involved putting the crankshaft at the chimney end. The locomotive comprised a <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a> with a single <a href="/wiki/Return_flue_boiler" class="mw-redirect" title="Return flue boiler">return flue</a> mounted on a four-wheel frame. At one end, a single <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(engine)" title="Cylinder (engine)">cylinder</a> with very long stroke was mounted partly in the boiler, and a <a href="/wiki/Piston_rod" title="Piston rod">piston rod</a> <a href="/wiki/Crosshead" title="Crosshead">crosshead</a> ran out along a slidebar, an arrangement that looked like a giant trombone. As there was only one cylinder, this was coupled to a large <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a> mounted on one side. The <a href="/wiki/Rotational_inertia" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotational inertia">rotational inertia</a> of the flywheel would even out the movement that was transmitted to a central <a href="/wiki/Cog-wheel" class="mw-redirect" title="Cog-wheel">cog-wheel</a> that was, in turn connected to the driving wheels. It used a high-pressure cylinder without a condenser; the exhaust steam was sent up the chimney assisting the draught through the fire, increasing efficiency even more.\n</p><p>The bet was won. Despite many people\'s doubts, it had been shown that, provided that the <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> was sufficiently gentle, it was possible to successfully haul heavy carriages along a smooth iron road using the adhesive weight alone of a suitably heavy and powerful steam locomotive. Trevithick\'s was probably the first to do so;<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby,_pp_274-275_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby,_pp_274-275-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> however some of the short cast iron <a href="/wiki/Plateway" title="Plateway">plates</a> of the tramroad broke under the locomotive as they were intended only to support the lighter axle load of horse-drawn wagons and so the tramroad returned to horse power after the initial test run.\n</p><p>Homfray was pleased he won his bet. The engine was placed on blocks and reverted to its original <a href="/wiki/Stationary_engine" title="Stationary engine">stationary</a> job of driving hammers.\n</p><p>In modern-day Merthyr Tydfil, behind the monument to Trevithick\'s locomotive lies a stone wall, the sole remainder of the former boundary wall of Homfray\'s <a href="/wiki/Penydarren_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Penydarren House">Penydarren House</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A full-scale working reconstruction of the Pen-y-darren locomotive was commissioned in 1981 and delivered to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Industrial_and_Maritime_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Welsh Industrial and Maritime Museum (page does not exist)">Welsh Industrial and Maritime Museum</a> in Cardiff; when that closed, it was moved to the <a href="/wiki/National_Waterfront_Museum" title="National Waterfront Museum">National Waterfront Museum</a> in Swansea. Several times a year it is run on a 40m length of rail outside the museum.\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".22Newcastle.22_locomotive"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Newcastle&quot;_locomotive">"Newcastle" locomotive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: &quot;Newcastle&quot; locomotive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Blackett" title="Christopher Blackett">Christopher Blackett</a>, proprietor of the <a href="/wiki/Wylam" title="Wylam">Wylam</a> colliery near Newcastle, heard of the success in Wales and wrote to Trevithick asking for locomotive designs. These were sent to John Whitfield at Gateshead, Trevithick\'s agent, who in 1804 built what was probably the first locomotive to have flanged wheels.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Blackett was using wooden rails for his tramway and, once again, Trevithick\'s machine was to prove too heavy for its track.<sup id="cite_ref-Spartacus_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spartacus-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catch_Me_Who_Can"><i>Catch Me Who Can</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Catch Me Who Can">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg/220px-Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg/330px-Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg/440px-Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="442" data-file-height="374" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trevithick%27s_steam_circus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trevithick\'s steam circus</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1808, Trevithick publicised his steam railway locomotive expertise by building a new locomotive called <i><a href="/wiki/Catch_Me_Who_Can" title="Catch Me Who Can">Catch Me Who Can</a></i>, built for him by John Hazledine and <a href="/wiki/John_Urpeth_Rastrick" title="John Urpeth Rastrick">John Urpeth Rastrick</a> at <a href="/wiki/Bridgnorth" title="Bridgnorth">Bridgnorth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, and named by <a href="/wiki/Davies_Gilbert" title="Davies Gilbert">Davies Giddy</a>\'s daughter. The configuration differed from the previous locomotives in that the cylinder was mounted vertically and drove a pair of wheels directly without a flywheel or gearing.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This was probably Trevithick\'s fourth locomotive, after those used at Coalbrookdale, Pen-y-darren ironworks, and the Wylam colliery. He ran it on a circular track just south of the present-day <a href="/wiki/Euston_Square_tube_station" title="Euston Square tube station">Euston Square tube station</a> in London. The site in <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury" title="Bloomsbury">Bloomsbury</a> has recently been identified <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeologically</a> as that occupied by the <a href="/wiki/UCL_Main_Building" title="UCL Main Building">Chadwick Building</a>, part of <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tyler,_2007_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tyler,_2007-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Admission to the "steam circus" was one <a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">shilling</a> including a ride and it was intended to show that rail travel was faster than by horse. This venture also suffered from weak tracks and public interest was limited.\n</p><p>Trevithick was disappointed by the response and designed no more railway locomotives. It was not until 1812 that twin-cylinder steam locomotives, built by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Murray" title="Matthew Murray">Matthew Murray</a> in <a href="/wiki/Holbeck" title="Holbeck">Holbeck</a>, successfully started replacing horses for hauling coal wagons on the <a href="/wiki/Edge_rail" class="mw-redirect" title="Edge rail">edge railed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rack_and_pinion" title="Rack and pinion">rack and pinion</a> <a href="/wiki/Middleton_Railway" title="Middleton Railway">Middleton Railway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Middleton,_West_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Middleton, West Yorkshire">Middleton</a> colliery to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_projects">Engineering projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Engineering projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thames_tunnel">Thames tunnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Thames tunnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Vazie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Vazie (page does not exist)">Robert Vazie</a>, another Cornish engineer, was selected by the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Archway_Company" title="Thames Archway Company">Thames Archway Company</a> in 1805 to drive a tunnel under the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rotherhithe" title="Rotherhithe">Rotherhithe</a>. Vazie encountered serious problems with water influx, and had got no further than sinking the end shafts when the directors called in Trevithick for consultation. The directors agreed to pay Trevithick \xc2\xa31000 (the equivalent of \xc2\xa373,038 in 2016<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>) if he could successfully complete the tunnel, a length of 1,220 feet (370&#160;m). In August 1807, he began driving a small pilot tunnel or driftway 5 feet (1.5&#160;m) high tapering from 2&#160;feet 6&#160;inches (0.76&#160;m) at the top to 3 feet (0.91&#160;m) at the bottom. By 23 December, after it had progressed 950 feet (290&#160;m), progress was delayed after a sudden inrush of water; and only one month later on 26 January 1808, at 1,040 feet (320&#160;m), a more serious inrush occurred. The tunnel was flooded; Trevithick, being the last to leave, was nearly drowned. Clay was dumped on the river bed to seal the hole, and the tunnel was drained, but mining was now more difficult. Progress stalled, and a few of the directors attempted to discredit Trevithick, but the quality of his work was eventually upheld by two <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">colliery</a> engineers from the North of England. Despite suggesting various building techniques to complete the project, including a submerged <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Tubing_(material)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tubing (material)">tube</a>, Trevithick\'s links with the company ceased and the project was never actually completed.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Completion">Completion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Completion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The first successful tunnel under the Thames was started by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Marc_Isambard_Brunel" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Marc Isambard Brunel">Sir Marc Isambard Brunel</a> in 1823, 0.75 miles (1,200&#160;m) upstream, assisted by his son <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a> (who also nearly died in a tunnel collapse). Marc Brunel finally completed it in 1843, the delays being due to problems with funding.\n</p><p>Trevithick\'s suggestion of a submerged tube approach was successfully implemented for the first time across the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_River" title="Detroit River">Detroit River</a> between <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> in the United States and <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> in Canada with the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Central_Railway_Tunnel" title="Michigan Central Railway Tunnel">Michigan Central Railway Tunnel</a>, under the engineering supervision of The New York Central Railway\'s engineering vice president, <a href="/wiki/William_J_Wilgus" class="mw-redirect" title="William J Wilgus">William J Wilgus</a>. Construction began in 1903 and was completed in 1910. The <a href="/wiki/Detroit%E2%80%93Windsor_Tunnel" title="Detroit\xe2\x80\x93Windsor Tunnel">Detroit\xe2\x80\x93Windsor Tunnelwhich</a> was completed in 1930 for automotive traffic, and the tunnel under the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Harbour" title="Victoria Harbour">Hong Kong Harbour</a> were also submerged-tube designs.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_London">Return to London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Return to London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Trevithick went on to research other projects to exploit his high-pressure steam engines: boring <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a> manufacture, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a> crushing, rolling <a href="/wiki/Mill_(factory)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mill (factory)">mills</a>, forge hammers, <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> blowers as well as the <a href="/wiki/Traditional_mining" title="Traditional mining">traditional mining</a> applications. He also built a <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a> powered by <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer" title="Paddle steamer">paddle wheels</a> and several <a href="/wiki/Dredge" class="mw-redirect" title="Dredge">dredgers</a>.\n</p><p>Trevithick saw opportunities in London and persuaded his wife and four children reluctantly to join him in 1808 for two and a half years lodging first in <a href="/wiki/Rotherhithe" title="Rotherhithe">Rotherhithe</a> and then in <a href="/wiki/Limehouse" title="Limehouse">Limehouse</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nautical_projects">Nautical projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Nautical projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1808, Trevithick entered a partnership with Robert Dickinson, a West India merchant. Dickinson supported several of Trevithick\'s patents. The first of these was the <i>Nautical Labourer</i>; a steam <a href="/wiki/Tug" class="mw-redirect" title="Tug">tug</a> with a floating <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">crane</a> propelled by paddle wheels. However, it did not meet the fire regulations for the docks, and the Society of Coal Whippers, worried about losing their livelihood, even threatened the life of Trevithick.\n</p><p>Another patent was for the installation of iron tanks in ships for storage of cargo and water instead of in wooden <a href="/wiki/Casks" class="mw-redirect" title="Casks">casks</a>. A small works was set up at <a href="/wiki/Limehouse" title="Limehouse">Limehouse</a> to manufacture them, employing three men. The tanks were also used to raise sunken <a href="/wiki/Shipwrecks" class="mw-redirect" title="Shipwrecks">wrecks</a> by placing them under the wreck and creating <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> by pumping them full of air. In 1810 a wreck near <a href="/wiki/Margate" title="Margate">Margate</a> was raised in this way but there was a dispute over payment and Trevithick was driven to cut the lashings loose and let it sink again.\n</p><p>In 1809, Trevithick worked on various ideas on improvements for ships: iron floating docks, iron ships, telescopic iron masts, improved ship structures, iron <a href="/wiki/Buoys" class="mw-redirect" title="Buoys">buoys</a> and using heat from the ships boilers for cooking.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Illness.2C_financial_difficulties_and_return_to_Cornwall"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Illness,_financial_difficulties_and_return_to_Cornwall">Illness, financial difficulties and return to Cornwall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Illness, financial difficulties and return to Cornwall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In May 1810, Trevithick caught <a href="/wiki/Typhoid" class="mw-redirect" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a> and nearly died. By September, he had recovered sufficiently to travel back to Cornwall by ship, and in February 1811 he and Dickinson were declared <a href="/wiki/Bankrupt" class="mw-redirect" title="Bankrupt">bankrupt</a>. They were not discharged until 1814, Trevithick having paid off most of the partnership debts from his own funds.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_boiler_and_engine">Cornish boiler and engine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cornish boiler and engine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In about 1812, Trevithick designed the \xe2\x80\x98<a href="/wiki/Cornish_boiler" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish boiler">Cornish boiler</a>\xe2\x80\x99. These were horizontal, cylindrical boilers with a single internal fire tube or flue passing horizontally through the middle. Hot exhaust gases from the fire passed through the flue thus increasing the surface area heating the water and improving efficiency. These types were installed in the Boulton and Watt pumping engines at <a href="/wiki/Dolcoath_mine" title="Dolcoath mine">Dolcoath</a> and more than doubled their efficiency.\n</p><p>Again in 1812, he installed a new \'high-pressure\' experimental condensing steam engine at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheal_Prosper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wheal Prosper (page does not exist)">Wheal Prosper</a>. This became known as the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_engine" title="Cornish engine">Cornish engine</a>, and was the most efficient in the world at that time. Other Cornish engineers contributed to its development but Trevithick\'s work was predominant. In the same year he installed another high-pressure engine, though non-condensing, in a <a href="/wiki/Threshing" title="Threshing">threshing</a> machine on a farm at <a href="/wiki/Probus,_Cornwall" title="Probus, Cornwall">Probus, Cornwall</a>. It was very successful and proved to be cheaper to run than the horses it replaced. It ran for 70 years and was then exhibited at the Science Museum.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recoil_engine">Recoil engine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Recoil engine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In one of Trevithick\'s more unusual projects, he attempted to build a \'recoil engine\' similar to the <a href="/wiki/Aeolipile" title="Aeolipile">aeolipile</a> described by <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Hero of Alexandria</a> in about <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">AD</a> 50. Trevithick\'s engine comprised a boiler feeding a hollow <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> to route the steam to a <a href="/wiki/Catherine_wheel_(firework)" title="Catherine wheel (firework)">catherine wheel</a> with two fine-<a href="/wiki/Bore_(engines)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bore (engines)">bore</a> steam <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jets</a> on its circumference. The first wheel was 15 feet (4.6&#160;m) in diameter and a later attempt was 24 feet (7.3&#160;m) in diameter. To get any usable <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a>, steam had to issue from the nozzles at a very high <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> and in such large volume that it proved not to operate with adequate efficiency. Today this would be recognised as a <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine#Reaction_turbines" title="Steam turbine">reaction turbine</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Draining_the_Peruvian_silver_mines">Draining the Peruvian silver mines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Draining the Peruvian silver mines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1811 draining water from the rich <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> mines of <a href="/wiki/Cerro_de_Pasco" title="Cerro de Pasco">Cerro de Pasco</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> at an altitude of 4,330 metres (14,210&#160;ft) posed serious problems for the man in charge, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Uville" title="Francisco Uville">Francisco Uville</a>. The low-pressure condensing engines by Boulton and Watt developed so little power as to be useless at this altitude, and they could not be dismantled into sufficiently small pieces to be transported there along <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mule</a> tracks. Uville was sent to England to investigate using Trevithick\'s high-pressure steam engine. He bought one for 20 guineas, transported it back and found it to work quite satisfactorily. In 1813 Uville set sail again for England and, having fallen ill on the way, broke his journey via <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. When he had recovered he boarded the <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> <a href="/wiki/Packet_ship" class="mw-redirect" title="Packet ship">packet ship</a> \'Fox\' coincidentally with one of Trevithick\'s cousins on board the same vessel. Trevithick\'s home was just a few miles from Falmouth so Uville was able to meet him and tell him about the project.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trevithick_leaves_for_South_America">Trevithick leaves for South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Trevithick leaves for South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 20 October 1816 Trevithick left <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Whaler" title="Whaler">whaler</a> ship <i>Asp</i> accompanied by a lawyer named Page and a boilermaker bound for Peru. He was received by Uville with honour initially but relations soon broke down and Trevithick left in disgust at the accusations directed at him. He travelled widely in Peru acting as a consultant on mining methods. The government granted him certain mining rights and he found mining areas, but did not have the funds to develop them, with the exception of a copper and silver mine at <a href="/wiki/Caxatambo" class="mw-redirect" title="Caxatambo">Caxatambo</a>. After a time serving in the army of Simon Bolivar he returned to Caxatambo but due to the unsettled state of the country and presence of the Spanish army he was forced to leave the area and abandon \xc2\xa35,000 worth of ore ready to ship. Uville died in 1818 and Trevithick soon returned to Cerro de Pasco to continue mining. However, the war of liberation denied him several objectives. Meanwhile, back in England, he was accused of neglecting his wife Jane and family in Cornwall.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploring_the_isthmus_of_Costa_Rica_on_foot">Exploring the isthmus of Costa Rica on foot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Exploring the isthmus of Costa Rica on foot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After leaving Cerro de Pasco, Trevithick passed through <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> on his way to <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogot\xc3\xa1">Bogot\xc3\xa1</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>. He arrived in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> in 1822 hoping to develop mining machinery. He spent time looking for a practical route to transport ore and equipment, settling on using the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_River_(Nicaragua)" title="San Juan River (Nicaragua)">San Juan River</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sarapiqui_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarapiqui River">Sarapiqui River</a>, and then a railway to cover the remaining distance. In a biography his son wrote that Trevithick had in mind a steam-driven railway and not mule-driven.\n</p><p>The initial party comprised Trevithick, Scottish mining projector James Gerard,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> two schoolboys: <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Montealegre_Fern%C3%A1ndez" class="mw-redirect" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Mar\xc3\xada Montealegre Fern\xc3\xa1ndez">Jos\xc3\xa9 Maria Montealegre</a> (a future president of Costa Rica) and his brother Mariano, whom Gerard intended to enrol in <a href="/wiki/Highgate_School" title="Highgate School">Highgate School</a>, North London,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and seven natives, three of whom returned home after guiding them through the first part of their journey. The journey was treacherous \xe2\x80\x93 one of the party was drowned in a raging torrent and Trevithick was nearly killed on at least two occasions. In the first he was saved from drowning by Gerard, and in the second he was nearly devoured by an <a href="/wiki/Alligator" title="Alligator">alligator</a> following a dispute with a local man whom he had in some way offended. Still in the company of Gerard, he made his way to <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Colombia" title="Cartagena, Colombia">Cartagena</a> where he chanced to meet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephenson" title="Robert Stephenson">Robert Stephenson</a> who was himself on his way home from Colombia, following a failed three-year mining venture. It had been many years since they last met (when Stephenson was just a baby), and the two men were judged by witnesses to their meeting to have little in common. Despite this Stephenson gave Trevithick \xc2\xa350 to help his passage home. Whilst Stephenson and Gerard booked passage via New York, Trevithick took ship direct to Falmouth, arriving there in October 1827 with few possessions other than the clothes he was wearing. He never returned to Costa Rica.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_projects">Later projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Later projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Taking encouragement from earlier inventors who had achieved some successes with similar endeavours, Trevithick petitioned Parliament for a grant but he was unsuccessful in acquiring one.\n</p><p>In 1829 he built a closed cycle steam engine followed by a vertical tubular boiler.\n</p><p>In 1830 he invented an early form of storage room <a href="/wiki/Storage_heater" title="Storage heater">heater</a>. It comprised a small fire tube boiler with a detachable flue which could be heated either outside or indoors with the flue connected to a chimney. Once hot the hot water container could be wheeled to where heat was required and the issuing heat could be altered using adjustable doors.\n</p><p>To commemorate the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Bill</a> in 1832 he designed a massive column to be 1,000 feet (300&#160;m) high, being 100 feet (30&#160;m) in diameter at the base tapering to 12 feet (3.7&#160;m) at the top where a statue of a horse would have been mounted. It was to be made of 1500 10-foot-square (3 m) pieces of cast iron and would have weighed 6000 tons. There was substantial public interest in the proposal, but it was never built.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_project">Final project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Final project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>About the same time he was invited to do some development work on an engine of a new vessel at Dartford by John Hall, the founder of <a href="/wiki/J_%26_E_Hall" title="J &amp; E Hall">J &amp; E Hall</a> Limited. The work involved a reaction turbine for which Trevithick earned \xc2\xa31200. He lodged at The Bull hotel in the High Street, <a href="/wiki/Dartford" title="Dartford">Dartford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stedmemomorial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Stedmemomorial.jpg/220px-Stedmemomorial.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Stedmemomorial.jpg/330px-Stedmemomorial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Stedmemomorial.jpg/440px-Stedmemomorial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stedmemomorial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The plaque at St Edmund\'s Burial Ground, East Hill, <a href="/wiki/Dartford" title="Dartford">Dartford</a>. With the words "<i>Richard Trevithick. Approximately 25ft from this wall lie the remains of Richard Trevithick. The great engineer and pioneer of high-pressure steam. He died at the Bull Inn, Dartford and was carried here by fellow workers of Halls Engineering Works. To a paupers grave. Born Illogan, Cornwall April 13th 1771. Died <a href="/wiki/Dartford" title="Dartford">Dartford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> April 22nd 1833</i>".</div></div></div>\n<p>After he had been working in <a href="/wiki/Dartford" title="Dartford">Dartford</a> for about a year, Trevithick was taken ill with <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> and had to retire to bed at The Bull hotel, where he was lodging at the time. Following a week\'s confinement in bed he died on the morning of 22 April 1833. He was penniless, and no relatives or friends had attended his bedside during his illness. His colleagues at Hall\'s works made a collection for his funeral expenses and acted as <a href="/wiki/Pallbearer" title="Pallbearer">bearers</a>. They also paid a night watchman to guard his grave at night to deter <a href="/wiki/Grave_robber" class="mw-redirect" title="Grave robber">grave robbers</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Body_snatching" title="Body snatching">body snatching</a> was common at that time.\n</p><p>Trevithick was buried in an unmarked grave in St Edmund\'s Burial Ground, East Hill, Dartford. The burial ground closed in 1857, with the gravestones being removed in the 1960s. A plaque marks the approximate spot believed to be the site of the grave.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The plaque lies on the side of the park, near the East Hill gate, and an unlinked path.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg/170px-TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg" width="170" height="236" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg/255px-TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg/340px-TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="924" data-file-height="1285" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TrevithickStatueNew2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Richard Trevithick\'s statue by the public library at Camborne, Cornwall</div></div></div>\n<p>In Camborne, outside the public library, a statue by Leonard Stanford Merrifield depicting Trevithick holding one of his small-scale models<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> was unveiled in 1932 by <a href="/wiki/Prince_George,_Duke_of_Kent" title="Prince George, Duke of Kent">Prince George, Duke of Kent</a>, in front of a crowd of thousands of local people.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 17 March 2007, Dartford Borough Council invited the Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Trevithick_Society" title="Trevithick Society">Trevithick Society</a>, Phil Hosken, to unveil a <a href="/wiki/Blue_Plaque" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue Plaque">Blue Plaque</a> at the Royal Victoria and Bull hotel (formerly The Bull) marking Trevithick\'s last years in Dartford and the place of his death in 1833. The Blue Plaque is prominently displayed on the Hotel\'s front facade. There is also a plaque at <a href="/wiki/Dartford#Places_of_worship" title="Dartford">Holy Trinity Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_University" title="Cardiff University">Cardiff University</a> Engineering, Computer Science and Physics departments are based around the Trevithick Building which also holds the Trevithick Library, named after Richard Trevithick.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Gower_Street_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gower Street (London)">Gower Street</a> in London, on the wall of the <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College</a> building, an elaborate wall plaque carries the legend: "Close to this place Richard Trevithick (Born 1771 \xe2\x80\x93 Died 1833) Pioneer of High Pressure Steam ran in the year 1808 the first steam locomotive to draw passengers." It was erected by "The Trevithick Centenary Memorial Committee".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the oldest depictions of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Piran%27s_Flag" title="Saint Piran&#39;s Flag">Saint Piran\'s Flag</a> can be seen in a stained glass window at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>, 1888, commemorating Richard Trevithick. The window depicts <a href="/wiki/Michael_(archangel)" title="Michael (archangel)">St Michael</a> at the top and nine Cornish saints, Piran, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petroc" title="Saint Petroc">Petroc</a>, Pinnock,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Germanus, Julian, <a href="/wiki/Quiricus_and_Julietta" class="mw-redirect" title="Quiricus and Julietta">Cyriacus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Constantine_(British_saint)" title="Constantine (British saint)">Constantine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Non" title="Saint Non">Nonna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geraint_of_Dumnonia" title="Geraint of Dumnonia">Geraint</a> in tiers below. The head of St Piran appears to be a portrait of Trevithick himself and the figure carries the banner of Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is a plaque and memorial situated in Abercynon, outside the fire station. It says "In commemoration of the achievements of Richard Trevithick who having constructed the first steam locomotive did on February 21st 1804 successfully hail 10 tons of iron and numerous passengers along a tramroad from Merthyr to this precinct where was situated the loading point of the Glamorgan Canal". There is also a building in Abercynon called Ty Trevithick, named in his honour.\n</p><p>A replica of Trevithick\'s first full-size steam road locomotive was first displayed at Camborne Trevithick Day 2001, the day chosen for the celebration of Trevithick\xe2\x80\x99s public demonstration of the use of high-pressure steam. The team consisting of John Woodward, Mark Rivron and Sean Oliver, have continued to maintain and display the engine at various steam fairs across the country. The <i>Puffing Devil</i> has proudly led the parade of steam engines at every subsequent Trevithick day up to and including 2014.\n</p><p>Trevithick Drive in Temple Hill, Dartford, was named after Richard Trevithick.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Trevithick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Trevithick_Society" title="Trevithick Society">Trevithick Society</a>, a forerunner of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Archaeology" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial Archaeology">Industrial Archaeology</a> organizations that was initially formed to rescue the <a href="/wiki/Levant_Mine_and_Beam_Engine" title="Levant Mine and Beam Engine">Levant winding engine</a> from being scrapped was named for Richard Trevithick.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> They publish a newsletters, a journal and many books on Cornish engines, the mining industry, engineers, and other industrial archaeological topics.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> There is also a street named after him in Merthyr Tydfil.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Harry_Turtledove" title="Harry Turtledove">Harry Turtledove</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternate history</a> short story <i><a href="/wiki/A_Different_Flesh#The_Iron_Elephant" title="A Different Flesh">The Iron Elephant</a></i> has Richard Trevithick inventing his steam engine in 1782. This character was born sometime before 1771, and is American rather than British, indicating he is an analog (a common Turtledove plot device) rather than the historical figure.\n</p>\n<h2><span
