navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Sweyn Asleifsson</b> or <b>Sveinn \xc3\x81sleifarson</b> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1115 \xe2\x80\x93 1171) was a twelfth-century Viking who appears in the <i><a href="/wiki/Orkneyinga_Saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkneyinga Saga">Orkneyinga Saga</a></i>. \n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweyn_Asleifsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Sweyn was born in <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a> in the early twelfth century, to Olaf Hrolfsson and his wife \xc3\x85sleik. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Orkneyinga_Saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkneyinga Saga">Orkneyinga Saga</a></i>, he came to prominence when he murdered <a href="/wiki/Paul_Haakonsson" title="Paul Haakonsson">Earl Paul of Orkney</a>\'s cup-bearer c. 1134 in a quarrel over a drinking game, and fled to <a href="/wiki/Tiree" title="Tiree">Tiree</a> to take refuge with Holdbodi Hundason.<sup id="cite_ref-Orkneyinga_Saga_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyinga_Saga-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1140, Holdbodi called on Sweyn to join him raiding the coast of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, but they were beaten off, Holdbodi withdrawing to the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and Sweyn to <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Lewis" title="Isle of Lewis">Lewis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Orkneyinga_Saga_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyinga_Saga-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the early summer of 1141, Sweyn arrived in Man to join Holdbodi, but the Hebridean had been persuaded to join forces with the <a href="/wiki/Cambro-Norman" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambro-Norman">Norman-Welsh</a> lord Robert who had defeated them in the previous year, and attacked Sweyn. This created a feud between the former friends.<sup id="cite_ref-Orkneyinga_Saga_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyinga_Saga-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quarrels_and_feuds">Quarrels and feuds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweyn_Asleifsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quarrels and feuds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some years later, after falling out with his own captains (led by his brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Thorbjorn_Thorsteinsson" title="Thorbjorn Thorsteinsson">Thorbjorn Thorsteinsson</a>), Sweyn was driven out of Orkney by <a href="/wiki/Rognvald_Kali_Kolsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Rognvald Kali Kolsson">Rognvald Kali Kolsson</a>, but <a href="/wiki/King_David_I" class="mw-redirect" title="King David I">King David I</a> persuaded them to make peace.<sup id="cite_ref-Orkneyinga_Saga_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyinga_Saga-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Olvir_Rosta" title="Olvir Rosta">Olvir Rosta</a> killed  Sweyn\'s father; later Sweyn attacked Olvir, killed his grandmother, and Olvir fled to the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>.\n</p><p>In 1153, there was a falling-out between the three Earls of Orkney (Rognvald Kali Kolsson, <a href="/wiki/Erlend_Haraldsson" title="Erlend Haraldsson">Erlend Haraldsson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harald_Maddadsson" title="Harald Maddadsson">Harald Maddadsson</a>). Sweyn, backed by the new King, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Malcolm IV">Malcolm IV</a>, threw in his lot with Erlend, attacking the shipping of the other two Earls and raiding the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in his company.<sup id="cite_ref-Orkneyinga_Saga_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyinga_Saga-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Orkneyinga_saga" title="Orkneyinga saga">Orkneyinga saga</a></i> records a raid on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_May" title="Isle of May">Isle of May</a>, by Sweyn Asleifsson and Margad Grimsson, after they had been expelled from <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> by <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6gnvald_Kali_Kolsson" title="R\xc3\xb6gnvald Kali Kolsson">Earl R\xc3\xb6gnvald</a>, that they went raiding on the eastern seaboard of Scotland,\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"<i>They sailed south off Scotland until they came to M\xc3\xa1eyar (the Isle of May). There was a monastery, the head of which was an abbot, by name, Baldwin. Swein and his men were detained there seven nights by stress of bad weather. They said they had been sent by Earl R\xc3\xb6gnvald to the King of Scots. The monks suspected their tale, and thinking they were pirates, sent to the mainland for men. When Swein and his comrades became aware of this, they went hastily aboard their ship, after having plundered much treasure from the monastery. They went along Myrkvifj\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0 (the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Firth of Forth</a>), and found <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">David</a>, the King of Scots, in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>. He received Swein well, and requested him to stay with him. He told the King explicitly the reason of his visit, how matters had gone between him and Earl R\xc3\xb6gnvald before they parted, and also that they had plundered in M\xc3\xa1eyar. Swein and Margad stayed for a while with the King of Scots, and were well treated. King David sent men to those who had been robbed by Swein, and told them to estimate their loss themselves, and then of his own money, he made good to everyone his loss</i>"</dd></dl>\n<p>After Erlend\'s death in 1154, Harald drove Sweyn into hiding for a while, but he soon regained his power \xe2\x80\x93 the saga claims that he raided as far as the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>. By the time Rognvald died in 1158, Harald and Sweyn were reconciled: and Sweyn even became foster-father to Harald\'s son Hakon.<sup id="cite_ref-Orkneyinga_Saga_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orkneyinga_Saga-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweyn_Asleifsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The circumstances of Sweyn\'s death are confused. It is clear that the events described in the saga are those of the brief recapture of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> by its last <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> king, <a href="/wiki/Ascall_mac_Ragnaill" title="Ascall mac Ragnaill">Ascall mac Ragnaill</a>, in 1171: but there are many discrepancies between the Orcadian and Anglo-Norman accounts. It is possible that the warlord referred to by <a href="/wiki/Giraldus_Cambrensis" class="mw-redirect" title="Giraldus Cambrensis">Giraldus Cambrensis</a> as "John the Furious" was none other than Sweyn. Be that as it may, when the Normans retook <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, both Sweyn and Hakon were killed.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweyn_Asleifsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <i>Orkneying Saga</i>, also known as <i>Jarla S\xc7\xabgur Orkneyja</i>, was written by an unknown Icelandic scribe c. 1200.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Much of the material describes events that are up to three centuries earlier than this date and its historical accuracy is dubious.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, as the document was written only a few decades after \xc3\x81sleifarson\'s death it is reasonable to suppose the later chapters may more accurately reflect the events they describe. In fact, \xc3\x81sleifarson\'s activities take up fully one quarter of the saga and the oldest version ends with his death.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Beuermann148_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beuermann148-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>He is depicted as the quintessential Viking, a freebooter whose activities include drunkenness, murder and plundering and whose support is crucial to the politics of the Orkney earldom. His tale is closely bound up with that of Earl R\xc3\xb6gnvald, a more rounded character who is also a <a href="/wiki/Troubadour" title="Troubadour">troubadour</a> and ultimately a saint, and it may be that the saga writers were seeking to portray them as exemplars of the Viking lifestyle. However, there may also be a more complex moral to the story. Although \xc3\x81sleifarson\'s dying words are "Be it known to all men... that I belong to the bodyguard of Saint R\xc3\xb6gnvald the Earl", he also blackmailed R\xc3\xb6gnvald and caused him a great deal of trouble. It is possible that the saga intends to cast R\xc3\xb6gnvald as a weak leader who was unable control his nobles.<sup id="cite_ref-Beuermann148_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beuermann148-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another interpretation of the narrative is that rather than seeing these two men as <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonist</a> that together they live in a  golden age where the  earl is a cultured ruler and <i>primus inter pares</i> but who owes his position in part to his band of  "worthy warriors" and is by no means a despot who rules by divine right. In this case the whole story may be seen as a reaction to the Norwegian royal propaganda of the age that sought to promote the central authority of the crown.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
