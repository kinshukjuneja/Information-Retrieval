<small>(father)</small><br />Christiana Pearson <small>(mother)</small></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Mary Anne Evans</b> (22 November 1819 \xe2\x80\x93 22 December 1880; alternatively "Mary Ann" or "Marian"), known by her <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a> <b>George Eliot</b>, was an English novelist, poet, journalist, translator, and one of the leading writers of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>. She authored seven novels, including <i><a href="/wiki/Adam_Bede" title="Adam Bede">Adam Bede</a></i> (1859), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mill_on_the_Floss" title="The Mill on the Floss">The Mill on the Floss</a></i> (1860), <i><a href="/wiki/Silas_Marner" title="Silas Marner">Silas Marner</a></i> (1861), <i><a href="/wiki/Middlemarch" title="Middlemarch">Middlemarch</a></i> (1871\xe2\x80\x9372), and <i><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Deronda" title="Daniel Deronda">Daniel Deronda</a></i> (1876), most of which are set in provincial England and known for their <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realism</a>  and psychological insight.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Evans used a male pen name, she said, to ensure that her works would be taken seriously.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Although female authors were published under their own names during her lifetime, she wanted to escape the stereotype of women\'s writing being limited to lighthearted romances. She also wanted to have her fiction judged separately from her already extensive and widely known work as an editor and critic. Another factor in her use of a pen name may have been a desire to shield her private life from public scrutiny, thus avoiding the scandal that would have arisen because of her relationship with the married <a href="/wiki/George_Henry_Lewes" title="George Henry Lewes">George Henry Lewes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Eliot\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Middlemarch" title="Middlemarch">Middlemarch</a></i> has been described by the novelists <a href="/wiki/Martin_Amis" title="Martin Amis">Martin Amis</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Julian_Barnes" title="Julian Barnes">Julian Barnes</a><sup id="cite_ref-jbarnes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jbarnes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as the greatest novel in the English language.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Mary Ann Evans was born in <a href="/wiki/Nuneaton" title="Nuneaton">Nuneaton</a>, Warwickshire, England. She was the third child of Robert Evans (1773\xe2\x80\x931849) and Christiana Evans (<i>n\xc3\xa9e</i> Pearson, 1788\xe2\x80\x931836), the daughter of a local mill-owner. Mary Ann\'s name was sometimes shortened to Marian.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Her full siblings were Christiana, known as Chrissey (1814\xe2\x80\x9359), Isaac (1816\xe2\x80\x931890), and twin brothers who survived a few days in March 1821. She also had a half-brother, Robert (1802\xe2\x80\x9364), and half-sister, Fanny (1805\xe2\x80\x9382), from her father\'s previous marriage to Harriet Poynton (?1780\xe2\x80\x931809). Her father Robert Evans, of Welsh ancestry, was the manager of the <a href="/wiki/Arbury_Hall" title="Arbury Hall">Arbury Hall</a> Estate for the <a href="/wiki/Newdigate_family" class="mw-redirect" title="Newdigate family">Newdigate family</a> in Warwickshire, and Mary Ann was born on the estate at South Farm. In early 1820 the family moved to a house named <a href="/wiki/Griff_House" title="Griff House">Griff House</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Nuneaton" title="Nuneaton">Nuneaton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bedworth" title="Bedworth">Bedworth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The young Evans was obviously intelligent, a voracious reader. Because she was not considered physically beautiful, Evans was thus not thought to have much chance of marriage, and because of her intelligence, her father invested in an education not often afforded women.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> From ages five to nine, she boarded with her sister Chrissey at Miss Latham\'s school in <a href="/wiki/Attleborough,_Warwickshire" title="Attleborough, Warwickshire">Attleborough</a>, from ages nine to thirteen at Mrs. Wallington\'s school in Nuneaton, and from ages thirteen to sixteen at Miss Franklin\'s school in <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a>. At Mrs. Wallington\'s school, she was taught by the <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">evangelical</a> Maria Lewis \xe2\x80\x94 to whom her earliest surviving letters are addressed. In the religious atmosphere of the Miss Franklin\'s school, Evans was exposed to a quiet, disciplined belief opposed to evangelicalism.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After age sixteen, Evans had little formal education.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Thanks to her father\'s important role on the estate, she was allowed access to the library of Arbury Hall, which greatly aided her self-education and breadth of learning. Her classical education left its mark; Christopher Stray has observed that "George Eliot\'s novels draw heavily on Greek literature (only one of her books can be printed correctly without the use of a Greek typeface), and her themes are often influenced by Greek tragedy".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Her frequent visits to the estate also allowed her to contrast the wealth in which the local landowner lived with the lives of the often much poorer people on the estate, and different lives lived in parallel would reappear in many of her works. The other important early influence in her life was religion. She was brought up within a <a href="/wiki/Low_church" title="Low church">low church</a> <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> family, but at that time the <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">Midlands</a> was an area with a growing number of <a href="/wiki/English_Dissenters" title="English Dissenters">religious dissenters</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_Coventry">Move to Coventry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Move to Coventry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1836 her mother died and Evans (then 16) returned home to act as housekeeper, but she continued correspondence with her tutor Maria Lewis. When she was 21, her brother Isaac married and took over the family home, so Evans and her father moved to <a href="/wiki/Foleshill" title="Foleshill">Foleshill</a> near <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a>. The closeness to Coventry society brought new influences, most notably those of Charles and Cara Bray. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bray" title="Charles Bray">Charles Bray</a> had become rich as a ribbon manufacturer and had used his wealth in the building of schools and in other philanthropic causes. Evans, who had been struggling with religious doubts for some time, became intimate friends with the radical, free-thinking Brays, whose "Rosehill" home was a haven for people who held and debated radical views. The people whom the young woman met at the Brays\' house included <a href="/wiki/Robert_Owen" title="Robert Owen">Robert Owen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">Herbert Spencer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Martineau" title="Harriet Martineau">Harriet Martineau</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a>. Through this society Evans was introduced to more liberal and agnostic theologies and to writers such as <a href="/wiki/David_Strauss" title="David Strauss">David Strauss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Feuerbach" title="Ludwig Feuerbach">Ludwig Feuerbach</a>, who cast doubt on the literal truth of Biblical stories. In fact, her first major literary work was an English translation of Strauss\'s <i>The Life of Jesus</i> (1846), which she completed after it had been left incomplete by another member of the "Rosehill Circle"; later she translated Feuerbach\xe2\x80\x99s <i>The Essence of Christianity</i> (1854). As a product of their friendship, Bray published some of Evans\'s earliest writing, such as reviews, in his newspaper the <i><a href="/wiki/Coventry_Herald_and_Observer" class="mw-redirect" title="Coventry Herald and Observer">Coventry Herald and Observer</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Eliot_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eliot-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When Evans began to question her religious faith, her father threatened to throw her out of the house, but his threat was not carried out. Instead, she respectfully attended church and continued to keep house for him until his death in 1849, when she was 30. Five days after her father\'s funeral, she travelled to Switzerland with the Brays. She decided to stay on in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> alone, living first on the lake at Plongeon (near the present-day United Nations buildings) and then on the second floor of a house owned by her friends Fran\xc3\xa7ois and Juliet d\'Albert Durade on the rue de Chanoines (now the rue de la Pelisserie). She commented happily that "one feels in a downy nest high up in a good old tree". Her stay is commemorated by a plaque on the building. While residing there, she read avidly and took long walks in the beautiful Swiss countryside, which was a great inspiration to her. Fran\xc3\xa7ois Durade painted her portrait there as well.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_London_and_editorship_of_the_Westminster_Review">Move to London and editorship of the <i>Westminster Review</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Move to London and editorship of the Westminster Review">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On her return to England the following year (1850), she moved to London with the intent of becoming a writer, and she began referring to herself as Marian Evans. She stayed at the house of <a href="/wiki/John_Chapman_(publisher)" title="John Chapman (publisher)">John Chapman</a>, the radical publisher whom she had met earlier at Rosehill and who had published her Strauss translation. Chapman had recently purchased the campaigning, left-wing journal <i><a href="/wiki/The_Westminster_Review" title="The Westminster Review">The Westminster Review</a></i>, and Evans became its assistant editor in 1851. Although Chapman was officially the editor, it was Evans who did most of the work of producing the journal, contributing many essays and reviews beginning with the January 1852 issue and continuing until the end of her employment at the Review in the first half of 1854.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Women writers were common at the time, but Evans\'s role as the female editor of a literary magazine was quite unusual. During this period, she formed a number of unreciprocated emotional attachments, including one with Chapman (who was married but lived with both his wife and his mistress), and another with <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">Herbert Spencer</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_George_Lewes">Relationship with George Lewes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationship with George Lewes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg/220px-George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg" width="220" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg/330px-George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg/440px-George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg 2x" data-file-width="616" data-file-height="609" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Eliot_by_Samuel_Laurence.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of George Eliot by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Laurence" title="Samuel Laurence">Samuel Laurence</a>, c. 1860</div></div></div>\n<p>The philosopher and critic <a href="/wiki/George_Henry_Lewes" title="George Henry Lewes">George Henry Lewes</a> (1817\xe2\x80\x9378) met Evans in 1851, and by 1854 they had decided to live together. Lewes was already married to Agnes Jervis. They had an <a href="/wiki/Open_marriage" title="Open marriage">open marriage</a>. In addition to the three children they had together, Agnes also had four children by <a href="/wiki/Thornton_Leigh_Hunt" title="Thornton Leigh Hunt">Thornton Leigh Hunt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  In July 1854, Lewes and Evans travelled to <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> and Berlin together for the purpose of research. Before going to Germany, Evans continued her theological work with a translation of Feuerbach\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Essence_of_Christianity" title="The Essence of Christianity">The Essence of Christianity</a></i>, and while abroad she wrote essays and worked on her translation of <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ethics_(Spinoza)" title="Ethics (Spinoza)">Ethics</a></i>, which she completed in 1856, but which was not published in her lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The trip to Germany also served as a honeymoon for Evans and Lewes, and they now considered themselves married, with Evans calling herself Mary Ann Evans Lewes, and referring to Lewes as her husband. It was not unusual for men in Victorian society to have adulterous affairs; <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bray" title="Charles Bray">Charles Bray</a>, John Chapman, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a> all had adulterous affairs, though they were much more discreet than Lewes and Evans were. It was this lack of discretion and their public admission of the relationship which created accusations of polygamy and adultery, which earned them the moral scorn of English society.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_publication">First publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While continuing to contribute pieces to the <i>Westminster Review</i>, Evans resolved to become a novelist, and she set out a manifesto for herself in one of her last essays for the <i>Review</i>, "Silly Novels by Lady Novelists"<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> (1856). The essay criticised the trivial and ridiculous plots of contemporary fiction by women. In other essays, she praised the <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realism</a> of novels that were being written in Europe at the time, and it became clear in her subsequent fiction that she placed an emphasis on realistic storytelling. She also adopted a nom-de-plume, the one for which she would become known: George Eliot. This pen-name was said by some to be an homage to George Lewes. In addition to adopting his first name, the last name, Eliot, could possibly have been a code for "to L\xe2\x80\x94I owe it".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1857, when she was 37, "The Sad Fortunes of the Reverend Amos Barton", the first of the <i><a href="/wiki/Scenes_of_Clerical_Life" title="Scenes of Clerical Life">Scenes of Clerical Life</a></i>, was published in <i><a href="/wiki/Blackwood%27s_Magazine" title="Blackwood&#39;s Magazine">Blackwood\'s Magazine</a></i> and, along with the other <i>Scenes</i>, it was well received (it was published in book form early in 1858). Her first complete novel, published in 1859, was <i><a href="/wiki/Adam_Bede" title="Adam Bede">Adam Bede</a></i>; it was an instant success, but it prompted intense interest in who this new author might be. <i>Scenes of Clerical Life</i> was widely believed to have been written by a country <a href="/wiki/Parson" title="Parson">parson</a> or perhaps the wife of a parson. With the release of the incredibly popular <i>Adam Bede</i>, speculation increased, and there was even a pretender to the authorship, one Joseph Liggins. In the end, the real George Eliot stepped forward: Marian Evans Lewes admitted she was the author. The revelations about Eliot\'s private life surprised and shocked many of her admiring readers, but this did not affect her popularity as a novelist. Eliot\'s relationship with Lewes afforded her the encouragement and stability she so badly needed to write fiction, and to ease her self-doubt, but it would be some time before they were accepted into polite society. Acceptance was finally confirmed in 1877 when they were introduced to <a href="/wiki/Princess_Louise,_Duchess_of_Argyll" title="Princess Louise, Duchess of Argyll">Princess Louise</a>, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>. The queen herself was an avid reader of all of George Eliot\'s novels and was so impressed with <i>Adam Bede</i> that she commissioned the artist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Henry_Corbould" title="Edward Henry Corbould">Edward Henry Corbould</a> to paint scenes from the book.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg/220px-George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg" width="220" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg/330px-George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg/440px-George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1732" data-file-height="1568" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Eliot_31_Wimbledon_Park_Road_blue_plaque.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blue plaque, Holly Lodge, 31 Wimbledon Park Road, London</div></div></div>\n<p>After the success of <i>Adam Bede</i>, Eliot continued to write popular novels for the next fifteen years. Within a year of completing <i>Adam Bede</i>, she finished <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mill_on_the_Floss" title="The Mill on the Floss">The Mill on the Floss</a></i>, dedicating the manuscript: "To my beloved husband, George Henry Lewes, I give this MS. of my third book, written in the sixth year of our life together, at Holly Lodge, South Field, Wandsworth, and finished 21 March 1860."\n</p><p>Her last novel was <i><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Deronda" title="Daniel Deronda">Daniel Deronda</a></i>, published in 1876, after which she and Lewes moved to <a href="/wiki/Witley" title="Witley">Witley</a>, Surrey. By this time Lewes\'s health was failing, and he died two years later, on 30 November 1878. Eliot spent the next two years editing Lewes\'s final work, <i>Life and Mind</i>, for publication, and she found solace and companionship with <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Walter_Cross&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Walter Cross (page does not exist)">John Walter Cross</a>, a Scottish commission agent<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> whose mother had recently died.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_to_John_Cross_and_death">Marriage to John Cross and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marriage to John Cross and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg/220px-Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg/330px-Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg/440px-Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3840" data-file-height="5760" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Highgate_Cemetery_-_East_-_George_Eliot_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eliot\'s grave in <a href="/wiki/Highgate_Cemetery" title="Highgate Cemetery">Highgate Cemetery</a></div></div></div>\n<p>On 16 May 1880 Eliot courted controversy once more by marrying John Cross, a man twenty years her junior, and again changing her name, this time to Mary Anne Cross. The legal marriage at least pleased her brother Isaac, who had broken off relations with her when she had begun to live with Lewes, but now sent congratulations. While the couple were honeymooning in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, Cross, in a fit of depression, jumped from the hotel balcony into the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canal_(Venice)" title="Grand Canal (Venice)">Grand Canal</a>. He survived, and the newlyweds returned to England. They moved to a new house in Chelsea, but Eliot fell ill with a throat infection. This, coupled with the <a href="/wiki/Kidney_disease" title="Kidney disease">kidney disease</a> with which she had been afflicted for several years, led to her death on 22 December 1880 at the age of 61.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Eliot was not buried in <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> because of her denial of the Christian faith and her adulterous affair with Lewes. She was buried in <a href="/wiki/Highgate_Cemetery" title="Highgate Cemetery">Highgate Cemetery</a> (East), Highgate, London, in the area reserved for societal outcasts, religious dissenters and agnostics, beside the love of her life, <a href="/wiki/George_Henry_Lewes" title="George Henry Lewes">George Henry Lewes</a>. The graves of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and her friend Herbert Spencer are nearby. <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In 1980, on the centenary of her death, a memorial stone was established for her in the <a href="/wiki/Poets%27_Corner" title="Poets&#39; Corner">Poets\' Corner</a>.\n</p><p>Several landmarks in her birthplace of Nuneaton are named in her honour. These include The George Eliot School,  Middlemarch Junior School, George Eliot Hospital, (formerly Nuneaton Emergency Hospital),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and George Eliot Road, in <a href="/wiki/Foleshill" title="Foleshill">Foleshill</a>, Coventry.\n</p><p>A statue of Eliot is in Newdegate Street, Nuneaton, and <a href="/wiki/Nuneaton_Museum_%26_Art_Gallery" title="Nuneaton Museum &amp; Art Gallery">Nuneaton Museum &amp; Art Gallery</a> has a display of artifacts related to her.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_assessment">Literary assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Eliot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Literary assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Eliot_7.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/George_Eliot_7.jpg/220px-George_Eliot_7.jpg" width="220" height="301" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/George_Eliot_7.jpg/330px-George_Eliot_7.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/George_Eliot_7.jpg/440px-George_Eliot_7.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1395" data-file-height="1909" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Eliot_7.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_Burton" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick William Burton">Frederick William Burton</a>, 1864</div></div></div>\n<p>Throughout her career, Eliot wrote with a politically astute pen. From <i><a href="/wiki/Adam_Bede" title="Adam Bede">Adam Bede</a></i> to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mill_on_the_Floss" title="The Mill on the Floss">The Mill on the Floss</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Silas_Marner" title="Silas Marner">Silas Marner</a></i>, Eliot presented the cases of social outsiders and small-town persecution. <i><a href="/wiki/Felix_Holt,_the_Radical" title="Felix Holt, the Radical">Felix Holt, the Radical</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Legend_of_Jubal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Legend of Jubal (page does not exist)">The Legend of Jubal</a></i> were overtly political, and political crisis is at the heart of <i><a href="/wiki/Middlemarch" title="Middlemarch">Middlemarch</a></i>, in which she presents the stories of a number of inhabitants of a small English town on the eve of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Bill of 1832</a>; the novel is notable for its deep psychological insight and sophisticated character portraits. The roots of her realist philosophy can be found in her review of <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Modern_Painters" title="Modern Painters">Modern Painters</a></i> in <i><a href="/wiki/Westminster_Review" class="mw-redirect" title="Westminster Review">Westminster Review</a></i> in 1856.\n</p><p>Readers in the Victorian era praised her novels for their depictions of rural society. Much of the material for her prose was drawn from her own experience. She shared with <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">Wordsworth</a> the belief that there was much value and beauty to be found in the mundane details of ordinary country life. Eliot did not, however, confine herself to stories of the English countryside. <i><a href="/wiki/Romola" title="Romola">Romola</a></i>, an historical novel set in late fifteenth century <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, was based on the life of the Italian priest <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">Girolamo Savonarola</a>. In <i>The Spanish Gypsy</i>, Eliot made a foray into verse, but her poetry\'s initial popularity has not endured.\n</p><p>Working as a translator, Eliot was exposed to German texts of religious, social, and moral philosophy such as <a href="/wiki/David_Strauss" title="David Strauss">Friedrich Strauss</a>\xe2\x80\x99s <i>Life of Jesus</i>, Feuerbach\xe2\x80\x99s <i>The Essence of Christianity</i>, and Spinoza\xe2\x80\x99s <i>Ethics</i>. Elements from these works show up in her fiction, much of which is written with her trademark sense of <a href="/wiki/Agnostic" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnostic">agnostic</a> <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a>. She had taken particular notice of Feuerbach\xe2\x80\x99s conception of Christianity, positing that our understanding of the nature of the divine was to be found ultimately in the nature of humanity projected onto a divine figure. An example of this philosophy appeared in her novel <i>Romola</i>, in which Eliot\xe2\x80\x99s protagonist displayed a \xe2\x80\x9csurprisingly modern readiness to interpret religious language in humanist or secular ethical terms.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Though Eliot herself was not religious, she had respect for religious tradition and its ability to maintain a sense of social order and morality. \nThe religious elements in her fiction also owe much to her upbringing, with the experiences of Maggie Tulliver from <i>The Mill on the Floss</i> sharing many similarities with the young Mary Ann Evans. Eliot also faced a quandary similar to that of Silas Marner, whose alienation from the church simultaneously meant his alienation from society. Because Eliot retained a vestigial respect for religion, German philosopher <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a> excoriated her system of morality for figuring sin as a debt that can be expiated through suffering, which he demeaned as characteristic of "little moralistic females \xc3\xa0 la Eliot."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>She was at her most autobiographical in <i>Looking Backwards</i>, part of her final published work <i><a href="/wiki/Impressions_of_Theophrastus_Such" title="Impressions of Theophrastus Such">Impressions of Theophrastus Such</a></i>. By the time of <i><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Deronda" title="Daniel Deronda">Daniel Deronda</a></i>, Eliot\'s sales were falling off, and she had faded from public view to some degree. This was not helped by the posthumous biography written by her husband, which portrayed a wonderful, almost saintly, woman totally at odds with the scandalous life people knew she had led. In the 20th century she was championed by a new breed of critics, most notably by <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a>, who called <i>Middlemarch</i> "one of the few English novels written for grown-up people".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 1994, literary critic <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bloom" title="Harold Bloom">Harold Bloom</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Western_Canon:_The_Books_and_School_of_the_Ages" title="The Western Canon: The Books and School of the Ages">placed Eliot among the most important Western writers of all time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In a 2007 authors\' poll by <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">TIME</a></i>, <i>Middlemarch</i> was voted the tenth greatest literary work ever written.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
