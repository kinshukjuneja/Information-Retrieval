Kingdom began a military intervention in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a> on 7 May 2000 under the codename <b>Operation Palliser</b>. Although small numbers of British personnel had been deployed previously, Palliser was the first large-scale intervention by British forces in the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Civil_War" title="Sierra Leone Civil War">Sierra Leone Civil War</a>. In early May 2000, the <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_United_Front" title="Revolutionary United Front">Revolutionary United Front</a> (RUF)\xe2\x80\x94one of the main parties to the civil war\xe2\x80\x94advanced on the country\'s capital, <a href="/wiki/Freetown" title="Freetown">Freetown</a>, prompting the British government to dispatch an "operational reconnaissance and liaison team" (ORLT) to prepare to evacuate foreign citizens. On 6 May, the RUF blocked the road connecting Freetown to the country\'s main airport, <a href="/wiki/Lungi_International_Airport" title="Lungi International Airport">Lungi</a>. The next day, British soldiers began to secure the airport and other areas essential to an evacuation. The majority of those who wished to leave were evacuated within the first two days of the operation, but many chose to stay following the arrival of British forces.\n</p><p>After the effective completion of the evacuation, the mandate of the British forces began to expand. They assisted with the evacuation of besieged peacekeepers\xe2\x80\x94including several British ceasefire observers\xe2\x80\x94and began to assist the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Mission_in_Sierra_Leone" title="United Nations Mission in Sierra Leone">United Nations Mission in Sierra Leone</a> (UNAMSIL) and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Sierra Leone Army">Sierra Leone Army</a> (SLA). Despite the mission expansion, it was not until 17 May that British soldiers came into direct contact with the RUF. The rebels attacked a British position near Lungi airport, but were forced to retreat after a series of firefights. On the same day, the RUF\'s leader, <a href="/wiki/Foday_Sankoh" title="Foday Sankoh">Foday Sankoh</a>, was captured by Sierra Leonean forces, leaving the RUF in disarray. After deciding that the RUF would not disarm voluntarily, the British began training the SLA for a confrontation. During the training mission, a patrol returning from a visit to Jordanian peacekeepers was taken captive by a militia group known as the <a href="/wiki/West_Side_Boys" title="West Side Boys">West Side Boys</a>. Negotiations achieved the release of five of the eleven soldiers, and three weeks into the crisis, British special forces launched a mission codenamed <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barras" title="Operation Barras">Operation Barras</a>, freeing the remaining six. The success of Operation Barras restored confidence in the British mission; one academic suggested that its failure would have forced the British government to withdraw all its forces from Sierra Leone.\n</p><p>The overall British operation was mostly completed by September 2000. The RUF began to disarm after political pressure, and later economic sanctions, were exerted on <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>\xe2\x80\x94which had supported the RUF in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Conflict_diamonds" class="mw-redirect" title="Conflict diamonds">conflict diamonds</a> smuggled out of Sierra Leone. The Sierra Leonean government eventually signed a ceasefire with the RUF that obliged the latter to enter the <a href="/wiki/Disarmament,_Demobilisation,_and_Reintegration" class="mw-redirect" title="Disarmament, Demobilisation, and Reintegration">Disarmament, Demobilisation, and Reintegration</a> (DDR) process. By September 2001, when the British training teams were replaced by an international force, the DDR process was almost complete. British forces continued to be involved in Sierra Leone by providing the largest contribution of personnel to the international training team and advising on a restructuring of Sierra Leone\'s armed forces. A small force was deployed to the area in 2003 to ensure stability while several indictments and arrests were made by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Court_for_Sierra_Leone" title="Special Court for Sierra Leone">Special Court for Sierra Leone</a>. The success of British operations in Sierra Leone vindicated several concepts, including the retention of high-readiness forces. The Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, was keen to see Western interventions in other conflicts, and\xe2\x80\x94along with France\xe2\x80\x94supported the creation of several <a href="/wiki/EU_Battlegroup" title="EU Battlegroup">European Union Battlegroups</a> for the purpose. As it happened, political opposition and later British commitments in Afghanistan and Iraq prevented further British operations in Africa.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" Leone">History of Sierra Leone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Civil_War" title="Sierra Leone Civil War">Sierra Leone Civil War</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sierra_Leone-Mappa.gif" class="image"><img alt="A map of Sierra Leone" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Sierra_Leone-Mappa.gif/220px-Sierra_Leone-Mappa.gif" width="220" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ee/Sierra_Leone-Mappa.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="330" data-file-height="355" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sierra_Leone-Mappa.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map of Sierra Leone showing the capital Freetown, Lungi airport, and several other locations of British operations</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a> is a country in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, close to the equator, with an area of 71,740 square kilometres (27,700 square miles)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\xe2\x80\x94similar in size to South Carolina or Scotland. It shares land borders with <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a> and Liberia and is bordered to the west by the Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The country became a <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Colony_and_Protectorate" title="Sierra Leone Colony and Protectorate">British colony</a> in 1808, though British influence began in the late 18th century when former slaves were settled in the area that became known as <a href="/wiki/Freetown" title="Freetown">Freetown</a>, now the capital city.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Freetown lies on a peninsula, and is separated from the country\'s main airport, <a href="/wiki/Lungi_International_Airport" title="Lungi International Airport">Lungi</a>, by the estuary of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_River" title="Sierra Leone River">Sierra Leone River</a>, which is several miles wide.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_10_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The colony was granted independence from the United Kingdom in 1961 and Sir <a href="/wiki/Milton_Margai" title="Milton Margai">Milton Margai</a> was appointed its first prime minister. He was replaced in 1962 by his brother, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Margai" title="Albert Margai">Albert</a>, who was defeated by <a href="/wiki/Siaka_Stevens" title="Siaka Stevens">Siaka Stevens</a> in the 1967 general election. Stevens was overthrown within hours by the commander of the army, but was later reinstated after the commander was himself overthrown. Sierra Leone became a republic in 1971, and Stevens was installed as its first president.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1978, Sierra Leone formally became a one-party state and the <a href="/wiki/All_People%27s_Congress" title="All People&#39;s Congress">All People\'s Congress</a> (APC) became the only legal political party. Stevens retired in 1985 and appointed <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Momoh" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Momoh">Joseph Momoh</a> as his successor. Momoh was accused of corruption and abuse of power, and the <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_United_Front" title="Revolutionary United Front">Revolutionary United Front</a> (RUF) was formed later in the decade with the aim of overthrowing him.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Sponsored by Liberia, the RUF began attacking settlements along the border in 1991 and quickly took control of the diamond mines, whose products they smuggled through Liberia and traded for weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Williams_145_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_145-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The following years saw a series of coups and interventions by <a href="/wiki/Private_military_companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Private military companies">private military companies</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_145_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_145-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Nigeria, the <a href="/wiki/Economic_Community_of_West_African_States" title="Economic Community of West African States">Economic Community of West African States</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> (UN), while a bloody civil war devastated the country.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 7 July 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Lom%C3%A9_Peace_Accord" title="Lom\xc3\xa9 Peace Accord">Lom\xc3\xa9 Peace Accord</a> was signed.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Among other provisions, the agreement mandated an immediate ceasefire between the main parties to the civil war and the disarmament of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Sierra_Leone_Armed_Forces" title="Republic of Sierra Leone Armed Forces">Sierra Leone Army</a> (SLA) and the RUF. It also gave the RUF status as a legitimate political party, a role in the Sierra Leone Government, and four of the twenty-two seats in the cabinet. <a href="/wiki/Foday_Sankoh" title="Foday Sankoh">Foday Sankoh</a>, leader of the RUF, was given responsibility for the diamond mines\xe2\x80\x94an appointment much criticised by observers and the international media given the RUF\'s history of diamond smuggling.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Hain" title="Peter Hain">Peter Hain</a>, Minister of State for Africa, suggested that the British government had no choice but to endorse the Lom\xc3\xa9 agreement given the RUF\'s dominance, and that the only alternative was continued civil war. A military intervention by the United Kingdom in 1999 was ruled out as the British government had received no request for military assistance and felt it lacked the backing of the international community for a unilateral intervention. An intervention was also deemed to be politically and militarily impractical given the British military\'s involvement with <a href="/wiki/NATO_bombing_of_Yugoslavia" title="NATO bombing of Yugoslavia">NATO operations in Yugoslavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tony_Blair_and_the_British_Armed_Forces">Tony Blair and the British Armed Forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tony Blair and the British Armed Forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The intervention in Sierra Leone was the fourth expeditionary operation and the second significant deployment undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Armed_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Armed Forces">Her Majesty\'s Armed Forces</a> under <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, who was elected as Prime Minister in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_13_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_13-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-beeb_I_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeb_I-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The first two were relatively minor operations: a series of air strikes against Iraq in 1998 codenamed <a href="/wiki/Operation_Desert_Fox" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Desert Fox">Operation Desert Fox</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and a deployment of a company of <a href="/wiki/Gurkha" title="Gurkha">Gurkhas</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Special_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="British Special Forces">special forces</a> on peacekeeping operations in <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a> in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_9_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_9-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The third operation, the first major deployment under Blair, was in Kosovo in 1999, where British forces led a <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> intervention in the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_War" title="Kosovo War">Kosovo War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_13_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_13-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the British operations in Kosovo, Blair delivered a speech in Chicago, in which he outlined his "Doctrine of the International Community".<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_13_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_13-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kampfner_52_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kampfner_52-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Blair advocated a greater use of <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian_intervention" title="Humanitarian intervention">humanitarian intervention</a>\xe2\x80\x94the use of armed force to protect a civilian population, rather than exclusively to protect national interests.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Kosovo did not diminish Blair\'s belief in the use of military force for humanitarian purposes "where a strong moral case could be made",<sup id="cite_ref-Blair_246_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair_246-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and he outlined a set of criteria for intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-Kampfner_52_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kampfner_52-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The intervention in Sierra Leone, according to Andrew M. Dorman of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King\'s College London</a>, "appears to embody much of the ethos contained within [the Chicago speech]".<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_13_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_13-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_British_deployments">Previous British deployments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Previous British deployments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The intervention in May 2000 was the first major deployment of British forces to Sierra Leone during the civil war, but was not the first time British personnel had served there. In May 1997, a two-man training team from the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> was sent to train SLA officers but discovered that the SLA\'s strength was much lower than it had reported. The government was overthrown in a coup before any training could take place.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Penfold_15_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfold_15-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Following the restoration of the elected government by the <a href="/wiki/Economic_Community_of_West_African_States_Monitoring_Group" title="Economic Community of West African States Monitoring Group">Economic Community of West African States Monitoring Group</a> (ECOMOG) in February 1998, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Cornwall_(F99)" title="HMS Cornwall (F99)">HMS <i>Cornwall</i></a> sailed to Freetown with food and medical supplies.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Her crew assisted with infrastructure repairs and her helicopter was used to move people and supplies around Sierra Leone until she left in mid-April.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> As the security situation in Sierra Leone deteriorated later in the year, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> (RAF) conducted a non-combatant evacuation operation under the codename Operation Spartic over Christmas 1998. Approximately 80 people\xe2\x80\x94predominantly British citizens, many of them staff or dependants from the British High Commission\xe2\x80\x94were evacuated over two days.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_10_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_46_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_46-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 1999, the RUF attacked Freetown. They were pushed back to the eastern edges of the city by ECOMOG, after which <a href="/wiki/HMS_Norfolk_(F230)" title="HMS Norfolk (F230)">HMS <i>Norfolk</i></a> was sent to offer assistance. On arrival, a team from the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_International_Development" title="Department for International Development">Department for International Development</a> (DfID) was based on board the ship to assist the crew\'s efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-Penfold_118_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfold_118-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Penfold" title="Peter Penfold">Peter Penfold</a>, the British High Commissioner\xe2\x80\x94who had been evacuated to Guinea\xe2\x80\x94temporarily lived on the ship before it was deemed safe for him to return to his residence in Freetown. He flew ashore on the <i>Norfolk</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s helicopter for daily meetings, with a detachment of Royal Marines ensuring security.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> HMS <i>Norfolk</i> was replaced by <a href="/wiki/HMS_Westminster_(F237)" title="HMS Westminster (F237)">HMS <i>Westminster</i></a>, shortly after which Penfold moved back to his residence and the Royal Marines took over security of the compound temporarily.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fighting was eventually ended by the Lom\xc3\xa9 Peace Accord, which was signed in July 1999. The United Nations Observer Mission to Sierra Leone was replaced with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Mission_in_Sierra_Leone" title="United Nations Mission in Sierra Leone">United Nations Mission in Sierra Leone</a> (UNAMSIL), which included a force of 260 military observers. The observers were unarmed and tasked with monitoring the ceasefire mandated by the Lom\xc3\xa9 Agreement. The observer force, like UNAMSIL itself, was primarily made up of personnel from other African nations, but the United Kingdom contributed a small number of officers from the British Army and Royal Marines.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_26_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_26-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_48_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_48-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In addition to the observers in Sierra Leone, personnel from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Logistic_Corps" title="Royal Logistic Corps">Royal Logistic Corps</a> were serving in New York, assisting UNAMSIL with organising air-lifts to bring the mission up to its authorised strength.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_64_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_64-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Build-up_to_the_intervention">Build-up to the intervention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span a brigadier) commanded British operations in Sierra Leone.</div></div></div>\n<p>In accordance with the Lom\xc3\xa9 Agreement, UNAMSIL set up disarmament camps throughout Sierra Leone that were intended to disarm the Sierra Leone Army, the RUF, and the militia groups operating in the country. The SLA and some militia groups began to enter the camps but the RUF did not. In April 2000, 10 members of the RUF entered a UNAMSIL camp without the knowledge of the RUF\'s leadership. Upon locating its fighters, the RUF demanded their return. The military observers refused, and the RUF responded by besieging the camp and attacking other UNAMSIL bases in the area. They took large numbers of UN personnel prisoner, and then began to advance into areas previously controlled by the Sierra Leone government. On 3 May, the RUF took control of the town of <a href="/wiki/Kambia_(Sierra_Leone)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kambia (Sierra Leone)">Kambia</a>. Foreign diplomats in the country estimated that the RUF could be in Freetown within a week, since the SLA had been confined to barracks and had handed over most of its weapons in accordance with the Lom\xc3\xa9 Agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_54_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_54-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The United Nations issued a statement condemning the violence, after which Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a> told the British representative to the UN that it expected the United Kingdom, as the former colonial power, to intervene in Sierra Leone directly, rather than relying on the international community.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_58_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_58-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 5 May 2000, the British government continued to state that it would provide only logistical and technical support to UNAMSIL,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> but was privately exploring its options for a military deployment. The United Kingdom had a greater level of political involvement in Sierra Leone than in any other African country and, with the country\'s stability deteriorating, it was reluctant to see that investment wasted.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_66_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_66-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kampfner_70_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kampfner_70-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In addition, an estimated 1,000 entitled personnel<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup> were in Sierra Leone, and the government feared for their safety.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_66_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_66-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Academics have since suggested that the credibility of UNAMSIL and future UN peacekeeping operations would have been at stake had the mission in Sierra Leone been allowed to fail.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The British Armed Forces were not as widely deployed in 2000 as they were to be later in the decade. The British Army had two brigades serving with NATO in the Balkans, and the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> (MoD) had ongoing commitments to Cyprus, the Falkland Islands, and elsewhere,<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_66_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_66-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> but the armed forces\xe2\x80\x94particularly units threatened by proposed cuts to the defence budget\xe2\x80\x94were keen to participate in an operation. Senior officers thus advised the government that an operation in Sierra Leone was feasible.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_66_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_66-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kampfner_70_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kampfner_70-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Over the following days, there was debate within the British government as to what the aims of a military deployment to Sierra Leone would be. The <a href="/wiki/Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office" title="Foreign and Commonwealth Office">Foreign and Commonwealth Office</a> (FCO) advocated a full-scale intervention to assist UNAMSIL, arguing that a non-combatant evacuation operation would not be sufficient and would undermine the UN, but the MoD believed that the armed forces would be unable to sustain a larger-scale operation.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_66_39-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_66-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Two large military helicopters on a runway." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg/220px-Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg" width="220" height="126" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg/330px-Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg/440px-Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1495" data-file-height="853" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chinook.hc2.za677.arp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An RAF CH-47 Chinook; Chinooks were essential to the evacuation and supported later British operations in Sierra Leone, but as the RAF lacked the capacity to transport them intact, two had to fly to Freetown from the UK.</div></div></div>\n<p>The British government\'s emergency committee, <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office_Briefing_Room" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet Office Briefing Room">COBRA</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;note 3&#93;</a></sup> was convened and presented with three options for an evacuation of entitled persons\xe2\x80\x94deployment of aircraft and special forces to conduct an evacuation via  Lungi airport, deployment of regular ground forces for a similar operation, or re-routing the Amphibious Ready Group (ARG).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;note 4&#93;</a></sup> COBRA concluded that it lacked sufficient information to recommend one of the three options and instructed the MoD to continue to develop them, while also recommending that an "operational reconnaissance and liaison team" (ORLT) be sent to Sierra Leone to assess the situation and advise on how the military could be useful. The Prime Minister, Tony Blair, approved the ORLT, which was led by Brigadier <a href="/wiki/David_Richards_(British_Army_officer)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Richards (British Army officer)">David Richards</a>, Chief of Joint Force Operations.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_10_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Richards had previously visited Sierra Leone twice during the civil war\xe2\x80\x94first on the HMS <i>Norfolk</i> in early 1999 and again in early 2000\xe2\x80\x94and was familiar with the political leadership of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-Penfold_118_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfold_118-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> He and his team left from <a href="/wiki/RAF_Northolt" title="RAF Northolt">RAF Northolt</a> eight hours later accompanied by a close protection force, and arrived in Freetown in the early hours of 6 May.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_10_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_71_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_71-51">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_134_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_134-52">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The ORLT established itself in the British High Commission in Freetown, where daily political\xe2\x80\x93military coordination meetings were held throughout the operation.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_136_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_136-53">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The readiness of several other assets was heightened on 5 May. Two <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> vessels\xe2\x80\x94the aircraft carrier <a href="/wiki/HMS_Illustrious_(R06)" title="HMS Illustrious (R06)">HMS <i>Illustrious</i></a> and the frigate <a href="/wiki/HMS_Argyll_(F231)" title="HMS Argyll (F231)">HMS <i>Argyll</i></a>\xe2\x80\x94were ordered to sail to the area,  as was the ARG (which had been on exercise in southern France). The standby special forces squadron and <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion,_Parachute_Regiment" title="1st Battalion, Parachute Regiment">1st Battalion, Parachute Regiment</a> (1 PARA), were both ordered to prepare for a potential operation in Sierra Leone; and several RAF transport aircraft were taken off other duties and ordered to be prepared to airlift special forces and/or 1 PARA to Lungi airport. At the same time, four RAF <a href="/wiki/CH-47_Chinook" class="mw-redirect" title="CH-47 Chinook">CH-47 Chinooks</a> were ordered to deploy to Sierra Leone\xe2\x80\x94two from the Balkans and two from their base in the United Kingdom. The RAF lacked aircraft large enough to transport Chinooks and so the helicopter crews flew themselves to Freetown. The 3,000-mile (4,800&#160;km) flight undertaken by the two aircraft based in the UK was the longest self-deployment of helicopters in British history.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;note 5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_74_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_74-54">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Palliser">Operation Palliser</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operation Palliser">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 6 May 2000, the RUF blocked the road connecting <a href="/wiki/Freetown" title="Freetown">Freetown</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lungi_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Lungi Airport">Lungi Airport</a>, prompting <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Mission_in_Sierra_Leone" title="United Nations Mission in Sierra Leone">UNAMSIL</a> staff to evacuate to the Mamy Yoko Hotel in preparation for a total withdrawal from Sierra Leone if the RUF advance continued towards Freetown. In response to the deterioration Richards requested that British troops be sent to <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, Senegal, to decrease the time required to launch an operation in Sierra Leone. Richards also spoke to the <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion,_Parachute_Regiment" title="1st Battalion, Parachute Regiment">1 PARA</a> command to update them on the situation. Following the conversation, 1 PARA (with 2 PARA\'s D Company replacing A Company, who were on exercise in Jamaica, and with several attached assets including artillery) moved to the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Gloucester_Barracks" title="Duke of Gloucester Barracks">Air Movements Centre</a> in South Cerney, Gloucestershire. The following day Richards was designated Joint Task Force Commander and his ORLT became the forward headquarters for a British deployment. At the same time, the authority to launch an evacuation operation was delegated to Richards and the British High Commissioner, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Jones_(diplomat)" title="Alan Jones (diplomat)">Alan Jones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_78_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_78-56">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Williams_154_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_154-57">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the RUF rapidly advancing on Freetown and controlling most of the interior of Sierra Leone, the only means of rapidly evacuating entitled persons or reinforcing UNAMSIL was by air via Lungi airport. Thus, the enhanced 1 PARA was flown to Dakar on 7 May, where C Company and the special forces squadron were almost immediately put aboard RAF <a href="/wiki/Hercules_C-130" class="mw-redirect" title="Hercules C-130">Hercules C-130s</a> with orders to secure the airport. They arrived at Lungi before sunset and were joined by the remaining elements of 1 PARA the next morning.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_78_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_78-56">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The soldiers were able to deploy rapidly and with minimal equipment, knowing that they would not have to wait long for the ARG\'s reinforcements and supplies should they be needed.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The soldiers immediately set about securing the areas that would be vital to an evacuation, including the Mamy Yoko hotel, which became the evacuation centre, and Lungi airport.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_11_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_11-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Jones requested in the afternoon of 8 May that Richards commence the evacuation\xe2\x80\x94codenamed Operation Palliser\xe2\x80\x94which Richards did almost immediately. Entitled persons who wished to leave were instructed to assemble at the Mamy Yoko hotel. From there, they would be helicoptered to the airport by Chinooks and then flown to Dakar.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_78_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_78-56">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over the course of a week, British forces evacuated approximately 500 entitled persons from Sierra Leone\xe2\x80\x94almost 300 of whom left in the first two days of the operation.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_82_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_82-60">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Connaughton_86_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Connaughton_86-61">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The arrival of British soldiers boosted morale in the country, and many foreign citizens opted to stay.<sup id="cite_ref-beeb_I_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeb_I-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_82_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_82-60">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The operation took on a slower pace after the first two days, but personnel and aircraft remained ready to evacuate any entitled persons who had been unable to reach Freetown earlier and to evacuate the British High Commission if the security situation deteriorated.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_11_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_11-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_expansion">Mission expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mission expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Westminster</a>, the three government departments concerned with the British role in Sierra Leone\xe2\x80\x94the MoD, the FCO, and DfID\xe2\x80\x94struggled to agree on the objectives of the military deployment beyond the evacuation, which led to delays in issuing orders. Richards did not receive precise instructions until after Operation Palliser had commenced, and <a href="/wiki/Rules_of_engagement" title="Rules of engagement">rules of engagement</a> (ROEs) were not issued before the start of the operation. Commanders defaulted to the ROEs used in Northern Ireland, their most recent relevant experience.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the evacuation largely complete, the British government turned its attention to the four British United Nations Military Observers (<a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Military_Observers" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Military Observers">UNMOs</a>) being held by the RUF. British forces in Freetown helped facilitate the escape of four UNMOs (three British and one from New Zealand) from a UNAMSIL camp at <a href="/wiki/Makeni" title="Makeni">Makeni</a>, which had been besieged by the RUF since ten RUF fighters had been accepted into the disarmament process. After consulting with the British command in Freetown, the four officers left the camp and covertly passed the RUF line before trekking west. They arrived at the UN base at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mile_91&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mile 91 (page does not exist)">Mile 91</a> almost 24 hours later, and an RAF Chinook picked them up and flew them to Freetown. No longer having the unarmed observers to protect, the Kenyan UNAMSIL detachment at Makeni fought their way out of the siege and proceeded west to join other UNAMSIL forces.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_83_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_83-66">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> With the three British officers from Makeni freed, only one British UNMO\xe2\x80\x94Major Andy Harrison\xe2\x80\x94remained a prisoner of the RUF, and the British government began discreetly attempting to establish his location.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_83_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_83-66">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_12_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_12-67">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Harrison and ten other UNMOs had initially been held by the RUF at the latter\'s base until Harrison convinced the RUF to allow the observers to join the Indian UNAMSIL contingent at <a href="/wiki/Kailahun" title="Kailahun">Kailahun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_84_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_84-68">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to the missing UNMOs, the British government faced political and diplomatic problems. The deployment of British troops to Sierra Leone had lifted morale and halted the RUF advance on Freetown, and there were concerns that violence would resume once the British left. Another consequence of the British operation was that it effectively sidelined UNAMSIL. The United Nations and several of the contributing nations to UNAMSIL applied pressure on the British government to integrate its forces into UNAMSIL, but the MoD lacked faith in the competence of UNAMSIL headquarters and was unwilling to place its troops under UNAMSIL command. The MoD was also reluctant to deploy the brigade-sized force necessary to take command of UNAMSIL, given the armed forces\' commitments elsewhere, and thus the British force in Sierra Leone remained outside UNAMSIL.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_88_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_88-69">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The British government was also reluctant to commit British troops to an open-ended peacekeeping operation, especially given the opposition in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Commons (United Kingdom)">House of Commons</a>\xe2\x80\x94particularly from the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>\xe2\x80\x94to the initial deployment to Sierra Leone, and accusations from the British media of "<a href="/wiki/Mission_creep" title="Mission creep">mission creep</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, the operation was well-received on the international stage,<sup id="cite_ref-Kampfner_70_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kampfner_70-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> and met with the approval of the UN Security Council.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 12 May, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Symons,_Baroness_Symons_of_Vernham_Dean" title="Elizabeth Symons, Baroness Symons of Vernham Dean">Baroness Symons</a>, a junior minister in the MoD, told the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> that British forces would remain in Sierra Leone, mainly to ensure the security of Lungi airport while UNAMSIL brought in reinforcements. Soldiers also remained at the evacuation point in Freetown to ensure its security, while others patrolled the streets of Freetown in an attempt to reassure residents. HMS <i>Illustrious</i>, with her air group, and the ARG both arrived on 14 May, bringing the number of British personnel in the operational area to approximately 4,500.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_12_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_12-67">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_90_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_90-74">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Harriers from <i>Illustrious</i> began flying reassurance patrols over Freetown and the ARG supplemented British firepower, particularly at Lungi, with the provision of artillery.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_90_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_90-74">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> In the field, the British forces divided their efforts between three lines of operation: support to UNAMSIL, support to the SLA, and preparation to provide humanitarian assistance, should it be necessary,<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_135_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_135-75">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_13_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_13-76">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> though the expanded mandate only became formal government policy several days later.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_136_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_136-53">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> During the following week, the RUF began to remobilise in the north of the country. The UN and the Sierra Leonean government feared that UNAMSIL troops between the RUF and Freetown might not be able to stand up to an assault by the RUF, and so the RAF Chinooks\xe2\x80\x94in the country to conduct the evacuation\xe2\x80\x94were used to ferry reinforcements from Lungi as they arrived. Meanwhile, President <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Kabbah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ahmad Kabbah">Ahmad Kabbah</a> formed an alliance of militia groups (including the self-styled <a href="/wiki/Civil_Defence_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Defence Force">Civil Defence Force</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Side_Boys" title="West Side Boys">West Side Boys</a>) and the remnants of the SLA, totalling around 6,000 personnel, to assist UNAMSIL forces in blocking the RUF advance. The British also provided reconnaissance for UNAMSIL using ground-based signals and intelligence personnel and special forces as well as flights by <a href="/wiki/Harrier_Jump_Jet" title="Harrier Jump Jet">Harriers</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Nimrod_R1" class="mw-redirect" title="Nimrod R1">Nimrod R1</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SeaHarrier.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A fighter jet taking off vertically" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/SeaHarrier.jpg/220px-SeaHarrier.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/SeaHarrier.jpg/330px-SeaHarrier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/SeaHarrier.jpg/440px-SeaHarrier.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SeaHarrier.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Harrier_Jump_Jet" title="Harrier Jump Jet">Harrier Jump Jet</a>; Harriers were used for reconnaissance and reassurance during the British intervention.</div></div></div>\n<p>The RUF continued to advance, resulting in sporadic confrontations with UNAMSIL and government forces, until on 17 May they came into direct contact with British forces. The <a href="/wiki/Pathfinder_Platoon" title="Pathfinder Platoon">Pathfinder Platoon</a> had stationed itself at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lungi_Lol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lungi Lol (page does not exist)">Lungi Lol</a>, a village 12 miles (19&#160;km) north of Freetown close to Lungi airport, and shortly after were <a href="/wiki/Lungi_Lol_confrontation" title="Lungi Lol confrontation">confronted by a group of RUF members</a>. The resulting series of firefights lasted several hours, after which the RUF withdrew, having suffered 30 casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_94_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_94-78">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kampfner_71_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kampfner_71-79">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> According to Richards, the British success in the confrontation provided an "immense" psychological victory and a deterrent against further attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_94_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_94-78">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Foday Sankoh, leader of the RUF, was captured later the same day by forces loyal to President Kabbah and handed over to the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Police" title="Sierra Leone Police">Sierra Leone Police</a>, but had to be evacuated by an RAF Chinook after a hostile crowd gathered outside the building in which he was held.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_13_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_13-76">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Sankoh\'s capture created a power vacuum at the top of the RUF and the subsequent in-fighting provided an opportunity for the MoD to order a rotation of forces deployed in Sierra Leone. The 1 PARA battlegroup was ordered back to the United Kingdom to resume its spearhead role as the permanent stand-by battalion that would form the basis of any emergency deployment, while <a href="/wiki/42_Commando" title="42 Commando">42 Commando</a>, Royal Marines, came ashore to replace the soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-Connaughton_86_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Connaughton_86-61">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_94_78-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_94-78">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Whitehall, the British government laid out its longer-term objectives for the military intervention in Sierra Leone on 23 May. These were: to establish sustainable peace and security in Sierra Leone, to support UNAMSIL operations, to prevent another humanitarian disaster in Freetown, to see the release of  captive UN personnel, and finally to avoid British casualties and devise an exit strategy that avoided "mission creep" without undermining UNAMSIL or the Sierra Leonean government.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_96_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_96-80">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Khukri">Operation Khukri</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Operation Khukri">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Khukri" title="Operation Khukri">Operation Khukri</a></div>\n<p>The British government was particularly keen to secure the release of Major Andy Harrison\xe2\x80\x94the last British UNMO being held by the RUF. Harrison\'s contingent of UNMOs was protected by an Indian Army detachment. The <a href="/wiki/8_Gorkha_Rifles" class="mw-redirect" title="8 Gorkha Rifles">Indian Army Gurkhas</a>\xe2\x80\x94attached to UNAMSIL\xe2\x80\x94were under siege by the RUF at their base in Kailahun. The British and Indian commands in Sierra Leone had devised a plan to extract the UNMOs. British special forces remained in the country, ready to carry it out, but the UN and the British command feared that the RUF would retaliate against other UNAMSIL forces they had besieged if the UNMOs were extracted. Thus, Major General <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vijay_Kumar_Jetley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vijay Kumar Jetley (page does not exist)">Vijay Kumar Jetley</a>, Commander of UNAMSIL, was allowed to continue negotiations for the release of the other besieged UNAMSIL contingents.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_96_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_96-80">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When the last besieged garrison (aside from Kailahun) was evacuated on 30 May, preparations for a military extraction\xe2\x80\x94should Jetley\'s negotiations fail\xe2\x80\x94began to increase. The operation (codenamed Operation Khukri) was eventually launched on 10 July. Two RAF Chinooks transported Indian special forces to the outskirts of Kailahun. The helicopters returned to Freetown with Harrison, his fellow UNMOs, and several Gurkhas who had been wounded during the siege. Harrison was safely extracted and the 600 Gurkhas successfully fought their way out of Kailahun, suffering one casualty in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_97_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_97-81">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_the_SLA">Training the SLA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Training the SLA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The British government had decided that the RUF could not be trusted, and would have to be confronted and forced to enter the UN\'s <a href="/wiki/Disarmament,_Demobilisation,_and_Reintegration" class="mw-redirect" title="Disarmament, Demobilisation, and Reintegration">Disarmament, Demobilisation, and Reintegration</a> (DDR) process.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> They assessed that there were three options available to achieve this\xe2\x80\x94to deploy British forces against the RUF, for UNAMSIL to expand its operations and confront the RUF, or for the Sierra Leonean government to use loyal forces (the SLA, former SLA personnel, the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Revolutionary_Council" title="Armed Forces Revolutionary Council">Armed Forces Revolutionary Council</a>, and several other militia groups)<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_11_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_11-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> to take on the RUF. Richards estimated that a British deployment against the RUF would require at least a brigade-sized force (in excess of 5,000 troops). Nevertheless, British forces\' involvement in Sierra Leone was politically unpopular in Westminster and the MoD could not assemble such a force while maintaining its commitments elsewhere, so the use of British forces to directly confront the RUF was ruled out. Also ruled out was a UNAMSIL-led confrontation. Although UNAMSIL\'s mandate would have allowed it to enter into combat with the RUF, the national contingents were reluctant to leave their bases and its focus remained on peacekeeping rather than the peace enforcement that the British and others believed was necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_97_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_97-81">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This left the SLA and the alliance of militia groups\xe2\x80\x94which became known as the "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Unholy_Alliance_(Sierra_Leone)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Unholy Alliance (Sierra Leone) (page does not exist)">Unholy Alliance</a>", and was directed by a "Joint Military Committee"<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_11_59-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_11-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\xe2\x80\x94the only forces capable of confronting the RUF. The SLA had been disarmed through the UN\'s DDR process; to restructure it and allow it to re-arm, the UN lifted its arms embargo on Sierra Leone, and the British forces began advising and training the SLA.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_10_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_98_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_98-83">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> An international team had been planned to deploy to Sierra Leone to assist the SLA with longer-term development and democratic accountability, and a British Short-Term Training Team (STTT) deployed simultaneously to improve the SLA\'s infantry skills.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_98_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_98-83">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stewart_364_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart_364-84">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The STTT mission was codenamed Operation Basilica and based at the Benguema Training Centre, an abandoned barracks near <a href="/wiki/Waterloo,_Sierra_Leone" title="Waterloo, Sierra Leone">Waterloo</a> that had been refurbished for the purpose. The first unit to take on the role was based around 2nd Battalion, The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Anglian_Regiment" title="Royal Anglian Regiment">Royal Anglian Regiment</a>, and comprised approximately 250 personnel, including 45 instructors and a force protection company.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_98_83-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_98-83">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> The Anglians arrived at Benguema on 15 June to train 1,000 SLA recruits, and the ARG withdrew. The training at Benguema included instruction on the Geneva Convention, unit cohesiveness, and other skills and knowledge to build the SLA into a professional army.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_100_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_100-85">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite the British training, the SLA was not large enough or strong enough to enter into combat with the RUF while also holding the ground it had recaptured, so the British persuaded UNAMSIL to move forward behind the advancing SLA to defend its recaptured ground. Additional British liaison officers were attached to UNAMSIL, and the British facilitated a daily coordination meeting for SLA and UNAMSIL commanders, while also assisting the UN forces in drawing up a campaign plan.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_11_59-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_11-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_135_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_135-75">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Barras">Operation Barras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Operation Barras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barras" title="Operation Barras">Operation Barras</a></div>\n<p>The Anglians were replaced by a detachment of 1st Battalion, The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Regiment_(1992)" title="Royal Irish Regiment (1992)">Royal Irish Regiment</a>, formed around C Company. On 25 August, a patrol from the Royal Irish went to visit a militia group known as the West Side Boys (WSB). At the village of Magbeni, where the WSB were based, the Royal Irish were overpowered and taken captive. British officers undertook negotiations with the WSB, leading to the release of five of the eleven soldiers on 31 August.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 9 September, the WSB\'s spokesman stated that the remaining six members of the patrol, who had now been held for over a fortnight, would only be released after a new government was formed in Sierra Leone, leading negotiators to conclude that the increasingly unrealistic demands were stalling tactics rather than a serious attempt to conclude the crisis. At around the same time, teams that had been observing the West Side Boys\' base for four days reported that they had seen no sign of the captive soldiers in that time. There were also concerns that an assault would become more dangerous if the West Side Boys moved the hostages. The combination of these factors led <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office_Briefing_Room" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet Office Briefing Room">COBRA</a> to order an extraction mission.<sup id="cite_ref-thirty_two_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thirty_two-87">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The mission, codenamed Operation Barras, was undertaken by D Squadron of 22 <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service" title="Special Air Service">Special Air Service</a> Regiment, who assaulted the village of Gberi Bana to extract the soldiers, while a company group formed around A Company, <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion,_Parachute_Regiment" title="1st Battalion, Parachute Regiment">1 PARA</a>, assaulted Magbeni, on the opposite side of <a href="/wiki/Rokel_River" title="Rokel River">Rokel Creek</a>. The operation was successful and all the British captives were extracted, along with their <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Sierra_Leone_Armed_Forces" title="Republic of Sierra Leone Armed Forces">SLA</a> liaison and 22 Sierra Leonean civilians, while the WSB were defeated as a military force.<sup id="cite_ref-fifty_one_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fifty_one-88">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> A British soldier and at least 25 West Side Boys were killed in the operation.<sup id="cite_ref-fifty_one_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fifty_one-88">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Many other West Side Boys fled and later surrendered to Jordanian peacekeepers. The Jordanians had received 30 by the end of the day, and 371\xe2\x80\x94including 57 children\xe2\x80\x94had surrendered within a fortnight. Some of those who surrendered went on to volunteer for the new Sierra Leone Army, and those who were accepted went into the British-run training programme at Benguema.<sup id="cite_ref-fifty_five_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fifty_five-90">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Following Operation Barras, two SLA battalions\xe2\x80\x94graduates of the British short-term training programme\xe2\x80\x94swept the area surrounding the West Side Boys\' camp to clear it of any remaining gang members.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The risks of Operation Barras were acknowledged by the MoD and by officers involved in the planning and the assault. It was described by an SAS soldier as "not a clinical, black balaclava, <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Embassy_siege" title="Iranian Embassy siege">Princes Gate</a> type operation. It was a very grubby, green operation with lots of potential for things to go wrong".<sup id="cite_ref-fifty_nine_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fifty_nine-92">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Despite the risks, Richard Connaughton observed in <i>Small Wars &amp; Insurgencies</i> that the operation showed the Blair government was not averse to the possibility of casualties when they felt the cause was just.<sup id="cite_ref-fifty_nine_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fifty_nine-92">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> During the crisis and its immediate aftermath, the British government came under pressure from opposition politicians to end the deployment to Sierra Leone,<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_154_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_154-57">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_114_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_114-94">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> and Dorman suggested that the success or failure of Operation Barras was "inextricably linked" to the fate of the wider British operation. He suggested that, had the British forces been defeated, the United Kingdom would have been forced to withdraw all its forces from Sierra Leone.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_9_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_9-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_114_94-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_114-94">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Confronting_the_RUF">Confronting the RUF</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Confronting the RUF">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The capture of the Royal Irish patrol reinforced to the British government that its efforts so far\xe2\x80\x94and those of the international community\xe2\x80\x94would not be sufficient to bring the civil war to an end. In Westminster, meanwhile, opposition politicians renewed their objection to the continued presence of British forces in Sierra Leone. The government was seeking an exit strategy that would end a politically unpopular deployment without abandoning Sierra Leone.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In August 2000, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> passed <a href="/wiki/UNSCR_1313" class="mw-redirect" title="UNSCR 1313">Resolution 1313</a>, which blamed the RUF for the continuing conflict in Sierra Leone, citing multiple breaches of the Lom\xc3\xa9 Peace Accord. The resolution authorised an increase in the size of UNAMSIL and strengthened its mandate, which prompted the UN to once again apply pressure to the British government for a  troop contribution. Several countries were reluctant to send their own troops to Sierra Leone without contributions from Western nations, and felt that the United Kingdom in particular should be contributing to the UN mission.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite the political pressure, the MoD continued to lack confidence in UNAMSIL leadership. Thus, the British government refused to place combat troops under UN command, but did second additional staff officers to UNAMSIL, to the UN headquarters in New York, and to the SLA. The officers attached to UNAMSIL were tasked with assisting its commanders in planning and mounting operations and were led by a <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_(United_Kingdom)" title="Brigadier (United Kingdom)">brigadier</a> who became UNAMSIL\'s chief of staff, while in New York, the officers attached to UN headquarters provided planning support for logistics operations to bring UNAMSIL up to its mandated strength. At the same time, the focus of the British training programme shifted. Although six battalions had been trained, the SLA still lacked many combat support functions as well as command and control capabilities. The STTTs set out to improve the SLA\'s capabilities in these areas by providing the next set of recruits with more specialised training in addition to basic infantry training provided to the first intake.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> The British trainers also constructed an operations room at the SLA\'s headquarters and provided other support to improve the SLA\'s communications and logistical capabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_135_75-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_135-75">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Resolution 1313 was a significant shift in attitude for UNAMSIL, away from its previous neutrality to support of the Sierra Leone government, a shift that made the governments of several troop-contributing nations uncomfortable. In particular, the governments of Jordan and India\xe2\x80\x94two of the largest contributors, with nearly 5,000 troops between them serving with UNAMSIL\xe2\x80\x94were moved to withdraw their forces. The withdrawal coincided with the end of the rainy season, after which there were fears that the RUF would resume its advance towards Freetown, and the UN and British government feared that UNAMSIL would be vulnerable. As a deterrent, the ARG was once more deployed off the coast, and was instructed to conduct amphibious landing demonstrations as a show of force.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceasefire">Ceasefire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ceasefire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A man panning for diamonds" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg/220px-Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg/330px-Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg/440px-Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="295" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hands_ondiamonds_350.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A man panning for diamonds in Sierra Leone; the exploitation of the country\'s diamonds inspired the terms "<a href="/wiki/Blood_diamond" title="Blood diamond">blood diamond</a>" and "conflict diamond".</div></div></div>\n<p>The RUF was coming under increasing pressure from political angles as well as from the British-trained SLA. It was heavily dependent on Sierra Leone\'s south-eastern neighbour <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Taylor_(Liberian_politician)" title="Charles Taylor (Liberian politician)">Charles Taylor</a>, and derived the majority of its income from the sale of diamonds smuggled through Liberia, which became known as <a href="/wiki/Blood_diamonds" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood diamonds">blood diamonds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_145_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_145-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_122_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_122-99">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> In late 2000, the Sierra Leone government\xe2\x80\x94supported by the British, UNAMSIL, and the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS)\xe2\x80\x94entered into talks with the RUF. On 10 November, the two parties signed a 30-day ceasefire that provided for UNAMSIL to deploy throughout the entire country (it had previously been prevented from operating in many RUF-controlled areas), for the RUF to return seized weapons and equipment to UNAMSIL, and for the RUF to enter the DDR  process. The ceasefire was later extended by a further 90 days.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> The UN Security Council embargoed Liberian diamonds in <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1343" title="United Nations Security Council Resolution 1343">Resolution 1343</a> in March 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_121_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_121-101">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> Shortly afterwards, the RUF began large-scale disarmament and agreed to a simultaneous disarmament with the Civil Defence Force, a militia group loyal to the government.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_121_101-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_121-101">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> By September, over 16,000 militia members (including around 6,500 RUF) had been through the DDR process and the combatants in the diamond-producing areas had all disarmed.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_122_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_122-99">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By March 2002, over 50,000 people had been through the DDR process and the RUF had been entirely disarmed.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_122_99-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_122-99">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> A company of <a href="/wiki/Brigade_of_Gurkhas" title="Brigade of Gurkhas">Gurkhas</a> and a Royal Navy frigate were sent to the area in March 2003 to ensure stability while several prominent people\xe2\x80\x94including Charles Taylor of Liberia, cabinet minister <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hinga_Norman" title="Samuel Hinga Norman">Samuel Hinga Norman</a>, and several former RUF leaders\xe2\x80\x94were arrested and indicted by the <a href="/wiki/Special_Court_for_Sierra_Leone" title="Special Court for Sierra Leone">Special Court for Sierra Leone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;note 6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The last STTT, formed around 2nd Battalion, <a href="/wiki/The_Light_Infantry" title="The Light Infantry">The Light Infantry</a>, left Sierra Leone at the end of September 2001. Approximately 8,500 SLA personnel were trained by the STTTs, which were replaced by the International Military Assistance and Training Team (IMATT)\xe2\x80\x94an organisation formed of personnel from countries including Australia, Canada, and the United States, with the UK providing the largest contingent as well as an infantry company for force protection. The STTTs also formed a small <a href="/wiki/Special_forces" title="Special forces">special forces</a> unit within the SLA\xe2\x80\x94the Force Reconnaissance Unit (FRU)\xe2\x80\x94to provide a morale boost and to give soldiers something to which they could aspire.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> Later in 2001, the British Army advised the Sierra Leonean government on a merger of Sierra Leone\'s armed forces into a unified command, which became the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Sierra_Leone_Armed_Forces" title="Republic of Sierra Leone Armed Forces">Republic of Sierra Leone Armed Forces</a> in early 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> In 2008, the permanent British contingent in Sierra Leone was reduced to 100 personnel.<sup id="cite_ref-Stewart_364_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart_364-84">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> British soldiers remained in Sierra Leone as of 2013, continuing to form part of the IMATT, whose size has further reduced in accordance with the increased capabilities of the Sierra Leone armed forces.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TonyBlairBasra.JPG" class="image"><img alt="A man in a suit and tie" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/TonyBlairBasra.JPG/200px-TonyBlairBasra.JPG" width="200" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/TonyBlairBasra.JPG/300px-TonyBlairBasra.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/TonyBlairBasra.JPG/400px-TonyBlairBasra.JPG 2x" data-file-width="408" data-file-height="593" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TonyBlairBasra.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tony Blair, then British prime minister, ordered the deployment of British forces to Sierra Leone. The humanitarian intervention in Sierra Leone was widely considered successful.</div></div></div>\n<p>According to Penfold, who served as High Commissioner until the week before the deployment of British troops, "The fact that the major country in the region, i.e. Nigeria, and a permanent member of the UN Security Council, i.e. the UK, took an active interest was crucial in resolving the conflict". On the other hand, he believed that the international community had failed to recognise that the Sierra Leone Civil War was part of a larger conflict in the sub-region, and "it was not until the problem of Charles Taylor and Liberia was addressed that the conflict was resolved".<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> In a later book, Penfold praised Richards\' leadership of the operation, stating that it was "extremely fortunate that Operation Palliser was under the command of an officer of the calibre of David Richards, with his knowledge of the situation and his experience and commitment. David Richards knew that with the resources available he could do more than just assist an evacuation&#160;... he realised that he could actively stabilise the situation".<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> Richards received the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Service_Order" title="Distinguished Service Order">Distinguished Service Order</a> for his leadership of British forces in Sierra Leone, while several other personnel received decorations for gallantry or distinguished service.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The intervention in Sierra Leone was the fourth deployment of British forces abroad during the premiership of Tony Blair, and the largest operation undertaken by the United Kingdom alone since the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> (1982).<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_4_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_4-113">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> It was the second major operation of the Blair government, after Kosovo. During his remaining time in office, British forces undertook operations in Afghanistan and Iraq, but Sierra Leone was the only unilateral operation.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_4_113-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_4-113">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> Unlike Afghanistan and Iraq, the intervention in Sierra Leone was widely regarded as successful.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> It became a "benchmark" for successful expeditionary operations, and was cited by Blair in his rationale for later deployments to Afghanistan and Iraq.<sup id="cite_ref-Penfold_192_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfold_192-115">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> Success in Sierra Leone encouraged the Blair government to continue its support to Africa, particularly with regard to resolving conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_128_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_128-116">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sierra Leone also encouraged Blair\'s policy of humanitarian intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_128_116-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_128-116">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> Critics claimed that it led Blair to see military force as "just another foreign policy option"<sup id="cite_ref-beeb_I_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeb_I-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and that the apparent ease of the success shifted his focus towards the effectiveness of the use of force rather than the political and military risks.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_129_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_129-117">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> In his autobiography, Blair described the operation as one of the least-discussed aspects of his time in office but one of the things of which he is most proud.<sup id="cite_ref-Blair_246_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair_246-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He was keen to intervene in other African nations where civilian populations were at risk, particularly Darfur and Zimbabwe,<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_128_116-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_128-116">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> but a lack of political support, combined with the pressure of large deployments to Afghanistan and Iraq after the <a href="/wiki/11_September_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="11 September attacks">11 September attacks</a> on the United States, prevented further interventions in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;note 7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kampfner_70_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kampfner_70-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_128_116-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_128-116">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> It was not until 2011\xe2\x80\x94when <a href="/wiki/Operation_Ellamy" title="Operation Ellamy">Operation Ellamy</a> was launched as part of a <a href="/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya" title="2011 military intervention in Libya">multi-national intervention</a> in Libya\xe2\x80\x94that the United Kingdom undertook another military intervention in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Penfold_192_115-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfold_192-115">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The experience in Sierra Leone proved the effectiveness of relatively small numbers of well trained and equipped soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> It inspired the British government to work more closely with European allies, particularly France after the latter led <a href="/wiki/Operation_Artemis" title="Operation Artemis">Operation Artemis</a>, a UN-mandated intervention in the Democratic Republic of Congo in 2003. After a summit in 2003, the two governments called on the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU) to develop the capability to rapidly deploy a battle group of around 1,500 personnel able to respond to crises, particularly in Africa. The member states of the EU approved the creation of <a href="/wiki/EU_Battlegroup" title="EU Battlegroup">13 battle groups</a> in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_(11).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_%2811%29.jpg/220px-ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_%2811%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_%2811%29.jpg/330px-ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_%2811%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_%2811%29.jpg/440px-ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_%2811%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ACOTA_Training_in_Sierra_Leone_-_Flickr_-_US_Army_Africa_(11).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soldiers from the British and United States armies training Sierra Leonean soldiers to use a mortar during 2011</div></div></div>\n<p>Sierra Leone convinced Blair and his Defence Secretary <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Hoon" title="Geoff Hoon">Geoff Hoon</a> of the need to alter the focus of British defence policy towards less conventional conflicts and away from more traditional wars between states. The MoD published a <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">white paper</a> in 2003, <i>Delivering Security in a Changing World</i>, which revisited aspects of the 1998 "<a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defence_Review" title="Strategic Defence Review">Strategic Defence Review</a>" (SDR). The SDR had focused on the Middle East and North Africa, and had not envisaged a need to deploy troops to sub-Saharan Africa other than for a potential non-combatant evacuation operation in Zimbabwe.<sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup> Thus, the white paper recommended preparations for relatively short, intense operations against forces with inferior technology, with a particular focus on Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The rapidity with which forces were required to deploy to Sierra Leone emphasised the need for the United Kingdom to retain high-readiness forces. That need also vindicated concepts such as the ARG and the spearhead battalion (the capacity in which 1 PARA was serving when it deployed), and protected 1 PARA in the 2004 review of the infantry structure. The 2004 review reduced the total number of British Army battalions from 40 to 36 and created the <a href="/wiki/Special_Forces_Support_Group" title="Special Forces Support Group">Special Forces Support Group</a> (SFSG), which was inspired by the success of 1 PARA in Operation Barras. The SFSG\xe2\x80\x94initially formed around 1 PARA\xe2\x80\x94provides specialist capabilities or acts as a force multiplier for British special forces on large or complex operations.<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> As the largest unilateral operation undertaken by the United Kingdom since their creation, the intervention in Sierra Leone was the first major test of the ORLT and joint task force concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_00_10_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_00_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_134_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_134-52">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Both were created as a result of the 1997 SDR and provide a headquarters staff at very high readiness to command an expeditionary operation at <a href="/wiki/Operational_level_of_war" title="Operational level of war">operational level</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_134_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_134-52">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> According to Richards, both were "thoroughly validated" and were vital in coordinating the large numbers of assets deployed at short notice and reporting back to the <a href="/wiki/Permanent_Joint_Headquarters" title="Permanent Joint Headquarters">Permanent Joint Headquarters</a> in Northwood.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_01_134_52-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_01_134-52">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A 2015 study in the <i>Journal of Strategic Studies</i> finds that the intervention in Sierra Leone was a success.<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">After President Kabbah was informed of the true strength of the SLA, he reduced its rice rations (each soldier received a monthly allowance of rice, commensurate with rank, in addition to their salary). The senior officers reduced the allowance of the junior ranks rather than their own, which led to a revolt in the junior ranks. The soldiers who revolted formed the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Revolutionary_Council" title="Armed Forces Revolutionary Council">Armed Forces Revolutionary Council</a> (AFRC), which went on to overthrow the government.<sup id="cite_ref-Penfold_15_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfold_15-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-42"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-42">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"Entitled personnel", sometimes referred to as "entitled persons", are defined by the British government as: British nationals (including dual nationals); nationals of the Commonwealth, European Union, and United States as part of multi-national agreements; and other nationalities dependant on space available and guarantees from their governments to reimburse the British government for the cost of their evacuation.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-47"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-47">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">COBRA is a British government committee convened to handle national crises. The committee is named after the room in which it meets\xe2\x80\x94the Cabinet Office Briefing Room\xe2\x80\x94and usually known as "COBRA" or "Cobra" or sometimes "COBR". Similarly to the SAS, COBRA first became known to the public during the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Embassy_siege" title="Iranian Embassy siege">Iranian Embassy siege</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-49"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-49">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The ARG is an amphibious force based around a Royal Marine commando (similar in size to an infantry battalion) with supporting assets.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-55"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-55">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The RAF later acquired six <a href="/wiki/C-17_Globemaster" class="mw-redirect" title="C-17 Globemaster">C-17 Globemasters</a> for large airlifts.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_74_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_74-54">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-104"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-104">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Special Court for Sierra Leone was modelled on the <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">International Criminal Court</a> (ICC); the latter could not be used in Sierra Leone as many of the alleged war crimes occurred before the establishment of the ICC.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-119"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-119">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The UK did contribute logistics personnel to the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> mission in Darfur.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorman_129_117-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorman_129-117">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup></span>\n</li>\n</ol></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bibliography">Bibliography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_military_intervention_in_the_Sierra_Leone_Civil_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Bibliography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><cite class="citation journal">Abrahamsen, Rita;
