href="/wiki/Middle_power" title="Middle power">Middle power</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Small_power" title="Small power">Small power</a></div>\n<p><b>Power in international relations</b> is defined in several different ways. Modern discourse generally speaks in terms of state power, indicating both economic and military power. Those states that have significant amounts of power within the international system are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Small_power" title="Small power">small powers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_power" title="Middle power">middle powers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regional_power" title="Regional power">regional powers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great powers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Superpower" title="Superpower">superpowers</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hegemons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hegemons">hegemons</a>, although there is no commonly accepted standard for what defines a powerful state. <a href="/wiki/NATO_Quint" title="NATO Quint">NATO Quint</a>, the <a href="/wiki/G7" class="mw-redirect" title="G7">G7</a>, the <a href="/wiki/BRICS" title="BRICS">BRICS</a> nations and the <a href="/wiki/G20" title="G20">G20</a> are seen by academics as forms of governments that exercise varying degrees of influence within the international system.\n</p><p>Entities other than states can also be relevant in power acquisition in international relations. Such entities can include <a href="/wiki/Multilateralism" title="Multilateralism">multilateral</a> <a href="/wiki/International_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="International organizations">international organizations</a>, military alliance organizations like <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporations</a> like <a href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" class="mw-redirect" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-governmental organizations">non-governmental organizations</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, or other institutions such as the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> and technology companies like <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concepts of political power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">Political scientists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a>, and practitioners of <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a> (<a href="/wiki/Diplomat" title="Diplomat">diplomats</a>) have used the following concepts of <a href="/wiki/Political_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Political power">political power</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Power as a <b>goal</b> of states or leaders;</li>\n<li>Power as a measure of <b><a href="/wiki/Social_influence" title="Social influence">influence</a></b> or control over outcomes, events, actors and issues;</li>\n<li>Power as victory in conflict and the attainment of <b><a href="/wiki/International_security" title="International security">security</a></b>;</li>\n<li>Power as control over resources and <b>capabilities</b>;</li>\n<li>Power as <b>status</b>, which some states or actors possess and others do not.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_as_a_goal">Power as a goal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Power as a goal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Primary usage of "power" as a goal in international relations belongs to political theorists, such as <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccol\xc3\xb2 Machiavelli">Niccol\xc3\xb2 Machiavelli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Morgenthau" title="Hans Morgenthau">Hans Morgenthau</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Especially among Classical <a href="/wiki/Realism_in_international_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Realism in international relations">Realist</a> thinkers, power is an inherent goal of mankind and of states.  Economic growth, military growth, cultural spread etc. can all be considered as working towards the ultimate goal of international power.  The German military thinker <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Clausewitz" title="Carl von Clausewitz">Carl von Clausewitz</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is considered to be the quintessential projection of European growth across the continent.  In more modern times, <a href="/wiki/Baron_Moser" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron Moser">Claus Moser</a> has elucidated theories centre of distribution of power in Europe after the Holocaust, and the power of universal learning as its counterpoint.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jean_Monnet" title="Jean Monnet">Jean Monnet</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> was a French left-wing social theorist, stimulating expansive Eurocommunism, who followed on the creator of modern European community, the diplomat and statesman <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schuman" title="Robert Schuman">Robert Schuman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_as_influence">Power as influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Power as influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_(orthographic_projection).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg/170px-North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg.png" width="170" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg/255px-North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg/340px-North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="550" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_(orthographic_projection).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> accounts for over 70% of <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditures" title="List of countries by military expenditures">global military expenditure</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-sipri1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sipri1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> alone accounting for 43% of global military expenditure.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">Political scientists</a> principally use "power" in terms of an actor\'s ability to exercise <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence" title="Sphere of influence">influence</a> over other actors within the <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international system</a>. This influence can be <a href="/wiki/Coercive" class="mw-redirect" title="Coercive">coercive</a>, attractive, <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Competitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Competitive">competitive</a>. Mechanisms of influence can include the threat or use of force, economic interaction or pressure, diplomacy, and cultural exchange.\n</p><p>Under certain circumstances, states can organize a <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence" title="Sphere of influence">sphere of influence</a> or a bloc within which they exercise predominant influence. Historical examples include the spheres of influence recognized under the <a href="/wiki/Concert_of_Europe" title="Concert of Europe">Concert of Europe</a>, or the recognition of spheres during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta Conference</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a>, the "<a href="/wiki/Free_World" title="Free World">Free World</a>", and the <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">Non-Aligned Movement</a> were the blocs that arose out of the Cold War contest. Military alliances like <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> and the Warsaw Pact are another forum through which influence is exercised. However, "<a href="/wiki/Political_realism" class="mw-redirect" title="Political realism">realist</a>" theory attempted to maintain the <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_(international_relations)" title="Balance of power (international relations)">balance of power</a> from the development of meaningful diplomatic relations that can create a <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> within the region. <a href="/wiki/British_foreign_policy" class="mw-redirect" title="British foreign policy">British foreign policy</a>, for example, dominated Europe through the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> after the defeat of France.  They continued the balancing act with the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Berlin" title="Congress of Berlin">Congress of Berlin</a> in 1878, to appease Russia and Germany from attacking Turkey.  Britain has sided against the aggressors on the European continent\xe2\x80\x94i.e. the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleonic France">Napoleonic France</a> or <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg Austria">Habsburg Austria</a>, known during the Great War as the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a> and, in the World War Two were called the <a href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" class="mw-redirect" title="Axis Powers">Axis Powers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_as_security">Power as security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Power as security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Power</i> is also used when describing states or actors that have achieved <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> victories or security for their state in the international system. This general usage is most commonly found among the writings of historians or popular writers.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_as_capability">Power as capability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Power as capability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>American author <a href="/wiki/Charles_W._Freeman,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Charles W. Freeman, Jr.">Charles W. Freeman, Jr.</a> described power as the following:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Power is the capacity to direct the decisions and actions of others. Power derives from strength and will. Strength comes from the transformation of resources into capabilities. Will infuses objectives with resolve. Strategy marshals capabilities and brings them to bear with precision. Statecraft seeks through strategy to magnify the mass, relevance, impact, and irresistibility of power. It guides the ways the state deploys and applies its power abroad. These ways embrace the arts of war, espionage, and diplomacy. The practitioners of these three arts are the paladins of statecraft.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p><i>Power</i> is also used to describe the <a href="/wiki/Resource_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resource (economics)">resources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capability_(systems_engineering)" title="Capability (systems engineering)">capabilities</a> of a state. This definition is quantitative and is most often<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (February 2017)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Power_(international_relations)#&#39;&#39;Power_as_resources_most_often_used_by_geopoliticians_and_the_military&#39;&#39;" title="Talk:Power (international relations)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> used by geopoliticians and the military. Capabilities are thought of in tangible terms\xe2\x80\x94they are measurable, weighable, quantifiable assets. A good example for this kind of measurement is the <i>Composite Indicator on Aggregate Power</i>, which involves 54 indicators and covers the capabilities of 44 states in <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a> from 1992 to 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Fels2017a_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fels2017a-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Hard power can be treated as a potential and is not often enforced on the international stage.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">Chinese</a> strategists have such a concept of national power that can be measured <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_property" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantitative property">quantitatively</a> using an index known as <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_national_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Comprehensive national power">comprehensive national power</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_as_status">Power as status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Power as status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Much effort in academic and popular writing is devoted to deciding which countries have the status of "power", and how this can be measured. If a country has "power" (as influence) in military, diplomatic, cultural, and economic spheres, it might be called a "power" (as status). There are several categories of power, and inclusion of a state in one category or another is fraught with difficulty and controversy. In his famous 1987 work, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rise_and_Fall_of_the_Great_Powers" title="The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers">The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers</a>,</i> British-American historian <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kennedy" title="Paul Kennedy">Paul Kennedy</a> charts the relative status of the various powers from AD 1500 to 2000. He does not begin the book with a <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_definition" title="Theoretical definition">theoretical definition</a> of a "great power"; however he does list them, separately, for many different eras. Moreover, he uses different <a href="/wiki/Working_definitions" class="mw-redirect" title="Working definitions">working definitions</a> of a great power for different eras. For example:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"France was not strong enough to oppose Germany in a one-to-one struggle... If the mark of a Great Power is country which is willing to take on any other, then France (like Austria-Hungary) had slipped to a lower position. But that definition seemed too abstract in 1914 to a nation geared up for war, militarily stronger than ever, wealthy, and, above all, endowed with powerful allies."<sup id="cite_ref-Kennedy_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories_of_power">Categories of power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Categories of power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the modern geopolitical landscape, a number of terms are used to describe various types of powers, which include the following:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Superpower" title="Superpower">Superpower</a></b></i>: In 1944, <a href="/wiki/William_T._R._Fox" title="William T. R. Fox">William T. R. Fox</a> defined <i>superpower</i> as "great power plus great mobility of power" and identified three states, the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> With the decolonisation of the British Empire following World War II, and then the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a> in 1991, the United States is currently the only country considered to be a superpower.<sup id="cite_ref-Nossal_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nossal-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">Great power</a></b></i>: In historical mentions, the term <i>great power</i> refers to the states that have strong political, cultural and economical influence over nations around them and across the world.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Middle_power" title="Middle power">Middle power</a></b></i>: A subjective description of influential second-tier states that could not quite be described as great or small powers. A middle power has sufficient strength and authority to stand on its own without the need of help from others (particularly in the realm of <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">security</a>) and takes diplomatic leads in regional and global affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Fels2017_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fels2017-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Clearly not all middle powers are of equal status; some are members of forums such as the <a href="/wiki/G20" title="G20">G20</a> and play important roles in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and other international organisations such as the <a href="/wiki/WTO" class="mw-redirect" title="WTO">WTO</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rudd_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rudd-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Small_power" title="Small power">Small power</a></b></i>: The International System is for the most part made up by <i>small powers</i>. They are instruments of the other powers and may at times be dominated; but they cannot be ignored.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_categories">Other categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Regional_power" title="Regional power">Regional power</a></b></i>: This term is used to describe a nation that exercises influence and power within a region. Being a <i>regional power</i> is not mutually exclusive with any of the other categories of power. The majority of them exert a strategic degree of influence as minor or secondary regional powers. A primary regional power (like <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>) has often an important role in international affairs outside of its region too.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i><b>Cultural superpower</b></i>: Refers to a country whose <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Arts">arts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a> have worldwide appeal, significant international popularity or large influence on much of the world. Countries such as <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> have often been described as cultural superpowers, although it is sometimes debated on which one meets such criteria. Unlike traditional forms of national power, the term cultural superpower is in reference to a nation\'s <a href="/wiki/Soft_power" title="Soft power">Soft power</a> capabilities.</li>\n<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Energy_superpower" title="Energy superpower">Energy superpower</a></b></i>: Describes a country that supplies large amounts of energy <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">resources</a> (<a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, etc.) to a significant number of other states, and therefore has the potential to influence world markets to gain a political or economic advantage. <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, are generally acknowledged as the world\'s current energy superpowers, given their abilities to globally influence or even directly control prices to certain countries. <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> are potential energy superpowers due to their large natural resources.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span id="Hard.2C_soft_and_smart_power"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard,_soft_and_smart_power">Hard, soft and smart power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hard, soft and smart power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Hard_power" title="Hard power">Hard power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soft_power" title="Soft power">Soft power</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smart_power" title="Smart power">Smart power</a></div>\n<p>Some political scientists distinguish between two types of power: Hard and Soft. The former is coercive (example: <a href="/wiki/Military_invasion" class="mw-redirect" title="Military invasion">military invasion</a>) while the latter is attractive (example: <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_media" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadcast media">broadcast media</a> or cultural invasion).\n</p><p>Hard power refers to coercive tactics: the threat or use of <a href="/wiki/Armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed forces">armed forces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic">economic</a> pressure or <a href="/wiki/International_sanctions" title="International sanctions">sanctions</a>, assassination and subterfuge, or other forms of intimidation. Hard power is generally associated to the stronger of nations, as the ability to change the domestic affairs of other nations through military threats. Realists and <a href="/wiki/Neorealism_(international_relations)" title="Neorealism (international relations)">neorealists</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/John_Mearsheimer" title="John Mearsheimer">John Mearsheimer</a>, are advocates of the use of such power for the balancing of the international system.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Nye" title="Joseph Nye">Joseph Nye</a> is the leading proponent and theorist of soft power. Instruments of soft power include debates on cultural values, dialogues on ideology, the attempt to influence through good example, and the appeal to commonly accepted human values. Means of exercising soft power include diplomacy, dissemination of information, analysis, propaganda, and cultural programming to achieve political ends.\n</p><p>Others have synthesized soft and hard power, including through the field of <a href="/wiki/Smart_power" title="Smart power">smart power</a>. This is often a call to use a holistic spectrum of statecraft tools, ranging from soft to hard.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_powers_of_the_modern_age">European powers of the modern age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: European powers of the modern age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the time of the Renaissance, powers in Europe included <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">France</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg Empire">Habsburg Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish\xe2\x80\x93Lithuanian Commonwealth">Poland\xe2\x80\x93Lithuania</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. Bolstered by shipments of gold and silver from the Americas, the Spanish Habsburg dynasty emerged as a dominant force and regularly launched military interventions to project its power and defend Catholicism, while its rival, France, was torn apart by religious civil war. Meanwhile,\nin Eastern Europe, the Ottoman Empire reached its zenith and completed its conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Balkan peninsula">Balkan</a> region.\n</p><p>During the 17th century the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Sweden</a> were added to the group, whilst the Ottomans, Poland and Spain gradually declined in power and influence. France progressively grew stronger and by the latter part of the century found itself repeatedly facing alliances designed to hold its military power in check.\n</p><p>In the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a> (formed from a union of England and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a>) progressively gained strength and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a> also saw their importance increase, while Sweden and the Dutch Republic declined. Great Britain and France increasingly struggled for dominance both on the continent and abroad (notably in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>). By the century\'s end, the British had established themselves as the foremost naval power while the French were dominant on land, conquering many of their neighbors during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a> and establishing client republics. The struggle between the two nations ended only in 1815 with the final defeat of the French under <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>.\n</p><p>During the 19th century, there was an informal convention recognising five Great Powers in Europe: the <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> (later the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>). In the late 19th century the newly united <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> was added to this group.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_in_international_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Balance of power in international relations">Balance of power in international relations</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/International_relations_of_the_Great_Powers_(1814%E2%80%931919)" title="International relations of the Great Powers (1814\xe2\x80\x931919)">International relations of the Great Powers (1814\xe2\x80\x931919)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lateral_pressure_theory" title="Lateral pressure theory">Lateral pressure theory</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/National_power" title="National power">National power</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Peace_through_strength" title="Peace through strength">Peace through strength</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">Power (social and political)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Power_transition_theory" title="Power transition theory">Power transition theory</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_(international_relations)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
