MCC teams held <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket#Important_matches_classification" title="First-class cricket">important match status</a> from 1787 to 1894;<sup id="cite_ref-ACS1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACS1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ACS2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACS2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class status</a> from 1895.<sup id="cite_ref-ACS2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACS2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> To mark the beginning of each English season, MCC plays the reigning <a href="/wiki/County_Championship" title="County Championship">County Champions</a>.\n</p><p>In 1788, the MCC took responsibility for the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Cricket" title="Laws of Cricket">Laws of Cricket</a>, issuing a revised version that year. Although changes to the Laws are now determined by the <a href="/wiki/International_Cricket_Council" title="International Cricket Council">International Cricket Council</a> (ICC), the copyright is still owned by MCC.<sup id="cite_ref-Laws_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laws-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The club was formerly the governing body of cricket in England and Wales and, as the sport\'s legislator, held considerable global influence.\n</p><p>For much of the 20th century, commencing with the <a href="/wiki/English_cricket_team_in_Australia_in_1903%E2%80%9304" title="English cricket team in Australia in 1903\xe2\x80\x9304">1903\xe2\x80\x9304 tour of Australia</a> and ending with the <a href="/wiki/English_cricket_team_in_India_and_Sri_Lanka_in_1976%E2%80%9377" title="English cricket team in India and Sri Lanka in 1976\xe2\x80\x9377">1976\xe2\x80\x9377 tour of India</a>, MCC organised international tours in which the <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England cricket team</a> played <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test matches</a>. On these tours, the England team was called MCC in non-international matches. In 1993, its administrative and governance functions were transferred to the ICC and the <a href="/wiki/Test_and_County_Cricket_Board" title="Test and County Cricket Board">Test and County Cricket Board</a> (TCCB).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The origin of MCC was as a gentlemen\'s club that had flourished through most of the 18th century, including, at least in part, an existence as the original <a href="/wiki/London_Cricket_Club" title="London Cricket Club">London Cricket Club</a>, which had played at the <a href="/wiki/Artillery_Ground" title="Artillery Ground">Artillery Ground</a> through the middle years of the century. Many of its members became involved with the <a href="/wiki/Hambledon_Club" title="Hambledon Club">Hambledon Club</a> through the 1770s and then, in the early 1780s, had returned to the London area where the <a href="/wiki/White_Conduit_Club" title="White Conduit Club">White Conduit Club</a> had begun in <a href="/wiki/Islington" title="Islington">Islington</a>. It is not known for certain when the White Conduit was founded but it seems to have been after 1780 and certainly by 1785. According to <a href="/wiki/Pelham_Warner" title="Pelham Warner">Pelham Warner</a>, it was formed in 1782 as an offshoot from a <a href="/wiki/West_End_of_London" title="West End of London">West End</a> convivial club called the <i>Je-ne-sais-quoi</i>, some of whose members frequented the White Conduit House in <a href="/wiki/Islington" title="Islington">Islington</a> and played matches on the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/White_Conduit_Fields" title="White Conduit Fields">White Conduit Fields</a>, which had been a prominent venue for cricket in the 1720s.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Haygarth" title="Arthur Haygarth">Arthur Haygarth</a> said in <i>Scores and Biographies</i> that "the Marylebone Club was founded in 1787 from the White Conduit\'s members" but the date of the formation of the White Conduit "could not be found".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This gentlemen\'s club, which was multi-purpose, had a social meeting place at the <i>Star and Garter</i> on <a href="/wiki/Pall_Mall,_London" title="Pall Mall, London">Pall Mall</a>. It was the same club that was responsible for drafting the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Cricket" title="Laws of Cricket">Laws of Cricket</a> at various times, most notably in 1744 and 1774, and this lawgiving responsibility was soon to be vested in the MCC as the final repose of these cricketing gentlemen. When the White Conduit began, its leading lights were <a href="/wiki/George_Finch,_9th_Earl_of_Winchilsea" title="George Finch, 9th Earl of Winchilsea">George Finch, 9th Earl of Winchilsea</a> (1752\xe2\x80\x931826) and the Hon. Colonel Charles Lennox (1764\xe2\x80\x931819), who later became the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lennox,_4th_Duke_of_Richmond" title="Charles Lennox, 4th Duke of Richmond">4th Duke of Richmond</a>. White Conduit was nominally an exclusive club that only "gentlemen" might play for, but the club did employ professionals and one of these was the bowler <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lord" title="Thomas Lord">Thomas Lord</a>, a man who was recognised for his business acumen (he became a successful wine and provisions merchant) as well as his bowling ability.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DB47_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB47-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The new club might have continued except that White Conduit Fields was an open area allowing members of the public, including the rowdier elements, to watch the matches and to voice their opinions on the play and the players. The White Conduit gentlemen were not amused by such interruptions and decided to look for a more private venue of their own.<sup id="cite_ref-DB47_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB47-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Winchilsea and Lennox asked Lord to find a new ground and offered him a guarantee against any losses he may suffer in the venture.<sup id="cite_ref-HSA51_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSA51-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DB47_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB47-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Lord took a lease from the <a href="/wiki/Portman_Estate" title="Portman Estate">Portman Estate</a> on some land at Dorset Fields where <a href="/wiki/Dorset_Square" title="Dorset Square">Dorset Square</a> is now sited; and <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Old_Ground" title="Lord&#39;s Old Ground">the ground</a> was prepared and opened in 1787. It was initially called the New Cricket Ground, perhaps because it was off what was then called "the New Road" in <a href="/wiki/Marylebone" title="Marylebone">Marylebone</a>, when the first known match was played there on 21 May but, by the end of July, it was known as Lord\'s.<sup id="cite_ref-GB110_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB110-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As it was in Marylebone, the White Conduit members who relocated to it soon decided to call themselves the "Mary-le-bone Club".<sup id="cite_ref-GB115_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB115-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The exact date of MCC\'s foundation is lost but seems to have been sometime in the late spring or the summer of 1787.<sup id="cite_ref-PW18_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW18-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On 10 &amp; 11 July 1837, a <a href="/wiki/North_v_South" title="North v South">South v North</a> match was staged at <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s" title="Lord&#39;s">Lord\'s</a> to commemorate the MCC\'s Golden Jubilee. Warner described it as "a Grand Match to celebrate the Jubilee of the Club" and reproduced the full scorecard.<sup id="cite_ref-PW18_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW18-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On Wednesday, 25 April 1787, the London <i>Morning Herald</i> newspaper carried a notice: "The Members of the Cricket Club are desired to meet at the <i>Star and Garter</i>, Pall Mall, on Mon., April 30. Dinner on table exactly at half past five o\'clock. N.B. The favour of an answer is desired".<sup id="cite_ref-GB110_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB110-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The agenda is unknown but, only three weeks later on Saturday, 19 May, the <i>Morning Herald</i> advertised: "A grand match will be played on Monday, 21 May in the New Cricket Ground, the New Road, Mary-le-bone, between eleven Noblemen of the White Conduit Club and eleven Gentlemen of the County of Middlesex with two men given, for 500 guineas a side. The wickets to be pitched at ten o\'clock, and the match to be played out". No post-match report has been found but, as <a href="/wiki/G._B._Buckley" title="G. B. Buckley">G. B. Buckley</a> said, it was "apparently the first match to be played on Lord\'s new ground".<sup id="cite_ref-GB110_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB110-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A total of eight matches are known to have been played at Lord\'s in 1787, one of them a <a href="/wiki/Single_wicket_cricket" title="Single wicket cricket">single wicket</a> event. The only one which featured the Mary-le-bone Club took place on Monday, 30 July. It was advertised in <i>The World</i> on Friday, 27 July 1787: "On Monday, 30 July will be played (at Lord\'s) a match between 11 gentlemen of the Mary-le-bone Club and 11 gentlemen of the Islington Club".<sup id="cite_ref-GB115_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB115-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Buckley stated that "this is the earliest notice of the Marylebone Club".<sup id="cite_ref-GB115_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB115-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As with the inaugural match at Lord\'s, no post-match report of the inaugural MCC match has been found.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grounds">Grounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dorset_square_plaque.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Dorset_square_plaque.jpg/200px-Dorset_square_plaque.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Dorset_square_plaque.jpg/300px-Dorset_square_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Dorset_square_plaque.jpg/400px-Dorset_square_plaque.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dorset_square_plaque.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plaque in Dorset Square marks the site of the original Lord\'s Ground and commemorates the founding of the MCC</div></div></div>\n<p>There have been three Lord\'s grounds: the original on the Portman Estate and two on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyre_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eyre Estate (page does not exist)">Eyre Estate</a>. All three sites lie to the west of <a href="/wiki/Regent%27s_Park" title="Regent&#39;s Park">Regent\'s Park</a>. Thomas Lord leased the original ground, now referred to as <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Old_Ground" title="Lord&#39;s Old Ground">Lord\'s Old Ground</a>, from the Portman Estate in 1787 and MCC played there until 1810 when Lord, after objecting to a rent increase, decided on termination of the lease to lift his turf and move out.<sup id="cite_ref-PW18_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW18-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Over 200 matches are known to have been played there, mostly involving MCC and/or <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_county_cricket_teams" title="Middlesex county cricket teams">Middlesex</a>. The Old Ground was on the site now occupied by <a href="/wiki/Dorset_Square" title="Dorset Square">Dorset Square</a> which is east of <a href="/wiki/Marylebone_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Marylebone Station">Marylebone Station</a> and west of <a href="/wiki/Baker_Street" title="Baker Street">Baker Street</a>. To commemorate the association, a plaque was unveiled in Dorset Square on 9 May 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Strauss" title="Andrew Strauss">Andrew Strauss</a>.\n</p><p>Lord had been aware some years before 1810 that the Portman Estate intended to let the site on building leases which would command the much higher rent of over \xc2\xa3600 per annum.<sup id="cite_ref-PW18_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW18-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On 15 October 1808, he rented two fields in the North Bank area of the St John\'s Wood Estate, which belonged to Richard Eyre, a local landowner after whom <a href="/wiki/Eyre%27s_Tunnel" title="Eyre&#39;s Tunnel">Eyre\'s Tunnel</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Regent%27s_Canal" title="Regent&#39;s Canal">Regent\'s Canal</a> was named. Rental on the Eyre site was only \xc2\xa354 per annum for a term of eighty years and free of both land tax and tithe.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The new ground was ready for use in 1809 and so Lord had two grounds at his disposal for the 1809 and 1810 seasons. The North Bank ground was sub-let to <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_Wood_Cricket_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="St John&#39;s Wood Cricket Club">St John\'s Wood Cricket Club</a>, which eventually merged with MCC.<sup id="cite_ref-PW19_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW19-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Lord officially took over his second ground on 8 May 1811 by literally re-laying there his turf from the Old Ground. He did this so that "the noblemen and gentlemen of the MCC should be able to play on the same footing as before".<sup id="cite_ref-PW19_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW19-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> According to Warner, however, the relocation was unpopular with many MCC members and, as a result, the club played no matches there in either 1811 or 1812.<sup id="cite_ref-PW19_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW19-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This may have been so but cricket generally was in decline at the time because of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Cricket_Statisticians_and_Historians" class="mw-redirect" title="Association of Cricket Statisticians and Historians">Association of Cricket Statisticians and Historians</a> (the ACS) holds that "(from) 1810 to 1814 the game was all but dead", largely because of the war and "the very real threat of civil unrest in England".<sup id="cite_ref-ACS4_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACS4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The second venue is now generally known as <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Middle_Ground" title="Lord&#39;s Middle Ground">Lord\'s Middle Ground</a>. In the three years that Lord controlled it, only six matches are known to have taken place there and just three of these (all in 1813) involved MCC. The Middle Ground\'s exact location is uncertain but it is understood to have been in North Bank at the north end of <a href="/wiki/Lisson_Grove" title="Lisson Grove">Lisson Grove</a> and that the Regent\'s Canal has been cut through it. This means that it was partially on the canal route and somewhere in the area now bounded by Lisson Grove (the B507) to south-west, Lodge Road to north-west, Park Road (<a href="/wiki/A41_road" title="A41 road">the A41</a>) to north-east and the Regent\'s Canal to south-east. It was less than 300 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metres</a> from the site of the modern <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s" title="Lord&#39;s">Lord\'s</a> ground.<sup id="cite_ref-PW19_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW19-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lord was forced to abandon the Middle Ground because of the canal construction. The decision on the route was made by Parliament in 1813. Lord approached the Eyre family who were willing to lease him another plot nearby in <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_Wood" title="St John&#39;s Wood">St John\'s Wood</a>, but at an increased rent of \xc2\xa3100 per annum. Lord accepted and again removed and relaid his turf in time for the start of the 1814 season. This third ground was the present <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s" title="Lord&#39;s">Lord\'s</a>, now home to MCC for over 200 years.<sup id="cite_ref-PW19_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PW19-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laws_of_Cricket"><i>Laws of Cricket</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Laws of Cricket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Cricket" title="Laws of Cricket">Laws of Cricket</a></div>\n<p>MCC is the custodian of, and remains the copyright holder of, the <i><a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Cricket" title="Laws of Cricket">Laws of Cricket</a></i>. Its Laws sub-committee is responsible for debating and drafting changes to the Laws, with the Main Committee then voting on any changes proposed.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MCC_member.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/MCC_member.jpg/175px-MCC_member.jpg" width="175" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/MCC_member.jpg/263px-MCC_member.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/MCC_member.jpg/350px-MCC_member.jpg 2x" data-file-width="763" data-file-height="864" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MCC_member.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>MCC member in distinctive MCC colours</div></div></div>\n<p>MCC has 18,000 full members and 5,000 associate members. Members have voting rights and can use the <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Pavilion" title="Lord&#39;s Pavilion">Pavilion</a> and other stands at Lord\'s to attend all matches played at the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-HTJ_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HTJ-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In order to join the waiting list of candidates for membership one must obtain the vote (of which each full member has one a year) of three members, and the additional sponsorship of a person on the List of MCC Sponsors (which consists of members of all MCC Sub-Committees; MCC Committee; MCC Out-Match Representatives; and the Current, Past, and Designate President). As the demand for membership always outstrips supply each year, there continues to be a substantial waiting list for Full Ordinary Membership, currently around 27 years.<sup id="cite_ref-HTJ_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HTJ-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There are, however, ways to lessen the time it takes to become a full member: one may qualify as a Playing Member, or Out-Match Member (although this carries none of the privileges of membership, apart from being able to play for the club).<sup id="cite_ref-HTJ_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HTJ-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In addition, membership rules allow a certain number of people each year to be elected ahead of their turn; beneficiaries have included Sir <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">Mick Jagger</a> and, notably, in 2018 prime minister <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a>. MCC also grants limited honorary membership to people who have had distinguished cricket careers. The club was a male-only bastion until the 1990s but it now recognises achievement in <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_cricket" title="Women&#39;s cricket">women\'s cricket</a> with, for example, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Edwards" title="Charlotte Edwards">Charlotte Edwards</a> an inductee in the 2010s.<sup id="cite_ref-HTJ_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HTJ-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The club\'s members persistently refused to allow female membership well into the 1990s, with club ballots on the change unable to attract the two-thirds majority amongst the membership required for implementation.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The move to change was spearheaded by <a href="/wiki/Rachael_Heyhoe_Flint,_Baroness_Heyhoe_Flint" title="Rachael Heyhoe Flint, Baroness Heyhoe Flint">Rachael Heyhoe Flint</a> who applied as "R Flint" to slip into the male-only application system. A 70% majority of members eventually voted to allow female membership in September 1998, so ending 212 years of male exclusivity, and 10 honorary life members were immediately admitted, including Heyhoe Flint. Up until this time, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">the Queen</a>, who is the club\'s patron, was the only woman (other than domestic staff) permitted to enter the Pavilion during play.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In February 1999, five women were invited to join as playing members.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Further controversy occurred in 2005 when the club was criticised (including by a few of its own members)<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> for siding with the <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales_Cricket_Board" title="England and Wales Cricket Board">England and Wales Cricket Board</a> (ECB) over the latter\'s decision to award television rights for <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test cricket</a> to <a href="/wiki/British_Sky_Broadcasting" class="mw-redirect" title="British Sky Broadcasting">British Sky Broadcasting</a>, thus removing Test cricket from terrestrial television. The Secretary and Chief Executive of MCC at the time, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Knight" title="Roger Knight">Roger Knight</a>, represented the club on the board of the ECB and was party to this controversial and much criticised decision,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> prior to which Test cricket had been shown free to viewers on British television for more than half a century.\n</p><p>Another controversy was MCC\'s decision to allow members and other spectators to continue to bring limited amounts of alcoholic drinks into the ground at all matches. This decision challenged the <a href="/wiki/International_Cricket_Council" title="International Cricket Council">ICC</a>, which was attempting to implement a ban on this practice at all international matches around the world. MCC has opted to write to the ICC on an annual basis to seek permission for members and spectators to import alcohol into <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s" title="Lord&#39;s">Lord\'s</a>. No other Ground Authority has thought it necessary to seek permission from the ICC for their members and spectators to import alcohol into their cricket ground, there being money to be made out of selling alcohol themselves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Given its heritage, MCC continues to participate in the administration of English cricket, and in 2010 offered Lord\'s as a neutral venue for Pakistan to stage a "home" <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test match</a>, as scheduled by the ICC, versus Australia; the club\'s initial offering was made with the intention that Pakistan, whose terrorist-stricken country had rendered it a no-go area for international cricket, could remain within the international cricketing fold.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The Secretary &amp; Chief Executive of the club has a place on the administrative board of the England and Wales Cricket Board and it is reported that <a href="/wiki/Keith_Bradshaw_(cricketer)" title="Keith Bradshaw (cricketer)">Keith Bradshaw</a> (the outgoing Secretary &amp; Chief Executive) may have been influential in the removal from office of England Coach <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Fletcher" title="Duncan Fletcher">Duncan Fletcher</a> in April 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2012, MCC made headlines over a controversial redevelopment plan, <i>Vision for Lord\'s</i>, that would have increased capacity but included construction of residential flats on some of the MCC site. Internal strife over the process of making a decision on the proposal led to the resignation of former prime minister <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a> from the main committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Tel_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tel-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coaching">Coaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Coaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MCC_Coaching_book.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/MCC_Coaching_book.jpg/125px-MCC_Coaching_book.jpg" width="125" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/MCC_Coaching_book.jpg/188px-MCC_Coaching_book.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/MCC_Coaching_book.jpg/250px-MCC_Coaching_book.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1206" data-file-height="2315" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MCC_Coaching_book.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/MCC_coaching_manual" class="mw-redirect" title="MCC coaching manual">MCC coaching manuals</a></div></div></div>\n<p>MCC has long had a deep involvement in coaching the game of cricket. The club\'s head coach leads an extensive operation involving the running of an indoor-cricket school and a team of coaches in England and around the world. The club has traditionally produced a coaching manual, the <i><a href="/wiki/MCC_Cricket_Coaching_Book" class="mw-redirect" title="MCC Cricket Coaching Book">MCC Cricket Coaching Book</a></i>, a bible for cricket skills, and runs training programmes for young cricketers, including many at its Lord\'s Indoor Centre.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> MCC continues to tour around England, playing matches against various state and private schools. This tradition has been followed since the 19th century. The club has other sporting interests with both a <a href="/wiki/Real_tennis" title="Real tennis">real tennis</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Squash_(sport)" title="Squash (sport)">squash</a> court on site at Lord\'s, and <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(card_game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridge (card game)">bridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Backgammon" title="Backgammon">backgammon</a> societies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_colours">Club colours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Club colours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From the beginning of the 20th century, MCC organised the <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England cricket team</a> and, outside <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test matches</a>, the touring England team officially played as "MCC" up to and including the 1976/77 tour of India. The <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England touring team</a> wore the distinctive red and yellow stripes of the Marylebone Cricket Club as their colours for the last time on the <a href="/wiki/English_cricket_team_in_New_Zealand_in_1996%E2%80%9397" title="English cricket team in New Zealand in 1996\xe2\x80\x9397">tour to New Zealand in 1996/97</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The true provenance of MCC\'s colours is (and probably will remain) unknown, but its players often turned out sporting sky blue until well into the 19th century. The club eventually settled on the now well-recognised colours of scarlet and gold.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> One theory is that MCC adopted these colours from <a href="/wiki/J%26W_Nicholson_%26_Co" title="J&amp;W Nicholson &amp; Co">J&amp;W Nicholson &amp; Co</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Gin" title="Gin">gin</a> after the company\'s chairman, MCC benefactor <a href="/wiki/William_Nicholson_(distiller)" title="William Nicholson (distiller)">William Nicholson</a> (1825\xe2\x80\x931909), secured the club\'s position at Lord\'s with a loan.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Another theory, which chimes with the club\'s origins, is that MCC borrowed its colours from the livery colours (racing) of a founding patron, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lennox,_4th_Duke_of_Richmond" title="Charles Lennox, 4th Duke of Richmond">Charles Lennox, 4th Duke of Richmond</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Goodwood_House" title="Goodwood House">Goodwood</a> fame.<sup id="cite_ref-Lads_to_Lord&#39;s_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lads_to_Lord&#39;s-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Image">Image</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Image">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Often viewed as overly staid and pontifical (i.e. "<a href="/wiki/The_Establishment" title="The Establishment">Establishment</a>"), the club has of late improved its image in the eyes of the public and media, partly because it remains a citadel for tradition in a fast-changing landscape and partly because it has made a concerted move towards image-improvement. "It would be overstating things to claim that the MCC has come full circle," admitted Andrew Miller at the beginning of October 2008, "but at a time of massive upheaval in the world game, the... colours of NW8 have ceased to represent everything that is wrong with cricket, and instead have become a touchstone for those whose greatest fear is the erosion of the game\'s traditional values."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Until 2013 the MCC was a private members club (and this meant that it had the status of an <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_associations_in_English_law" title="Unincorporated associations in English law">unincorporated association</a>). This status had several limitations. Since an unincorporated association is not a legal entity, it could not own property (such as Lord\'s Cricket Ground itself) in its own name. It could not sue anybody, or indeed be sued (any legal action had to be taken against the Secretary &amp; Chief Executive personally). In the event that a claim was successful, the committee and even the members themselves would have had to fund any financial shortfall. The club therefore called a Special General Meeting in June 2012 to consider petitioning the Queen in Council to incorporate the club by <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">Royal Charter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The charter would remove many of the barriers and simplify the administration of the club.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Resulting from the petition, in December 2012 the club was granted a Royal charter, two previous attempts having been unsuccessful. As a result, the club became an incorporated association and is now able to hold assets, including the Lord\'s ground, in its own name instead of via a custodian trustee. It also meant that the individual members, as the club\'s owners, no longer have a potential liability should the club ever get into serious financial trouble.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MCC_Universities">MCC Universities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: MCC Universities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since 2005 the MCC has funded six university cricket academies known as the MCC Universities (MCCUs). (Prior to 2010 they were known as the University Centres of Cricketing Excellence (UCCEs).) They are based at <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Cricket_Club" title="Cambridge University Cricket Club">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_MCC_University" title="Cardiff MCC University">Cardiff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durham_MCC_University" title="Durham MCC University">Durham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds/Bradford_MCC_University" title="Leeds/Bradford MCC University">Leeds/Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loughborough_MCC_University" title="Loughborough MCC University">Loughborough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Cricket_Club" title="Oxford University Cricket Club">Oxford</a>, and incorporate a total of thirteen educational establishments.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> As of  2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, all six MCCUs have been granted first-class status by the ECB. Each MCCU plays a trio of matches against professional county sides at the beginning of each season, with first-class status conferred on the first two of these matches.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Officers_of_the_club">Officers of the club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marylebone_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Officers of the club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Presidents serve a twelve-month term and cannot normally serve two terms in succession. Notable exceptions occurred during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In 1914, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Hawke,_7th_Baron_Hawke" title="Martin Hawke, 7th Baron Hawke">Lord Hawke</a> was appointed President and was asked to remain in the post till the end of the war.<sup id="cite_ref-P&amp;D_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P&amp;D-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> As a result, Hawke was MCC President for five years from 1914 to 1918 inclusive and was succeeded in 1919 by the former Hampshire slow left-arm bowler <a href="/wiki/Henry_Forster,_1st_Baron_Forster" title="Henry Forster, 1st Baron Forster">Henry Forster</a>, who shortly afterwards succeeded to the title of Lord Forster of Lepe.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the war, Lord\'s was used for military purposes, including training and recreation. Problems frequently arose but, in <i>Wisden\'s</i> view, Hawke was "the greatest help in giving wise counsel towards their solution".<sup id="cite_ref-wistrib_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wistrib-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Hawke\'s tenure was exceeded by that of <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Christopherson" title="Stanley Christopherson">Stanley Christopherson</a> who was appointed in 1939 and remained <i>in situ</i> for seven years until 1945 before he was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Forbes_Adam" title="Ronald Forbes Adam">General Ronald Adam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWC49_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWC49-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In his <i>Barclays World of Cricket</i> essay about the MCC Presidency, <a href="/wiki/E._W._Swanton" title="E. W. Swanton">E. W. Swanton</a> said that "there is no pretence of democracy about it" and commented on how few were untitled up to the Second World War. As he said, membership of the aristocracy was a more important factor than any cricketing prowess.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In the 21st century there have been MCC Presidents who as players were wholly professional: <a href="/wiki/Tom_Graveney" title="Tom Graveney">Tom Graveney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Underwood" title="Derek Underwood">Derek Underwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Gatting" title="Mike Gatting">Mike Gatting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Fleming" title="Matthew Fleming">Matthew Fleming</a>.\n</p><p>Each President is required to nominate his successor at the Annual General Meeting (AGM) which takes place during his term of office.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The club chairman and the treasurer serve a three-year term. Both are appointed by the committee (but subject to approval of the voting members). Both can serve terms in succession. The secretary and chief executive (a joint role) is the senior employee of the club and is appointed solely by the MCC committee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The committee consists of the above officers plus the chairmen of any other committees that may exist at the time of any meeting plus twelve elected members. Elected committee members are appointed for a three-year term. An elected committee member cannot be re-elected upon retirement unless there is a gap of at least one year between terms of office.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
