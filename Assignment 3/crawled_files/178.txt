white-toothed shrew</b> (<i>Crocidura suaveolens</i>) is a tiny <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrew</a> with a widespread distribution in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harris_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Its preferred habitat is <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrubland">scrub</a> and gardens and it feeds on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slug" title="Slug">slugs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newt" title="Newt">newts</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>. The closely related Asian lesser white-toothed shrew (<i><a href="/wiki/Crocidura_shantungensis" class="mw-redirect" title="Crocidura shantungensis">Crocidura shantungensis</a></i>) was once included in this species, but is now considered to be a separate species.\n</p><p>Like the <a href="/wiki/Common_shrew" title="Common shrew">common shrew</a>, a female lesser white-toothed shrew and her young may form a "caravan" when foraging for food or seeking a place of safety; each shrew grips the tail of the shrew in front so that the group stays together.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_white-toothed_shrew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Occurs widely from France and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, in the west, across Europe and Asia to Japan and also in North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There is one isolated <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> population in the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> and another two populations off the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French coast</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yalden_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yalden-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The preferred habitat is dry ground, including scrub and gardens, and within the Isles of Scilly can be found on <a href="/wiki/Shingle_beach" title="Shingle beach">shingle beaches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">sand dunes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Scilly_shrew">The Scilly shrew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_white-toothed_shrew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Scilly shrew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The population found on the Isles of Scilly, off the south-west coast of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, was once thought to be a sub-species, <i>Crocidura suaveolens cassiteridum</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and is known as the Scilly shrew. Skull and tooth measurements of individuals from Scilly are found to be intermediate in size of those in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> and the darker fur of the Scilly specimens is not considered a valid reason for the naming of a sub-species.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is unusual in that it can be found on the islands\' beaches.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Scillonian name for the animal is "<i>teak</i>" or "<i>teke</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Archaeological remains indicate that it was present on the islands in the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">bronze Age</a>, so it may have been present before the Isles of Scilly became separated from the European continent, or may have migrated from the Channel Islands or Europe on board ships.<sup id="cite_ref-IOSWT_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOSWT-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Although if shrews had survived through the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">last glaciation</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>, it would seem that northerly distributed species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sorex_araneus" class="mw-redirect" title="Sorex araneus">Sorex araneus</a></i> would have been more likely to survive, rather than a southerly distributed species such as <i>Crocidura suaveolens</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-yalden_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yalden-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 1924 W N Blair found an unknown species of shrew on <a href="/wiki/Gugh" title="Gugh">Gugh</a> and sent it to the mammal expert, Mr <a href="/wiki/Martin_Hinton" title="Martin Hinton">Hinton</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>. This specimen, held at the museum, is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type</a> for the species.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Ten years earlier H N Robinson found an unknown rodent at Old Town <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s,_Isles_of_Scilly" title="St Mary&#39;s, Isles of Scilly">St Mary\'s</a> and sent it to Mr F W Smalley "who had the largest collection of rodents in the country". \nIn 2010, a Scilly shrew made headlines on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Cornwall" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC Cornwall">BBC Cornwall</a> when it <a href="/wiki/Stowaway" title="Stowaway">stowed away</a> on the passenger ferry <a href="/wiki/RMV_Scillonian_III" title="RMV Scillonian III">RMV <i>Scillonian III</i></a>. It was only discovered as the ship was about to arrive in Penzance. The shrew was flown back to the Isles of Scilly the next day on a <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly_Skybus" title="Isles of Scilly Skybus">Skybus</a> plane and then released back into its natural environment.<sup id="cite_ref-shrew_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shrew-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_white-toothed_shrew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-msw3-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-msw3_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite id="CITEREFHutterer2005" class="citation book">Hutterer, R.
