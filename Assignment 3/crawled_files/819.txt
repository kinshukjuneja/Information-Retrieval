class="firstHeading" lang="en">Forestry in the United Kingdom</h1>\t\t\t<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" data-file-height="2098" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Epping_Forest_Centenary_Walk_2_-_Sept_2008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest" title="Epping Forest">Epping Forest</a> in 2008</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;Notes 1&#93;</a></sup> being in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, is ideal for tree growth, thanks to its mild winters, plentiful rainfall, fertile soil and hill-sheltered topography.  Growth rates for <a href="/wiki/Hardwood" title="Hardwood">broadleaved (hardwood) trees</a> exceed those of mainland Europe, while <a href="/wiki/Softwood" title="Softwood">conifer (softwood)</a> growth rates are three times those of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and five times those of <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>.  In the absence of people, much of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> would be covered with mature <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oaks</a>, except for Scotland.  Although conditions for <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a> are good, trees do face damage threats arising from <a href="/wiki/Forest_pathology" title="Forest pathology">fungi, parasites and pests</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hart_68_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hart_68-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFitter200210_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFitter200210-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nowadays, about 12.9% of Britain\'s land surface is wooded.  The country\'s supply of timber was severely depleted during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second</a> World Wars, when imports were difficult, and the forested area bottomed out at under 5% of Britain\'s land surface in 1919.  That year, the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> was established to produce a strategic reserve of timber. Other European countries average from 25% to 37% of their area as woodland.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nix_22_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nix_22-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nix_932_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nix_932-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHart1994321_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart1994321-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EG2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EG2-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Of the 31,380 square kilometres (12,120&#160;sq&#160;mi) of forest in Britain, around 30% is publicly owned and 70% is in the private sector.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;Notes 2&#93;</a></sup>  More than 40,000 people work on this land.  Broadleaves account for 29% of Britain\'s woodlands, the rest being conifers, but considering only <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, the figures are 55% broadleaf and 45% conifer.  Britain\'s native tree flora comprises 32 species, of which 29 are broadleaves.  Britain\'s industry and populace uses at least 50 million tonnes of timber a year.  More than 75% of this is softwood, and Britain\'s forests cannot supply the demand; in fact, less than 10% of the timber used in Britain is home-grown.  Paper and paper products make up more than half the wood consumed in Britain by volume.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nix_942_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nix_942-10">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hart_12_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hart_12-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHart199413_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart199413-12">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2010, the new coalition government of the UK suggested it might sell off around half the Forestry Commission-owned woodland in the UK.  A wide variety of groups were vocal about their disapproval, and by February 2011, the government abandoned the idea.  Instead, it set up the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Panel_on_Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Independent Panel on Forestry (page does not exist)">Independent Panel on Forestry</a> led by <a href="/wiki/James_Jones_(bishop)" title="James Jones (bishop)">Rt Rev James Jones</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Liverpool" title="Bishop of Liverpool">Bishop of Liverpool</a>.  This body published its report in July 2012.  Among other suggestions, it recommended that the forested portion of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> should rise to 15% of the country\'s land area by 2060.<sup Timber Corps</a> in the Second World War</div></div></div> \n<p>Throughout most of British history, people have most commonly created farmland at the expense of forest. Furthermore, variations in the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> climate have led to significant changes in the ranges of many species. This makes it complex to estimate the likely extent of natural <a href="/wiki/Forest_cover" title="Forest cover">forest cover</a>. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> four main areas have been identified: oak dominated forest south of the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scots_Pine" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots Pine">Scots Pine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Central Highlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazel</a>/oak or <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a>/<a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a>/oak assemblages in the north-east and south-west Highlands, and birch in the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">Northern Isles</a> and far north of the mainland. Furthermore, the effects of fire, human clearance and grazing probably limited forest cover to about 50% of the land area of Scotland even at its maximum. The stock of woodland declined alarmingly during the First World War<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and "a Forestry Subcommittee was added\nto the Reconstruction Committee to advise on policy when the war was over. The Subcommittee, better known as the Acland Committee after its chairman <a href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Dyke_Acland,_13th_Baronet" title="Sir Arthur Dyke Acland, 13th Baronet">Sir A. H. D. Acland</a>, came to the conclusion that, in order to secure the double purpose of being able to be independent from foreign supplies for three years and a reasonable insurance against a timber famine, the woods of Great Britain should be gradually increased from three million acres to four and three quarter millions at the end of the war".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Following the Acland Report of 1918 the Forestry Commission was formed in 1919 to meet this need.  State forest parks were established in 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmout_et_al.200726\xe2\x80\x9328_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmout_et_al.200726\xe2\x80\x9328-15">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup Timber Committee</i>, September 1916.</div></div></div>\n<p>Emergency felling controls had been introduced in the First and Second World Wars, and these were made permanent in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_Act_1951&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Forestry Act 1951 (page does not exist)">Forestry Act 1951</a>.  Landowners were also given financial incentives to devote land to forests under the Dedication Scheme, which in 1981 became the Forestry Grant Scheme.  By the early 1970s, the annual rate of planting exceeded 40,000 hectares (99,000 acres) per annum.  Most of this planting comprised fast-growing conifers.  Later in the century the balance shifted, with fewer than 20,000 hectares (49,000 acres) per annum being planted during the 1990s, but broadleaf planting actually increased, exceeding 1,000 hectares (2,500 acres) per year in 1987. By the mid-1990s, more than half of new planting was broadleaf.<sup id="cite_ref-Nix_942_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nix_942-10">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nix_95_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nix_95-18">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:372px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png/370px-Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png" width="370" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png/555px-Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png/740px-Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png 2x" data-file-width="1510" data-file-height="969" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodland_as_a_percentage_of_land_area_in_England.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historical woodland cover of England. The government believes 12% can be reached again by 2060.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1988, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodland_Grant_Scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Woodland Grant Scheme (page does not exist)">Woodland Grant Scheme</a> replaced the Forestry Grant Scheme, paying nearly twice as much for broadleaf woodland as conifers.  (In England, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodland_Grant_Scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Woodland Grant Scheme (page does not exist)">Woodland Grant Scheme</a> was Programme">England Rural Development Programme</a> is the current overarching grants scheme that includes money for forested land within it.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_woodland">Ancient woodland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient woodland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_woodland" title="Ancient woodland">Ancient woodland</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_woodland" title="Ancient woodland">Ancient woodland</a> is defined as any woodland that has been continuously forested since 1600. It is recorded on either the Register of Ancient Semi-Natural Woodland or the Register of Planted Woodland Sites.  There is no woodland in Britain that has not been profoundly affected by human intervention. Apart from certain native pinewoods in Scotland, it is predominantly broadleaf. Such woodland is less productive, in terms of timber yield, but ecologically rich, typically containing a number of "<a href="/wiki/Indicator_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Indicator species">indicator species</a>" of indigenous wildlife.  It comprises roughly 20% of the forested area.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hart_146_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hart_146-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_and_historic_tree_species">Native and historic tree species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Native and historic tree species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Britain is relatively impoverished in terms of native species. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A white bracket fungus" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg/220px-Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg/330px-Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg/440px-Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="425" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heterobasidion_annosum_2_-_Lindsey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>White Rot Fungus, <i><a href="/wiki/Heterobasidion_annosum" title="Heterobasidion annosum">Heterobasidion annosum</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>Most serious disease threats to British woodland involve fungus.  For conifers, the greatest threat is White Rot Fungus (<i><a href="/wiki/Heterobasidion_annosum" title="Heterobasidion annosum">Heterobasidion annosum</a></i>).  <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Elm_Disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch Elm Disease">Dutch Elm Disease</a> arises from two related species of fungi in the genus <i>Ophiostoma</i>, spread by <a href="/wiki/Elm_Bark_Beetle" class="mw-redirect" title="Elm Bark Beetle">Elm Bark Beetles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acute_oak_decline" title="Acute oak decline">acute oak decline</a> has a <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> cause.  Another fungus, <i><a href="/wiki/Nectria_coccinea" title="Nectria coccinea">Nectria coccinea</a></i>, causes <a href="/wiki/Beech_bark_disease" title="Beech bark disease">Beech bark disease</a>, as does <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulgaria_polymorpha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bulgaria polymorpha (page does not exist)">Bulgaria polymorpha</a></i>.  <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_canker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ash canker (page does not exist)">Ash canker</a> results from <i><a href="/wiki/Nectria_galligena" class="mw-redirect" title="Nectria galligena">Nectria galligena</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_savastanoi" title="Pseudomonas savastanoi">Pseudomonas savastanoi</a></i>, and most trees are vulnerable to Honey Fungus (<i><a href="/wiki/Armillaria_mellea" title="Armillaria mellea">Armillaria mellea</a></i>). The <a href="/wiki/Oomycete" title="Oomycete">oomycete</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_ramorum" title="Phytophthora ramorum">Phytophthora ramorum</a></i> (responsible for "Sudden oak death" in the USA) has killed large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Larix_kaempferi" title="Larix kaempferi">Japanese Larch</a> trees in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHart1994229_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart1994229-32">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hart_230_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hart_230-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-James_142_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James_142-35">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1966143_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1966143-36">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Beetles, moths and weevils can also damage trees, but the majority do not cause serious harm.  Notable exceptions include the Large Pine Weevil (<i><a href="/wiki/Hylobius_abietis" title="Hylobius abietis">Hylobius abietis</a></i>), which can kill young conifers, the Spruce Bark Beetle (<i><a href="/wiki/Ips_typographus" class="mw-redirect" title="Ips typographus">Ips typographus</a></i>) which can kill <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruces</a>, and the Cockchafer (<i><a href="/wiki/Melolontha_melolontha" class="mw-redirect" title="Melolontha melolontha">Melolontha melolontha</a></i>) which eats young tree roots and can kill in a dry season.  <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">Rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apodemus" title="Apodemus">field mice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Girdling#By_animals" title="Girdling">deer</a>, and farm animals can pose a significant threat to trees.  <a href="/wiki/Forest_dieback" title="Forest dieback">Air pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a> represent the main <a href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_health_hazards" class="mw-redirect" title="List of environmental health hazards">environmental hazards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-James_158_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James_158-37">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHibbert199199_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHibbert199199-38">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Timber_industry">Timber industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Timber industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2013, the UK produced 3,582,000 cubic metres of sawn wood, 3,032,000 cubic metres of wood-based panels and  4,561,000 tonnes of paper and paperboard.  The UK does not produce enough timber to satisfy domestic demand, and the country has been a net importer of timber and paper for many years.  In 2008 the country imported sawn and other wood to a value of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\xc2\xa3</a>1,243 million and exported \xc2\xa398 million; imported \xc2\xa3832 million of wood-based panels and exported \xc2\xa3104 million; and imported paper and paper-based products to a value of \xc2\xa34,273 million and exported \xc2\xa31,590 million.  In 2012 approximately 15,000 people were employed in forestry and 26,000 in primary wood production in the country, resulting in a gross value added to the country of \xc2\xa31,936 million.  With the ongoing closure of sawmills, the biomass industry is likely to be a key driver for future growth.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_9_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_9-39">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_7_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_7-40">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_12_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_12-41">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_13_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForestry_Commission2009table_13-42">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EG2_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EG2-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planting">Planting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Planting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Successful forestry requires healthy, well-formed trees that are resistant to diseases and parasites.  The best wood has a straight, circular stem without a spiral grain or fluting, and small, evenly spaced branches.  The chances of achieving these are maximised by planting good-quality seed in the best possible growing environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Hart_572_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hart_572-43">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Commercial seed is relatively inexpensive,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;Notes 5&#93;</a></sup> but some foresters still prefer to collect their own seed.  This should be done in dry weather, the seed kept in a well-ventilated place away from damp; broadleaved seeds do not keep well in long-term storage (apart from oak, sweet chestnut and sycamore, which can be kept).  Conifer seed does tend to be storable.  Stored seed is best pre-treated by being chilled and moistened before planting.\n</p><p>Tree breeding programmes, to ensure the best seed, are hampered by the trees\' long life-cycles.  However, particularly since the 1950s, the Forestry Commission among other organisations has been running a programme of breeding, propagation, induced flowering and controlled pollination with the aim of producing healthy, disease-resistant, fast-growing stock.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stewardship_and_management">Stewardship and management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stewardship and management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> manages almost 700,000 hectares (about 1.7 million acres) of land in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, making it the country\'s biggest land manager.<sup id="cite_ref-countrylife_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrylife-45">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The majority of the land (70%) is in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, 30% of the landholding is in England.<sup id="cite_ref-Stats2011_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stats2011-46">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Activities carried out on the forest estate include maintenance and improvement of the natural environment and the provision of recreation, timber <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvesting</a> to supply domestic industry, regenerating brownfield and replanting of harvested areas.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">Afforestation</a> was the main reason for the creation of the commission in 1919. Britain had only 5% of its original forest cover left and the government at that time wanted to create a strategic resource of timber.<sup id="cite_ref-FCHistory_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FCHistory-47">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Since then forest coverage has doubled and the commission\'s remit expanded to include greater focus on sustainable forest management and maximising public benefits. Woodland creation continues to be an important role of the commission, however, and works closely with government to achieve its goal of 12% forest coverage by 2060,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> championing initiatives such as <a href="/wiki/The_Big_Tree_Plant" title="The Big Tree Plant">The Big Tree Plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodland_Carbon_Code" title="Woodland Carbon Code">Woodland Carbon Code</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> is also the government body responsible for the regulation of private forestry; felling is generally illegal without first obtaining a licence from the Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-Felling_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Felling-49">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The Commission is also responsible for encouraging new private forest growth and development. Part of this role is carried out by providing grants in support of private forests and woodlands.\n</p><p>In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Wales" title="Natural Resources Wales">Natural Resources Wales</a> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Cyfoeth Naturiol Cymru</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a> sponsored body, for the management of all the natural resources of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. It was formed from a merger of the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_Council_for_Wales" title="Countryside Council for Wales">Countryside Council for Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environment_Agency_Wales" title="Environment Agency Wales">Environment Agency Wales</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission Wales</a>, and also assumes some other roles formerly taken by Welsh Government.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Forest_Stewardship_Council" title="Forest Stewardship Council">Forest Stewardship Council</a>, more specifically FSC UK, sets forest management standards for the UK, promotes the system and provides an information service. It looks at the environmental, social and economic impacts of the timber industry. It has issued 2,340 Certificates of Conformity encompassing 1,609,414 ha of forest.\n</p><p>Plans featuring "good forestry practice" must always consider the needs of other stakeholders such as nearby communities or rural residents living within or adjacent to woodland areas. Foresters consider tree felling and environmental legislation when developing plans. Plans instruct the sustainable harvesting and replacement of trees. They indicate whether road building or other forest engineering operations are required.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg/220px-Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg/330px-Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg/440px-Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="425" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Southbound_timber_train_at_Greenholme_-_geograph.org.uk_-_738830.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A timber train at Greenholme, near Kendal</div></div></div>\n<p>Currently, the vast majority of Britain\'s timber uses road haulage. As forests are located in rural areas, the heavy timber vehicles have severely damaged many single lane tracks, especially in the Highlands. In order to combat this, companies are being forced to provide funding for repairs, as well as using alternative transport systems such as rail and coastal shipping. Despite the number of <a href="/wiki/Forest_railway" title="Forest railway">forest railways</a> plummeting after the <a href="/wiki/Beeching_cuts" title="Beeching cuts">Beeching Axe</a>, rail\'s share of timber transport has risen from 3% in 2002 with the opening of new lines in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">the Pennines</a>, Scotland and South Wales by <a href="/wiki/Colas_Rail" title="Colas Rail">Colas Rail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_values">Land values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Land values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The price of woodland has risen out of proportion to its productivity, and in 2012 reached peak prices over \xc2\xa310,000 per acre.  Woodland prices are affected by its very favourable tax treatment and its high amenity value.<sup id="cite_ref-EG2_8-3" class="reference"><a
