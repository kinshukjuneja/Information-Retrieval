forced to endure the stink of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Campbell-Bannerman" title="Henry Campbell-Bannerman">Henry Campbell-Bannerman</a>\'s "cigar" of Irish Home Rule.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Irish Home Rule movement</b> was a movement that campaigned for <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">self-government</a> for Ireland within the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>. It was the dominant political movement of <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Irish nationalism</a> from 1870 to the end of World War I.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Butt" title="Isaac Butt">Isaac Butt</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Home_Government_Association" title="Home Government Association">Home Government Association</a> in 1870. This was succeeded in 1873 by the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_League" title="Home Rule League">Home Rule League</a>, and in 1882 by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a>. These organisations campaigned for home rule in the British House of Commons. Under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Charles Stewart Parnell</a>, the movement came close to success when the Liberal government under <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/First_Home_Rule_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="First Home Rule Bill">First Home Rule Bill</a> in 1886, but the bill was defeated in the House of Commons after a split in the Liberal Party. After Parnell\'s death, Gladstone introduced the <a href="/wiki/Second_Home_Rule_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Home Rule Bill">Second Home Rule Bill</a> in 1893; it passed the Commons but was defeated in the House of Lords. After the removal of the Lords\' veto in 1911, the <a href="/wiki/Third_Home_Rule_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Home Rule Bill">Third Home Rule Bill</a> was introduced in 1912, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_Crisis" title="Home Rule Crisis">Home Rule Crisis</a>. On the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> it was enacted, but suspended until the conclusion of the war. Following the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> of 1916, public support shifted from the Home Rule movement to the more radical <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> party. In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1918" title="Irish general election, 1918">1918 General Election</a> the Irish Parliamentary Party suffered a crushing defeat, only a handful of MP\'s surviving. This was effectively the death of the Home Rule movement. The elected Sinn F\xc3\xa9in MPs were not content merely with home rule within the framework of the United Kingdom. They instead set up their own legislature, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann">D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann</a>, and declared Ireland an independent republic. Britain passed a Fourth Home Rule Bill, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>, aimed at creating separate parliaments for <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and Southern Ireland. The former was established in 1921, and the state continues to this day as part of the United Kingdom. The latter never functioned. Following the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> that ended the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Irish War">Anglo-Irish War</a>, the 26 southern counties of Ireland became the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, which evolved into the present <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1800">Act of Union 1800</a> the separate Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a> were merged on 1 January 1801, to form the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the 19th century Irish opposition to the Union was strong, occasionally erupting in violent insurrection. In the 1830s and 1840s attempts had been made under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O&#39;Connell">Daniel O\'Connell</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Repeal_Association" title="Repeal Association">Repeal Association</a> to repeal the Act of Union and restore the Kingdom of Ireland, without breaking the monarchical connection with Great Britain (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a>). The movement collapsed when O\'Connell called off a meeting at <a href="/wiki/Clontarf,_Dublin" title="Clontarf, Dublin">Clontarf, Dublin</a>, which had been banned by the authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Until the 1870s, most Irish voters elected as their members of parliament (MP\'s) <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> who belonged to the main British political parties. The Conservatives, for example, won a majority in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1859_(Ireland)" title="United Kingdom general election, 1859 (Ireland)">1859 general election in Ireland</a>. Conservatives and (after 1886) <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Unionist_Party" title="Liberal Unionist Party">Liberal Unionists</a> fiercely resisted any dilution of the Act of Union, and in 1891 formed the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Unionist_Alliance" title="Irish Unionist Alliance">Irish Unionist Alliance</a> to oppose home rule.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_concepts">Different concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Different concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "Home Rule" (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Rialtas D\xc3\xbachais</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>), first used in the 1860s, meant an Irish legislature with responsibility for domestic affairs. It was variously interpreted, from the 1870s was seen to be part of a federal system for the United Kingdom: a domestic Parliament for Ireland while the Imperial Parliament at <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Westminster</a> would continue to have responsibility for Imperial affairs. The Republican concept as represented by the <a href="/wiki/Fenian" title="Fenian">Fenians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Brotherhood" title="Irish Republican Brotherhood">Irish Republican Brotherhood</a>, strove to achieve total separation from Great Britain, if necessary by physical force, and complete autonomy for Ireland. For a while they were prepared to co-operate with <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_League" title="Home Rule League">Home Rulers</a> under the <a href="/wiki/New_Departure_(Ireland)" title="New Departure (Ireland)">"New Departure"</a>. In 1875 <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Connor_Power" title="John O&#39;Connor Power">John O\'Connor Power</a> told a New York audience that \'[Ireland]has elected a body of representatives whose mission is simply \xe2\x80\x93 I almost said solely \xe2\x80\x93 but certainly whose mission is particularly to offer unrelenting hostility to every British Ministry while one link of the imperial chain remains to fetter the constitutional freedom of the Irish nation.\'<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Charles Stewart Parnell</a> sought through the \'constitutional movement\', as an interim measure a parliament in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> with limited legislative powers. For <a href="/wiki/Unionism_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Unionism (Ireland)">Unionists</a> Home Rule meant a Dublin parliament dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> to the detriment of Ireland\'s economic progress, a threat to their cultural identity as both British and Irish and possible discrimination against them as a religious minority.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In England the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> under <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">William Ewart Gladstone</a> was fully committed to introducing Home Rule whereas the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> tried to alleviate any need for it through \'constructive unionism\', passing many acts of parliament beneficial to Ireland.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb In May 1870, he established a new moderate nationalist movement, the <a href="/wiki/Home_Government_Association" title="Home Government Association">Irish Home Government Association</a>. In November 1873, under the chairmanship of <a href="/wiki/William_Shaw_(Irish_politician)" title="William Shaw (Irish politician)">William Shaw</a>, it reconstituted itself as the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_League" title="Home Rule League">Home Rule League</a>. The League\'s goal was limited self-government for Ireland as part of the United Kingdom. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1874" title="United Kingdom general election, 1874">1874 general election</a>, League-affiliated candidates won 53 seats in Parliament.\n</p><p>Butt died in 1879. In 1880, a radical young Protestant landowner, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Charles Stewart Parnell</a> became chairman, and in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1880_(Ireland)" title="United Kingdom general election, 1880 (Ireland)">1880 general election</a>, the League won 63 seats. In 1882, Parnell turned the Home Rule League into the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a> (IPP), a formally organized party which became a major political force . The IPP came to dominate Irish politics, to the exclusion of the previous Liberal, Conservative, and Unionist parties that had existed there. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1885" title="United Kingdom general election, 1885">1885 general election</a>, the IPP won 85 out of the 103 Irish seats; another Home Rule MP was elected for <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Scotland_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Liverpool Scotland (UK Parliament constituency)">Liverpool Scotland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adversary_Lords">Adversary Lords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adversary Lords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Gladstone, impressed by Parnell, had become personally committed to granting Irish home rule in 1885. With his famous three-hour <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_Speech" class="extiw" title="s:Irish Home Rule Speech">Irish Home Rule speech</a> Gladstone beseeched parliament to pass the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Government_Bill_1886" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Government Bill 1886">Irish Government Bill 1886</a>, and grant home rule to Ireland in honour rather than being compelled to do so one day in humiliation. His bill was defeated in the Commons by 30 votes.\n</p><p>The Bill resulted in <a href="/wiki/1886_Belfast_riots" title="1886 Belfast riots">serious riots in Belfast</a> during the summer and autumn of 1886 in which many were killed, and caused the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Unionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal Unionist">Liberal Unionist</a> Association to split from the main Liberal party. They allied with the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Salisbury" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Salisbury">Lord Salisbury</a>\'s Conservatives until 1914 on the issue of Home Rule.\n</p><p>The defeat of the bill caused Gladstone to temporarily lose power. Having returned to power after the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1892" title="United Kingdom general election, 1892">1892 general election</a> Gladstone, undaunted, made a second attempt to introduce Irish Home Rule following Parnell\'s death with the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Government_Bill_1893" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Government Bill 1893">Irish Government Bill 1893</a> which he controversially drafted in secret and thereby flawed. Eventually it was steered through <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">the Commons</a> by <a href="/wiki/William_O%27Brien" title="William O&#39;Brien">William O\'Brien</a>, with a majority of 30 votes, only to be defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a>\'s pro-<a href="/wiki/Unionists_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Unionists (Ireland)">unionist</a> majority controlled <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.\n</p><p>On this defeat the new Liberal leader <a href="/wiki/Lord_Rosebery" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Rosebery">Lord Rosebery</a> adopted the policy of promising Salisbury that the majority vote of English MPs would have a veto on any future Irish Home Rule Bills. The Nationalist movement divided in the 1890s. The Liberals lost the 1895 General Election and their Conservative opponents remained in power until 1905.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_Rule_Bills">Home Rule Bills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Home Rule Bills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The four Irish Home Rule <a href="/wiki/Bill_(proposed_law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill (proposed law)">bills</a> introduced in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons of the United Kingdom</a> during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, were intended to grant self-government and national autonomy to the whole of Ireland within the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a> and reverse parts of the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union 1800</a>. Of the two that passed the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> the Third Bill, enacted as the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Government of Ireland Act 1914</a> and then suspended, while the Fourth Bill, enacted as the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a> established two separate Home Rule territories in Ireland, of which the one was implemented by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a>, but the second <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Southern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Southern Ireland">Parliament of Southern Ireland</a> was not implemented in the rest of Ireland. The bills were:\n</p>\n<ul><li>1886: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Bill_1886" title="Government of Ireland Bill 1886">First Irish Home Rule Bill</a> defeated in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> and never introduced in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.</li>\n<li>1893: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Bill_1893" title="Government of Ireland Bill 1893">Second Irish Home Rule Bill</a> passed the House of Commons, but defeated in the House of Lords.</li>\n<li>1912\xe2\x80\x9314: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1914" title="Government of Ireland Act 1914">Third Irish Home Rule Bill</a> passed under the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949" title="Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949">Parliament Act</a> after House of Lords defeats, with <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> as the Government of Ireland Act 1914 but never came into force, due to the intervention of World War I (1914\xe2\x80\x9318) and of the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> in Dublin (1916).</li>\n<li>1920: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Fourth Irish Home Rule Act</a> (replaced Third Act, passed and implemented as the Government of Ireland Act 1920) which established <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> as a Home Rule entity within the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland, and attempted to establish <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> as another but instead resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Irish independence</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State_Constitution_Act_1922" title="Irish Free State Constitution Act 1922">Irish Free State Constitution Act 1922</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>In 1920 the unionist peer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Spring_Rice,_2nd_Baron_Monteagle_of_Brandon" title="Thomas Spring Rice, 2nd Baron Monteagle of Brandon">Lord Monteagle of Brandon</a> proposed his own Dominion of Ireland Bill in the House of Lords, at the same time as the Government bill was passing through the house.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This bill would have given a united Ireland extensive home rule over all domestic matters as a dominion within the empire, with foreign affairs and defence remaining the responsibility of the Westminster government. Lord Monteagle\'s bill was defeated at second reading.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_Rule_in_sight">Home Rule in sight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Home Rule in sight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Following the 1895 general election, the Conservatives were in power for ten years. The significant <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Ireland)_Act_1898" title="Local Government (Ireland) Act 1898">Local Government (Ireland) Act 1898</a> (following the English Act of 1888) introduced for the first time the enfranchisement of local electors, bringing about a system of localised home rule in many areas. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1906" title="United Kingdom general election, 1906">1906 general election</a> the Liberals were returned with an overall majority, but Irish Home Rule was not on their agenda until after the second <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_December_1910" title="United Kingdom general election, December 1910">1910 general election</a> when the nationalist <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a> under its leader <a href="/wiki/John_Redmond" title="John Redmond">John Redmond</a> held the balance of power in the House of Commons. Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a> came to an understanding with Redmond, that if he supported his move to break the power of the Lords to have the finance bill passed, Asquith would then in return introduce a new Home Rule Bill. The <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act 1911</a> forced the Lords to agree to a curtailment of their powers. Now their unlimited veto was replaced with a delaying one lasting only two years.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_Act_1914" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Rule Act 1914">Third Home Rule Bill</a> introduced in 1912 was as in 1886 and 1893 ferociously opposed by <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster unionists</a>, for whom Home Rule was synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Rome_Rule" title="Rome Rule">Rome Rule</a> as well as being indicative of economic decline and a threat to their cultural and industrial identity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Carson" title="Edward Carson">Edward Carson</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Craig,_1st_Viscount_Craigavon" title="James Craig, 1st Viscount Craigavon">James Craig</a>, leaders of the unionists, were instrumental in organising the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Covenant" title="Ulster Covenant">Ulster Covenant</a> against the "coercion of Ulster", at which time Carson reviewed Orange and Unionist volunteers in various parts of Ulster. These were united into a single body known as the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteers" title="Ulster Volunteers">Ulster Volunteers</a> at the start of 1912.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This was followed in the south by the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Volunteers" title="Irish Volunteers">Irish Volunteers</a> to restrain Ulster. Both Nationalists and Republicans, except for the <a href="/wiki/All-for-Ireland_League" title="All-for-Ireland League">All-for-Ireland Party</a>, brushed unionist concerns aside with "no concessions for Ulster", treating their threat as a bluff. The Act received <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> and was placed on the statute books on 18 September 1914, but under the <a href="/wiki/Suspensory_Act_1914" title="Suspensory Act 1914">Suspensory Act</a> was deferred for no longer than the duration of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> which had broken out in August. The widely held assumption at the time was that the war would be short lived.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changed_realities">Changed realities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Changed realities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_Crisis" title="Home Rule Crisis">Home Rule Crisis</a></div>\n<p>With the participation of <a href="/wiki/Ireland_in_World_War_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Ireland in World War I">Ireland in the First World War</a>, the southern Irish Volunteers split into the larger <a href="/wiki/National_Volunteers" title="National Volunteers">National Volunteers</a> and followed Redmond\'s call to support the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allied war effort</a> to ensure the future implementation of Home Rule by voluntarily enlisting in <a href="/wiki/Ireland_and_World_War_I#Regiments_garrisoned_in_Ireland" title="Ireland and World War I">Irish regiments</a> of the <a href="/wiki/10th_(Irish)_Division" title="10th (Irish) Division">10th (Irish) Division</a> or the <a href="/wiki/16th_(Irish)_Division" title="16th (Irish) Division">16th (Irish) Division</a> of Kitchener\'s <a href="/wiki/Kitchener%27s_Army" title="Kitchener&#39;s Army">New Service Army</a>. The men of the Ulster Volunteers joined the <a href="/wiki/36th_(Ulster)_Division" title="36th (Ulster) Division">36th (Ulster) Division</a>. Between 1914 and 1918 Irish regiments suffered severe losses.\n</p><p>A core element of the remaining Irish Volunteers who opposed the nationalist constitutional movement towards independence and the Irish support for the war effort, staged the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> of 1916 in Dublin. Initially widely condemned in both Britain and Ireland, the <a href="/wiki/British_government" class="mw-redirect" title="British government">British government</a>\'s mishandling of the aftermath of the Rising, including the rushed executions of its leaders by <a href="/wiki/John_Maxwell_(British_Army_officer)" title="John Maxwell (British Army officer)">General Maxwell</a>, led to a rise in popularity for an <a href="/wiki/Irish_republican" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish republican">Irish republican</a> movement named <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a>, a small separatist party taken over by the survivors of the Easter Rising. Britain made two futile attempts to implement Home Rule, both of which failed because of Ulster unionists\' protesting against its proposed implementation for the whole island of Ireland; first after the Rising and then at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Convention" title="Irish Convention">Irish Convention</a> of 1917\xe2\x80\x931918. With the collapse of the allied front during the German <a href="/wiki/Spring_Offensive" title="Spring Offensive">Spring Offensive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Michael" title="Operation Michael">Operation Michael</a>, the British Army had a serious manpower shortage, and the Cabinet agreed on 5 April to enact Home Rule immediately, linked in with a "dual policy" of extending <a href="/wiki/Conscription_Crisis_of_1918" title="Conscription Crisis of 1918">conscription to Ireland</a>. This signalled the end of a political era,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> which resulted in a swing of public opinion towards Sinn F\xc3\xa9in and <a href="/wiki/Physical_force_Irish_republicanism" title="Physical force Irish republicanism">physical force separatism</a>. Interest in Home Rule began to fade as a result.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_Rule_enacted">Home Rule enacted</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Home_Rule_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Home Rule enacted">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After the end of the war in November 1918 Sinn F\xc3\xa9in secured a majority of 73 Irish seats in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1918" title="Irish general election, 1918">general election</a>, with 25 of these seats taken uncontested. The IPP was decimated, falling to only six seats; it disbanded soon afterward.\n</p><p>In January 1919 twenty-seven Sinn F\xc3\xa9in MPs assembled in Dublin and proclaimed themselves unilaterally as an <a href="/wiki/First_D%C3%A1il" title="First D\xc3\xa1il">independent parliament</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republic" title="Irish Republic">Irish Republic</a>. This was ignored by Britain. The <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a> (1919\xe2\x80\x931921) ensued.\n</p><p>Britain went ahead with its commitment to implement Home Rule by passing a new Fourth Home Rule Bill, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>, largely shaped by the <a href="/wiki/Walter_Long,_1st_Viscount_Long" title="Walter Long, 1st Viscount Long">Walter Long</a> Committee which followed findings contained in the report of the Irish Convention. Long, a firm unionist, felt free to shape Home Rule in Ulster\'s favour, and formalised <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">dividing Ireland</a> into <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a>. The latter never functioned, but was replaced under the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> which later became the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Home Rule <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a> came into being in June 1921. At its inauguration, in <a href="/wiki/Belfast_City_Hall" title="Belfast City Hall">Belfast City Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King George V</a> made a famous appeal drafted by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">Lloyd George</a> for Anglo-Irish and north\xe2\x80\x93south reconciliation. The Anglo-Irish Treaty had provided for Northern Ireland\'s Parliament to opt out of the new Free State, which was a foregone conclusion. The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a> (1922\xe2\x80\x931923) followed.\n</p><p>The Parliament of Northern Ireland continued in operation until 30 March 1972, when it was suspended in favour of direct rule by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Office" title="Northern Ireland Office">Northern Ireland Office</a> during <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a>. It was subsequently abolished under the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Constitution_Act_1973" title="Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973">Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973</a>. Various versions of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> re-established home rule in 1973\xe2\x80\x9374, 1982\xe2\x80\x9386, intermittently from 1998\xe2\x80\x932002, and from 2007 onward.  The Assembly attempts to balance the interests of the unionist and republican factions through a "power sharing" agreement.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
