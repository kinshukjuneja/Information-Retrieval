other type of structure designed to emit light from a system of lamps and <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a> and to serve as a <a href="/wiki/Navigational_aid" title="Navigational aid">navigational aid</a> for <a href="/wiki/Maritime_pilot" title="Maritime pilot">maritime pilots</a> at sea or on inland waterways.\n</p><p>Lighthouses mark dangerous coastlines, hazardous <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">shoals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a>, and safe entries to harbors; they also assist in <a href="/wiki/Aerial_navigation" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerial navigation">aerial navigation</a>.  Once widely used, the number of operational lighthouses has declined due to the expense of maintenance and use of electronic navigational systems.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input Since raising the fire would improve the visibility, placing the fire on a platform became a practice that led to the development of the lighthouse.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In antiquity, the lighthouse functioned more as an entrance marker to ports than as a warning signal for <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">promontories</a>, unlike many modern lighthouses. The most famous lighthouse structure from antiquity was the <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse_of_Alexandria" title="Lighthouse of Alexandria">Pharos</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, which collapsed following a series of earthquakes between 956 and 1323.\n</p><p>The intact <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_Hercules" title="Tower of Hercules">Tower of Hercules</a> at <a href="/wiki/A_Coru%C3%B1a" title="A Coru\xc3\xb1a">A Coru\xc3\xb1a</a>, Spain gives insight into ancient lighthouse construction; other evidence about lighthouses exists in depictions on coins and mosaics, of which many represent the lighthouse at <a href="/wiki/Ostia_Antica_(archaeological_site)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ostia Antica (archaeological site)">Ostia</a>. Coins from Alexandria, Ostia, and <a href="/wiki/Latakia" title="Latakia">Laodicea in Syria</a> also exist.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_construction">Modern construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The modern era of lighthouses began at the turn of the 18th century, as lighthouse construction boomed in lockstep with burgeoning levels of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">transatlantic</a> commerce. Advances in structural engineering and new and efficient lighting equipment allowed for the creation of larger and more powerful lighthouses, including ones exposed to the sea. The function of lighthouses shifted toward the provision of a visible warning against shipping hazards, such as rocks or reefs.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg/170px-Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg" width="170" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg/255px-Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg/340px-Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1116" data-file-height="1528" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eddystone_lighthouse00.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse#Winstanley&#39;s_lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Winstanley\'s lighthouse</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Rocks" title="Eddystone Rocks">Eddystone Rocks</a> marked the beginning in a new phase of lighthouse development.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Rocks" title="Eddystone Rocks">Eddystone Rocks</a> were a major shipwreck hazard for mariners sailing through the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse#Winstanley&#39;s_lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">first lighthouse built there</a> was an octagonal wooden structure, anchored by 12 iron stanchions secured in the rock, and was built by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Winstanley" title="Henry Winstanley">Henry Winstanley</a> from 1696 to 1698. His lighthouse was the first tower in the world to have been fully exposed to the open sea.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">civil engineer</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a>, rebuilt the <a href="/wiki/Smeaton%27s_Tower" title="Smeaton&#39;s Tower">lighthouse</a> from 1756\xe2\x80\x9359;<sup id="cite_ref-MAJ_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAJ-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> his tower marked a major step forward in the design of lighthouses and remained in use until 1877. He modelled the shape of his lighthouse on that of an oak tree, using granite blocks. He rediscovered and used "<a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_lime" title="Hydraulic lime">hydraulic lime</a>," a form of concrete that will set under water used by the Romans, and developed a technique of securing the granite blocks together using <a href="/wiki/Dovetail_joint" title="Dovetail joint">dovetail joints</a> and marble <a href="/wiki/Dowels" class="mw-redirect" title="Dowels">dowels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TRI_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TRI-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The dovetailing feature served to improve the <a href="/wiki/Structural_stability" title="Structural stability">structural stability</a>, although Smeaton also had to taper the thickness of the tower towards the top, for which he curved the tower inwards on a gentle gradient. This profile had the added advantage of allowing some of the energy of the waves to dissipate on impact with the walls. His lighthouse was the prototype for the modern lighthouse and influenced all subsequent engineers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Smeaton%27s_Lighthouse00.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Smeaton%27s_Lighthouse00.jpg/170px-Smeaton%27s_Lighthouse00.jpg" width="170" height="266" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Smeaton%27s_Lighthouse00.jpg/255px-Smeaton%27s_Lighthouse00.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/61/Smeaton%27s_Lighthouse00.jpg 2x" data-file-width="321" data-file-height="503" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Smeaton%27s_Lighthouse00.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a>\'s rebuilt version of the <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a>, 1759. This represented a great step forward in lighthouse design.</div></div></div>\n<p>One such influence was <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stevenson_(civil_engineer)" title="Robert Stevenson (civil engineer)">Robert Stevenson</a>, himself a seminal figure in the development of lighthouse design and construction.<sup id="cite_ref-NLB_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NLB-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His greatest achievement was the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Bell_Rock_Lighthouse" title="Bell Rock Lighthouse">Bell Rock Lighthouse</a> in 1810, one of the most impressive feats of engineering of the age. This structure was based upon Smeaton\'s design, but with several improved features, such as the incorporation of rotating lights, alternating between red and white.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Stevenson worked for the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Lighthouse_Board" title="Northern Lighthouse Board">Northern Lighthouse Board</a> for nearly fifty years<sup id="cite_ref-NLB_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NLB-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> during which time he designed and oversaw the construction and later improvement of numerous lighthouses. He innovated in the choice of light sources, mountings, reflector design, the use of <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lenses</a>, and in rotation and shuttering systems providing lighthouses with individual signatures allowing them to be identified by seafarers. He also invented the movable jib and the balance crane as a necessary part for lighthouse construction.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mitchell_(engineer)" title="Alexander Mitchell (engineer)">Alexander Mitchell</a> designed the first <a href="/wiki/Screw-pile_lighthouse" title="Screw-pile lighthouse">screw-pile lighthouse</a> \xe2\x80\x93 his lighthouse was built on piles that were <a href="/wiki/Screw" title="Screw">screwed</a> into the sandy or muddy seabed. Construction of his design began in 1838 at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a> and was known as the <a href="/wiki/Maplin_Sands" title="Maplin Sands">Maplin Sands</a> lighthouse, and first lit in 1841.<sup id="cite_ref-Tomlinson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tomlinson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Although its construction began later, the <a href="/wiki/Wyre_Light_(Fleetwood)" title="Wyre Light (Fleetwood)">Wyre Light</a> in Fleetwood, Lancashire, was the first to be lit (in 1840).<sup id="cite_ref-Tomlinson_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tomlinson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting_improvements">Lighting improvements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lighting improvements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The source of illumination had generally been wood pyres or burning coal. The <a href="/wiki/Argand_lamp" title="Argand lamp">Argand lamp</a>, invented in 1782 by the Swiss scientist, <a href="/wiki/Aim%C3%A9_Argand" title="Aim\xc3\xa9 Argand">Aim\xc3\xa9 Argand</a>, revolutionized lighthouse illumination with its steady smokeless flame. Early models used ground glass which was sometimes tinted around the wick. Later models used a mantle of <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">thorium dioxide</a> suspended over the flame, creating a bright, steady light.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Argand lamp used <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">whale oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colza" class="mw-redirect" title="Colza">colza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> or other <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oil</a> as fuel which was supplied by a <a href="/wiki/Gravity_feed" title="Gravity feed">gravity feed</a> from a reservoir mounted above the burner. The lamp was first produced by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Boulton" title="Matthew Boulton">Matthew Boulton</a>, in partnership with Argand, in 1784 and became the standard for lighthouses for over a century.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/South_Foreland_Lighthouse" title="South Foreland Lighthouse">South Foreland Lighthouse</a> was the first tower to successfully use an electric light in 1875. The lighthouse\'s <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">carbon arc lamps</a> were powered by a steam-driven <a href="/wiki/Magneto_(generator)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magneto (generator)">magneto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Richardson_Wigham" title="John Richardson Wigham">John Richardson Wigham</a> was the first to develop a system for <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">gas</a> illumination of lighthouses. His improved gas \'crocus\' burner at the <a href="/wiki/Baily_Lighthouse" title="Baily Lighthouse">Baily Lighthouse</a> near Dublin was 13 times more powerful than the most brilliant light then known.<sup id="cite_ref-cil_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cil-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg/220px-Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg" width="220" height="394" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg/330px-Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg/440px-Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2310" data-file-height="4132" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sumburgh_Lighthouse_Lamp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 85mm Chance Brothers <i>Incandescent Petroleum Vapour Installation</i> which produced the light for the <a href="/wiki/Sumburgh_Head" title="Sumburgh Head">Sumburgh Head</a> lighthouse until 1976. The lamp (made in approx. 1914) burned vaporized <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> (paraffin); the vaporizer was heated by a <a href="/wiki/Denatured_alcohol" title="Denatured alcohol">denatured alcohol</a> (methylated spirit) burner to light. When lit some of the vaporised fuel was diverted to a <a href="/wiki/Bunsen_burner" title="Bunsen burner">Bunsen burner</a> to keep the vaporizer warm and the fuel in vapor form. The fuel was forced up to the lamp by air; the keepers had to pump the air container up every hour or so. This in turn pressurized the paraffin container to force the fuel to the lamp. The "white sock" is in fact an unburnt mantle on which the vapor burned.</div></div></div>\n<p>The vaporized <a href="/wiki/Oil_burner" title="Oil burner">oil burner</a> was invented in 1901 by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Kitson" title="Arthur Kitson">Arthur Kitson</a>, and improved by David Hood at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a>. The fuel was vaporized at high pressure and burned to heat the mantle, giving an output of over six times the luminosity of traditional oil lights. The use of gas as illuminant became widely available with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Dal%C3%A9n_light" title="Dal\xc3\xa9n light">Dal\xc3\xa9n light</a> by Swedish engineer, <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Dal%C3%A9n" title="Gustaf Dal\xc3\xa9n">Gustaf Dal\xc3\xa9n</a>. He used <a href="/wiki/Agamassan" title="Agamassan">Agamassan</a> (Aga), a <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(materials_science)" title="Substrate (materials science)">substrate</a>, to absorb the gas allowing safe storage and hence commercial exploitation. Dal\xc3\xa9n also invented the \'<a href="/wiki/Sun_valve" title="Sun valve">sun valve</a>\', which automatically regulated the light and turned it off during the daytime. The technology was the predominant form of light source in lighthouses from the 1900s through the 1960s, when electric lighting had become dominant.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_systems">Optical systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Optical systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png/220px-Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png/330px-Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png/440px-Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png 2x" data-file-width="492" data-file-height="578" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fresnel_lighthouse_lens_diagram.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram depicting how a spherical <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a> collimates light</div></div></div>\n<p>With the development of the steady illumination of the Argand lamp, the application of optical lenses to increase and focus the light intensity became a practical possibility. <a href="/wiki/William_Hutchinson_(privateer)" title="William Hutchinson (privateer)">William Hutchinson</a> developed the first practical optical system in 1763, known as a <a href="/wiki/Catoptrics" title="Catoptrics">catoptric</a> system. This rudimentary system effectively collimated the emitted light into a concentrated beam, thereby greatly increasing the light\'s visibility.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The ability to focus the light led to the first revolving lighthouse beams, where the light would appear to the mariners as a series of intermittent flashes. It also became possible to transmit complex signals using the light flashes.\n</p><p>French physicist and engineer <a href="/wiki/Augustin-Jean_Fresnel" title="Augustin-Jean Fresnel">Augustin-Jean Fresnel</a> developed the multi-part <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a> for use in lighthouses. His design allowed for the construction of lenses of large <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a> and short <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a>, without the mass and volume of material that would be required by a lens of conventional design. A Fresnel lens can be made much thinner than a comparable conventional lens, in some cases taking the form of a flat sheet. A Fresnel lens can also capture more oblique light from a light source, thus allowing the light from a lighthouse equipped with one to be visible over greater distances.\n</p><p>The first Fresnel lens was used in 1823 in the <a href="/wiki/Cordouan_lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Cordouan lighthouse">Cordouan lighthouse</a> at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Gironde_estuary" title="Gironde estuary">Gironde estuary</a>; its light could be seen from more than 20 miles (32&#160;km) out.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Fresnel\xe2\x80\x99s invention increased the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of the lighthouse lamp by a factor of four and his system is still in common use.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent">Recent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The advent of electrification, and <a href="/wiki/Automatic_lamp_changer" title="Automatic lamp changer">automatic lamp changers</a> began to make lighthouse keepers obsolete. For many years, lighthouses still had keepers, partly because lighthouse keepers could serve as a <a href="/wiki/Emergency_service" title="Emergency service">rescue service</a> if necessary. Improvements in maritime navigation and safety such as the <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a> (GPS) have led to the phasing out of non-automated lighthouses across the world.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In Canada, this trend has been stopped and there are still 50 staffed light stations, with 27 on the west coast alone.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Remaining modern lighthouses usually have solar-charged batteries and have a single stationary flashing light sitting on a steel skeleton tower.<sup id="cite_ref-Crompton_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crompton-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Famous_lighthouse_builders">Famous lighthouse builders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Famous lighthouse builders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a> is noteworthy for having designed the third and most famous <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a>, but some builders are well known for their work in building multiple lighthouses. The Stevenson family (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Stevenson_(civil_engineer)" title="Robert Stevenson (civil engineer)">Robert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Stevenson" title="Alan Stevenson">Alan</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Stevenson_(engineer)" title="David Stevenson (engineer)">David</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stevenson" title="Thomas Stevenson">Thomas</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Alan_Stevenson" title="David Alan Stevenson">David Alan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alexander_Stevenson" title="Charles Alexander Stevenson">Charles</a>) made lighthouse building a three-generation profession in Scotland. Irishman <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mitchell_(engineer)" title="Alexander Mitchell (engineer)">Alexander Mitchell</a> invented and built a number of screwpile lighthouses despite his blindness. Englishman <a href="/wiki/James_Nicholas_Douglass" title="James Nicholas Douglass">James Douglass</a> was knighted for his work on lighthouses.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">United States Army Corps of Engineers</a> Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/George_Meade" title="George Meade">George Meade</a> built numerous lighthouses along the Atlantic and Gulf coasts before gaining wider fame as the winning general at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Battle of Gettysburg</a>.  Colonel <a href="/wiki/Orlando_M._Poe" class="mw-redirect" title="Orlando M. Poe">Orlando M. Poe</a>, engineer to <a href="/wiki/General_William_Tecumseh_Sherman" class="mw-redirect" title="General William Tecumseh Sherman">General William Tecumseh Sherman</a> in the Siege of Atlanta, designed and built some of the most exotic lighthouses in the most difficult locations on the U.S. Great Lakes.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>French merchant navy officer <a href="/wiki/Marius_Michel_Pasha" title="Marius Michel Pasha">Marius Michel Pasha</a> built almost a hundred lighthouses along the coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> in a period of twenty years after the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> (1853\xe2\x80\x931856).<sup id="cite_ref-hnet_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hnet-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighthouse_technology">Lighthouse technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lighthouse technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power">Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In a lighthouse, the source of light is called the "lamp" (whether electric or fuelled by oil) and the concentration of the light is by the "lens" or "optic". Originally lit by open fires and later candles, the <a href="/wiki/Argand_lamp" title="Argand lamp">Argand hollow wick lamp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic reflector</a> were introduced in the late 18th century.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">Whale oil</a> was also used with wicks as the source of light. <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">Kerosene</a> became popular in the 1870s and electricity and carbide (<a href="/wiki/Acetylene_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Acetylene gas">acetylene gas</a>) began replacing kerosene around the turn of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Crompton_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crompton-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Carbide was promoted by the <a href="/wiki/Dal%C3%A9n_light" title="Dal\xc3\xa9n light">Dal\xc3\xa9n light</a> which automatically lit the lamp at nightfall and extinguished it at dawn.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold-War</a>, many remote Soviet lighthouses were powered by <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">radioisotope thermoelectric generators</a> (RTGs). These had the advantage of providing power day or night and did not need refuelling or maintenance. However, after the breakdown of the Soviet Union, there are no official records of the locations or condition of all of these lighthouses.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> As time passes, their condition is degrading; many have fallen victim to vandalism and scrap metal thieves, who may not be aware of the dangerous radioactive contents.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lens">Lens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cape_Meares_Lighthouse_lens_-_Oregon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cape_Meares_Lighthouse_lens_-_Oregon.jpg/220px-Cape_Meares_Lighthouse_lens_-_Oregon.jpg" width="220" height="281" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cape_Meares_Lighthouse_lens_-_Oregon.jpg/330px-Cape_Meares_Lighthouse_lens_-_Oregon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/Cape_Meares_Lighthouse_lens_-_Oregon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="510" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cape_Meares_Lighthouse_lens_-_Oregon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cape_Meares_Lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Meares Lighthouse">Cape Meares Lighthouse</a>; first-order Fresnel lens</div></div></div>\n<p>Before modern <a href="/wiki/Strobe_light" title="Strobe light">strobe lights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a> were used to concentrate the light from a continuous source.  Vertical light rays of the lamp are redirected into a horizontal plane, and horizontally the light is focused into one or a few directions at a time, with the <a href="/wiki/Light_beam" title="Light beam">light beam</a> swept around. As a result, in addition to seeing the side of the light beam, the light is directly visible from greater distances, and with an identifying <a href="/wiki/Light_characteristic" title="Light characteristic">light characteristic</a>.\n</p><p>This concentration of light is accomplished with a rotating lens assembly. In early lighthouses, the light source was a <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_lamp" title="Kerosene lamp">kerosene lamp</a> or, earlier, an animal or vegetable oil Argand lamp, and the lenses rotated by a weight driven clockwork assembly wound by <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse_keeper" title="Lighthouse keeper">lighthouse keepers</a>, sometimes as often as every two hours. The lens assembly sometimes floated in <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">liquid mercury</a> to reduce friction.  In more modern lighthouses, electric lights and motor drives were used, generally powered by diesel electric generators. These also supplied electricity for the lighthouse keepers.<sup id="cite_ref-Crompton_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crompton-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Efficiently concentrating the light from a large omnidirectional light source requires a very large diameter lens. This would require a very thick and heavy lens if a conventional lens were used.  The <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lens</a> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;f&#39; in &#39;find&#39;">f</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/e\xc9\xaa/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">e\xc9\xaa</span><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/\xc9\x9b/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">\xc9\x9b</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>) focused 85% of a lamp\'s light versus the 20% focused with the parabolic reflectors of the time. Its design enabled construction of lenses of large size and short focal length without the weight and volume of material in conventional lens designs.<sup id="cite_ref-mhp_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mhp-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Fresnel lighthouse lenses are ranked by <i>order</i>, a measure of refracting power, with a first order lens being the largest, most powerful and expensive; and a sixth order lens being the smallest.  The order is based on the focal length of the lens. A first order lens has the longest focal length, with the sixth being the shortest. Coastal lighthouses generally use first, second, or third order lenses, while harbor lights and beacons use fourth, fifth, or sixth order lenses.<sup id="cite_ref-ll_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ll-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some lighthouses, such as those at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Race_Lighthouse" title="Cape Race Lighthouse">Cape Race</a>, Newfoundland, and <a href="/wiki/Makapuu_Point_Light" title="Makapuu Point Light">Makapuu Point</a>, Hawaii, used a more powerful <a href="/wiki/Hyperradiant_Fresnel_lens" title="Hyperradiant Fresnel lens">hyperradiant Fresnel lens</a> manufactured by the firm of <a href="/wiki/Chance_Brothers" title="Chance Brothers">Chance Brothers</a>.\n</p><p>In recent times, many Fresnel lenses have been replaced by rotating <a href="/wiki/Aerobeacon" title="Aerobeacon">aerobeacons</a> which require less maintenance.  In modern automated lighthouses, this system of rotating lenses is often replaced by a high intensity light that emits brief omnidirectional flashes (concentrating the light in time rather than direction). These lights are similar to obstruction lights used to warn aircraft of tall structures. Recent innovations are "Vega Lights", and initial experiments with <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diode</a> (LED) panels.<sup id="cite_ref-Crompton_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crompton-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_characteristics">Light characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Light characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Light_characteristic" title="Light characteristic">Light characteristic</a></div>\n<p>In any of these designs an observer, rather than seeing a continuous weak light, sees a brighter light during short time intervals. These instants of bright light are arranged to create a <a href="/wiki/Light_characteristic" title="Light characteristic">light characteristic</a> or pattern specific to a lighthouse.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Scheveningen_Lighthouse" title="Scheveningen Lighthouse">Scheveningen Lighthouse</a> flashes are alternately 2.5 and 7.5&#160;seconds. Some lights have sectors of a particular color (usually formed by colored panes in the lantern) to distinguish safe water areas from dangerous shoals. Modern lighthouses often have unique reflectors or <a href="/wiki/Racon" class="mw-redirect" title="Racon">Racon transponders</a> so the radar signature of the light is also unique.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building">Building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For effectiveness, the lamp must be high enough to be seen before the danger is reached by a mariner. The minimum height is calculated by <a href="/wiki/Horizon#Distance_to_the_horizon" title="Horizon">trigonometric formula</a> <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\\displaystyle d=1.17{\\sqrt {H}}}">\n  <semantics>\n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n        <mi>d</mi>\n        <mo>=</mo>\n        <mn>1.17</mn>\n        <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n          <msqrt>\n            <mi>H</mi>\n          </msqrt>\n        </mrow>\n      </mstyle>\n    </mrow>\n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle d=1.17{\\sqrt {H}}}</annotation>\n  </semantics>\n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0573a9876bc6c705d49cfacaa7c5505f0a14762" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:12.448ex; height:3.009ex;" alt="d=1.17{\\sqrt  {H}}"/></span> where <i>H</i> is the height above water in feet, and <i>d</i> is the distance to the horizon in nautical miles.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Where dangerous shoals are located far off a flat sandy beach, the prototypical tall masonry coastal lighthouse is constructed to assist the navigator making a landfall after an ocean crossing.  Often these are cylindrical to reduce the effect of wind on a tall structure, such as <a href="/wiki/Cape_May_Light" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape May Light">Cape May Light</a>. Smaller versions of this design are often used as harbor lights to mark the entrance into a harbor, such as <a href="/wiki/New_London_Harbor_Light" title="New London Harbor Light">New London Harbor Light</a>.\n</p><p>Where a tall cliff exists, a smaller structure may be placed on top such as at <a href="/wiki/Horton_Point_Light" title="Horton Point Light">Horton Point Light</a>. Sometimes, such a location can be too high, for example along the west coast of the United States, where frequent low clouds can obscure the light. In these cases, lighthouses are placed below clifftop to ensure that they can still be seen at the surface during periods of fog or low clouds, as at <a href="/wiki/Point_Reyes_Lighthouse" title="Point Reyes Lighthouse">Point Reyes Lighthouse</a>. Another victim of fog was the <a href="/wiki/Old_Point_Loma_lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Point Loma lighthouse">Old Point Loma lighthouse</a>, which was replaced in 1891 with a lower lighthouse, <a href="/wiki/New_Point_Loma_lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="New Point Loma lighthouse">New Point Loma lighthouse</a>.\n</p><p>As technology advanced, prefabricated skeletal iron or steel structures tended to be used for lighthouses constructed in the 20th century. These often have a narrow cylindrical core surrounded by an open lattice work bracing, such as <a href="/wiki/Finns_Point_Range_Light" title="Finns Point Range Light">Finns Point Range Light</a>.\n</p><p>Sometimes a lighthouse needs to be constructed in the water itself.  Wave-washed lighthouses are masonry structures constructed to withstand water impact, such as <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a> in Britain and the <a href="/wiki/St._George_Reef_Light" title="St. George Reef Light">St. George Reef Light</a> off California.  In shallower bays, <a href="/wiki/Screw-pile_lighthouse" title="Screw-pile lighthouse">Screw-pile lighthouse</a> ironwork structures are screwed into the seabed and a low wooden structure is placed above the open framework, such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Point_Shoal_Lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Point Shoal Lighthouse">Thomas Point Shoal Lighthouse</a>. As screw piles can be disrupted by ice, steel caisson lighthouses such as <a href="/wiki/Orient_Point_Light" title="Orient Point Light">Orient Point Light</a> are used in cold climates.  <a href="/wiki/Orient_Long_Beach_Bar_Light" title="Orient Long Beach Bar Light">Orient Long Beach Bar Light</a> (Bug Light) is a blend of a screw pile light that was converted to a caisson light because of the threat of ice damage.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In waters too deep for a conventional structure, a <a href="/wiki/Lightvessel" title="Lightvessel">lightship</a> might be used instead of a lighthouse, such as the former <a href="/wiki/United_States_lightship_Columbia_(WLV-604)" title="United States lightship Columbia (WLV-604)">lightship <i>Columbia</i></a>.  Most of these have now been replaced by fixed light platforms (such as <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Light" title="Ambrose Light">Ambrose Light</a>) similar to those used for offshore oil exploration.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png/220px-Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png" width="220" height="306" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png/330px-Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png/440px-Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png 2x" data-file-width="642" data-file-height="892" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lighthouse_lantern_room_with_Fresnel_lens.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lighthouse lantern room from mid-1800s</div></div></div>\n<p>While lighthouse buildings differ depending on the location and purpose, they tend to have common components.\n</p><p>A light station comprises the lighthouse tower and all outbuildings, such as the keeper\'s living quarters, fuel house, boathouse, and <a href="/wiki/Fog_horn" class="mw-redirect" title="Fog horn">fog-signaling</a> building. The Lighthouse itself consists of a tower structure supporting the lantern room where the light operates.\n</p><p>The lantern room is the glassed-in housing at the top of a lighthouse tower containing the lamp and lens. Its glass storm panes are supported by metal <i><a href="/wiki/Astragal" title="Astragal">Astragal</a></i> bars running vertically or diagonally. At the top of the lantern room is a stormproof ventilator designed to remove the smoke of the lamps and the heat that builds in the glass enclosure. A <a href="/wiki/Lightning_rod" title="Lightning rod">lightning rod</a> and grounding system connected to the metal cupola roof provides a safe conduit for any lightning strikes.\n</p><p>Immediately beneath the lantern room is usually a Watch Room or Service Room where fuel and other supplies were kept and where the keeper prepared the lanterns for the night and often stood watch. The clockworks (for rotating the lenses) were also located there. On a lighthouse tower, an open platform called the gallery is often located outside the watch room (called the Main Gallery) or Lantern Room (Lantern Gallery). This was mainly used for cleaning the outside of the windows of the Lantern Room.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lighthouses near to each other that are similar in shape are often painted in a unique pattern so they can easily be recognized during daylight, a marking known as a <a href="/wiki/Daymark" title="Daymark">daymark</a>. The black and white barber pole spiral pattern of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras_Lighthouse" title="Cape Hatteras Lighthouse">Cape Hatteras Lighthouse</a> is one example. <a href="/wiki/Race_Rocks_Light" title="Race Rocks Light">Race Rocks Light</a> in western Canada is painted in horizontal black and white bands to stand out against the horizon.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_lights">Range lights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Range lights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Range_lights" class="mw-redirect" title="Range lights">Range lights</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg/220px-Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg/330px-Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg/440px-Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Margaree_Harbour_Range_Lights.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Range Lights in <a href="/wiki/Margaree_Harbour,_Nova_Scotia" title="Margaree Harbour, Nova Scotia">Margaree Harbour, Nova Scotia</a>. When the vessel is on the correct course, the two lights line up above one another.</div></div></div>\n<p>Aligning two fixed points on land provides a navigator with a <a href="/wiki/Line_of_position" class="mw-redirect" title="Line of position">line of position</a> called a range in the U.S. and a <a href="/wiki/Navigational_transit" class="mw-redirect" title="Navigational transit">transit</a> in Britain. Ranges can be used to precisely align a vessel within a narrow channel such as in a river. With landmarks of a range illuminated with a set of fixed lighthouses, nighttime navigation is possible.\n</p><p>Such paired lighthouses are called <a href="/wiki/Leading_lights" title="Leading lights">range lights</a> in the U.S. and <a href="/wiki/Leading_lights" title="Leading lights">leading lights</a> in the United Kingdom.  The closer light is referred to as the beacon or front range; the furthest away is called the rear range. The rear range light is almost always taller than the front.\n</p><p>When the vessel is on the correct course, the two lights line up vertically. But when the observer is out of position, the difference in alignment indicates the proper direction of travel to correct the course.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">Maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the United States, lighthouses are maintained by the United States Coast Guard (USCG).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\nThose in England and Wales are looked after by <a href="/wiki/Trinity_House" title="Trinity House">Trinity House</a>; in Scotland, by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Lighthouse_Board" title="Northern Lighthouse Board">Northern Lighthouse Board</a>; and in Ireland by the <a href="/wiki/Commissioners_of_Irish_Lights" title="Commissioners of Irish Lights">Commissioners of Irish Lights</a>. In Canada, they are managed by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Coast_Guard" title="Canadian Coast Guard">Canadian Coast Guard</a>. In Australia, lighthouses are conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Maritime_Safety_Authority" title="Australian Maritime Safety Authority">Australian Maritime Safety Authority</a>.\n</p><p>The Soviet Union built a number of automated lighthouses powered by <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">radioisotope thermoelectric generators</a> in remote locations. They operated for long periods without external support with great reliability.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> However numerous installations deteriorated, were stolen, or vandalized. Some cannot be found due to poor record keeping.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation">Preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As lighthouses became less essential to navigation, many of their historic structures faced demolition or neglect. In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Lighthouse_Preservation_Act" title="National Historic Lighthouse Preservation Act">National Historic Lighthouse Preservation Act</a> of 2000 provides for the transfer of lighthouse structures to local governments and private non-profit groups, while the USCG continues to maintain the lamps and lenses. In Canada, the <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia_Lighthouse_Preservation_Society" title="Nova Scotia Lighthouse Preservation Society">Nova Scotia Lighthouse Preservation Society</a> won heritage status for <a href="/wiki/Sambro_Island_Lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Sambro Island Lighthouse">Sambro Island Lighthouse</a>, and sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Lighthouse_Protection_Act" title="Heritage Lighthouse Protection Act">Heritage Lighthouse Protection Act</a> to change Canadian federal laws to protect lighthouses.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many groups formed to restore and save lighthouses around the world. They include the <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Lighthouse_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="World Lighthouse Society (page does not exist)">World Lighthouse Society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Lighthouse_Society" title="United States Lighthouse Society">United States Lighthouse Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> A further international group is the Amateur Radio Lighthouse Society, which sends amateur radio operators to publicize the preservation of remote lighthouses throughout the world.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_good">Public good</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Public good">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Lighthouses were once regarded as an archetypal <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public good</a>, because ships could benefit from the light without being forced to pay. The Confederate States Constitution explicitly allowed public funds to be spent on navigation, including lighthouses.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lists_of_lighthouses" title="Lists of lighthouses">Lists of lighthouses</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lists_of_lightvessels" title="Lists of lightvessels">Lists of lightvessels</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Day_beacon" title="Day beacon">Day beacon</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lens_lantern" title="Lens lantern">Lens lantern</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pintsch_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Pintsch gas">Pintsch gas</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pharology" title="Pharology">Pharology</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Foghorn" title="Foghorn">Foghorn</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lighthouse_keeper" title="Lighthouse keeper">Lighthouse keeper</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lighthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<dl><dt>Notes</dt></dl>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 33em; -webkit-column-width: 33em; column-width: 33em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Trethewey, K.
