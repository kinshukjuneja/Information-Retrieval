visitors to <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales_Tourist_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Tourist Board">Wales Tourist Board</a> destinations in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The industry has been estimated to have an annual turnover of \xc2\xa33.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2005 <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Wales" title="Economy of Wales">economy of Wales</a> supporting over 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Service_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Service sector">service-sector</a> jobs, more than 8% of the country\'s workforce. The most popular activities undertaken by tourists in Wales were walking, shopping, hiking in the mountains and visiting historic attractions, museums and galleries. Over 1.1 million trips were made to Wales by overseas tourists in 2006. The main countries of origin of overseas visitors were the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The majority of tourism however, is from other parts of the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a> (predominantly <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The capital, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> is the most popular area in Wales for tourists, with 14.6&#160;million visitors in 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> which provides 26,300 jobs in the sector. In 2004, tourists spent the most money in <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Conwy" title="Conwy">Conwy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" There are three national parks: the <a href="/wiki/Brecon_Beacons_National_Park" title="Brecon Beacons National Park">Brecon Beacons National Park</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowdonia National Park">Snowdonia National Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire_Coast_National_Park" title="Pembrokeshire Coast National Park">Pembrokeshire Coast National Park</a>. Popular activities in the national parks include <a href="/wiki/Hill_walking" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill walking">hill walking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canoeing" title="Canoeing">canoeing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain biking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kayaking" title="Kayaking">kayaking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climbing" title="Climbing">climbing</a>. Wales is also becoming increasingly popular for \'extreme\' sports, such as <a href="/wiki/Surfing" title="Surfing">surfing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hang_gliding" title="Hang gliding">hang gliding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Downhill_cycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Downhill cycling">downhill cycling</a> (in which Wales hosts the \'Dragon Downhill Series\'). The terrain of Wales has also attracted the <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally Championship</a> (WRC). The <a href="/wiki/Wales_Rally_GB" title="Wales Rally GB">Wales Rally GB</a> is held annually. The 2005 Wales Rally GB saw the first WRC stage to be set indoors, at the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Stadium" title="Millennium Stadium">Millennium Stadium</a>.  In <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, the regenerated <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Bay" title="Cardiff Bay">Cardiff Bay</a> area is one of the most popular destinations.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>\' <a href="/wiki/History_of_Wales" title="History of Wales">history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh culture">culture</a> also attract tourists. The <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Welsh_Life" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum of Welsh Life">Museum of Welsh Life</a>, which focuses largely on the industrial past of Wales, is currently the most popular tourist attraction in Wales, attracting over 600,000&#160;visitors annually. The scars of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> and Wales\' <a href="/wiki/Industrial_heritage" title="Industrial heritage">industrial heritage</a> can still be seen on parts of the Welsh landscape today. Many other places of historical interest attract large numbers of tourists: for example the many <a href="/wiki/Castles_in_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Castles in Wales">castles</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Caernarfon_Castle" title="Caernarfon Castle">Caernarfon Castle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caerphilly_Castle" title="Caerphilly Castle">Caerphilly Castle</a>\xe2\x80\x94most of them built to enable or to consolidate the English conquest of Wales, during the reign of the English king <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>.\n</p><p>Another increasingly popular reason for visiting Wales, as with the rest of the United Kingdom\xe2\x80\x94especially for those from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>\xe2\x80\x94is <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a>, with many visitors coming to Wales to explore their family and ancestral roots. 1.8 million <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> citizens are estimated to have Welsh ancestral roots, including former presidents, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> and <a The area became widely known after <i>Observations on the River Wye</i> by the Reverend <a href="/wiki/William_Gilpin_(clergyman)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Gilpin (clergyman)">William Gilpin</a> was published in 1782. The first illustrated tour guide to be published in Britain, it helped travellers locate and enjoy the most <a href="/wiki/Picturesque" title="Picturesque">picturesque</a> places, such as <a href="/wiki/Tintern_Abbey" title="Tintern Abbey">Tintern Abbey</a>. A particular attraction of the Wye Valley was its river scenery, and the many guidebooks, engravings and paintings encouraged a continuing steady stream of visitors which grew after the building of a new <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Turnpike road">turnpike road</a> up the valley in 1822 and the opening of a rail line in 1865.\n</p><p>However, when <a href="/wiki/George_Borrow" title="George Borrow">George Borrow</a> wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Wild_Wales" title="Wild Wales">Wild Wales</a></i> in 1862 it is clear from his descriptions that the notion of tourism in more mountainous parts of Wales hardly existed except for the most intrepid traveller. Indeed, he records that many locals regarded the mountainous and wild landscapes as monstrous and ugly rather than romantic or picturesque. However, later in the 19th century the concept of mountains and valleys as both interesting and visually pleasing landscapes developed; and <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a> in particular benefited, as towns and villages such as <a href="/wiki/Betws-y-Coed" title="Betws-y-Coed">Betws-y-Coed</a> developed to accommodate the increasing numbers of visitors.\n</p><p>The changing face of industrialisation in the <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">Midlands</a>, with increasing pay rates and the provision of <a href="/wiki/Paid_time_off" title="Paid time off">paid time off</a> for industrial workers, allowed many people to enjoy an annual holiday for the first time. Many chose to visit <a href="/wiki/Seaside_resort" title="Seaside resort">seaside resorts</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Llandudno" title="Llandudno">Llandudno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prestatyn" title="Prestatyn">Prestatyn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhyl" title="Rhyl">Rhyl</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barmouth" title="Barmouth">Barmouth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mid_Wales" title="Mid Wales">Mid Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barry,_Vale_of_Glamorgan" title="Barry, Vale of Glamorgan">Barry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tenby" title="Tenby">Tenby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penarth" title="Penarth">Penarth</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>, which among others were developed to respond to this trend.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tourism_in_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> is connected to the rest of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (its principal tourist market) by <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a> and domestic flights. The <a href="/wiki/M4_Motorway" class="mw-redirect" title="M4 Motorway">M4 Motorway</a> connects South and West Wales with <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">Southern England</a> and <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. The <a href="/wiki/A55_road" title="A55 road">A55 road</a> is the principal route linking <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a> with <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a>.\n</p><p>There are several <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Wales#Rail_links_with_England" title="Transport in Wales">rail links</a> between England and Wales, and trains run to <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Central_railway_station" title="Cardiff Central railway station">Cardiff Central</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newport_railway_station" title="Newport railway station">Newport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swansea_railway_station" title="Swansea railway station">Swansea</a> from <a href="/wiki/Paddington_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Paddington railway station">London Paddington</a>, and to Cardiff Central from <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Harbour_railway_station" title="Portsmouth Harbour railway station">Portsmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucester_railway_station" title="Gloucester railway station">Gloucester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_New_Street_railway_station" title="Birmingham New Street railway station">Birmingham New Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Piccadilly_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Piccadilly railway station">Manchester Picadilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Nottingham railway station">Nottingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_railway_station" title="Newcastle railway station">Newcastle</a>.\nCardiff Central offers connections to the <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_Valleys" title="South Wales Valleys">South Wales Valleys</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Glamorgan" title="Vale of Glamorgan">Vale of Glamorgan</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Wales" title="West Wales">West Wales</a>, and Swansea offers connections to <a href="/wiki/West_Wales_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="West Wales Line">West Wales</a>. There are direct services from <a href="/wiki/Euston_railway_station" title="Euston railway station">London Euston</a> and Birmingham to <a href="/wiki/Holyhead_railway_station" title="Holyhead railway station">Holyhead</a> via the North Wales Coast. Internally, there are services from Cardiff to Holyhead.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Airport" title="Cardiff Airport">Cardiff Airport</a> has domestic and some international flights. Some <a href="/wiki/Budget_airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Budget airlines">budget airlines</a> operate from Cardiff to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Internally, there are twice daily return flights from Cardiff to Anglesey with <a href="/wiki/Highland_Airways" title="Highland Airways">Highland Airways</a>. Many daily flight operate to and from other major UK cities such as <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_Airport" title="Newcastle Airport">Newcastle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Airport" title="Edinburgh Airport">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Airport" title="Glasgow Airport">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Airport" title="Aberdeen Airport">Aberdeen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belfast_International_Airport" title="Belfast International Airport">Belfast</a>.\n</p><p>The country is also connected to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> by car ferry services operating daily from Welsh ports, principally <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a>.
