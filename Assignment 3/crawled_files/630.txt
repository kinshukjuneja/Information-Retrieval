href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> has three <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_legal_systems" title="List of national legal systems">legal systems</a>, each of which applies to a particular geographical area.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a> and (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_law" title="Welsh law">Welsh law</a>) applies in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_law" title="Northern Ireland law">Northern Ireland law</a> applies in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a> applies in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. While these three systems diverge in the more detailed rules, there are also substantive fields of law which apply across the United Kingdom.\n</p><p>The United Kingdom does not have a single legal system because it was created by the political union of previously independent countries. Article 19 of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Union" title="Treaty of Union">Treaty of Union</a>, put into effect by the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union</a> in 1707, created the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a>, but guaranteed the continued existence of Scotland\'s separate legal system.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union</a> of 1800, which combined Great Britain and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a> into the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>, contained no equivalent provisions but preserved the principle of separate courts to be held in Ireland, of which the part called Northern Ireland remains part of the United Kingdom.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> is the highest court in the land for all criminal and civil cases in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> and Northern Ireland, and for all civil cases in Scots law.<sup id="cite_ref-supreme_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-supreme-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Supreme Court came into being in October 2009, replacing the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">Appellate Committee of the House of Lords</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_England_and_Wales" title="Courts of England and Wales">court system</a> is headed by the <a href="/wiki/Senior_Courts_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior Courts of England and Wales">Senior Courts of England and Wales</a>, consisting of the Court of Appeal, the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> (for civil cases) and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a> (for criminal cases). The <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Courts of Northern Ireland">Courts of Northern Ireland</a> follow the same pattern.  In Scotland the chief <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Scotland" title="Courts of Scotland">courts</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a>, for civil cases, and the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a>, for criminal cases.  <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_court" title="Sheriff court">Sheriff courts</a> have no equivalent outside Scotland, as they deal with both criminal and civil caseloads.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> is the highest court of appeal for several independent <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries, the <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territories">British overseas territories</a>, and the British <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>. There are also immigration courts with UK-wide jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x94 the <a href="/wiki/Asylum_and_Immigration_Tribunal" title="Asylum and Immigration Tribunal">Asylum and Immigration Tribunal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Special_Immigration_Appeals_Commission" title="Special Immigration Appeals Commission">Special Immigration Appeals Commission</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Employment_tribunal" title="Employment tribunal">Employment tribunals</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Employment_Appeal_Tribunal" title="Employment Appeal Tribunal">Employment Appeal Tribunal</a> have jurisdiction throughout <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, but not Northern Ireland.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/European_Union_law" title="European Union law">European Union law</a> is actively transposed into the UK legal systems under the UK parliament\'s law-making power, in fulfillment of its EU treaty obligations, not inherently by acts of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="European Union Parliament">European Union Parliament</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Three legal systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Legal_systems_of_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal systems of the world">Legal systems of the world</a></div>\n<p>There are three distinct legal jurisdictions in the United Kingdom: <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Each has its own <a href="/wiki/Legal_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal system">legal system</a>, distinct history and origins.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_law">English law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: English law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/English_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="English Law">English Law</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg/220px-Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg/330px-Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg/440px-Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_courts_of_justice.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Courts_of_Justice" title="Royal Courts of Justice">Royal Courts of Justice</a> in London, home of the <a href="/wiki/Senior_Courts_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior Courts of England and Wales">Senior Courts of England and Wales</a></div></div></div>\n<p>"English law" is a term of art. It refers to the <a href="/wiki/Legal_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal system">legal system</a> administered by the courts in England and Wales, which rule on both civil and criminal matters. English law is renowned as being the mother of the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> and is based on those principles. English law can be described as having its own legal doctrine, distinct from <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> legal systems since 1189.\n</p><p>There has been no major <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codification</a> of the law, rather the law is developed by <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judges</a> in <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">court</a>, applying <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom legislation">statute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a> and case-by-case reasoning to give explanatory judgments of the relevant legal principles. These judgments are binding in future similar cases (<i><a href="/wiki/Stare_decisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Stare decisis">stare decisis</a></i>), and for this reason are often reported.\n</p><p>In the early centuries, the justices and judges were responsible for adapting the <a href="/wiki/Forms_of_action" class="mw-redirect" title="Forms of action">Writ system</a> to meet everyday needs, applying a mixture of precedent and common sense to build up a body of internally consistent law, e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Law_Merchant" class="mw-redirect" title="Law Merchant">Law Merchant</a> began in the Pie-Powder Courts, see <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Piepowder" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Piepowder">Court of Piepowder</a> (a corruption of the <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">Law French</a> "pieds-poudr\xc3\xa9s" or "dusty feet", meaning ad hoc marketplace courts). As <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a> developed in strength, subject to the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">separation of powers</a>, legislation gradually overtook judicial law-making, so that today judges are only able to innovate in certain very narrowly defined areas. The year 1189 was defined in 1276 as being the boundary of <a href="/wiki/Time_immemorial" title="Time immemorial">time immemorial</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_England_and_Wales" title="Courts of England and Wales">courts of England and Wales</a> are headed by the <a href="/wiki/Senior_Courts_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior Courts of England and Wales">Senior Courts of England and Wales</a>, consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> (for civil cases) and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a> (for criminal cases). The Supreme Court is the highest court in the land for both criminal and civil appeal cases in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and any decision it makes is binding on every other court in the same jurisdiction, and often has persuasive effect in its other jurisdictions. On appeal, a court may overrule the decisions of its inferior courts, such as county courts (civil) and magistrates\' courts (criminal). The High Court may also quash on <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a> both administrative decisions of the Government and delegated legislation. The ultimate body of appeal for all criminal and civil cases in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> (and Northern Ireland, and for all civil cases in Scots law) is the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>, which took over this function from the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">Appellate Committee of the House of Lords</a> (usually just referred to as "The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>") in October 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-supreme_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-supreme-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union,_in_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Union, in 1707">Acts of Union, in 1707</a> English law became one of two legal systems in different parts of the same, united, kingdom and has been influenced by Scots law, most notably in the development and integration of the law merchant by <a href="/wiki/William_Murray,_1st_Earl_of_Mansfield" title="William Murray, 1st Earl of Mansfield">Lord Mansfield</a> and in time the development of the law of <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">negligence</a>.  Scottish influence may have influenced the abolition of the <a href="/wiki/Form_of_action" title="Form of action">forms of action</a> in the nineteenth century and extensive procedural reforms in the twentieth.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland_law">Northern Ireland law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Courts_of_Justice,_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Royal_Courts_of_Justice%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg/220px-Royal_Courts_of_Justice%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Royal_Courts_of_Justice%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg/330px-Royal_Courts_of_Justice%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Royal_Courts_of_Justice%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg/440px-Royal_Courts_of_Justice%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="479" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Courts_of_Justice,_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1304238.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Royal Courts of Justice in Belfast, Northern Ireland.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_law" title="Northern Ireland law">Northern Ireland law</a></div>\n<p>The law of Northern Ireland is a <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> system. It is administered by the courts of Northern Ireland, with ultimate appeal to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> in both civil and criminal matters. The law of Northern Ireland is closely similar to English law, the rules of common law having been imported into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> under English rule.  However, there are still important differences.\n</p><p>The sources of the law of Northern Ireland are Irish common law, and statute law. Of the latter, statutes of the Parliaments of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Ireland</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> are in force, and latterly statutes of the devolved Assembly. The <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Courts of Northern Ireland">courts of Northern Ireland</a> are headed by the Court of Judicature of Northern Ireland, consisting of the Northern Ireland Court of Appeal, the Northern Ireland High Court of Justice and the Northern Ireland Crown Court. Below that are county courts and magistrates\' courts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scots_law">Scots law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scots law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parliament_House,_Edinburgh.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Parliament_House%2C_Edinburgh.JPG/220px-Parliament_House%2C_Edinburgh.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Parliament_House%2C_Edinburgh.JPG/330px-Parliament_House%2C_Edinburgh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Parliament_House%2C_Edinburgh.JPG/440px-Parliament_House%2C_Edinburgh.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parliament_House,_Edinburgh.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Parliament_House,_Edinburgh" title="Parliament House, Edinburgh">Parliament House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> is the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Courts_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Supreme Courts of Scotland">Supreme Courts of Scotland</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Scots law is a unique <a href="/wiki/Legal_systems_of_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal systems of the world">legal system</a> with an ancient basis in <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>. Grounded in <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">uncodified</a> <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> dating back to the <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Corpus Juris Civilis</a></i>, it also features elements of <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> with <a href="/wiki/Legal_institutions_of_Scotland_in_the_High_Middle_Ages" title="Legal institutions of Scotland in the High Middle Ages">medieval</a> sources. Thus <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> has a <a href="/wiki/Legal_pluralism" title="Legal pluralism">pluralistic</a>, or \'mixed\', legal system, comparable to that of <a href="/wiki/Law_of_South_Africa" title="Law of South Africa">South Africa</a>, and, to a lesser degree, the partly <a href="/wiki/Legal_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal code">codified</a> pluralistic systems of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana#Law_and_government" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Quebec" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of Quebec">Quebec</a>. Since the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> under the 1707 <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union</a>, Scots Law has shared a legislature with <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, and while each retained fundamentally different legal systems, the 1707 Union brought English &amp; Welsh influence upon Scots law, and vice versa. In recent years, Scots law has also been affected by both <a href="/wiki/European_law" class="mw-redirect" title="European law">European law</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Rome" title="Treaty of Rome">Treaty of Rome</a>, and also following the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, which legislates within domestic <a href="/wiki/Reserved_matters_(Westminster)#Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Reserved matters (Westminster)">areas of legislative competence</a>.\n</p><p>The chief courts are the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session_(Scotland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Session (Scotland)">Court of Session</a>, for civil cases,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a>, for criminal cases.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> serves as the highest court of appeal for civil cases under Scots law, with leave to appeal from the Court of Session not required as a general rule.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheriff Court">Sheriff courts</a> deal with most civil and criminal cases including conducting criminal <a href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trial (law)">trials</a> with a jury, known as sheriff solemn court, or with a sheriff and no jury, known as sheriff summary Court. The sheriff courts provide a local court service with 49 sheriff courts organised across six <a href="/wiki/Sheriffdom" title="Sheriffdom">sheriffdoms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Scots legal system is unique in having three possible <a href="/wiki/Verdict" title="Verdict">verdicts</a> for a criminal trial: "<a href="/wiki/Guilt_(law)" title="Guilt (law)">guilty</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Acquittal" title="Acquittal">not guilty</a>" and "<i><a href="/wiki/Not_proven" title="Not proven">not proven</a></i>". Both "not guilty" and "not proven" result in an <a href="/wiki/Acquittal" title="Acquittal">acquittal</a> with no possibility of <a href="/wiki/Trial_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trial (law)">retrial</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Secretary_for_Justice" title="Cabinet Secretary for Justice">Cabinet Secretary for Justice</a> is the member of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> responsible for <a href="/wiki/Police_Scotland" title="Police Scotland">Police Scotland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Scotland" title="Courts of Scotland">courts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_criminal_law" title="Scottish criminal law">criminal justice</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Prison_Service" title="Scottish Prison Service">Scottish Prison Service</a>, which manages the prisons in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_Law">Welsh Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Law">Welsh Law</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cardiff_Crown_Court.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cardiff_Crown_Court.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The main entrance to Cardiff Crown Court</div></div></div>\n<p>Welsh law is the primary and secondary legislation generated by the National Assembly for Wales, using devolved authority granted in the Government of Wales Act 2006 and in effect since May 2007. Each piece of Welsh legislation is known as an Act of the Assembly.\n</p><p>However, as there is no criminal law within contemporary Welsh law, Wales is not generally considered a fourth jurisdiction of the United Kingdom. This is because the judiciary and the courts follow England and Wales law, which is made by the United Kingdom Parliament, and is not specific to Wales. Although Welsh law is recognised as separate in operation, this is not sufficient for Wales to constitute a separate legal jurisdiction.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> is <a href="/wiki/Bicameral" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicameral">bicameral</a>, with an <a href="/wiki/Upper_house" title="Upper house">upper house</a>, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Lower_house" title="Lower house">lower house</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>. The House of Lords includes two different types of members: the <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Lords Spiritual</a> (the senior <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Lords_Temporal" title="Lords Temporal">Lords Temporal</a> (members of the <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">Peerage</a>); its members are not elected by the population at large. The House of Commons is a democratically elected chamber. The two Houses meet in separate chambers in the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a> (commonly known as the "Houses of Parliament"), in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. By <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_custom)" title="Constitutional convention (political custom)">constitutional convention</a>, all <a href="/wiki/Political_minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Political minister">government ministers</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>, are members of the House of Commons or House of Lords.\n</p><p>Parliament evolved from the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">early medieval</a> councils that advised the sovereigns of <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="List of monarchs of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of monarchs of Scotland">Scotland</a>. In theory, power is vested not in Parliament, but in the "<a href="/wiki/Queen-in-Parliament" title="Queen-in-Parliament">Queen-in-Parliament</a>" (or "King-in-Parliament"). The Queen-in-Parliament is, according to the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">parliamentary sovereignty</a>, completely sovereign with the power to make and unmake any law other than to bind itself.\n</p><p>In modern times, real power is vested in the House of Commons; the Sovereign acts only as a figurehead and the powers of the House of Lords are greatly limited.  The parliament retains some law-making powers for some jurisdictions outside of the United Kingdom proper.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland_Assembly">Northern Ireland href="#cite_note-irish-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster Scots (linguistics)">Ulster Scots</a>: <i>Norlin Airlann Semmlie</i>)<sup It sits at <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Buildings_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Parliament Buildings (Northern Ireland)">Parliament Buildings</a> at <a href="/wiki/Stormont,_Belfast" class="mw-redirect" title="Stormont, Belfast">Stormont</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>.\n</p><p>The latest incarnation of the Assembly was established under the <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Belfast Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> of 1998, an accord aimed at bringing an end to Northern Ireland\'s violent 30-year <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">Troubles</a>. As a result, 73 MSPs represent individual geographical <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_constituencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Parliament constituencies">constituencies</a> elected by the <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">plurality voting system</a> ("first past the post"), with a further 56 returned from eight <a href="/wiki/Additional_member_system_(Scottish_Parliament)" class="mw-redirect" title="Additional member system (Scottish Parliament)">additional member</a> regions, each electing seven MSPs.<sup id="cite_ref-ConstituencyMSPs_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConstituencyMSPs-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Scottish Parliament, as it was created by devolution and an act of parliament, does not get its legislative powers by virtue of sovereignty or by virtue of `being the Scottish Parliament`. Rather, it legally exists as a subset of Westminster and derives it`s powers as such.\n</p><p>The original <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of early thirteenth century until the Kingdom of Scotland merged with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament of England</a>, to form the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain" title="Parliament of Great Britain">Parliament of The National Assembly, first elected in 1999, is a democratically elected body of 60 members who are known as Assembly Members or AMs. Members are elected for four-year terms under the Additional Member System of proportional representation. As a result, 40 AMs represent individual geographical constituencies elected by the plurality voting system ("first past the post"), with a further 20 returned from five additional member regions, each region electing four AMs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_legal_systems">Related legal systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Related legal systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After centuries of settlement and conquest, the United Kingdom has legal relationships to many territories outside its borders.  These include <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign states</a> that do and do not share a monarch and judicial institutions with the UK, and <a href="/wiki/Dependent_territory" title="Dependent territory">dependencies</a> where the UK government, parliament, and crown do retain some power.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_sovereigns_with_British_legal_history">Independent sovereigns with British legal history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Independent sovereigns with British legal history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_that_have_gained_independence_from_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of countries that have gained independence from the United Kingdom">countries that have gained independence from the UK</a> are no longer subject to the British parliament, monarchy, nor courts.  They consist of a mix of <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republics</a> (for example <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>) and local <a href="/wiki/Monarchies" class="mw-redirect" title="Monarchies">monarchies</a> (for example <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>) with no relationship to the royal <a href="/wiki/House_of_Windsor" title="House of Windsor">House of Windsor</a>.  The colonies and possessions were created and separated from the UK under a wide variety of circumstances, resulting in a spectrum of influence of British law in domestic law.\n</p><p>At the strongly influenced end of the spectrum, for example, is the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Proclamation_of_1763" title="Royal Proclamation of 1763">Royal Proclamation of 1763</a> explicitly applied <a href="/wiki/English_Common_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="English Common Law">English Common Law</a> to all British overseas colonies, and affirmed some degree of local law-making.  The <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> resulted in a unilateral separation recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Paris_(1783)" title="Peace of Paris (1783)">Peace of Paris (1783)</a>, but the English system continued to be used as the basis for court decisions.  Over time, it was modified by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>, state constitutions, and federal and state court decisions particular to their own jurisdictions.  Colonial land grants of the British kings still remained relevant in some later boundary disputes of the former <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a>, though adjudicated by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>.  But some parts of the United States not subject to British rule have laws based on other traditions, such as French civil law in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, and Native American law in <a href="/wiki/Tribal_sovereignty_in_the_United_States" title="Tribal sovereignty in the United States">areas of tribal sovereignty</a>.\n</p><p>Some countries were granted independence by an act of the UK parliament (for example, the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster 1931</a>) and have likewise diverged from UK law either under or after British rule. An example at the other end of the spectrum, despite occasional control for geopolitical reasons, British law had little impact on the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Afghanistan" title="Law of Afghanistan">law of Afghanistan</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_sovereigns_with_shared_institutions">Independent sovereigns with shared institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Independent sovereigns with shared institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By special agreement, the UK-based <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> acts as the court of highest appeal for three former colonies which are now republics (<a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, and for constitutional rights, <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>) and has a special consultation agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Brunei" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Brunei">Sultan of Brunei</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commonwealth_realms">Commonwealth realms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Commonwealth realms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a> (for example <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>) are former colonies which are now sovereign states fully independent of the UK parliament. However, they share other legal institutions with the UK, to varying degrees.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> remains the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitutional monarch">constitutional monarch</a> of each realm in its own right, and retains a limited set of powers (<a href="/wiki/Royal_prerogative" title="Royal prerogative">royal prerogative</a>) to be exercised either personally or through a local <a href="/wiki/Viceroy" title="Viceroy">viceroy</a>.  Most powers are irrevocably delegated to a parliament more or less modeled on the <a href="/wiki/Westminster_system" title="Westminster system">Westminster system</a>.\n</p><p>Crimes in Commonwealth realms are prosecuted in the name of the crown, and the crown remains the notional arbiter of disputes.  In some realms appeals may be directed to the monarch as a last resort. Adjudication of these appeals is delegated to <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> which draws judges from the UK and across the Commonwealth.  In other realms, a domestic court has been made the highest court of appeal.  (See <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council#Overseas_jurisdiction" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council \xc2\xa7&#160;Overseas jurisdiction</a> for full list.)\n</p><p>The "Imperial" Privy Council based in England advises the shared monarch on the use of <a href="/wiki/Royal_prerogative" title="Royal prerogative">royal prerogatives</a> and parliament-authorized powers in the form of <a href="/wiki/Orders_in_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Orders in Council">Orders in Council</a>, and can also issue its own delegated <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Orders of Council">Orders of Council</a>.  In some countries, a domestic council performs this function, namely:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Federal_Executive_Council_(Australia)" title="Federal Executive Council (Australia)">Federal Executive Council (Australia)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Privy_Council_for_Canada" title="Queen&#39;s Privy Council for Canada">Queen\'s Privy Council for Canada</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_the_Falkland_Islands" title="Executive Council of the Falkland Islands">Executive Council of the Falkland Islands</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_New_Zealand" title="Executive Council of New Zealand">Executive Council of New Zealand</a></li></ul>\n<p>Similar to other former colonies, Commonwealth realms also share a common legal history with the UK.  For example, Canada underwent a long period of patriation of its constitution, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Act,_1867" title="Constitution Act, 1867">Constitution Act, 1867</a> and ending with the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Act,_1982" title="Constitution Act, 1982">Constitution Act, 1982</a>.  Like their southern neighbors, the Proclamation of 1763 extended English Common law to all the Canadian colonies, including <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> (which being Scottish might have operated under <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  (<a href="/wiki/French_civil_law" class="mw-redirect" title="French civil law">French civil law</a> was later re-applied to <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_dependencies">Crown dependencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Crown dependencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> are held by the British Monarch by virtue of inheriting the <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> title of <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Normandy" title="Duke of Normandy">Duke of Normandy</a>.  These were never part of England, Wales, Scotland, Ireland, or the United Kingdom. Most of the historical <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Duchy of Normandy</a> is on the European continent and was conquered by France.  The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> is held by the British Monarch by virtue of inheriting the feudal title of <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Mann" title="Lord of Mann">Lord of Mann</a>.  It was previously ruled by Norway, England, and Scotland, before the feudal rights were purchased from Scottish dukes (after English-Scottish unification) by the United Kingdom in 1765.  Due to local opposition, it was never merged into England as previously planned, and remains a distinct possession of the monarchy.\n</p><p>Each jurisdiction has a locally elected parliament with broad but not unlimited autonomy.  The British monarchy retains responsibility for defense, citizenship law, and foreign affairs of the dependencies, and has delegated these responsibilities to the UK government and parliament.  The UK parliament generally acts in consultation or gains the consent of the local government when passing laws that have effect in the dependencies.  Residents of the dependencies do not have representation in the UK parliament. UK law does not apply to the dependencies unless explicitly stated, and such laws are almost always executed by the monarch in the form of an <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a>.  Whether the UK parliament retains the power to pass laws against the will of the local governments is disputed, and was tested with the <a href="/wiki/Marine,_%26c.,_Broadcasting_(Offences)_Act_1967" title="Marine, &amp;c., Broadcasting (Offences) Act 1967">Marine, &amp;c., Broadcasting (Offences) Act 1967</a>.\n</p><p>Legal cases may be appealed to the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>.  Residents are treated the same as residents of the UK for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British nationality law</a>, though local governments control local immigration and employment.  This makes citizens of the British dependencies EU citizens, but the exchange of people and goods with the EU and UK is subject to <a href="/wiki/Special_member_state_territories_and_the_European_Union#Special_cases" title="Special member state territories and the European Union">special arrangements</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Overseas_Territories">British Overseas Territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: British Overseas Territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a></div>\n<p>Though not considered internal to the boundaries of United Kingdom, the UK maintains control over British Overseas Territories.  Unlike Commonwealth realms, BOTs fall within the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">Monarchy of the United Kingdom</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> is the court of final appeal.  Three of the BOT are uninhabited, and <a href="/wiki/Akrotiri_and_Dhekelia" title="Akrotiri and Dhekelia">Akrotiri and Dhekelia</a> is military property; in these places, the UK government rules directly and on all matters.\n</p><p>The inhabited British Overseas Territories do not have representation in the UK parliament, and are thus on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_list_of_Non-Self-Governing_Territories" title="United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories">United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories</a>.  Residents of Gibraltar, as the only BOT which is part of the European Union, vote for a representative in the European Parliament in the <a href="/wiki/South_West_England_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="South West England (European Parliament constituency)">South West England</a> district.  All citizens of British Overseas Territories are EU citizens, even though <a href="/wiki/European_Union_law" title="European Union law">European Union law</a> only applies in Gibraltar and the United Kingdom proper.\n</p><p>The inhabited territories each have their own legal system (based largely on English common law), with autonomy varying considerably with the size of the population.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, Gibraltar, and the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> are autonomously governed by their locally elected parliaments, with the UK responsible only for defense and foreign affairs (and granting limited autonomy to local governments to have relations with other countries and international organizations).  On the sparsely populated <a href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands" title="Pitcairn Islands">Pitcairn Islands</a>, the representative of the UK government has nearly unlimited powers.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">Citizenship and nationality law</a> is governed by the UK parliament, but immigration is controlled by local governments.  The UK parliament retains the ultimate legislative power, and ensures <a href="/wiki/Good_governance" title="Good governance">good governance</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
