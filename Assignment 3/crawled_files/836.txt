For the proposed veto of Catholic bishops in the 19th-century United Kingdom, see <a href="/wiki/Royal_veto_of_the_appointment_of_bishops" title="Royal veto of the appointment of bishops">Royal veto of the appointment of bishops</a>.</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the requirement in the United Kingdom for the monarch to consent to the marriage of first six persons in line to the throne, see <a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_Crown_Act_2013" title="Succession to the Crown Act 2013">Succession to the Crown Act 2013</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RoyalVisitSenate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/RoyalVisitSenate.jpg/220px-RoyalVisitSenate.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/RoyalVisitSenate.jpg/330px-RoyalVisitSenate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/RoyalVisitSenate.jpg/440px-RoyalVisitSenate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="751" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RoyalVisitSenate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/King_George_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="King George VI">King George VI</a>, accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Bowes-Lyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon">Queen Elizabeth</a>, grants royal assent to laws in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Canada" title="Senate of Canada">Canadian Senate</a>, 19 May 1939</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Royal assent</b> or <b>sanction</b> is the method by which a country\'s <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> (possibly through a delegated official) formally approves an act of that nation\'s parliament. In certain nations, such assent makes the act law (<a href="/wiki/Promulgation" title="Promulgation">promulgation</a>) while in other nations assent is distinct from promulgation. In the vast majority of contemporary monarchies, this act is considered to be little more than a formality; even in those nations which still permit their monarchs to withhold royal assent (such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a>), the monarch almost never does so, save in a dire political emergency or upon the advice of their government. While the power to <a href="/wiki/Veto" title="Veto">withhold royal assent</a> was once exercised often in <a href="/wiki/Monarchies_in_Europe" title="Monarchies in Europe">European monarchies</a>, it is exceedingly rare in the modern, democratic political atmosphere that has developed there since the 18th century.\n</p><p>Royal assent is sometimes associated with elaborate ceremonies. In the United Kingdom, for instance, the sovereign may appear personally in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> or may appoint <a href="/wiki/Lords_Commissioners" title="Lords Commissioners">Lords Commissioners</a>, who announce that royal assent has been granted at a ceremony held at the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a> for this purpose. However, royal assent is usually granted less ceremonially by <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>. In other nations, such as <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Australia" title="Governor-General of Australia">governor-general</a> merely signs the bill. In Canada, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">governor general</a> may give assent either in person at a ceremony held in the Senate or by a written declaration notifying parliament of his or her agreement to the bill.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent_Act_1967" title="Royal Assent Act 1967">Royal Assent Act 1967</a></div>\n<p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent_by_Commission_Act_1541" title="Royal Assent by Commission Act 1541">Royal Assent by Commission Act of 1541</a> became law, assent was always required to be given by the sovereign in person before Parliament.\n</p><p>The last time royal assent was given by the sovereign in person in Parliament was in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> at a prorogation on 12 August 1854.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Act was repealed and replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent_Act_1967" title="Royal Assent Act 1967">Royal Assent Act 1967</a>. However section 1(2) of that Act does not prevent the sovereign from declaring assent in person if he or she so desires.\n</p><p>Royal assent is the final step required for a parliamentary bill to become law. Once a bill is presented to the sovereign or the sovereign\'s representative, he or she has the following formal options:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the sovereign may grant royal assent, thereby making the bill an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a>.</li>\n<li>the sovereign may (in theory) delay the bill\'s assent through the use of his or her (theoretical) <a href="/wiki/Reserve_power#United_Kingdom" title="Reserve power">reserve powers</a>, thereby vetoing the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>the sovereign may (in theory) refuse royal assent on the (hypothetical) advice of his or her ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Under modern constitutional conventions, the sovereign acts on the advice of his or her ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Since these ministers most often enjoy the support of parliament and obtain the passage of bills, it is improbable that they would advise the sovereign to withhold assent. Hence, in modern practice, royal assent is never withheld.<sup id="cite_ref-bennion1981_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bennion1981-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The sovereign is generally believed not to legally have the power to withhold assent from a bill against the advice of ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The last bill that was refused assent by the sovereign (on the advice of ministers) was the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Militia_Bill" title="Scottish Militia Bill">Scottish Militia Bill</a> during <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne\'s</a> reign in 1708.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_development">Historical development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Originally, legislative power was exercised by the sovereign acting on the advice of the <i><a href="/wiki/Curia_regis" title="Curia regis">Curia Regis</a></i>, or Royal Council, in which important magnates and clerics participated and which evolved into parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Membership of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Model_Parliament" title="Model Parliament">Model Parliament</a> included bishops, abbots, earls, barons, two knights from each <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shire</a> and two burgesses from each <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">borough</a>. In 1265, <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_6th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester">the Earl of Leicester</a> <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort%27s_Parliament" title="Simon de Montfort&#39;s Parliament">irregularly called a full parliament</a> without royal authorisation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The body eventually came to be divided into two branches: bishops, abbots, earls, and barons formed the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, while the shire and borough representatives formed the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The King would seek the advice and consent of both houses before making any law. During <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">Henry VI</a>\'s reign, it became regular practice for the two houses to originate legislation in the form of bills, which would not become law unless the sovereign\'s assent was obtained, as the sovereign was, and still remains, the enactor of laws. Hence, all Acts include the clause "Be it enacted by the Queen\'s (King\'s) most Excellent Majesty, by and with the advice and consent of the <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual" title="Lords Spiritual">Lords Spiritual</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lords_Temporal" title="Lords Temporal">Temporal</a>, and Commons, in this present Parliament assembled, and by the authority of the same, as follows...".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949" title="Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949">Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949</a> provide a second potential preamble if the House of Lords were to be excluded from the process.\n</p><p>The power of parliament to pass bills was often thwarted by monarchs. <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> dissolved parliament in 1629, after it passed motions and bills critical of\xe2\x80\x94and seeking to restrict\xe2\x80\x94his arbitrary exercise of power. During the eleven years of <a href="/wiki/Personal_rule_of_Charles_I,_1629%E2%80%931640" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal rule of Charles I, 1629\xe2\x80\x931640">personal rule</a> that followed, Charles performed legally dubious actions such as raising taxes without Parliament\'s approval.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, it was accepted that parliament should be summoned to meet regularly, but it was still commonplace for monarchs to refuse royal assent to bills. In 1678, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> withheld his assent from a bill "for preserving the Peace of the Kingdom by raising the <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">Militia</a>, and continuing them in Duty for Two and Forty Days,"<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> suggesting that he, not parliament, should control the militia.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The last Stuart monarch, <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Anne</a>, similarly withheld on 11 March 1708, on the advice of her ministers, her assent to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Militia_Bill" title="Scottish Militia Bill">Scottish Militia Bill</a>. No monarch has since withheld royal assent on a bill passed by the British parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the rule of the succeeding <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hanover" title="House of Hanover">Hanoverian dynasty</a>, power was gradually exercised more by parliament and the government. The first Hanoverian monarch, <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">George I</a>, relied on his ministers to a greater extent than had previous monarchs. Later Hanoverian monarchs attempted to restore royal control over legislation: <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV</a> both openly opposed <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Emancipation" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic Emancipation">Catholic Emancipation</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Giv_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giv-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and asserted that to grant assent to a Catholic emancipation bill would violate the <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_British_monarch" title="Coronation of the British monarch">Coronation Oath</a>, which required the sovereign to preserve and protect the established <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> from Papal domination, and would grant rights to individuals who were in league with a foreign power which did not recognise their legitimacy. However, George IV reluctantly granted his assent upon the advice of his ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-Giv_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giv-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Thus, as the concept of ministerial responsibility has evolved, the power to withhold royal assent has fallen into disuse, both in the United Kingdom and in the other Commonwealth realms.\n</p><p>In 1914, <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> did take legal advice on withholding royal assent from the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_Act_1914" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Rule Act 1914">Government of Ireland Bill</a>, a highly contentious piece of legislation that the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> government intended to push through parliament by means of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act 1911</a>. The King decided that he should not withhold assent without "convincing evidence that it would avert a national disaster, or at least have a tranquillising effect on the distracting conditions of the time".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Royal assent is the final stage in the legislative process for acts of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish parliament">Scottish parliament</a>. The process is governed by sections 28, 32, and 33 of the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> After a bill has been passed, the <a href="/wiki/Presiding_Officer_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Presiding Officer of the Scottish Parliament">Presiding Officer of the Scottish Parliament</a> submits it to the monarch for royal assent after a four-week period, during which the <a href="/wiki/Advocate_General_for_Scotland" title="Advocate General for Scotland">Advocate General for Scotland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Advocate" title="Lord Advocate">Lord Advocate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Attorney General">Attorney General</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> may refer the bill to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> (prior to 1 October 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>) for review of its legality. href="/wiki/Church_of_England_Assembly_(Powers)_Act_1919" title="Church of England Assembly (Powers) Act 1919">Church of England Assembly (Powers) Act 1919</a> a <a of England">General Synod of the Church of England</a> becomes law once it has received royal assent in the same way as an Act of Parliament.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Jersey_and_Guernsey">Jersey and Guernsey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Jersey and Guernsey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>The lieutenant governors of the <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick_of_Jersey" class="mw-redirect" title="Bailiwick of Jersey">Bailiwick of Jersey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick_of_Guernsey" title="Bailiwick of Guernsey">Bailiwick and Islands of Guernsey</a> do not by and under their own authority grant assent, nor, as proxies, as the British crown\'s representative, deliver royal assent, to legislation emanating from the respective legislatures of these islands.\n</p><p>The States of Jersey Law 2005 abolishes the power of the lieutenant governor to directly impose a formal veto to a resolution of the States of Jersey.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The monarch of the United Kingdom, sitting in (together with members of the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cabinet of the United Kingdom">Cabinet</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> of the United Kingdom of the day, and who are also members of) the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a>, grants the equivalent of the royal assent to Jersey and Guernsey legislation (under the formula, or other words to the effect: <i>Her Majesty, having taken the report into consideration, was pleased, by and with the advice of Her Privy Council, to approve and ratify this Act (a copy of which is annexed to this Order) and to order that it, together with this Order, shall be entered on the Register of the Island of Jersey (or of the Islands of Guernsey) and observed accordingly. Her Majesty\'s Officers in the Island (or Islands), and all other whom it may concern, are therefore to take notice of Her Majesty\'s Order and to proceed accordingly</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The equivalent of the royal assent is formally granted or formally refused on the formal advice of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Committee_of_Council_for_the_Affairs_of_Jersey_and_Guernsey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Committee of Council for the Affairs of Jersey and Guernsey (page does not exist)">Committee of Council for the Affairs of Jersey and Guernsey</a> in pursuance of Queen Elizabeth II\'s order-in-council of 22 February 1952. A recent example when the equivalent of the royal assent was refused was in 2007, concerning reforms to the constitution of the <a href="/wiki/Sark#Chief_Pleas" title="Sark">Chief Pleas of Sark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> (A revised version of the proposed reforms was subsequently given the equivalent of the royal assent.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>)\n</p><p>In 2011, campaigners against a law that sought to reduce the number of senators in the states of Jersey petitioned the Privy Council to advise the Queen to refuse the equivalent of the royal assent.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> An Order in Council of 13 July 2011 established new rules for the consideration of petitions against the granting of the equivalent of the royal assent.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Legislation in Jersey and Guernsey entitled "Laws" would seem to require the formal equivalent of the royal assent from the British monarch sitting in the Privy Council; other legislation such as regulations and orders (in Jersey) and ordinances (in Guernsey) would not seem to require such formal equivalent of the royal assent.\n</p><p>There is a proposal that the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Guernsey" title="Lieutenant Governor of Guernsey">Lieutenant Governor of Guernsey</a> should be granted the delegated power of granting the equivalent of the royal assent, formally from the monarch and the Privy Council, to enable laws to be granted formal approval within six weeks if no objection was raised, rather than having to formally refer every law for formal consideration in London and then formal approval also (usually) in London. "At present there is a situation where Channel Island law-making depends, ultimately, on the UK government of the day, unelected &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93; by the islands."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Isle_of_Man">Isle of Man</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Isle of Man">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>Special procedures apply to legislation passed by the <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>. Before the lordship of the Island was purchased by the British Crown in 1765 (the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Isle_of_Man#Revestment" title="History of the Isle of Man">Revestment</a>), the assent of the Lord of Mann to a bill was signified by letter to the governor.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> After 1765, the equivalent of the royal assent was at first signified by the letter from the Secretary of State to the governor;<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> but, during the <a href="/wiki/British_Regency" class="mw-redirect" title="British Regency">British Regency</a>, the practice began of granting the equivalent of the royal assent to Manx legislation by Orders in Council,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> which continues to this day, though limited to exceptional cases since 1981.\n</p><p>In 1981, an Order in Council delegated to the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Lieutenant Governor of the Isle of Man">lieutenant governor</a> the power to grant royal assent to bills passed by <a href="/wiki/Tynwald" title="Tynwald">Tynwald</a>. The lieutenant governor must however refer any bill impacting on reserved powers (defence, foreign relations, nationality law, the relationship between the island and the United Kingdom and any matters relating to the monarch) to the British government for advice, on which he is required to act.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The above procedures are not sufficient to cause an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Tynwald" title="Act of Tynwald">Act of Tynwald</a> to come into full force of law. By ancient custom, an Act did not come into force until it had been <i>promulgated</i> at an open-air sitting of the Tynwald, historically held on <a href="/wiki/Tynwald_Hill" class="mw-redirect" title="Tynwald Hill">Tynwald Hill</a> at St John\'s on St John\'s Day (24 June), but, since the adoption of the Gregorian calendar in 1753,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> on 5 July (or on the following Monday<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> if 5 July is a Saturday or Sunday). <i>Promulgation</i> originally consisted of the reading of the Act in English and <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a>; but after 1865 the reading of the title of the Act and a summary of each section was sufficient.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> This was reduced in 1895 to the titles and a memorandum of the object and purport of the Act,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and since 1988 only the short title and a summary of the long title have been read.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An emergency procedure enabling an Act to come into force at the same moment as the receipt of the equivalent of the royal assent, when it is being announced at an ordinary sitting of the Tynwald, subject to its being promulgated within 12 months, was introduced in 1916;<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> since 1988, this has been the normal procedure, but an Act ceases to have effect unless promulgated within 18 months after the equivalent of the royal assent is announced in the Tynwald.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Sodor_and_Man" title="Diocese of Sodor and Man">Sodor and Man</a> <a href="/wiki/Diocesan_Synod" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocesan Synod">Diocesan Synod</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_York" title="Province of York">Province of York</a> has had power to enact measures making provision "with respect to any matter concerning the Church of England in the Island". If approved by the Tynwald, a measure "shall have the force and effect of an Act of Tynwald upon the Royal Assent thereto being announced to the Tynwald".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Between 1979 and 1993, the Synod had similar powers, but limited to the extension to the Isle of Man of measures of the <a href="/wiki/General_Synod_of_the_Church_of_England" title="General Synod of the Church of England">General Synod</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Before 1994, the equivalent of the royal assent was granted by Order in Council, as for a bill, but the power to grant the equivalent of the royal assent to measures has now been delegated to the lieutenant governor.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> A Measure does not require <i>promulgation</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="British_overseas_territories">British overseas territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: British overseas territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The governors (or the acting governors) of <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territories">British overseas territories</a> grant, withhold, or formally refuse the grant of their own <i>Governor\'s assent</i>, under their own official personal authority as governors, for "colonial" or local legislation. Although the governor\'s assent is also normally granted, this is altogether different in nature to the royal assent.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Commonwealth_realms">Other Commonwealth realms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other Commonwealth realms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a> other than the UK, royal assent is granted or withheld either by the realm\'s sovereign or, more frequently, by the representative of the sovereign, the <a href="/wiki/Governor-General#Commonwealth_realms" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor-General">governor-general</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federated</a> realms, assent in each state, province, or territory is granted or withheld by the representatives of the sovereign. In Australia, this is the <a href="/wiki/Governors_of_the_Australian_states" title="Governors of the Australian states">governors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="States of Australia">states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Administrator_(Australia)#Administrators_of_a_Territory" title="Administrator (Australia)">administrators</a> of the territories, or the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor General of Australia">governor-general</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>. For Canada, this is the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_governor_(Canada)" title="Lieutenant governor (Canada)">lieutenant governors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">provinces</a>. A lieutenant governor may defer assent to the governor general,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> and the governor general may defer assent to federal bills to the sovereign.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>If the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General of Canada</a> is unable to give assent, it can be done by either the <a href="/wiki/Deputy_of_the_Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Deputy of the Governor General of Canada">Deputy of the Governor General of Canada</a>\xe2\x80\x94the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_Canada" title="Chief Justice of Canada">Chief Justice of Canada</a>\xe2\x80\x94or <a href="/wiki/List_of_Justices_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="List of Justices of the Supreme Court of Canada">another justice of the Supreme Court of Canada</a>. It is not actually necessary for the governor general to sign a bill passed by a legislature, the signature being merely an attestation. In each case, the parliament must be apprised of the granting of assent before the bill is considered to have become law.<sup id="cite_ref-Senate53_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Senate53-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Two methods are available: the sovereign\'s representatives may grant assent in the presence of both houses of parliament; alternatively, each house may be notified separately, usually by the speaker of that house. However, though both houses must be notified on the same day, notice to the House of Commons while it is not in session may be given by way of publishing a special issue of the <i>Journals of the House of Commons</i>, whereas the Senate must be sitting and the governor general\'s letter read aloud by the speaker.<sup id="cite_ref-Senate53_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Senate53-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At both state and federal realms in Australia, assent is used as the means of enforcing a <a href="/wiki/Referendums_in_Australia" title="Referendums in Australia">referendum</a> that is required. This is done by providing that it will not be lawful to even submit the law for viceregal assent unless and until it has been approved by the required percentage of the voting populace at a referendum.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While royal assent has not been withheld in the United Kingdom since 1708, it has often been withheld in British colonies and former colonies by governors acting on royal instructions. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">United States Declaration of Independence</a>, colonists complained that George III "has refused his Assent to Laws, the most wholesome and necessary for the public good [and] has forbidden his Governors to pass Laws of immediate and pressing importance, unless suspended in their operation till his Assent should be obtained; and when so suspended, he has utterly neglected to attend to them."<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Even after colonies such as Canada, Australia, New Zealand, the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_South_Africa" title="Union of South Africa">Union of South Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> were granted <a href="/wiki/Responsible_government" title="Responsible government">responsible government</a>, the British government continued to sometimes advise governors-general on the granting of assent; assent was also occasionally reserved to allow the British government to examine a bill before advising the governor-general.\n</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Declaration_of_1926" title="Balfour Declaration of 1926">Balfour Declaration of 1926</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster 1931</a></i>, all the Commonwealth realms have been sovereign kingdoms, the monarch and governors-general acting solely on the advice of the local ministers, who generally maintain the support of the legislature and are the ones who secure the passage of bills. They therefore are unlikely to advise the sovereign, or his or her representative, to withhold assent. The power to withhold the royal assent was exercised by <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Alberta" title="Lieutenant Governor of Alberta">Alberta\'s lieutenant governor</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_C._Bowen" title="John C. Bowen">John C. Bowen</a>, in 1937, in respect of three bills passed in the legislature dominated by <a href="/wiki/William_Aberhart" title="William Aberhart">William Aberhart</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Social_Credit" class="mw-redirect" title="Social Credit">Social Credit</a> party. Two bills sought to put banks under the authority of the province, thereby interfering with the federal government\'s powers. The third, the Accurate News and Information Bill, purported to force newspapers to print government rebuttals to stories to which the provincial cabinet objected. The unconstitutionality of all three bills was later confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a> and by the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Australia, a technical issue arose with the royal assent in both 1976 and 2001. In 1976, a bill originating in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_House_of_Representatives" title="Australian House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> was mistakenly submitted to the Governor-General and assented to. However, it was later discovered that it had not been passed by the Senate. The error arose because two bills of the same title had originated from the House. The Governor-General revoked the first assent, before assenting to the bill which had actually passed the Senate and the House. The same procedure was followed to correct a similar error which arose in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceremony">Ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_2">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reform_Act_1832.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Reform_Act_1832.jpg/220px-Reform_Act_1832.jpg" width="220" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Reform_Act_1832.jpg/330px-Reform_Act_1832.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Reform_Act_1832.jpg/440px-Reform_Act_1832.jpg 2x" data-file-width="499" data-file-height="631" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reform_Act_1832.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Start of the parchment roll of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a>, with the clerk\'s record of the royal assent of King <a href="/wiki/William_IV_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William IV of England">William IV</a> written above the bill, reading in full <i>Le Roy le Veult soit baill\xc3\xa9 aux Seigneurs. A cette Bille avecque des amendemens les Seigneurs sont assentuz. A ces Amendemens les Communes sont assentuz.</i></div></div></div>\n<p>In the United Kingdom, a bill is presented for royal assent after it has passed all the required stages in both the House of Commons and the House of Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Under the Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949, the House of Commons may, under certain circumstances, direct that a bill be presented for assent despite lack of passage by the House of Lords. Officially, assent is granted by the sovereign or by <a href="/wiki/Lords_Commissioners" title="Lords Commissioners">Lords Commissioners</a> authorised to act by <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>. It may be granted in parliament or outside parliament; in the latter case, each house must be separately notified before the bill takes effect.\nThe Clerk of the Parliaments, an official of the House of Lords, traditionally states a formula in <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">Law French</a>, indicating the sovereign\'s decision. The granting of royal assent to a <a href="/wiki/Supply_bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Supply bill">supply bill</a> is indicated with the words "La Reyne remercie ses bons sujets, accepte leur benevolence, et ainsi le veult",<sup id="cite_ref-bennion1981_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bennion1981-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> translated as "The Queen thanks her good subjects, accepts their bounty, and wills it so." For other public or <a href="/wiki/Private_bill" title="Private bill">private bills</a>, the formula is simply "<a href="/wiki/La_Reyne_le_veult" title="La Reyne le veult">La Reyne le veult</a>" ("the Queen wills it"). For <a href="/wiki/Local_and_personal_Acts_of_Parliament_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Local and personal Acts of Parliament in the United Kingdom">personal bills</a>, the phrase is "Soit fait comme il est d\xc3\xa9sir\xc3\xa9" ("let it be as it is desired"). The appropriate formula for withholding assent is the euphemistic "La Reyne s\'avisera" ("the Queen will consider it").<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> When the sovereign is male, <i>Le Roy</i> is substituted for <i>La Reyne</i>.\nBefore the reign of Henry VIII, the sovereign always granted his or her assent in person. The sovereign, wearing the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_State_Crown" title="Imperial State Crown">Imperial State Crown</a>, would be seated on the throne in the Lords chamber, surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Herald" title="Herald">heralds</a> and members of the royal court\xe2\x80\x94a scene that nowadays is repeated only at the annual <a href="/wiki/State_Opening_of_Parliament" title="State Opening of Parliament">State Opening of Parliament</a>. The Commons, led by their <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Speaker of the British House of Commons">speaker</a>, would listen from the Bar of the Lords, just outside the chamber. The Clerk of the Parliaments presented the bills awaiting assent to the monarch, save that supply bills were traditionally brought up by the speaker. The Clerk of the Crown, standing on the sovereign\'s right, then read aloud the titles of the bills (in earlier times, the entire text of the bills). The Clerk of the Parliaments, standing on the sovereign\'s left, responded by stating the appropriate Norman French formula.<sup id="cite_ref-so29_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-so29-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hans_Holbein,_the_Younger,_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Hans_Holbein%2C_the_Younger%2C_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Hans_Holbein%2C_the_Younger%2C_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="220" height="309" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Hans_Holbein%2C_the_Younger%2C_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Hans_Holbein%2C_the_Younger%2C_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Hans_Holbein%2C_the_Younger%2C_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Hans_Holbein%2C_the_Younger%2C_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2994" data-file-height="4203" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hans_Holbein,_the_Younger,_Around_1497-1543_-_Portrait_of_Henry_VIII_of_England_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> introduced a new method of granting royal assent.</div></div></div>\n<p>A new device for granting assent was created during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">King Henry VIII</a>. In 1542, Henry sought to execute his fifth wife, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Howard" title="Catherine Howard">Catherine Howard</a>, whom he accused of committing adultery; the execution was to be authorised not after a trial but by a <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_attainder" title="Bill of attainder">bill of attainder</a>, to which he would have to personally assent after listening to the entire text. Henry decided that "the repetition of so grievous a Story and the recital of so infamous a crime" in his presence "might reopen a Wound already closing in the Royal Bosom".<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, parliament inserted a clause into the Act of Attainder, providing that assent granted by Commissioners "is and ever was and ever shall be, as good" as assent granted by the sovereign personally.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The procedure was used only five times during the 16th century, but more often during the 17th and 18th centuries, especially when George III\'s health began to deteriorate. <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> became the last monarch to personally grant assent in 1854.<sup id="cite_ref-Richardson_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\nWhen granting assent by commission, the sovereign authorises three or more (normally five) lords who are <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Counsellors</a> to declare assent in his or her name. The <a href="/wiki/Lords_Commissioners" title="Lords Commissioners">Lords Commissioners</a>, as the monarch\'s representatives are known, wear scarlet parliamentary robes and sit on a bench between the throne and the <a href="/wiki/Woolsack" title="Woolsack">Woolsack</a>. The Lords Reading Clerk reads the commission aloud; the senior commissioner then states, "My Lords, in obedience to Her Majesty\'s Commands, and by virtue of the Commission which has been now read, We do declare and notify to you, the Lords Spiritual and Temporal and Commons in Parliament assembled, that Her Majesty has given Her Royal Assent to the several Acts in the Commission mentioned."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\nDuring the 1960s, the ceremony of assenting by commission was discontinued and is now only employed once a year, at the end of the annual parliamentary session. In 1960, the Gentleman Usher of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Rod" title="Black Rod">Black Rod</a> arrived to summon the House of Commons during a heated debate and several members protested against the disruption by refusing to attend the ceremony. The debacle was repeated in 1965; this time, when the Speaker left the chair to go to the House of Lords, some members continued to make speeches. As a result, the Royal Assent Act 1967 was passed, creating an additional form for the granting of royal assent. As the attorney-general explained, "there has been a good deal of resentment not only at the loss of Parliamentary time that has been involved but at the breaking of the thread of a possibly eloquent speech and the disruption of a debate that may be caused."<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The granting of assent by the monarch in person, or by commission, is still possible, but this third form is used on a day-to-day basis.\nUnder the Royal Assent Act 1967, royal assent can be granted by the sovereign in writing, by means of letters patent, that are presented to the presiding officer of each house of parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-so29_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-so29-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Then, the presiding officer makes a formal, but simple statement to the house, acquainting each house that royal assent has been granted to the acts mentioned. Thus, unlike the granting of royal assent by the monarch in person or by Royal Commissioners, the method created by the Royal Assent Act 1967 does not require both houses to meet jointly for the purpose of receiving the notice of royal assent. The standard text of the letters patent is set out in The Crown Office (Forms and Proclamations Rules) Order 1992,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> with minor amendments in 2000. In practice this remains the standard method, a fact that is belied by the wording of the letters patent for the appointment of the Royal Commissioners and by the wording of the letters patent for the granting of royal assent in writing under the 1967 Act ("... And forasmuch as We cannot at this time be present in the Higher House of Our said Parliament being the accustomed place for giving Our Royal Assent...").<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\nWhen the act is assented to by the sovereign in person, or by empowered Royal Commissioners, royal assent is considered given at the moment when the assent is declared in the presence of both houses jointly assembled. When the procedure created by the Royal Assent Act 1967 is followed, assent is considered granted when the presiding officers of both houses, having received the letters patent from the king or queen signifying the assent, have notified their respective house of the grant of royal assent.<sup id="cite_ref-bennion1981_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bennion1981-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Thus, if each presiding officer makes the announcement at a different time (for instance because one house is not sitting on a certain date), assent is regarded as effective when the second announcement is made. This is important because, under British Law, unless there is any provision to the contrary, an act takes effect on the date on which it receives royal assent and that date is not regarded as being the date when the letters patent are signed, or when they are delivered to the presiding officers of each house, but the date on which both houses have been formally acquainted of the assent.\nIndependently of the method used to signify royal assent, it is the responsibility of the Clerk of the Parliaments, once the assent has been duly notified to both houses, not only to endorse the act in the name of the monarch with the formal Norman French formula, but to certify that assent has been granted.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The clerk signs one authentic copy of the bill and inserts the date (in English) on which the assent was notified to the two houses after the title of the act.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> When an act is published, the signature of the clerk is omitted, as is the Norman French formula, should the endorsement have been made in writing. However, the date on which the assent was notified is printed in brackets.\n</p><p>The House of Commons speaker John Bercow said the EU withdrawal bill, passed by both houses of parliament in 2018, had been signed into law by the monarch, her Majesty the Queen Elizabeth II. \xe2\x80\x9cI have to notify the House in accordance with the Royal Assent Act 1967 that her Majesty has signified her royal assent to the following acts ... European Union Withdrawal Act 2018,\xe2\x80\x9d Commons Speaker John Bercow told lawmakers during a session of the house. <sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_other_Commonwealth_realms">In the other Commonwealth realms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In the other Commonwealth realms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In Commonwealth realms, assent may be granted by the sovereign in person, by the governor-general in person, or by a deputy acting for the governor-general. In all of the realms, however, assent is more often granted or signified outside the legislature, with each house being notified separately. \n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia_and_New_Zealand">Australia and New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Australia and New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>In Australia, the formal ceremony of granting assent in parliament has not been regularly used since the early 20th century. Now, the bill is sent to the governor-general\'s residence by the house in which it originated. The governor-general then signs the bill, sending messages to the president of the Senate and the speaker of the House of Representatives, who notify their respective houses of the governor-general\'s action.<sup id="cite_ref-Journal_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Journal-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> A similar practice is followed in New Zealand, where the governor-general has not granted the Royal Assent in person in parliament since 1875.<sup id="cite_ref-Journal_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Journal-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg/220px-Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg/330px-Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg/440px-Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kevin_MacLeod_in_Canadian_Senate_Chamber_2009.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Usher_of_the_Black_Rod_(Canada)" title="Usher of the Black Rod (Canada)">Usher of the Black Rod</a> (then <a href="/wiki/Kevin_S._MacLeod" title="Kevin S. MacLeod">Kevin McLeod</a>) is a key element of the Royal Assent ceremony.</div></div></div>\n<p>In Canada, the traditional ceremony for granting assent in parliament was regularly used until the 21st century, long after it had been discontinued in the United Kingdom and other Commonwealth realms. One result, conceived as part of a string of acts intended to demonstrate Canada\'s status as an independent realm, was that King <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a> personally assented to nine bills of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Canada" title="Parliament of Canada">Canadian parliament</a> during his <a href="/wiki/1939_royal_tour_of_Canada" title="1939 royal tour of Canada">1939 tour of Canada</a>\xe2\x80\x9485 years after his great-grandmother, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>, had last granted royal assent personally in the United Kingdom. Under the Royal Assent Act 2002, however, the alternative practice of granting assent in writing, with each house being notified separately (the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_Senate_of_Canada" title="Speaker of the Senate of Canada">Speaker of the Senate</a> or a representative reads to the senators the letters from the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">governor general</a> regarding the written declaration of Royal Assent<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>), was brought into force. As the act also provides, royal assent is to be signified\xe2\x80\x94by the governor general, or, more often, by a deputy, usually a Justice of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Richardson_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> at least twice each calendar year: for the first appropriation measure and for at least one other act, usually the first non-appropriation measure passed. However, the act provides that a grant of royal assent is not rendered invalid by a failure to employ the traditional ceremony where required.\n</p><p>The Royal Assent ceremony takes place in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Canada" title="Senate of Canada">Senate</a>, as the sovereign is traditionally barred from the House of Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> On the day of the event, the Speaker of the Senate will read to the chamber a notice from the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_to_the_Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Secretary to the Governor General of Canada">secretary to the governor general</a> indicating when the viceroy or a deputy thereof will arrive. The Senate thereafter cannot adjourn until after the ceremony. The speaker moves to sit beside the throne; the Mace Bearer, with mace in hand, stands adjacent to him or her; and the governor general enters to take the speaker\'s chair. The <a href="/wiki/Usher_of_the_Black_Rod_(Canada)" title="Usher of the Black Rod (Canada)">Usher of the Black Rod</a> is then commanded by the speaker to summon the members of parliament, who follow Black Rod back to the Senate, the <a href="/wiki/Serjeant-at-arms#Canada" title="Serjeant-at-arms">Sergeant-at-Arms</a> carrying the mace of the House of Commons. In the Senate, those from the commons stand behind the bar, while Black Rod proceeds to stand next to the governor general, who then nods his or her head to signify Royal Assent to the presented bills (which do not include appropriations bills). Once the list of bills is complete, the Clerk of the Senate states: "in Her Majesty\'s name, His [or Her] Excellency the Governor General [or the deputy] doth assent to these bills." If there are any appropriation bills to receive Royal Assent, the Speaker of the House of Commons will read their titles and the Senate clerk repeats them to the governor general, who nods his or her head to communicate Royal Assent. When these bills have all been assented to, the Clerk of the Senate recites "in Her Majesty\'s name, His [or Her] Excellency the Governor General [or the deputy] thanks her loyal subjects, accepts their benevolence and assents to these bills." The governor general or his or her deputy then depart parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In some monarchies\xe2\x80\x94such as <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, Japan, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, Spain, and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>\xe2\x80\x94promulgation is required as well as royal assent. In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, however, the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Sweden" title="Monarchy of Sweden">monarch</a> is <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitution of Sweden">since 1975</a> removed from the process and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Sweden" title="Government of Sweden">Government</a> (i.e the cabinet chaired by the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Sweden" title="Prime Minister of Sweden">Prime Minister</a>) officially promulgates laws. In both cases, however, the process of assent and promulgation is usually a formality, whether by constitutional convention or by an explicit provision of the constitution.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium">Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to Article 109 of the constitution: "The King <i>sanctions</i> and <i>promulgates</i> laws". In Belgium, the royal assent is called <i>sanction royale</i> / <i>koninklijke bekrachtiging</i> (Royal Sanction), and is granted by the King signing the proposed statute. The Belgian constitution requires a theoretically possible refusal of royal sanction to be countersigned\xe2\x80\x94as any other act of the monarch\xe2\x80\x94by a minister responsible before the House of Representatives. The monarch promulgates the law, meaning that he or she formally orders that the law be officially published and executed. In 1990, when <a href="/wiki/Baudouin_of_Belgium" title="Baudouin of Belgium">King Baudouin</a> advised his cabinet he could not, in conscience, sign a bill decriminalising abortion (a refusal patently not covered by a responsible minister), the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_federal_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian federal government">Council of Ministers</a>, at the King\'s own request, declared Baudouin incapable of exercising his powers. In accordance with the Belgian constitution, upon the declaration of the sovereign\'s incapacity, the Council of Ministers assumed the powers of the head of state until parliament could rule on the King\'s incapacity and appoint a regent. The bill was then assented to by all members of the Council of Ministers "on behalf of the Belgian People".<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> In a joint meeting, both houses of parliament declared the King capable of exercising his powers again the next day.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jordan">Jordan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Jordan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The constitution of <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> grants its monarch the right to withhold assent to laws passed by its parliament. Article 93 of that document gives the Jordanian sovereign six months to sign or veto any legislation sent to him from the National Assembly; if he vetoes it within that timeframe, the assembly may override his veto by a two-thirds vote of both houses; otherwise, the law does not go into effect (but it may be reconsidered in the next session of the assembly). If the monarch fails to act within six months of the bill being presented to him, it becomes law without his signature.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Liechtenstein">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to Article 9 of the Constitution of <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a>, "every law shall require the sanction of the Reigning Prince to attain legal force." Liechtenstein allows its monarch to withhold royal assent of his own will.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Prince_Hans_Adam_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Hans Adam II">Prince Hans Adam II</a>, in an unprecedented move for the constitutional monarchy, refused to give royal assent to a bill legalising abortion, he pushed for a bill to give him sweeping powers in the government beyond only ceremonial matters, including the power to appoint judges. The bill passed and the Prince now has many additional powers, including the power to withhold royal assent on his own accord.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Luxembourg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While Article 34 of the constitution of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> formerly required the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Luxembourg" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duke of Luxembourg">grand duke or duchess</a> to <i>sanction</i> and <i>promulgate</i> a new law for it to take effect, the required sanction was removed in 2008, after Grand Duke <a href="/wiki/Henri,_Grand_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Henri, Grand Duke of Luxembourg">Henri</a> informed his <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Luxembourg" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister of Luxembourg">prime minister</a> that he could not in good conscience assent to a bill to permit <a href="/wiki/Euthanasia" title="Euthanasia">euthanasia</a> in the country. The subsequent constitutional amendment removed the need for assent while retaining the need for the Grand Duke to promulgate new laws.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> The Grand-Duke\'s signature is still required, but does not imply assent, only <a href="/wiki/Promulgation" title="Promulgation">promulgation</a> (announcement that the law has been enacted by Parliament).<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The Grand-Duke did sign the Euthanasia Act under this new constitutional arrangement.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Netherlands">The Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: The Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Royal assent in the Netherlands is required, under article 87 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Netherlands" title="Constitution of the Netherlands">Dutch constitution</a>, for a bill to become law. After a law has been approved by the Council of Ministers and has received a positive advice from the advisory <a href="/wiki/Council_of_State_of_the_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of State of the Netherlands">Council of State</a>, the government then sends it to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_(Netherlands)" title="House of Representatives (Netherlands)">lower house</a> of <a href="/wiki/States_General_of_the_Netherlands" title="States General of the Netherlands">parliament</a> in the name of the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_Netherlands" title="Monarchy of the Netherlands">monarch</a> with the following text:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Aan de Tweede Kamer der Staten-Generaal<br />\n</p><p>Wij bieden U hiernevens ter overweging aan een voorstel van wet houdende [topic of the law] in verband met [reason and purpose of the law]<br />\nDe memorie van toelichting (en bijlagen) die het wetsvoorstel vergezelt, bevat de gronden waarop het rust.<br />\nEn hiermede bevelen Wij U in Godes heilige bescherming.<br />\n[location and date] [signed <a href="/wiki/Willem-Alexander_of_the_Netherlands" title="Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands">Willem-Alexander</a>].\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>To the Second Chamber of the States General<i><br /></i>\n</p><p>Herewith We offer to You for consideration a proposal of law containing [topic of the law] in relation to [reason and purpose of the law]<br />\nThe explanatory memorandum (and addenda) that accompanies the proposal of law, contains the grounds on which it is based.<br />\nAnd herewith We command You in God\'s holy protection.<br />\n[location and date] [signed <a href="/wiki/Willem-Alexander_of_the_Netherlands" title="Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands">Willem-Alexander</a>].\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>After the House of Representatives has debated the law, it either approves it and sends it to the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate of the Netherlands">Senate</a> with the text "The Second Chamber of the States General sends the following approved proposal of law to the First Chamber", or it rejects it and returns it to the government with the text "The Second Chamber of the States General has rejected the accompanying proposal of law." If the upper house then approves the law, it sends it back to the government with the text "To the King, The States General have accepted the proposal of law as it is offered here."\n</p><p>The government, consisting of the monarch and the ministers, will then usually approve the proposal and the sovereign and one of the ministers signs the proposal with the addition of an <a href="/wiki/Enacting_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Enacting clause">enacting clause</a>, thereafter notifying the States General that "The King assents to the proposal." It has happened in exceptional circumstances that the government does not approve a law that has been passed in parliament. In such a case, neither the monarch nor a minister will sign the bill, notifying the States General that "The King will keep the proposal under advisement." A law that has received royal assent will be published in the State Magazine, with the original being kept in the archives of the King\'s Offices.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Articles 77\xe2\x80\x9379 of the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwegian Constitution">Norwegian Constitution</a> specifically grant the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Norway" title="Monarchy of Norway">monarch of Norway</a> the right to withhold royal assent from any bill passed by the <a href="/wiki/Storting" title="Storting">Storting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NW_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NW-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> Should the sovereign ever choose to exercise this privilege, Article 79 provides a means by which his veto may be over-ridden: "If a Bill has been passed unaltered by two sessions of the Storting, constituted after two separate successive elections and separated from each other by at least two intervening sessions of the Storting, without a divergent Bill having been passed by any Storting in the period between the first and last adoption, and it is then submitted to the King with a petition that His Majesty shall not refuse his assent to a Bill which, after the most mature deliberation, the Storting considers to be beneficial, it shall become law even if the Royal Assent is not accorded before the Storting goes into recess."<sup id="cite_ref-NW_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NW-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In Part II of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">1978 Spanish constitution</a>, among provisions concerning the Crown, Article 62(a) invests the <i>sanction</i> (i.e. Royal Assent) and <i>promulgation</i> of laws with the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Spain" title="Monarchy of Spain">monarch of Spain</a>. Chapter 2 of Part III, concerning the Drafting of Bills, outlines the method by which bills are passed. According to Article 91, the monarch shall give his or her assent and promulgate the new law within fifteen days of passage of a bill by the Cortes Generales. Article 92 invests the monarch with the right to call for a referendum, on the advice of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Spain" title="Prime Minister of Spain">president of the government</a> (commonly referred to in English as the prime minister) and the authorisation of the cortes.\n</p><p>No provision within the constitution grants the monarch an ability to veto legislation directly; however, no provision prohibits the sovereign from withholding royal assent, which effectively constitutes a veto. When the Spanish media asked King <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos</a> if he would endorse the bill legalising <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Spain" title="Same-sex marriage in Spain">same-sex marriages</a>, he answered "<i>Soy el Rey de Espa\xc3\xb1a y no el de B\xc3\xa9lgica</i>" ("I am the King of Spain and not that of Belgium")\xe2\x80\x94a reference to King <a href="/wiki/Baudouin_I_of_Belgium" class="mw-redirect" title="Baudouin I of Belgium">Baudouin I of Belgium</a>, who had refused to sign the Belgian law legalising abortion.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> The King gave royal assent to Law 13/2005 on 1 July 2005; the law was <a href="/wiki/Gazette" title="Gazette">gazetted</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bolet%C3%ADn_Oficial_del_Estado" title="Bolet\xc3\xadn Oficial del Estado">Bolet\xc3\xadn Oficial del Estado</a></i> on 2 July and came into effect on 3 July 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, in 2010, King Juan Carlos gave royal assent to a law permitting abortion.\n</p><p>If the Spanish monarch ever refused in conscience to grant royal assent, a procedure similar to the Belgian handling of King Baudouin\'s objection would not be possible under the current constitution. If the sovereign were ever declared incapable of discharging royal authority, his or her powers would not be transferred to the Cabinet, pending the parliamentary appointment of a regency. Instead, the constitution mandates the next person of age in the line of succession would immediately become regent. Therefore, had Juan Carlos followed the Belgian example in 2005 or 2010, a declaration of incapacity would have transferred power to <a href="/wiki/Felipe_VI_of_Spain" title="Felipe VI of Spain">Felipe</a>, then the heir apparent.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tonga">Tonga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_assent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Tonga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Articles 41 and 68 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Tonga" title="Constitution of Tonga">constitution</a> empower the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Tonga" class="mw-redirect" title="Monarchy of Tonga">sovereign</a> to withhold royal assent from bills adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Tonga" title="Legislative Assembly of Tonga">Legislative Assembly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, the kingdom moved towards greater democracy, with King <a href="/wiki/George_Tupou_V" title="George Tupou V">George Tupou V</a> saying that he would be guided by his <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Tonga" title="Prime Minister of Tonga">prime minister</a> in the exercising of his powers. Nonetheless, this does not preclude an independent royal decision to exercise a right of veto. In November 2011, the assembly adopted an Arms and Ammunitions (Amendment) Bill, which reduced the possible criminal sentences for the illicit possession of firearms. The bill was adopted by ten votes to eight. Two members of the assembly had recently been charged with the illicit possession of firearms. The Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Siale%CA%BBataongo_Tu%CA%BBivakan%C5%8D" title="Siale\xca\xbbataongo Tu\xca\xbbivakan\xc5\x8d">Lord Tu\xca\xbbivakan\xc5\x8d</a>, voted in favour of the amendment.
