Jersey lies in the Bay of <a href="/wiki/Mont_Saint-Michel" class="mw-redirect" title="Mont Saint-Michel">Mont Saint-Michel</a> and is the largest of the Channel Islands. It has enjoyed  self-government since the division of the Duchy of Normandy in 1204.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg/220px-Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg/330px-Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg/440px-Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dolmen_Faldouet_Jersey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>La Pouquelaye de Faldouet was constructed on a site on the east coast looking across to the <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">Cotentin Peninsula</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Archaeology_of_the_Channel_Islands" title="Archaeology of the Channel Islands">Archaeology of the Channel Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Dolmens" class="mw-redirect" title="Jersey Dolmens">Jersey Dolmens</a></div>\n<p>The earliest evidence of human activity in Jersey dates to about 250,000 years ago (before Jersey became an island) when bands of nomadic hunters used the caves at <a href="/wiki/La_Cotte_de_St_Brelade" title="La Cotte de St Brelade">La Cotte de St Brelade</a> as a base for hunting <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woolly_rhinoceros" title="Woolly rhinoceros">woolly rhinoceros</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Rising sea levels resulted in it has been an island for approximately 6,000 years and at its current extremes it measures 10 miles east to west and six miles north to south. Evidence dating from the Ice Age period of engravings dating from at least 12,000 BC have been found,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> showing occupation by <a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a>.\n</p><p>Evidence also exists of settled communities in the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period, which is marked by the building of the ritual burial sites known as <a href="/wiki/Dolmen" title="Dolmen">dolmens</a>. The number, size, and visible locations of these megalithic monuments (especially <a href="/wiki/La_Hougue_Bie" title="La Hougue Bie">La Hougue Bie</a>) have suggested that social organisation over a wide area, including surrounding coasts, was required for the construction.  Archaeological evidence also shows that trading links with <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and the south coast of England existed during this time.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hoards">Hoards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hoards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_hoards_in_the_Channel_Islands" title="List of hoards in the Channel Islands">List of hoards in the Channel Islands</a></div>\n<p>Evidence of occupation and wealth has been discovered in the form of <a href="/wiki/Hoards" class="mw-redirect" title="Hoards">hoards</a>. In 1889, during construction of a house in Saint Helier, a 746-g gold <a href="/wiki/Torc" title="Torc">torc</a> of Irish origin was unearthed. A <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Europe" title="Bronze Age Europe">Bronze Age</a> hoard consisting of 110 implements, mostly spears and swords, was discovered in Saint Lawrence in 1976 - probably a smith\'s stock. Hoards of coins were discovered at La Marquanderie, in Saint Brelade, Le C\xc3\xa2tel, in Trinity, and Le C\xc3\xa2tillon, in Grouville (1957).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1998_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1998-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2012, two <a href="/wiki/Metal_detectorist" class="mw-redirect" title="Metal detectorist">metal detectorists</a> announced that they had uncovered what could be Europe\'s largest <a href="/wiki/Grouville_Hoard" title="Grouville Hoard">hoard</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Celtic_coinage" title="Celtic coinage">Celtic coins</a>. 70,000 late Iron Age and Roman coins. The hoard is thought to have belonged to a <a href="/wiki/Curiosolitae" title="Curiosolitae">Curiosolitae</a> tribe fleeing <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>\'s armies around 50 to 60 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2012, another metal detectorist reported an earlier Bronze Age find, the <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Hoard" title="Trinity Hoard">Trinity Hoard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallo-Roman_and_Early_Middle_Ages">Gallo-Roman and Early Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gallo-Roman and Early Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although Jersey was part of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> world, there is a lack of evidence to give a better understanding of the island during the Gallo-Roman and early Middle Ages. The tradition that the island was called <i>Caesarea</i> by the Romans appears to have no basis in fact. The Roman name for the Channel Islands was <i>I. Lenuri</i> (Lenur Islands)<sup id="cite_ref-GRTV_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRTV-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> and were occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Britons</a> during their migration to Brittany (5th-6th century).\n</p><p>Various saints such as the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> <a href="/wiki/Samson_of_Dol" title="Samson of Dol">Samson of Dol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Branwalator" title="Branwalator">Branwalator</a> (Brelade) were active in the region. Tradition has it that <a href="/wiki/Helier" title="Helier">Saint Helier</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tongeren" title="Tongeren">Tongeren</a> in modern-day Belgium first brought Christianity to the island in the 6th century, part of the walls of the <a href="/wiki/Fishermen%27s_Chapel" title="Fishermen&#39;s Chapel">Fishermen\'s Chapel</a> dates from this period and <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> sent his emissary to the island (at that time called <i>Angia</i>, also spelt <i>Agna</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> in 803. A chapel built around 911, now forms part of the nave of the <a href="/wiki/Parish_Church_of_St_Clement" title="Parish Church of St Clement">Parish Church of St Clement</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Normans">Normans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Normans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island took the name Jersey as a result of <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> activity in the area between the 9th and 10th centuries. The Channel Islands remained politically linked to <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> until 933, when <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_Normandy" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of Normandy">William Longsword</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Normandy" title="Duke of Normandy">Duke of Normandy</a> seized the <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Cotentin peninsula">Cotentin</a> and the islands and added them to his domain; in 1066, Duke <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> defeated <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold</a> at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Hastings</a> to become king of England; however, he continued to rule his French possessions as a separate entity,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Fealty" title="Fealty">fealty</a> was owed as a Duke, to the King of France.\n</p><p>According to the Rolls of the Norman Exchequer, in 1180 Jersey was divided for administrative purposes into three ministeria:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i>de Gorroic</i>, <i>de Groceio</i> and <i>de Crapau Doit</i> (possibly containing four parishes each).<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup> This was a time of building or extending churches with most parish churches in the island being built/rebuilt in a Norman style chosen by the abbey or priory to which each church had been granted. St Mary and St Martin being given to Cerisy Abbey.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>21</span></sup>\n</p><p>The islands remained part of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Duchy of Normandy</a> until 1204, when King <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_Augustus" class="mw-redirect" title="Philip II Augustus">Philip II Augustus</a> of France conquered the duchy from King <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">John of England</a>; thanks to <a href="/wiki/Peter_de_Preaux" title="Peter de Preaux">Pierre de Pr\xc3\xa9aux</a> who decided to support King John, the islands remained in the personal possession of the English king<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup> and were described as being a Peculiar of the Crown.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The so-called <i>Constitutions of King John</i> are the foundation of modern self-government.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Feudal_Age">The Feudal Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Feudal Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg/220px-Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg/330px-Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg/440px-Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1531" data-file-height="916" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mont_Orgueil_castle_Jersey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mont Orgueil dominates the small harbour of <a href="/wiki/Gorey,_Jersey" title="Gorey, Jersey">Gorey</a> and guards Jersey from attack from the French coast opposite</div></div></div>\n<p>From 1204 onwards, the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> ceased to be a peaceful backwater and became a potential flashpoint on the international stage between England and France. In the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1259)" title="Treaty of Paris (1259)">Treaty of Paris</a> (1259), the King of France gave up claim to the Channel Islands. The claim was based upon his position as feudal overlord of the Duke of Normandy. The King of England gave up claim to mainland Normandy and appointed a Warden, a position now termed <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Jersey" title="Lieutenant Governor of Jersey">Lieutenant Governor of Jersey</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Bailiff" title="Bailiff">bailiff</a> to govern in his stead. The Channel Islands were never absorbed into the Kingdom of England. However the churches in Jersey were left under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Coutances" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Coutances">Diocese of Coutances</a> for another 300 years.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>27</span></sup>\n</p><p>The existing Norman customs and laws were allowed to continue, with the exception that the ultimate head of the legal system was the King of England rather than the Duke of Normandy. There was no attempt to introduce English law. The law was conducted through 12 jurats, constables (<i>conn\xc3\xa9table</i>) and a bailiff (<i>Baill\xc3\xa9</i>). These titles have different meanings and duties to those in England.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>27\xe2\x80\x938</span></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mont_Orgueil" title="Mont Orgueil">Mont Orgueil</a> castle was built at this time to serve as a royal fortress and military base. This was needed as the Island had few defences and had previously been suppressed by a fleet commanded by a French exile, <a href="/wiki/Eustace_the_Monk" title="Eustace the Monk">Eustace the Monk</a> working with the English King until in 1212 he changed sides and raided the Channel Islands on behalf of the French King.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25\xe2\x80\x938</span></sup> A "warden" was appointed to represent the King in the island, this title sometimes called "Captain" and later became "Governor" of the island, the duties were primarily military with the power to represent the King to appoint a bailiff who was normally an islander.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>32</span></sup> Any oppression by a bailiff or a warden was to be resolved locally or failing that, by appeal to the King who appointed commissioners to report on disputes.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years\' War</a>, the island was attacked many times <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> resulting in the formal creation of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Militia_of_the_Island_of_Jersey" title="Royal Militia of the Island of Jersey">Island Militia</a> in 1337, which was compulsory for the next 600 years for all men of military age. In March 1338, a French force landed on Jersey, intent on capturing the island.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Although the island was overrun, Mont Orgueil remained in English hands.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The French remained until September, when they sailed off to conquer Guernsey, Alderney, and Sark. In 1339, the French returned, allegedly with 8,000 men in 17 <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoese</a> galleys and 35 French ships. Again, they failed to take the castle and, after causing damage, withdrew.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was 1348 when the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> reached the Island, ravaging the population. The change in England to a written language in "English" was not taken up in Jersey, where Norman-French continued until the 20th-century.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>44</span></sup> In July 1373, <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_du_Guesclin" title="Bertrand du Guesclin">Bertrand du Guesclin</a> overran Jersey and besieged Mont Orgueil. His troops succeeded in breaching the outer defences, forcing the garrison back to the keep. The garrison came to an agreement that they would surrender if not relieved by <a href="/wiki/Michaelmas" title="Michaelmas">Michaelmas</a> and du Guesclin sailed back to Brittany, leaving a small force to carry on the siege. An English relief fleet arrived in time.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> On 7 October 1406, 1,000 French men at arms led by Pero Nino, a Castilian nobleman turned corsair, invaded Jersey, landing at St Aubin\'s Bay and defeated the 3,000 defenders but failed to capture the island.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>50\xe2\x80\x931</span></sup>\n</p><p>The rise of <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_Arc" title="Joan of Arc">Joan of Arc</a> inspired France to evict the English from mainland France, with the exception of Calais, putting Jersey back in the front line.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>54</span></sup> The French did not succeed in capturing Jersey during the Hundred Years\' War, but by taking advantage of the split in England during the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Roses" title="Wars of the Roses">Wars of the Roses</a> captured Mont Orgueil in the summer of 1461, allegedly as part of a secret deal between <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Anjou" title="Margaret of Anjou">Margaret of Anjou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Br%C3%A9z%C3%A9" title="Pierre de Br\xc3\xa9z\xc3\xa9">Pierre de Br\xc3\xa9z\xc3\xa9</a> to gain French support for the Lancastrian cause. The island was held by the French until 1468, when Yorkist forces and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Militia_of_the_Island_of_Jersey" title="Royal Militia of the Island of Jersey">local militia</a> were able to recapture the castle.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to the island\'s strategic importance to the English crown, the islanders were able to negotiate, over a number of centuries, the right to retain privileges and improve on certain benefits, such as trade rights, from the King.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reformation_to_the_civil_war">Reformation to the civil war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reformation to the civil war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 16th century, ideas of the reformation of the church coupled with the split with the Catholic Faith by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a>, resulted in the islanders adopting the <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> religion, in 1569 the churches moved under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Winchester" title="Diocese of Winchester">Diocese of Winchester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>81</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> in Jersey meant that life became very austere. Laws were strictly enforced, punishment for wrong doers was severe, but education was improved.<sup id="cite_ref-BHJ_12-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHJ-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>83\xe2\x80\x936</span></sup>\n</p><p>The excommunication of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a> by the Pope increased the military threat to the island and the increasing use of gunpowder on the battlefield meant that the fortifications on the island had to be adapted. A new fortress was built to defend <a href="/wiki/Saint_Aubin,_Jersey" title="Saint Aubin, Jersey">St Aubin\'s Bay</a>, the new <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Castle" title="Elizabeth Castle">Elizabeth Castle</a> was named after the queen by Sir <a href="/wiki/Walter_Raleigh" title="Walter Raleigh">Walter Raleigh</a> when he was governor. The island militia was reorganised on a parish basis and each parish had two cannon which were usually housed in the church - one of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter,_Jersey" title="Saint Peter, Jersey">St Peter</a> cannon can still be seen at the bottom of Beaumont Hill.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG/250px-Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG" width="250" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG/375px-Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG/500px-Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG 2x" data-file-width="841" data-file-height="588" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Jersey_by_G_Mercator_1639..JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This map of Jersey, published in 1639, shows interior details such as Le Mont \xc3\xa8s Pendus (the gallows hill, now called Westmount). At first sight, the coastline appears wildly inaccurate, but if the image is rotated a little clockwise, the shape becomes much closer to what is known today.</div></div></div>\n<p>One of the favourable trade deals with England was the ability to import wool (England needing an export market but was at war with most of Europe).<sup id="cite_ref-RL_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RL-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>108</span></sup> The production of knitwear in the island reached such a scale that it threatened the island\'s ability to produce its own food, so laws were passed regulating who could knit with whom and when. The name "Jersey" synonymous for a sweater, shows its importance. The islanders also became involved with the <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> fisheries at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The boats left the island in February/March following a church service in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Br%C3%A9lade,_Jersey" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Br\xc3\xa9lade, Jersey">St Brelade</a>\'s church and they did not return again until September/October. Colonies were established in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Civil_War.2C_interregnum_and_restoration"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_War,_interregnum_and_restoration">Civil War, interregnum and restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Civil War, interregnum and restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands_in_the_Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Channel Islands in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Channel Islands in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms</a></div>\n<p>During the 1640s, England, Ireland and Scotland were embroiled in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" class="mw-redirect" title="War of the Three Kingdoms">War of the Three Kingdoms</a>. The civil war also divided Jersey, and while the sympathy of islanders lay with Parliament, the de Carterets (see Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Carteret" title="George Carteret">George Carteret</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Carteret_II" title="Philippe de Carteret II">Philippe de Carteret II</a>) held the island for the king.\n</p><p>The Prince of Wales, the future <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> visited the island in 1646 and again in October 1649 following the <a href="/wiki/Trial_and_execution_of_Charles_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Trial and execution of Charles I">trial and execution</a> of his father, <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>. In the Royal Square in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helier" title="Saint Helier">St. Helier</a> on 17 February 1649, Charles was publicly proclaimed king after his father\'s death (following the first public proclamation in Edinburgh on 5 February 1649).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Parliamentarian forces eventually captured the island in 1651 and Elizabeth Castle seven weeks later. In recognition for all the help given to him during his exile, Charles II gave <a href="/wiki/George_Carteret" title="George Carteret">George Carteret</a>, Bailiff and governor, a large grant of land in the American colonies, which he promptly named <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, now part of the United States of America.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Towards the end of the 17th century, Jersey strengthened its links with the Americas when many islanders emigrated to New England and north east Canada. The Jersey merchants built up a thriving business empire in the Newfoundland and <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gasp\xc3\xa9 Peninsula">Gasp\xc3\xa9</a> fisheries. Companies such as Robins and the Le Boutilliers set up thriving businesses.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century">18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jersey_round_tower.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Jersey_round_tower.jpg/220px-Jersey_round_tower.jpg" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Jersey_round_tower.jpg/330px-Jersey_round_tower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Jersey_round_tower.jpg/440px-Jersey_round_tower.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1080" data-file-height="1410" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jersey_round_tower.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Round towers were built along the coasts to protect the Island from French attack.</div></div></div>\n<p>By the 1720s, a discrepancy in coinage values between Jersey and France was threatening economic stability. The States of Jersey therefore resolved to <a href="/wiki/Devaluation" title="Devaluation">devalue</a> the <a href="/wiki/Liard_(coin)" title="Liard (coin)">liard</a> to six to the <a href="/wiki/Sou_(coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sou (coin)">sou</a>. The legislation to that effect implemented in 1729 caused popular riots that shook the establishment.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The devaluation was therefore cancelled.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Chamber of Commerce">Chamber of Commerce</a> founded 24 February 1768 is the oldest in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a>.\n</p><p>The <i>Code</i> of 1771 laid down for the first time in one place the extant laws of Jersey, and from this time, the functions of the Royal Court and the States of Jersey were delimited, with sole legislative power vested in the States.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a> arrived in Jersey in 1774, brought by fishermen returning from <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>. Conflict with the authorities ensued when men refused to attend militia drill when that coincided with chapel meetings. The Royal Court attempted to proscribe Methodist meetings, but King George III refused to countenance such interference with liberty of religion. The first Methodist minister in Jersey was appointed in 1783, and <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John Wesley</a> preached in Jersey in August 1789, his words being interpreted into the vernacular for the benefit of those from the country parishes. The first building constructed specifically for Methodist worship was erected in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ouen,_Jersey" title="Saint Ouen, Jersey">St. Ouen</a> in 1809.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg/220px-John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg/330px-John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg/440px-John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1836" data-file-height="1256" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_Major_Peirson" class="mw-redirect" title="The Death of Major Peirson">The Death of Major Peirson</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/John_Singleton_Copley" title="John Singleton Copley">John Singleton Copley</a>, 1782-1784.</div></div></div>\n<p>The 18th century was a period of political tension between Britain and France, as the two nations clashed all over the world as their ambitions grew. Because of its position, Jersey was more or less on a continuous war footing.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Wars_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American Wars of Independence">American Wars of Independence</a>, two attempted invasions of the island were made. In 1779, the Prince of Orange <a href="/wiki/William_V,_Prince_of_Orange" title="William V, Prince of Orange">William V</a> was prevented from landing at St Ouen\'s Bay; on 6 January 1781, a force led by <a href="/wiki/Baron_de_Rullecourt" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron de Rullecourt">Baron de Rullecourt</a> captured St Helier in a daring dawn raid, but was defeated by a British army led by Major <a href="/wiki/Francis_Peirson" title="Francis Peirson">Francis Peirson</a> in  the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jersey" title="Battle of Jersey">Battle of Jersey</a>. A short-lived peace was followed by the French Revolutionary Wars and the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> which, when they had ended, had changed Jersey forever. In 1799-1800, over 6000 Russian troops under the command of <a href="/wiki/Charles_du_Houx_de_Viom%C3%A9nil" title="Charles du Houx de Viom\xc3\xa9nil">Charles du Houx de Viom\xc3\xa9nil</a> were quartered in Jersey after an evacuation of Holland.\n</p><p>The first printing press was introduced to Jersey in 1784.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg/250px-Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg" width="250" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg/375px-Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg/500px-Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1496" data-file-height="718" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jersey_cattle_judging-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jersey_cattle" title="Jersey cattle">Jersey cattle</a> were developed as a breed during the 19th century. Judging the quality of cows remains a feature of rural life.</div></div></div>\n<p>The number of English-speaking soldiers stationed on the island and the number of retired officers and English-speaking labourers who came to the islands in the 1820s led to the island gradually moving towards an English-speaking culture in the town.\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Livre_tournois" title="Livre tournois">livre tournois</a></i> had been used as the legal <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> for centuries. However, it was abolished during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolutionary</a> period. Although the coins were no longer minted, they remained the legal currency in Jersey until 1837, when dwindling supplies and consequent difficulties in trade and payment obliged the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Jersey_pound" title="Jersey pound">pound sterling</a> as <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a>.\n</p><p>The military roads constructed (on occasion at gunpoint in the face of opposition from landowners) by the governor, <a href="/wiki/George_Don_(British_Army_officer)" title="George Don (British Army officer)">General George Don</a>, to link coastal fortifications with St. Helier harbour had an unexpected effect on agriculture once peace restored reliable trade links. Farmers in previously isolated valleys were able to swiftly transport crops grown in the island\'s <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a> to waiting ships and then on to the markets of London and Paris ahead of the competition. In conjunction with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steamships</a> and the development of the French and British <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> systems, Jersey\'s agriculture was no longer as isolated as before.\n</p><p>The population of Jersey rose rapidly, from 47,544 in 1841 to 56,078 20 years later, despite a 20% mortality rate amongst new born children. Life expectancy was 35 years. Both immigration and emigration increased.<sup id="cite_ref-RL_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RL-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>186</span></sup>\n</p><p>The town expanded with many new streets and houses in a Georgian style, in 1843 it was agreed to erect street names. The Theatre Royal was built, as were <a href="/wiki/Victoria_College,_Jersey" title="Victoria College, Jersey">Victoria College, Jersey</a> in 1852 and extensions to the harbour, which were called Victoria Harbour.<sup id="cite_ref-RL_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RL-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>181\xe2\x80\x935</span></sup> Jersey issued its first coins in 1841, and exhibited 34 items at <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Exhibition" title="The Great Exhibition">The Great Exhibition</a> in 1851, the world\'s first ever <a href="/wiki/Pillar_box" title="Pillar box">Pillar box</a> was installed in 1852 and a paid police force was created in 1854.<sup id="cite_ref-RL_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RL-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>178\xe2\x80\x93185</span></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jersey_Railway_map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Jersey_Railway_map.png/250px-Jersey_Railway_map.png" width="250" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Jersey_Railway_map.png/375px-Jersey_Railway_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Jersey_Railway_map.png/500px-Jersey_Railway_map.png 2x" data-file-width="1584" data-file-height="1172" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jersey_Railway_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the former railway lines of Jersey</div></div></div>\n<p>Two railways, the <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Railway" title="Jersey Railway">Jersey Western Railway</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> in 1870, and the <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Eastern_Railway" title="Jersey Eastern Railway">Jersey Eastern Railway</a><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> in 1874, were opened. The western railway from <a href="/wiki/St_Helier_(Weighbridge)_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="St Helier (Weighbridge) railway station">St Helier (Weighbridge)</a> to <a href="/wiki/La_Corbi%C3%A8re" title="La Corbi\xc3\xa8re">La Corbi\xc3\xa8re</a> and the eastern railway from <a href="/wiki/St_Helier_(Snow_Hill)_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="St Helier (Snow Hill) railway station">St Helier (Snow Hill)</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gorey,_Jersey" title="Gorey, Jersey">Gorey</a> Pier. The two railways have never been connected.  Buses started running on the island in the 1920s, and the railways could not cope with the competition. The eastern railway closed in 1926 and the western railway in 1936 after a fire disaster that year.\n</p><p>Jersey was the fourth-largest shipbuilding area in the 19th-century <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> building over 900 vessels around the island. Shipbuilding declined with the coming of iron ships and steam. A number of banks on Jersey, guarantors of an industry both onshore and off, failed in 1873<sup id="cite_ref-RL_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RL-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>193</span></sup> and 1886, even causing strife and discord in far-flung societies. The population fell slightly in the 20 years to 1881.\n</p><p>In the late 19th century, as the former thriving <a href="/wiki/Cider" title="Cider">cider</a> and wool industries declined, island farmers benefited from the development of two luxury products - <a href="/wiki/Jersey_cattle" title="Jersey cattle">Jersey cattle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Royal" title="Jersey Royal">Jersey Royal</a> potatoes. The former was the product of careful and selective breeding programmes; the latter was a total fluke. \n</p><p>The anarchist philosopher, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a>, who visited the Channel Islands in 1890, 1896, and 1903, described the agriculture of Jersey in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Conquest_of_Bread" title="The Conquest of Bread">The Conquest of Bread</a></i>.\n</p><p>The 19th century also saw the rise of <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> as an important industry (linked with the improvement in passenger ships)<sup id="cite_ref-RL_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RL-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>196</span></sup> which reached its climax in the period from the end of the Second World War to the 1980s.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Elementary education became obligatory in 1899, and free in 1907. The years before the First World War saw the foundation of cultural institutions, the Battle of Flowers and the Jersey Eisteddfod. The first aeroplanes arrived in Jersey in 1912.\n</p><p>In 1914, the British garrison was withdrawn at the start of the war and the militia were mobilised. Jersey men served in the British and French armed forces. Numbers of German prisoners of war were interned in Jersey. The influenza epidemic of 1918 added to the toll of war.\n</p><p>In 1919, <a href="/wiki/Imperial_measurements" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial measurements">imperial measurements</a> replaced, for the most part, the tradition Jersey system of weights and measures; women aged over 30 were given the vote; and the endowments of the ancient grammar schools were repurposed as scholarships for Victoria College.\n</p><p>In 1921, the visit of King <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> was the occasion for the design of the parish crests.\n</p><p>In 1923, the British government asked Jersey to contribute an annual sum towards the costs of the Empire. The States of Jersey refused and offered instead a one-off contribution to war costs. After negotiations, Jersey\'s one-off contribution was accepted.\n</p><p>The first motor car had arrived in 1899, and by the 1930s, competition from motor buses had rendered the railways unprofitable, with final closure coming in 1935 (except for the later German reintroduction of rail during the military occupation). <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Airport" title="Jersey Airport">Jersey Airport</a> was opened in 1937 to replace the use of the beach of Saint Aubin\'s bay as an airstrip at low tide.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1998_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1998-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>English was first permitted in debates in the States of Jersey in 1901, and the first legislation to be drawn up primarily in English was the Income Tax Law of 1928.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupation_1940-1945">Occupation 1940-1945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span defences by the British government and German bombardment, Jersey was <a href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_the_Channel_Islands" title="German occupation of the Channel Islands">occupied by German troops</a> between 1940 and 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The Channel Islands were the only British soil occupied by German troops in World War II. This period of occupation had about 8,000 islanders evacuated, 1,200 islanders deported to camps in Germany, and over 300 islanders sentenced to the prison and <a href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Concentration camp">concentration camps</a> of mainland Europe. Twenty died as a result. The islanders endured near-starvation in the winter of 1944-45, after the Channel Islands had been cut off from German-occupied Europe by Allied forces advancing from the Normandy beachheads, avoided only by the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> supply ship <i>Vega</i> in December 1944. <a href="/wiki/Liberation_Day_(Channel_Islands)" title="Liberation Day (Channel Islands)">Liberation Day</a> - 9 May is marked as a public holiday.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Liberation_growth">Post-Liberation growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Jersey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Post-Liberation growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The event which has had the most far-reaching effect on Jersey in modern times is the growth of the finance industry in the island from the 1960s onwards.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
