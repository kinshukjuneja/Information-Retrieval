Paddle steamers like this were formerly extremely common on the Clyde.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Islands of the Firth of Clyde</b> are the fifth largest of the major <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> island groups after the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Hebrides" title="Inner Hebrides">Inner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Orkney_islands" title="List of Orkney islands">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Shetland_islands" title="List of Shetland islands">Shetland</a>. They are situated in the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Firth of Clyde</a> between <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Bute" title="Argyll and Bute">Argyll and Bute</a>. There are about forty islands and <a href="/wiki/Skerry" title="Skerry">skerries</a>, of which only four are inhabited and only nine larger than 40 hectares (99 acres).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;Note 1&#93;</a></sup> The largest and most populous are <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Bute" title="Isle of Bute">Bute</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Great_Cumbrae" title="Great Cumbrae">Great Cumbrae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holy_Isle,_Firth_of_Clyde" title="Holy Isle, Firth of Clyde">Holy Isle</a> are also served by dedicated ferry routes.<sup id="cite_ref-CMac1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMac1-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HI_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HI-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the four larger Scottish archipelagos, none of the isles in this group are connected to one another or to the mainland by bridges.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> of the area is complex and the islands and the surrounding sea <a href="/wiki/Loch" title="Loch">lochs</a> each have distinctive features. The influence of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Drift</a> create a mild, damp oceanic climate.\n</p><p>The larger islands have been continuously inhabited since <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> times, were influenced by the emergence of the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Riata" title="D\xc3\xa1l Riata">D\xc3\xa1l Riata</a> from 500 AD and then absorbed into the emerging <a href="/wiki/Origins_of_the_Kingdom_of_Alba" title="Origins of the Kingdom of Alba">Kingdom of Alba</a> under <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_MacAlpin" title="Kenneth MacAlpin">Kenneth MacAlpin</a>. They experienced <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> incursions during the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> and then became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a> in the 13th century. There is a diversity of wildlife, including three species of rare endemic tree.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology_and_geography">Geology and geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology and geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Firthofclydemap.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Firthofclydemap.png/220px-Firthofclydemap.png" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Firthofclydemap.png/330px-Firthofclydemap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Firthofclydemap.png/440px-Firthofclydemap.png 2x" data-file-width="541" data-file-height="611" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Firthofclydemap.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Firth of Clyde</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Boundary Fault</a> runs past Bute and through the northern part of Arran, so from a geological perspective some of the islands are in the Highlands and some in the <a href="/wiki/Central_Lowlands" title="Central Lowlands">Central Lowlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As a result, Arran is sometimes referred to as "Scotland in miniature" and the island is a popular destination for <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a>, who come to see <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">intrusive igneous landforms</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dykes</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> and metasedimentary rocks ranging widely in age.<sup id="cite_ref-McK300_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McK300-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Visiting in 1787, the geologist <a href="/wiki/James_Hutton" title="James Hutton">James Hutton</a> found his first example of an <a href="/wiki/Hutton%27s_Unconformity" title="Hutton&#39;s Unconformity">unconformity</a> there and this spot is one of the most famous places in the study of geology.<sup id="cite_ref-monty_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-monty-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Waymarking_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waymarking-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A group of weakly metamorphosed rocks that form the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault#Highland_Border_Complex" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Border Complex</a> lie discontinuously along the Highland Boundary Fault. One of the most prominent exposures is along Loch Fad on Bute.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ailsa_Craig" title="Ailsa Craig">Ailsa Craig</a>, which lies some 25 kilometres (16&#160;mi) south of Arran, has been quarried for a rare type of <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">micro-granite</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Riebeckite" title="Riebeckite">riebeckite</a> known as "Ailsite" which is used to make <a href="/wiki/Curling" title="Curling">curling</a> stones. As of 2004, 60 to 70% of all curling stones in use were made from granite from the island.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In common with the rest of Scotland the Firth of Clyde was covered by ice sheets during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice ages">ice ages</a> and the landscape is much affected by <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Arran\'s highest peaks may have been <a href="/wiki/Nunatak" title="Nunatak">nunataks</a> at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> After the last retreat of the ice sea level changes and the <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic</a> rise of land makes charting <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">post glacial</a> coastlines a complex task but the resultant clifflines behind <a href="/wiki/Raised_beach" title="Raised beach">raised beaches</a> are a prominent feature of the entire coastline.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The soils of the islands reflect the diverse geology. Bute has the most productive land, and a pattern of deposits that is typical of the southwest of Scotland. There is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Boulder_clay" title="Boulder clay">boulder clay</a> and other glacial deposits in the eroded valleys, and raised beach and marine deposits elsewhere, especially to the south and west which result in a <a href="/wiki/Machair" title="Machair">machair</a> landscape in places, inland from the sandy bays, such as Stravanan.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Firth of Clyde, in which these island lie, is north of the Irish Sea and has numerous branching inlets, some of them substantial features in their own right. These include <a href="/wiki/Loch_Goil" title="Loch Goil">Loch Goil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loch_Long" title="Loch Long">Loch Long</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gare_Loch" title="Gare Loch">Gare Loch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loch_Fyne" title="Loch Fyne">Loch Fyne</a> and the estuary of the <a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">River Clyde</a>. In places the effect of glaciation on the seabed is pronounced. For example, the Firth is 320 metres (1,050&#160;ft) deep between Arran and Bute, although they are only 8 kilometres (5.0&#160;mi) apart.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The islands are all exposed to wind and tide and various <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouses</a>, such as those on Ailsa Craig, <a href="/wiki/Pladda" title="Pladda">Pladda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Davaar_Island" title="Davaar Island">Davaar</a> act as an aid to navigation.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Castleisland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Castleisland.jpg/220px-Castleisland.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Castleisland.jpg/330px-Castleisland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Castleisland.jpg/440px-Castleisland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="424" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Castleisland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Castle_Island,_Scotland" title="Castle Island, Scotland">Castle Island</a> from <a href="/wiki/Little_Cumbrae" title="Little Cumbrae">Little Cumbrae</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The Firth of Clyde lies between 55 and 56 degrees north, at the same latitude as <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> in Canada and north of the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>, but the influence of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Drift</a>\xe2\x80\x94the northern extension of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>\xe2\x80\x94ameliorates the winter weather and the area enjoys a mild, damp oceanic climate. Temperatures are generally cool, averaging about 6&#160;<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">\xc2\xb0C</a> (43&#160;<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">\xc2\xb0F</a>) in January and 14&#160;\xc2\xb0C (57&#160;\xc2\xb0F) in July at sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-met_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-met-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Snow seldom lies at sea level and frosts are generally less frequent than the mainland. In common with most islands of the west coast of Scotland, rainfall is generally high at between 1,300&#160;mm (51&#160;in) per annum on Bute, the Cumbraes and in the south of Arran and 1,900&#160;mm (75&#160;in) per annum in the north of Arran. The Arran mountains are wetter still with the summits receiving over 2,550&#160;mm (100&#160;in) annually. May, June and July are the sunniest months, with upwards of 200 hours of bright sunshine being recorded on average, southern Bute benefiting from a particularly high level of sunny days.<sup id="cite_ref-met_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-met-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MachrieMoorStones.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/MachrieMoorStones.jpg/220px-MachrieMoorStones.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/MachrieMoorStones.jpg/330px-MachrieMoorStones.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/MachrieMoorStones.jpg/440px-MachrieMoorStones.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MachrieMoorStones.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Machrie Moor <a href="/wiki/Standing_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing stone">standing stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> humans arrived in the Firth of the Clyde during the fourth millennium BC, probably from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. This was followed by a wave of <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> peoples using the same route and there is some evidence that the Firth of Clyde was a significant route via which mainland Scotland was colonised at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A particular style of megalithic structure developed in Argyll, the Clyde estuary and elsewhere in western Scotland that has become known as the <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairn#Clyde-Carlingford" title="Chambered cairn">Clyde cairn</a>. They are rectangular or trapezoidal in shape with a small enclosing chamber faced with large slabs of stone set on end and sometimes subdivided into smaller compartments. A forecourt area may have been used for displays or rituals associated with the interment of the dead, who were placed inside the chambers.<sup id="cite_ref-N104_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N104-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> They are concentrated in Arran, Bute and Kintyre and it is likely that the Clyde cairns were the earliest forms of Neolithic monument constructed by incoming settlers although few of the 100 or so examples have been given a <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a>. An example at Monamore on Arran has been dated to 3160 BC, although it was almost certainly built earlier than that, possibly c. 4000BC.<sup id="cite_ref-N104_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N104-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Murr113_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murr113-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> There are also numerous <a href="/wiki/Standing_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing stone">standing stones</a> dating from prehistoric times, including six <a href="/wiki/Stone_circle" title="Stone circle">stone circles</a> on Machrie Moor, Arran and other examples on Great Cumbrae and Bute.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cow27_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cow27-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> settlers also constructed megaliths at various sites, many of them dating from the second millennium BC, although the <a href="/wiki/Chambered_cairns" class="mw-redirect" title="Chambered cairns">chambered cairns</a> were replaced by burial <a href="/wiki/Cist" title="Cist">cists</a>, found on for example, Inchmarnock. Settlement evidence, especially from the early part of this era is however poor.<sup id="cite_ref-Cow27_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cow27-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The Queen of the Inch necklace is an article of jewellery made of <a href="/wiki/Jet_(lignite)" title="Jet (lignite)">jet</a> found on Bute that dates from circa 2000 BC. During the early <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Brython" class="mw-redirect" title="Brython">Brythonic</a> culture held sway, there being no evidence that the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> occupation of southern Scotland extended to these islands.<sup id="cite_ref-Murr113_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murr113-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Scots_rule">Early Scots rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Scots rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dalriada.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Dalriada.jpg/220px-Dalriada.jpg" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Dalriada.jpg/330px-Dalriada.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Dalriada.jpg/440px-Dalriada.jpg 2x" data-file-width="509" data-file-height="531" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dalriada.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of D\xc3\xa1l Riata at its height, c. 580\xe2\x80\x93600. <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Pictish</a> regions are marked in yellow</div></div></div>\n<p>During the 2nd century AD Irish influence was at work in the region and by the 6th century the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Riata" title="D\xc3\xa1l Riata">D\xc3\xa1l Riata</a> was established. Unlike the <a href="/wiki/P-Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="P-Celtic">P-Celtic</a> speaking Brythons, these Gaels spoke a form of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic Language">Gaelic</a> that still survives in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>. Through the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ninian" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Ninian">Saint Ninian</a> and others Christianity slowly supplanted <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">Druidism</a>. D\xc3\xa1l Riata flourished from the time of <a href="/wiki/Fergus_M%C3%B3r" title="Fergus M\xc3\xb3r">Fergus M\xc3\xb3r</a> in the late fifth century until the <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> incursions that commenced in the late eighth century.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Islands close to the shores of modern <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a> would have remained part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Strathclyde" title="Kingdom of Strathclyde">Kingdom of Strathclyde</a> during this period, whilst the main islands became part of the emerging <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Alba" title="Kingdom of Alba">Kingdom of Alba</a> founded by Kenneth MacAlpin (Cin\xc3\xa1ed mac Ailp\xc3\xadn).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viking_influence">Viking influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Viking influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RothesayCastleS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/RothesayCastleS.jpg/220px-RothesayCastleS.jpg" width="220" height="92" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/RothesayCastleS.jpg/330px-RothesayCastleS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/RothesayCastleS.jpg/440px-RothesayCastleS.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1356" data-file-height="567" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RothesayCastleS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 13th century curtain wall of <a href="/wiki/Rothesay_Castle" title="Rothesay Castle">Rothesay Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Bute" title="Isle of Bute">Bute</a></div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Isles" title="Kingdom of the Isles">Kingdom of the Isles</a></div>\n<p>The Islands of the Clyde historically formed the border zone between the Norse <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Isles" title="Kingdom of the Isles">Su\xc3\xb0reyjar</a></i> and Scotland. As such many of these islands fell under Norse hegemony between the 9th and 13th centuries.\n</p><p>The islands of the Clyde may well have formed the power base of <a href="/wiki/Somhairle_mac_Giolla_Brighde" class="mw-redirect" title="Somhairle mac Giolla Brighde">Somhairle mac Giolla Brighde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clann_Somhairle" title="Clann Somhairle">his descendants</a> by the last half of the 12th century. At about this time period, the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Steward_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Steward of Scotland">Steward of Scotland</a> seems to have encroached into the region; and there is reason to suspect that, by the turn of the 13th century, the islands were consumed by the expanding <a href="/wiki/Clan_Stewart" title="Clan Stewart">Stewart</a> lordship.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The western extension of Scottish authority appears to have been one of the factors behind a Norwegian invasion of the region in 1230, in which the invaders seized <a href="/wiki/Rothesay_Castle" title="Rothesay Castle">Rothesay Castle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1263 Norwegian troops commanded by <a href="/wiki/Haakon_IV_of_Norway" title="Haakon IV of Norway">Haakon Haakonarson</a> repeated the feat but the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Largs" title="Battle of Largs">Battle of Largs</a> between Scots and Norwegian forces, which took place on the shores of the Firth of Clyde, was inconclusive as a military contest.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> This marked an ultimately terminal weakening of Norwegian power in Scotland. Haakon retreated to <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, where he died in December 1263, entertained on his death bed by recitations of the sagas. Following this ill-fated expedition, all rights that the Norwegian Crown "had of old therein" in relation to the islands were yielded to the Kingdom of Scotland as a result of the 1266 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Perth" title="Treaty of Perth">Treaty of Perth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Scotland">Modern Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg/220px-J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg/330px-J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg/440px-J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2600" data-file-height="1988" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:J_M_Briscoe_-_Clyde_puffer_VIC32_moored_at_Corpach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Steam Lighter VIC32, the last seagoing coal fired steam <i><a href="/wiki/Clyde_Puffer" class="mw-redirect" title="Clyde Puffer">Clyde Puffer</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>From the mid-thirteenth century to the present day all of the islands of the Clyde have remained part of Scotland.\n</p><p>From the commencement of the early medieval period until 1387 all of these isles were part of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Sodor_and_Man" title="Bishop of Sodor and Man">Diocese of Sodor and Man</a>, based at <a href="/wiki/Peel,_Isle_of_Man" title="Peel, Isle of Man">Peel</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>. Thereafter, the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_the_Isles" title="Diocese of the Isles">Bishopric of the Isles</a> was relocated to the north, firstly to <a href="/wiki/Snizort" title="Snizort">Snizort</a> on <a href="/wiki/Skye" title="Skye">Skye</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> a state of affairs which continued until the 16th century <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Scottish Reformation</a>.\n</p><p>The century following 1750 was time of significant change. New forms of transport, industry and agriculture brought sweeping changes, and an end to traditional ways of life that had endured for centuries. The aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Culloden" title="Battle of Culloden">Battle of Culloden</a> marked the beginning of the end for the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_clan" title="Scottish clan">clan</a> system and whilst there were marked improvements in living standards for some, these transformations came at a cost for others.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In the early 19th century <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Douglas-Hamilton,_10th_Duke_of_Hamilton" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander Douglas-Hamilton, 10th Duke of Hamilton">Alexander, 10th Duke of Hamilton</a> (1767\xe2\x80\x931852) embarked on a programme of <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">clearances</a> that had a devastating effect on Arran\'s population. Whole villages were removed and the Gaelic culture of the island dealt a terminal blow. A memorial to this early form of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing" title="Ethnic cleansing">ethnic cleansing</a> has been constructed on the shore at <a href="/wiki/Lamlash" title="Lamlash">Lamlash</a>, paid for by a Canadian descendant of the emigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the 1850s to the late 20th century the <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Puffer" class="mw-redirect" title="Clyde Puffer">Clyde Puffer</a>, made famous by the <i><a href="/wiki/Vital_Spark" title="Vital Spark">Vital Spark</a></i>, was the workhorse of the islands, carrying all kinds of produce and products to and from the islands. The <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Steam_Packet_Company" title="Caledonian Steam Packet Company">Caledonian Steam Packet Company</a> (CSP) was formed in May 1889 to operate steamer services to and from Gourock for the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Railway" title="Caledonian Railway">Caledonian Railway</a> and soon expanded by taking over rival steamer operators.<sup id="cite_ref-scran_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scran-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_MacBrayne_Ltd" class="mw-redirect" title="David MacBrayne Ltd">David MacBrayne Ltd</a> operated the Glasgow to <a href="/wiki/Ardrishaig" title="Ardrishaig">Ardrishaig</a> steamer service, as part of the "Royal Route" to Oban.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> During the 20th century many of the islands were developed as tourist resorts for Glaswegians who went "Doon the Watter", in parallel to mainland resorts such as <a href="/wiki/Largs" title="Largs">Largs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Troon" title="Troon">Troon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\nIn 1973 CSP and MacBraynes commenced joint Clyde and West Highland operations under the new name of <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_MacBrayne" title="Caledonian MacBrayne">Caledonian MacBrayne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Government-owned_corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Government-owned corporation">publicly owned</a> company, they serve Great Cumbrae, Arran and Bute as well as running mainland-to-mainland ferries across the firth.<sup id="cite_ref-CMac1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMac1-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Private companies operate services from Arran to Holy Isle<sup id="cite_ref-HI_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HI-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and from McInroy\'s Point (Gourock) to Hunter\'s Quay on the Cowal peninsula.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The majority of the islands at one time made up the traditional <a href="/wiki/County_of_Bute" title="County of Bute">County of Bute</a>. Today the islands are split more or less equally between the modern unitary authorities of <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Bute" title="Argyll and Bute">Argyll and Bute</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Ayrshire" title="North Ayrshire">North Ayrshire</a> with only Ailsa Craig and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Isle" title="Lady Isle">Lady Isle</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Ayrshire" title="South At its southern end, the loch opens into the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Firth of Clyde</a>, via the <a href="/wiki/Rhu" title="Rhu">Rhu</a> narrows.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> It contains two islets: Green Island and Perch Rock.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kilbrannan_Sound" title="Kilbrannan Sound">Kilbrannan Sound</a>, which lies between Arran and the <a href="/wiki/Kintyre" title="Kintyre">Kintyre</a> peninsula, contains several islets: An Struthlag, Cour Island, Eilean Carrach (<a href="/wiki/Carradale" title="Carradale">Carradale</a>), Eilean Carrach (<a href="/wiki/Skipness" title="Skipness">Skipness</a>), Eilean Grianain, Eilean Sunadale, Gull Isle, Island Ross and Thorn Isle. In the late 11th century <a href="/wiki/Magnus_III_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus III of Norway">Magnus Barefoot</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Norway">King of Norway</a>, made an arrangement with King <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_III_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm III of Scotland">Malcolm III of Scotland</a> that he could take possession of land on the west coast around which a ship could sail. He had his <a href="/wiki/Longship" title="Longship">longship</a> dragged across the 1.5 kilometres (0.93&#160;mi) long isthmus in the north of Kintyre between <a href="/wiki/East_Loch_Tarbert,_Argyll" title="East Loch Tarbert, Argyll">East Loch Tarbert</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Loch_Tarbert,_Argyll" title="West Loch Tarbert, Argyll">West Loch Tarbert</a> as part of a campaign to increase his possessions. Magnus declared that Kintyre had "better land than the best of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>", and by taking command of his ship\'s tiller and "sailing" across the isthmus he was able to claim the entire peninsula was an island, which remained under Norse rule for more than a dozen years as a result.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;Note 3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Loch Fyne, which extends 65 kilometres (40&#160;mi) inland from the <a href="/wiki/Sound_of_Bute" title="Sound of Bute">Sound of Bute</a> is the longest of Scotland\'s sea lochs and contains several islets and skerries.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> These are Duncuan Island, Eilean Ardgaddan, Eilean a\' Bhuic, Eilean Aoghainn, Eilean a\' Chomhraig, Eilean an D\xc3\xbanain, Eilean Buidhe (Ardmarnock), Eilean Buidhe (<a href="/wiki/Portavadie" title="Portavadie">Portavadie</a>), Eilean Fraoch, Eilean Math-ghamhna, Eilean M\xc3\xb3r, Glas Eilean, Heather Island, Inverneil Island, Kilbride Island and Liath Eilean.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Ayrshire" title="North Ayrshire">North Ayrshire</a> islets of Broad Rock, East Islet, Halftide Rock, High Rock and North Islet are all found surrounding <a href="/wiki/Horse_Isle" title="Horse Isle">Horse Isle</a>. Lady Isle, which lies off the South Ayrshire coast near <a href="/wiki/Troon" title="Troon">Troon</a> once housed "ane old chapell with an excellent spring of water".<sup id="cite_ref-History_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> However, in June 1821 someone set fire to the "turf and pasture", and permanently destroyed the island\'s grazing, with gales blowing much of the island\'s soil into the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-Old_Troon_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Old_Troon-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Neither Loch Goil nor Loch Long, which are <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a>-like arms of the firth to the north, contain islands.<sup id="cite_ref-OS_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OS-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-islands">Non-islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sorbus_arranensis.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" href="/wiki/European_badger" title="European badger">badger</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_otter" class="mw-redirect" title="European otter">otter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vipera_berus" title="Vipera Offshore there are <a href="/wiki/Harbour_porpoise" title="Harbour porpoise">harbour porpoises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basking_shark" title="Basking shark">basking sharks</a> and various species of <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wild_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wild-76">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Davaar is home to a population of wild <a including <a href="/wiki/Black_guillemot" title="Black guillemot">black guillemot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_eider" title="Common eider">eider</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine href="#cite_note-wild-76">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In 1981 there were 28 <a href="/wiki/Rock_ptarmigan" title="Rock ptarmigan">ptarmigan</a> on Arran, but in 2009 it was reported that extensive surveys href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> species of tree, the <a href="/wiki/Arran_Whitebeams" class="mw-redirect" title="Arran Whitebeams">Arran Whitebeams</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Scotsman_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman-80">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> These are the <a href="/wiki/Sorbus_arranensis" title="Sorbus arranensis">Scottish or Arran whitebeam</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sorbus_pseudofennica" title="Sorbus pseudofennica">cut-leaved whitebeam</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sorbus_pseudomeinichii" title="Sorbus pseudomeinichii">Catacol whitebeam</a>, which are amongst the most endangered tree species in the world. They are found in a protected <a href="/wiki/National_nature_reserve_(United_Kingdom)" title="National nature reserve (United Kingdom)">national nature reserve</a>, and are monitored by staff from <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Natural_Heritage" title="Scottish Natural Heritage">Scottish Natural Heritage</a>. Only 283 Arran whitebeam and 236 cut-leaved whitebeam were recorded as mature trees in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-Bignal_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bignal-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> The Catacol whitebeam was discovered in 2007 and steps have been taken to protect the two known specimens.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SWT_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SWT-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Islands_of_the_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Cumbraes.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/The_Cumbraes.jpg/220px-The_Cumbraes.jpg" width="220" height="62" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/The_Cumbraes.jpg/330px-The_Cumbraes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/The_Cumbraes.jpg/440px-The_Cumbraes.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6038" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Cumbraes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Cumbraes" title="The Cumbraes">The Cumbraes</a> with <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Bute" title="Isle of Bute">Bute</a> beyond</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> historian <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> refers to the <i>Clota</i> meaning the Clyde. seawater on a daily basis, but not necessarily at all stages of the tide, excluding human devices such as bridges and causeways. Various other definitions are used in the Scottish context. For example, the General Register Office for Scotland define an island as "a mass of land surrounded by water, separate from the Scottish mainland" but although they include islands linked by bridges etc., this is not clear from this definition.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Haswell-Smith (2004) uses "an Island is a piece of land or group of pieces of land which is entirely surrounded by water at Lowest Astronomical Tide and to which there is no permanent means of dry access".
