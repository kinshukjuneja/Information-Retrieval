class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ireland_%28MODIS%29.jpg/220px-Ireland_%28MODIS%29.jpg" width="220" height="303" class="thumbimage" this satellite image.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> is an island in <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Europe" title="Northwestern Europe">Northwestern Europe</a> in the north Atlantic Ocean. The island lies on the European continental shelf, part of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>. The island\'s main <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> features include low central <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plains</a> surrounded by coastal mountains. The highest peak is <a href="/wiki/Carrauntoohil" title="Carrauntoohil">Carrauntoohil</a> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Corr\xc3\xa1n Tuathail</i>), which is 1,041 meters (3,415&#160;ft) above sea level. The western <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastline</a> is rugged, with many islands, <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsulas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">headlands and bays</a>. The island is bisected by the <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">River Shannon</a>, which at 360.5&#160;km (224&#160;mi) with a 102.1&#160;km (63&#160;mi) <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> is the longest river in Ireland and flows south from <a href="/wiki/County_Cavan" title="County Cavan">County Cavan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> to meet the Atlantic just south of <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>. There are a number of sizeable lakes along Ireland\'s rivers, of which <a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a> is the largest.\n</p><p>Politically, the island consists of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, with jurisdiction over about five-sixths of the island; and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">constituent country</a> (and an unconfirmed <a href="/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave#&quot;Practical&quot;_enclaves,_exclaves_and_inaccessible_districts" title="Enclave and exclave">"practical" exclave</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, with jurisdiction over the remaining sixth. Located west of the island of Great Britain, it is located at approximately <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;params=53_N_8_W_type:isle"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">53\xc2\xb0N</span> <span class="longitude">8\xc2\xb0W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">53\xc2\xb0N 8\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">53; -8</span></span></span></a></span><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;params=53_N_8_W_type:isle"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">53\xc2\xb0N</span> <span class="longitude">8\xc2\xb0W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">53\xc2\xb0N 8\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">53; -8</span></span></span></a></span></span></span>. It has a total area of 84,421&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (32,595&#160;sq&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-irlgov_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irlgov-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is separated from Great Britain by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Mainland_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Mainland Europe">mainland Europe</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Sea" title="Celtic Sea">Celtic Sea</a>.\nIreland and Great Britain, together with nearby islands, are known collectively as the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>; as <a href="/wiki/British_Isles_naming_dispute" title="British Isles naming dispute">the term British Isles is controversial</a> in relation to Ireland, the alternative term \'Britain and Ireland\' is increasingly preferred.<sup id="cite_ref-postwarculture_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-postwarculture-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geological development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Ireland" title="Geology of Ireland">Geology of Ireland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SlieveLeague1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/SlieveLeague1.jpg/220px-SlieveLeague1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/SlieveLeague1.jpg/330px-SlieveLeague1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/SlieveLeague1.jpg/440px-SlieveLeague1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SlieveLeague1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Slieve_League" title="Slieve League">Slieve League</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Banagh" title="Banagh">Banagh</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The geology of Ireland is diverse. Different regions contain rocks belonging to different geologic periods, dating as far back almost 2 billion years. The oldest known Irish <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> is about 1.7 billion years old and is found on <a href="/wiki/Inishtrahull_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Inishtrahull Island">Inishtrahull Island</a> off the north coast of <a href="/wiki/Inishowen" title="Inishowen">Inishowen</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and on the mainland at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Annagh_Head&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Annagh Head (page does not exist)">Annagh Head</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mullet_Peninsula" title="Mullet Peninsula">Mullet Peninsula</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The newer formations are the <a href="/wiki/Drumlin" title="Drumlin">drumlins</a> and glacial valleys as a result of the last ice age, and the sinkholes and cave formations in the limestone regions of Clare.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ireland\'s geologic history covers everything from <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a> and tropical seas to the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>. Ireland was formed in two distinct parts and slowly joined together, uniting about 440 million years ago. As a result of tectonics and the effect of ice, the sea level has risen and fallen. In every area of the country the rocks which formed can be seen as a result. Finally, the impact of the glaciers shaped the landscape seen today.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This variation in the two areas along with the differences between volcanic areas and shallow seas gives Ireland a range of soils as well. There are wide <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a> and free-draining brown earths. The mountains are <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> with <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> areas, and <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> formations.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lion\'s share of Ireland has with most likehood been above sea level during the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">last 60 million years</a>. As such its landscapes have been shaped by <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> on land.<sup id="cite_ref-SimmsCoxon_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SimmsCoxon-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Protracted erosion does also means most of the <a href="/wiki/Paleogene" title="Paleogene">Paleogene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> sediments have been eroded away or, as known in a few cases, buried by <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchell1980_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell1980-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Before the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciations</a> affected Ireland the landscape had developed thick weathered <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> on the uplands and <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> in the lowlands.<sup id="cite_ref-SimmsCoxon_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SimmsCoxon-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> There have been some controversy regarding the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Planation_surface" title="Planation surface">planation surfaces</a> found on Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchell1980_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell1980-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> While some have argued for an origin in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marine_planation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marine planation (page does not exist)">marine planation</a> others regard these surfaces as <a href="/wiki/Peneplain" title="Peneplain">peneplains</a> formed by weathering and <a href="/wiki/Cycle_of_erosion" title="Cycle of erosion">fluvial erosion</a>. Not only is their origin disputed but also their actual extent and the relative role of <a href="/wiki/Eustacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Eustacy">sea-level change</a> and tectonics in their shaping.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchell1980_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell1980-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Most river systems in Ireland formed in the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> before the Quaternary glaciations. Rivers follow for most of their course <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">structural</a> features of the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Ireland" title="Geology of Ireland">geology of Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SimmsCoxon_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SimmsCoxon-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Marine erosion since the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> may have made Ireland\'s western coast retreat more than 100 km. Pre-Quaternary relief was more dramatic than today\'s <a href="/wiki/Glacial_erosion" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial erosion">glacier-smoothened</a> landscapes.<sup id="cite_ref-SimmsCoxon_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SimmsCoxon-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_geography">Physical geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain_ranges">Mountain ranges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mountain ranges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountains_in_Ireland" title="List of mountains in Ireland">List of mountains in Ireland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ireland_physical_medium.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Ireland_physical_medium.png/220px-Ireland_physical_medium.png" width="220" height="273" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Ireland_physical_medium.png/330px-Ireland_physical_medium.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7d/Ireland_physical_medium.png 2x" data-file-width="373" data-file-height="463" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ireland_physical_medium.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mountains, lakes, rivers and other physical features of Ireland are shown on this map. Some mountain ranges are further inland in the south of Ireland, such as the <a href="/wiki/Galtee_Mountains" title="Galtee Mountains">Galtee Mountains</a> (the highest inland range),<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silvermine_Mountains" title="Silvermine Mountains">Silvermine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slieve_Bloom_Mountains" title="Slieve Bloom Mountains">Slieve Bloom Mountains</a>. The highest peak <a href="/wiki/Carrauntoohil" title="Carrauntoohil">Carrauntoohil</a>, 1,038&#160;m (3,405&#160;ft) high,<sup id="cite_ref-OSIFAQS_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSIFAQS-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> is in the Macgillycuddy\'s Reeks, a range of glacier-carved sandstone mountains. Ireland\'s mountains are not high \xe2\x80\x93 only three peaks are over 1,000&#160;m (3,281&#160;ft)<sup id="cite_ref-OSIFAQS_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSIFAQS-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and another 457 exceed 500&#160;m (1,640&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a Other major rivers include the <a href="/wiki/River_Liffey" title="River Liffey">River Liffey</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Lee_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="River Lee (Ireland)">River Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munster_Blackwater" title="Munster Blackwater">River Blackwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Nore" title="River Nore">River Nore</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Suir" title="River Suir">River Suir</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Barrow" title="River Barrow">River Barrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Bann" title="River Bann">River Bann</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Foyle" title="River Foyle">River Foyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Erne" title="River Erne">River Erne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/River_Boyne" title="River Boyne">River Boyne</a> (see the <a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of rivers in Ireland">list of rivers in Ireland</a>).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a>, in Ulster,<sup id="cite_ref-OSIFAQS_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSIFAQS-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> is the largest lake in Ireland and Britain. href="/wiki/Malin_Head" title="Malin Head">Malin Head</a> is the most northerly point in Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> while <a to Mizen head" (or the reverse) is used for anything applying to the island of Ireland as a whole. <a href="/wiki/Carnsore_Point" title="Carnsore Point">Carnsore Point</a> is another <a href="/wiki/Extreme_points_of_Ireland" title="Extreme points of Ireland">extreme point of Ireland</a>, being the southeastern most point of Ireland. Further along the coast is <a href="/wiki/Hook_Head" title="Hook Head">Hook Head</a> while the <a href="/wiki/Old_Head_of_Kinsale" title="Old Head of Kinsale">Old Head of Kinsale</a> is one of many headlands along the south coast of Ireland.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Loop_Head" title="Loop Head">Loop Head</a> is the headland at which County Clare comes to a point on the west coast of Ireland, with The island is inhabited, and is connected to the mainland by a bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Some of the next largest islands are the <a href="/wiki/Aran_Islands" title="Aran Islands">Aran Islands</a>, off the coast of southern <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a>, host to an Irish-speaking community, or <a href="/wiki/Gaeltacht" title="Gaeltacht">Gaeltacht</a>. <a href="/wiki/Valentia_Island" title="Valentia Island">Valentia Island</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Iveragh_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Iveragh peninsula">Iveragh peninsula</a> is also one of Ireland\'s larger islands, and is relatively settled, as well as being connected by a bridge at its southeastern end.  <a href="/wiki/Omey_Island" title="Omey Island">Omey Island</a>, off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Connemara" title="Connemara">Connemara</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Tidal_island" title="Tidal island">tidal island</a>.\n</p><p>Some of the best-known peninsulas in Ireland are in County Kerry; the <a href="/wiki/Dingle_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Dingle peninsula">Dingle peninsula</a>, the aforementioned Iveragh peninsula and the <a href="/wiki/Beara_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Beara peninsula">Beara peninsula</a>. Ireland\'s most northerly land feature is Inishtrahull island, off Malin Head. <a href="/wiki/Rockall" title="Rockall">Rockall</a> Island may deserve this honour but its status is disputed, being claimed by the United Kingdom, Republic of Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> (for the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. The most southerly point is the <a href="/wiki/Fastnet_Rock" title="Fastnet Rock">Fastnet Rock</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> off <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Scotland" title="Geography of Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> off <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Wales" title="Geography of Wales">Wales</a> were grouped with Ireland ("<a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a>") by the <a href="/wiki/Greek_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek people">Greco</a>-<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Romans" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Romans">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_geography" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek geography">geographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> but this is no longer common.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Ireland" title="Climate of Ireland">Climate of Ireland</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Ireland" title="Climate of Ireland">climate of Ireland</a> is mild, moist and changeable with abundant <a href="/wiki/Rainfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> and a lack of <a href="/wiki/Temperature_extremes" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperature extremes">temperature extremes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>\'s climate is defined as a <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a>, or <i>Cfb</i> on the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification">K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification</a> system, a classification it shares with most of northwest Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-marine_climatology_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marine_climatology-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  The country receives generally warm summers and mild winters. It is considerably warmer than other areas at the same <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> on the other side of the Atlantic, such as in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, because it lies downwind of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. It is also warmer than maritime climates near the same latitude, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> as a result of heat released by the Atlantic overturning circulation that includes the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>. For comparison, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> is 9&#160;\xc2\xb0C warmer than <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="St. John&#39;s, Newfoundland and Labrador">St. John\'s</a> in Newfoundland in winter and 4&#160;\xc2\xb0C warmer than <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> in the Pacific Northwest in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The influence of the North Atlantic Current also ensures the coastline of Ireland remains ice-free throughout the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The climate in Ireland does not experience extreme weather, with tornadoes and similar weather features being rare.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> However, Ireland is prone to eastward moving cyclones which come in from the North atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The prevailing wind comes from the southwest, breaking on the high mountains of the west coast.<sup id="cite_ref-marine_climatology_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marine_climatology-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Rainfall is therefore a particularly prominent part of western Irish life, with <a href="/wiki/Valentia_Island" title="Valentia Island">Valentia Island</a>, off the west coast of <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a>, getting almost twice as much annual rainfall as Dublin on the east (1,400&#160;mm or 55.1&#160;in vs. 762&#160;mm or 30.0&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>January and February are the coldest months of the year, and mean daily air temperatures fall between 4 and 7&#160;\xc2\xb0C (39.2 and 44.6&#160;\xc2\xb0F) during these months. July and August are the warmest, with mean daily temperatures of 14 to 16&#160;\xc2\xb0C (57.2 to 60.8&#160;\xc2\xb0F), whilst mean daily maximums in July and August vary from 17 to 18&#160;\xc2\xb0C (62.6 to 64.4&#160;\xc2\xb0F) near the coast, to 19 to 20&#160;\xc2\xb0C (66.2 to 68.0&#160;\xc2\xb0F) inland.  The sunniest months are May and June, with an average of five to seven hours sunshine per day.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Though extreme weather events in Ireland are comparatively rare when compared with other countries in the European Continent, they do occur. Atlantic depressions, occurring mainly in the months of December, January and February, can occasionally bring winds of up to 160&#160;km/h or 99&#160;mph to Western coastal counties; while the summer months, and particularly around late July/early August, thunderstorms can develop.<sup id="cite_ref-Storm_Katia_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Storm_Katia-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Storm_Darwin_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Storm_Darwin-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thunderstorms_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thunderstorms-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The table shows mean climate figures for the Dublin Airport weather station over a thirty-year period. Ireland</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bogs">Bogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BordnaMona_2930.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/BordnaMona_2930.jpg/220px-BordnaMona_2930.jpg" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/BordnaMona_2930.jpg/330px-BordnaMona_2930.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/BordnaMona_2930.jpg/440px-BordnaMona_2930.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2167" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BordnaMona_2930.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bord_na_M%C3%B3na" title="Bord na M\xc3\xb3na">Bord na M\xc3\xb3na</a> peat-harvesting in the <a href="/wiki/Bog_of_Allen" title="Bog of Allen">Bog of Allen</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Ireland has 12,000&#160;km\xc2\xb2 (4,633&#160;miles\xc2\xb2) of <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> consisting of two distinct types, blanket bogs and raised bogs. Blanket bogs are the more widespread of the two types. They are essentially a product of human activity aided by the moist Irish climate. Blanket bogs formed on sites where <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> farmers cleared trees for farming. As the land so cleared fell into disuse, the soil began to leach and become more acidic, producing a suitable environment for the growth of <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juncaceae" title="Juncaceae">rushes</a>. The debris from these plants accumulated and a layer of <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> formed.  One of the largest expanses of Atlantic blanket bog in Ireland is to be found in <a href="/wiki/County_Mayo" title="County Mayo">County Mayo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Raised bogs are most common in the Shannon basin. They formed when depressions left behind after the ice age filled with water to form lakes. Debris from reeds in these lakes formed a layer at the bottom of the water. This eventually choked the lakes and raised above the surface, forming raised bogs.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the 17th century, peat has been cut for fuel for domestic heating and cooking and it is called turf when so used. The process accelerated as commercial exploitation of bogs grew. In the 1940s, machines for cutting turf were introduced and larger-scale exploitation became possible. In the Republic, this became the responsibility of a semi-state company called <a href="/wiki/Bord_na_M%C3%B3na" title="Bord na M\xc3\xb3na">Bord na M\xc3\xb3na</a>. In addition to domestic uses, commercially extracted turf is used in a number of industries, especially electricity generation<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> though peat is being combined with <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> for dual-firing electricity generation.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In recent years, the high level of bog being destroyed by cutting has raised environmental concerns. The problem is particularly acute for raised bogs as they yield a higher-grade fuel than blanket bogs. Plans are now in place in both the Republic and Northern Ireland to conserve most of the remaining raised bogs on the island.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Oil.2C_natural_gas_and_minerals"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil,_natural_gas_and_minerals">Oil, natural gas and minerals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Oil, natural gas and minerals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Offshore exploration for natural gas began in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-sch_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sch-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The first major discovery was the <a href="/wiki/Kinsale_Head_gas_field" title="Kinsale Head gas field">Kinsale Head gas field</a> in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Next was the smaller <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ballycotton_gas_field&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ballycotton gas field (page does not exist)">Ballycotton gas field</a> in 1989,<sup id="cite_ref-sch_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sch-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Corrib_gas_project" title="Corrib gas project">Corrib gas field</a> in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Exploitation of the Corrib project has yet to get off the ground because the <a href="/wiki/Corrib_gas_controversy" title="Corrib gas controversy">controversial proposal</a> to refine the gas onshore, rather than at sea has been met with <a href="/wiki/Shell_To_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Shell To Sea">widespread opposition</a>. Gas from these fields is pumped ashore and used for both domestic and industrial purposes. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helvick_oil_field&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Helvick oil field (page does not exist)">Helvick oil field</a>, estimated to contain over 28 million barrels (4,500,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of oil, is a 2000 discovery.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Ireland is the largest European producer of <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> with three operating zinc-lead mines at <a href="/wiki/Navan" title="Navan">Navan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galmoy" title="Galmoy">Galmoy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lisheen" class="mw-redirect" title="Lisheen">Lisheen</a>. Other mineral deposits with actual or potential commercial value include gold, silver, <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>, roofing <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> aggregate, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_stone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Building stone (page does not exist)">building stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 2007 the Department of Communications, Marine and Natural Resources (now replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Communications,_Energy_and_Natural_Resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Communications, Energy and Natural Resources">Department of Communications, Energy and Natural Resources</a>) reported that there may be volumes over 130 billion barrels (2.1<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">\xc3\x97</span>10<sup>10</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of petroleum and 50&#160;trillion cubic feet (1,400&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) of natural gas in Irish waters<sup id="cite_ref-oilbonanza_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oilbonanza-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> \xe2\x80\x93 worth trillions of <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a>, if true. The minimum \'guaranteed\' amount of oil in the Irish Atlantic waters is 10 billion barrels (1.6<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">\xc3\x97</span>10<sup>9</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>), worth over \xe2\x82\xac450 billion. There are also areas of petroleum and natural gas on shore, for example the <a href="/wiki/Lough_Allen" title="Lough Allen">Lough Allen</a> basin, with 9.4&#160;trillion cubic feet (270&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) of gas and 1.5 billion barrels (240,000,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of oil, valued at \xe2\x82\xac74.4 billion. Already some fields are being exploited, such as the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Point,_County_Clare" title="Spanish Point, County Clare">Spanish Point</a> field, with 1.25&#160;trillion cubic feet (35&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) of gas and 206 million barrels (32,800,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of oil, valued at \xe2\x82\xac19.6 billion. The Corrib Basin is also quite large, worth anything up to \xe2\x82\xac87 billion, while the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunquin_gas_field&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dunquin gas field (page does not exist)">Dunquin gas field</a> contains 25&#160;trillion cubic feet (710&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) of natural gas and 4.13 billion barrels (657,000,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of petroleum.<sup id="cite_ref-oilbonanza_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oilbonanza-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2012 the first commercial oil well was drilled 70&#160;km off the Cork coast by Providence Resources.
