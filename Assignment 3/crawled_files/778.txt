role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about a type of system of law, contrasting with common law. For non-criminal law in a common-law system, see <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">Civil law (common law)</a>. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Civil law (disambiguation)">Civil law (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p><b>Civil law</b>, or <b>civilian law</b>, is a legal system originating in Europe, intellectualised within the framework of <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>, the main feature of which is that its core principles are <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codified</a> into a referable system which serves as the primary source of law. This can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> systems; the intellectual framework of which comes from judge-made <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">decisional law</a>, and gives <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedential</a> authority to prior court decisions, on the principle that it is unfair to treat similar facts differently on different occasions (doctrine of judicial <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Stare_decisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Stare decisis">stare decisis</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Historically, a civil law is the group of legal ideas and systems ultimately derived from the <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Corpus Juris Civilis</a></i>, but heavily overlaid by <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Code" title="Napoleonic Code">Napoleonic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Early_Germanic_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Germanic law">Germanic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">canonical</a>, feudal, and local practices,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> as well as doctrinal strains such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_law" title="Natural law">natural law</a>, codification, and <a href="/wiki/Legal_positivism" title="Legal positivism">legal positivism</a>.\n</p><p>Conceptually, civil law proceeds from abstractions, formulates general principles, and distinguishes <a href="/wiki/Substantive_law" title="Substantive law">substantive rules</a> from <a href="/wiki/Procedural_law" title="Procedural law">procedural rules</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It holds <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a> secondary and subordinate to <a href="/wiki/Statutory_law" title="Statutory law">statutory law</a>. Civil law is often paired with the <a href="/wiki/Inquisitorial_system" title="Inquisitorial system">inquisitorial system</a>, but the terms are not synonymous.\n</p><p>There are key differences between a <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a> and a codal article. The most pronounced features of civil systems are their <a href="/wiki/Legal_codes" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal codes">legal codes</a>, with brief legal texts that typically avoid factually specific scenarios.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The short articles in a civil law code deal in generalities and stand in contrast with statutory systems, which are often very long and very detailed.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_law_(legal_system)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The purpose of codification is to provide all citizens with manners and written collection of the laws which apply to them and which judges must follow. It is the most widespread system of law in the world, in force in various forms in about 150 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It draws heavily from Roman law, arguably the most intricate known legal system dating from before the modern era.\n</p><p>Where codes exist, the primary source of law is the <a href="/wiki/Civil_code" title="Civil code">law code</a>, a systematic collection of interrelated articles,<sup id="cite_ref-Neubauer,_David_W._2007,_pg.28_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neubauer,_David_W._2007,_pg.28-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> arranged by subject matter in some pre-specified order,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> that explain the principles of law, rights and entitlements, and how basic legal mechanisms work. Law codes are simply laws enacted by a <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a>, even if they are in general much longer than other laws. Other major legal systems in the world include <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">Halakha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Canon_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Canon Law">canon law</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_the_Legal_systems_of_the_world_(en).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Map_of_the_Legal_systems_of_the_world_%28en%29.png/300px-Map_of_the_Legal_systems_of_the_world_%28en%29.png" width="300" height="157" systems of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Civil law based systems are in turquoise.</div></div></div>\n<p>Civil law countries can be divided into:\n</p>\n<ul><li>those where Roman law in some form is still living law but there has been no attempt to create a <a href="/wiki/Civil_code" title="Civil code">civil code</a>: <a href="/wiki/Andorra" title="Andorra">Andorra</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">San Marino</a></li>\n<li>those with uncodified mixed systems in which civil law is an academic source of authority but common law is also influential: Scotland and the <a href="/wiki/Roman-Dutch_law" title="Roman-Dutch law">Roman-Dutch law</a> countries (<a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>)</li>\n<li>those with codified mixed systems in which civil law is the background law but has its public law heavily influenced by common law: <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a></li>\n<li>those with comprehensive codes that exceed a single civil code, such as France, Germany, Greece, Italy, Japan, Mexico, Russia, Spain: it is this last category that is normally regarded as typical of civil law systems, and is discussed in the rest of this article.</li></ul>\n<p>The Scandinavian systems are of a hybrid character since their background law is a mix of civil law and Scandinavian customary law and they have been partially codified. Likewise, the laws of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>) mix <a href="/wiki/Norman_law" title="Norman law">Norman customary law</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_civil_law" class="mw-redirect" title="French civil law">French civil law</a>.\n</p><p>A prominent example of a civil-law is the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Code" title="Napoleonic Code">Napoleonic Code</a> (1804), named after French emperor <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>. The code comprises three components: \n</p>\n<ul><li>the law of persons</li>\n<li>property law</li>\n<li>commercial law</li></ul>\n<p>Rather than a compendium of statutes or catalog of <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a>, the code sets out general principles as rules of law.<sup id="cite_ref-Neubauer,_David_W._2007,_pg.28_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neubauer,_David_W._2007,_pg.28-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> systems, civil law jurisdictions deal with case law apart from any <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a> value. Civil law courts generally decide cases using codal provisions on a case-by-case basis, without reference to other (or even superior) judicial decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In actual practice, an increasing degree of precedent is creeping into civil law <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a>, and is generally seen in many nations\' highest courts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> While the typical <a href="/wiki/French-speaking" class="mw-redirect" title="French-speaking">French-speaking</a> <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme court</a> decision is short, concise and devoid of explanation or justification, in <a href="/wiki/Germanic-speaking_Europe" title="Germanic-speaking Europe">Germanic Europe</a>, the supreme courts can and do tend to write more verbose opinions, supported by legal reasoning.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A line of similar case decisions, while not precedent <i>per se</i>, constitute <i><a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence_constante" title="Jurisprudence constante">jurisprudence constante</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> While civil law jurisdictions place little reliance on court decisions, they tend to generate a phenomenal number of reported <a href="/wiki/Legal_opinion" title="Legal opinion">legal opinions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, this tends to be uncontrolled, since there is no statutory requirement that any case be reported or published in a <a href="/wiki/Law_report" title="Law report">law report</a>, except for the councils of state and constitutional courts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds199858-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Except for the highest courts, all publication of legal opinions are unofficial or commercial.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds199859_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds199859-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Civil law is sometimes referred to as neo-Roman law, Romano-Germanic law or Continental law. The expression "civil law" is a translation of Latin <i>jus civile</i>, or "citizens\' law", which was the <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">late imperial</a> term for its legal system, as opposed to the laws governing conquered peoples (<i><a href="/wiki/Jus_gentium" title="Jus gentium">jus gentium</a></i>); hence, the Justinian Code\'s title <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">Corpus Juris Civilis</a></i>. Civil law practitioners, however, traditionally refer to their system in a broad sense as <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_commune" title="Jus commune">jus commune</a></i>, literally "common law", meaning the general principles of law as opposed to laws specific to particular areas.  (The use of "common law" for the Anglo-Saxon systems may or may not be influenced by this usage.)\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_law_(legal_system)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Civil law takes as its major inspiration classical <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> (<i>c</i>. AD 1\xe2\x80\x93250), and in particular <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian</a> <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis" title="Corpus Juris Civilis">law</a> (6th century AD), and further expanded and developed in the late <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Roman_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval Roman Law">Middle Ages</a> under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Justinian Code\'s doctrines provided a sophisticated model for <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contracts</a>, rules of procedure, <a href="/wiki/Family_law" title="Family law">family law</a>, wills, and a strong <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">monarchical</a> <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitutional</a> system.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Roman law was received differently in different countries. In some it went into force wholesale by legislative act, i.e., it became <a href="/wiki/Positive_law" title="Positive law">positive law</a>, whereas in others it was diffused into society by increasingly influential legal experts and scholars.\n</p><p>Roman law continued without interruption in the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> until its final fall in the 15th century. However, given the multiple incursions and occupations by Western European powers in the late medieval period, its laws became widely implemented in the West. It was first received in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> partly because it was considered <a href="/wiki/Roman_law#classical_Roman_law" title="Roman law">imperial law</a>, and it spread in Europe mainly because its students were the only trained lawyers. It became the basis of <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a>, though partly rivaled by received feudal <a href="/wiki/Norman_law" title="Norman law">Norman law</a>. In England, it was taught academically at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, but underlay only <a href="/wiki/Probate" title="Probate">probate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">matrimonial</a> law insofar as both were inherited from canon law, and <a href="/wiki/Maritime_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime law">maritime law</a>, adapted from <i><a href="/wiki/Lex_mercatoria" title="Lex mercatoria">lex mercatoria</a></i> through the <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> trade.\n</p><p>Consequently, neither of the two waves of Roman influence completely dominated in Europe. Roman law was a secondary source that was applied only when local customs and laws were found lacking on a certain subject. However, after a time, even local law came to be interpreted and evaluated primarily on the basis of Roman law, since it was a common European legal tradition of sorts, and thereby in turn influenced the main source of law. Eventually, the work of civilian <a href="/wiki/Glossator" title="Glossator">glossators</a> and commentators led to the development of a common body of law and writing about law, a common legal language, and a common method of teaching and scholarship, all termed the <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_commune" title="Jus commune">jus commune</a></i>, or law common to Europe, which consolidated canon law and Roman law, and to some extent, <a href="/wiki/Feudal_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal law">feudal law</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Codification">Codification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_law_(legal_system)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Codification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An important common characteristic of civil law, aside from its origins in Roman law, is the comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codification</a> of received Roman law, i.e., its inclusion in civil codes. The earliest <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codification</a> known is the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Hammurabi" title="Code of Hammurabi">Code of Hammurabi</a>, written in ancient <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a> during the 18th century BC. However, this, and many of the codes that followed, were mainly lists of civil and criminal wrongs and their punishments. The codification typical of modern civilian systems did not first appear until the Justinian Code.\n</p><p>Germanic codes appeared over the 6th and 7th centuries to clearly delineate the law in force for Germanic privileged classes versus their Roman subjects and regulate those laws according to <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_law" title="Anglo-Saxon law">folk-right</a>. Under <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> law, a number of private <a href="/wiki/Custumal" title="Custumal">custumals</a> were compiled, first under the <a href="/wiki/Norman_law" title="Norman law">Norman empire</a> (<i>Tr\xc3\xa8s ancien coutumier</i>, 1200\xe2\x80\x931245), then elsewhere, to record the <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorial</a> \xe2\x80\x93 and later regional \xe2\x80\x93 customs, court decisions, and the legal principles underpinning them. Custumals were commissioned by lords who presided as lay judges over manorial courts in order to inform themselves about the court process. The use of custumals from influential towns soon became commonplace over large areas. In keeping with this, certain monarchs consolidated their kingdoms by attempting to compile custumals that would serve as the law of the land for their realms, as when <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII of France</a> in1454 commissioned an official custumal of Crown law. Two prominent examples include the <i>Coutume de Paris</i> (written 1510; revised 1580), which served as the basis for the Napoleonic Code, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Sachsenspiegel" title="Sachsenspiegel">Sachsenspiegel</a></i> (c. 1220) of the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric">bishoprics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halberstadt" title="Halberstadt">Halberstadt</a> which was used in northern Germany, Poland, and the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>.\n</p><p>The concept of codification was further developed during the 17th and 18th centuries AD, as an expression of both <a href="/wiki/Natural_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural Law">natural law</a> and the ideas of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>. The political ideals of that era was expressed by the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>, protection of <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a>. Those ideals required certainty of law, recorded, uniform law. So, the mix of Roman law and customary and local law gave way to law codification.\n</p><p>Also, the notion of a <a href="/wiki/Nation-state" class="mw-redirect" title="Nation-state">nation-state</a> implied recorded <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> that would be applicable to that state.\n</p><p>There was also a reaction to law codification. The proponents of codification regarded it as conducive to certainty, unity and systematic recording of the law; whereas its opponents claimed that codification would result in the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ossification" class="extiw" title="wikt:ossification">ossification</a> of the law.\n</p><p>In the end, despite whatever resistance to codification, the codification of European private laws moved forward. Codifications were completed by Denmark (1687), Sweden (1734), Prussia (1794), France (1804), and <a href="/wiki/Allgemeines_b%C3%BCrgerliches_Gesetzbuch" title="Allgemeines b\xc3\xbcrgerliches Gesetzbuch">Austria</a> (1811). The French codes were imported into areas conquered by Napoleon and later adopted with modifications in Poland (<a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Warsaw" title="Duchy of Warsaw">Duchy of Warsaw</a>/<a href="/wiki/Congress_Poland" title="Congress Poland">Congress Poland</a>; Kodeks cywilny 1806/1825), Louisiana (1807), Canton of Vaud (Switzerland; 1819), the <a href="/wiki/Burgerlijk_Wetboek" title="Burgerlijk Wetboek">Netherlands</a> (1838), <a href="/wiki/Serbian_civil_law" title="Serbian civil law">Serbia</a> (1844), Italy and Romania (1865), Portugal (1867) and Spain (1888). <a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCrgerliches_Gesetzbuch" title="B\xc3\xbcrgerliches Gesetzbuch">Germany</a> (1900), and <a href="/wiki/Swiss_civil_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss civil code">Switzerland</a> (1912) adopted their own codifications. These codifications were in turn imported into colonies at one time or another by most of these countries. The Swiss version was adopted in Brazil (1916) and Turkey (1926).\n</p><p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. states</a> began codification with New York\'s "<a href="/wiki/David_Dudley_Field_II" title="David Dudley Field II">Field Code</a>" (1850), followed by California\'s <a href="/wiki/California_Codes" title="California Codes">codes</a> (1872), and the federal <a href="/wiki/Revised_Statutes_of_the_United_States" title="Revised Statutes of the United States">revised statutes</a> (1874) and the current <a href="/wiki/United_States_Code" title="United States Code">United States Code</a> (1926).\n</p><p>In Japan, at the beginning of the Meiji Era, European legal systems\xe2\x80\x94especially the civil law of Germany and France\xe2\x80\x94were the primary models for the judicial and legal systems. In China, the German Civil Code was introduced in the later years of the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>, emulating Japan. In addition, it formed the basis of the law of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_(1912%E2%80%9349)" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of China (1912\xe2\x80\x9349)">Republic of China</a>, which remains in force in Taiwan. Furthermore, Korea, Taiwan, and Manchuria, former Japanese colonies, have been strongly influenced by the Japanese legal system.\n</p><p>Some authors consider civil law the foundation for <a href="/wiki/Socialist_law" title="Socialist law">socialist law</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> countries, which in this view would basically be civil law with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Marxism-Leninism" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxism-Leninism">Marxist-Leninist</a> ideals. Even if this is so, civil law was generally the legal system in place before the rise of socialist law, and some Eastern European countries reverted to the pre-socialist civil law following the fall of socialism, while others continued using a socialist legal systems.\n</p><p>Several civil-law mechanisms seem to have been borrowed from medieval Islamic <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Sharia</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fiqh" title="Fiqh">fiqh</a></i>. Despite their differences, the two systems are quite similar from a historical point of view. Both evolved in much the same way, though at different paces. The Roman law underlying civil law developed mainly from customary law that was refined with case law and legislation. Canon law further refined court procedure. Similarly, English law developed from Anglo-Saxon customary law, <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_law" title="Norman law">Norman law</a>, further refined by case law and legislation. The differences are\n</p>\n<ul><li>Roman law had crystallized many of its principles and mechanisms in the form of the Justinian Code, which drew from case law, scholarly commentary, and senatorial statutes</li>\n<li>civilian case law has persuasive authority, not binding authority as under common law</li></ul>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">Codification</a>, however, is by no means a defining characteristic of a civil law system. The Basic Law of Hong Kong ensures the use and status of common law in Hong Kong. Macau continues to have a Portuguese legal system of civil law.</li></ul>\n<p>However, some of these legal systems are often and more correctly said to be of hybrid nature:\n</p><p><b>Napoleonic to Germanistic influence</b>: The Italian civil code of 1942 replaced the original one of 1865, introducing germanistic elements due to the geopolitical alliances of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This approach has been imitated by other countries, including Portugal (1966), the Netherlands (1992), Brazil (2002) and Argentina (2014). Most of them have innovations introduced  by the Italian legislation, including the unification of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_code" title="Civil code">civil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_code_(law)" title="Commercial code (law)">commercial codes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>Germanistic to Napoleonic influence</b>: The <a href="/wiki/Swiss_civil_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss civil code">Swiss civil code</a> is considered mainly influenced by the German civil code and partly influenced by the French civil code.  The civil code of the Republic of Turkey is a slightly modified version of the Swiss code, adopted in 1926 during <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atat\xc3\xbcrk">Mustafa Kemal Atat\xc3\xbcrk</a>\'s presidency as part of the government\'s progressive reforms and secularization.\n</p><p>Some systems of civil law do not fit neatly into this typology, however. <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Poland" title="Law of Poland">Polish law</a> developed as a mixture of French and German civil law in the 19th century. After the reunification of Poland in 1918, five legal systems (French Napoleonic Code from the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Warsaw" title="Duchy of Warsaw">Duchy of Warsaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCrgerliches_Gesetzbuch" title="B\xc3\xbcrgerliches Gesetzbuch">German BGB</a> from Western Poland, Austrian ABGB from Southern Poland, Russian law from Eastern Poland, and Hungarian law from <a href="/wiki/Spisz" class="mw-redirect" title="Spisz">Spisz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orava_(region)" title="Orava (region)">Orawa</a>) were merged into one. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_Netherlands" title="Law of the Netherlands">Dutch law</a>, while originally codified in the Napoleonic tradition, has been heavily altered under influence from the Dutch native tradition of <a href="/wiki/Roman-Dutch_law" title="Roman-Dutch law">Roman-Dutch law</a> (still in effect in its former colonies). <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">civil law tradition</a> borrowed heavily from Roman-Dutch law. Swiss law is categorized as Germanistic, but it has been heavily influenced by the Napoleonic tradition, with some indigenous elements added in as well.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Louisiana_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Louisiana law">Louisiana private law</a> is primarily a Napoleonic system. <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> is the only <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> partially based on <a href="/wiki/Law_of_France" title="Law of France">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Spain" title="Law of Spain">Spanish</a> codes and ultimately <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a>, as opposed to English <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In Louisiana, private law was codified into the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Civil_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Louisiana Civil Code">Louisiana Civil Code</a>. Current Louisiana law has converged considerably with American law, especially in its <a href="/wiki/Public_law" title="Public law">public law</a>, judicial system, and adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Commercial_Code" title="Uniform Commercial Code">Uniform Commercial Code</a> (except for Article 2) and certain legal devices of American common law.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In fact, any innovation, whether private or public, has been decidedly common law in origin.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Most innovations in Louisiana law come from the Louisiana State Law Institute, a civil law organization, so this statement appears to be incorrect (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> law, whose private law is also of French civil origin, has developed along the same lines,  adapting in the same way as Louisiana to the public law and judicial system of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian law">Canadian common law</a>. By contrast, Quebec private law has innovated mainly from civil sources. To a lesser extent, other states formerly part of the Spanish Empire, such as Texas and California, have also retained aspects of Spanish civil law into their legal system, for example <a href="/wiki/Community_property" title="Community property">community property</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Law of Puerto Rico">legal system of Puerto Rico</a> exhibits similarities to that of Louisiana: a civil code whose interpretations rely on both the civil and common law systems. Because <a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Puerto_Rico&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Civil Code of Puerto Rico (page does not exist)">Puerto Rico</a>\'s Civil Code is based on the Spanish Civil Code of 1889, available jurisprudence has tended to rely on common law innovations due to the code\'s age and in many cases, obsolete nature.\n</p><p>Several Islamic countries have civil law systems that contain elements of <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> As an example, the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Civil_Code" title="Egyptian Civil Code">Egyptian Civil Code</a> of 1810 that developed in the early 19th century&#8212;which remains in force in Egypt is the basis for the civil law in many countries of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a> where the civil law is used&#8212; is based on the Napoleonic Code, but its primary author <a href="/wiki/Abd_El-Razzak_El-Sanhuri" title="Abd El-Razzak El-Sanhuri">Abd El-Razzak El-Sanhuri</a> attempted to integrate principles and features of Islamic law in deference to the unique circumstances of Egyptian society.\n</p><p>Japanese Civil Code was considered as a mixture of roughly 60 percent of the German civil code and roughly 30 percent of the French civil code and 8 percent of Japanese customary law and 2 percent of the English law.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The code includes the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Ultra_vires" title="Ultra vires">ultra vires</a> and a precedent of <a href="/wiki/Hadley_v_Baxendale" title="Hadley v Baxendale">Hadley v Baxendale</a> from English common law system.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_law_(legal_system)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Civil_law_notary" title="Civil law notary">Civil law notary</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rule_according_to_higher_law" title="Rule according to higher law">Rule according to higher law</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">Tort</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_national_legal_systems" title="List of national legal systems">List of national legal systems</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_law_(legal_system)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
