Synthetic polymer paint and silkscreen ink on wood, 10 inches \xc3\x97 19 inches \xc3\x97 9\xc2\xbd inches (25.4 \xc3\x97 48.3 \xc3\x97 24.1 cm), <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Pop art</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Art_movement" title="Art movement">art movement</a> that emerged in <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">Britain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during the mid- to late-1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pop_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The movement presented a challenge to traditions of <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">fine art</a> by including imagery from <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mass_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass culture">mass culture</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comic_books" class="mw-redirect" title="Comic books">comic books</a> and mundane cultural objects. One of its aims is to use images of <i>popular</i> (as opposed to elitist) culture in art, emphasizing the banal or <a href="/wiki/Kitsch" title="Kitsch">kitschy</a> elements of any culture, most often through the use of <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">irony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ages_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ages-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is also associated with the artists\' use of mechanical means of reproduction or rendering techniques. In pop art, material is sometimes visually removed from its known context, isolated, or combined with unrelated material.<sup id="cite_ref-pop_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ages_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ages-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Among the early artists that shaped the pop art movement were <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Paolozzi" title="Eduardo Paolozzi">Eduardo Paolozzi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hamilton_(artist)" title="Richard Hamilton (artist)">Richard Hamilton</a> in <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">Britain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Larry_Rivers" title="Larry Rivers">Larry Rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rauschenberg" title="Robert Rauschenberg">Robert Rauschenberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Johns" title="Jasper Johns">Jasper Johns</a> among others in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Pop art is widely interpreted as a reaction to the then-dominant ideas of <a href="/wiki/Abstract_expressionism" title="Abstract expressionism">abstract expressionism</a>, as well as an expansion of those ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-iha_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iha-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Due to its utilization of <a href="/wiki/Found_objects" class="mw-redirect" title="Found objects">found objects</a> and images, it is similar to <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dada</a>. Pop art and <a href="/wiki/Minimalism" title="Minimalism">minimalism</a> are considered to be art movements that precede <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_art" title="Postmodern art">postmodern art</a>, or are some of the earliest examples of postmodern art themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pop art often takes imagery that is currently in use in advertising. Product labeling and logos figure prominently in the imagery chosen by pop artists, seen in the labels of <i><a href="/wiki/Campbell%27s_Soup_Cans" title="Campbell&#39;s Soup Cans">Campbell\'s Soup Cans</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>. Even the labeling on the outside of a shipping box containing food items for retail has been used as subject matter in pop art, as demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Warhol\'s</a> <i>Campbell\'s Tomato Juice Box,</i> 1964 (pictured).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The origins of pop art in North America developed differently from Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-ages_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ages-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, pop art was a response by artists; it marked a return to <a href="/wiki/Hard-edge_painting" title="Hard-edge painting">hard-edged</a> composition and <a href="/wiki/Representation_(arts)" title="Representation (arts)">representational art</a>. They used impersonal, mundane reality, <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">irony</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> to "defuse" the personal symbolism and "<a href="/wiki/Painterly" class="mw-redirect" title="Painterly">painterly</a> looseness" of <a href="/wiki/Abstract_expressionism" title="Abstract expressionism">abstract expressionism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iha_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iha-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-high_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-high-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the U.S., some artwork by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Rivers" title="Larry Rivers">Larry Rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Katz" title="Alex Katz">Alex Katz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">Man Ray</a> anticipated pop art.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By contrast, the origins of pop art in <a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">post-War</a> Britain, while employing irony and parody, were more academic. Britain focused on the dynamic and paradoxical imagery of American <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">pop culture</a> as powerful, manipulative symbolic devices that were affecting whole patterns of life, while simultaneously improving the prosperity of a society.<sup id="cite_ref-high_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-high-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Early pop art in Britain was a matter of ideas fueled by <a href="/wiki/American_popular_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="American popular culture">American popular culture</a> <i>when viewed from afar</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-iha_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iha-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Similarly, pop art was both an extension and a repudiation of <a href="/wiki/Dadaism" class="mw-redirect" title="Dadaism">Dadaism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iha_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iha-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> While pop art and Dadaism explored some of the same subjects, pop art replaced the destructive, satirical, and anarchic impulses of the Dada movement with a detached affirmation of the artifacts of mass culture.<sup id="cite_ref-iha_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iha-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Among those artists in Europe seen as producing work leading up to pop art are: <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">Marcel Duchamp</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schwitters" title="Kurt Schwitters">Kurt Schwitters</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-pop">Proto-pop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NY_Met_demuth_figure_5_gold.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Demuth" title="Charles Demuth">Charles title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Although both British and American pop art began during the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">Marcel Duchamp</a> and others in Europe like <a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">Francis Picabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">Man Ray</a> predate the movement; in addition there were some earlier American <i>proto-pop</i> origins which utilized "as found" cultural objects.<sup id="cite_ref-iha_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iha-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During the 1920s, American artists <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Henry_Bruce" title="Patrick Henry Bruce">Patrick Henry Bruce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Murphy" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerald Murphy">Gerald Murphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Demuth" title="Charles Demuth">Charles Demuth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Davis_(painter)" title="Stuart Davis (painter)">Stuart Davis</a> created paintings that contained pop culture imagery (mundane objects culled from American commercial products and advertising design), almost "prefiguring" the pop art movement.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom:_the_Independent_Group">United Kingdom: the Independent Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom: the Independent Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hamilton-appealing2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A collage of many different styles shows a mostly naked man and woman in a house." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/ff/Hamilton-appealing2.jpg/220px-Hamilton-appealing2.jpg" width="220" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/ff/Hamilton-appealing2.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hamilton-appealing2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Richard_Hamilton_(artist)" title="Richard Hamilton (artist)">Richard Hamilton</a>\'s collage <i><a href="/wiki/Just_what_is_it_that_makes_today%27s_homes_so_different,_so_appealing%3F" title="Just what is it that makes today&#39;s homes so different, so appealing?">Just what is it that makes today\'s homes so different, so appealing?</a></i> (1956) is one of the earliest works to be considered "pop art".</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Independent_Group" title="Independent Group">Independent Group</a> (IG), founded in London in 1952, is regarded as the precursor to the pop art movement.<sup id="cite_ref-pop_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mod_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They were a gathering of young painters, sculptors, architects, writers and critics who were challenging prevailing modernist approaches to culture as well as traditional views of fine art. Their group discussions centered on pop culture implications from elements such as mass advertising, movies, product design, comic strips, science fiction and technology. At the first Independent Group meeting in 1952, co-founding member, artist and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Paolozzi" title="Eduardo Paolozzi">Eduardo Paolozzi</a> presented a lecture using a series of <a href="/wiki/Collage" title="Collage">collages</a> titled <i>Bunk!</i> that he had assembled during his time in Paris between 1947 and 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-pop_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mod_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This material of "found objects" such as advertising, comic book characters, magazine covers and various mass-produced graphics mostly represented <i>American</i> popular culture. One of the collages in that presentation was Paolozzi\'s <i>I was a Rich Man\'s Plaything</i> (1947), which includes the first use of the word "pop", appearing in a cloud of smoke emerging from a revolver.<sup id="cite_ref-pop_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Following Paolozzi\'s seminal presentation in 1952, the IG focused primarily on the imagery of American popular culture, particularly mass advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-high_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-high-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the son of <a href="/wiki/John_McHale_(artist)" title="John McHale (artist)">John McHale</a>, the term "pop art" was first coined by his father in 1954 in conversation with <a href="/wiki/Frank_Cordell" title="Frank Cordell">Frank Cordell</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Warholstars.org_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warholstars.org-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> although other sources credit its origin to British critic <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Alloway" title="Lawrence Alloway">Lawrence Alloway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> (Both versions agree that the term was used in <a href="/wiki/Independent_Group" title="Independent Group">Independent Group</a> discussions by mid-1955.)\n</p><p>"Pop art" as a moniker was then used in discussions by IG members in the Second Session of the IG in 1955, and the specific term "pop art" first appeared in published print in the article "But Today We Collect Ads" by IG members <a href="/wiki/Alison_and_Peter_Smithson" title="Alison and Peter Smithson">Alison and Peter Smithson</a> in Ark magazine in 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> However, the term is often credited to <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Art_critic" title="Art critic">art critic</a>/<a href="/wiki/Curator" title="Curator">curator</a> <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Alloway" title="Lawrence Alloway">Lawrence Alloway</a> for his 1958 essay titled <i>The Arts and the Mass Media</i>, even though the precise language he uses is "popular mass culture".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> "Furthermore, what I meant by it then is not what it means now. I used the term, and also \'Pop Culture\' to refer to the products of the mass media, not to works of art that draw upon popular culture. In any case, sometime between the winter of 1954-55 and 1957 the phrase acquired currency in conversation..."<sup id="cite_ref-Honnef_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honnef-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, Alloway was one of the leading critics to defend the inclusion of the imagery of mass culture in the fine arts. Alloway clarified these terms in 1966, at which time Pop Art had already transited from art schools and small galleries to a major force in the artworld. But its success had not been in England. Practically simultaneously, and independently, New York City had become the hotbed for Pop Art.<sup id="cite_ref-Honnef_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honnef-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In London, the annual <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_British_Artists" title="Royal Society of British Artists">Royal Society of British Artists (RBA)</a> exhibition of young talent in 1960 first showed American pop influences. In January 1961, the most famous RBA-<i>Young Contemporaries</i> of all put <a href="/wiki/David_Hockney" title="David Hockney">David Hockney</a>, the American <a href="/wiki/R_B_Kitaj" class="mw-redirect" title="R B Kitaj">R B Kitaj</a>, New Zealander <a href="/wiki/Billy_Apple" title="Billy Apple">Billy Apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Jones_(sculptor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Allen Jones (sculptor)">Allen Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Boshier" title="Derek Boshier">Derek Boshier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Tilson" title="Joe Tilson">Joe Tilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Caulfield" title="Patrick Caulfield">Patrick Caulfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Phillips_(artist)" title="Peter Phillips (artist)">Peter Phillips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Peter Blake</a> on the map; Apple designed the posters and invitations for both the 1961 and 1962 Young Contemporaries exhibitions.<sup id="cite_ref-Barton_2010_11\xe2\x80\x9321_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barton_2010_11\xe2\x80\x9321-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Hockney, Kitaj and Blake went on to win prizes at the John-Moores-Exhibition in Liverpool in the same year. Apple and Hockney traveled together to New York during the Royal College\'s 1961 summer break, which is when Apple first made contact with Andy Warhol \xe2\x80\x93 both later moved to the United States and Apple became involved with the New York pop art scene.<sup id="cite_ref-Barton_2010_11\xe2\x80\x9321_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barton_2010_11\xe2\x80\x9321-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although pop art began in the early 1950s, in America it was given its greatest impetus during the 1960s. The term "pop art" was officially introduced in December 1962; the occasion was a "Symposium on Pop Art" organized by the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SchermanTony_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchermanTony-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> By this time, American advertising had adopted many elements and inflections of modern art and functioned at a very sophisticated level. Consequently, American artists had to search deeper for dramatic styles that would distance art from the well-designed and clever commercial materials.<sup id="cite_ref-high_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-high-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As the British viewed American popular culture imagery from a somewhat removed perspective, their views were often instilled with romantic, sentimental and humorous overtones. By contrast, American artists, bombarded every day with the diversity of mass-produced imagery, produced work that was generally more bold and aggressive.<sup id="cite_ref-mod_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roy_Lichtenstein_Drowning_Girl.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A woman&#39;s crying face is overwhelmed by waves as she thinks, &quot;I don&#39;t care! I&#39;d rather sink than call Brad for help!&quot;" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/df/Roy_Lichtenstein_Drowning_Girl.jpg/220px-Roy_Lichtenstein_Drowning_Girl.jpg" width="220" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/df/Roy_Lichtenstein_Drowning_Girl.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="314" data-file-height="318" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roy_Lichtenstein_Drowning_Girl.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Roy_Lichtenstein" title="Roy Lichtenstein">Roy Lichtenstein</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drowning_Girl" title="Drowning Girl">Drowning Girl</a></i>, 1963, on display at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum of Modern Art, New York">Museum of Modern Art, New York</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Two important painters in the establishment of America\'s pop art vocabulary were <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Johns" title="Jasper Johns">Jasper Johns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rauschenberg" title="Robert Rauschenberg">Robert Rauschenberg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mod_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> While the paintings of Rauschenberg have relationships to the earlier work of <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schwitters" title="Kurt Schwitters">Kurt Schwitters</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dada</a> artists, his concern was for the social issues of the moment. His approach was to create art out of ephemeral materials. By using topical events in the life of everyday America, he gave his work a unique quality.<sup id="cite_ref-mod_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Johns\' and Rauschenberg\'s work of the 1950s is classified as <a href="/wiki/Neo-Dada" title="Neo-Dada">Neo-Dada</a>, and is visually distinct from the prototypical American pop art which exploded in the early 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheddar_Cheese_crop_from_Campbells_Soup_Cans_MOMA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d1/Cheddar_Cheese_crop_from_Campbells_Soup_Cans_MOMA.jpg/220px-Cheddar_Cheese_crop_from_Campbells_Soup_Cans_MOMA.jpg" width="220" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d1/Cheddar_Cheese_crop_from_Campbells_Soup_Cans_MOMA.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="242" data-file-height="278" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheddar_Cheese_crop_from_Campbells_Soup_Cans_MOMA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Cheddar Cheese</i> canvas from <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>\'s <i>Campbell\'s Soup Cans</i>, 1962.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Roy_Lichtenstein" title="Roy Lichtenstein">Roy Lichtenstein</a> is of equal importance to American pop art. His work, and its use of <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a>, probably defines the basic premise of pop art better than any other.<sup id="cite_ref-mod_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Selecting the old-fashioned comic strip as subject matter, Lichtenstein produces a hard-edged, precise composition that documents while also parodying in a soft manner. Lichtenstein used <a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magna_paint" class="mw-redirect" title="Magna paint">Magna paint</a> in his best known works, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Drowning_Girl" title="Drowning Girl">Drowning Girl</a></i> (1963), which was <a href="/wiki/Appropriation_(art)" title="Appropriation (art)">appropriated</a> from the lead story in <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>\' <i>Secret Hearts</i> #83. (<i>Drowning Girl</i> is part of the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>.)<sup id="cite_ref-rlf-Hendrickson31_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rlf-Hendrickson31-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> His work features thick outlines, bold colors and <a href="/wiki/Ben-Day_dots" title="Ben-Day dots">Ben-Day dots</a> to represent certain colors, as if created by photographic reproduction. Lichtenstein said, "[abstract expressionists] put things down on the canvas and responded to what they had done, to the color positions and sizes. My style looks completely different, but the nature of putting down lines pretty much is the same; mine just don\'t come out looking calligraphic, like Pollock\'s or Kline\'s."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Pop art merges popular and mass culture with fine art while injecting humor, irony, and recognizable imagery/content into the mix.\n</p><p>The paintings of Lichtenstein, like those of Andy Warhol, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wesselmann" title="Tom Wesselmann">Tom Wesselmann</a> and others, share a direct attachment to the commonplace image of American popular culture, but also treat the subject in an impersonal manner clearly illustrating the idealization of mass production.<sup id="cite_ref-mod_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Andy Warhol is probably the most famous figure in pop art. In fact, art critic Arthur Danto once called Warhol "the nearest thing to a philosophical genius the history of art has produced".<sup id="cite_ref-SchermanTony_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchermanTony-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  Warhol attempted to take pop beyond an artistic style to a life style, and his work often displays a lack of human affectation that dispenses with the irony and parody of many of his peers.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_U.S._exhibitions">Early U.S. exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early U.S. exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Claes_Oldenburg" title="Claes Oldenburg">Claes Oldenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Dine" title="Jim Dine">Jim Dine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wesselmann" title="Tom Wesselmann">Tom Wesselmann</a> had their first shows in the <a href="/wiki/Judson_Memorial_Church#Sponsorship_of_the_arts" title="Judson Memorial Church">Judson Gallery</a> in 1959 and 1960 and later in 1960 through 1964 along with <a href="/wiki/James_Rosenquist" title="James Rosenquist">James Rosenquist</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Segal_(artist)" title="George Segal (artist)">George Segal</a> and others at the <a href="/wiki/Green_Gallery" title="Green Gallery">Green Gallery</a> on 57th Street in Manhattan. In 1960, Martha Jackson showed <a href="/wiki/Installation_art" title="Installation art">installations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assemblage_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Assemblage art">assemblages</a>, <i>New Media - New Forms</i> featured <a href="/wiki/Hans_Arp" class="mw-redirect" title="Hans Arp">Hans Arp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schwitters" title="Kurt Schwitters">Kurt Schwitters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Johns" title="Jasper Johns">Jasper Johns</a>, Claes Oldenburg, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rauschenberg" title="Robert Rauschenberg">Robert Rauschenberg</a>, Jim Dine and <a href="/wiki/May_Wilson" title="May Wilson">May Wilson</a>. 1961 was the year of Martha Jackson\'s spring show, <i>Environments, Situations, Spaces</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Andy Warhol held his first solo exhibition in Los Angeles in July 1962 at <a href="/wiki/Ferus_Gallery" title="Ferus Gallery">Irving Blum\'s Ferus Gallery</a>, where he showed 32 paintings of Campell\'s soup cans, one for every flavor. Warhol sold the set of paintings to Blum for $1,000; in 1996, when the Museum of Modern Art acquired it, the set was valued at $15 million.<sup id="cite_ref-SchermanTony_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchermanTony-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Donald Factor, the son of <a href="/wiki/Max_Factor,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Max Factor, Jr.">Max Factor, Jr.</a>, and an art collector and co-editor of <a href="/wiki/Avant_garde" class="mw-redirect" title="Avant garde">avant garde</a> <a href="/wiki/Literary_magazine" title="Literary magazine">literary magazine</a> <a href="/wiki/Nomad_(magazine)" title="Nomad (magazine)"><i>Nomad</i></a>, wrote an essay in the magazine\'s last issue, <i>Nomad/New York</i>. The essay was one of the first on what would become known as pop art, though Factor did not use the term. The essay, "Four Artists", focused on Roy Lichtenstein, <a href="/wiki/James_Rosenquist" title="James Rosenquist">James Rosenquist</a>, Jim Dine, and Claes Oldenburg.<sup id="cite_ref-Diggory_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diggory-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1960s, Oldenburg, who became associated with the pop art movement, created many <i><a href="/wiki/Happening" title="Happening">happenings</a></i>, which were <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance art</a>-related productions of that time. The name he gave to his own productions was "Ray Gun Theater". The cast of colleagues in his performances included: artists <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Samaras" title="Lucas Samaras">Lucas Samaras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wesselman" class="mw-redirect" title="Tom Wesselman">Tom Wesselman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carolee_Schneemann" title="Carolee Schneemann">Carolee Schneemann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oyvind_Fahlstrom" class="mw-redirect" title="Oyvind Fahlstrom">Oyvind Fahlstrom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Artschwager" title="Richard Artschwager">Richard Artschwager</a>; dealer Annina Nosei; <a href="/wiki/Art_critic" title="Art critic">art critic</a> <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Rose" title="Barbara Rose">Barbara Rose</a>; and screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Wurlitzer" title="Rudy Wurlitzer">Rudy Wurlitzer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-articles.latimes.com_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-articles.latimes.com-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> His first wife, Patty Mucha, who sewed many of his early soft sculptures, was a constant performer in his happenings. This brash, often humorous, approach to art was at great odds with the prevailing sensibility that, by its nature, art dealt with "profound" expressions or ideas. In December 1961, he rented a store on Manhattan\'s <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a> to house <i>The Store</i>, a month-long installation he had first presented at the Martha Jackson Gallery in New York, stocked with sculptures roughly in the form of consumer goods.<sup id="cite_ref-articles.latimes.com_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-articles.latimes.com-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Opening in 1962, <a href="/wiki/Willem_de_Kooning" title="Willem de Kooning">Willem de Kooning\'s</a> New York art dealer, the <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Janis" title="Sidney Janis">Sidney Janis</a> Gallery, organized the groundbreaking <i>International Exhibition of the New Realists</i>, a survey of new-to-the-scene American, French, Swiss, Italian <i><a href="/wiki/New_Realism" class="mw-redirect" title="New Realism">New Realism</a></i>, and British pop art. The fifty-four artists shown included <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindner_(painter)" title="Richard Lindner (painter)">Richard Lindner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Thiebaud" title="Wayne Thiebaud">Wayne Thiebaud</a>, Roy Lichtenstein (and his painting <a href="/wiki/Blam_(Roy_Lichtenstein)" title="Blam (Roy Lichtenstein)">Blam</a>), Andy Warhol, Claes Oldenburg, <a href="/wiki/James_Rosenquist" title="James Rosenquist">James Rosenquist</a>, Jim Dine, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Indiana" title="Robert Indiana">Robert Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wesselmann" title="Tom Wesselmann">Tom Wesselmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Segal_(artist)" title="George Segal (artist)">George Segal</a>, Peter Phillips, Peter Blake (<i>The Love Wall</i> from 1961), <a href="/wiki/Yves_Klein" title="Yves Klein">Yves Klein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arman" title="Arman">Arman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Spoerri" title="Daniel Spoerri">Daniel Spoerri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christo" class="mw-redirect" title="Christo">Christo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mimmo_Rotella" title="Mimmo Rotella">Mimmo Rotella</a>. The show was seen by Europeans <a href="/wiki/Martial_Raysse" title="Martial Raysse">Martial Raysse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niki_de_Saint-Phalle" class="mw-redirect" title="Niki de Saint-Phalle">Niki de Saint-Phalle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Tinguely" title="Jean Tinguely">Jean Tinguely</a> in New York, who were stunned by the size and look of the American artwork. Also shown were <a href="/wiki/Marisol_Escobar" title="Marisol Escobar">Marisol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mario_Schifano" title="Mario Schifano">Mario Schifano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Baj" title="Enrico Baj">Enrico Baj</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%96yvind_Fahlstr%C3%B6m" title="\xc3\x96yvind Fahlstr\xc3\xb6m">\xc3\x96yvind Fahlstr\xc3\xb6m</a>. Janis lost some of his abstract expressionist artists when <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rothko" title="Mark Rothko">Mark Rothko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Motherwell" title="Robert Motherwell">Robert Motherwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adolph_Gottlieb" title="Adolph Gottlieb">Adolph Gottlieb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Guston" title="Philip Guston">Philip Guston</a> quit the gallery, but gained Dine, Oldenburg, Segal and Wesselmann.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> At an opening-night soiree thrown by collector Burton Tremaine, Willem de Kooning appeared and was turned away by Tremaine, who ironically owned a number of de Kooning\'s works. Rosenquist recalled: "at that moment I thought, something in the art world has definitely changed".<sup id="cite_ref-SchermanTony_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchermanTony-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Turning away a respected abstract artist proved that, as early as 1962, the pop art movement had begun to dominate art culture in New York.\n</p><p>A bit earlier, on the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a>, Roy Lichtenstein, Jim Dine and Andy Warhol from New York City; <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Hefferton" title="Phillip Hefferton">Phillip Hefferton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dowd_(artist)" title="Robert Dowd (artist)">Robert Dowd</a> from Detroit; <a href="/wiki/Edward_Ruscha" title="Edward Ruscha">Edward Ruscha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Goode" title="Joe Goode">Joe Goode</a> from Oklahoma City; and Wayne Thiebaud from California were included in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Painting_of_Common_Objects" title="New Painting of Common Objects">New Painting of Common Objects</a></i> show. This first pop art museum exhibition in America was curated by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hopps" title="Walter Hopps">Walter Hopps</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Pasadena_Art_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Pasadena Art Museum">Pasadena Art Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  Pop art was ready to change the art world. New York followed Pasadena in 1963, when the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Museum" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum">Guggenheim Museum</a> exhibited <i>Six Painters and the Object</i>, curated by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Alloway" title="Lawrence Alloway">Lawrence Alloway</a>. The artists were Jim Dine, Jasper Johns, Roy Lichtenstein, Robert Rauschenberg, James Rosenquist, and Andy Warhol.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Another pivotal early exhibition was <i>The American Supermarket</i> organised by the Bianchini Gallery in 1964. The show was presented as a typical small supermarket environment, except that everything in it\xe2\x80\x94the produce, canned goods, meat, posters on the wall, etc.\xe2\x80\x94was created by prominent pop artists of the time, including Apple, Warhol, Lichtenstein, Wesselmann, Oldenburg, and Johns. This project was recreated in 2002 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a>\'s <i>Shopping: A Century of Art and Consumer Culture</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 1962, pop artists started exhibiting in commercial galleries in New York and Los Angeles; for some, it was their first commercial one-man show. The <a href="/wiki/Ferus_Gallery" title="Ferus Gallery">Ferus Gallery</a> presented Andy Warhol in Los Angeles (and <a href="/wiki/Ed_Ruscha" class="mw-redirect" title="Ed Ruscha">Ed Ruscha</a> in 1963). In New York, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Gallery" title="Green Gallery">Green Gallery</a> showed Rosenquist, Segal, Oldenburg, and Wesselmann. The <a href="/wiki/Stable_Gallery" title="Stable Gallery">Stable Gallery</a> showed R. Indiana and Warhol (in his first New York show). The <a href="/wiki/Leo_Castelli" title="Leo Castelli">Leo Castelli Gallery</a> presented Rauschenberg, Johns, and Lichtenstein. Martha Jackson showed Jim Dine and Allen Stone showed Wayne Thiebaud. By 1966, after the Green Gallery and the Ferus Gallery closed, the Leo Castelli Gallery represented Rosenquist, Warhol, Rauschenberg, Johns, Lichtenstein and Ruscha. The Sidney Janis Gallery represented Oldenburg, Segal, Dine, Wesselmann and Marisol, while Allen Stone continued to represent Thiebaud, and Martha Jackson continued representing Robert Indiana.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1968, the <i><a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_Art_Biennial" title="S\xc3\xa3o Paulo Art Biennial">S\xc3\xa3o Paulo 9 Exhibition \xe2\x80\x93 Environment U.S.A.: 1957\xe2\x80\x931967</a></i> featured the "Who\'s Who" of pop art. Considered as a summation of the <i>classical phase</i> of the American pop art period, the exhibit was curated by William Seitz. The artists were <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hopper" title="Edward Hopper">Edward Hopper</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Gill_(artist)" title="James Gill (artist)">James Gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Indiana" title="Robert Indiana">Robert Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Johns" title="Jasper Johns">Jasper Johns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Lichtenstein" title="Roy Lichtenstein">Roy Lichtenstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claes_Oldenburg" title="Claes Oldenburg">Claes Oldenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rauschenberg" title="Robert Rauschenberg">Robert Rauschenberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wesselmann" title="Tom Wesselmann">Tom Wesselmann</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Nouveau_r%C3%A9alisme" title="Nouveau r\xc3\xa9alisme">Nouveau r\xc3\xa9alisme</a></i> refers to an artistic movement founded in 1960 by the art critic <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Restany" title="Pierre Restany">Pierre Restany</a><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and the artist <a href="/wiki/Yves_Klein" title="Yves Klein">Yves Klein</a> during the first collective exposition in the Apollinaire gallery in Milan. Pierre Restany wrote the original manifesto for the group, titled the "Constitutive Declaration of New Realism," in April 1960, proclaiming, "Nouveau R\xc3\xa9alisme\xe2\x80\x94new ways of perceiving the real."<sup id="cite_ref-Stremmel13_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stremmel13-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This joint declaration was signed on 27 October 1960, in Yves Klein\'s workshop, by nine people: <a href="/wiki/Yves_Klein" title="Yves Klein">Yves Klein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arman" title="Arman">Arman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martial_Raysse" title="Martial Raysse">Martial Raysse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Restany" title="Pierre Restany">Pierre Restany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Spoerri" title="Daniel Spoerri">Daniel Spoerri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Tinguely" title="Jean Tinguely">Jean Tinguely</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ultra-Lettrist" title="Ultra-Lettrist">Ultra-Lettrists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francois_Dufr%C3%AAne" class="mw-redirect" title="Francois Dufr\xc3\xaane">Francois Dufr\xc3\xaane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Hains" title="Raymond Hains">Raymond Hains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_de_la_Villegl%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacques de la Villegl\xc3\xa9">Jacques de la Villegl\xc3\xa9</a>; in 1961 these were joined by <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Baldaccini" title="C\xc3\xa9sar Baldaccini">C\xc3\xa9sar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mimmo_Rotella" title="Mimmo Rotella">Mimmo Rotella</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Niki_de_Saint_Phalle" title="Niki de Saint Phalle">Niki de Saint Phalle</a> and <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Deschamps" title="G\xc3\xa9rard Deschamps">G\xc3\xa9rard Deschamps</a>. The artist <a href="/wiki/Christo" class="mw-redirect" title="Christo">Christo</a> showed with the group. It was dissolved in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-Stremmel13_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stremmel13-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Contemporary of American Pop Art\xe2\x80\x94often conceived as its transposition in France\xe2\x80\x94new realism was along with <a href="/wiki/Fluxus" title="Fluxus">Fluxus</a> and other groups one of the numerous tendencies of the avant-garde in the 1960s. The group initially chose <a href="/wiki/Nice_(France)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nice (France)">Nice</a>, on the French Riviera, as its home base since Klein and Arman both originated there; new realism is thus often retrospectively considered by historians to be an early representative of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89cole_de_Nice&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="\xc3\x89cole de Nice (page does not exist)">\xc3\x89cole de Nice</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_de_Nice" class="extiw" title="fr:\xc3\x89cole de Nice">fr</a>)</span> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In spite of the diversity of their plastic language, they perceived a common basis for their work; this being a method of direct <a href="/wiki/Appropriation_(art)" title="Appropriation (art)">appropriation</a> of reality, equivalent, in the terms used by Restany; to a "poetic recycling of urban, industrial and advertising reality".<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Spain, the study of pop art is associated with the "new figurative", which arose from the roots of the crisis of <a href="/wiki/Informalism" title="Informalism">informalism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Arroyo" title="Eduardo Arroyo">Eduardo Arroyo</a> could be said to fit within the pop art trend, on account of his interest in the environment, his critique of our media culture which incorporates icons of both <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a> communication and the history of painting, and his scorn for nearly all established artistic styles. However, the Spanish artist who could be considered most authentically part of "pop" art is Alfredo Alca\xc3\xadn, because of the use he makes of popular images and empty spaces in his compositions.\n</p><p>Also in the category of Spanish pop art is the "Chronicle Team" (<i>El Equipo Cr\xc3\xb3nica</i>), which existed in <a href="/wiki/Valencia_(city_in_Spain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Valencia (city in Spain)">Valencia</a> between 1964 and 1981, formed by the artists <a href="/wiki/Manolo_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Manolo Vald\xc3\xa9s">Manolo Vald\xc3\xa9s</a> and Rafael Solbes. Their movement can be characterized as "pop" because of its use of comics and publicity images and its simplification of images and photographic compositions. <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">Filmmaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Almod%C3%B3var" title="Pedro Almod\xc3\xb3var">Pedro Almod\xc3\xb3var</a> emerged from Madrid\'s "La Movida" subculture of the 1970s making low budget <a href="/wiki/Super_8_mm_film" class="mw-redirect" title="Super 8 mm film">super 8</a> pop art movies, and he was subsequently called the Andy Warhol of Spain by the media at the time. In the book <i>Almodovar on Almodovar</i>, he is quoted as saying that the 1950s film "Funny Face" was a central inspiration for his work. One pop trademark in Almodovar\'s films is that he always produces a fake commercial to be inserted into a scene.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Japan, pop art evolved from the nation\'s prominent <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> scene. The use of images of the modern world, copied from magazines in the photomontage-style paintings produced by <a href="/wiki/Harue_Koga" title="Harue Koga">Harue Koga</a> in the late 1920s and early 1930s, foreshadowed elements of pop art.<sup id="cite_ref-eskolahk_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eskolahk-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The work of <a href="/wiki/Yayoi_Kusama" title="Yayoi Kusama">Yayoi Kusama</a> contributed to the development of pop art and influenced many other artists, including Andy Warhol.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In the mid-1960s, graphic designer <a href="/wiki/Tadanori_Yokoo" title="Tadanori Yokoo">Tadanori Yokoo</a> became one of the most successful pop artists and an international symbol for Japanese pop art. He is well known for his advertisements and creating artwork for pop culture icons such as commissions from <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Taylor" title="Elizabeth Taylor">Elizabeth Taylor</a>, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Another leading pop artist at that time was <a href="/wiki/Keiichi_Tanaami" title="Keiichi Tanaami">Keiichi Tanaami</a>. Iconic characters from Japanese <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> have also become symbols for pop art, such as <a href="/wiki/Speed_Racer" title="Speed Racer">Speed Racer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy" title="Astro Boy">Astro Boy</a>. Japanese manga and anime also influenced later pop artists such as <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Murakami" title="Takashi Murakami">Takashi Murakami</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Superflat" title="Superflat">superflat</a> movement.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Italy, by 1964, pop art was known and took different forms, such as the "Scuola di Piazza del Popolo" in Rome, with pop artists such as <a href="/wiki/Mario_Schifano" title="Mario Schifano">Mario Schifano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franco_Angeli" title="Franco Angeli">Franco Angeli</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giosetta_Fioroni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Giosetta Fioroni (page does not exist)">Giosetta Fioroni</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tano_Festa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tano Festa (page does not exist)">Tano Festa</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Claudio_Cintoli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Claudio Cintoli (page does not exist)">Claudio Cintoli</a>, and some artworks by <a href="/wiki/Piero_Manzoni" title="Piero Manzoni">Piero Manzoni</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucio_Del_Pezzo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lucio Del Pezzo (page does not exist)">Lucio Del Pezzo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mimmo_Rotella" title="Mimmo Rotella">Mimmo Rotella</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valerio_Adami" title="Valerio Adami">Valerio Adami</a>.\n</p><p>Italian pop art originated in 1950s culture \xe2\x80\x93 the works of the artists <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Baj" title="Enrico Baj">Enrico Baj</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mimmo_Rotella" title="Mimmo Rotella">Mimmo Rotella</a> to be precise, rightly considered the forerunners of this scene. In fact, it was around 1958\xe2\x80\x931959 that Baj and Rotella abandoned their previous careers (which might be generically defined as belonging to a <i>non-representational genre</i>, despite being thoroughly post-Dadaist), to catapult themselves into a new world of images, and the reflections on them, which was springing up all around them. Rotella\'s torn posters showed an ever more figurative taste, often explicitly and deliberately referring to the great icons of the times. Baj\'s compositions were steeped in contemporary <i>kitsch</i>, which turned out to be a "gold mine" of images and the stimulus for an entire generation of artists.\n</p><p>The novelty came from the new visual panorama, both inside "domestic walls" and out-of-doors. Cars, road signs, television, all the "new world", everything can belong to the world of art, which itself is new. In this respect, Italian pop art takes the same ideological path as that of the international scene. The only thing that changes is the iconography and, in some cases, the presence of a more critical attitude toward it. Even in this case, the prototypes can be traced back to the works of Rotella and Baj, both far from neutral in their relationship with society. Yet this is not an exclusive element; there is a long line of artists, including <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gianni_Ruffi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gianni Ruffi (page does not exist)">Gianni Ruffi</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roberto_Barni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Roberto Barni (page does not exist)">Roberto Barni</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Silvio_Pasotti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Silvio Pasotti (page does not exist)">Silvio Pasotti</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umberto_Bignardi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Umberto Bignardi (page does not exist)">Umberto Bignardi</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Claudio_Cintoli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Claudio Cintoli (page does not exist)">Claudio Cintoli</a>, who take on reality as a toy, as a great pool of imagery from which to draw material with disenchantment and frivolity, questioning the traditional linguistic role models with a renewed spirit of "let me have fun" \xc3\xa0 la <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Palazzeschi" title="Aldo Palazzeschi">Aldo Palazzeschi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium">Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, pop art was represented by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Van_Hoeydonck" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Van Hoeydonck">Paul Van Hoeydonck</a>, whose sculpture <i><a href="/wiki/Fallen_Astronaut" title="Fallen Astronaut">Fallen Astronaut</a></i> was left on the moon during one of the moon missions. Internationally recognized artists such as <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Broodthaers" title="Marcel Broodthaers">Marcel Broodthaers</a> (<i> \'vous \xc3\xaates doll? "</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Panamarenko" title="Panamarenko">Panamarenko</a> are indebted to the pop art movement; Broodthaers\'s great influence was <a href="/wiki/George_Segal" title="George Segal">George Segal</a>.  Another well-known artist, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Raveel" title="Roger Raveel">Roger Raveel</a>, mounted a birdcage with a real live pigeon in one of his paintings. By the end of the 1960s and early 1970s, pop art references disappeared from the work of these artists when they started to adopt a more critical attitude towards America because of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>\'s increasingly gruesome character. Panamarenko, however, has retained the irony inherent in the pop art movement up to the present day.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While there was no formal pop art movement in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, there were a group of artists that spent time in New York during the early years of pop art, and drew inspiration from the international pop art movement. Representatives of Dutch pop art include <a href="/wiki/Daan_van_Golden" title="Daan van Golden">Daan van Golden</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustave_Asselbergs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gustave Asselbergs (page does not exist)">Gustave Asselbergs</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacques_Frenken&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jacques Frenken (page does not exist)">Jacques Frenken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Cremer" title="Jan Cremer">Jan Cremer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wim_T._Schippers" title="Wim T. Schippers">Wim T. Schippers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Woody_van_Amen" title="Woody van Amen">Woody van Amen</a>. They opposed the Dutch <a href="/wiki/Petit_bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Petit bourgeois">petit bourgeois</a> mentality by creating humorous works with a serious undertone. Examples of this nature include <i>Sex O\'Clock,</i> by Woody van Amen, and <i>Crucifix / Target</i>, by Jacques Frenken.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_Federation">Russian Federation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Russian Federation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> was a little late to become part of the pop art movement, and some of the artwork that resembles pop art only surfaced around the early 1970s. Russia was a communist country at that point and bold artistic statements were closely monitored. Russia\'s own version of pop art was <a href="/wiki/Soviet" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet">Soviet</a>-themed and was referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Sots_Art" title="Sots Art">Sots Art</a></i>. After 1991, the Communist Party lost its power and the Russian revolution was beginning, and with it came a freedom to express. That is when pop art in Russia took on another form, epitomised by <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Vrubel" title="Dmitri Vrubel">Dmitri Vrubel</a> with his painting titled <i><a href="/wiki/My_God,_Help_Me_to_Survive_This_Deadly_Love" title="My God, Help Me to Survive This Deadly Love">My God, Help Me to Survive This Deadly Love</a></i> in 1990. One might argue that the Soviet posters made in the 1950s to promote the wealth of the nation were in itself a form of pop art.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_artists">Notable artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
