S. Thomas</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_poet" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh poet">Welsh poet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglican</a> priest who was noted for his nationalism, spirituality and deep dislike of the <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicisation</a> of Wales. <a href="/wiki/John_Betjeman" title="John Betjeman">John Betjeman</a>, in his 1955 introduction to <i>Song at the Year\'s Turning</i>, the first collection of Thomas\'s poetry to be produced by a major publisher, predicted that Thomas would be remembered long after he himself was forgotten. M. Wynn Thomas said: "He was the <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Solzhenitsyn" title="Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn">Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn</a> of Wales because he was such a troubler of the Welsh conscience. He was one of the major English language and European poets of the 20th century."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._S._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>R. S. Thomas was born in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, the only child of Thomas Hubert and Margaret (n\xc3\xa9e Davies). The family moved to <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a> in 1918 because of his father\'s work in the <a href="/wiki/Merchant_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Merchant Navy">Merchant Navy</a>. He was awarded a bursary in 1932 to study at <a href="/wiki/Bangor_University" title="Bangor University">Bangor University</a>, where he read <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. In 1936, after he completed his theological training at <a href="/wiki/St._Michael%27s_College,_Llandaff" title="St. Michael&#39;s College, Llandaff">St. Michael\'s College, Llandaff</a>, he was ordained as a priest in the <a href="/wiki/Church_in_Wales" title="Church in Wales">Church in Wales</a>. From 1936 to 1940 he was the curate of <a href="/wiki/Chirk" title="Chirk">Chirk</a>, Denbighshire, where he met his future wife, <a href="/wiki/Mildred_Eldridge" title="Mildred Eldridge">Mildred "Elsi" Eldridge</a>, an English artist. He subsequently became curate-in charge of <a href="/wiki/Willington_Worthenbury" title="Willington Worthenbury">Tallarn Green</a>, Flintshire, as part of his duties as curate of <a href="/wiki/Hanmer,_Wales" title="Hanmer, Wales">Hanmer</a>.\n</p><p>In Hanmer he was an assistant to the Revd Thomas Meredith-Morris, the grandfather of writer <a href="/wiki/Lorna_Sage" title="Lorna Sage">Lorna Sage</a>, a fact later described by <a href="/wiki/Byron_Rogers_(author)" title="Byron Rogers (author)">Byron Rogers</a> as a "crossing of paths of two of Wales\'s strangest clergymen", Whilst Sage devotes a great deal of her autobiography <i>Bad Blood</i> to her late relative, however, she does not mention Thomas at all, who was in any case in Hanmer before Sage was born. Her memoir gives some insight into the strange environment in which Thomas worked as a young priest.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Thomas never wrote a great deal of his curacies and nothing is known of the relationship between him and Meredith-Morris.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thomas and Eldridge were married in 1940 and remained together until her death in 1991. Their son, (Andreas) Gwydion (died 15 September 2016),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> was born on 29 August 1945. Gwydion often spoke about his difficult upbringing with a family living in near poverty, while he was sent to a number of traumatic boarding schools.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Thomas family lived on a tiny income and lacked the comforts of modern life, largely through their own choice. One of the few household amenities the family ever owned, a vacuum cleaner, was rejected because Thomas decided it was too noisy.<sup id="cite_ref-dalrymple_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalrymple-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 1942 to 1954 Thomas was rector of <a href="/wiki/St_Michael%27s_Church,_Manafon" title="St Michael&#39;s Church, Manafon">St Michael\'s Church, Manafon</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Welshpool" title="Welshpool">Welshpool</a> in rural <a href="/wiki/Montgomeryshire" title="Montgomeryshire">Montgomeryshire</a>. It was during his time in Manafon that he first began to study Welsh and that he published his first three volumes of poetry, <i>The Stones of the Field</i> (1946), <i>An Acre of Land</i> (1952) and <i>The Minister</i> (1953). Thomas\'s poetry achieved a breakthrough with the publication, in 1955, of his fourth book, <i>Song at the Year\'s Turning</i>, in effect a collected edition of his first three volumes, which was critically very well received and opened with Betjeman\'s famous introduction. His position was also helped by winning the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Literature" title="Royal Society of Literature">Royal Society of Literature</a>\'s Heinemann Award.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg/220px-Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg/330px-Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg/440px-Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aberdaron_church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_13372.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Hywyn,_Aberdaron" class="mw-redirect" title="Church of St Hywyn, Aberdaron">St Hywyn\'s Church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aberdaron" title="Aberdaron">Aberdaron</a> where Thomas was vicar from 1967 to 1978</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div id="mwe_player_0" class="PopUpMediaTransform" style="width:220px;" videopayload="&lt;div class=&quot;mediaContainer&quot; The 1960s saw him working in a predominantly Welsh-speaking community and he later wrote two prose works in Welsh, <i>Neb</i> (<i>Nobody</i>), an ironic and revealing autobiography written in the third person, and <i>Blwyddyn yn Ll\xc5\xb7n</i> (<i>A Year in Ll\xc5\xb7n</i>). In 1964 he won the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Gold_Medal_for_Poetry" title="Queen&#39;s Gold Medal for Poetry">Queen\'s Gold Medal for Poetry</a>. From 1967 to 1978 he was vicar of <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Hywyn,_Aberdaron" class="mw-redirect" title="Church of St Hywyn, Aberdaron">St Hywyn\'s Church</a> (built 1137) in <a href="/wiki/Aberdaron" title="Aberdaron">Aberdaron</a> at the western tip of the <a href="/wiki/Ll%C5%B7n_Peninsula" title="Ll\xc5\xb7n Peninsula">Ll\xc5\xb7n Peninsula</a>.\n</p><p>Thomas retired from church ministry in 1978 and he and his wife relocated to <a href="/wiki/Y_Rhiw" title="Y Rhiw">Y Rhiw</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> into "a tiny, unheated cottage in one of the most beautiful parts of Wales, where, however, the temperature sometimes dipped below freezing," according to <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Dalrymple" title="Theodore Dalrymple">Theodore Dalrymple</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dalrymple_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalrymple-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Free from the constraints of the church he was able to become more political and active in the campaigns that were important to him. He became a fierce advocate of <a href="/wiki/Welsh_nationalism" title="Welsh nationalism">Welsh nationalism</a>, although he never supported <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>, because he believed they did not go far enough in their opposition to England.\n</p><p>Thomas was nominated for the 1996 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the winner of which was <a href="/wiki/Wislawa_Szymborska" class="mw-redirect" title="Wislawa Szymborska">Wislawa Szymborska</a>. He received the 1996 <a href="/wiki/Lannan_Literary_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Lannan Literary Award">Lannan Literary Award</a> for Lifetime Achievement.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thomas died on 25 September 2000, aged 87, at his home in Pentrefelin near <a href="/wiki/Criccieth" title="Criccieth">Criccieth</a>, survived by his second wife, Elizabeth Vernon.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He had been ill with a heart condition and had been treated at <a href="/wiki/Ysbyty_Gwynedd" class="mw-redirect" title="Ysbyty Gwynedd">Ysbyty Gwynedd</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bangor,_Gwynedd" title="Bangor, Gwynedd">Bangor</a> until two weeks before he died.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A memorial event celebrating his life and poetry was held at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> with readings from Heaney, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Motion" title="Andrew Motion">Andrew Motion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gillian_Clarke" title="Gillian Clarke">Gillian Clarke</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Burnside_(writer)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Burnside (writer)">John Burnside</a>. Thomas\'s ashes are buried close to the door of St John\'s Church, <a href="/wiki/Porthmadog" title="Porthmadog">Porthmadog</a>, Gwynedd.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs_and_contribution_to_spirituality">Beliefs and contribution to spirituality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._S._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beliefs and contribution to spirituality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_views">Religious views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._S._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religious views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Thomas\'s son, Gwydion, a resident of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, recalls his father\'s sermons, in which he would "drone on" to absurd lengths about the evil of refrigerators, washing machines, televisions and other modern devices. Thomas preached that they were all part of the temptation of scrambling after gadgets rather than attending to more spiritual needs. "It was the Machine, you see," Gwydion explained to a biographer. "This to a congregation that didn\'t have any of these things and were longing for them."<sup id="cite_ref-dalrymple_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalrymple-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Although he may have taken some ideas to extreme lengths, Theodore Dalrymple wrote, Thomas "was raising a deep and unanswered question: What is life for? Is it simply to consume more and more, and divert ourselves with ever more elaborate entertainments and gadgetry? What will this do to our souls?"<sup id="cite_ref-dalrymple_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalrymple-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He had a reputation, which perhaps he cultivated, of being not always charitable and for being awkward and taciturn. Some critics have interpreted photographs of him as indicating he was "formidable, bad-tempered, and apparently humorless."<sup id="cite_ref-dalrymple_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalrymple-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In terms of religion, although he sometimes appeared to lack charity and patience, Thomas served as a <a href="/wiki/Church_in_Wales" title="Church in Wales">Church in Wales</a> parish priest all his working life. His training at <a href="/wiki/St._Michael%27s_College,_Llandaff" title="St. Michael&#39;s College, Llandaff">St. Michael\'s College, Llandaff</a> placed him somewhat in the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Movement" title="Oxford Movement">Tractarian Tradition</a>, though he does not seem to have been more than central in his position as regards the conduct of services.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Although a fervent Welsh Nationalist, he appears to have preferred the 1662 <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> (even in Welsh translation) over the 1966 order for the Holy Eucharist which the Church in Wales produced for itself and which came into use during his final year at <a href="/wiki/Eglwys_Fach" title="Eglwys Fach">Eglwys Fach</a>. In one of his autobiographical books, he asserted that (in retirement) he found that he could no longer bring himself to go to <a href="/wiki/Holy_Communion" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Communion">Holy Communion</a> on account of the changes, although one of his successors at Aberdaron did indicate that Thomas always retained the bishop\'s permission to officiate and occasionally did so at <a href="/wiki/Llanfaelrhys" title="Llanfaelrhys">Llanfaelrhys</a>, when no one else could be found.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> His primary objection to the revised services was that since the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> (1962\xe2\x80\x9365) \xe2\x80\x93 which also had liturgical repercussions within Anglicanism \xe2\x80\x93 he could not bear to see the priest facing the people when in reality he should be leading the people towards God from the traditional east-facing position. "It is to God that the mystery belongs," he wrote pointedly, "and woe to man when he interferes with that mystery. As T.S. Eliot says: \'Humankind cannot bear very much reality.\'"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Thomas seems early on to have become interested in <a href="/wiki/Theosophy_(Blavatskian)" title="Theosophy (Blavatskian)">Theosophy</a> (an interest he did not pursue beyond some interfaith study) and over time he appears to have had some sympathy with the theological explorations of one-time <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Woolwich" title="Bishop of Woolwich">Bishop of Woolwich</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Robinson_(bishop_of_Woolwich)" title="John Robinson (bishop of Woolwich)">John A.T. Robinson</a> in his 1963 <i>Honest to God</i>, on one occasion going as far as to describe the <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Resurrection of Christ">Resurrection of Christ</a> as a "metaphor". In some poems Thomas seems to abandon theism altogether, but in a letter to a theological student in 1993 he denied he held similar views to the non-realist Cambridge theologian and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Don_Cupitt" title="Don Cupitt">Don Cupitt</a>. "I believe in revelation," Thomas wrote, "and therefore one cannot describe all one\'s insights as entirely human."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Above all, his main influence appears to have been the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Kierkegaard" class="mw-redirect" title="Kierkegaard">Kierkegaard</a> \xe2\x80\x93 and his "leap of faith" \xe2\x80\x93 although he also appears to have concerned himself with the limitations of religious language in the face of an era which was becoming progressively more post-Christian in the face of science and philosophy. Yet for all his explorations, his sermons and practice as a priest do not seem to have been in any way heterodox,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> even if in retirement he was to write to his long-term friend, the poet <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Garlick" title="Raymond Garlick">Raymond Garlick</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> to give him "the address of a retired Christian".\n</p><p>As a priest, it seems that Thomas did not believe he was there to promote his own views, but those of the church he served, and for all his vaunted crabbiness, he seems to have been well enough regarded by parishioners, even if the biographies offer notable exceptions. He has been credited by some as a capable listener and counsellor at a time when such things were not in common vogue among the clergy, and to have been also a devoted visitor to the sick. His tendency, however, to be remote did lead to one of his successors saying that she had, as parish priest of Aberdaron, to "do a lot of healing". Yet in spite of this, his influence as a poet had a considerable impact on spirituality, to the extent that, at the centenary of his birth, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Wales" title="Archbishop of Wales">Archbishop of Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Morgan" title="Barry Morgan">Barry Morgan</a>, who had known the poet personally, paid tribute to Thomas:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>R. S. Thomas continues to articulate through his poetry questions that are inscribed on the heart of most Christian pilgrims in their search for meaning and truth. We search for God and feel Him near at hand, only then to blink and find Him gone. This poetry persuades us that we are not alone in this experience of faith \xe2\x80\x93 the poet has been there before us.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_views">Other views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._S._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Thomas believed in what he called "the true Wales of my imagination",<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> a Welsh-speaking aboriginal community that was in tune with the natural world. He viewed western (specifically English) materialism and greed, represented in the poetry by his mythical "Machine", as the destroyers of community. He could tolerate neither the English who bought up Wales, and in his view stripped it of its wild and essential nature, nor the Welsh whom he saw as all too eager to kowtow to English money and influence.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thomas was an ardent supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a> (CND) and described himself as a <a href="/wiki/Pacifist" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacifist">pacifist</a>, but also supported the <i><a href="/wiki/Meibion_Glynd%C5%B5r" title="Meibion Glynd\xc5\xb5r">Meibion Glynd\xc5\xb5r</a></i> fire bombings of English-owned holiday cottages in rural Wales. On this subject he said in 1998, "What is one death against the death of the whole Welsh nation?"<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He was also active in wildlife preservation and worked with the <a href="/wiki/RSPB" class="mw-redirect" title="RSPB">RSPB</a> and Welsh volunteer organisations for the preservation of the <a href="/wiki/Red_kite" title="Red kite">red kite</a>. He resigned his RSPB membership over their plans to introduce non-native kites to Wales.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._S._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:280px;&#32;; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9c ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9d ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>\n<div class="quotebox-quote quoted left-aligned" style=""><div class="poem">\n<p><b>Evans</b><br />\n<br />\nEvans? Yes, many a time<br />\nI came down his bare flight<br />\nOf stairs into the gaunt kitchen<br />\nWith its wood fire, where crickets sang<br />\nAccompaniment to the black kettle\'s<br />\nWhine, and so into the cold<br />\nDark to smother in the thick tide<br />\nOf night that drifted about the walls<br />\nOf his stark farm on the hill ridge.<br />\n<br />\nIt was not the dark filling my eyes<br />\nAnd mouth appalled me; not even the drip<br />\nOf rain like blood from the one tree<br />\nWeather-tortured. It was the dark<br />\nSilting the veins of that sick man<br />\nI left stranded upon the vast<br />\nAnd lonely shore of his bleak bed.<br />\n</p>\n</div></div>\n<p><cite class="right-aligned" style="">"Evans" from <i>Poetry for Supper</i> (1958)</cite>\n</p>\n</div>\n<p>Almost all of Thomas\'s work concerns the Welsh landscape and the Welsh people, themes with both political and spiritual subtext. His views on the position of the Welsh people, as a conquered people are never far below the surface. As a cleric, his religious views are also present in his works. His earlier works focus on the personal stories of his parishioners, the farm labourers and working men and their wives, challenging the cosy view of the traditional pastoral poem with harsh and vivid descriptions of rural lives. The beauty of the landscape, although ever-present, is never suggested as a compensation for the low pay or monotonous conditions of farm work. This direct view of "country life" comes as a challenge to many English writers writing on similar subjects and challenging the more pastoral works of contemporary poets such as <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">Dylan Thomas</a>.\n</p><p>Thomas\'s later works were of a more <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> nature, more experimental in their style and focusing more overtly on his spirituality. <i>Laboratories of the Spirit</i> (1975) gives, in its title, a hint at this development and also reveals Thomas\'s increasing experiments with scientific metaphor. He described this shift as an investigation into the "adult geometry of the mind". Fearing that poetry was becoming a dying art, inaccessible to those who most needed it, "he attempted to make spiritually minded poems relevant within, and relevant to, a science-minded, post-industrial world," to represent that world both in form and in content even as he rejected its machinations.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite his nationalism Thomas could be hard on his fellow countrymen. Often his works read as more of a criticism of Welshness than a celebration. He himself said there is a "lack of love for human beings" in his poetry. Other critics have not been so harsh. <a href="/wiki/Al_Alvarez" title="Al Alvarez">Al Alvarez</a> said, "He was wonderful, very pure, very bitter, but the bitterness was beautifully and very sparely rendered. He was completely authoritative, a very, very fine poet, completely off on his own, out of the loop but a real individual. It\'s not about being a major or minor poet. It\'s about getting a work absolutely right by your own standards and he did that wonderfully well."<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thomas\'s final works commonly sold 20,000 copies in Britain alone.<sup id="cite_ref-dalrymple_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalrymple-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R._S._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
