showing Hayle and St Ives Bay</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Hayle</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Heyl</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <abbr title="Literally">lit.</abbr> "estuary") is a small <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> and cargo port in west <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.  It is situated at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Hayle_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Hayle River">Hayle River</a> (which discharges into <a href="/wiki/St_Ives_Bay" title="St Ives Bay">St Ives Bay</a>) and is approximately seven miles (11&#160;km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hayle parish was created in 1888 from part of the now defunct <a href="/wiki/Phillack" title="Phillack">Phillack</a> parish, with which it was later combined in 1935, and incorporated part of <a href="/wiki/St_Erth" title="St Erth">St Erth</a> in 1937. The modern parish shares boundaries with <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cornwall" title="St Ives, Cornwall">St Ives</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/St_Erth" title="St Erth">St Erth</a> to the south, <a href="/wiki/Gwinear-Gwithian" class="mw-redirect" title="Gwinear-Gwithian">Gwinear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gwithian" title="Gwithian">Gwithian</a> in the east, and is bounded to the north by the <a Evidence of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> settlement exists at the fort on the hill above <a href="/wiki/Carnsew_Pool" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnsew Pool">Carnsew Pool</a> where the Plantation now stands. \n</p><p>It is thought that Hayle, was an important centre for the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> tin industry, trading not only <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breton_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Breton people">Breton people</a>, but also the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a> of the eastern Mediterranean. Evidence of this comes from finds of imported pottery including Romano/Grecian Amphorae - containers for wine and oil.\n</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> never conquered Cornwall they may have had a military presence in the Hayle Estuary, and it is thought that the rectangular churchyard at <a href="/wiki/St_Uny%27s_Church,_Lelant" title="St Uny&#39;s Church, Lelant">St Uny\'s Church, Lelant</a> on the western shore of the estuary is built within the outline of a Roman fort.<sup id="cite_ref-HayleHistAss_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HayleHistAss-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In those times the estuary looked a lot different from that of today. It appears that the estuary was deeper and it was possible for boats to go up the <a href="/wiki/River_Hayle" title="River Hayle">River Hayle</a> as far as where <a href="/wiki/St_Erth" title="St Erth">St Erth</a> Bridge is now situated; the tide used to flow in and out of what is now Foundry Square in the town, and at <a href="/wiki/Gwithian" title="Gwithian">Gwithian</a> reached inland some considerable distance toward <a href="/wiki/Connor_Downs" title="Connor Downs">Connor Downs</a>.\n</p><p>The departure of the Romans was followed by an influx of <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Missionaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Missionaries">missionaries</a>, most of whom are said to have had <a href="/wiki/History_of_Roman_Catholicism_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Roman Catholicism in Ireland">Irish origins</a> and after whom many Cornish towns take their present name. The lives of <a href="/wiki/Samson_of_Dol" title="Samson of Dol">Saint Samson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petroc" title="Saint Petroc">Saint Petroc</a> report that both saints arrived in Cornwall at the Hayle Estuary, indicating that it was an established port at least by the end of the 5th century.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>During the mid-6th century, however, the area was held by the <a href="/wiki/Cornouaille" title="Cornouaille">Breton</a> exile <a href="/wiki/Tewdwr_Mawr" title="Tewdwr Mawr">Tewdwr Mawr</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Teudar</i> or <i><i lang="kw" title="Cornish language text">Teudaric</i></i>), who was said to have <a href="/wiki/Martyr_of_the_Faith" class="mw-redirect" title="Martyr of the Faith">martyred</a> many of them&#8212;particularly the members of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Breaca" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Breaca">Saint Breaca</a>\'s mission&#8212;before returning to <a href="/wiki/Cornouaille" title="Cornouaille">his patrimony</a> around 577.<sup id="cite_ref-nash_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nash-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A number of inscribed stones from this period have been found in the area. Two early stones have been found at <a href="/wiki/Phillack" title="Phillack">Phillack</a>, one bearing a \'Constantine\' form of a Chi-Rho cross which may date to the 5th Century. The most noteworthy inscribed stone is one uncovered during the construction of a road in the grounds of Carnsew, and is now set into a bank at The Plantation, a public park. The stone was discovered in December 1843 by workmen, lying in a horizontal position at the depth of four feet. When the stone was moved it broke into three parts.\n</p><p>A Mr Harvey had it fixed into the wall of his path on Carnsew cliff, within a few feet of the spot where it was discovered, and added a more recent replica which lies next to it, where it has remained since. The stone bears an inscription in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, but it is now unreadable. The version that appears on the replica is translated as "Here Cenui fell asleep who was born in 500. Here in his tomb he lies, he lived 33 years." However, in her discussion of this inscription Elisabeth Okasha passes over this transcription in silence, and mentions only three early drawings of this inscription and the results of more recent inspections, then tentatively offers her translation: "Here in peace has rested Cunatdo [or <i>Cunaide</i>]. Here he lies in the tomb. He lived for 33 years."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While physical and documentary evidence indicates that the port continued to be of importance through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, it was the Industrial Revolution that saw the town and port of Hayle grow to resemble the town as seen today.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_period">Medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hayle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Domesday_survey" class="mw-redirect" title="Domesday survey">Domesday survey</a> in 1086 shows that the town of Hayle was not yet in existence. The manor of Connerton ("Conarditone") is recorded as including the Hayle Estuary with the manor centred on Conerton, close to the present day village of Gwithian. This was held by the King and was the headmanor of the hundred of Penwith.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>It is from Conerton that the name of the present day settlement of <a href="/wiki/Connor_Downs" title="Connor Downs">Connor Downs</a> is derived. A number of scattered farmsteads are recorded but no substantial settlement. By the 13th century Conerton was owned by the <a href="/wiki/Arundel" title="Arundel">Arundel</a> family until it was purchased by the Cornish Copper Company in the early 19th century.\n</p><p>The first documentary evidence of any settlements around the Hayle Estuary is in 1130 when Phillack Church and surrounding dwellings were recorded as "Egloshayle", meaning the church (eglos) on the estuary (heyl), with the church being dedicated originally to <a href="/wiki/Felec_of_Cornwall" title="Felec of Cornwall">St Felec</a>, as appears in a 10th-century <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Library" title="Vatican Library">Vatican codex</a>. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_revolution">Industrial revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hayle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small><i>(May 2010)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Hayle was initially a coal importing and ore exporting port but Hayle was initially dwarfed by nearby <a href="/wiki/Angarrack" title="Angarrack">Angarrack</a>, where a tin smelter was built in 1704 and mills and stamps converted/constructed to process the ore. Hayle\'s role was simply to serve as a convenient point to land coal from South Wales, which was then taken to Angarrack by mule. In 1710 a <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> <a href="/wiki/Smelter" class="mw-redirect" title="Smelter">smelter</a> was built at Mellanear Farm on the Mellanear stream which prospered for many years\n</p><p>Perhaps the first major development at Hayle was the construction of the first modern <a href="/wiki/Quay" class="mw-redirect" title="Quay">quay</a> by John "Merchant" Curnow, in the 1740s, to service the growing mining industry. In 1758 the Cornish Copper Company (CCCo) moved from <a href="/wiki/Camborne" title="Camborne">Camborne</a> and set up a <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> smelter at Ventonleague (Copperhouse Creek) and this proved very successful, so much so that a <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a> was built to bring vessels right up to the works and additional land was purchased on both sides of the creek for industrial use and providing housing for the workers.\n</p><p>The smelting process generated large amounts of waste. The copper slag was cast into large heavy dark bricks or "Scoria Blocks" which were to prove a very useful building material which were used and re-used in the town and can be seen in many buildings. The blocks were sold at 9d (about 4p) for 20 and given free to employees of the CCCo to build their own houses. Sea Lane or Black Road (and Black Bridge) as it is now known was built using these and waste used to fill in the upper reaches of Copperhouse Creek creating Wilson\'s Pool and dividing it from Copperhouse Pool. Copperhouse Pool was subsequently modified to serve as a tidal reservoir both to allow ships to travel up as far as the dock, (where the Co-op supermarket now stands), and to flush or sluice the channel to keep it clear of sand and silt.\n</p><p>In 1779 <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_(ironfounder)" title="John Harvey (ironfounder)">John Harvey</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmith</a> from nearby <a href="/wiki/Carnhell_Green" title="Carnhell Green">Carnhell Green</a>, established a small <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundry</a> and engineering works in the area, now known as Foundry, to supply the local mining industry. The business flourished and by 1800 employed more than 50 people. It went from strength to strength through both professional and family partnerships with a series of great engineers and entrepreneurs. <a href="/wiki/Harvey_%26_Co" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvey &amp; Co">Harvey &amp; Co</a> may be best remembered for producing <a href="/wiki/Beam_engine" title="Beam engine">beam engines</a>,  which not only served locally but were exported worldwide. The company also produced a range of products ranging from hand tools to oceangoing ships, including the <a href="/wiki/SS_Cornubia_(ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="SS Cornubia (ship)">SS <i>Cornubia</i></a> and the world\'s first steam-powered rock boring machine.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As Harvey\'s and the Cornish Copper Company continued to thrive, the rivalry between the two grew into open hostility. Disputes regularly erupted over access to the sea as The Cornish Copper Company controlled the dock and the tidal sluice which they had built at <a href="/wiki/Copperhouse" title="Copperhouse">Copperhouse</a>. Harveys acted to break the Cornish Copper Company\'s <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a> by constructing their own harbour by deepening Penpol Creek and building a dock. They even constructed their own tidal reservoir and sluice by creating Carnsew Pool. Harvey\'s operated a "Company Store policy" forcing workers to buy their provisions from Harvey\'s Emporium and prohibiting the development of any independent shops. When this policy was finally brought to an end a number of shops quickly established. \n</p><p>Prior to 1825 anyone wanting to go from Hayle to St Ives or Penzance had to cross the sands of Hayle Estuary or make a significant detour crossing the River Hayle at the ancient St Erth Bridge. Guides took travellers across the sands, but, even with guides, it was sometimes a perilous journey and the shifting sand and racing tide claimed several lives. Because of this major obstacle to trade, a <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Turnpike trust">turnpike trust</a> was formed, with Henry Harvey a trustee, to build the causeway which now takes the road below the plantation west to the Old Quay House. Costing \xc2\xa35000 in 1825, the investors charged a toll to use the causeway to recover their costs.\n</p><p>As Hayle\'s prosperity grew the foundry and smelter owners invested in the nearby mining industry. There was relativity little mining in and around Hayle itself, with <a href="/wiki/Wheal_Alfred" title="Wheal Alfred">Wheal Alfred</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheal_Prosper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wheal Prosper (page does not exist)">Wheal Prosper</a> (near Gwithian), being the only mine of any note, the nearest significant mines being around Helston. As Hayle\'s involvement in the mining industry around <a href="/wiki/Helston" title="Helston">Helston</a> grew it eventually reached the point in 1833 that it replaced Helston as the local <a href="/wiki/Tin_coinage" title="Tin coinage">tin coinage</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stannary" title="Stannary">Stannary</a>) town, although this was short-lived as the Stannary system was abolished in 1838.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg/220px-Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg/330px-Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg/440px-Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hayle_viaduct_cornwall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hayle viaduct from the modern station platform</div></div></div>\n<p>From 1831-61 the <a href="/wiki/Hayle_and_Bristol_Steam_Packet_Company" title="Hayle and Bristol Steam Packet Company">Hayle and Bristol Steam Packet Company</a> operated Steam Packet services which from 1837 connected with the newly opened Hayle-Redruth Railway. Designed from the outset to carry both goods and passengers the Hayle Railway\'s terminus was in Foundry Square under the present <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaduct</a>. Steam was introduced onto the Hayle Section in 1843 but the construction of the railway meant that only light engines could be used, whilst the incline at <a href="/wiki/Angarrack" title="Angarrack">Angarrack</a> also remained a problem. \n</p><p>In 1852 a new railway was opened spanning the valley on the impressive <a href="/wiki/Angarrack_viaduct" title="Angarrack viaduct">Angarrack viaduct</a> and passing through Hayle on a new wooden supports over Foundry Square which were later replaced with the current stone pillars. The Harbour Branch line was closed in 1982 and the station buildings and signal box were demolished at the same time. The original station in Foundry Square remained until after the Second World War when it was demolished.\n</p><p>Harvey\'s of Hayle reached their peak in the early/mid-19th century but, along with the other foundries and engineering works in Hayle, began a long and slow decline. Harvey\'s acquired the Cornish Copper Company in 1875 but the downturn continued. The engineering works and Foundry were closed in 1903, although the company continued to trade as general and builders merchant, eventually merging with UBM to become Harvey-UBM in 1969.\n</p><p><span id="Lifeboat"></span>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Lifeboat_Institution" title="Royal National Lifeboat Institution">Royal National Lifeboat Institution</a> stationed a <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboat</a> at Hayle in 1866. A boat house was built for it in 1897, but after it was closed in 1920 it was moved to a site near the power station where it was used as a store for about 60 years before being demolished.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The first lifeboat (<i>Isis</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> was replaced in November 1887;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the third and final boat, the <i>Admiral Rodd</i> arrived in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A memorial to Hayle\'s volunteer lifeboat crews has been placed in the Isis Gardens beneath the viaduct on the site of the town\xe2\x80\x99s first railway station.\n</p><p>In 1888, the National Explosive works were established on <a href="/wiki/Upton_Towans" class="mw-redirect" title="Upton Towans">Upton Towans</a> (giving it the alternative name "Dynamite Towans"). Originally built to supply the local mining industry, it soon grew to supply the military and, during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, employed over 1500 people. The remote location on the Towans proved a wise move as there were a number of accidents resulting in explosions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_20th_century">The 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hayle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hayle_Reflection.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Hayle_Reflection.jpg/170px-Hayle_Reflection.jpg" width="170" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Hayle_Reflection.jpg/255px-Hayle_Reflection.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Hayle_Reflection.jpg/340px-Hayle_Reflection.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1166" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hayle_Reflection.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reflection of Hayle in the Copperhouse Pool at high-tide</div></div></div>\n<p>Explosive manufacture ceased in 1920, although parts of the site were used as an explosives store until the 1960s. The area is now a <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserve</a> over which people are encouraged to roam. \n</p><p>1910 saw the opening of <a href="/wiki/Hayle_Power_Station" title="Hayle Power Station">Hayle Power Station</a> on Harvey\'s Towans. It was coal-fired and the coal was supplied by ship from South Wales until the station was closed in 1977. At the same time Hayle Harbour was also closed to commercial shipping, although a locally important <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> fleet, specialising mainly in <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> remained.\n</p><p>Until the early 20th century Hayle had two very distinct areas of settlement around the competing foundries but slowly buildings began to appear between the two communities. St Elwyn\xe2\x80\x99s Church, the <a href="/wiki/Passmore_Edwards" class="mw-redirect" title="Passmore Edwards">Passmore Edwards</a> Institute and a new Drill Hall all appeared within a few years of each other, and housing followed. The Passmore Edwards Institute was just one of a series of institutes and libraries built throughout Cornwall by its eponymous benefactor, who had made a fortune in the publishing business. The town council used it for offices for many years but moved to the Community Centre in April 2008.\n</p><p>In the years between the World Wars a number of small works were established on North Quay, including a glass works, a small oil depot and an <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">ICI</a> plant for producing <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a>\xe2\x80\x93a fuel additive for high <a href="/wiki/Octane" title="Octane">octane</a> aviation fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This additive increased the power of aircraft such as <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Hurricane" title="Hawker Hurricane">Hurricanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Spitfires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancasters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mosquito_bomber" class="mw-redirect" title="Mosquito bomber">Mosquitoes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> All are now closed and most of the buildings have been demolished. The metalworking business of J &amp; F Pool, founded in 1862, survived in Copperhouse producing perforated and fabricated metal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The engineering tradition continues with the more recent small specialist firms of Bassett Engineering and Rigibore which specialise in tooling and precision engineering products from the Guildford Road Industrial Estate. Rigibore provides tooling to a global market and offers revolutionary products for hole boring.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Bassett Engineering offer a wide range of engineering services to the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Indeed, Hayle still has much holiday accommodation. The sand dunes or <a href="/wiki/The_Towans" title="The Towans">Towans</a> are the favoured location for a number of holiday villages and caravan and camping sites. The Gwithian beach near Godrevy is picturesque and a popular area for water-related sports including surfing, windsurfing and body-boarding.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. In the 1980s, well-known businessman <a href="/wiki/Peter_de_Savary" title="Peter de Savary">Peter de Savary</a> fronted an attempt to develop the harbour area but ultimately failed to attract financial support to bring his plans to fruition.  Despite several other similar schemes, today the harbour is still not regenerated. In 2004, ING Real Estate, an international <a href="/wiki/Property_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Property development">property development</a> company, became the owners of Hayle harbour <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.hayleharbour.com/">[1]</a> and started to purchase land within the immediate vicinity of their planned project area.\n</p><p>In April 2008, ING submitted an outline planning application to the planning department of <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a> District Council. The design of the building had to fit in to its local surroundings because it is situated in an area which is part of a UNESCO world heritage site. A new petrol station plan for the store was submitted in 2017.
