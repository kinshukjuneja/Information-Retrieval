ministry</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a> was invited by <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">King George VI</a> to form the <b>Attlee ministry</b> in the United Kingdom in July 1945,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJefferys20147_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJefferys20147-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> succeeding <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> as <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister of the United Kingdom</a>. The Labour Party won a landslide victory at the 1945 general election, enacting much of the <a href="/wiki/Post-war_consensus" title="Post-war consensus">post-war consensus</a> policies, especially the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> and nationalisation of some industries.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The government was marked by post-war <a href="/wiki/Austerity" title="Austerity">austerity</a> measures, in giving <a href="/wiki/Indian_Independence_Act_1947" title="Indian Independence Act 1947">independence to India</a>, and engagement in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> against Soviet Communism.\n</p><p>Attlee went on to win a narrow majority of five seats at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1950" title="United Kingdom general election, 1950">1950 general election</a>, forming the <b>second Attlee ministry</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJefferys201451_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJefferys201451-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Just twenty months after that election, Attlee called <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1951" title="United Kingdom general election, 1951">a new election</a> for 25 October 1951 in an attempt to gain a larger majority, but was narrowly defeated by the Conservatives.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Leaders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> came to power in the United Kingdom after its unexpected victory in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1945" title="United Kingdom general election, 1945">July 1945 general election</a>. Party leader <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a> became Prime Minister replacing <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> in late July. <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Bevin" title="Ernest Bevin">Ernest Bevin</a> was <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Foreign Secretary</a> until shortly before his death in April 1951. <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Dalton" title="Hugh Dalton">Hugh Dalton</a> became <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>, but had to resign in 1947, while <a href="/wiki/James_Chuter_Ede" class="mw-redirect" title="James Chuter Ede">James Chuter Ede</a> was <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a> for the whole duration of the Attlee ministries\' stay in power.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other notable figures in the government included: <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Morrison" title="Herbert Morrison">Herbert Morrison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Deputy Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Deputy Prime Minister</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_House_of_Commons" title="Leader of the House of Commons">Leader of the House of Commons</a>, who replaced Bevin as Foreign Secretary in March 1951; <a href="/wiki/Stafford_Cripps" title="Stafford Cripps">Sir Stafford Cripps</a> was initially <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Board_of_Trade" title="President of the Board of Trade">President of the Board of Trade</a> but replaced Dalton as Chancellor of the Exchequer in 1947; <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Gaitskell" title="Hugh Gaitskell">Hugh Gaitskell</a> held several minor posts before replacing Cripps as Chancellor in 1950; <a href="/wiki/Aneurin_Bevan" title="Aneurin Bevan">Nye Bevan</a> was <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Health">Minister for Health</a>; <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Greenwood" title="Arthur Greenwood">Arthur Greenwood</a> was <a href="/wiki/Lord_Privy_Seal" title="Lord Privy Seal">Lord Privy Seal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paymaster_General" title="Paymaster General">Paymaster General</a> while future Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a> became the youngest member of the cabinet in the 20th century (at the age of 31) when he was made <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Board_of_Trade" title="President of the Board of Trade">President of the Board of Trade</a> in 1947. The most notable of the few female members of the government was <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Wilkinson" title="Ellen Wilkinson">Ellen Wilkinson</a>, who was <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Education_and_Skills" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Education and Skills">Minister for Education</a> until her early death in 1947.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_history_of_the_United_Kingdom_(1945%E2%80%93present)" title="Political history of the United Kingdom (1945\xe2\x80\x93present)">Political history of the United Kingdom (1945\xe2\x80\x93present)</a></div>\n<p>It was an "age of austerity," as wartime rationing was continued despite the Allied Forces\' victory, and was even expanded upon to include bread. Living conditions were poor, instead of expansion, it was a matter of replacing the national wealth destroyed or used up during the war. The Great Depression did not return, and full employment was created. Returning veterans were successfully reabsorbed into the postwar society.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\nThe Attlee government nationalised about 20% of the economy, including coal, railways, road transport, the Bank of England, civil aviation, electricity and gas, and steel.  However, there was no money for investment to modernise these industries, and there was no effort made to turn control over to union members. The Attlee government greatly expanded the welfare state, with the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_Act_1946" title="National Health Service Act 1946">National Health Service Act 1946</a>, which nationalised the hospitals and provided for free universal healthcare. The <a href="/wiki/National_Insurance_Act_1946" title="National Insurance Act 1946">National Insurance Act 1946</a> provided sickness and unemployment benefits for adults, plus retirement pensions. The <a href="/wiki/National_Assistance_Act_1948" title="National Assistance Act 1948">National Assistance Act 1948</a> provided a safety net for anyone not otherwise covered. More council housing was built, and plans were made through the <a href="/wiki/New_Towns_Act_1946" title="New Towns Act 1946">New Towns Act</a> of 1946 for the growth of suburbs, and to reduce overcrowding in major cities such as London and Glasgow. Since there was little money for detailed planning, the government adopted Keynesianism, which allowed for planning in the sense of overall control of the national deficit and surplus.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>   Two laws written by the Conservatives during the war were expanded, the <a href="/wiki/Family_Allowances_Act_1945" title="Family Allowances Act 1945">Family Allowances Act 1945</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Education_Act_1944" title="Education Act 1944">Education Act 1944</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Transport_Act_1947" title="Transport Act 1947">Transport Act 1947</a> established the <a href="/wiki/British_Transport_Commission" title="British Transport Commission">British Transport Commission</a>, which took control over the railways from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Four_(British_railway_companies)" title="Big Four (British railway companies)">Big Four</a> \xe2\x80\x94 <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a>, <a href="/wiki/London,_Midland_and_Scottish_Railway" title="London, Midland and Scottish Railway">London, Midland and Scottish Railway</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_and_North_Eastern_Railway" title="London and North Eastern Railway">London and North Eastern Railway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(UK)" title="Southern Railway (UK)">Southern Railway</a> \xe2\x80\x94 to form <a href="/wiki/British_Railways" class="mw-redirect" title="British Railways">British Railways</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In foreign affairs, the government was active in the United Nations and negotiated a $5,000,000,000 loan from the United States and Canada in 1946. It eagerly joined the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Plan" title="Marshall Plan">Marshall Plan</a> in 1948. It could no longer afford to support the Greek government and encouraged the U.S. to take its place through the <a href="/wiki/Truman_Doctrine" title="Truman Doctrine">Truman Doctrine</a> in 1947. It took an active role in joining the United States in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> and forming <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>. It gave independence to India, Pakistan, Ceylon and Burma and moved to strengthen the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth.</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nationalisation_projects">Nationalisation projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nationalisation projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dt>1945\xe2\x80\x9351</dt>\n<dd>The Labour Party comes to power with a programme for nationalising weak sectors of the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n<dl><dt>1946</dt>\n<dd>Coal industry under the <a href="/wiki/National_Coal_Board" title="National Coal Board">National Coal Board</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> created (with separate units in <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_(England)" title="National Health Service (England)">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Wales" title="NHS Wales">Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/NHS_Scotland" title="NHS Scotland">Scotland</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Care_NI" class="mw-redirect" title="Health and Care NI">Northern Ireland</a>) taking over hospitals and making medical services free. NHS started operations in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></dd>\n<dt>1947</dt>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/British_Electricity_Authority" title="British Electricity Authority">British Electricity Authority</a> and area electricity boards.<sup id="cite_ref-Brady,_pp_132-8_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brady,_pp_132-8-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Cable_%26_Wireless_plc" title="Cable &amp; Wireless plc">Cable &amp; Wireless</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></dd>\n<dt>1948</dt>\n<dd>National rail, inland (not marine) water transport, some road haulage, some road passenger transport and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cook_%26_Son" title="Thomas Cook &amp; Son">Thomas Cook &amp; Son</a> under the <a href="/wiki/British_Transport_Commission" title="British Transport Commission">British Transport Commission</a>. Separate elements operated as <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Railways</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Road_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="British Road Services">British Road Services</a>, and <a href="/wiki/British_Waterways" title="British Waterways">British Waterways</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></dd>\n<dt>1949</dt>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Gas_board" title="Gas board">Local authority gas supply undertakings</a> in England, Scotland and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-Brady,_pp_132-8_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brady,_pp_132-8-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></dd>\n<dt>1951</dt>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Iron_and_Steel_Corporation_of_Great_Britain" title="Iron and Steel Corporation of Great Britain">Iron and Steel Corporation of Great Britain</a> (privatised by the Conservative Government in 1955, and renationalised by Labour in 1967 as <a href="/wiki/British_Steel_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="British Steel Corporation">British Steel Corporation</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_policies">Social policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Social policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg/200px-Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg/300px-Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg/400px-Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trafford_General_Hospital_-_geograph.org.uk_-_21987.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Trafford_General_Hospital" title="Trafford General Hospital">Trafford General Hospital</a>, known as the birthplace of the NHS.</div></div></div>\n<p>Attlee\'s <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Health">Health Minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aneurin_Bevan" title="Aneurin Bevan">Aneurin Bevan</a>, fought hard against the general disapproval of the medical establishment, including the <a href="/wiki/British_Medical_Association" title="British Medical Association">British Medical Association</a>, by creating the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> (NHS) in 1948. This was a <a href="/wiki/Publicly_funded_health_care" title="Publicly funded health care">publicly funded healthcare system</a>, which offered treatment free of charge for all, regardless of income at the point-of-use. Reflecting pent-up demand that had long existed for medical services, the NHS treated some 8,500,000 dental patients and dispensed more than 5,000,000 pairs of spectacles during its first year of operation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Consultants benefited from the new system by being paid salaries that provided an acceptable standard of living without the need for them to resort to private practice.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The NHS brought major improvements in the health of working-class people, with deaths from diphtheria, pneumonia, and tuberculosis significantly reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Although there were often disputes about its organisation and funding, British political parties continued to voice their general support for the NHS in order to remain electable.<sup id="cite_ref-healthaffairs_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthaffairs-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the field of health care, funds were allocated to modernisation and extension schemes aimed at improving administrative efficiency. Improvements were made in nursing accommodation in order to recruit more nurses and reduce labour shortages which were keeping 60,000 beds out of use, and efforts were made to reduce the imbalance "between an excess of fever and tuberculosis (TB) beds and a shortage of maternity beds."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/BCG_vaccinations" class="mw-redirect" title="BCG vaccinations">BCG vaccinations</a> were introduced for the protection of medical students, midwives, nurses, and contacts of patients with tuberculosis,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> a pension scheme was set up for employees of the newly established NHS,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and the Radioactive Substances Act of 1948 set out general provisions to control radioactive substances.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Numerous lesser reforms were also introduced, some of which were of great benefit to certain segments of British society, such as the mentally deficient and the blind.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1964_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1964-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Between 1948\xe2\x80\x9351, Attlee\'s government increased spending on health from \xc2\xa36,000,000,000 to \xc2\xa311,000,000,000: an increase of over 80%, and from 2.1% to 3.6% of GDP.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Welfare">Welfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Welfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The government set about implementing <a href="/wiki/William_Beveridge" title="William Beveridge">William Beveridge</a>\'s plans for the creation of a \'cradle to grave\' <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>, and set in place an entirely new system of <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a>. Among the most important pieces of legislation was the <a href="/wiki/National_Insurance_Act_1946" title="National Insurance Act 1946">National Insurance Act 1946</a>, in which people in work paid a flat rate of <a href="/wiki/National_insurance" class="mw-redirect" title="National insurance">national insurance</a>. In return, they (and the wives of male contributors) were eligible for flat-rate pensions, sickness benefit, unemployment benefit, and funeral benefit. Various other pieces of legislation provided for <a href="/wiki/Child_benefit" title="Child benefit">child benefit</a> and support for people with no other source of income.<sup id="cite_ref-A_History_of_the_British_Labour_Party_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_the_British_Labour_Party-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In 1949, unemployment, sickness and maternity benefits were exempted from taxation.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A block grant introduced in 1948 helped the social services provided by local authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Personal Social Services or welfare services were developed in 1948 for individual and families in general, particularly special groups such as the mentally disordered, deprived children, the elderly, and the handicapped.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Attlee Government increased pensions and other benefits, with pensions raised to become more of a living income than they had ever been. War pensions and allowances (for both World Wars) were increased by an Act of 1946 which gave the wounded man with an allowance for his wife and children if he married after he had been wounded, thereby removing a grievance of more than twenty years standing.<sup id="cite_ref-SocialismThe_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SocialismThe-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Other improvements were made in war pensions during Attlee\'s tenure as prime minister. A Constant Attendance Allowance was tripled, an Unemployability Allowance was tripled from 10s to 30s a week, and a special hardship allowance of up to \xc2\xa31 a week was introduced. In addition, the 1951 Budget made further improvements in the supplementary allowances for many war pensioners. From 1945 onwards, three out of every four pension claims had been successful, whilst after the First World War only one pension claim in three was allowed.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Under the Superannuation (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act of 1948, employees of a body representative of local authorities or of the officers of local authorities could be admitted "on suitable terms to the superannuation fund of a local authority."<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In 1951, a comforts allowance was introduced that was automatically paid to war pensioners "receiving unemployability supplement and constant attendance allowance."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A more extensive system of social welfare benefits was established by the Attlee Government, which did much to reduce acute social deprivation. The cumulative impact of the Attlee\'s Government\'s health and welfare policies was such that all the indices of health (such as statistics of school medical or dental officers, or of medical officers of health) showed signs of improvement, with continual improvements in survival rates for infants and increased life expectancy for the elderly.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The success of the Attlee Government\'s welfare legislation in reducing poverty was such that, in the general election of 1950, according to one study, "Labour propaganda could make much of the claim that social security had eradicated the most abject destitution of the 1930s".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing">Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The New Towns Act of 1946 set up development corporations to construct new towns, while the Town and Country Planning Act of 1947 instructed county councils to prepare development plans and also provided compulsory purchase powers.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1900_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1900-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The Attlee Government also extended the powers of local authorities to requisition houses and parts of houses, and made the acquisition of land less difficult than before.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1945_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1945-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The Housing (Scotland) Act of 1949 provided grants of 75% (87.5% in the Highlands and Islands) towards modernisation costs payable by the Treasury to local authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1949, local authorities were empowered to provide people suffering from poor health with <a href="/wiki/Public_housing" title="Public housing">public housing</a> at <a href="/wiki/Subsidized_housing" title="Subsidized housing">subsidised rents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To assist home ownership, the limit on the amount of money that people could borrow from their local authority in order to purchase or build a home was raised from \xc2\xa3800 to \xc2\xa31,500 in 1945, and to \xc2\xa35,000 in 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Under the National Assistance Act of 1948, local authorities had a duty "to provide emergency temporary accommodation for families which become homeless through no fault of their own."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A large house-building programme was carried out with the intention of providing millions of people with high-quality homes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> A housing bill passed in 1946 increased Treasury subsidies for the construction of local authority housing in England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1900_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1900-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Four out of five houses constructed under Labour were council properties built to more generous specifications than before the Second World War, and subsidies kept down council rents. Altogether, these policies provided public-sector housing with its biggest ever boost up until that point, while low-wage earners particularly benefited from these developments. Although the Attlee Government failed to meet its targets, primarily due to economic constraints, over 1,000,000 new homes were built between 1945-51 (a significant achievement under the circumstances) which ensured that decent, affordable housing was available to many low-income families for the first time ever.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJefferys2014-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Women_and_children">Women and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Women and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A number of reforms were embarked upon to improve conditions for women and children. In 1946, universal family allowances were introduced to provide financial support to households for raising children.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> These benefits had been legislated for the previous year by Churchill\'s <a href="/wiki/Family_Allowances_Act_1945" title="Family Allowances Act 1945">Family Allowances Act 1945</a>, and was the first measure pushed through parliament by Attlee\'s government.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The Conservatives would later criticise Labour for having been "too hasty" in introducing family allowances.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1945_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1945-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A Married Women (Restraint Upon Anticipation) Act was passed in 1949 "to equalise, to render inoperative any restrictions upon anticipation or alienation attached to the enjoyment of property by a woman," while the Married Women (Maintenance) Act of 1949 was enacted with the intention of improving the adequacy and duration of financial benefits for married women.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Criminal Law (Amendment) Act of 1950 amended an Act of 1885 to bring prostitutes within the law and safeguard them from abduction and abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The Criminal Justice Act of 1948 restricted imprisonment for juveniles and brought improvements to the probation and remand centre systems, while the passage of the Justices of the Peace Act of 1949 led to extensive reforms of magistrates courts.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The Attlee Government also abolished the marriage bar in the Civil Service, thereby enabling married women to work in that institution.<sup id="cite_ref-google_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1946, the government set up a National Institute of Houseworkers as a means of providing a socially democratic variety of domestic service.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By late 1946, agreed standards of training were established, which was followed by the opening of a training headquarters and the opening of an additional nine training centres in Wales, Scotland, and then nationwide throughout Great Britain. The National Health Service Act of 1946 indicated that domestic help should be provided for households where that help is required "owing to the presence of any person who is ill, lying-in, an expectant mother, mentally defective, aged or a child not over compulsory school age". \'Home help\' therefore included the provision of home-helps for nursing and expectant mothers and for mothers with children under the age of five, and by 1952 some 20,000 women were engaged in this service.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning_and_development">Planning and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Planning and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Development rights were nationalised while the government attempted to take all development profits for the state. Strong planning authorities were set up to control land use, and issued manuals of guidance which stressed the importance of safeguarding agricultural land. A strong chain of regional offices was set up within its planning ministry to provide a strong lead in regional development policies.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Comprehensive Development Areas (CDAs), a designation under the Town and Country Planning Act of 1947, allowed local authorities to acquire property in the designated areas using powers of compulsory purchase in order to re-plan and develop urban areas suffering from urban blight or war damage.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Workers.27_rights"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Workers\'_rights">Workers\' rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Workers&#039; rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Various measures were carried out to improve conditions in the workplace. Entitlement to sick leave was greatly extended, and sick pay schemes were introduced for local authority administrative, professional and technical workers in 1946 and for various categories of manual workers in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Worker\'s compensation was also significantly improved.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Fair Wages Resolution of 1946 required any contractor working on a public project to at least match the pay rates and other employment conditions set in the appropriate collective agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> In 1946, <a href="/wiki/Purchase_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Purchase tax">purchase tax</a> was removed completely from kitchen fittings and crockery, while the rate was reduced on various gardening items.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fire_Services_Act_1947" title="Fire Services Act 1947">Fire Services Act 1947</a> introduced a new pension scheme for firefighters,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_Act_1947" title="Electricity Act 1947">Electricity Act 1947</a> introduced better retirement benefits for workers in that industry.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> A Workers\' Compensation (Supplementation) Act was passed in 1948 that introduced benefits for workers with certain asbestos-related diseases which had occurred before 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> The Merchant Shipping Act of 1948 and the Merchant Shipping (Safety Convention) Act of 1949 were passed to improve conditions for seamen. The Shops Act of 1950 consolidated previous legislation which provided that no one could be employed in a shop for more than six hours without having a break for at least 20 minutes. The legislation also required a lunch break of at least 45 minutes for anyone for worked between 11:30am and 2:30pm, and a half-hour tea break for anyone working between 4pm and 7pm.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> The government also strengthened a Fair Wages Resolution, with a clause that required all employers getting government contracts to recognise the rights of their workers to join trade unions.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Trade_Disputes_and_Trade_Unions_Act_1927" title="Trade Disputes and Trade Unions Act 1927">Trades Disputes Act 1927</a> was repealed, and a Dock Labour Scheme was introduced in 1947 to put an end to the casual system of hiring labour in the docks.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> This scheme gave registered dockers the legal right to minimum work and decent conditions. Through the National Dock Labour Board (on which trade unions and employers had equal representation) the unions acquired control over recruitment and dismissal. Registered dockers laid off by employers within the Scheme had the right either to be taken on by another, or to generous compensation.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> All dockers were registered under the Dock Labour Scheme, giving them a legal right to minimum work, holidays and sick pay.<sup id="cite_ref-thespiritof45_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thespiritof45-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Wages for members of the police force were significantly increased.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The introduction of a Miner\'s Charter in 1946 instituted a five-day work week for miners and a standardised day wage structure,<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and in 1948 a Colliery Workers Supplementary Scheme was approved, providing supplementary allowances to disabled coal-workers and their dependants.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> In 1948, a pension scheme was set up to provide pension benefits for employees of the new NHS, as well as their dependents.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Under the Coal Industry Nationalisation (Superannuation) Regulations of 1950, a pension scheme for mineworkers was established.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Improvements were also made in farmworkers\' wages,<sup id="cite_ref-auto_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and the Agricultural Wages Board in 1948 not only safeguarded wage levels, but also ensured that workers were provided with accommodation.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A number of regulations aimed at safeguarding the health and safety of people at work were also introduced during Attlee\'s time in office. Regulations were issued in February 1946 applying to factories involved with "manufacturing briquettes or blocks of fuel consisting of coal, coal dust, coke or slurry with pitch as a binding .substance," and which concerned "dust and ventilation, washing facilities and clothing accommodation, medical supervision and examination, skin and eye protection and messrooms."<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Magnesium (Grinding of Castings and Other Articles) (Special Regulations) Order of December 1946 contained special measures "respecting the maintenance of plant and apparatus; precautions against causing sparks; the interception and removal of dust; automatic operation of appliances; protective clothing; and prohibition of smoking, open lights and fires."<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> For those workers engaged in luminising processes, the Factories (Luminising) Special Regulations (1947) prohibited the employment of those under the age of 18 and ordered "an initial medical examination to be carried out before the seventh day of employment; subsequent examinations are to be carried out once a month."Under the terms of the Blasting (Castings and Other Articles) Special Regulations (1949) "no sand or other substance containing free silica is to be employed in any blasting process," while the Foundries (Parting Materials) Special Regulations (1950) prohibited the use of certain parting powders "which give rise to a substantial risk of silicosis."<sup id="cite_ref-staging.ilo.org_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-staging.ilo.org-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Building (Safety, Health &amp; Welfare) Regulations of 1948 required that measures should be taken to minimise exposure to potentially harmful dust or fumes,<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> while the Pottery (Health) Special Regulations (1947) prohibited the use "except in the manufacture of glazed tiles" of all "but leadless or low solubility glazes and prescribe certain processes in which ground or powdered flint or quartz are not to be employed."<sup id="cite_ref-staging.ilo.org_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-staging.ilo.org-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> while the Pottery (Health and Welfare) Special Regulations of 1950 made provision for the health and safety of workers employed in factories "in which there is carried on the manufacture or decoration of pottery or certain allied manufactures or processes."<sup id="cite_ref-legislation_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legislation-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Various law reforms were also carried out by Attlee\'s government. The Criminal Justice Act of 1948 provided for new methods to deal with offenders, and abolished hard labour, penal servitude, prison divisions and whipping.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> The Law Reform (Personal Injuries) Act 1948 enabled employees to sue their employers in cases where they experienced injury due to the negligence of a fellow employee.<sup id="cite_ref-google2_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google2-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The Legal Aid and Advice Act of 1949 introduced a state aided scheme to assist those who couldn\'t afford legal services.<sup id="cite_ref-google3_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google3-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_consensus">Post-war consensus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Post-war consensus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post-war_consensus" title="Post-war consensus">Post-war consensus</a></div>\n<p>Most historians argue that the main domestic policies (except nationalisation of steel) reflected a broad bipartisan consensus. The <i>post-war consensus</i> is a historians\' model of political agreement from 1945 to the late-1970s.  In 1979 newly elected Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> rejected and reversed it.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>  The concept claims there was a widespread consensus that covered support for coherent package of policies that were developed in the 1930s, promised during the Second World War, and enacted under Attlee. The policies dealt with a mixed economy, Keynesianism, and a broad welfare state.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> In recent years the validity of the interpretation has been debated by historians.\n</p><p>The historians\' model of the post-war consensus was most fully developed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Addison" title="Paul Addison">Paul Addison</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> The basic argument is that in the 1930s, Liberal Party intellectuals led by <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Beveridge" title="William Beveridge">William Beveridge</a> developed a series of plans that became especially attractive as the wartime government promised a much better post-war Britain and saw the need to engage every sector of society. The coalition government during the war, headed by Churchill and Attlee, signed off on a series of white papers that promised Britain a much improved welfare state. After the war, the promises included the National Health Service, and expansion of education, housing, and a number of welfare programmes. It did not include the nationalisation of iron and steel, which was approved only by the Labour Party.\n</p><p>The model states that from 1945 until the arrival of Thatcher in 1979, there was a broad multi-partisan national consensus on social and economic policy, especially regarding the welfare state, nationalised health services, educational reform, a mixed economy, government regulation, Keynesian macroeconomic, policies, and full employment. Apart from the question of nationalisation of some industries, these policies were broadly accepted by the three major parties, as well as by industry, the financial community and the labour movement. Until the 1980s, historians generally agreed on the existence and importance of the consensus. Some historians such as <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Miliband" title="Ralph Miliband">Ralph Miliband</a> expressed disappointment that the consensus was a modest or even conservative package that blocked a fully socialized society.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>  Historian <a href="/wiki/Angus_Calder" title="Angus Calder">Angus Calder</a> complained bitterly that the post-war reforms were an inadequate reward for the wartime sacrifices, and a cynical betrayal of the people\'s hope for a more just post-war society.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> In recent years, there has been a historiographical  debate on whether such a consensus ever existed.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fate">Fate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attlee_ministry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Labour Party narrowly defeated the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1950" title="United Kingdom general election, 1950">February 1950 general election</a>. However, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1951" title="United Kingdom general election, 1951">October 1951 general elections</a> the Conservatives returned to power under Winston Churchill. Labour was to remain out of office for the next thirteen years, until 1964, when Harold Wilson became Prime Minister.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cabinets">Cabinets</span><span
