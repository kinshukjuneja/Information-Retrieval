class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid_(flipped).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bust of <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a> sculpted in 1927 by <a href="/wiki/William_Lamb_(sculptor)" title="William Lamb (sculptor)">William Lamb</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Scottish Renaissance</b> was a mainly <a href="/wiki/Literary_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary movement">literary movement</a> of the early to mid-20th century that can be seen as the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> version of <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a>. It is sometimes referred to as the <b>Scottish literary renaissance</b>, although its influence went beyond literature into music, visual arts, and politics (among other fields). The writers and artists of the Scottish Renaissance displayed a profound interest in both modern philosophy and technology, as well as incorporating <a href="/wiki/Scottish_folklore" title="Scottish folklore">folk influences</a>, and a strong concern for the fate of Scotland\'s <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">declining languages</a>.\n</p><p>It has been seen as a parallel to other movements elsewhere, including the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Literary_Revival" title="Irish Literary Revival">Irish Literary Revival</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Renaissance" title="Harlem Renaissance">Harlem Renaissance</a> (in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">America</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Bengal_Renaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengal Renaissance">Bengal Renaissance</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Jindyworobak_Movement" title="Jindyworobak Movement">Jindyworobak Movement</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), which emphasised indigenous folk traditions.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "Scottish Renaissance" was brought into critical prominence by the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a> poet and scholar <a href="/wiki/Denis_Saurat" title="Denis Saurat">Denis Saurat</a> in his article "<i lang="fr" title="French language text">Le Groupe de la Renaissance \xc3\x89cossaise</i>", which was published in the <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revue_Anglo-Am%C3%A9ricaine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Revue Anglo-Am\xc3\xa9ricaine (page does not exist)">Revue Anglo-Am\xc3\xa9ricaine</a></i></i> in April 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term had appeared much earlier, however, in the work of the <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymathic</a> <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Geddes" title="Patrick Geddes">Patrick Geddes</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and in a 1922 book review by <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Christopher Murray Grieve</a> ("Hugh MacDiarmid") for the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Chapbook&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Scottish Chapbook (page does not exist)">Scottish Chapbook</a></i> that predicted a "Scottish Renascence as swift and irresistible as was the Belgian Revival between 1880 and 1910",<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> involving such figures as <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Spence" title="Lewis Spence">Lewis Spence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marion_Angus" title="Marion Angus">Marion Angus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>These earlier references make clear the connections between the Scottish Renaissance and the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Literary_Revival" title="Irish Literary Revival">Celtic Twilight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Revival" title="Celtic Revival">Celtic Revival</a> movements of the late 19th century, which helped reawaken a spirit of cultural <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a> among Scots of the modernist generations. Where these earlier movements had been steeped in a sentimental and nostalgic <a href="/wiki/Celtic_revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic revival">Celticism</a>, however, the modernist-influenced Renaissance would seek a rebirth of Scottish national culture that would both look back to the medieval "<a href="/wiki/Makar" title="Makar">makar</a>" poets, <a href="/wiki/William_Dunbar" title="William Dunbar">William Dunbar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Henryson" title="Robert Henryson">Robert Henrysoun</a>, as well as look towards such contemporary influences as <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a>, and <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence" title="D. H. Lawrence">D. H. Lawrence</a>, or (more locally) <a href="/wiki/R._B._Cunninghame_Graham" class="mw-redirect" title="R. B. Cunninghame Graham">R. B. Cunninghame Graham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The turn of the 20th century saw the first stirrings of a new era in Scottish arts and letters. As writers such as <a href="/wiki/George_Douglas_Brown" title="George Douglas Brown">George Douglas Brown</a> railed against the "<a href="/wiki/Kailyard_school" title="Kailyard school">Kailyard school</a>" that had come to dominate Scottish letters, producing satiric, realist accounts of Scottish rural life in novels like <i><a href="/wiki/The_House_with_the_Green_Shutters" title="The House with the Green Shutters">The House with the Green Shutters</a></i> (1901), Scots language poets such as <a href="/wiki/Violet_Jacob" title="Violet Jacob">Violet Jacob</a> and Marion Angus undertook a quiet revival of regionally inflected poetry in the Lowland vernacular. The aforementioned Patrick Geddes would continue his foundational work in town and regional planning, developing the triad "Place - Work - Folk" as a matrix for new thinking about the relationships between people and their local environments. In the realm of visual arts, <a href="/wiki/John_Duncan_(painter)" title="John Duncan (painter)">John Duncan</a> would refine his <a href="/wiki/Celtic_myth" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic myth">Celtic myth</a> inspired <a href="/wiki/Symbolist" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbolist">Symbolist</a> painting to include an increasing emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Collage" title="Collage">collage</a> and the flatness of the image. In architecture and the decorative arts, the towering figures of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rennie_Mackintosh" title="Charles Rennie Mackintosh">Charles Rennie Mackintosh</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Four" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow Four">Glasgow Four</a> would give Scotland its very own "school" of modern design and help create the "<a href="/wiki/Glasgow_School" title="Glasgow School">Glasgow style</a>". Scotland in the early 20th century was experiencing an efflorescence of creative activity, but there was not yet a sense of a particular shared movement or an overt national inflection to all of this artistic effort.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_renaissance">Literary renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Literature_in_modern_Scotland" title="Literature in modern Scotland">Literature in modern Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scots-language_literature" title="Scots-language literature">Scots-language literature</a></div>\n<p>It was not until the literary efforts of <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a> that the Scottish Renaissance can properly be said to have begun. Starting in 1920, C. M. Grieve (having not yet adopted his nom de plume of Hugh MacDiarmid) began publishing a series of three short anthologies entitled <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Numbers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Northern Numbers (page does not exist)">Northern Numbers</a>: Being Representative Selections from Certain Living Scottish Poets</i> (including works by <a href="/wiki/John_Buchan" title="John Buchan">John Buchan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violet_Jacob" title="Violet Jacob">Violet Jacob</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Munro_(Hugh_Foulis)" class="mw-redirect" title="Neil Munro (Hugh Foulis)">Neil Munro</a>, and Grieve himself). These anthologies, which appeared one each year from 1920\xe2\x80\x9322, along with his founding and editing of the <i>Scottish Chapbook</i> review (in the <a href="/wiki/Annus_mirabilis" title="Annus mirabilis">annus mirabilis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernism</a>, 1922), established Grieve/MacDiarmid as the father and central figure of the burgeoning Scottish Renaissance movement that he had prophesied.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> By about 1925, MacDiarmid had largely abandoned his <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> poetry and began to write in a kind of "synthetic Scots" known as <a href="/wiki/Lallans" title="Lallans">Lallans</a>, that was a hybrid of regional <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lexicography" title="Lexicography">lexicographical</a> artifacts exhumed from <i>Jamieson\'s Dictionary of the Scottish Language</i>, often grafted onto a <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a> grammatical structure. His poetic works included "<a href="/wiki/A_Drunk_Man_Looks_at_the_Thistle" title="A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle">A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle</a>" (1936). This had an electrifying effect on the <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literary</a> landscape of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other writers soon followed in MacDiarmid\'s footsteps and also wrote in Lallans, including the poets <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Muir" title="Edwin Muir">Edwin Muir</a> (1887\xe2\x80\x931959) and <a href="/wiki/William_Soutar" title="William Soutar">William Soutar</a> (1898\xe2\x80\x931943), who pursued an exploration of identity, rejecting nostalgia and parochialism and engaging with social and political issues.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Some writers that emerged after the Second World War followed MacDiarmid by writing in Scots, including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Garioch" title="Robert Garioch">Robert Garioch</a> (1909\xe2\x80\x931981) and <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Goodsir_Smith" title="Sydney Goodsir Smith">Sydney Goodsir Smith</a> (1915\xe2\x80\x931975). The Glaswegian poet <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Morgan_(poet)" title="Edwin Morgan (poet)">Edwin Morgan</a> (1920\xe2\x80\x932010) became known for translations of works from a wide range of European languages. He was also the first <a href="/wiki/Scots_Makar" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots Makar">Scots Makar</a> (the official <a href="/wiki/National_poet" title="National poet">national poet</a>), appointed by the inaugural Scottish government in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gray_(poet)" title="Alexander Gray (poet)">Alexander Gray</a> was an academic and poet, but is chiefly remembered for his translations into Scots from the German and Danish ballad traditions into Scots, including <i>Arrows. A Book of German Ballads and Folksongs Attempted in Scots</i> (1932) and <i>Four-and-Forty. A Selection of Danish Ballads Presented in Scots</i> (1954).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Scottish Renaissance increasingly concentrated on the novel, particularly after the 1930s when Hugh MacDiarmid was in isolation in Shetland and its leadership moved to novelist <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gunn" class="mw-redirect" title="Neil Gunn">Neil Gunn</a> (1891\xe2\x80\x931973). Gunn\'s novels, beginning with <i>The Grey Coast</i> (1926), and including <i>Highland River</i> (1937) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Isle_of_the_Great_Deep" title="The Green Isle of the Great Deep">The Green Isle of the Great Deep</a></i> (1943), were largely written in English and not the Scots preferred by MacDiarmid, focused on the Highlands of his birth and were notable for their narrative experimentation.<sup id="cite_ref-Craig2001bpp157-9_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craig2001bpp157-9-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Other major figures associated with the movement include George Blake, <a href="/wiki/A._J._Cronin" title="A. J. Cronin">A. J. Cronin</a> (1896\xe2\x80\x931981), <a href="/wiki/Eric_Linklater" title="Eric Linklater">Eric Linklater</a> (1899\xe2\x80\x931974) and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Grassic_Gibbon" title="Lewis Grassic Gibbon">Lewis Grassic Gibbon</a> (1901\xe2\x80\x9335). There were also a large number of female authors associated with the movement, who demonstrated a growing feminine consciousness. They included <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Carswell" title="Catherine Carswell">Catherine Carswell</a> (1879\xe2\x80\x931946), <a href="/wiki/Willa_Muir" title="Willa Muir">Willa Muir</a> (1890\xe2\x80\x931970),<sup id="cite_ref-Craig2001bpp157-9_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craig2001bpp157-9-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nan_Shepherd" title="Nan Shepherd">Nan Shepherd</a> (1893\xe2\x80\x931981)<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and most prolifically <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Mitchison" title="Naomi Mitchison">Naomi Mitchison</a> (1897\xe2\x80\x931999).<sup id="cite_ref-Craig2001bpp157-9_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craig2001bpp157-9-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> All were born within a fifteen-year period and, although they cannot be described as members of a single school, they all pursued an exploration of identity, rejecting nostalgia and parochialism and engaging with social and political issues.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Physician A. J. Cronin is now often seen as sentimental, but his early work, particularly his first novel <i><a href="/wiki/Hatter%27s_Castle" title="Hatter&#39;s Castle">Hatter\'s Castle</a></i> (1931) and his most successful <i><a href="/wiki/The_Citadel_(novel)" title="The Citadel (novel)">The Citadel</a></i> (1937) were a deliberate reaction against the Kailyard tradition, exposing the hardships and vicissitudes of the lives of ordinary people,<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford2009p587_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford2009p587-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He was the most translated Scottish author in the twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> George Blake pioneered the exploration of the experiences of the working class in his major works such as <i>The Shipbuilders</i> (1935). Eric Linklater produced comedies of the absurd including <i>Juan in America</i> (1931) dealing with <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">prohibition</a> America, and a critique of modern war in <i><a href="/wiki/Private_Angelo" title="Private Angelo">Private Angelo</a></i> (1946). Lewis Grassic Gibbon, the pseudonym of James Leslie Mitchell, produced one of the most important realisations of the ideas of the Scottish Renaissance in his trilogy <i><a href="/wiki/A_Scots_Quair" title="A Scots Quair">A Scots Quair</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Sunset_Song" title="Sunset Song">Sunset Song</a></i>, 1932, <i>Cloud Howe</i>, 1933 and <i>Grey Granite</i>, 1934), which mixed different Scots dialects with the narrative voice.<sup id="cite_ref-Craig2001bpp157-9_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craig2001bpp157-9-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Other works that investigated the working class included James Barke\'s (1905\xe2\x80\x9358), <i>Major Operation</i> (1936) and <i>The Land of the Leal</i> (1939) and <a href="/wiki/J._F._Hendry" title="J. F. Hendry">J. F. Hendry</a>\'s (1912\xe2\x80\x9386) <i>Fernie Brae</i> (1947).<sup id="cite_ref-Craig2001bpp157-9_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craig2001bpp157-9-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The parallel revitalisation of Gaelic poetry, known as the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_Renaissance" title="Scottish Gaelic Renaissance">Scottish Gaelic Renaissance</a>, was largely due to the work of <a href="/wiki/Sorley_Maclean" class="mw-redirect" title="Sorley Maclean">Sorley Maclean</a> (Somhairle MacGill-Eain, 1911\xe2\x80\x9396). A native of Skye and a native Gaelic speaker, he abandoned the stylistic conventions of the tradition and opened up new possibilities for composition with his poem <i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">D\xc3\xa0in do Eimhir</i> (<i>Poems to Eimhir</i>, 1943). His work inspired a new generation to take up <i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">nua bh\xc3\xa0rdachd</i> (the new poetry). These included <a href="/wiki/George_Campbell_Hay" title="George Campbell Hay">George Campbell Hay</a> (<span lang="gd" style="font-style:normal;" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">De\xc3\xb2rsa Mac Iain Dhe\xc3\xb2rsa</span>, 1915\xe2\x80\x931984), Lewis-born poets <a href="/wiki/Derick_Thomson" title="Derick Thomson">Derick Thomson</a> (<span lang="gd" style="font-style:normal;" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">Ruaraidh MacTh\xc3\xb2mais</span>, 1921\xe2\x80\x932012) and <a href="/wiki/Iain_Crichton_Smith" title="Iain Crichton Smith">Iain Crichton Smith</a> (<span lang="gd" style="font-style:normal;" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">Iain Mac a\' Ghobhainn</span>, 1928\xe2\x80\x9398). They all focused on the issues of exile, the fate of the Gaelic language and <a href="/wiki/Bi-culturalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Bi-culturalism">bi-culturalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MacDonald2001pp255-7_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald2001pp255-7-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Art_in_modern_Scotland" title="Art in modern Scotland">Art in modern Scotland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cursiter,_The_Regatta.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2c/Cursiter%2C_The_Regatta.jpg/220px-Cursiter%2C_The_Regatta.jpg" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2c/Cursiter%2C_The_Regatta.jpg/330px-Cursiter%2C_The_Regatta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2c/Cursiter%2C_The_Regatta.jpg/440px-Cursiter%2C_The_Regatta.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="415" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cursiter,_The_Regatta.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cursiter" title="Stanley Cursiter">Stanley Cursiter</a>, <i>Regatta</i>, (1913)</div></div></div>\n<p>The ideas of a distinctive modern Scottish art were expressed in the inter-war period by figures including <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cursiter" title="Stanley Cursiter">Stanley Cursiter</a> (1887\xe2\x80\x931976), <a href="/wiki/William_McCance" title="William McCance">William McCance</a> (1894\xe2\x80\x931970), <a href="/wiki/William_Johnstone_(artist)" title="William Johnstone (artist)">William Johnstone</a> (1897\xe2\x80\x931981) and <a href="/wiki/J._D._Fergusson" class="mw-redirect" title="J. D. Fergusson">J. D. Fergusson</a> (1874\xe2\x80\x931961).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Stanley Cursiter was influenced by the Celtic revival, post-impressionism and <a href="/wiki/Futurism_(art)" class="mw-redirect" title="Futurism (art)">Futurism</a>, as can be seen in his <i>Rain on Princes Street</i> (1913) and <i>Regatta</i> (1913). He went on to be a major painter of the coastline of his native Orkney, director of the National Gallery of Scotland and proposed the creation of a National Gallery of Modern Art in 1930.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p173_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p173-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Fergusson was one of the few British artists who could claim to have played a part in the creation of modernism and probably played a major part in the formulation of MacDiarmid\'s thought. His interest in machine imagery can be seen in paintings like <i>Damaged Destroyer</i> (1918). He co-operated with MacDiarmid on the journal <i>Scottish Art and Letters</i> and MacDiarmid quoted extensively from his work.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>William McCance\'s early work was in a bold post-impressionist style. After World War I he moved to London with his wife, fellow student <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Miller_Parker" title="Agnes Miller Parker">Agnes Miller Parker</a> (1895-1980), where he joined the same circles as Fergusson, vorticist <a href="/wiki/Wyndham_Lewis" title="Wyndham Lewis">Wyndham Lewis</a> (1882-1957) and nationalist composer <a href="/wiki/Francis_George_Scott" title="Francis George Scott">Francis George Scott</a>. Under these influences his work became increasingly abstract and influenced by vorticism, as can be seen in <i>Women on an Elevator</i> (1925) and <i>The Engineer and his Wife</i> (1925).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> William Johnstone (1897\xe2\x80\x931981) was a cousin of F. G. Scott and met MacDiarmid while a student at Edinburgh. He studied cubism, surrealism and was introduced to new American art by his wife the sculptor Flora Macdonald. He moved towards <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstraction</a>, attempting to utilise aspects of landscape, poetry and Celtic art. His most significant work, <i>A Point in Time</i> (1929\xe2\x80\x9338), has been described by art historian <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Macmillan_(art_historian)" title="Duncan Macmillan (art historian)">Duncan Macmillan</a> as "one of the most important Scottish pictures of the century and one of the most remarkable pictures by any British painter in the period".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p173_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p173-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other artists strongly influenced by modernism included <a href="/wiki/James_McIntosh_Patrick" title="James McIntosh Patrick">James McIntosh Patrick</a> (1907\xe2\x80\x9398) and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Baird_(artist)" title="Edward Baird (artist)">Edward Baird</a> (1904\xe2\x80\x9349).<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p173_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p173-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Both trained in Glasgow, but spent most of their careers in and around their respective native cities of Dundee and Montrose. Both were influenced by surrealism and the work of <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder" title="Pieter Bruegel the Elder">Bruegel</a> and focused on landscape, as can be seen in McIntosh Patrick\'s <i>Traquair House</i> (1938) and more overtly Baird\'s <i>The Birth of Venus</i> (1934). Before his success in painting, McIntosh Patrick gained a reputation as an etcher. Leading figures in the field in the inter-war period included <a href="/wiki/William_Wilson_(artist)" title="William Wilson (artist)">William Wilson</a> (1905\xe2\x80\x9372) and Ian Fleming (1906-94).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_dance">Music and dance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music and dance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Classical_music_in_Scotland" title="Classical music in Scotland">Classical music in Scotland</a></div>\n<p>The ideas of the Scottish Renaissance were brought to classical music by Francis George Scott (1880\xe2\x80\x931958), MacDiarmid\'s former teacher, who adapted several of the poet\'s works into songs.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n<a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Stevenson" title="Ronald Stevenson">Ronald Stevenson</a> (b. 1938) collaborated with Scott and both wrote in <a href="/wiki/Atonality" title="Atonality">12-tone modernism</a>. Stevenson developed a tonality from Scottish music, creating settings of folk songs including concertos for his instrument the piano (1966 and 1972). He also adapted work by Scottish renaissance poets such as MacDiarmid, <a href="/wiki/Sorley_Maclean" class="mw-redirect" title="Sorley Maclean">Sorley Maclean</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Soutar" title="William Soutar">William Soutar</a>. The influence of <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a> (1906\xe2\x80\x9375) was evident in the initials used in his large-scale piano work <i><a href="/wiki/Passacaglia_on_DSCH" title="Passacaglia on DSCH">Passacaglia on DSCH</a></i> (1963).<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p193_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p193-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Robin_Orr" title="Robin Orr">Robin Orr</a> (1909\xe2\x80\x932006) and <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Thorpe_Davie" title="Cedric Thorpe Davie">Cedric Thorpe Davie</a> (1913\xe2\x80\x9383) were influenced by modernism and Scottish musical cadences.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p193_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p193-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The influence of modernism can also be heard in the work of <a href="/wiki/Erik_Chisholm" title="Erik Chisholm">Erik Chisholm</a> (1904\xe2\x80\x9365) in his <i>Pibroch Piano Concerto</i> (1930) and the <i>Straloch suite for Orchestra</i> (1933) and the sonata <i>An Riobhan Dearg</i> (1939). In 1928 he founded the Scottish Ballet Society (later the Celtic Ballet) with choreographer <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Morris_(dancer)" title="Margaret Morris (dancer)">Margaret Morris</a>, the long term partner of J. D. Fergusson. Together they created several ballets, including <i>The Forsaken Mermaid</i> (1940). He was also instrumental in the foundation of the <i>Active Society for the Propagation of Contemporary Music</i>, for which he brought leading composers to Glasgow to perform their work.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p193_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p193-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_and_influence">Decline and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scotpoetlibrary.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Scotpoetlibrary.jpg/300px-Scotpoetlibrary.jpg" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Scotpoetlibrary.jpg/450px-Scotpoetlibrary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Scotpoetlibrary.jpg/600px-Scotpoetlibrary.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scotpoetlibrary.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Poetry_Library" title="Scottish Poetry Library">Scottish Poetry Library</a>, Crichton\'s Close, Edinburgh. The Scottish Renaissance revived interest in Scottish poetry</div></div></div>\n<p><br />\nAlthough many of the participants were to live until the 1970s and later, the truly revolutionary aspect of the Scottish Renaissance can be said to have been over by the 1960s, when it became eclipsed by various other movements, often international in nature.\n</p><p>The most famous clash was at the 1962 Edinburgh Writers Festival, where <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a> denounced <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Trocchi" title="Alexander Trocchi">Alexander Trocchi</a>, a younger Scottish writer, as "cosmopolitan scum", and Trocchi himself claimed "sodomy" as a basis for his writing. This is often seen as a clash of the generations, although it is rarely reported that the two writers corresponded with each other later, and became friends. Both were controversialists of sorts.\n</p><p>The Scottish Renaissance also had a profound effect on the Scottish independence movement, and the roots of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> may be said to be firmly in it.\n</p><p>The revival in both Scotland\'s indigenous languages is partly drawn from the renaissance.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_figures">Major figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Major figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Other people connected with the Scottish renaissance, not mentioned previously, are listed below.\n</p><p>Note: These figures were not all contemporaries of the first generation of Scottish Renaissance writers and artists who emerged in the 1920s and 1930s. href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Scottish Renaissance has been criticized for its rejection of women and femininity \xe2\x80\x93 something which might be associated in part with its reaction against the domestic sentimentality of the <a
