Artists">FBA</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Henry Spencer Moore</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Order_of_Merit" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of the Order of Merit">OM</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Companion_of_Honour" class="mw-redirect" title="Companion of Honour">CH</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">FBA</a></span> (30 July 1898 \xe2\x80\x93 31 August 1986) was an English artist. He is best known for his semi-<a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract</a> monumental bronze sculptures which are located around the world as public works of art.  As well as sculpture, Moore produced many drawings, including a series depicting Londoners sheltering from <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">the Blitz</a> during the Second World War, along with other graphic works on paper.\n</p><p>His forms are usually abstractions of the human figure, typically depicting mother-and-child or reclining figures. Moore\'s works are usually suggestive of the female body, apart from a phase in the 1950s when he sculpted family groups. His forms are generally pierced or contain hollow spaces. Many interpreters liken the undulating form of his reclining figures to the landscape and hills of his birthplace, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>.\n</p><p>Moore was born in <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, the son of a coal miner. He became well-known through his carved marble and larger-scale abstract cast bronze sculptures, and was instrumental in introducing a particular form of <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a> to the United Kingdom. His ability in later life to fulfil large-scale commissions made him exceptionally wealthy. Despite this, he lived frugally; most of the money he earned went towards endowing the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore_Foundation" title="Henry Moore Foundation">Henry Moore Foundation</a>, which continues to support education and promotion of the arts.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Henry Moore was born in <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, England, to Mary Baker and Raymond Spencer Moore. His father was of Irish origin and became pit deputy and then under-manager of the Wheldale <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">colliery</a> in Castleford. He was an <a href="/wiki/Autodidact" class="mw-redirect" title="Autodidact">autodidact</a> with an interest in music and literature. Determined that his sons would not work in the mines, he saw formal education as the route to their advancement.<sup id="cite_ref-G15_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G15-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Henry was the seventh of eight children in a family that often struggled with poverty. He attended infant and elementary schools in Castleford, where he began modelling in <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> and carving in wood. He professed to have decided to become a sculptor when he was eleven after hearing of <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>\'s achievements at a Sunday School reading.<sup id="cite_ref-HMF-website-bio-childhood_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HMF-website-bio-childhood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On his second attempt he was accepted at <a href="/wiki/Castleford_Academy" title="Castleford Academy">Castleford Grammar School</a>, which several of his siblings had attended, where his headmaster soon noticed his talent and interest in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">medieval sculpture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His art teacher broadened his knowledge of art, and with her encouragement, he determined to make art his career; first by sitting for examinations for a scholarship to the local art college.<sup id="cite_ref-Berthoud-19_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berthoud-19-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Moore\'s earliest recorded carvings \xe2\x80\x93 a plaque for the Scott Society at Castleford Secondary School, and a Roll of Honour commemorating the boys who went to fight in the First World War from the school \xe2\x80\x93 were executed around this time.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite his early promise, Moore\'s parents had been against him training as a sculptor, a vocation they considered manual labour with few career prospects. After a brief introduction as a student teacher, Moore became a teacher at the school he had attended.<sup id="cite_ref-Berthoud-19_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berthoud-19-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Upon turning eighteen, Moore volunteered for army service. He was the youngest man in the <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales%27_Own_Civil_Service_Rifles" title="Prince of Wales&#39; Own Civil Service Rifles">Prince of Wales\' Own Civil Service Rifles</a> regiment and was injured in 1917 in a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">gas attack</a>, on 30 November at <a href="/wiki/Bourlon" title="Bourlon">Bourlon</a> Wood,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cambrai_(1917)" title="Battle of Cambrai (1917)">Battle of Cambrai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> After recovering in hospital, he saw out the remainder of the war as a <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">physical training</a> instructor, only returning to France as the Armistice was signed. He recalled later, "for me the war passed in a romantic haze of trying to be a hero."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This attitude changed as he reflected on the destructiveness of war and in 1940 he wrote, in a letter to his friend Arthur Sale, that "a year or two after [the war] the sight of a khaki uniform began to mean everything in life that was wrong and wasteful and <i>anti-life</i>. And I still have that feeling."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings_as_a_sculptor">Beginnings as a Itza</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>After the Great War, Moore received an ex-serviceman\'s grant to continue his education and in 1919 he became a student at the Leeds School of Art (now <a href="/wiki/Leeds_College_of_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds College of Art">Leeds College of Art</a>), which set up a sculpture studio especially for him. At the college, he met <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth" title="Barbara Hepworth">Barbara Hepworth</a>, a fellow student who would also become a well-known British sculptor, and began a friendship and gentle professional rivalry that lasted for many years. In Leeds, Moore also had access to the modernist works in the collection of Sir <a href="/wiki/Michael_Ernest_Sadler" class="mw-redirect" title="Michael Ernest Sadler">Michael Sadler</a>, the University <a href="/wiki/Vice-Chancellor" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice-Chancellor">Vice-Chancellor</a>, which had a pronounced effect on his development.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1921, Moore won a scholarship to study at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Art" title="Royal College of Art">Royal College of Art</a> in London, along with Hepworth and other Yorkshire contemporaries. While in London, Moore extended his knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Primitivism" title="Primitivism">primitive art</a> and sculpture, studying the <a href="/wiki/Ethnographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnographic">ethnographic</a> collections at the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.\n</p><p>The student sculptures of both Moore and Hepworth followed the standard romantic <a href="/wiki/Victorian_decorative_arts" title="Victorian decorative arts">Victorian</a> style, and included natural forms, landscapes and figurative modelling of animals. Moore later became uncomfortable with classically derived ideals; his later familiarity with primitivism and the influence of sculptors such as <a href="/wiki/Constantin_Br%C3%A2ncu%C8%99i" title="Constantin Br\xc3\xa2ncu\xc8\x99i">Constantin Br\xc3\xa2ncu\xc8\x99i</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Epstein" title="Jacob Epstein">Jacob Epstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Gaudier-Brzeska" title="Henri Gaudier-Brzeska">Henri Gaudier-Brzeska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Dobson_(sculptor)" title="Frank Dobson (sculptor)">Frank Dobson</a> led him to the method of <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_sculpting" title="Glossary of sculpting">direct carving</a>, in which imperfections in the material and marks left by tools became part of the finished sculpture. Having adopted this technique, Moore was in conflict with academic tutors who did not appreciate such a modern approach. During one exercise set by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Derwent_Wood" title="Francis Derwent Wood">Derwent Wood</a> (the professor of sculpture at the Royal College), Moore was asked to reproduce a marble <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">relief</a> of <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Rosselli" title="Domenico Rosselli">Domenico Rosselli</a>\'s <i>The Virgin and Child</i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> by first modelling the relief in <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a>, then reproducing it in marble using the mechanical aid known as a "<a href="/wiki/Pointing_machine" title="Pointing machine">pointing machine</a>", a technique called "pointing". Instead, he carved the relief directly, even marking the surface to simulate the prick marks that would have been left by the pointing machine.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1924, Moore won a six-month travelling scholarship which he spent in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Northern Italy</a> studying the great works of <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giotto_di_Bondone" class="mw-redirect" title="Giotto di Bondone">Giotto di Bondone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pisano" title="Giovanni Pisano">Giovanni Pisano</a> and several other <a href="/wiki/Old_Masters" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Masters">Old Masters</a>. During this period he also visited Paris, took advantage of the timed-sketching classes at the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Colarossi" title="Acad\xc3\xa9mie Colarossi">Acad\xc3\xa9mie Colarossi</a>, and viewed, in the <a href="/wiki/Trocad%C3%A9ro#The_old_Palais_du_Trocad\xc3\xa9ro" title="Trocad\xc3\xa9ro">Trocadero</a>, a plaster cast of a <a href="/wiki/Toltec" title="Toltec">Toltec</a>-<a href="/wiki/Maya_civilisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Maya civilisation">Maya</a> sculptural form, the <a href="/wiki/Chac_Mool" class="mw-redirect" title="Chac Mool">Chac Mool</a>, which he had previously seen in book illustrations. The reclining figure was to have a profound effect upon Moore\'s work, becoming the primary motif of his sculpture.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hampstead">Hampstead</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hampstead">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On returning to London, Moore undertook a seven-year teaching post at the Royal College of Art. He was required to work two days a week, which allowed him time to spend on his own work. His first public commission, <i>West Wind</i> (1928\xe2\x80\x9329), was one of the eight reliefs of the \'four winds\' high on the walls of <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>\'s headquarters at <a href="/wiki/55_Broadway,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="55 Broadway, London">55 Broadway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The other \'winds\' were carved by contemporary sculptors including <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gill" title="Eric Gill">Eric Gill</a> with the ground-level pieces provided by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Epstein" title="Jacob Epstein">Epstein</a>.  1928 saw Moore\'s first solo exhibition, held at the Warren Gallery in London.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  In July 1929, Moore married Irina Radetsky, a painting student at the Royal College. Irina was born in <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> in 1907 to Ukrainian\xe2\x80\x93Polish parents. Her father did not return from the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">Russian Revolution</a> and her mother was evacuated to Paris where she married a British army officer. Irina was smuggled to Paris a year later and went to school there until she was 16, after which she was sent to live with her stepfather\'s relatives in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg/220px-HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg/330px-HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg/440px-HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg 2x" data-file-width="648" data-file-height="507" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryMoore_WestWind.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>West Wind</i>, 1928\xe2\x80\x9329; Moore\'s first public commission was carved from <a href="/wiki/Portland_stone" title="Portland stone">Portland stone</a> and shows the influence of  <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>\'s figures for the <a href="/wiki/Medici_Chapel" title="Medici Chapel">Medici Chapel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chac_Mool" class="mw-redirect" title="Chac Mool">Chac Mool</a> figure.</div></div></div>\n<p>Irina found security in her marriage to Moore and was soon posing for him. Shortly after they married, the couple moved to a studio in <a href="/wiki/Hampstead" title="Hampstead">Hampstead</a> at 11a Parkhill Road NW3, joining a small colony of <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> artists who were taking root there. Shortly afterward, Hepworth and her second husband <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nicholson" title="Ben Nicholson">Ben Nicholson</a> moved into a studio around the corner from Moore, while <a href="/wiki/Naum_Gabo" title="Naum Gabo">Naum Gabo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Penrose" title="Roland Penrose">Roland Penrose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Stephenson" title="Cecil Stephenson">Cecil Stephenson</a> and the art critic <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Read" title="Herbert Read">Herbert Read</a> also lived in the area (Read referred to the area as "a nest of gentle artists").<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This led to a rapid cross-fertilization of ideas that Read would publicise, helping to raise Moore\'s public profile. The area was also a stopping-off point for many refugee artists, architects and designers from continental Europe en route to America\xe2\x80\x94some of whom would later commission works from Moore.\n</p><p>In 1932, after six year\'s teaching at the Royal College, Moore took up a post as the Head of the Department of Sculpture at the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_School_of_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelsea School of Art">Chelsea School of Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Artistically, Moore, Hepworth and other members of The <a href="/wiki/Seven_and_Five_Society" title="Seven and Five Society">Seven and Five Society</a> would develop steadily more abstract work,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> partly influenced by their frequent trips to Paris and their contact with leading progressive artists, notably <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Braque" title="Georges Braque">Georges Braque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Arp" title="Jean Arp">Jean Arp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Giacometti" title="Alberto Giacometti">Alberto Giacometti</a>. Moore flirted with <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">Surrealism</a>, joining <a href="/wiki/Paul_Nash_(artist)" title="Paul Nash (artist)">Paul Nash</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a> movement "<a href="/wiki/Unit_One" title="Unit One">Unit One</a>", in 1933. In 1934, Moore visited Spain; he visited the <a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Altamira" title="Cave of Altamira">cave of Altamira</a> (which he described as the "Royal Academy of Cave Painting"), Madrid, Toledo and Pamplona.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Moore and Nash were on the organising committee of the International Surrealist Exhibition, which took place in London in 1936. In 1937, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Penrose" title="Roland Penrose">Roland Penrose</a> purchased an abstract \'Mother and Child\' in stone from Moore that he displayed in the front garden of his house in Hampstead. The work proved controversial with other residents and the local press ran a campaign against the piece over the next two years. At this time Moore gradually transitioned from direct carving to casting in bronze, modelling preliminary <a href="/wiki/Maquette" title="Maquette">maquettes</a> in clay or plaster rather than making preparatory drawings.\n</p><p>In 1938, Moore met <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> From this time, Clark became an unlikely but influential champion of Moore\'s work,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and through his position as member of the <a href="/wiki/Arts_Council_of_Great_Britain" title="Arts Council of Great Britain">Arts Council of Great Britain</a> he secured exhibitions and commissions for the artist.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span A Miner Pushing a Tub</i> (1942) (Art.IWM ART LD 2240)</div></div></div>\n<p>At the outbreak of the Second World War the Chelsea School of Art was evacuated to Northampton and Moore resigned his teaching post. During the war, Moore produced powerful drawings of Londoners sleeping in the London Underground while sheltering from <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">the Blitz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a>, the chairman of the <a href="/wiki/War_Artists%27_Advisory_Committee" title="War Artists&#39; Advisory Committee">War Artists\' Advisory Committee</a> (WAAC), had previously tried to recruit  Moore as a full-time salaried war artist and now agreed to purchase some of the shelter drawings and issued contracts for further examples. The shelter drawings WAAC acquired were completed between the autumn of 1940 and the spring of 1941 and are regarded as among the finest products of the WAAC scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-Foss_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foss-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In August 1941 WAAC commissioned Moore to draw miners working underground at the Wheldale Colliery in Yorkshire, where his father had worked at the start of the century. Moore drew the people in the shelters as passively waiting the all-clear while miners aggressively worked the coal-faces.<sup id="cite_ref-WW2Art_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WW2Art-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> These drawings helped to boost Moore\'s international reputation, particularly in America where examples were included in the WAAC <i>Britain at War</i> exhibition which toured North America throughout the war.<sup id="cite_ref-Foss_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foss-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After their Hampstead home was hit by bomb shrapnel in September 1940, Moore and Irina moved out of London to live in a farmhouse called Hoglands in the hamlet of <a href="/wiki/Perry_Green,_Hertfordshire" title="Perry Green, Hertfordshire">Perry Green</a> near <a href="/wiki/Much_Hadham" title="Much Hadham">Much Hadham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This was to become Moore\'s home and workshop for the rest of his life. Despite acquiring significant wealth later in life, Moore never felt the need to move to larger premises and, apart from the addition of a number of outbuildings and studios, the house changed little over the years. In 1943 he received a commission from <a href="/wiki/St_Matthew%27s_Church,_Northampton" title="St Matthew&#39;s Church, Northampton">St Matthew\'s Church, Northampton</a>, to carve a Madonna and Child; this sculpture was the first in an important series of family-group sculptures.<sup id="cite_ref-Henry_Moore_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry_Moore-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Moore,_Family_Group_(1950).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Henry_Moore%2C_Family_Group_%281950%29.jpg/220px-Henry_Moore%2C_Family_Group_%281950%29.jpg" width="220" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Henry_Moore%2C_Family_Group_%281950%29.jpg/330px-Henry_Moore%2C_Family_Group_%281950%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Henry_Moore%2C_Family_Group_%281950%29.jpg/440px-Henry_Moore%2C_Family_Group_%281950%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="856" data-file-height="1295" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Moore,_Family_Group_(1950).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Family_Group_(Moore)" title="Family Group (Moore)">Family Group</a></i> (1950) bronze, <a href="/wiki/The_Barclay_School" title="The Barclay School">Barclay School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stevenage" title="Stevenage">Stevenage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Hertfordshire, England">Hertfordshire</a>. Moore\'s first large-scale commission after World War II.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shahbanu_Farah,_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Shahbanu_Farah%2C_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png/220px-Shahbanu_Farah%2C_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Shahbanu_Farah%2C_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png/330px-Shahbanu_Farah%2C_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Shahbanu_Farah%2C_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png/440px-Shahbanu_Farah%2C_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png 2x" data-file-width="621" data-file-height="471" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shahbanu_Farah,_Opening_ceremony_of_Henry_Moore_Gallery_in_Tehran.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Farah_Pahlavi" title="Farah Pahlavi">Shahbanu Farah</a> in  Henry Moore\'s Gallery, Tehran, May 1971.</div></div></div>\n<p>After the war and following several earlier miscarriages, Irina gave birth to their daughter, Mary Moore, in March 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The child was named after Moore\'s mother, who had died two years earlier. Both the loss of his mother and the arrival of a baby focused Moore\'s mind on the family, which he expressed in his work by producing many "mother-and-child" compositions, although reclining and internal/external figures also remained popular. In the same year, Moore made his first visit to America when a retrospective exhibition of his work opened at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Before the war, Moore had been approached by educator <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morris_(education)" title="Henry Morris (education)">Henry Morris</a>, who was trying to reform education with his concept of the <a href="/wiki/Village_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Village College">Village College</a>. Morris had engaged <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gropius" title="Walter Gropius">Walter Gropius</a> as the architect for his second village college at <a href="/wiki/Impington_Village_College" title="Impington Village College">Impington</a> near <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, and he wanted Moore to design a major public sculpture for the site. The County Council, however, could not afford Gropius\'s full design, and scaled back the project when Gropius emigrated to America. Lacking funds, Morris had to cancel Moore\'s sculpture, which had not progressed beyond the maquette stage.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  Moore was able to reuse the design in 1950 for a similar commission outside a secondary school for the new town of <a href="/wiki/Stevenage" title="Stevenage">Stevenage</a>. This time, the project was completed and <i><a href="/wiki/Family_Group_(Moore)" title="Family Group (Moore)">Family Group</a></i> became Moore\'s first large-scale public bronze.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1950s, Moore began to receive increasingly significant commissions.  He exhibited <a href="/wiki/Reclining_Figure:_Festival" title="Reclining Figure: Festival">Reclining Figure: Festival</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Festival_of_Britain" title="Festival of Britain">Festival of Britain</a> in 1951,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and in 1958 produced a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_Reclining_Figure_1957-58" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO Reclining Figure 1957-58">large marble reclining figure</a> for the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> building in Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  With many more public works of art, the scale of Moore\'s sculptures grew significantly and he started to employ an increasing number of assistants to work with him at Much Hadham, including <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Caro" title="Anthony Caro">Anthony Caro</a><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wentworth_(artist)" title="Richard Wentworth (artist)">Richard Wentworth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On the campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> in December 1967, 25 years to the minute<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> after the team of physicists led by <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a> achieved the first controlled, self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction, Moore\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Energy_(sculpture)" title="Nuclear Energy (sculpture)">Nuclear Energy</a></i> was unveiled on the site of what was once the university\'s football field stands, in the <a href="/wiki/Rackets_(sport)" title="Rackets (sport)">rackets court</a> beneath which the experiments had taken place.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This 12-foot-tall piece in the middle of a large, open plaza is often thought to represent a <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_cloud" title="Mushroom cloud">mushroom cloud</a> topped by a massive human skull, but Moore\'s interpretation was very different. He once told a friend that he hoped viewers would "go around it, looking out through the open spaces, and that they may have a feeling of being in a cathedral."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>, Moore also commemorated science with a large bronze sundial, locally named <i><a href="/wiki/Man_Enters_the_Cosmos" title="Man Enters the Cosmos">Man Enters the Cosmos</a></i> (1980), which was commissioned to recognise the <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg/220px-Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg" width="220" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg/330px-Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg/440px-Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4550" data-file-height="5075" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Moore_Allan_Warren.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Moore in his studio in England (1975), by <a href="/wiki/Allan_Warren" title="Allan Warren">Allan Warren</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The last three decades of Moore\'s life continued in a similar vein; several major retrospectives took place around the world, notably a very prominent exhibition in the summer of 1972 in the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Belvedere_(fort)" title="Belvedere (fort)">Forte di Belvedere</a> overlooking <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>. Following the pioneering documentary \'Henry Moore\', produced by John Read in 1951, he appeared in many films. In 1964, for instance, Moore was featured in the documentary "5 British Sculptors (Work and Talk)" by American filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Warren_Forma" title="Warren Forma">Warren Forma</a>. By the end of the 1970s, there were some 40 exhibitions a year featuring his work. The number of commissions continued to increase; he completed <i><a href="/wiki/Knife_Edge_Two_Piece_1962%E2%80%9365" title="Knife Edge Two Piece 1962\xe2\x80\x9365">Knife Edge Two Piece</a></i> in 1962 for <a href="/wiki/College_Green_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="College Green (London)">College Green</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Houses of Parliament</a> in London. According to Moore, "When I was offered the site near the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>&#160;... I liked the place so much that I didn\'t bother to go and see an alternative site in <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a>\xe2\x80\x94one lonely sculpture can be lost in a large park. The House of Lords site is quite different. It is next to a path where people walk and it has a few seats where they can sit and contemplate it."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As his wealth grew, Moore began to worry about his legacy. With the help of his daughter Mary, he set up the Henry Moore Trust in 1972, with a view to protecting his estate from <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_Tax_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inheritance Tax (United Kingdom)">death duties</a>. By 1977, he was paying close to a million pounds a year in <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a>; to mitigate his tax burden, he established the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore_Foundation" title="Henry Moore Foundation">Henry Moore Foundation</a> as a registered charity with Irina and Mary as trustees. The Foundation was established to encourage the public appreciation of the visual arts and especially the works of Moore. It now runs his house and estate at Perry Green, with a gallery, sculpture park and studios.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1979, Henry Moore became abruptly known in Germany when his sculpture <i>Large Two Forms</i> was installed in the forecourt of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Chancellery_(Bonn)" title="Federal Chancellery (Bonn)">German Chancellery</a> in Bonn, which was the capital city of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> prior to German reunification in October 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Henry Moore died on 31 August 1986, at the age of 88, in his home near <a href="/wiki/Much_Hadham" title="Much Hadham">Much Hadham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> where his body is interred in Perry Green churchyard.\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:702px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry-moore-ago.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Henry-moore-ago.jpg/700px-Henry-moore-ago.jpg" width="700" height="114" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Henry-moore-ago.jpg/1050px-Henry-moore-ago.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/39/Henry-moore-ago.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1245" data-file-height="202" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry-moore-ago.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Art_Gallery_of_Ontario" title="Art Gallery of Ontario">Art Gallery of Ontario</a>\'s Henry Moore collection is the largest public collection of his works in the world.</div></div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg/220px-Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg" width="220" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg/330px-Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg/440px-Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="774" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stuttgart-henry-moore-liegende.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Moore\'s bronze <i><a href="/wiki/Draped_Reclining_Woman_1957-58" class="mw-redirect" title="Draped Reclining Woman 1957-58">Draped Reclining Woman 1957-58</a></i> ("Die Liegende") in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, typical of his early reclining figures</div></div></div>\n<p>Moore\'s signature form is a reclining figure. Moore\'s exploration of this form, under the influence of the Toltec-Mayan figure he had seen at the Louvre, was to lead him to increasing abstraction as he turned his thoughts towards experimentation with the elements of design. Moore\'s earlier reclining figures deal principally with mass, while his later ones contrast the solid elements of the sculpture with the space, not only round them but generally through them as he pierced the forms with openings.\n</p><p>\nEarlier figures are pierced in a conventional manner, in which bent limbs separate from and rejoin the body. The later, more abstract figures are often penetrated by spaces directly through the body, by which means Moore explores and alternates concave and convex shapes. These more extreme piercings developed in parallel with <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth" title="Barbara Hepworth">Barbara Hepworth</a>\'s sculptures.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Hepworth first pierced a torso after misreading a review of one of Henry Moore\'s early shows. The  plaster <i><a href="/wiki/Reclining_Figure:_Festival" title="Reclining Figure: Festival">Reclining Figure: Festival</a></i> (1951) in the <a href="/wiki/Tate" title="Tate">Tate</a>, is characteristic of Moore\'s later sculptures: an abstract female figure intercut with voids. As with much of the post-War work, there are several bronze casts of this sculpture. When Moore\'s niece asked why his sculptures had such simple titles, he replied, </p><blockquote> <p><i>"All art should have a certain mystery and should make demands on the spectator. Giving a sculpture or a drawing too explicit a title takes away part of that mystery so that the spectator moves on to the next object, making no effort to ponder the meaning of what he has just seen. Everyone thinks that he or she looks but they don\'t really, you know."</i> <sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Moore\'s early work is focused on <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_sculpting#direct_carving" title="Glossary of sculpting">direct carving</a>, in which the form of the sculpture evolves as the artist repeatedly whittles away at the block. In the 1930s, Moore\'s transition into <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a> paralleled that of Barbara Hepworth; the two exchanged new ideas with each other and several other artists then living in Hampstead. Moore made many preparatory <a href="/wiki/Sketch_(drawing)" title="Sketch (drawing)">sketches</a> and drawings for each sculpture. Most of these sketchbooks have survived and provide insight into Moore\'s development. He placed great importance on drawing; in old age, when he had arthritis, he continued to draw.<sup id="cite_ref-sabbath_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabbath-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg/300px-Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg" width="300" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg/450px-Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg/600px-Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2805" data-file-height="1429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rotterdam_kunstwerk_Wall_Relief_no.1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Wall Relief No. 1</i>, (1955), Bouwcentrum, <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a></div></div></div>\n<p>After the Second World War, Moore\'s bronzes took on their larger scale, which was particularly suited for public art commissions. As a matter of practicality, he largely abandoned direct carving, and took on several assistants to help produce the larger forms based on maquettes. By the end of the 1940s, he produced sculptures increasingly by modelling, working out the shape in clay or plaster before casting the final work in bronze using the <a href="/wiki/Lost-wax_casting" title="Lost-wax casting">lost wax</a> technique. These maquettes often began as small forms shaped by Moore\'s hands\xe2\x80\x94a process which gives his work an organic feeling. They are from the body.\nAt his home in Much Hadham, Moore built up a collection of natural objects; skulls, driftwood, pebbles, rocks and shells, which he would use to provide inspiration for organic forms. For his largest works, he usually produced a half-scale, working model before scaling up for the final <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">moulding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">casting</a> at a bronze <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundry</a>. Moore often refined the final full plaster shape and added surface marks before casting.\n</p><p>Moore produced at least three significant examples of architectural sculpture during his career. In 1928, despite his own self-described "extreme reservations", he accepted his first public commission for <i>West Wind</i> for the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> Building at <a href="/wiki/55_Broadway" title="55 Broadway">55 Broadway</a> in London, joining the company of <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Epstein" title="Jacob Epstein">Jacob Epstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Gill" title="Eric Gill">Eric Gill</a>. In 1953, he completed a four-part concrete screen for the Time-Life Building in New Bond Street, London, and in 1955 Moore turned to his first and only work in carved brick, "Wall Relief" at the Bouwcentrum in Rotterdam. The brick relief was sculpted with 16,000 bricks by two Dutch bricklayers under Moore\'s supervision.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg/220px-HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg" width="220" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg/330px-HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg/440px-HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1176" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryMoore_RecliningFigure_1951.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Large_Reclining_Figure" class="mw-redirect" title="Large Reclining Figure">Large Reclining Figure</a></i> (1984, based on a smaller model of 1938), <a href="/wiki/Fitzwilliam_Museum,_Cambridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Fitzwilliam Museum, Cambridge">Fitzwilliam Museum, Cambridge</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The aftermath of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">The Holocaust</a>, and the age of the atomic bomb instilled in the sculpture of the mid-1940s a sense that art should return to its pre-cultural and pre-rational origins. In the literature of the day, writers such as <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a> advocated a similar reductive philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-C34_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C34-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> At an introductory speech in New York City for an exhibition of one of the finest <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernist</a> sculptors, <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Giacometti" title="Alberto Giacometti">Alberto Giacometti</a>, Sartre spoke of "The beginning and the end of history".<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Moore\'s sense of England emerging undefeated from siege led to his focus on pieces characterised by endurance and continuity.<sup id="cite_ref-C34_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C34-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caro_DreamCity_1996.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Abstract sculpture made of rusting steel" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Caro_DreamCity_1996.jpg/220px-Caro_DreamCity_1996.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Caro_DreamCity_1996.jpg/440px-Caro_DreamCity_1996.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="406" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caro_DreamCity_1996.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Dream City</i> by Anthony Caro, (1996), rusting steel, at the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Sculpture_Park" title="Yorkshire Sculpture Park">Yorkshire Sculpture Park</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Most sculptors who emerged during the height of Moore\'s fame, and in the aftermath of his death, found themselves cast in his shadow. By the late 1940s, Moore was a worldwide celebrity; he was the voice of British sculpture, and of British modernism in general. The next generation was constantly compared against him, and reacted by challenging his legacy, his "establishment" credentials and his position. At the 1952 <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a>, eight new British sculptors produced their <i><a href="/wiki/Geometry_of_Fear" title="Geometry of Fear">Geometry of Fear</a></i> works as a direct contrast to the ideals behind Moore\'s idea of <i>Endurance, Continuity</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> his large bronze <i>Double Standing Figure</i> stood outside the British pavilion, and contrasted strongly with the rougher and more angular works inside.<sup id="cite_ref-arts_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arts-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Yet Moore had a direct influence on several generations of sculptors of both British and international reputation. Among the artists who have acknowledged Moore\'s importance to their work are <a href="/wiki/Sir_Anthony_Caro" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Anthony Caro">Sir Anthony Caro</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phillip_King_(artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Phillip King (artist)">Phillip King</a><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Witkin" title="Isaac Witkin">Isaac Witkin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> all three having been assistants to Moore. Other artists whose work was influenced by him include <a href="/wiki/Helaine_Blumenfeld" title="Helaine Blumenfeld">Helaine Blumenfeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drago_Marin_Cherina" title="Drago Marin Cherina">Drago Marin Cherina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Chadwick" title="Lynn Chadwick">Lynn Chadwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Paolozzi" title="Eduardo Paolozzi">Eduardo Paolozzi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Meadows" title="Bernard Meadows">Bernard Meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reg_Butler" title="Reg Butler">Reg Butler</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Turnbull_(artist)" title="William Turnbull (artist)">William Turnbull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Adams_(sculptor_and_designer)" title="Robert Adams (sculptor and designer)">Robert Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Armitage" title="Kenneth Armitage">Kenneth Armitage</a>, and Geoffrey Clarke.<sup id="cite_ref-Tate_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tate-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Draped_Seated_Woman_1957-58" class="mw-redirect" title="Draped Seated Woman 1957-58">Draped Seated Woman 1957-58</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Sculpture_Park" title="Yorkshire Sculpture Park">Yorkshire Sculpture Park</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In 2012, the council of the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Tower_Hamlets" title="London Borough of Tower Hamlets">London Borough of Tower Hamlets</a> announced its plans to sell another version of <i><a href="/wiki/Draped_Seated_Woman_1957%E2%80%9358" title="Draped Seated Woman 1957\xe2\x80\x9358">Draped Seated Woman 1957\xe2\x80\x9358</a></i>, a 1.6-tonne bronze sculpture.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Moore, a well-known socialist, had sold the sculpture at a fraction of its market value to the former <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London County Council</a> on the understanding that it would be displayed in a public space and might enrich the lives of those living in a socially deprived area. Nicknamed <i>Old Flo</i>, it was installed on the Stifford council estate in 1962 but was vandalised and moved to the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Sculpture_Park" title="Yorkshire Sculpture Park">Yorkshire Sculpture Park</a> in 1997. Tower Hamlets Council later had considered moving <i><a href="/wiki/Draped_Seated_Woman" class="mw-redirect" title="Draped Seated Woman">Draped Seated Woman</a></i> to private land in <a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf" title="Canary Wharf">Canary Wharf</a> but instead chose to "explore options" for a sale.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> In response to the announcement an open letter was published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, signed by Mary Moore, the artist\'s daughter, by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Nicholas_Serota" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Nicholas Serota">Sir Nicholas Serota</a>, Director of the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a>, by filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Danny_Boyle" title="Danny Boyle">Danny Boyle</a>, and by artists including <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Deller" title="Jeremy Deller">Jeremy Deller</a>. The letter said that the sale "goes against the spirit of Henry Moore\'s original sale" of the work.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_interest">Popular interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Popular interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Today, the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore_Foundation" title="Henry Moore Foundation">Henry Moore Foundation</a> manages the artist\'s former home at Perry Green in Hertfordshire as a visitor destination, with 70 acres of sculpture grounds as well as his restored house and studios. It also runs the Henry Moore Institute in Leeds which organises exhibitions and research activities in international sculpture. Popular interest in Moore\'s work was perceived by some to have declined for a while in the UK  but has been revived in recent times by exhibitions including Henry Moore at Tate Britain in 2010 and Moore at Kew and Hatfield in 2007 and 2011 respectively. The Foundation he endowed continues to play an essential role in promoting contemporary art in the United Kingdom and abroad through its grants and exhibitions programme.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections">Collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span 1966.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The world\'s largest collection of Moore\'s work is open to the public and is housed in the house and grounds of the 60-acre estate, that was Moore\'s home for forty years, in <a href="/wiki/Perry_Green,_Hertfordshire" title="Perry Green, Hertfordshire">Perry Green, Hertfordshire</a>. The site and the collection are now owned by the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore_Foundation" title="Henry Moore Foundation">Henry Moore Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In December 2005, thieves entered a courtyard at the Henry Moore Foundation and stole a cast of Moore\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Reclining_Figure_1969-70" class="mw-redirect" title="Reclining Figure 1969-70">Reclining Figure 1969-70</a></i> (LH 608) &#8211; a 3.6 metre-long, 2.1-tonne bronze sculpture. Closed-circuit-television footage showed that they used a crane to lower the piece onto a stolen flatbed truck. A substantial reward was offered by the Foundation for information leading to its recovery. By May 2009, after a thorough investigation, British officials said they believe the work, once valued at \xc2\xa33 million (US$5.3 million), was probably sold for <a href="/wiki/Scrap_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrap metal">scrap metal</a>, fetching about \xc2\xa35,000.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In July 2012 the 22 inches (56&#160;cm) bronze <i>Sundial 1965</i>, valued at \xc2\xa3500,000, was stolen from the Moore Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Later that year, following the details of the theft being publicised on the BBC <a href="/wiki/Crimewatch" title="Crimewatch">Crimewatch</a> television programme, the work was recovered, and the thieves were sentenced to twelve months\' custody.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Moore presented 36 sculptures, as well as drawings, maquettes and other works to the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a> in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toronto">Toronto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Toronto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Henry Moore Sculpture Centre in the <a href="/wiki/Art_Gallery_of_Ontario" title="Art Gallery of Ontario">Art Gallery of Ontario</a>, Toronto, opened in 1974. It comprises the world\'s largest public collection of Moore\'s work, most of it donated by him between 1971 and 1974. Moore\'s <i>Three Way Piece No. 2 (The Archer)</i> has also been on display in <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Phillips_Square" title="Nathan Phillips Square">Nathan Phillips Square</a> at <a href="/wiki/Toronto_City_Hall" title="Toronto City Hall">Toronto City Hall</a> since 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" International Sculpture Prize at the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> He turned down a <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knighthood</a> in 1951 because he felt that the bestowal would lead to a perception of him as an establishment figure and that "such a title might tend to cut me off from fellow artists whose work has aims similar to mine".<sup id="cite_ref-Tate_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tate-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> He was, however, awarded the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Companions_of_Honour" title="Order of the Companions of Honour">Companion of Honour</a> in 1955, the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit" title="Order of Merit">Order of Merit</a> in 1963 and <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Prize" title="Erasmus Prize">Erasmus Prize</a> in 1968.\n</p><p>He was a trustee of both the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="National Gallery, London">National Gallery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> His proposal that a wing of the latter should be devoted to his sculptures aroused hostility among some artists. In 1975, he became the first President of the Turner Society,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> which had been founded to campaign for a separate museum in which the whole Turner Bequest<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> might be reunited, an aim defeated by the National Gallery and Tate Gallery.\n</p><p>Given to the City of London by Moore and the Contemporary Art Society in 1967, <i><a href="/wiki/Knife_Edge_Two_Piece_1962%E2%80%9365" title="Knife Edge Two Piece 1962\xe2\x80\x9365">Knife Edge Two Piece 1962\xe2\x80\x9365</a></i> is displayed in <a href="/wiki/College_Green_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="College Green (London)">Abingdon Street Gardens</a>, opposite the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Houses of Parliament</a>, where its regular appearance in the background of televised news reports from Westminster makes it Moore\'s most prominent piece in Britain. The ownership of <i>Knife Edge Two Piece 1962\xe2\x80\x9365</i> was disputed until its 2011 acquisition by the Parliamentary Art Collection.<sup id="cite_ref-ParlNews_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ParlNews-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_market">Art market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Art market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>By the end of his career, Moore was the world\'s most successful living artist at auction. In 1982, four years before his death, <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby&#39;s">Sotheby\'s</a> in New York sold a 6&#160;ft <i>Reclining Figure</i> (1945), for $1.2 million to collector Wendell Cherry. Although a first record of $4.1 million was set in 1990, Moore\'s market slumped during the recession that followed. In 2012, his eight-foot bronze, <i><a href="/wiki/Reclining_Figure:_Festival" title="Reclining Figure: Festival">Reclining Figure: Festival</a></i> (1951) sold for a record \xc2\xa319.1 million at <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s" title="Christie&#39;s">Christie\'s</a>, making him the second most expensive 20th-century British artist after <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(artist)" title="Francis Bacon (artist)">Francis Bacon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
