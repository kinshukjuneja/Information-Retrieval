class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about deep pit, <a href="/wiki/Underground_mining_(soft_rock)" For <a href="/wiki/Surface_mining" title="Surface mining">surface mining</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Open-pit_coal_mining_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Open-pit coal mining in the United Kingdom">Open-pit coal mining in the United Kingdom</a>.</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_coal_mining#Great_Britain" title="History of coal mining">History of coal mining \xc2\xa7&#160;Great Britain</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg/220px-British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg" width="220" height="408" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg/330px-British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg/440px-British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1088" data-file-height="2017" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British.coalfields.19th.century.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coalfields of the United Kingdom in the 19th century</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Coal mining in the United Kingdom</b> dates back to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> times and occurred in many different parts of the country. Britain\'s <a href="/wiki/List_of_coalfields#Great_Britain" title="List of coalfields">coalfields</a> are associated with <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Durham,_England" title="Durham, England">Durham</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Belt" title="Central Belt">the Scottish Central Belt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>.  After 1970, coal mining quickly collapsed and practically disappeared in the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian41013_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian41013-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The consumption of coal &#8211; mostly for electricity &#8211; fell from 157 million tonnes in 1970 to 18 million tonnes in 2016. Of the coal mined, 77% of  supplies were imported<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Of the coal mined in the UK in 2016 all was from open-cast coal mines. Employment in coal mines fell from a peak of 1,191,000 in 1920 to 695,000 in 1956, 247,000 in 1976, 44,000 in 1993, and to 2,000 in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Almost all onshore coal resources in the UK occur in rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous age</a>, some of which extend under the North Sea. <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">Bituminous coal</a> is present in most of Britain\xe2\x80\x99s coalfields and is 86% to 88% carbon. In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, there are extensive deposits of <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> which is less energy-dense based on oxidation (combustion) at ordinary combustion temperatures (i.e. for the oxidation of carbon - see <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The last deep coal mine in the UK closed on 18 December 2015. Twenty-six <a href="/wiki/Open-pit_coal_mining_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Open-pit coal mining in the United Kingdom">open cast mines</a> still remain open <sup id="cite_ref-:2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and Banks Mining said in 2018 they planned to start mining a new site in <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_mining_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extent and geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The United Kingdom\'s onshore coal resources occur in rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous age</a>, some of which extend under the North Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-MineralsUK_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MineralsUK-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The carbon content of the bituminous coal present in most of the coalfields is 86% to 88%.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\nBritain\'s <a href="/wiki/List_of_coalfields#Great_Britain" title="List of coalfields">coalfields</a> are associated with <a href="/wiki/Northumberland_Coalfield" title="Northumberland Coalfield">Northumberland</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Durham_Coalfield" title="Durham Coalfield">Durham</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Wales_Coalfield" title="North Wales Coalfield">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_Coalfield" title="South Wales Coalfield">South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire_Coalfield" title="South Yorkshire Coalfield">Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Belt" title="Central Belt">the Scottish Central Belt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_Coalfield" title="Lancashire Coalfield">Lancashire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kent_Coalfield" title="Kent Coalfield">Kent</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_mining_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Stone and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> flint axes have been discovered embedded in coal, showing that it was mined in Britain before the Roman invasion. Early miners first extracted coal already exposed on the surface and then followed the seams underground.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is probable that the Romans used outcropping coal when working <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lime_kiln" title="Lime kiln">burning lime</a> for building purposes. Evidence to support these theories comes mostly from ash discovered at excavations of Roman sites.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is no mention of coal mining in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1086 although <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and iron mines are recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In the 13th century there are records of coal digging in <a href="/wiki/Durham_Coalfield" title="Durham Coalfield">Durham</a><sup id="cite_ref-durham_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-durham-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Northumberland_Coalfield" title="Northumberland Coalfield">Northumberland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nbland_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbland-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Nottinghamshire and Derbyshire, <a href="/wiki/South_Staffordshire_Coalfield" title="South Staffordshire Coalfield">Staffordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_Coalfield" title="Lancashire Coalfield">Lancashire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean_Coalfield" title="Forest of Dean Coalfield">Forest of Dean</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Wales_Coalfield" title="North Wales Coalfield">North</a><sup id="cite_ref-nwales_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nwales-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_coalfield" class="mw-redirect" title="South Wales coalfield">South Wales</a>. At this time coal was referred to as <i>sea cole</i>, a reference to coal washed ashore on the north east coast of England from either the cliffs or undersea outcrops. As the supply of coal on the surface became used up, settlers followed the seam inland by digging up the shore. Generally the seam continued underground, encouraging the settlers to dig to find coal, the precursor to modern operations.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The early mines would have been <a href="/wiki/Drift_mines" class="mw-redirect" title="Drift mines">drift mines</a> or <a href="/wiki/Adit" title="Adit">adits</a> where coal seams outcropped or by shallow <a href="/wiki/Bell_pit" title="Bell pit">bell pits</a> where coal was close to the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Shafts lined with tree trunks and branches have been found in Lancashire in workings dating from early 17th century and by 1750 brick lined shafts to 150 foot depth were common.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_mining_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Coal_Production.png" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Coal_Production.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Annual UK coal production (in red) and imports (black), DECC data.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Coal_Mining_Jobs.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/UK_Coal_Mining_Jobs.png/220px-UK_Coal_Mining_Jobs.png" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Coal_Mining_Jobs.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coal mining employment in the UK, 1880-2012 (DECC data)</div></div></div>\n<p>Coal production increased dramatically in the 19th century as the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> gathered pace, as a fuel for <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Newcomen_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcomen engine">Newcomen engine</a>, and later, the <a href="/wiki/Watt_steam_engine" title="Watt steam engine">Watt steam engine</a>.  To produce firewood in the 1860s equivalent in energy terms to domestic consumption of coal would have required 25 million acres of land per year, nearly the entire farmland area of England (26 m. acres).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A key development was the invention at <a href="/wiki/Coalbrookdale" title="Coalbrookdale">Coalbrookdale</a> in the early 18th century of <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> which could be used to make <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a>. The development of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a> by <a href="/wiki/Trevithick" title="Trevithick">Trevithick</a> early in the 19th century gave added impetus, and coal consumption grew rapidly as the railway network expanded through the Victorian period. Coal was widely used for domestic heating owing to its low cost and widespread availability. The manufacture of coal also provided <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a>, which could be used for heating and lighting. Most of the workers were children and men.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A mining business owner, Jordan Geoff of Upton colliery, West Yorkshire was prosecuted in 1927 for manslaughter for inhumane treatment of his workforce.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_mining_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_(15552330801).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_%2815552330801%29.jpg/220px-Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_%2815552330801%29.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_%2815552330801%29.jpg/330px-Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_%2815552330801%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_%2815552330801%29.jpg/440px-Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_%2815552330801%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="602" data-file-height="439" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aberaman_Miners%27_Training_Centre_(15552330801).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aberaman Miners\' Training Centre S.Wales 1951</div></div></div>\n<p>UK coal production peaked in 1913 at 287 million tonnes.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Until the late 1960s, coal was the main source of energy produced in the UK, peaking at 228 million tonnes in 1952. Ninety-five per cent of this came from roughly 1,334 deep-mines that were operational at the time, with the rest from around 92 surface mines.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> After modernisation of underground mining, a deep shaft mine could produce 700 million tonnes annually.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1986, Kellingley colliery achieved 404,000 tonnes in a single shift.\n</p><p>In the 1950s and 1960s, around a hundred North East coal mines were closed.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In March 1968, the last pit in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Country" title="Black Country">Black Country</a> closed and pit closures were a regular occurrence in many other areas.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Beginning with wildcat action in 1969, the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Mineworkers_(Great_Britain)" title="National Union of Mineworkers (Great Britain)">National Union of Mineworkers</a> became increasingly militant, and was successful in gaining increased wages in their strikes in <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1972)" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1972)">1972</a> and <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1974)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1974)">1974</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Closures were less common in the 1970s, and new investments were made in sites such as the <a href="/wiki/Selby_Coalfield" title="Selby Coalfield">Selby Coalfield</a>. In early 1984, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> government of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> announced plans to close 20 coal pits which led to the <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1984%E2%80%9385)" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1984\xe2\x80\x9385)">year-long miners\' strike</a> which ended in March 1985. The strike was unsuccessful in stopping the closures and led to an end to the <a href="/wiki/Closed_shop" title="Closed shop">closed shop</a> in <a href="/wiki/British_Coal" title="British Coal">British Coal</a>, as the breakaway <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Democratic_Mineworkers" title="Union of Democratic Mineworkers">Union of Democratic Mineworkers</a> was formed by miners who objected to the NUM\'s handling of the strike.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Numerous pit closures followed, and in August 1989 coal mining ended in the <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> coalfield.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Since 1981 production fell sharply from 128 to 17.8 million tonnes in 2009.\n</p><p>In 1994, <a href="/wiki/UK_Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a> privatised British Coal after announcing closures, with the majority of operations transferred to the new company <a href="/wiki/UK_Coal" title="UK Coal">UK Coal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, by this time British Coal had closed all but the most economical of coal pits.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The pit closures caused coal production to slump to the lowest rate in more than a century, further declining towards the end of the 1980s and into the 1990s. This coincided with initiatives for cleaner energy generation as power stations switched to gas and biomass. A total of 100 million tons was produced in 1986, but by 1995 the amount was around 50 million tons.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The last deep mine in <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a> closed when the coal was exhausted in January 2008. The mine was closed by British Coal in the privatisation of the industry fourteen years earlier and re-opened after being bought by the miners who had worked at the pit.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the limitations to the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Mineworkers_(Great_Britain)" title="National Union of Mineworkers (Great Britain)">National Union of Mineworkers\'</a> power, British coal-dependent industries have turned to cheaper imported coal.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT41613_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT41613-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  In 2001, production was exceeded by imports for the first time. In 2014, coal imported was three times more than the coal mined in Britain, despite large resources in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, companies were licensed to extract 125 million tonnes of coal in operating underground mines and 42 million tonnes at opencast locations.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Coal mining employed 4,000 workers at 30 locations in 2013, extracting 13 million tonnes of coal.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The three deep-pit mines were <a href="/wiki/Hatfield_Colliery" title="Hatfield Colliery">Hatfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kellingley_Colliery" title="Kellingley Colliery">Kellingley Collieries</a> in Yorkshire and <a href="/wiki/Thoresby_Colliery" title="Thoresby Colliery">Thoresby</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian41013_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian41013-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> There were 26 opencast sites in 2014, mainly in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> British coal mines achieve the most economically produced coal in Europe, with a level of productivity of 3,200 tonnes per man year.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  Most coal is used for electricity generation and steel-making, but its use to heat homes has decreased because of pollution concerns. The <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> is also used for <a href="/wiki/Fertiliser" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertiliser">fertilisers</a>, chemicals, <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmaceutical drug">medicines</a> and road surfaces. Hatfield Colliery closed in June 2015, as did Thoresby, and in December 2015, Kellingley, bringing to an end deep coal mining in the UK. On 20 December, thousands of people turned out for a march in Yorkshire to mark the occasion. The march began in Knottingley and finished with a rally and party at Kellingley Miners Welfare club.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The demand for coal is likely to fall with increasing focus on <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> or low-carbon sources and loss of industry due to globalisation. Oil and gas reserves are predicted to run out long before coal,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> so gas could be produced from coal by <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">gasification</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 21 April 2017, Britain went a full day without using coal power to generate electricity for the first time since the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, according to the <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_(Great_Britain)" title="National Grid (Great Britain)">National Grid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In April 2018, a new record was set of 55 hours without coal, which in turn was broken by a period of more than 72 hours without coal a few days later.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
