width="200" height="46" data-file-width="168" data-file-height="39" /></a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Goldsmiths, University of London,</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university">research university</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, England, specialising in the arts, design, humanities, and social sciences. It is a <a href="/wiki/Constituent_college" class="mw-redirect" title="Constituent college">constituent college</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>. It was founded in 1891 as <b>Goldsmiths\' Technical and Recreative Institute</b> by the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Goldsmiths" title="Worshipful Company of Goldsmiths">Worshipful Company of Goldsmiths</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Cross" title="New Cross">New Cross</a>, London. It was acquired by the University of London in 1904 and was renamed <b>Goldsmiths\' College</b>. The word <i>College</i> was dropped from its branding in 2006, but Goldsmiths\' College, with the <a href="/wiki/Apostrophe" title="Apostrophe">apostrophe</a>, remains the institution\'s formal legal name.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nearly 20% of students come from outside the UK, and 52% of all undergraduates are mature students (aged 21 or over at the start of their studies). one of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> <a href="/wiki/Livery_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Livery Companies">Livery Companies</a>, founded <i>Goldsmiths\' Technical and Recreative Institute</i> (more commonly referred to simply as the "Goldsmiths\' Institute"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>). The Goldsmiths\' Company was established in the 12th century as a medieval guild for goldsmiths, silversmiths and jewellers. The Livery Company dedicated the foundation of its new Institute to "the promotion of technical skill, knowledge, health and general well-being among men and women of the industrial, working and artisan classes". The original Institute was based in New Cross at the former <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_School" title="Royal Naval School">Royal Naval School</a> building. (This building, which was designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/John_Shaw_Jr" class="mw-redirect" title="John Shaw Jr">John Shaw Jr</a>, is now known as the <i>Richard Hoggart Building</i> and 1904, the Institute was merged with the University of London and was re-established as <b>Goldsmiths\' College</b>. (The apostrophe was removed in 1993, and the word \'College\' dropped in a rebranding in 2006). At this point Goldsmiths was the largest teacher training institution in the country. Training functions were later expanded to include refresher courses for teachers, the University Postgraduate Certificate in Education and an Art teacher\'s Certificate course. The College also ran its own Nursery School.\n</p><p>Shortly after the merger, in 1907, Goldsmiths added a new Arts building, designed by Sir <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Blomfield" title="Reginald Blomfield">Reginald Blomfield</a>, at the back of the main building. During the Second World War it was decided to evacuate the faculty and students of the College to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">University College, Nottingham</a>, a decision which proved wise both at the time and in hindsight, since the main building was struck by an <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Incendiary bomb">incendiary bomb</a> and gutted in 1940 (and not finally repaired until 1947).\n</p><p>During the 1960s Goldsmiths experienced a rapid expansion in student numbers. It is during this period that Goldsmiths began to establish its reputation in the arts and <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> fields, as well as offering a number of new teacher training qualifications. The original main building was expanded, and the Lockwood Building, Whitehead Building, Education Building, Warmington Tower and St James\'s Hall were all built to accommodate the influx of new students. The university also acquired a number of historic buildings in the surrounding area, including the splendid former <a href="/wiki/Deptford" title="Deptford">Deptford</a> <a href="/wiki/Town_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town Hall">Town Hall</a> and Laurie Grove Baths buildings. The Richard Hoggart Building, Deptford Town Hall and the Laurie Grove Baths all retain <a href="/wiki/Grade_II_listed" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade II listed">Grade II listed</a> building status.\n</p><p>In 1988, Goldsmiths became a full College of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> and in 1990 received its <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a>. Among its <a href="/wiki/Warden_(college)" title="Warden (college)">wardens</a> have been <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hoggart" title="Richard Hoggart">Richard Hoggart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Rutherford_(English_scholar)" title="Andrew Rutherford (English scholar)">Andrew Rutherford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Pimlott" title="Ben Pimlott">Ben Pimlott</a>. The current Warden is <a href="/wiki/Pat_Loughrey" title="Pat Loughrey">Pat Loughrey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2018, the former boiler house and public laundry of Laurie Grove Baths was refurbished and opened as <a href="/wiki/Goldsmiths_CCA" title="Goldsmiths CCA">Goldsmiths CCA</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_and_location">Campus and location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldsmiths,_University_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Campus and location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg/220px-Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg/330px-Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg/440px-Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1417" data-file-height="945" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Deptford_Town_Hall_Building.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Deptford Town Hall Building</div></div></div>\n<p>Goldsmiths is situated in <a href="/wiki/New_Cross" title="New Cross">New Cross</a>, a highly populated area of south-east London with a considerable art and music scene. The area is served by <a href="/wiki/London_Overground" title="London Overground">London Overground</a> trains at <a href="/wiki/New_Cross_railway_station" title="New Cross railway station">New Cross</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Cross_Gate_railway_station" title="New Cross Gate railway station">New Cross Gate</a>.\n</p><p>The main building, the <i>Richard Hoggart Building</i>, was originally designed as a school (opened in 1844) by the architect <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Shaw,_Jr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Shaw, Jr (page does not exist)">John Shaw, Jr</a> (1803\xe2\x80\x931870). The former Deptford Town Hall Building, designed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Vaughan_Lanchester" title="Henry Vaughan Lanchester">Henry Vaughan Lanchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Alfred_Rickards" title="Edwin Alfred Rickards">Edwin Alfred Rickards</a>, acquired in 1998, is used for academic seminars and conferences. In addition to this Goldsmiths has built several more modern buildings to develop the campus, including the <a href="/wiki/RIBA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIBA">RIBA</a> award-winning Rutherford Building completed in 1997, the <i>Ben Pimlott Building</i> designed by <a href="/wiki/Will_Alsop" title="Will Alsop">Will Alsop</a> and completed in 2005, and the <i>Professor Stuart Hall Building</i> (formerly the <i>New Academic Building)</i> which was completed in 2010.\n</p><p>The library, or the <i>Rutherford Building</i>, has three floors and gives students access to an extensive range of printed and electronic resources. The third-floor library is believed to house the largest collection of audio-visual material in the UK. Goldsmiths\' students, like all other students in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>, have full access to the collections at <a href="/wiki/Senate_House_(University_of_London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate House (University of London)">Senate House</a> Library at <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury" title="Bloomsbury">Bloomsbury</a> in central London.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg/220px-Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg/330px-Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg/440px-Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1417" data-file-height="945" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Goldsmiths_Pimlott_Building.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Ben_Pimlott" title="Ben Pimlott">Ben Pimlott</a> Building</div></div></div>\n<p>The seven-storey <i>Ben Pimlott Building</i> on New Cross Road, complete with its distinctive "scribble in the sky" (made from 229 separate pieces of metal) has become a signature of modern Goldsmiths. It contains studio and teaching space for the Department of Art, as well as housing the Goldsmiths Digital Studios<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and the Centre for Cognition, Computation and Culture.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Professor Stuart Hall Building</i> (formerly the <i>New Academic Building)</i>, situated next to the green, is home to the Media and Communications Department and the Institute for Creative and Cultural Entrepreneurship (ICCE).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Facilities include a 250-seat lecture theatre, seminar and teaching rooms, as well as a cafe with outdoor seating.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_profile">Academic profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldsmiths,_University_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Department of Art at Goldsmiths is one of the leading fine art teaching and practice-based research centres in the world. The Head of Department is Dr Richard Noble. Notable alumni include <a href="/wiki/Damien_Hirst" title="Damien Hirst">Damien Hirst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Lucas" title="Sarah Lucas">Sarah Lucas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_McQueen_(director)" title="Steve McQueen (director)">Steve McQueen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gillian_Wearing" title="Gillian Wearing">Gillian Wearing</a>, Sabina Goldsmith and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Coxon" title="Graham Coxon">Graham Coxon</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldsmiths,_University_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The Department of Design\'s approach to design practice grew from a concern for ethical and environmentalist design. This developed alongside research by <a href="/wiki/John_Wood_(design_theorist)" title="John Wood (design theorist)">John Wood</a>, and others, which informs their research into <a href="/wiki/Metadesign" title="Metadesign">metadesign</a>. TERU, the Technology Education Research Unit, has been instrumental in understanding how design and technology work in schools, how to encourage learners towards creative interventions that improve the made world, and how to help teachers to support that process. The Writing Purposefully in Art and Design Network (Writing-PAD) has its main Centre at Goldsmiths. The Network now spans some 70 institutions across the art and design sector with 6 national and 2 International Writing PAD Centres.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" In the area of Psychology there is <a href="/wiki/Chris_French" title="Chris French">Chris French</a> who specialises in the psychology of <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">paranormal</a> beliefs and experiences, cognition and emotion.<sup id="cite_ref-work_at_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-work_at-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saul_Newman" title="Saul Newman">Saul Newman</a> - notable for developing the concept of <a href="/wiki/Post-anarchism" title="Post-anarchism">post-anarchism</a> - is currently leading the department of politics.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="English_and_comparative_literature">English and comparative literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldsmiths,_University_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: English and comparative literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The English &amp; Comparative Literature Department is one of the university\'s largest and it covers English, comparative literature, American literature, creative writing and linguistics. The Union runs 18 sports clubs, 11 of which compete in either <a href="/wiki/University_of_London_Union" title="University of London Union">University of London Union</a> or <a href="/wiki/British_Universities_%26_Colleges_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="British Universities &amp; Colleges Sport">BUCS</a> leagues.\n</p><p>The Students\' Union runs 35 societies, ranging from political societies and identity-based societies (for example the Jewish society and the <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> society) to interest-based societies (the Drama Society and the on-campus radio station Wired) and more.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_media">Student media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldsmiths,_University_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Student media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><span id="The_Leopard"></span>\nGoldsmiths has a long history of student-led media platforms, including <i>Smiths Magazine</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Leopard</i> newspaper,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and Wired radio.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The student media is run independently by students at the college.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_housing">Student housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldsmiths,_University_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Student housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The university owns seven halls of residence:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Batavia Mews</li>\n<li>Chesterman House</li>\n<li>Dean House</li>\n<li>Loring Hall</li>\n<li>Surrey House</li>\n<li>Surrey House Annexe</li>\n<li>Raymont Hall</li></ul>\n<p>Several of the halls include electricity bills and gas bills as part of rent. Free internet is also offered in some of the halls. Visiting international students are generally assigned accommodation in Loring Hall, Dean House or Chesterman House. Loring Hall currently holds around 400 students.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Students.27_Union"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Students\'_Union">Students\' Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldsmiths,_University_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Students&#039; Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Goldsmiths_Students%27_Union" title="Goldsmiths Students&#39; Union">Goldsmiths Students\' Union</a></div>\n<p>The union provides, among other things, catering facilities, a <a href="/wiki/Chaplaincy" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaplaincy">chaplaincy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Medical_clinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical clinic">medical clinic</a>, an advice service on academic and welfare issues and a state of the art <a href="/wiki/Gym" title="Gym">gym</a> for students\' use.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2014, the union faced critical coverage, from student newspaper <a href="/wiki/The_Tab" title="The Tab">The Tab</a> after voting down a proposal to commemorate <a href="/wiki/Holocaust_Memorial_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust Memorial Day">Holocaust Memorial Day</a>, with Education Officer Sarah El-alfy describing it as "Eurocentric" and "colonialist."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The Tab failed to mention that it was their writer, for a couple of years a Goldsmiths student himself, who tabled the proposal in the first place. El-alfy offered to help put forward a redrafted version of the motion for the following Student Assembly meeting. The Union issued a statement claiming "Redrafting motions and re-entering them at a later date isn\xe2\x80\x99t unusual in Students\xe2\x80\x99 Unions and shouldn\xe2\x80\x99t be misinterpreted as opposition."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2015 the student union Welfare and Diversity Officer, <a href="/wiki/Bahar_Mustafa" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahar Mustafa">Bahar Mustafa</a>, was caught up in a media controversy initiated on imageboard website <a href="/wiki/4chan" title="4chan">4chan</a>, which was subsequently picked up by the Tab by the same writer as above and then UK tabloid the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a> for appearing to ban white people and men from a student union event, a tea drinking event specifically organised for <a href="/wiki/Black_and_Minority_Ethnic" class="mw-redirect" title="Black and Minority Ethnic">BME</a> female students.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Bahar Mustafa caused more public controversy through her justification of the ban, after being at the receiving end of a sustained online and telephone bullying wave which included rape and death threats as well as <a href="/wiki/Doxxing" class="mw-redirect" title="Doxxing">doxxing</a> of her and her family.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and through her use of the hash tag #KillAllWhiteMen. A group of students petitioned for a vote of no confidence in her, but the petition was signed by less than 3% of the student body and therefore failed to trigger a referendum.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
