class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Confiance_Kent_fight.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/East_Indiaman" title="East Indiaman">East Indiaman</a> <a href="/wiki/Kent_(1799_ship)" title="Kent (1799 ship)"><i>Kent</i></a> battling <a href="/wiki/Confiance_(1797_ship)" title="Confiance (1797 ship)"><i>Confiance</i></a>, a privateer vessel commanded by <a href="/wiki/French_corsairs" title="French corsairs">French corsair</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Surcouf" title="Robert Surcouf">Robert Surcouf</a> in October 1800, as depicted in a painting by <a href="/wiki/Ambroise_Louis_Garneray" title="Ambroise Louis Garneray">Ambroise Louis Garneray</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>privateer</b> is a private person or ship that engages in maritime warfare under a commission of war.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The commission, also known as a <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_marque" title="Letter of marque">letter of marque</a>, empowers the person to carry on all forms of hostility permissible at sea by the usages of war, including attacking foreign vessels during wartime and taking them as prizes. Historically, captured ships were subject to condemnation and sale under <a href="/wiki/Prize_(law)" title="Prize (law)">prize law</a>, with the proceeds divided between the privateer sponsors, shipowners, captains and crew. A percentage share usually went to the issuer of the commission. Since robbery under arms was once common to seaborne trade, all merchant ships were already armed. During war, naval resources were auxiliary to operations on land so privateering was a way of subsidizing state power by mobilizing armed ships and sailors.\n</p><p>In practice the legality and status of privateers historically has often been vague. Depending on the specific government and the time period, letters of marque might be issued hastily and/or the privateers might take actions beyond what was authorized by the letters. The privateers themselves were often simply <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirates</a> who would take advantage of wars between nations to gain semi-legal status for their enterprises. By the end of the 19th century the practice of issuing letters of marque had fallen out of favor because of the chaos it caused and its role in inadvertently encouraging piracy.\n</p><p>A privateer is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenary</a> except that, whereas a mercenary group receives a set fee for services and generally has a formal reporting structure within the entity that hires them, a privateer acts independently with generally no compensation unless the enemy\'s property is captured.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prize_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Prize law">Prize law</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_marque" title="Letter of marque">letter of marque</a> of a privateer would typically limit activity to one particular ship, and specified officers.  Typically, the owners or captain would be required to post a <a href="/wiki/Performance_bond" title="Performance bond">performance bond</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, letters of marque were revoked for various offences.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg/220px-Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg/330px-Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg/440px-Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1979" data-file-height="1269" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Triton-Hasard-stitched.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boarding of the <a href="/wiki/Triton_(1787_EIC_ship)" title="Triton (1787 EIC ship)"><i>Triton</i></a> (a British East Indiaman) by the French corsair <a href="/wiki/Cartier_(1787_ship)" title="Cartier (1787 ship)"><i>Hasard</i></a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Some crews were treated as harshly as naval crews of the time, while others followed the comparatively relaxed rules of merchant ships. Some crews were made up of professional merchant seamen, others of <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirates</a>, debtors, and <a href="/wiki/Convict" title="Convict">convicts</a>.  Some privateers ended up becoming pirates, not just in the eyes of their enemies but also of their own nations. <a href="/wiki/William_Kidd" title="William Kidd">William Kidd</a>, for instance, began as a legitimate British privateer but was later <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanged</a> for piracy.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Noted_privateers">Noted privateers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Noted privateers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Privateers who were considered legitimate by their governments include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a> (<a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pieter_van_der_Does" title="Pieter van der Does">Pieter van der Does</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_Empire" title="Dutch Empire">Dutch Empire</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Amaro_Pargo" class="mw-redirect" title="Amaro Pargo">Amaro Pargo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> or <i>Hispanic Monarchy</i>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa" title="Hayreddin Barbarossa">Hayreddin Barbarossa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Surcouf" title="Robert Surcouf">Robert Surcouf</a> (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lars_Gathenhielm" title="Lars Gathenhielm">Lars Gathenhielm</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ships">Ships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Entrepreneurs converted many different types of vessels into privateers, including obsolete warships and refitted merchant ships. The investors would arm the vessels and recruit large crews, much larger than a merchantman or a naval vessel would carry, in order to crew the prizes they captured.  Privateers generally cruised independently, but it was not unknown for them to form squadrons, or to co-operate with the regular navy. A number of privateers were part of the English fleet that opposed the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> in 1588. Privateers generally avoided encounters with warships, as such encounters would be at best unprofitable. Still, such encounters did occur. For instance, in 1815 <a href="/wiki/Chasseur_(1812_clipper)" title="Chasseur (1812 clipper)"><i>Chasseur</i></a> encountered <a href="/wiki/HMS_St_Lawrence_(1813)" title="HMS St Lawrence (1813)">HMS <i>St Lawrence</i></a>, herself a former American privateer, mistaking her for a merchantman until too late; in this instance, however, the privateer prevailed.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> used mixed <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(naval)" title="Squadron (naval)">squadrons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> and privateers in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. Following the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, French privateers became a menace to British and American shipping in the western Atlantic and the Caribbean, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Quasi-War" title="Quasi-War">Quasi-War</a>, a brief conflict between France and the United States, fought largely at sea, and to the Royal Navy\'s procuring <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_sloop" title="Bermuda sloop">Bermuda sloops</a> to combat the French privateers.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overall_history">Overall history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Overall history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb data-file-height="1188" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:16th_century_Portuguese_Spanish_trade_routes.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>16th-century trade routes prey to privateering: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_treasure_fleet" title="Spanish treasure fleet">Spanish treasure fleets</a> linking the Caribbean to <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India_Armadas" title="Portuguese India Armadas">Portuguese India Armadas</a> of 1498\xe2\x80\x931640 (blue)</div></div></div>\n<p>The practice dated to at least the 13th century but the word itself was coined sometime in the mid-17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, and later the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">United Kingdom</a>, used privateers to great effect and suffered much from other nations\' privateering. During the 15th century, "piracy became an increasing problem and merchant communities such as Bristol began to resort to self-help, arming and equipping ships at their own expense to protect commerce."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> These privately owned merchant ships, licensed by the crown, could legitimately take vessels that were deemed pirates. This constituted a "revolution in naval strategy" and helped fill the need for protection that the current administration was unable to provide as it "lacked an institutional structure and coordinated finance".<sup id="cite_ref-Loades,_D._M._2009._p._53_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loades,_D._M._2009._p._53-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The increase in competition for crews on armed merchant vessels and privateers was due, in a large part, because of the chance for a considerable payoff. "Whereas a seaman who shipped on a naval vessel was paid a wage and provided with victuals, the mariner on a merchantman or privateer was paid with an agreed share of the takings."<sup id="cite_ref-Loades,_D._M._2009._p._53_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loades,_D._M._2009._p._53-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This proved to be a far more attractive prospect and privateering flourished as a result.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth\'s</a> reign, she "encouraged the development of this supplementary navy".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Over the course of her rule, she had "allowed Anglo-Spanish relations to deteriorate" to the point where one could argue that a war with the Spanish was inevitable.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> By using privateers, if the Spanish were to take offense at the plundering of their ships, Queen Elizabeth could always deny she had anything to do with the actions of such independents. Some of the most famous privateers that later fought in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585%E2%80%931604)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585\xe2\x80\x931604)">Anglo-Spanish War (1585\xe2\x80\x931604)</a> included the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Dogs" title="Sea Dogs">Sea Dogs</a>.\n</p><p>In the late 16th century, English ships cruised in the Caribbean and off the coast of Spain, trying to intercept <a href="/wiki/Spanish_treasure_fleet" title="Spanish treasure fleet">treasure fleets</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Main" title="Spanish Main">Spanish Main</a>. At this early stage the idea of a regular navy (the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, as distinct from the <a href="/wiki/British_Merchant_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="British Merchant Navy">Merchant Navy</a>) was not present, so there is little to distinguish the activity of English privateers from regular naval warfare. Attacking Spanish ships, even during peacetime, was part of a policy of military and economic competition with Spain \xe2\x80\x93 which had been monopolizing the maritime <a href="/wiki/Trade_routes" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade routes">trade routes</a> along with the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> helping to provoke the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585)">first Anglo-Spanish War</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Capturing a Spanish treasure ship would enrich the Crown as well as strike a practical blow against Spanish domination of America. <a href="/wiki/Piet_Pieterszoon_Hein" title="Piet Pieterszoon Hein">Piet Pieterszoon Hein</a> was a brilliantly successful Dutch privateer who captured a Spanish treasure fleet. <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Heinason" title="Magnus Heinason">Magnus Heinason</a> was another privateer who served the Dutch against the Spanish. While their and others\' attacks brought home a great deal of money, they hardly dented the flow of gold and silver from Mexico to Spain.\n</p><p>Elizabeth was succeeded by the first Stuart monarchs, <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>, who did not permit privateering. There were a number of unilateral and bilateral declarations limiting privateering between 1785 and 1823. However, the breakthrough came in 1856 when the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Paris" class="mw-redirect" title="Declaration of Paris">Declaration of Paris</a>, signed by all major European powers, stated that "Privateering is and remains abolished". The US did not sign because a stronger amendment, protecting all private property from capture at sea, was not accepted. In the 19th century many nations passed laws forbidding their nationals from accepting commissions as privateers for other nations.  The last major power to flirt with privateering was <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> in the 1870 <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>, when Prussia announced the creation of a \'volunteer navy\' of ships privately owned and manned and eligible for prize money. The only difference between this and privateering was that these volunteer ships were under the discipline of the regular navy.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Privateer_Savannah.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Privateer_Savannah.jpg/220px-Privateer_Savannah.jpg" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Privateer_Savannah.jpg/330px-Privateer_Savannah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Privateer_Savannah.jpg/440px-Privateer_Savannah.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="516" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Privateer_Savannah.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CSS <i>Savannah</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Confederate_privateer" title="Confederate privateer">Confederate privateer</a>.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span id="17th.2C_18th_and_19th_centuries"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="17th,_18th_and_19th_centuries">17th, 18th and 19th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 17th, 18th and 19th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Privateers were a large part of the total military force at sea during the 17th and 18th centuries. In the <a href="/wiki/First_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="First Anglo-Dutch War">first Anglo-Dutch War</a>, English privateers attacked the trade on which the United Provinces entirely depended, capturing over 1,000 Dutch merchant ships. During the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1654)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1654)">war with Spain</a>, Spanish and Flemish privateers in the service of the Spanish Crown, including the notorious <a href="/wiki/Dunkirkers" title="Dunkirkers">Dunkirkers</a>, captured 1,500 English merchant ships, helping to restore Dutch international trade.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> British trade, whether coastal, Atlantic, or Mediterranean, was also attacked by Dutch privateers and others in the Second and Third Anglo-Dutch wars.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/King_George%27s_War" title="King George&#39;s War">King George\'s War</a>, approximately 36,000 Americans served aboard privateers at one time or another.<sup id="cite_ref-Privateer_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Privateer-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine Years War">Nine Years War</a>, the French adopted a policy of strongly encouraging privateers, including the famous <a href="/wiki/Jean_Bart" title="Jean Bart">Jean Bart</a>, to attack English and Dutch shipping. England lost roughly 4,000 merchant ships during the war.<sup id="cite_ref-Privateer_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Privateer-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the following <a href="/wiki/War_of_Spanish_Succession" class="mw-redirect" title="War of Spanish Succession">War of Spanish Succession</a>, privateer attacks continued, Britain losing 3,250 merchant ships.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Parliament passed an updated Cruisers and Convoys Act in 1708 allocating regular warships to the defence of trade.\n</p><p>In the subsequent conflict, the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Austrian_Succession" class="mw-redirect" title="War of Austrian Succession">War of Austrian Succession</a>, the Royal Navy was able to concentrate more on defending British ships. Britain lost 3,238 merchantmen, a smaller fraction of her merchant marine than the enemy losses of 3,434.<sup id="cite_ref-Privateer_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Privateer-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> While French losses were proportionally severe, the smaller but better protected Spanish trade suffered the least and it was Spanish privateers who enjoyed much of the best allied plunder of British trade, particularly in the West Indies.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> privateering took on several forms, including <a href="/wiki/Blockade_runners_of_the_American_Civil_War" title="Blockade runners of the American Civil War">blockade running</a> while privateering in general occurred in the interests of both the North and the South. <a href="/wiki/Letters_of_marque" class="mw-redirect" title="Letters of marque">Letters of marque</a> would often be issued to private shipping companies and other private owners of ships, authorizing them to engage vessels deemed to be unfriendly to the issuing government. Crews of ships were awarded the cargo and other prizes aboard any captured vessel as an incentive to search far and wide for ships attempting to supply the Confederacy, or aid the Union, as the case may be.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Britain">Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg/220px-Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg/330px-Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg/440px-Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2262" data-file-height="1533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Privateer_Captain_Woodes_Rogers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Woodes_Rogers" title="Woodes Rogers">Woodes Rogers</a>\' men search Spanish ladies for their jewels in <a href="/wiki/Guayaquil" title="Guayaquil">Guayaquil</a>, 1709</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a> practised privateering both separately and together after they <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">united</a> to create the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> in 1707. It was a way to gain for themselves some of the wealth the Spanish and Portuguese were taking from the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> before beginning their own trans-Atlantic settlement, and a way to assert naval power before a strong <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> emerged.\n</p><p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Barton_(privateer)" title="Andrew Barton (privateer)">Andrew Barton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Admiral_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord High Admiral of Scotland">Lord High Admiral of Scotland</a>, followed the example of his father, who had been issued with letters of marque by <a href="/wiki/James_III_of_Scotland" title="James III of Scotland">James III of Scotland</a> to prey upon English and Portuguese shipping in 1485; the letters in due course were reissued to the son. Barton was killed following an encounter with the English in 1511.\n</p><p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a>, who had close contact with the sovereign, was responsible for some damage to Spanish shipping, as well as attacks on Spanish settlements in the Americas in the 16th century. He participated in the successful English defence against the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> in 1588, though he was also partly responsible for the failure of the <a href="/wiki/English_Armada" title="English Armada">English Armada</a> against Spain in 1589.\n</p><p>Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Clifford,_3rd_Earl_of_Cumberland" title="George Clifford, 3rd Earl of Cumberland">George Clifford, 3rd Earl of Cumberland</a>, was a successful privateer against Spanish shipping in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. He is also famous for his <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Juan_(1598)" title="Battle of San Juan (1598)">short-lived 1598 capture</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Fort_San_Felipe_del_Morro" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort San Felipe del Morro">Fort San Felipe del Morro</a></i>, the citadel protecting <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>. He arrived in Puerto Rico on June 15, 1598, but by November of that year Clifford and his men had fled the island due to fierce civilian resistance. He gained sufficient prestige from his naval exploits to be named the official <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Champion" title="Queen&#39;s Champion">Champion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a>. Clifford became extremely wealthy through his buccaneering, but lost most of his money gambling on horse races.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg/220px-Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg/330px-Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg/440px-Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1642" data-file-height="1264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Willem_van_de_Velde_de_Jonge_-_Een_actie_van_een_Engels_schip_en_schepen_van_de_Barbarijse_zeerovers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An action between an English ship and vessels of the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_corsairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary corsairs">Barbary corsairs</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Captain <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Newport" title="Christopher Newport">Christopher Newport</a> led more attacks on Spanish shipping and settlements than any other English privateer. As a young man, Newport sailed with Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a> in the attack on the Spanish fleet at Cadiz and participated in England\xe2\x80\x99s defeat of the Spanish Armada.  During the war with Spain, Newport seized fortunes of Spanish and Portuguese treasure in fierce sea battles in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> as a privateer for Queen Elizabeth I. He lost an arm whilst capturing a Spanish ship during <a href="/wiki/Watts%27_West_Indies_and_Virginia_expedition" title="Watts&#39; West Indies and Virginia expedition">an expedition</a> in 1590, but despite this he continued on privateering, successfully <a href="/wiki/Blockade_of_Western_Cuba" title="Blockade of Western Cuba">blockading Western Cuba</a> the following year. In 1592, Newport captured the Portuguese carrack <i><a href="/wiki/Madre_de_Deus" title="Madre de Deus">Madre de Deus</a></i> (Mother of God), valued at \xc2\xa3500,000.\n</p><p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morgan" title="Henry Morgan">Henry Morgan</a> was a successful privateer. Operating out of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, he carried on a war against Spanish interests in the region, often using cunning tactics. His operation was prone to cruelty against those he captured, including torture to gain information about booty, and in one case using priests as <a href="/wiki/Human_shield" title="Human shield">human shields</a>. Despite reproaches for some of his excesses, he was generally protected by Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Modyford" title="Thomas Modyford">Thomas Modyford</a>, the governor of Jamaica. He took an enormous amount of booty, as well as landing his privateers ashore and attacking land fortifications, including the sack of the city of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> with only 1,400 crew<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>.\n</p><p>Other British privateers of note include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortunatus_Wright&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fortunatus Wright (page does not exist)">Fortunatus Wright</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Collier_(buccaneer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Collier (buccaneer)">Edward Collier</a>, Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Hawkins_(naval_commander)" title="John Hawkins (naval commander)">John Hawkins</a>, his son Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hawkins" title="Richard Hawkins">Richard Hawkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Geare" title="Michael Geare">Michael Geare</a>, and Sir <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Myngs" title="Christopher Myngs">Christopher Myngs</a>.  Notable British colonial privateers in <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> include <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Godfrey" title="Alexander Godfrey">Alexander Godfrey</a> of the brig <i><a href="/wiki/Rover_(privateering_ship)" title="Rover (privateering ship)">Rover</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Barss" title="Joseph Barss">Joseph Barss</a> of the schooner <i><a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Packet" title="Liverpool Packet">Liverpool Packet</a></i>. The latter schooner captured over 50 American vessels during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bermudians">Bermudians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bermudians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The English colony of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> (or the <i>Somers Isles</i>), settled accidentally in 1609, was used as a base for English privateers from the time it officially became part of the territory of the <a href="/wiki/London_Company" title="London Company">Virginia Company</a> in 1612, especially by ships belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Warwick" title="Earl of Warwick">Earl of Warwick</a>, for whom Bermuda\'s <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Parish,_Bermuda" class="mw-redirect" title="Warwick Parish, Bermuda">Warwick Parish</a> is named (the Warwick name had long been associated with commerce raiding, as exampled by the <a href="/wiki/Newport_Ship" title="Newport Ship">Newport Ship</a>, thought to have been taken from the Spanish by <i>Warwick the Kingmaker</i> in the 15th Century). Many Bermudians were employed as crew aboard privateers throughout the century, although the colony was primarily devoted to farming cash crops until turning from its failed agricultural economy to the sea after the 1684 dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/Somers_Isles_Company" title="Somers Isles Company">Somers Isles Company</a> (a spin-off of the Virginia Company which had overseen the colony since 1615). With a total area of 54 square kilometres (21&#160;sq&#160;mi) and lacking any natural resources other than the <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_cedar" class="mw-redirect" title="Bermuda cedar">Bermuda cedar</a>, the colonists applied themselves fully to the maritime trades, developing the speedy <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_sloop" title="Bermuda sloop">Bermuda sloop</a>, which was well suited both to commerce and to commerce raiding. Bermudian merchant vessels turned to privateering at every opportunity in the 18th century, preying on the shipping of Spain, France, and other nations during a series of wars, including: the 1688 to 1697 <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War" title="Nine Years&#39; War">Nine Years\' War</a> (<a href="/wiki/King_William%27s_War" title="King William&#39;s War">King William\'s War</a>); the 1702 to 1713 <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_War" title="Queen Anne&#39;s War">Queen Anne\'s War</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> the 1739 to 1748 <a href="/wiki/War_of_Jenkins%27_Ear" title="War of Jenkins&#39; Ear">War of Jenkins\' Ear</a>; the 1740 to 1748 <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession" title="War of the Austrian Succession">War of the Austrian Succession</a> (<a href="/wiki/King_George%27s_War" title="King George&#39;s War">King George\'s War</a>); the 1754 to 1763 <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years&#39; War">Seven Years\' War</a> (known in the United States as the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>, this conflict was devastating for the colony\'s merchant fleet. Fifteen privateers operated from Bermuda during the war, but losses exceeded captures); the 1775 to 1783 <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a>; and the 1796 to 1808 <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1796%E2%80%931808)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1796\xe2\x80\x931808)">Anglo-Spanish War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> By the middle of the 18th century, Bermuda was sending twice as many privateers to sea as any of the continental colonies. They typically left Bermuda with very large crews. This advantage in manpower was vital in overpowering the crews of larger vessels, which themselves often lacked sufficient crewmembers to put up a strong defence. The extra crewmen were also useful as <a href="/wiki/Prize_crew" title="Prize crew">prize crews</a> for returning captured vessels.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, which had been depopulated of its indigenous inhabitants by the Spanish, had been settled by England, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Eleutheran_Adventurers" title="Eleutheran Adventurers">Eleutheran Adventurers</a>, dissident <a href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">Puritans</a> driven out of Bermuda during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. Spanish and French attacks destroyed <a href="/wiki/New_Providence" title="New Providence">New Providence</a> in 1703, creating a stronghold for pirates, and it became a thorn in the side of British merchant trade through the area. In 1718, Britain appointed <a href="/wiki/Woodes_Rogers" title="Woodes Rogers">Woodes Rogers</a> as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_the_Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of the Bahamas">Governor of the Bahamas</a>, and sent him at the head of a force to reclaim the settlement. Before his arrival, however, the pirates had been forced to surrender by a force of Bermudian privateers who had been issued letters of marque by the Governor of Bermuda.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e0/Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg/220px-Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg" width="220" height="312" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e0/Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg/330px-Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e0/Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg/440px-Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg 2x" data-file-width="576" data-file-height="817" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bermuda_Gazette_-_12_November_1796.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bermuda Gazette of 12 November 1796, calling for privateering against Spain and its allies during the 1796 to 1808 <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1796%E2%80%931808)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1796\xe2\x80\x931808)">Anglo-Spanish War</a>, and with advertisements for crew for two privateer vessels.</div></div></div>\n<p>Bermuda was in de facto control of the <a href="/wiki/Turks_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Turks Islands">Turks Islands</a>, with their lucrative salt industry, from the late 17th century to the early 19th. The Bahamas made perpetual attempts to claim the Turks for itself. On several occasions, this involved seizing the vessels of Bermudian salt traders. A virtual state of war was said to exist between Bermudian and Bahamian vessels for much of the 18th century. When the Bermudian sloop <i>Seaflower</i> was seized by the Bahamians in 1701, the response of the Governor of Bermuda, Captain Benjamin Bennett, was to issue letters of marque to Bermudian vessels. In 1706, Spanish and French forces ousted the Bermudians, but were driven out themselves three years later by the Bermudian privateer Captain <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lewis_Middleton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lewis Middleton (page does not exist)">Lewis Middleton</a>. His ship, the <i>Rose</i>, attacked a Spanish and a French privateer holding a captive English vessel. Defeating the two enemy vessels, the <i>Rose</i> then cleared out the thirty-man garrison left by the Spanish and French.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite strong sentiments in support of the rebels, especially in the early stages, Bermudian privateers turned as aggressively on American shipping during the <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a>. The importance of privateering to the Bermudian economy had been increased not only by the loss of most of Bermuda\'s continental trade, but also by the <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Palliser#Governor_of_Newfoundland" title="Hugh Palliser">Palliser Act</a>, which forbade Bermudian vessels from fishing the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Banks" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Banks">Grand Banks</a>. Bermudian trade with the rebellious American colonies actually carried on throughout the war. Some historians credit the large number of Bermuda sloops (reckoned at over a thousand) built in Bermuda as privateers and sold illegally to the Americans as enabling the rebellious colonies to win their independence.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Also, the Americans were dependent on Turks salt, and one hundred barrels of gunpowder were stolen from a Bermudian magazine and supplied to the rebels as orchestrated by Colonel <a href="/wiki/Henry_Tucker_(of_The_Grove)" title="Henry Tucker (of The Grove)">Henry Tucker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>, and as requested by <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, in exchange for which the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> authorised the sale of supplies to Bermuda, which was dependent on American produce. The realities of this interdependence did nothing to dampen the enthusiasm with which Bermudian privateers turned on their erstwhile countrymen.\n</p><p>An American naval captain, ordered to take his ship out of <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harbor" title="Boston Harbor">Boston Harbor</a> to eliminate a pair of Bermudian privateering vessels that had been picking off vessels missed by the Royal Navy, returned frustrated, saying, "the Bermudians sailed their ships two feet for every one of ours".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Around 10,000 Bermudians emigrated in the years prior to American independence, mostly to the American colonies. Many Bermudians occupied prominent positions in American seaports, from where they continued their maritime trades (Bermudian merchants controlled much of the trade through ports like <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, and Bermudian shipbuilders influenced the development of American vessels, like the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Clipper" title="Baltimore Clipper">Chesapeake Bay schooner</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and in the Revolution they used their knowledge of Bermudians and of Bermuda, as well as their vessels, for the rebels\' cause. In the 1777 Battle of Wreck Hill, brothers Charles and Francis Morgan, members of a large Bermudian enclave that had dominated Charleston, South Carolina and its environs since settlement,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> captaining two sloops (the <i>Fair American</i> and the <i>Experiment</i>, respectively), carried out the only attack on Bermuda during the war. The target was a fort that guarded a little used passage through the encompassing reefline. After the soldiers manning the fort were forced to abandon it, they spiked its guns and fled themselves before reinforcements could arrive.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ea/Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg/220px-Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ea/Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg/330px-Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ea/Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg/440px-Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="774" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bermuda_sloop_-_privateer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_sloop" title="Bermuda sloop">Bermuda sloop</a> engaged as a privateer.</div></div></div>\n<p>When the Americans captured the Bermudian privateer <i>Regulator</i>, they discovered that virtually all of her crew were black slaves. Authorities in Boston offered these men their freedom, but all 70 elected to be treated as <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoner of War">prisoners of war</a>. Sent as such to New York on the sloop <i>Duxbury</i>, they seized the vessel and sailed it back to Bermuda.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> saw an encore of Bermudian privateering, which had died out after the 1790s. The decline of Bermudian privateering was due partly to the buildup of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Dockyard,_Bermuda" title="Royal Naval Dockyard, Bermuda">naval base in Bermuda</a>, which reduced the Admiralty\'s reliance on privateers in the western Atlantic, and partly to successful American legal suits and claims for damages pressed against British privateers, a large portion of which were aimed squarely at the Bermudians.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> During the course of the War of 1812, Bermudian privateers captured 298 ships, some 19% of the 1,593 vessels captured by British naval and privateering vessels between the Great Lakes and the West Indies.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Amongst the better known (native-born and immigrant) Bermudian privateers were <a href="/wiki/Hezekiah_Frith" title="Hezekiah Frith">Hezekiah Frith</a>, Bridger Goodrich,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jennings" title="Henry Jennings">Henry Jennings</a>, Thomas Hewetson,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Tew" title="Thomas Tew">Thomas Tew</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Providence_Island_colony">Providence Island colony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Providence Island colony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Bermudians were also involved in privateering from the short-lived English colony on <a href="/wiki/Providence_Island_colony#Origin_of_the_colony" title="Providence Island colony">Isla de Providencia</a>, off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. This colony was initially settled largely via Bermuda, with about eighty Bermudians moved to Providence in 1631. Although it was intended that the colony be used to grow cash crops, its location in the heart of the Spanish controlled territory ensured that it quickly became a base for privateering.\n</p><p>Bermuda-based privateer <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Elfrith" title="Daniel Elfrith">Daniel Elfrith</a>, while on a privateering expedition with Captain <a href="/wiki/Sussex_Camock" title="Sussex Camock">Sussex Camock</a> of the bark <i>Somer Ilands</i> (a rendering of "<i>Somers Isles</i>", the alternate name of the Islands of Bermuda) in 1625, discovered two islands off the coast of Nicaragua, 80 kilometres (50&#160;mi) apart from each other. Camock stayed with 30 of his men to explore one of the islands, San Andr\xc3\xa9s, while Elfrith took the Warwicke back to Bermuda bringing news of Providence Island. Bermuda Governor Bell wrote on behalf of Elfrith to Sir Nathaniel Rich, a businessman and cousin of the Earl of Warwick (the namesake of <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Parish,_Bermuda" class="mw-redirect" title="Warwick Parish, Bermuda">Warwick Parish</a>), who presented a proposal for colonizing the island noting its strategic location "lying in the heart of the Indies &amp; the mouth of the Spaniards". Elfrith was appointed admiral of the colony\'s military forces in 1631, remaining the overall military commander for over seven years. During this time, Elfrith served as a guide to other privateers and sea captains arriving in the Caribbean. Elfrith invited the well-known privateer Diego el Mulato to the island. Samuel Axe, one of the military leaders, also accepted letters of marque from the Dutch authorizing privateering.\n</p><p>The Spanish did not hear of the Providence Island colony until 1635, when they captured some Englishmen in <a href="/wiki/Portobelo,_Col%C3%B3n" title="Portobelo, Col\xc3\xb3n">Portobelo</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a>. <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Murga" title="Francisco de Murga">Francisco de Murga</a>, Governor and Captain General of <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Colombia" title="Cartagena, Colombia">Cartagena</a>, dispatched Captain <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gregorio_de_Castellar_y_Mantilla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gregorio de Castellar y Mantilla (page does not exist)">Gregorio de Castellar y Mantilla</a> and engineer Juan de Somovilla Texada to destroy the colony. <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOffen2011_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOffen2011-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The Spanish were repelled and forced to retreat "in haste and disorder".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200985\xe2\x80\x9386_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200985\xe2\x80\x9386-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> After the attack, King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a> issued <a href="/wiki/Letters_of_marque" class="mw-redirect" title="Letters of marque">letters of marque</a> to the Providence Island Company on 21 December 1635 authorizing raids on the Spanish in retaliation for a raid that had destroyed the English colony on <a href="/wiki/Tortuga_(Haiti)" title="Tortuga (Haiti)">Tortuga</a> earlier in 1635 (<a href="/wiki/Tortuga_(Haiti)" title="Tortuga (Haiti)">Tortuga</a> had come under the protection of the Providence Island Company. In 1635 a Spanish fleet raided Tortuga. 195 colonists were hung and 39 prisoners and 30 slaves were captured). The company could in turn issue letters of marque to subcontracting privateers who used the island as a base, for a fee.  This soon became an important source of profit. Thus the Company made an agreement with the merchant Maurice Thompson under which Thompson could use the island as a base in return for 20% of the booty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200986_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200986-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 1636 the Company dispatched Captain <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hunt_(governor)" title="Robert Hunt (governor)">Robert Hunt</a> on the <i>Blessing</i> to assume the governorship of what was now viewed as a base for privateering.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200983_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200983-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Depredations continued, leading to growing tension between England and Spain, which were still technically at peace.\n</p><p>On 11 July 1640 the Spanish Ambassador in London complained again, saying he\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>understands that there is lately brought in at the Isle of Wight by one, Captain James Reskinner &#91;<a href="/wiki/James_Reiskimmer" title="James Reiskimmer">James Reiskimmer</a>], a ship very richly laden with silver, gold, diamonds, pearls, jewels, and many other precious commodities taken by him in virtue of a commission of the said Earl [of Warwick] from the subjects of his Catholic Majesty ... to the infinite wrong and dishonour of his Catholic Majesty, to find himself thus injured and violated, and his subjects thus spoiled, robbed, impoverished and murdered in the highest time of peace, league and amity with your Majesty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200989\xe2\x80\x9390_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200989\xe2\x80\x9390-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Butler" title="Nathaniel Butler">Nathaniel Butler</a>, formerly Governor of Bermuda, was the last full governor of Providence Island, replacing Robert Hunt in 1638. Butler returned to England in 1640, satisfied that the fortifications were adequate, deputizing the governorship to Captain Andrew Carter.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKupperman1993242\xe2\x80\x9343_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKupperman1993242\xe2\x80\x9343-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1640, don <a href="/wiki/Melchor_de_Aguilera" title="Melchor de Aguilera">Melchor de Aguilera</a>, Governor and Captain-General of Cartagena, resolved to remove the intolerable infestation of pirates on the island. Taking advantage of having infantry from Castile and Portugal wintering in his port, he dispatched six hundred armed Spaniards from the fleet and the presidio, and two hundred black and mulatto militiamen under the leadership of don <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Maldonado_y_Tejada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Antonio Maldonado y Tejada (page does not exist)">Antonio Maldonado y Tejada</a>, his Sergeant Major, in six small frigates and a galleon.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConquista_de_la_Isla_de_Santa_Catalina_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConquista_de_la_Isla_de_Santa_Catalina-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The troops were landed on the island, and a fierce fight ensued. The Spanish were forced to withdraw when a gale blew up and threatened their ships. Carter had the Spanish prisoners executed. When the Puritan leaders protested against this brutality, Carter sent four of them home in chains.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamshere197248\xe2\x80\x9349_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamshere197248\xe2\x80\x9349-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_capture_of_Providencia" title="Spanish capture of Providencia">Spanish capture of Providencia</a></div>\n<p>The Spanish acted decisively to avenge their defeat. General <a href="/wiki/Francisco_D%C3%ADaz_Pimienta" title="Francisco D\xc3\xadaz Pimienta">Francisco D\xc3\xadaz Pimienta</a> was given orders by King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV of Spain</a>, and sailed from Cartagena to Providence with seven large ships, four <a href="/wiki/Pinnace_(ship%27s_boat)" title="Pinnace (ship&#39;s boat)">pinnaces</a>, 1,400 soldiers and 600 seamen, arriving on 19 May 1641. At first Pimienta planned to attack the poorly defended east side, and the English rushed there to improvise defenses. With the winds against him, Pimienta changed plans and made for the main New Westminster harbor and launched his attack on 24 May. He held back his large ships to avoid damage, and used the pinnaces to attack the forts. The Spanish troops quickly gained control, and once the forts saw the Spanish flag flying over the governor\'s house, they began negotiations for surrender.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200990_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200990-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 25 May 1641, Pimienta formally took possession and celebrated mass in the church.\nThe Spanish took sixty guns, and captured the 350 settlers who remained on the island \xe2\x80\x93 others had escaped to the Mosquito coast.  They took the prisoners to Cartagena.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200990\xe2\x80\x9391_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200990\xe2\x80\x9391-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\nThe women and children were given a passage back to England.\nThe Spanish found gold, indigo, cochineal and six hundred black slaves on the island, worth a total of 500,000 ducats, some of the accumulated booty from the English raids.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamshere197249_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamshere197249-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\nRather than destroy the defenses, as instructed, Pimienta left a small garrison of 150 men to hold the island and prevent occupation by the Dutch.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200990-91_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200990-91-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> \nLater that year, Captain <a href="/wiki/John_Humphrey_(Massachusetts)" title="John Humphrey (Massachusetts)">John Humphrey</a>, who had been chosen to succeed Captain Butler as governor, arrived with a large group of dissatisfied settlers from New England. He found the Spanish in occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKupperman1993250_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKupperman1993250-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\nPimienta\'s decision to occupy the island was approved in 1643 and he was made a knight of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Santiago" title="Order of Santiago">Order of Santiago</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELatimer200990\xe2\x80\x9391_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELatimer200990\xe2\x80\x9391-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain_and_its_colonies">Spain and its colonies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="854" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amaro_Pargo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Spanish <a href="/wiki/Amaro_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Felipe" title="Amaro Rodr\xc3\xadguez Felipe">Amaro Pargo</a> was one of the most famous corsairs of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Piracy" title="Golden Age of Piracy">Golden Age of Piracy</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Miguel_Enriquez.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" Enr\xc3\xadquez</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>When Spain issued a decree blocking foreign countries from trading, selling or buying merchandise in its Caribbean colonies, the entire region became engulfed in a power struggle among the naval superpowers.<sup id="cite_ref-banderas_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-banderas-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The newly independent United States later became involved in this scenario, complicating the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-banderas_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-banderas-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> As a consequence, Spain increased the issuing of privateering contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-banderas_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-banderas-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> These contracts allowed an income option to the inhabitants of these colonies that were not related to the Spanish conquistadores. The most well-known privateer corsairs of the eighteenth century in the Spanish colonies were <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Enr%C3%ADquez_(privateer)" title="Miguel Enr\xc3\xadquez (privateer)">Miguel Enr\xc3\xadquez</a> of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campuzano_Polanco_family#Jose_Campuzano_Polanco_(1689-1760)" title="Campuzano Polanco family">Jos\xc3\xa9 Campuzano-Polanco</a> of <a href="/wiki/Captaincy_General_of_Santo_Domingo" title="Captaincy General of Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Miguel Enr\xc3\xadquez was a Puerto Rican <a href="/wiki/Mulatto" title="Mulatto">mulatto</a> who abandoned his work as a shoemaker to work as a privateer. Such was the success of Enr\xc3\xadquez, that he became one of the wealthiest men in the New World. His fleet was composed of approx. 300 different ships during a career that expanded 35 years, becoming a military asset and reportedly outperforming the efficiency of the <a href="/wiki/Armada_de_Barlovento" title="Armada de Barlovento">Armada de Barlovento</a>. Enr\xc3\xadquez was knighted and received the title of <a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)" title="Don (honorific)">Don</a> from <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Spain" title="Philip V of Spain">Philip V</a>, something unheard of due to his ethnic and social background. One of the most famous privateers from mainland Spain was <a href="/wiki/Amaro_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Felipe" title="Amaro Rodr\xc3\xadguez Felipe">Amaro Pargo</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Privateer_prize_auction.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Privateer_prize_auction.jpg/170px-Privateer_prize_auction.jpg" width="170" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Privateer_prize_auction.jpg/255px-Privateer_prize_auction.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Privateer_prize_auction.jpg/340px-Privateer_prize_auction.jpg 2x" data-file-width="481" data-file-height="624" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Privateer_prize_auction.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Advertising for the auction of the prize <i>Chelmers of London</i>, brig captured by the French privateer <i>Junon</i> in 1810.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/French_corsairs" title="French corsairs">French corsairs</a></div>\n<p><i>Corsairs</i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: corsaire) were privateers, authorized to conduct raids on shipping of a nation at war with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, on behalf of the French Crown. Seized vessels and cargo were sold at auction, with the corsair captain entitled to a portion of the proceeds. Although not <a href="/wiki/French_Navy" title="French Navy">French Navy</a> personnel, corsairs were considered legitimate combatants in France (and allied nations), provided the commanding officer of the vessel was in possession of a valid <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_Marque" class="mw-redirect" title="Letter of Marque">Letter of Marque</a> (fr. <i>Lettre de Marque</i> or <i>Lettre de Course</i>), and the officers and crew conducted themselves according to contemporary <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_law" title="Admiralty law">admiralty law</a>. By acting on behalf of the French Crown, if captured by the enemy, they could claim treatment as <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a>, instead of being considered <a href="/wiki/Pirates" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirates">pirates</a>. Because corsairs gained a <a href="/wiki/Swashbuckling" class="mw-redirect" title="Swashbuckling">swashbuckling</a> reputation, the word "corsair" is also used generically as a more romantic or flamboyant way of referring to privateers, or even to pirates. The <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary pirate">Barbary pirates</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottomans</a> were sometimes called "Turkish corsairs".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malta">Malta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Malta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Corsairing (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">corso</i>) was an important aspect of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>\'s economy when the island was <a href="/wiki/History_of_Malta_under_the_Order_of_Saint_John" title="History of Malta under the Order of Saint John">ruled</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Order of St. John</a>, although the practice had begun earlier. Corsairs sailed on privately owned ships on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Grand_Masters_of_the_Sovereign_Military_Order_of_Malta" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Grand Masters of the Sovereign Military Order of Malta">Grand Master</a> of the Order, and were authorized to attack Muslim ships, usually merchant ships from the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. The corsairs included knights of the Order, native Maltese people, as well as foreigners. When they captured a ship, the goods were sold and the crew and passengers were ransomed or enslaved, and the Order took a percentage of the value of the booty.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Corsairing remained common until the end of the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PrideofBaltimore1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/PrideofBaltimore1.jpg/220px-PrideofBaltimore1.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/PrideofBaltimore1.jpg/330px-PrideofBaltimore1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/PrideofBaltimore1.jpg/440px-PrideofBaltimore1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1486" data-file-height="1183" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PrideofBaltimore1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pride_of_Baltimore" title="Pride of Baltimore"><i>Pride of Baltimore II</i></a>, replica United States topsail <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a>, favoured by privateers for its speed and ability to <a href="/wiki/Points_of_sail" class="mw-redirect" title="Points of sail">sail close to the wind</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a>, and some state governments (on their own initiative), issued privateering licenses, authorizing "legal piracy", to merchant captains in an effort to take prizes from the British Navy and <a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory</a> (Loyalist) privateers. This was done due to the relatively small number of commissioned American naval vessels and the pressing need for <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_exchange" title="Prisoner exchange">prisoner exchange</a>.\n</p><p>About 55,000 American seamen served aboard the privateers.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> They quickly sold their prizes, dividing their profits with the financier (persons or company) and the state (colony). <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Sound" title="Long Island Sound">Long Island Sound</a> became a hornets\' nest of privateering activity during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> (1775\xe2\x80\x931783), as most transports to and from New York went through the Sound. <a href="/wiki/New_London,_Connecticut" title="New London, Connecticut">New London, Connecticut</a> was a chief privateering port for the American colonies, leading to the British Navy blockading it in 1778\xe2\x80\x931779. Chief financiers of privateering included Thomas &amp; Nathaniel Shaw of New London and John McCurdy of <a href="/wiki/Lyme,_Connecticut" title="Lyme, Connecticut">Lyme</a>. In the months before the British raid on New London and Groton, a New London privateer took <i>Hannah</i> in what is regarded as the largest prize taken by any American privateer during the war. Retribution was likely part of Gov. Clinton\'s (NY) motivation for <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold%27s_Raid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arnold&#39;s Raid (page does not exist)">Arnold\'s Raid</a>, as the <i>Hannah</i> had carried many of his most cherished items.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg/300px-BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg" width="300" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg/450px-BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg/600px-BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BriggObserveregagingtheJack29May1782HalifaxPublRDodd1Sept1784BerleyRobisonCollectionUSNavalAcademy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Naval_battle_off_Halifax" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval battle off Halifax">Naval battle off Halifax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a></div></div></div>\n<p>American privateers are thought to have seized up to 300 British ships during the war. The British ship <i>Jack</i> was <a href="/wiki/Naval_battle_off_Cape_Breton" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval battle off Cape Breton">captured</a> and turned into an American privateer, only to be captured again by the British in the <a href="/wiki/Naval_battle_off_Halifax" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval battle off Halifax">naval battle off Halifax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. American privateers not only fought naval battles but also raided numerous communities in British colonies, such as the <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Lunenburg,_Nova_Scotia_(1782)" title="Raid on Lunenburg, Nova Scotia (1782)">Raid on Lunenburg, Nova Scotia (1782)</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a> authorized the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Congress of the United States">U.S. Congress</a> to grant letters of marque and reprisal. Between the end of the Revolutionary War and 1812, less than 30 years, Britain, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary States">Barbary States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> seized approximately 2,500 American ships.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Payments in ransom and tribute to the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbary states">Barbary states</a> amounted to 20% of United States government annual revenues in 1800<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> and would lead the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> to fight the Barbary states in the <a href="/wiki/First_Barbary_War" title="First Barbary War">First Barbary War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Barbary_War" title="Second Barbary War">Second Barbary Wars</a>.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, the British <a href="/wiki/Essex,_Connecticut#The_Great_Attack" title="Essex, Connecticut">attacked Essex, Connecticut, and burned the ships in the harbor</a>, due to the construction there of a number of privateers. This was the greatest financial loss of the entire War of 1812 suffered by the Americans. However, the private fleet of <a href="/wiki/James_De_Wolf" class="mw-redirect" title="James De Wolf">James De Wolf</a>, which sailed under the flag of the American government in 1812, was most likely a key factor in the naval campaign of the war. De Wolf\'s ship, the <i>Yankee</i>, was possibly the most financially successful ship of the war. Privateers proved to be far more successful than their US Navy counterparts, claiming three quarters of the 1600 British merchant ships taken during the war (although a third of these were recaptured prior to making landfall). One of the more successful of these ships was the <i><a href="/wiki/Prince_de_Neufchatel" title="Prince de Neufchatel">Prince de Neufchatel</a></i>, which once captured nine British prizes in swift succession in the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Whilst apparently successful the privateer campaign was ultimately a failure. British convoy systems honed during the Napoleonic Wars limited losses to singleton ships, and the effective blockade of American and continental ports prevented captured ships being taken in for sale. This ultimately led to orders forbidding US privateers from attempting to bring their prizes in to port, with captured ships instead having to be burnt. Over 200 American privateer ships were captured by the Royal Navy, many of which were turned on their former owners and used by the British blockading forces.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jean_Lafitte" title="Jean Lafitte">Jean Lafitte</a> and his privateers aided US General <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a> in the defeat of the British in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_New_Orleans" title="Battle of New Orleans">Battle of New Orleans</a> in order to receive full pardons for their previous crimes.<sup id="cite_ref-ramsay62_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay62-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ramsay69_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay69-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ramsay70_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay70-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ramsay71_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay71-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ramsay77_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay77-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Jackson formally requested clemency for Lafitte and the men who had served under him, and the US government granted them all a full pardon on February 6, 1815.<sup id="cite_ref-ramsay82_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay82-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:156px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JDe_Wolf.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/JDe_Wolf.jpg" width="154" height="225" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="154" data-file-height="225" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JDe_Wolf.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/James_De_Wolf" class="mw-redirect" title="James De Wolf">James De Wolf</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The US was not one of the initial signatories of the 1856 <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Paris" class="mw-redirect" title="Declaration of Paris">Declaration of Paris</a> which outlawed privateering, and the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_Constitution" title="Confederate States Constitution">Confederate Constitution</a> authorized use of privateers. However, the US did offer to adopt the terms of the Declaration during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederates</a> sent several <a href="/wiki/Confederate_privateer" title="Confederate privateer">privateers</a> to sea before putting their main effort in the more effective commissioned raiders.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> Confederate President <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Davis" title="Jefferson Davis">Jefferson Davis</a> issued letters of marque to anyone who would employ their ship to either attack Union shipping or bring badly needed supplies through the Union blockade into southern ports. Many of the supplies brought into the Confederacy were carried aboard privately owned vessels. When word came about that the Confederacy was willing to pay almost any price for military supplies, various interested parties designed and built specially designed light weight seagoing steamers, <a href="/wiki/Blockade_runners_of_the_American_Civil_War" title="Blockade runners of the American Civil War">blockade runners</a> specifically designed and built to outrun Union ships on <a href="/wiki/Union_blockade" title="Union blockade">blockade</a> patrol.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>No letter of marque has been legitimately issued by the United States since the 19th century.  The status of submarine hunting Goodyear airships in the early days of the second world war has created significant confusion.  According to one story, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_Marque" class="mw-redirect" title="Letter of Marque">Letter of Marque</a> to the Airship <a href="/wiki/Airship#World_War_II" title="Airship"><i>Resolute</i></a> on the West Coast of the United States at the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, making it the first time the US Navy commissioned a privateer since the War of 1812.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>  However, this story, along with various other accounts referring to the airships <i>Resolute</i> and <i>Volunteer</i> as operating under a "privateer status", is highly dubious.  Since neither the Congress nor the President appears to have authorized a privateer during the war, the Navy would not have had the authority to do so by itself.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Privateer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Insurgent_Privateer" class="mw-redirect" title="Insurgent Privateer">Insurgent Privateer</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:014Corsario.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/014Corsario.jpg/220px-014Corsario.jpg" width="220" height="259" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/014Corsario.jpg/330px-014Corsario.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/014Corsario.jpg/440px-014Corsario.jpg 2x" data-file-width="591" data-file-height="696" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:014Corsario.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Corsario" (Privateer) by Mexican artist <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_Garc%C3%ADa_Vega" title="Mauricio Garc\xc3\xada Vega">Mauricio Garc\xc3\xada Vega</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Warships were recruited by the insurgent governments during <a href="/wiki/Spanish_American_wars_of_independence" title="Spanish American wars of independence">Spanish American wars of independence</a> to destroy Spanish trade, and capture Spanish Merchant vessels. The private armed vessels came largely from the United States. Seamen from Britain, United States and France often manned these ships.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
