class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Bagehot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Bagehot was born in <a href="/wiki/Langport" title="Langport">Langport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, on 3 February 1826. His father, Thomas Watson Bagehot, was managing director and vice-chairman of <a href="/wiki/Stuckey%27s_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Stuckey&#39;s Bank">Stuckey\'s Bank</a>. He attended <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a> (UCL), where he studied mathematics, and in 1848 earned a master\'s degree in moral philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Bagehot was <a href="/wiki/Call_to_the_Bar" class="mw-redirect" title="Call to the Bar">called to the bar</a> by <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Inn" title="Lincoln&#39;s Inn">Lincoln\'s Inn</a>, but preferred to join his father in 1852 in his family\'s shipping and banking business.\n</p><p>In 1858, Bagehot married Elizabeth (Eliza) Wilson (1832\xe2\x80\x931921), whose father, <a href="/wiki/James_Wilson_(UK_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Wilson (UK politician)">James Wilson</a>, was the founder and owner of  <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>; the couple were happily married until Bagehot\'s untimely death at age 51, but had no children.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  A collection of their love-letters was published in 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalism">Journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Bagehot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1855, Bagehot founded the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review_(1855)" title="National Review (1855)">National Review</a></i> with his friend <a href="/wiki/Richard_Holt_Hutton" title="Richard Holt Hutton">Richard Holt Hutton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1861, he became editor-in-chief of <i>The Economist</i>. In the 17 years he served as its editor, Bagehot expanded <i>The Economist\'</i>s reporting on politics and increased its influence among policymakers.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/The_English_Constitution" title="The English Constitution">The English Constitution</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-English_Constitution_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_Constitution-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> a book that explores the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">monarchy</a>. It appeared at the same time that Parliament enacted the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_of_1867" class="mw-redirect" title="Reform Act of 1867">Reform Act of 1867</a>, requiring Bagehot to write an extended introduction to the second edition which appeared in 1872.\n</p><p>Bagehot also wrote <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physics_and_Politics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Physics and Politics (page does not exist)">Physics and Politics</a></i> (1872),<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> in which he examines how civilisations sustain themselves, arguing that in their earliest phase civilisations are very much in opposition to the values of modern liberalism, insofar as they are sustained by conformism and military success, but once they are secured it is possible for them to mature into systems which allow for greater diversity and freedom.\n</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Lombard_Street:_A_Description_of_the_Money_Market" title="Lombard Street: A Description of the Money Market">Lombard Street: A Description of the Money Market</a></i> (1873) Bagehot seeks to explain the world of finance and banking.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> His observations on finance are often cited by <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bankers</a>, most recently in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932010" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007\xe2\x80\x932010">global financial crisis</a> which began in 2007. Of particular importance is "<a href="/wiki/Lombard_Street:_A_Description_of_the_Money_Market#Lender_of_last_resort" title="Lombard Street: A Description of the Money Market">Bagehot\'s Dictum</a>" that in times of financial crisis central banks should lend freely to solvent <a href="/wiki/Depository_institutions" class="mw-redirect" title="Depository institutions">depository institutions</a>,
