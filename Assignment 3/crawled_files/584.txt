class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Malawian English</b> is the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> as spoken in <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>.  English is the country\'s <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a>.\n</p><p>English was introduced into Malawi towards the end of the 19th century, due to the influence of British explorers, missionaries, the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/African_Lakes_Corporation" title="African Lakes Corporation">African Lakes Corporation</a>, and colonial administrators present since the establishment in the 1890s of the <a href="/wiki/British_Central_Africa_Protectorate" title="British Central Africa Protectorate">British Central Africa Protectorate</a>.  The seventy years of <a href="/wiki/New_Imperialism#Britain_and_the_New_Imperialism" title="New Imperialism">British colonial rule</a> that followed the <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">Scramble for Africa</a>, set the groundwork for English to grow into the area\'s dominant and most socially prestigious language.\n</p><p>Since Malawian independence, the dominance of English has continued:\n</p>\n<ul><li>official government records are written in English,</li>\n<li>parliament conducts <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Malawi" title="Politics of Malawi">its deliberations</a> in English,</li>\n<li>the laws of Malawi are written in English,</li>\n<li>progression into <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> requires certification of competence in English,</li>\n<li>nearly all Malawian newspapers are published in English (though some include small Chichewa supplements),</li>\n<li>English remains the language of commerce in the country.</li></ul>\n<p>This remains true despite a large majority of Malawians speaking Chichewa and the small number of English speakers outside urban centres. Also, in Malawian government schools, students are taught in Chichewa, and learn English as a second language from about age 10. But in international schools in Malawi (like Saint Andrew\'s International High School in <a href="/wiki/Blantyre,_Malawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Blantyre, Malawi">Blantyre</a>) which follow the British curriculum, English is the language students are taught in, and do not learn Chichewa at all, as it is regarded as a local language.\n</p><p>English words are even replacing their equivalents in other Malawi languages.  One study of a <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">corpus</a> of Chichewa discourse captured over a ten-year period found that references to numbers greater than 3 were exclusively in English, at least in urban areas.\n<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Malawian English has a slight tinge of non-linguistic expressions that are still used, such as "eesh!", an exclamation meaning "oh my!"\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malawian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation journal">Simango, Silvester Ron (2000). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20080910035724/http://www.multilingual-matters.net/jmmd/021/0487/jmmd0210487.pdf">"<span class="cs1-kern-left">\'</span>My Madam is Fine\': The Adaptation of English Loans in Chichewa"</a> <span class="cs1-format">(PDF)</span>. <i>Journal of Multilingual and Multicultural Development</i>. <b>21</b> (6): 487\xe2\x80\x93507. <a href="/wiki/Digital_object_identifier" title="Digital object identifier">doi</a>:<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//doi.org/10.1080%2F01434630008666419">10.1080/01434630008666419</a>.
