Some 1.7 million workers went out, especially in transport and heavy industry. The government was prepared and enlisted middle class volunteers to maintain essential services.  There was little violence and the TUC gave up in defeat. Though nine days in, the TUC leadership knew \'the government could hold out longer than the workers\', it was perceived at the time as a \'brilliant failure\'. According to a leading <a href="/wiki/Trades_Union_Congress" title="Trades Union Congress">TUC</a> researcher, Walter Milne-Bailey, \'There has never been a more amazing display of labour solidarity and the effect of such a demonstration must inevitably be deep and enduring. Workers have learnt a new sense of their oneness and their power.\'<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1929" title="United Kingdom general election, 1929">1929 general election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> won more seats than any other party in Parliament for the first time in its history.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" href="/wiki/File:The_Subsidised_Mineowner.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Subsidised Mineowner\xe2\x80\x94Poor Beggar!</i> from <i>Trade Union Unity Magazine</i> (1925)</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>: The heavy domestic use of coal in the war meant that rich seams were depleted. Britain exported less coal in the war than it would have done in peacetime, allowing other countries to fill the gap. The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and their strong coal industries benefited, in particular.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Coal production was at its lowest ebb. Annual output per man had fallen to just 199 tonnes in 1920\xe2\x80\x931924, from 247 tonnes in the four years before the war, and a peak of 310 tons in the early 1880s.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Total coal output had been falling since 1914.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The fall in coal prices resulting from the 1924 <a href="/wiki/Dawes_Plan" title="Dawes Plan">Dawes Plan</a>. It allowed Germany to re-enter the international coal market by exporting "free coal" to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, as part of their reparations for the First World War.</li>\n<li>The reintroduction of the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a> in 1925 by <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, which made the British pound too strong for effective exporting to take place from Britain and (because of the economic processes involved in maintaining a strong currency) raised <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rates</a>, hurting some businesses.</li>\n<li>Mine owners wanted to maintain profits even during times of economic instability, which often took the form of wage reductions for miners in their employ. Coupled with the prospect of longer <a href="/wiki/Working_time" title="Working time">working hours</a>, the industry was thrown into disarray.</li>\n<li>Miners\' pay had lowered from \xc2\xa36.00 to \xc2\xa33.90 over seven years.</li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0c/Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg/220px-Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0c/Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg/330px-Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0c/Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg/440px-Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg 2x" data-file-width="776" data-file-height="593" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Special_Committee_of_the_General_Council_of_the_Trades_Union_Congress.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Special Committee of the General Council of the Trades Union Congress at Downing Street, ready to discuss the mining crisis with Baldwin</div></div></div>\n<p>Mine owners announced that their intention was to reduce miners\' wages. The <a href="/wiki/Miners_Federation_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Miners Federation of Great Britain">Miners Federation of Great Britain</a> rejected the terms: "Not a penny off the pay, not a minute on the day." The <a href="/wiki/Trades_Union_Congress" title="Trades Union Congress">Trades Union Congress</a> responded to the news by promising to support the miners in their dispute. The <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative government</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a>, decided to intervene by declaring that a nine-month subsidy would be provided to maintain the miners\' wages and that a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Commission">Royal Commission</a>, under the chairmanship of <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Samuel,_1st_Viscount_Samuel" title="Herbert Samuel, 1st Viscount Samuel">Sir Herbert Samuel</a>, would look into the problems of the mining industry and consider its impact on other industries, families and organisations dependent on coal supplies industry.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Commission" title="Samuel Commission">Samuel Commission</a> published a report on 10 March 1926 recommending that national agreements, the <a href="/wiki/Nationalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalisation">nationalisation</a> of royalties and sweeping reorganisation and improvement should be considered for the mining industry.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It also recommended a reduction by 13.5% of miners\' wages, along with the withdrawal of the government subsidy.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Two weeks later, the prime minister announced that the government would accept the report if other parties also did.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A previous royal commission, the <a href="/wiki/Sankey_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Sankey Commission">Sankey Commission</a>, had recommended nationalisation a few years earlier to deal with the problems of productivity and profitability in the industry, but Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">Lloyd George</a> rejected the report.\n</p><p>After the Samuel Commission\'s report, the mine owners declared that miners would be offered new terms of employment, which included lengthening the work day and reducing wages depending on various factors. The Miners\' Federation of Great Britain refused the wage reduction and regional negotiation.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="General_strike.2C_May_1926"></span><span class="mw-headline" data-file-height="283" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Foraging_for_coal_in_the_1926_General_Strike.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Foraging for <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> during the strike.</div></div></div>\n<p>The final negotiations began on 1 May but failed to achieve an agreement, leading to an announcement by the TUC that a <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> "in defence of miners\' wages and hours" was to begin on 3 May,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> a Monday, at one minute to midnight.<sup id="cite_ref-General_Strike_BBC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-General_Strike_BBC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The leaders of the <a href="/wiki/British_Labour_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="British Labour Party">British Labour Party</a> were unhappy about the proposed general strike because they were aware of the revolutionary elements within the union movement and of the damage that the association would do to the party\'s new reputation as a party of government. During the next two days, frantic efforts were made to reach an agreement between the government and the mining industry representatives. However, they failed, mainly because <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> of an eleventh-hour decision by printers of the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> to refuse to print an editorial ("For King and Country") condemning the general strike. They objected to the following passage: "A general strike is not an industrial dispute. It is a revolutionary move which can only succeed by destroying the government and subverting the rights and liberties of the people".\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Genral_Strike_1926.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Genral_Strike_1926.jpg/400px-Genral_Strike_1926.jpg" width="400" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Genral_Strike_1926.jpg/600px-Genral_Strike_1926.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Genral_Strike_1926.jpg/800px-Genral_Strike_1926.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3726" data-file-height="2338" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Genral_Strike_1926.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Troops on guard at a bus station; each bus had a police escort during the strike</div></div></div>\n<p>Baldwin was now concerned about the TUC and printers\' action interfering with the freedom of the press.\n</p><p>King <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">George V</a> tried to stabilise the situation and create balance saying, "Try living on their wages before you judge them."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The TUC feared that an all-out general strike would bring revolutionary elements to the fore and limited the participants to <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Railwaymen" title="National Union of Railwaymen">railwaymen</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Transport_Workers%27_Federation" title="National Transport Workers&#39; Federation">transport workers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Printer_(publisher)" class="mw-redirect" title="Printer (publisher)">printers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transport_and_General_Workers%27_Union" title="Transport and General Workers&#39; Union">dockers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ironworker" title="Ironworker">ironworkers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steelworker" class="mw-redirect" title="Steelworker">steelworkers</a>, as they were regarded as pivotal in the dispute.\n</p><p>The government had been preparing for the strike over the nine months in which it had provided a subsidy by creating organisations such as the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_the_Maintenance_of_Supplies" title="Organisation for the Maintenance of Supplies">Organisation for the Maintenance of Supplies</a>, and it did whatever it could to keep the country moving. It rallied support by emphasizing the revolutionary nature of the strikers. The armed forces and volunteer workers helped maintain basic services. It used the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Powers_Act_1920" title="Emergency Powers Act 1920">Emergency Powers Act 1920</a> to maintain essential supplies.\n</p><p>On 4 May 1926, the number of strikers was about 1.5\xe2\x80\x931.75 million. There were strikers "<a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End_to_John_o%27_Groats" title="Land&#39;s End to John o&#39; Groats">from John o\' Groats to Land\'s End</a>". The\' reaction to the strike call was immediate and overwhelming and surprised both the government and the TUC; the latter not being in control of the strike. On this first day, there were no major initiatives and no dramatic events except for the nation\'s transport being at a standstill.\n</p>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:22em;&#32;; background-color: #c6dbf7;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9c ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9d ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">\xe2\x80\x9cConstitutional Government is being attacked. Let all good citizens whose livelihood and labour have thus been put in peril bear with fortitude and patience the hardships with which they have been so suddenly confronted. Stand behind the Government, who are doing their part, confident that you will cooperate in the measures they have undertaken to preserve the liberties and privileges of the people of these islands. The laws of England are the people\'s birthright. The laws are in your keeping. You have made Parliament their guardian. The General Strike is a challenge to Parliament and is the road to anarchy and ruin.\xe2\x80\x9d</div>\n<p><cite class="right-aligned" style=""><a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a>, 6 May 1926, <i><a href="/wiki/British_Gazette" title="British Gazette">British Gazette</a></i></cite>\n</p>\n</div>\n<p>On 5 May 1926, both sides gave their views. Churchill (at that time <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>) commented as editor of the government newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/British_Gazette" title="British Gazette">British Gazette</a></i>: "I do not agree that the TUC have as much right as the Government to publish their side of the case and to exhort their followers to continue action. It is a very much more difficult task to feed the nation than it is to wreck it". Baldwin wrote, "The general strike is a challenge to the parliament and is the road to <a href="/wiki/Anarchy" title="Anarchy">anarchy</a>". The <i><a href="/wiki/British_Worker" title="British Worker">British Worker</a></i>, the TUC\'s newspaper, wrote: "We are not making war on the people. We are anxious that the ordinary members of the public shall not be penalized for the unpatriotic conduct of the mine owners and the government".\n</p><p>In the meantime, the government put in place a "<a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Special_constable" title="Special constable">special constables</a> called the Organisation for the Maintenance of Supplies (OMS) of volunteers to maintain order in the street. A special constable said: "It was not difficult to understand the strikers\' attitude toward us. After a few days I found my sympathy with them rather than with the employers. For one thing, I had never realized the appalling poverty which existed. If I had been aware of all the facts, I should not have joined up as a special constable".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It was decided that <a href="/wiki/Fascists" class="mw-redirect" title="Fascists">Fascists</a> would not be allowed to enlist in the OMS without first giving up their political beliefs, as the government feared a right-wing backlash so the fascists formed the so-called "Q Division" under <a href="/wiki/Rotha_Lintorn-Orman" title="Rotha Lintorn-Orman">Rotha Lintorn-Orman</a> to combat the strikers.\n</p><p>On 6 May 1926, there was a change of atmosphere. The government newspaper, <i><a href="/wiki/British_Gazette" title="British Gazette">British Gazette</a></i>, suggested that means of transport began to improve with volunteers and <a href="/wiki/Strikebreakers" class="mw-redirect" title="Strikebreakers">strikebreakers</a>, stating on the front page that there were \'200 buses on the streets\'.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> They were, however, figures of propaganda, as there were in fact only 86 buses running.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 7 May 1926, the TUC met with Samuel and worked out a set of proposals designed to end the dispute. The Miners\' Federation rejected the proposals. The <i>British Worker</i> was increasingly difficult to operate, as Churchill had requisitioned the bulk of the supply of the paper\'s newsprint so it reduced its size from eight pages to four.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In the meantime, the government took action to protect the men who decided to return to work.\n</p><p>On 8 May 1926, there was a dramatic moment on the London Docks. Lorries were protected by the army. They broke the picket line and transported food to Hyde Park. That showed that the government was in greater control of the situation. It was also a measure of Baldwin\'s rationalism, in place of Churchill\'s more reactionary stance. Churchill had wanted, in a move that could have proved unnecessarily antagonistic to the strikers, to arm the soldiers. Baldwin, however, had insisted otherwise.\n</p><p>On 11 May 1926, the <i><a href="/wiki/Flying_Scotsman_(train)" title="Flying Scotsman (train)">Flying Scotsman</a></i> was derailed by strikers near <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <i>British Worker</i>, alarmed at the fears of the General Council of the TUC that there was to be a mass drift back to work, claimed: "The number of strikers has not diminished; it is increasing. There are more workers out today than there have been at any moment since the strike began".\n</p><p>However, the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Seamen#The_National_Sailors&#39;_and_Firemen&#39;s_Union,_1894-1926" title="National Union of Seamen">National Sailors\' and Firemen\'s Union</a> applied for an injunction in the <a href="/wiki/Chancery_Division_of_the_High_Court_of_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancery Division of the High Court of Justice">Chancery Division of the High Court</a> to enjoin the General-Secretary of its <a href="/wiki/Tower_Hill" title="Tower Hill">Tower Hill</a> branch from calling its members out on strike. Mr <a href="/wiki/John_Meir_Astbury" title="John Meir Astbury">Justice Astbury</a> granted the injunction by ruling that no trade dispute could exist between the TUC and "the government of the nation"<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and that except for the strike in the coal industry, the general strike was not protected by <a href="/wiki/Trade_Disputes_Act_1906" title="Trade Disputes Act 1906">Trade Disputes Act 1906</a>. In addition, he ruled that the strike in the plaintiff union had been called in contravention of its own rules.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> As a result, the unions involved became liable, by common law, for incitement to breach of contract and faced potential <a href="/wiki/Sequestration_(law)" title="Sequestration (law)">sequestration</a> of their assets by employers.\n</p><p>On 12 May 1926, the TUC General Council visited <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a> to announce its decision to call off the strike if the proposals worked out by the Samuel Commission were respected and the government offered a guarantee there would be no victimization of strikers. The government stated that it had "no power to compel employers to take back every man who had been on strike". However, the TUC agreed to end the dispute without such an agreement.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_United_Kingdom_general_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The miners maintained resistance for a few months before being forced, by their own economic needs, to return to the mines. By the end of November, most miners were back at work. However, many remained unemployed for many years. Those still employed were forced to accept longer hours, lower wages and district wage agreements. The strikers felt that they had achieved nothing.\n</p><p>The effect on British coal mines was profound. By the late 1930s, employment in mining had fallen by more than a third from its pre-strike peak of 1.2 million miners, but productivity had rebounded from under 200 tons produced per miner, to over 300 tons by the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The split in the miners that resulted from <a href="/wiki/George_Alfred_Spencer" class="mw-redirect" title="George Alfred Spencer">Spencerism</a> and the agreement of the Nottinghamshire miners to return to work, against the policy of the Miners\' Federation of Great Britain divided the coal miners as a national bargaining force until the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Mineworkers_(Great_Britain)" title="National Union of Mineworkers (Great Britain)">National Union of Mineworkers</a>.\n</p><p>In some mining areas, strikebreakers were ostracised in their communities for the rest of their lives, and some were still being referred to as <i>scabs</i> at the time of the <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1984-85)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1984-85)">1984-5 strike</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (December 2017)">self-published source</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Trade_Disputes_and_Trade_Unions_Act_1927" title="Trade Disputes and Trade Unions Act 1927">Trade Disputes and Trade Unions Act 1927</a> banned <a href="/wiki/Sympathy_strikes" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympathy strikes">sympathy strikes</a> and mass <a href="/wiki/Picketing_(protest)" class="mw-redirect" title="Picketing (protest)">picketing</a>, and created a system whereby trade union members had to \'opt-in\' to paying the political levy to the Labour Party.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the long run, there was little impact on trade union activity or industrial relations. The TUC and the trade union movement remained intact and did not change their basic policies. Keith Laybourn says that historians mostly agree that "In no significant way could the General Strike be considered a turning point or watershed in British industrial history."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  It was the only general strike in British history, as union leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Bevin" title="Ernest Bevin">Ernest Bevin</a>, who had coordinated the strike, considered it a mistake. They decided that action by political parties was a better solution.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  However, the country came close to a one-day general strike on 31 July 1972. over the imprisonment of the <a href="/wiki/Pentonville_Five" title="Pentonville Five">Pentonville Five</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1926_United_Kingdom_general_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>The poet <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a> composed an ultimately pessimistic lyrical response to the general strike, which he incorporated into his long <a href="/wiki/Modernist" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernist">modernist</a> poem of the same year, "<a href="/wiki/A_Drunk_Man_Looks_at_the_Thistle" title="A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle">A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle</a>". (line 1119).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Harold_Heslop" title="Harold Heslop">Harold Heslop\'s</a> 1929 novel <i>The Gate of a Strange Field</i> is set during the Strike, and described the events from the viewpoint of striking miners.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ellen_Wilkinson" title="Ellen Wilkinson">Ellen Wilkinson\'s</a> 1929 novel <i>Clash</i> focuses on a woman activist\'s involvement with the Strike.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The strike functions as the "endpiece" of the satirical novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Apes_of_God" title="The Apes of God">The Apes of God</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Wyndham_Lewis" title="Wyndham Lewis">Wyndham Lewis</a>.
