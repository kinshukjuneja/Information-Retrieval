href="http://www.assembly.wales">www.assembly.wales</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>National Assembly for Wales</b> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Cynulliad Cenedlaethol Cymru</i>; commonly known as the <b>Welsh Assembly</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly" title="National Assembly">parliament</a> with power to make legislation and vary taxes in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. The Assembly comprises 60 members, who are known as <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Member of the National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Members</a>, or AMs (<i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Aelodau y Cynulliad</i>). Since 2011, Members are elected for five-year terms under an <a href="/wiki/Mixed_member_proportional_representation" class="mw-redirect" title="Mixed member proportional representation">additional members system</a>, in which 40 AMs represent geographical <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_constituencies_and_electoral_regions" title="National Assembly for Wales constituencies and electoral regions">constituencies</a> elected by the <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">plurality</a> system, and 20 AMs represent <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_constituencies_and_electoral_regions" title="National Assembly for Wales constituencies and electoral regions">five electoral regions</a> using the <a href="/wiki/D%27Hondt_method" title="D&#39;Hondt method">d\'Hondt method</a> of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>. Typically the largest party in the Assembly forms the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a>.\n</p><p>The Assembly was created by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a>, which followed a <a href="/wiki/Wales_referendum_1997" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales referendum 1997">referendum</a> in 1997. The Assembly had no powers to initiate <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a> until limited law-making powers were gained through the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a>. Its primary law-making powers were enhanced following a Yes vote in the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_2011" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 2011">referendum</a> on 3 March 2011, making it possible for it to <a href="/wiki/Act_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Act of the National Assembly for Wales">legislate</a> without having to consult the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">UK parliament</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a> in the 20 areas that are devolved.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It has been proposed by the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_Commission" title="Assembly Commission">Assembly Commission</a> that the name of the Assembly should change from the National Assembly for Wales to the <b>Welsh Parliament</b>, (<i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Senedd Cymru</i>), with the name change set to take place in 2020.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit Assembly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An appointed <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Wales_and_Monmouthshire" title="Council for Wales and Monmouthshire">Council for Wales and Monmouthshire</a> was established in 1949 to "ensure the government is adequately informed of the impact of government activities on the general life of the people of Wales". The council had 27 members nominated by local authorities in Wales, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wales" title="University of Wales">University of Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Eisteddfod_of_Wales" title="National Eisteddfod of Wales">National Eisteddfod Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Tourist_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Tourist Board">Welsh Tourist Board</a>. A post of Minister of Welsh Affairs was created in 1951 and the post of <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> were established in 1964 leading to the abolition of the Council for Wales.\nThe establishment of the Welsh Office effectively created the basis for the territorial governance of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Royal Commission on the Constitution</a> (the Kilbrandon Commission) was set up in 1969 by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>\'s Labour Government to investigate the possibility of devolution for Scotland and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-devolution_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devolution-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Its recommendations formed the basis of the 1974 <a href="/wiki/White_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="White Paper">White Paper</a> <i>Democracy and Devolution: proposals for Scotland and Wales</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-devolution_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devolution-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> which proposed the creation of a Welsh Assembly. href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997 general election</a>, the new Labour Government argued that an Assembly would be more democratically accountable than the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a>. For eleven years prior to 1997 Wales had been represented in the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cabinet of the United Kingdom">Cabinet of the United Kingdom</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State</a> who did not represent a Welsh constituency at <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\nA <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_1997" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 1997">second referendum</a> was held on 18 September 1997 in which voters approved the creation of the National Assembly for Wales with a total of 559,419 votes, or 50.3% of the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The following year the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act</a> was passed by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom parliament">United Kingdom parliament</a>, establishing the Assembly.\n</p><p>In July 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a> established an <a href="/wiki/Richard_Commission" title="Richard Commission">independent commission</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Richard,_Baron_Richard" title="Ivor Richard, Baron Richard">Lord Richard</a> (former leader of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>) as chair, to review the powers and electoral arrangements of the National Assembly to ensure that it is able to operate in the best interests of the people of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-richardspage_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richardspage-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Richard Commission reported in March 2004. It recommended that the National Assembly should have powers to legislate in certain areas, whilst others would remain the preserve of Westminster.<sup id="cite_ref-richardspage_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richardspage-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It also recommended changing the electoral system to the <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a> (STV) which would produce greater <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportionality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-richardspage_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richardspage-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In response, the British government, in its <i>Better Governance for Wales</i> White Paper, published on 15 June 2005, proposed a more permissive law-making system for the Welsh Assembly based on the use of Parliamentary Orders in Council.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In so doing, the Government rejected many of the cross party Richard Commission\'s recommendations. This has attracted criticism from opposition parties and others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_powers:_The_Government_of_Wales_Act_2006">Enhanced powers: The Government of Wales Act 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Enhanced powers: The Government of Wales Act 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> received <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> on 25 July 2006. It conferred on the Assembly legislative powers similar to other <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> legislatures through the ability to pass <a href="/wiki/Measure_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Measure of the National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Measures</a> concerning matters that are devolved. Requests for further legislative powers made through <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Competence_Order" title="Legislative Competence Order">legislative competence</a> requests were subject to the veto of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> or <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.\n</p><p>The Act reformed the assembly to a parliamentary-type structure, establishing the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a> as an entity separate from, but accountable to the National Assembly. It enables the Assembly to legislate within its devolved fields.\n</p><p>The Act also reforms the Assembly\'s electoral system. It prevents individuals from standing as candidates in both constituency and regional seats. This aspect of the act was subject to a great deal of criticism, most notably from the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Commission_(United_Kingdom)" title="Electoral Commission (United Kingdom)">Electoral Commission</a>.\n</p><p>The Act was heavily criticised. <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>, the Official Opposition in the National Assembly from 1999\xe2\x80\x932007, attacked it for not delivering a fully-fledged parliament. Many commentators have also criticised the Labour Party\'s allegedly partisan attempt to alter the electoral system. By preventing regional Assembly Members from standing in constituency seats the party has been accused of changing the rules to protect constituency representatives. Labour had 29 members in the Assembly at the time, all of whom held constituency seats.\n</p><p>The changes to the Assembly\'s powers were commenced on 4 May 2007, after the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Election,_2007" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly Election, 2007">election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_2011" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 2011">referendum on 3 March 2011</a>, the Welsh Assembly gained direct law making powers, without the need to consult Westminster.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reserved_powers_model:_The_Wales_Act_2017">Reserved powers model: The Wales Act 2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reserved powers model: The Wales Act 2017">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron\xe2\x80\x93Clegg coalition">Conservative-Liberal coalition government</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Devolution_in_Wales" title="Commission on Devolution in Wales">Commission on Devolution in Wales</a> (also known as Silk Commission), composed of 4 members of the Welsh Assembly and several leading legal and political experts, to "create a lasting devolution settlement for Wales". Following the first set of recommendations by the Commission, the UK government announced in November 2013 that some borrowing powers are to be devolved to the Assembly along with control of landfill tax and stamp duty. Additionally the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Act_2014" title="Wales Act 2014">Wales Act 2014</a> provides for a referendum to be held on the Assembly\'s ability to set a degree of <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> though there is a proposal for the requirement for a referendum to be removed.\n</p><p>Both the UK and Welsh governments supports the Silk Commission (Part 2) proposal to move to a "reserved powers" model of devolution (similar to that of the Scottish Parliament and Northern Ireland Assembly) where the UK government would have specific "reserved" powers and the Welsh Assembly would have control of all other matters.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This would replace the current model where certain powers are "conferred" and all others are assumed to be powers of the UK national government.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Wales_Act_2017" title="Wales Act 2017">Wales Act 2017</a>, based on the second set of recommendations of the Silk Commission, proposed devolving further areas of government, including some relating to water, marine affairs (ports, harbours, conservation), energy (subsidies, petroleum extraction, construction of smaller energy-generating facilities, etc.), rail franchising and road travel.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Following a public consultation, under powers granted by the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Act_2017" title="Wales Act 2017">Wales Act 2017</a>, the Assembly is expected to rename itself the Welsh Parliament, (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Senedd Cymru</i>), prior to the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Election,_2021" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly Election, 2021">next Welsh Assembly Election</a> in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It is expected that members of the renamed body will be known as Welsh Parliament Members (WPMs), or Aelodau Senedd Cymru (ASCau) in Welsh. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span The office is open to the public to access information about the Welsh Assembly.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elected_officials">Elected officials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elected officials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Presiding_Officer_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Presiding Officer of the National Assembly for Wales">Presiding Officer of the National Assembly for Wales</a></div>\n<p>After each election, the Assembly elects one Assembly Member to serve as <a href="/wiki/Presiding_Officer_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Presiding Officer of the National Assembly for Wales">Presiding Officer</a> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Llywydd</i>), and another to serve as a deputy. <a href="/wiki/Elin_Jones" title="Elin Jones">Elin Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> AM, has been Presiding Officer since the beginning of the 2016 term, having taken over from <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Butler_(politician)" title="Rosemary Butler (politician)">Rosemary Butler</a>. The Presiding Officer also acts as Chair of the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_Commission" title="Assembly Commission">National Assembly for Wales Commission</a>. Both the Presiding Officer and the Deputy Presiding Officer are expected not to vote.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_officials">Permanent officials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Permanent officials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The permanent administrative and support staff of the Welsh Assembly are employed by the Assembly Commission. They are not civil servants, although they enjoy similar terms and conditions of service to members of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Civil Service">UK Civil Service</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers_and_status">Powers and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Powers and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_2011" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 2011">Welsh devolution referendum, 2011</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Badge_of_Wales_(2008).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg/180px-Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg.png" width="180" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg/270px-Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg/360px-Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1009" data-file-height="1239" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Badge_of_Wales_(2008).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Badge_of_Wales" title="Royal Badge of Wales">Royal Badge of Wales</a> appears on <a href="/wiki/Act_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Act of the National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Acts</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The National Assembly consists of 60 elected members. They use the title <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Member of the National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Member</a> (AM) or <i>Aelod y Cynulliad</i> (AC).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The executive arm of the National Assembly for Wales, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a>, has been a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Labour" title="Welsh Labour">Labour</a> administration since its inception in 1999. Currently it is led by First Minister, <a href="/wiki/Carwyn_Jones" title="Carwyn Jones">Carwyn Jones</a>, since May 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The previous administration (then known as the Welsh Assembly Government), had been a coalition between <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a>, led by First Minister <a href="/wiki/Carwyn_Jones" title="Carwyn Jones">Carwyn Jones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>, led by Deputy First Minister <a href="/wiki/Ieuan_Wyn_Jones" title="Ieuan Wyn Jones">Ieuan Wyn Jones</a>, from December 2009 until May 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Cabinet_members_1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cabinet_members_1-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-News_Wales_1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-News_Wales_1-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The executive and civil servants are mainly based in Cardiff\'s <a href="/wiki/Cathays_Park" title="Cathays Park">Cathays Park</a> while the Assembly Members, the Assembly Commission and Ministerial support staff are based in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Bay" title="Cardiff Bay">Cardiff Bay</a>, where a new \xc2\xa367&#160;million Assembly Building, known as the <a href="/wiki/Senedd" title="Senedd">Senedd</a>, has been built.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One important feature of the National Assembly until 2007 was that there was no legal or constitutional separation of the legislative and executive functions, since it was a single corporate entity. Compared with other <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary systems">parliamentary systems</a>, and arrangements for devolution in other countries of the UK, this was unusual. In practice, however, there was separation of functions, and the terms "Assembly" and "Assembly Parliamentary Service" came into use to distinguish between the two arms. The Government of Wales Act 2006 regularised the separation when it came into effect following the 2007 Assembly Election.\n</p><p>Initially, the Assembly did not have primary legislative or fiscal powers, as these powers were reserved by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a>. The Assembly did have powers to pass <a href="/wiki/Secondary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary legislation">secondary legislation</a> in devolved areas. Sometimes secondary legislation could be used to amend <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>, but the scope of this was very limited. For example, the first Government of Wales Act gave the Assembly power to amend primary legislation relating to the merger of certain public bodies. However, most secondary powers were conferred on the executive by primary legislation to give the executive (i.e. Ministers) more powers, and the Assembly has had wider legislative powers than appearances might suggest. For example, the Assembly delayed local elections due to be held in 2003 for a year by use of secondary powers, so that they would not clash with Assembly elections. In 2001 the UK parliament used primary legislation to delay for one month local elections in England during the <a href="/wiki/Foot_and_Mouth_Disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Foot and Mouth Disease">Foot and Mouth Disease</a> epidemic.\n</p><p>The Assembly gained limited primary legislative powers following the 2007 election and the passage of the Government of Wales Act 2006. These laws are known as <a href="/wiki/Measure_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Measure of the National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Measures</a> and can be enacted in specific fields and matters within the legislative competency of the Assembly. New matters and fields can be devolved by Acts of the UK Parliament or by <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Competence_Order" title="Legislative Competence Order">LCOs</a> approved by Parliament.\n</p><p>While in principle the Assembly has no tax-varying powers, it can influence the rate of <a href="/wiki/Council_Tax" title="Council Tax">Council Tax</a> set by <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local authorities</a>, which are part-funded by a grant from the Welsh government.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-tcfwgtfct_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-tcfwgtfct-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> It also has some discretion over charges for government services. Notable examples in which this discretion has been used to create significant differences from other areas in the UK are:\n</p>\n<ol><li><i>Charges for NHS prescriptions in Wales</i> \xe2\x80\x93 these have now been abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>Charges for University Tuition</i> \xe2\x80\x93 are different for Welsh resident students studying at Welsh Universities, compared with students from or studying elsewhere in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>Charging for Residential Care</i> \xe2\x80\x93 In Wales there is a flat rate of contribution towards the cost of nursing care (roughly comparable to the highest level of English Contribution) for those who require residential care.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>This means in reality that there is a wider definition of "nursing care" than in England and therefore less dependence on means testing in Wales than in England, so that more people are entitled to higher levels of state assistance. These variations in the levels of charges may be viewed as <i>de facto</i> tax varying powers.\n</p><p>This model of more limited legislative powers was partly because Wales has had the same legal system as England since 1536, when it was <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535-1542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535-1542">merged</a> with England. <a href="/wiki/Ireland#Union_with_Britain_(1801-1922)" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1707">Scotland</a> were never merged with England, and so always retained some differences in their legal systems. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> both have deeper and wider powers.\n</p><p>The Assembly inherited the powers and budget of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a> and most of the functions of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a>. It has power to vary laws passed by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a> using secondary legislation. <a href="/wiki/Alun_Cairns" title="Alun Cairns">Alun Cairns</a>, who represents the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Glamorgan_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Vale of Glamorgan (UK Parliament constituency)">Vale of Glamorgan</a> constituency in the Westminster Parliament, is currently the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a>.\n</p><p>Following a referendum on 4 March 2011, the Welsh Assembly gained direct law-making powers (without the need to consult Westminster).\nOn 3 July 2012, the Welsh Assembly passed its first Act, the Local Government id="Members,_constituencies,_and_electoral_system">Members, constituencies, and electoral system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Members, constituencies, and electoral system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">mixed-member proportional representation</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Additional_Member_System" title="Additional Member System">Additional Member System</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-erf_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erf-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> forty of the AMs are elected from single-member constituencies on a <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">plurality voting system</a> (or <i>first past the post</i>) basis, the constituencies being equivalent to those used for the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> and twenty AMs are elected from regional <a href="/wiki/Closed_list" title="Closed list">closed lists</a> using an alternative party vote.<sup id="cite_ref-electionbbc_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-electionbbc-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> There are five regions: <a href="/wiki/Mid_and_West_Wales_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="Mid and West Wales (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">Mid and West Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Wales_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="North Wales (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">North Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_Central_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="South Wales Central (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">South Wales Central</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_East_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="South Wales East (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">South Wales East</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_West_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="South Wales West (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">South Wales West</a> (these are the same as the pre 1999 European Parliament constituencies for Wales), each of which returns four members.<sup id="cite_ref-electionbbc_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-electionbbc-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The additional members produce a degree of proportionality within each region.<sup id="cite_ref-electionbbc_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-electionbbc-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Whereas voters can choose any regional party list irrespective of their party vote in the constituency election, list AMs are not elected independently of the constituency element; rather, elected constituency AMs are deemed to be pre-elected list representatives for the purposes of calculating remainders in the <a href="/wiki/D%27Hondt_method" title="D&#39;Hondt method">d\'Hondt method</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-electionbbc_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-electionbbc-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Overall proportionality is limited by the low proportion of list members (33% of the Assembly compared with 43% in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and 50% in the German <i><a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a></i>) and the regionalisation of the list element.<sup id="cite_ref-richards_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richards-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, the Assembly as a whole has a greater degree of proportionality (based on proportions in the list elections) than the plurality voting system used for British parliamentary elections, but still deviates somewhat from proportionality.<sup id="cite_ref-richards_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richards-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a> system had been considered for the Assembly by the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> as early as 1995\xe2\x80\x9396, but according to the evidence given to the Richard Commission by <a href="/wiki/Ron_Davies_(Welsh_politician)" title="Ron Davies (Welsh politician)">Ron Davies</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Welsh Secretary</a>,\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Had we done that of course we would have had to have had a Boundary Commission and that process would have taken forever and a day and that would have frustrated our overall political timetable. So we had to settle on the existing constituency arrangements, parliamentary constituencies and European Constituencies.<sup id="cite_ref-richards_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richards-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_name_change">Proposed name change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Proposed name change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In a written statement by <a href="/wiki/Elin_Jones" title="Elin Jones">Elin Jones</a> AM, the Chair of the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_Commission" title="Assembly Commission">Assembly Commission</a>, entitled "Assembly Reform Programme" she confirmed in July 2016 that the Welsh Assembly had agreed unanimously to change the name of the Assembly from the National Assembly for Wales to the Welsh Parliament (in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh (language)">Welsh</a>: <i>Senedd Cymru</i>). The change should be legislated for before the end of the current Assembly in May 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-Vale_of_Glamorgan_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vale_of_Glamorgan-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There have been five elections to the Assembly, in <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_1999" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 1999">1999</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2003" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2003">2003</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2007" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2007">2007</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2011" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2011">2011</a> and 2016. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Government formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following the elections in May 2016, the composition of the Assembly changed in the most dramatic fashion since the beginning of the Assembly. Labour dropped from 30 to 29 seats, and Plaid Cymru moved from 11 to 12 seats. The Conservatives lost 3 seats, moving from 14 seats to 11, while the Liberal Democrats dropped from 5 to 1 seat. UKIP, who had not previously had representation, gained seven AMs.\n</p><p>Following an initial vote on the First Minister, in which Plaid Cymru\'s Leanne Wood and Labour\'s <a href="/wiki/Carwyn_Jones" title="Carwyn Jones">Carwyn Jones</a> both gained 29 votes from AMs each, a week of talks were held. A document was produced after Plaid Cymru&#8211;Labour talks entitled "Moving Wales Forward", which detailed policy concessions in exchange for allowing Carwyn Jones to become First Minister. Labour appointed <a href="/wiki/Kirsty_Williams" title="Kirsty Williams">Kirsty Williams</a> as Education Secretary, meaning that the minority government was a coalition between Welsh Labour and the Welsh Liberal Democrats. Plaid Cymru, the Conservatives and UKIP formed opposition groups.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_since_the_election">Changes since the election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assembly_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Changes since the election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After 4 months in the assembly, <a href="/wiki/North_Wales_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="North Wales (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">North Wales AM</a> <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Gill" title="Nathan Gill">Nathan Gill</a> left the <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">UKIP</a> group to sit as an independent, citing much infighting and distractions.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> He remained a member of the party and its leader in Wales, until Neil Hamilton was made Wales leader in September 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dafydd_Elis-Thomas" title="Dafydd Elis-Thomas">Dafydd Elis-Thomas</a> quit the Plaid Cymru group on 14 October 2016. As a result of the defection, <a href="/wiki/Leanne_Wood" title="Leanne Wood">Leanne Wood</a> lost the title of leader of the opposition. Two months later, he pledged to back the Welsh Labour-led Government, giving the new government an overall majority in the Welsh Assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Neil_McEvoy" title="Neil McEvoy">Neil McEvoy</a> was suspended by the Plaid Cymru group after tribunal found his guilty of bullying in his other role as a councillor for Cardiff. He was later expelled from the Plaid Cymru.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Reckless" title="Mark Reckless">Mark Reckless</a> quit the UKIP group in April 2017. He will now sit as a Conservative AM, but without being an official member of the Conservative Party.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Sargeant" title="Carl Sargeant">Carl Sargeant</a> was suspended from Welsh Labour following allegations about his personal conduct. On 7 November 2017, he was found dead.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Alyn_and_Deeside_by-election,_2018" title="Alyn and Deeside by-election, 2018">by-election was be held</a> in his former constituency of Alyn and Deeside on 6 February 2018 to choose a successor, which was won by the Labour candidate, his son, Jack.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> This increased the overall majority of the Government group in the Welsh Assembly to 2.\n</p><p>On the 27 December 2017 it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Gill" title="Nathan Gill">Nathan Gill</a> had resigned as an AM.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> As 3rd on UKIP\'s list for the <a href="/wiki/North_Wales_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="North Wales (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">North Wales region</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandy_Jones_(politician)" title="Mandy Jones (politician)">Mandy Jones</a> was sworn in as an <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Member of the National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Member</a> on the 29 December 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> On 9 January UKIP Wales announced that she would not be joining the UKIP group in the Assembly, due to employing members of other parties in her office.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Helen_Mary_Jones" title="Helen Mary Jones">Helen Mary Jones</a> replaced Simon Thomas as an Assembly Member in August 2018.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Caroline_Jones_(politician)" title="Caroline Jones (politician)">Caroline Jones</a> resigned as a member of UKIP and UKIP\'s group on 12 September 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
