<b>grammar school</b> is one of several different types of <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">school</a> in the history of education in the United Kingdom and other English-speaking countries, originally a school teaching <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, but more recently an academically-oriented <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary school</a>, differentiated in recent years from less academic <a href="/wiki/Secondary_Modern_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary Modern School">Secondary Modern Schools</a>. \n</p><p>The original purpose of <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> grammar schools was the teaching of Latin. Over time the <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">curriculum</a> was broadened, first to include <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>, and later English and other <a href="/wiki/European_language" class="mw-redirect" title="European language">European languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural sciences">natural sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, and other subjects. In the late <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> grammar schools were reorganised to provide secondary education throughout England and Wales; Scotland had developed a different system. Grammar schools of these types were also established in British territories overseas, where they have evolved in different ways.\n</p><p>Grammar schools became the selective tier of the <a href="/wiki/Tripartite_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Tripartite System">Tripartite System</a> of state-funded secondary education operating in England and Wales from the mid-1940s to the late 1960s and continuing in Northern Ireland. With the move to non-selective <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">comprehensive schools</a> in the 1960s and 1970s, some grammar schools became fully independent and charged fees, while most others were abolished or became comprehensive (or sometimes merged with a <a href="/wiki/Secondary_modern" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary modern">secondary modern</a> to form a new comprehensive school). In both cases, many of these schools kept "grammar school" in their names. More recently, a number of state grammar schools still retaining their selective intake gained <a href="/wiki/Academy_(English_school)" title="Academy (English school)">academy</a> status, meaning that they are independent of the <a href="/wiki/Local_education_authority" title="Local education authority">Local Education Authority (LEA)</a>. Some parts of England retain forms of the Tripartite System, and a few grammar schools survive in otherwise comprehensive areas. Some of the remaining grammar schools can trace their histories to before the 16th century.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_grammar_schools">Medieval grammar schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Medieval grammar schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although the term <i>scolae grammaticales</i> was not widely used until the 14th century, the earliest such schools appeared from the sixth century, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_School,_Canterbury" class="mw-redirect" title="King&#39;s School, Canterbury">King\'s School, Canterbury</a> (founded 597) and the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_School,_Rochester" title="King&#39;s School, Rochester">King\'s School, Rochester</a> (604).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gordon&amp;Lawton_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon&amp;Lawton-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\nThe schools were attached to cathedrals and monasteries, teaching Latin \xe2\x80\x93 the language of the church \xe2\x80\x93 to future priests and monks. Other subjects required for religious work were occasionally added, including music and verse (for liturgy), astronomy and mathematics (for the church calendar) and law (for administration).<sup id="cite_ref-Spens_Report_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spens_Report-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg/170px-Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg/255px-Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg/340px-Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Winchester_College_Chapel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Winchester College Chapel</div></div></div>\n<p>With the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient universities">ancient universities</a> from the late 12th century, grammar schools became the entry point to a <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts</a> education, with Latin seen as the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Trivium_(education)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trivium (education)">trivium</a>. Pupils were usually educated in grammar schools up to the age of 14, after which they would look to universities and the church for further study. Three of the first schools independent of the church \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Winchester_College" title="Winchester College">Winchester College</a> (1382), <a href="/wiki/Oswestry_School" title="Oswestry School">Oswestry School</a> (1407) and <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a> (1440) \xe2\x80\x93 were closely tied to the universities; they were <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">boarding schools</a>, so they could educate pupils from anywhere in the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-Spens_Report_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spens_Report-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_grammar_schools">Early Modern grammar schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early Modern grammar schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An example of an early grammar school founded by an early modern borough corporation unconnected with church or university is <a href="/wiki/Bridgnorth_Grammar_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridgnorth Grammar School">Bridgnorth Grammar School</a>, founded in 1503 by Bridgnorth Borough Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a> in the 16th century, most cathedral schools were closed and replaced by new foundations funded from the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_monasteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissolution of the monasteries">dissolution of the monasteries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Spens_Report_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spens_Report-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> For example, the oldest extant schools in Wales \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Christ_College,_Brecon" title="Christ College, Brecon">Christ College, Brecon</a> (founded 1541) and the <a href="/wiki/Friars_School,_Bangor" title="Friars School, Bangor">Friars School, Bangor</a> (1557) \xe2\x80\x93 were established on the sites of former <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> monasteries. King <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">Edward VI</a> made an important contribution to grammar schools, founding a series of schools during his reign (see <a href="/wiki/King_Edward%27s_School_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="King Edward&#39;s School (disambiguation)">King Edward\'s School</a>), and King <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a> founded a series of "Royal Schools" in Ulster, beginning with <a href="/wiki/The_Royal_School,_Armagh" title="The Royal School, Armagh">The Royal School, Armagh</a>. In theory these schools were open to all and offered free tuition to those who could not pay fees; however, few poor children attended school, because their labour was economically valuable to their families.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Scottish Reformation</a> schools such as the <a href="/wiki/High_School_of_Glasgow" title="High School of Glasgow">Choir School of Glasgow Cathedral</a> (founded 1124) and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_High_School_(Edinburgh)" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal High School (Edinburgh)">Grammar School of the Church of Edinburgh</a> (1128) passed from church control to <a href="/wiki/Burgh" title="Burgh">burgh</a> councils, and the burghs also founded new schools.\n</p><p>With the increased emphasis on studying the scriptures after the Reformation, many schools added Greek and, in a few cases, Hebrew. The teaching of these languages was hampered by a shortage of non-Latin type and of teachers fluent in the languages.\n</p><p>During the 16th and 17th centuries the setting-up of grammar schools became a common act of charity by nobles, wealthy merchants and <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a>; for example <a href="/wiki/The_Crypt_School" title="The Crypt School">The Crypt School</a>, Gloucester, founded by John and Joan Cook in 1539, <a href="/wiki/Spalding_Grammar_School" title="Spalding Grammar School">Spalding Grammar School</a>, founded by John Gamlyn and John Blanche in 1588, and <a href="/wiki/Blundell%27s_School" title="Blundell&#39;s School">Blundell\'s School</a>, founded in 1604 by wealthy Tiverton merchant <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blundell" title="Peter Blundell">Peter Blundell</a>. Many of these are still commemorated in annual "Founder\'s Day" services and ceremonies at surviving schools. The usual pattern was to create an endowment to pay the wages of a master to instruct local boys in Latin and sometimes Greek without charge.<sup id="cite_ref-Walford_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The school day typically ran from 6 a.m. to 5 p.m., with a two-hour break for lunch; in winter, school started at 7 a.m. and ended at 4 p.m.  Most of the day was spent in the <a href="/wiki/Rote_learning" title="Rote learning">rote learning</a> of Latin. To encourage fluency, some schoolmasters recommended punishing any pupil who spoke in English. The younger boys learned the <a href="/wiki/Parts_of_speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts of speech">parts of speech</a> and Latin words in the first year, learned to construct Latin sentences in the second year, and began translating English-Latin and Latin-English passages in the third year. By the end of their studies at age 14, they would be quite familiar with the great Latin authors, and with Latin drama and rhetoric.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Other skills, such as arithmetic and handwriting, were taught in odd moments or by travelling specialist teachers such as <a href="/wiki/Scrivener" title="Scrivener">scriveners</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar_schools_in_the_18th_and_19th_centuries">Grammar schools in the 18th and 19th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grammar schools in the 18th and 19th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1755 <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Johnson" title="Samuel Johnson">Samuel Johnson</a>\'s <i>Dictionary</i> defined a grammar school as <i>a school in which the learned languages are grammatically taught</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\nHowever, by this time demand for these languages had fallen greatly. A new commercial class required modern languages and commercial subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-Walford_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Most grammar schools founded in the 18th century also taught arithmetic and English.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland, the burgh councils updated the curricula of their schools so that Scotland no longer has grammar schools in any of the senses discussed here, though some, such as <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Grammar_School" title="Aberdeen Grammar School">Aberdeen Grammar School</a>, retain the name.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In England, urban middle-class pressure for a commercial curriculum was often supported by the school\'s trustees (who would charge the new students fees), but resisted by the schoolmaster, supported by the terms of the original endowment. Very few schools were able to obtain special acts of Parliament to change their statutes; examples are the <a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_School,_Macclesfield" class="mw-redirect" title="The King&#39;s School, Macclesfield">Macclesfield Grammar School</a> Act 1774 and the <a href="/wiki/Bolton_School" title="Bolton School">Bolton Grammar School</a> Act 1788.<sup id="cite_ref-Walford_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Such a dispute between the trustees and master of <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Grammar_School" title="Leeds Grammar School">Leeds Grammar School</a> led to a celebrated case in the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">Court of Chancery</a>. After 10 years, <a href="/wiki/John_Scott,_1st_Earl_of_Eldon" title="John Scott, 1st Earl of Eldon">Lord Eldon</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a>, ruled in 1805, "There is no authority for thus changing the nature of the Charity, and filling a School intended for the purpose of teaching Greek and Latin with Scholars learning the German and French languages, mathematics, and anything except Greek and Latin."<sup id="cite_ref-Matthews_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Although he offered a compromise by which some subjects might be added to a classical core, the ruling set a restrictive precedent for grammar schools across England; they seemed to be in terminal decline.<sup id="cite_ref-Spens_Report_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spens_Report-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However it should be borne in mind that the decline of the grammar schools in England and Wales was not uniform and that until the foundation of St Bees Clerical College, in 1817, and St David\'s College Lampeter, in 1828, specialist grammar schools in the north-west of England and South Wales were in effect providing tertiary education to men in their late teens and early twenties, which enabled them to be ordained as Anglican clergymen without going to university.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Victorian-era_grammar_schools">Victorian-era grammar schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Victorian-era grammar schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_and_Welsh_endowed_schools_(19th_century)" title="List of English and Welsh endowed schools (19th century)">List of English and Welsh endowed schools (19th century)</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg" class="image"><img alt="sepia photograph of a seated woman in conservative Victorian dress" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg/170px-Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg" width="170" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg/255px-Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg/340px-Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg 2x" data-file-width="380" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Frances_Mary_Buss.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Frances_Buss" title="Frances Buss">Frances Buss</a>, a pioneer of women\'s education and founding head of <a href="/wiki/North_London_Collegiate_School" title="North London Collegiate School">North London Collegiate School</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The 19th century saw a series of reforms to grammar schools, culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Endowed_Schools_Act_1869" title="Endowed Schools Act 1869">Endowed Schools Act 1869</a>.  Grammar schools were reinvented as academically oriented secondary schools following literary or scientific curricula, while often retaining classical subjects.\n</p><p>The Grammar Schools Act 1840 made it lawful to apply the income of grammar schools to purposes other than the teaching of classical languages, but change still required the consent of the schoolmaster. At the same time, the national schools were reorganising themselves along the lines of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Arnold" title="Thomas Arnold">Thomas Arnold</a>\'s reforms at <a href="/wiki/Rugby_School" title="Rugby School">Rugby School</a>, and the spread of the railways led to new boarding schools teaching a broader curriculum, such as <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_College" title="Marlborough College">Marlborough College</a> (1843). The first girls\' schools targeted at university entrance were <a href="/wiki/North_London_Collegiate_School" title="North London Collegiate School">North London Collegiate School</a> (1850) and <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Ladies%27_College" title="Cheltenham Ladies&#39; College">Cheltenham Ladies\' College</a> (from the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Beale" title="Dorothea Beale">Dorothea Beale</a> in 1858).<sup id="cite_ref-Walford_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Modelled on the <a href="/wiki/Clarendon_Commission" title="Clarendon Commission">Clarendon Commission</a>, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Public_Schools_Act_1868" title="Public Schools Act 1868">Public Schools Act 1868</a> which restructured the trusts of nine leading schools (including <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harrow_School" title="Harrow School">Harrow School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury_School" title="Shrewsbury School">Shrewsbury School</a>), the Taunton Commission was appointed to examine the remaining 782 endowed grammar schools. The commission reported that the distribution of schools did not match the current population, and that provision varied greatly in quality, with provision for girls being particularly limited.<sup id="cite_ref-Walford_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The commission proposed the creation of a national system of secondary education by restructuring the endowments of these schools for modern purposes. The result was the <a href="/wiki/Endowed_Schools_Act_1869" title="Endowed Schools Act 1869">Endowed Schools Act 1869</a>, which created the Endowed Schools Commission with extensive powers over endowments of individual schools. It was said that the commission "could turn a boys\' school in Northumberland into a girls\' school in Cornwall". Across England and Wales schools endowed to offer free classical instruction to boys were remodelled as fee-paying schools (with a few competitive scholarships) teaching broad curricula to boys or girls.<sup id="cite_ref-Walford_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bournemouth_School.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Bournemouth_School.jpg/220px-Bournemouth_School.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Bournemouth_School.jpg/330px-Bournemouth_School.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Bournemouth_School.jpg/440px-Bournemouth_School.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="978" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bournemouth_School.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bournemouth_School" title="Bournemouth School">Bournemouth School</a> opened on 22 January 1901, the day Queen Victoria died.</div></div></div>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> there was a great emphasis on the importance of <a href="/wiki/Self-improvement" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-improvement">self-improvement</a>, and parents keen for their children to receive a decent education organised the creation of new schools with modern curricula, though often retaining a classical core. These newer schools tended to emulate the great <a href="/wiki/Public_school_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Public school (UK)">public schools</a>, copying their curriculum, ethos and ambitions, and often took the title "grammar school" for historical reasons. A girls\' grammar school established in a town with an older boys\' grammar school would often be named a "high school".\n</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Education_(Administrative_Provisions)_Act_1907" title="Education (Administrative Provisions) Act 1907">Education (Administrative Provisions) Act 1907</a> all grant-aided secondary schools were required to provide at least 25 percent of their places as free scholarships for students from public elementary schools. Grammar schools thus emerged as one part of the highly varied education system of England and Wales before 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-Spens_Report_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spens_Report-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Tripartite_System">In the Tripartite System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In the Tripartite System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tripartite_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Tripartite System">Tripartite System</a></div>\n<p>The 1944 <a href="/wiki/Education_Act_1944" title="Education Act 1944">Education Act</a> created the first nationwide system of state-funded secondary education in England and Wales, echoed by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_(Northern_Ireland)_Act_1947&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Education (Northern Ireland) Act 1947 (page does not exist)">Education (Northern Ireland) Act 1947</a>. One of the three types of school forming the <a href="/wiki/Tripartite_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Tripartite System">Tripartite System</a> was called the grammar school, which sought to spread the academic ethos of the existing grammar schools. Grammar schools were intended to teach an academic curriculum to the most intellectually able 25 percent of the school population as selected by the <a href="/wiki/11-plus" class="mw-redirect" title="11-plus">11-plus</a> examination.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mgs_side_on_view_circa_2000.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Mgs_side_on_view_circa_2000.jpg/220px-Mgs_side_on_view_circa_2000.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/Mgs_side_on_view_circa_2000.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="309" data-file-height="221" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mgs_side_on_view_circa_2000.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Manchester Grammar School, the largest and most famous of the direct grant grammar schools</div></div></div>\n<p>Two types of grammar schools existed under the system:<sup id="cite_ref-New_Anatomy_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Anatomy-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-statistics_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-statistics-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>There were more than 1,200 maintained grammar schools, which were fully state-funded. Though some were quite old, most were either newly created or built since the Victorian period, seeking to replicate the studious, aspirational atmosphere found in the older grammar schools.</li>\n<li>There were also 179 <a href="/wiki/Direct-grant_grammar_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct-grant grammar school">direct-grant grammar schools</a>, which took between one quarter and one-half of their pupils from the state system, and the rest from fee-paying parents. They also exercised far greater freedom from local authorities, and were members of the <a href="/wiki/Headmasters%27_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Headmasters&#39; Conference">Headmasters\' Conference</a>. These schools included some very old schools encouraged to participate in the Tripartite System. The most famous example of a direct-grant grammar was <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Grammar_School" title="Manchester Grammar School">Manchester Grammar School</a>, whose headmaster, <a href="/wiki/Eric_James,_Baron_James_of_Rusholme" title="Eric James, Baron James of Rusholme">Lord James of Rusholme</a>, was one of the most outspoken advocates of the Tripartite System.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Grammar school pupils were given the best opportunities of any schoolchildren in the state system. Initially, they studied for the <a href="/wiki/School_Certificate_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="School Certificate (UK)">School Certificate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Higher_School_Certificate_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Higher School Certificate (UK)">Higher School Certificate</a>, replaced in 1951 by <a href="/wiki/General_Certificate_of_Education" title="General Certificate of Education">General Certificate of Education</a> examinations at <a href="/wiki/GCE_Ordinary_Level_(United_Kingdom)" title="GCE Ordinary Level (United Kingdom)">O-level</a> (Ordinary level) and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Level_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Level (UK)">A-level</a> (Advanced level). In contrast, very few students at <a href="/wiki/Secondary_modern_school" title="Secondary modern school">secondary modern schools</a> took public examinations until the introduction of the less academic and less prestigious <a href="/wiki/Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="Certificate of Secondary Education">Certificate of Secondary Education</a> (known as the CSE) in the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Until the implementation of the <a href="/wiki/Robbins_Report" title="Robbins Report">Robbins Report</a> in the 1960s, children from public and grammar schools effectively monopolised access to universities. These schools were also the only ones that offered an extra term of school to prepare pupils for the competitive entrance exams for <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Sampson" title="Anthony Sampson">Anthony Sampson</a>, in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Anatomy_of_Britain" title="Anatomy of Britain">Anatomy of Britain</a></i> (1965), there were structural problems within the testing process that underpinned the eleven plus which meant it tended to result in secondary modern schools being overwhelmingly dominated by the children of poor and working-class parents, while grammar schools were dominated by the children of wealthier middle class parents.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Tripartite System was largely abolished in England and Wales between 1965, with the issue of <a href="/wiki/Circular_10/65" title="Circular 10/65">Circular 10/65</a>, and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_Act_1976&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Education Act 1976 (page does not exist)">Education Act 1976</a>. Most maintained grammar schools were amalgamated with a number of other local schools, to form neighbourhood <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">comprehensive schools</a>, though a few were closed. This process proceeded quickly in Wales, with the closure of such schools as <a href="/wiki/Cowbridge_Grammar_School" title="Cowbridge Grammar School">Cowbridge Grammar School</a>. In England, implementation was less even, with some counties and individual schools successfully resisting conversion or closure.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Direct Grant Grammar Schools (Cessation of Grant) Regulations 1975 required direct grant schools to decide whether to convert into comprehensives under local authority control or become <a href="/wiki/Independent_school_(United_Kingdom)" title="Independent school (United Kingdom)">independent schools</a> funded entirely by fees. Of the direct grant schools remaining at that time, 51 became comprehensive, 119 opted for independence, and five were "not accepted for the maintained system and expected to become independent schools or to close".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Some of these schools retained the name "grammar" in their title but are no longer free of charge for all but a few pupils. These schools normally select their pupils by an entrance examination and sometimes by interview.\n</p><p>By the end of the 1980s, all of the grammar schools in Wales and most of those in England had closed or converted. Selection also disappeared from state-funded schools in Scotland in the same period. While many former grammar schools ceased to be selective, many of these also retained the name "grammar".  Most of these schools remain comprehensive, though a few became <a href="/wiki/Partially_selective_school_(England)" title="Partially selective school (England)">partially selective</a> or fully selective in the 1990s. The tripartite system of grammar and secondary modern schools does survive in a few areas, such as Kent, where the eleven-plus examination to divide children into grammar and secondary modern schools is known as the <a href="/wiki/Kent_Test" class="mw-redirect" title="Kent Test">Kent Test</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_British_grammar_schools">Current British grammar schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current British grammar schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Today, "grammar school" commonly refers to one of the 164 remaining fully <a href="/wiki/Selective_school" title="Selective school">selective</a> <a href="/wiki/State-funded_schools_(England)" title="State-funded schools (England)">state-funded schools</a> in England and the 69 remaining in Northern Ireland.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Grammar_Schools_Association" title="National Grammar Schools Association">National Grammar Schools Association</a> campaigns in favour of such schools,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> while Comprehensive Future and the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_State_Education" title="Campaign for State Education">Campaign for State Education</a> campaign against them.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a> study has shown that UK grammar school pupils gain no significant social or emotional advantages by the age of 14 over similarly gifted pupils in non-selective schools.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_grammar_schools_in_England" title="List of grammar schools in England">List of grammar schools in England</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LiverpoolBlueCoatEastFront.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/LiverpoolBlueCoatEastFront.jpg/440px-LiverpoolBlueCoatEastFront.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LiverpoolBlueCoatEastFront.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Liverpool_Blue_Coat_School" class="mw-redirect" title="The Liverpool Blue Coat School">The Liverpool Blue Coat School</a>, founded in 1708</div></div></div>\n<p>At the 1995 <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party However the party\'s manifesto for the 1997 election promised that "Any changes in the admissions policies of grammar schools will be decided by local parents."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Under the Labour government\'s <a href="/wiki/School_Standards_and_Framework_Act_1998" title="School Standards and Framework Act 1998">School Standards and Framework Act 1998</a>, grammar schools were for the first time to be designated by <a href="/wiki/Statutory_instrument" title="Statutory instrument">statutory instrument</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The Act also defined a procedure by which local communities could petition for a ballot for an end to selection at schools.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Petitions were launched in several areas, but only one received the signatures of 20% of eligible parents, the level needed to trigger a ballot.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Thus the only ballot held to date was for <a href="/wiki/Ripon_Grammar_School" title="Ripon Grammar School">Ripon Grammar School</a> in 2000, when parents rejected change by a ratio of 2 to 1.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> These arrangements were condemned by the Select Committee for Education and Skills as being ineffective and a waste of time and resources.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grammar_school_ballots_in_England.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Grammar_school_ballots_in_England.svg/300px-Grammar_school_ballots_in_England.svg.png" width="300" height="364" class="thumbimage" <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grammar_school_ballots_in_England.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grammar school areas and groups as identified by the Education (Grammar School Ballots) Regulations 1998. LEAs considered grammar areas are shown filled, while circles indicate isolated grammar schools or clusters of neighbouring schools.</div></div></div>\n<p>There are still 164 state-run grammar schools in existence.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Only a few areas keep a formal grammar school system along the lines of the Tripartite System. In these areas, the <a href="/wiki/Eleven_plus_exam" class="mw-redirect" title="Eleven plus exam">eleven plus exam</a> is used solely to identify a subset of children (around 25%) considered suitable for grammar education. When a grammar school has too many qualified applicants, other criteria are used to allocate places, such as siblings, distance or faith. Such systems still exist in Buckinghamshire, Rugby and Stratford districts of Warwickshire, the Salisbury district of Wiltshire and most of Lincolnshire, Kent, Reading and Medway.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\nOf metropolitan areas, Trafford and most of Wirral are selective.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In other areas, grammar schools survive mainly as very highly selective schools in an otherwise comprehensive county, for example in several of the outer boroughs of London. These schools are often heavily oversubscribed, and award places in rank order of performance in their entry tests. In some <a href="/wiki/Local_education_authority" title="Local education authority">LEAs</a>, as many as 10\xe2\x80\x9315% of 11-year-olds may attend grammar schools (for example in Gloucestershire), but in other LEAs it is as low as 2%. These very highly selective schools also tend to dominate the top positions in performance tables.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Further radical change was opposed by both Conservative and Labour governments until September 2016.  Although many on the left argue that the existence of selective schools undermines the comprehensive structure, Labour governments have delegated decisions on grammar schools to local processes, which have not yet resulted in any changes.\nMoreover, government education policy appears to accept the existence of some kind of hierarchy in secondary education, with <a href="/wiki/Specialist_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Specialist school">specialist schools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beacon_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Beacon school">beacon schools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_(English_school)" title="Academy (English school)">academies</a> and similar initiatives proposed as ways of raising standards.\nMany grammar schools have featured in these programmes, and a lower level of selection is permitted at specialist schools.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2016, prime minister <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a> reversed the previous <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> policy against expansion of grammar schools (except to cope with population expansion in wholly selective areas).<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_9/9/16_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_9/9/16-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\nThe government opened a consultation on proposals to allow existing grammar schools to expand and new ones to be set up.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> parties remain opposed to any expansion.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_9/9/16_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_9/9/16-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Ireland</a></div>\n<p>Attempts to move to a comprehensive system (as in the rest of the United Kingdom) have been delayed by shifts in the administration of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.\nAs a result, Northern Ireland still maintains the grammar school system, with many pupils being entered for academic selection exams similar to the 11-plus.\nSince the "open enrolment" reform of 1989, these schools (unlike those in England) have been required to accept pupils up to their capacity, which has also increased.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\nBy 2006, the 69 grammar schools took 42% of transferring children, and only 7 of them took all of their intake from the top 30% of the cohort.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 11-plus has long been controversial, and Northern Ireland\'s political parties have taken opposing positions. Unionists tend to lean towards preserving the grammar schools as they are, with academic selection at the age of 11, whereas nationalist politicians lean towards scrapping the 11-plus, despite vehement protestations from the majority of Catholic Grammar Schools, most notably by the board of governors at <a href="/wiki/Rathmore_Grammar_School" title="Rathmore Grammar School">Rathmore Grammar School</a> in Finaghy, (a South Belfast suburb) and Lumen Christi (although co-educational) in Derry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed for the whole sentence (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a> claimed to have ensured the continuation of the grammar school system in the Province as part of the <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews_Agreement" title="St Andrews Agreement">St Andrews Agreement</a> in October 2006. By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> claims to have secured the abolition of the 11-plus and a veto over any system which might follow it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The last government-run 11-plus exam was held in 2008 (for 2009 entry),<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\nbut the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> has not been able to agree on a replacement system for secondary transfer.\nThe grammar schools have organised groupings to run their own tests, the Post-Primary Transfer Consortium (mostly Catholic schools) and the Association for Quality Education.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\nThe Northern Ireland Commission for Catholic Education has accepted continued selection at Catholic grammar schools as a temporary measure, but wishes them to end the practice by 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> As of October 2014, the practice continues.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_countries_or_regions">In other countries or countries or regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Grammar schools were established in various British territories, and have developed in different ways since those territories became independent.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a mid-19th century, private schools were established in the Australian colonies to spare the wealthy classes from sending their sons to schools in Britain.\nThese schools took their inspiration from English <a href="/wiki/Public_school_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Public school (UK)">public schools</a>, and often called themselves "grammar schools".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\nEarly examples include <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Grammar_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Launceston Grammar School">Launceston Grammar School</a> (1846), <a href="/wiki/Pulteney_Grammar_School" title="Pulteney Grammar School">Pulteney Grammar School</a> (1847), <a href="/wiki/Geelong_Grammar_School" title="Geelong Grammar School">Geelong Grammar School</a> (1855) and <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Grammar_School" title="Melbourne Grammar School">Melbourne Grammar School</a> (1858).\n</p><p>With the exception of the non-denominational <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Grammar_School" title="Sydney Grammar School">Sydney Grammar School</a> (1857) and <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> grammar schools, all the grammar schools established in the 19th century were attached to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> (now the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Church_of_Australia" title="Anglican Church of Australia">Anglican Church of Australia</a>).\nIn Queensland, the <a href="/wiki/Grammar_Schools_Act_(Qld)_1860" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammar Schools Act (Qld) 1860">Grammar Schools Act 1860</a> provided for the state-assisted foundation of non-denominational grammar schools.\nBeginning with <a href="/wiki/Ipswich_Grammar_School" title="Ipswich Grammar School">Ipswich Grammar School</a> (1863), ten schools were founded, of which eight still exist.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\nThey include <a href="/wiki/Brisbane_Girls_Grammar_School" title="Brisbane Girls Grammar School">Brisbane Girls Grammar School</a> (1875), the first of several grammar schools for girls in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1920s grammar schools of other denominations were established, including members of the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Grammar_Schools_of_Victoria" title="Associated Grammar Schools of Victoria">Associated Grammar Schools of Victoria</a>, and the trend has continued to the present day.\nToday, the term is defined only in Queensland legislation.\nThroughout the country, "grammar schools" are generally high-cost private schools.\nThe equivalents of contemporary English grammar schools are <a href="/wiki/Selective_school" title="Selective school">selective schools</a>.\n</p><p>The New South Wales public education system also operates 19 selective public schools which resemble the English grammar-school system insofar as they engage in academic selection by way of centralised examination, they do not charge tuition fees and they are recipients of a greater degree of public funding per pupil than is afforded to non-selective government schools.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontario">Ontario</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ontario">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Grammar schools provided secondary education in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> until 1871.\n</p><p>The first lieutenant-governor of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Canada" title="Upper Canada">Upper Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Graves_Simcoe" title="John Graves Simcoe">John Graves Simcoe</a>, advocated grammar schools for the colony to save the wealthy from sending their sons to the United States to be educated, but was unable to convince his superiors in London.\nHe did however make a grant enabling John Stuart to set up <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Collegiate_and_Vocational_Institute" title="Kingston Collegiate and Vocational Institute">Kingston Grammar School</a> in 1795.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\nAfter several abortive attempts to raise funding, the District Schools Act of 1807 provided support for one grammar school teacher in each district (of which there were then eight), but they were then left to their own devices.\nFinding the grammar schools unsuitable as preparation for university,\nlieutenant-governor <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Colborne" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir John Colborne">Sir John Colborne</a> founded <a href="/wiki/Upper_Canada_College" title="Upper Canada College">Upper Canada College</a>\nas a superior grammar school.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Legislation in 1839 allowed for more than one grammar school in a district, triggering a rapid but unstructured growth in numbers over the following two decades, rising to 86 in 1861.\nThe schools became more independent of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, and also began to admit girls.\nHowever, the schools were unsupervised, often underfunded and of varying standards.\nSome, like <a href="/wiki/Galt_Collegiate_Institute_and_Vocational_School" title="Galt Collegiate Institute and Vocational School">Tassie\'s School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Galt,_Ontario" title="Galt, Ontario">Galt</a> provided a traditional classical education, while many provided a basic education of poor quality.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\nChief Superintendent of Education <a href="/wiki/Egerton_Ryerson" title="Egerton Ryerson">Egerton Ryerson</a> attempted to reform the schools in the 1850s and 1860s, moving control of the schools from counties (the former districts) to city authorities, securing their funding and introducing inspectors.\nHowever, his efforts to convert the schools into classical schools for only boys were unsuccessful.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\nIn recognition of the broad curricula offered, grammar schools were redesignated as <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">high schools</a> by the Act to Improve the Common and Grammar Schools of the Province of Ontario of 1871.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\nSchools able to offer classical studies were given additional funding as <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_institute#Canada" title="Collegiate institute">collegiate institutes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hong Hong Kong">Education in Hong Kong</a></div>\n<p>Mainstream schools in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> reflect the post-war British grammar system, focusing on a traditional curriculum rather than <a href="/wiki/Vocational" class="mw-redirect" title="Vocational">vocational</a> subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_of_Ireland">Republic of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DroghedaGrammarSchool.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DroghedaGrammarSchool.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drogheda Grammar School</div></div></div>\n<p>Education in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> has traditionally been organised on <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">denominational</a> lines.  Grammar schools along the lines of those in Great Britain were set up for members of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> prior to its <a href="/wiki/Disestablishment" class="mw-redirect" title="Disestablishment">disestablishment</a> in 1871.  Some schools remain, as private schools catering largely for Protestant students.  These are often fee-paying and accommodate boarders, given the scattered nature of the Protestant population in much of Ireland.  Such schools include <a href="/wiki/Bandon_Grammar_School" title="Bandon Grammar School">Bandon Grammar School</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Drogheda_Grammar_School" title="Drogheda Grammar School">Drogheda Grammar School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundalk_Grammar_School" title="Dundalk Grammar School">Dundalk Grammar School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sligo_Grammar_School" title="Sligo Grammar School">Sligo Grammar School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>  Others are among the many former fee-paying schools absorbed into larger state-funded <a href="/wiki/Community_school_(Ireland)" title="Community school (Ireland)">community schools</a> founded since the introduction of universal secondary education in the Republic by minister <a href="/wiki/Donogh_O%27Malley" title="Donogh O&#39;Malley">Donogh O\'Malley</a> in September 1967. They are generally not known as "grammar schools" but are similarly selective in their intake. Among the best known being the <a href="/wiki/Penang_Free_School" title="Penang Free School">Penang Free School</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Institution" title="Victoria Institution">Victoria Institution</a> (Kuala Lumpur), <a href="/wiki/Malay_College_Kuala_Kangsar" title="Malay College Kuala Kangsar">Malay College Kuala Kangsar</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=English_College,_Johor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="English College, Johor (page does not exist)">English College</a> (Johor Bahru), <a href="/wiki/King_George_V_School,_Seremban" title="King George V School, Seremban">King George V School</a> (Seremban) and <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_Institution,_Malaysia" class="mw-redirect" title="St. George&#39;s Institution, Malaysia">St George\'s Institution</a> (Taiping). Mission schools set up by various Christian denominations paralleled the selective state school albeit not with the same degree of selectivity. Notable mission schools include the <a href="/wiki/La_Sallian_educational_institutions#Malaysia" class="mw-redirect" title="La Sallian educational institutions">La Sallian family of schools</a> and those set up by the <a href="/wiki/Marist_Brothers" title="Marist Brothers">Marist Brothers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> missionaries. Before the 1970s when Malay was made the medium of instruction, many such selective schools became known for providing excellent English-medium education and have produced many notable alumni, including the current <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Malaysia" title="Prime Minister of Malaysia">Prime Minister of Malaysia</a> <a href="/wiki/Najib_Tun_Razak" class="mw-redirect" title="Najib Tun class="mw-headline" id="Singapore">Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar_school&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" The English, and later, the Scottish, set up cathedrals, churches, and elite grammar schools for the upper class. Initially, this was a racially segregated colonial system.\n</p><p>The British then opened up their schools to children from English mixed marriages, or to those with English descent. During the late colonial period, these schools expanded and also schooled descendants of the very few mixed English and Straits-Chinese families, and descendants of some rich Straits-born Chinese merchant class families, who were educated in Oxbridge or had London trade ties. Initially these schools were run exactly like their counterparts in England, and taught by the English.\n</p><p>The oldest of these, <a href="/wiki/Raffles_Institution" title="Raffles Institution">Raffles Institution</a>, was founded in 1823 by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Stamford_Raffles" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Stamford Raffles">Sir Stamford Raffles</a> to educate sons born and living in the Crown Colony, often to fathers in Crown colonial service. Single-sex classes were set up for daughters of the British ruling class in 1844, and later became <a href="/wiki/Raffles_Girls%27_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Raffles Girls&#39; School">Raffles Girls\' School</a>, officially founded in 1879. After independence it became the Raffles Girls\' School (Primary School), distinct from the branch school established by the local government after independence, the Raffles Girls\' School (Secondary School).\n</p><p>The French Roman Catholic Orders later opened up their ministries and boarding schools to children of mixed marriages, and racial segregation was also relaxed to some extent in the English schools. The French Catholic missions and schools, but not the English schools, also accepted orphans, foundlings, and illegitimate children abandoned by mothers ostracized for breaching racial purity laws.\n</p><p>The CHIJMES building has commemorative plaques for these abandoned babies. The children were termed "children of God" and raised as Catholics. When laws banning polygamy became strictly enforced in Singapore after 1965, these schools extended their English-speaking classes to girls from families of any socio-economic background.\n</p><p>The British grammar schools in Singapore imitated much of this education system, with Catholicism replaced by Anglicanism. Later the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_High_School,_Singapore" title="Anglican High School, Singapore">Anglican High School</a> of Singapore was set up.\n</p><p>Australian Methodist missionaries started UK-style grammar schools with American and British Methodist church funds. They founded the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Chinese_School" title="Anglo-Chinese School">Anglo-Chinese School</a> (1886) and <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Girls%27_School,_Singapore" title="Methodist Girls&#39; School, Singapore">Methodist Girls\' School</a> (1887).\n</p><p>When Singapore became independent from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the Singapore government established publicly funded bilingual schools based on the existing grammar school system. Since the 1960s their mission was to provide a high-quality elite education to the top 10% in national annual IQ tests, regardless of socio-economic background. These bilingual schools were influenced by the US educational system, and termed "high schools" rather than "grammar schools". Other, less elitist, state schools were called simply "secondary schools", similar to the UK equivalent of "comprehensive schools". High schools include <a href="/wiki/Dunman_High_School" title="Dunman High School">Dunman High School</a> (co-educational), <a href="/wiki/Nanyang_Girls%27_High_School" title="Nanyang Girls&#39; High School">Nanyang Girls\' High School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maris_Stella_High_School" title="Maris Stella High School">Maris Stella High School</a> (for boys only) and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_High_School,_Singapore" title="Catholic High School, Singapore">Catholic High School</a> (all-boys). Within these schools there are academically top classes, the very competitive Scholars\' class or "A" class. Graduates tend to become part of the upper class. These schools recruit pupils worldwide, particularly from large emerging Asian economies. Recruitment is carried out under the auspices of the Special Assistant Plan Scholars programme, the ASEAN Scholars programme, and the India and China Scholars programmes. Gifted pupils, such as winners of Mathematical Olympiads, international violin and piano competitions, Physics Olympiads and child inventors are particularly sought after.\n</p><p>In addition to religious missions and the new high schools, the less selective <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Chinese_Girls%27_School" title="Singapore Chinese Girls&#39; School">Singapore Chinese Girls\' School</a> was set up by several <a href="/wiki/Peranakan" title="Peranakan">Peranakan</a> business and community leaders.\n</p><p>The Ministry of Education published annual rankings, but discontinued them after criticism of excessive academic stress placed on schoolchildren, some of whom committed suicide in response to perceived failure.\n</p><p>After the 1990s all schools were integrated into a unified national school system, but the elite schools distinguished themselves by descriptions such as "independent" or "autonomous".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United School</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Grammar schools on the English and later British models were founded during the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirteen colonies">colonial period</a>, the first being the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Latin_School" title="Boston Latin School">Boston Latin School</a>, founded as the Latin Grammar School in 1635.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\nIn 1647 the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony" title="Massachusetts Bay Colony">Massachusetts Bay Colony</a> enacted the <a href="/wiki/Old_Deluder_Satan_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Deluder Satan Law">Old Deluder Satan Law</a>, requiring any township of at least 100 households to establish a grammar school, and similar laws followed in the other <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> colonies. These schools initially taught young men the classical languages as a preparation for university, but by the mid-18th century many had broadened their curricula to include practical subjects. Nevertheless, they declined in popularity owing to competition from the more practical academies.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorn_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorn-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The name "grammar school" was adopted by public schools for children from 10 to 14 years of age, following a primary stage from 5 to 9 years of age.  These types were combined around 1900 to form <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school_(United_States)" title="Elementary school (United States)">elementary schools</a>, which were also known as "grammar schools".<sup id="cite_ref-Dorn_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorn-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An analogous concept to the contemporary English grammar school is the <a href="/wiki/Magnet_school" title="Magnet school">magnet school</a>, a For the purpose of this article we will define Grammar School as one that follows the academic and cultural traditions established in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. As such those schools which came under the authority of the Auckland Grammar Schools\xe2\x80\x99 Board are included, and schools such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Roskill_Grammar_School" title="Mount Roskill Grammar School">Mount Roskill Grammar School</a>, established in 1954, are excluded. Auckland\'s Grammar Schools share a common line of descent from the British Grammar School system, with a strong focus on academics and traditional British sports. These schools used entry assessments and selected academic students from across the <a href="/wiki/Realm_of_New_Zealand" title="Realm of New Zealand">Realm of New Zealand</a>. The schools mentioned below all share the school motto: "Per Angusta Ad Augusta". They also share the same icon/logo, the "Auckland Grammar Lion". Today, all Grammar Schools in New Zealand are state schools, although they use school donations to supplement their government funding.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> did not establish a state education system until 1877. The absence of a national education system meant that the first sizable secondary education providers were Grammar Schools and other private institutions.  The first Grammar School in New Zealand, <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Grammar_School" title="Auckland Grammar School">Auckland Grammar School</a>, was established in 1850 and formally recognised as an educational establishment in 1868 through the Auckland Grammar School Appropriation Act. In 1888 <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Girls%27_Grammar_School" title="Auckland Girls&#39; Grammar School">Auckland Girls\' Grammar School</a> was established. In 1917 Auckland Grammar School\'s sister school was established, <a href="/wiki/Epsom_Girls%27_Grammar_School" title="Epsom Girls&#39; Grammar School">Epsom Girls\' Grammar School</a>. In 1922 <a href="/wiki/Mount_Albert_Grammar_School" title="Mount Albert Grammar School">Mount Albert Grammar School</a> was established as part of Auckland Grammar School. Since then the two schools have become separate institutions. <a href="/wiki/Takapuna_Grammar_School" title="Takapuna Grammar School">Takapuna Grammar School</a> was established in 1927 and was the first co-educational Grammar School to be established in New Zealand.\n</p><p>The term "Grammar School" does not entail special status within the New Zealand education system.
