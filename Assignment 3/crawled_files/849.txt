white-space:nowrap"><span class="fn org">Highlands</span><br /><span class="nickname"><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">A\' Gh\xc3\xa0idhealtachd</i>&#160;&#160;<span class="languageicon" style="font-size:81%;font-weight:normal;">(<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>)</span><br /><i lang="sco" title="Scots language text">Hielans</i>&#160;&#160;<span class="languageicon" style="font-size:81%;font-weight:normal;">(<a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>)</span></span></th></tr><tr class="mergedtoprow"><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center">\n<a href="/wiki/File:Scottish_Highlands_and_Lowlands.png" class="image" title="Lowland\xe2\x80\x93Highland divide"><img alt="Lowland\xe2\x80\x93Highland divide" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Scottish_Highlands_and_Lowlands.png/250px-Scottish_Highlands_and_Lowlands.png" width="250" height="329" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Scottish_Highlands_and_Lowlands.png/375px-Scottish_Highlands_and_Lowlands.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Scottish_Highlands_and_Lowlands.png/500px-Scottish_Highlands_and_Lowlands.png 2x" data-file-width="584" data-file-height="768" /></a><div><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowland</a>\xe2\x80\x93Highland divide</div></td></tr><tr class="mergedtoprow"><th scope="row"><a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">Demonym(s)</a></th><td>\nHighlander</td></tr><tr class="mergedtoprow"><th scope="row"><a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">Time zone</a></th><td>\nGMT/BST</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Highlands</b> (<a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>: <i lang="sco">the Hielands</i>; <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <i lang="gd">A\xe2\x80\x99 Gh\xc3\xa0idhealtachd</i> <small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA/Scottish Gaelic">[\xc9\x99 \xc9\xa3\xc9\x9b\xcb\x90\xc9\x99l\xcc\xaa\xcb\xa0t\xcc\xaa\xca\xb0\xc9\x99xk]</a></span>, "the place of the <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaels</a>") is a historic region of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Culturally, the Highlands and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowlands</a> diverged from the later <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Modern_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern period">modern period</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Lowland Scots</a> replaced <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> throughout most of the Lowlands. The term is also used for the area north and west of the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Boundary Fault</a>, although the exact boundaries are not clearly defined, particularly to the east. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Glen" title="Great Glen">Great Glen</a> divides the <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Mountains" title="Grampian Mountains">Grampian Mountains</a> to the southeast from the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Highlands" title="Northwest Highlands">Northwest Highlands</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> name of <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text"><a href="/wiki/A%27_Gh%C3%A0idhealtachd" class="mw-redirect" title="A&#39; Gh\xc3\xa0idhealtachd">A\' Gh\xc3\xa0idhealtachd</a></i></i> literally means "the place of the Gaels" and traditionally, from a Gaelic-speaking point of view, includes both the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a> and the Highlands.\n</p><p>The area is very sparsely populated, with many <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain ranges</a> dominating the region, and includes the highest mountain in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nevis" title="Ben Nevis">Ben Nevis</a>. Before the 19th century the Highlands was home to a much larger population, but from <i>circa</i> 1841 and for the next 160 years, the natural increase in population was exceeded by emigration (mostly to Canada, the USA and Australia) and migration to the industrial cities of Scotland and England.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_2013_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>xxiii, 414 and <i>passim</i></span></sup> The area is now one of the most sparsely populated in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. At 9.1 per km<sup>2</sup> in 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-density_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-density-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> in the Highlands and Islands is less than one seventh of Scotland\'s as a whole,<sup id="cite_ref-density_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-density-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> comparable with that of <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Highland_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Highland Council">Highland Council</a> is the administrative body for much of the Highlands, with its administrative centre at <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a>. However, the Highlands also includes parts of the <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Scotland" title="Subdivisions of Scotland">council areas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angus,_Scotland" title="Angus, Scotland">Angus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Bute" title="Argyll and Bute">Argyll and Bute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Ayrshire" title="North Ayrshire">North Ayrshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perth_and_Kinross" title="Perth and Kinross">Perth and Kinross</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Stirling_(council_area)" title="Stirling (council area)">Stirling</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Dunbartonshire" title="West Dunbartonshire">West Dunbartonshire</a>. \n</p><p>The Scottish highlands is the only area in the British Isles to have the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> biome as it features concentrated populations of <a href="/wiki/Scots_pine" title="Scots pine">Scots pine</a> forest: see <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Forest" title="Caledonian Forest">Caledonian Forest</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" Highlanders</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Between the 15th century and the 20th century, the area differed from most of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowlands</a> in terms of language. In Scottish Gaelic, the region is known as the <i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text"><a href="/wiki/G%C3%A0idhealtachd" title="G\xc3\xa0idhealtachd">G\xc3\xa0idhealtachd</a></i></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> because it was traditionally the Gaelic-speaking part of Scotland, although the language is now largely confined to <a href="/wiki/The_Hebrides" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hebrides">The Hebrides</a>. The terms are sometimes used interchangeably but have different meanings in their respective languages. <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">Scottish English</a> (in its <a href="/wiki/Highland_English" title="Highland English">Highland form</a>) is the predominant language of the area today, though Highland English has been influenced by Gaelic speech to a significant extent.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Historically, the "Highland line" distinguished the two Scottish cultures. While the Highland line broadly followed the geography of the Grampians in the south, it continued in the north, cutting off the north-eastern areas, that is <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, from the more Gaelic Highlands and Hebrides.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> risings, the British government enacted a series of laws to try to speed up the destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_clan" title="Scottish clan">clan system</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Disarming_Act" title="Disarming Act">bans on the bearing of arms</a> and the wearing of <a href="/wiki/Tartan" title="Tartan">tartan</a>, and limitations on the activities of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Episcopal_Church" title="Scottish Episcopal Church">Scottish Episcopal Church</a>. Most of this legislation was repealed by the end of the 18th century as the Jacobite threat subsided. There was soon a rehabilitation of Highland culture. Tartan was adopted for Highland regiments in the British Army, which poor Highlanders joined in large numbers in the era of the Revolutionary and <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> (1790\xe2\x80\x931815). Tartan had largely been abandoned by the ordinary people of the region, but in the 1820s, tartan and the <a href="/wiki/Kilt" title="Kilt">kilt</a> were adopted by members of the social elite, not just in Scotland, but across Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts2002pp193-4_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts2002pp193-4-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sievers2007_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sievers2007-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The international craze for tartan, and for idealising a romanticised Highlands, was set off by the <a href="/wiki/Ossian" title="Ossian">Ossian</a> cycle,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and further popularised by the works of <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>. His "staging" of the <a href="/wiki/Visit_of_King_George_IV_to_Scotland" title="Visit of King George IV to Scotland">visit of King George IV to Scotland</a> in 1822 and the king\'s wearing of tartan resulted in a massive upsurge in demand for kilts and tartans that could not be met by the Scottish woollen industry. Individual clan tartans were largely designated in this period and they became a major symbol of Scottish identity.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This "Highlandism", by which all of Scotland was identified with the culture of the Highlands, was cemented by Queen Victoria\'s interest in the country, her adoption of <a href="/wiki/Balmoral_Castle" title="Balmoral Castle">Balmoral</a> as a major royal retreat, and her interest in "tartenry".<sup id="cite_ref-Sievers2007_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sievers2007-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Highlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Highlands before 1800 were very poor and traditional, and were not much affected by the uplift of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> that was sweeping the Lowlands of Scotland. The period of the Napoleonic wars brought prosperity, optimism, and economic growth to the Highlands. The economy grew thanks to wages paid in industries such as kelping (in which <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> was burned for the useful chemicals obtained from the ashes), fisheries, and weaving, as well as large-scale infrastructure spending such as the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Canal" title="Caledonian Canal">Caledonian Canal</a> project.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This source seems to be a travel blog by someone with no particular knowledge of the subject. (September 2018)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On the East Coast, farmlands were improved, and high prices for cattle brought money to the area. Service in the Army was also attractive to young men from the Highlands, who sent pay home and retired there with their army pensions.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This prosperity ended after 1815, and long-term negative factors began to undermine the economic position of the poor tenant farmers, who typically rented a few <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a>, and were known as <a href="/wiki/Crofter" class="mw-redirect" title="Crofter">crofters</a>. Landowners were increasingly market-oriented in the century after 1750, and this tended to dissolve the traditional social and economic structure of the North-West Highlands and the Hebrides, causing great disruption for the crofters. The <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">Highland Clearances</a> and the end of the township system followed changes in land ownership and tenancy and the replacement of cattle by sheep.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Great Irish Famine</a> of the 1840s was caused by a plant disease that reached the Highlands in 1846, causing great distress. \n</p><p>The unequal concentration of land ownership remained an emotional and controversial subject, of enormous importance to the Highland economy, and eventually became a cornerstone of liberal radicalism. The poor crofters were politically powerless, and many of them turned to religion. They embraced the popularly oriented, fervently evangelical Presbyterian revival after 1800.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Most joined the breakaway "Free Church" after 1843. This <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">evangelical</a> movement was led by lay preachers who themselves came from the lower strata, and whose preaching was implicitly critical of the established order. The religious change energised the crofters and separated them from the landlords; it helped prepare them for their successful and violent challenge to the landlords in the 1880s through the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Land_League" title="Highland Land League">Highland Land League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\nViolence erupted, starting on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Skye" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Skye">Isle of Skye</a>, when Highland landlords cleared their lands for sheep and deer parks. It was quietened when the government stepped in, passing the <a href="/wiki/Crofters%27_Holdings_(Scotland)_Act,_1886" class="mw-redirect" title="Crofters&#39; Holdings (Scotland) Act, 1886">Crofters\' Holdings (Scotland) Act, 1886</a> to reduce rents, guarantee fixity of tenure, and break up large estates to provide crofts for the homeless.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This contrasted with the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Land_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Land War">Irish Land War</a> under way at the same time, where the Irish were intensely politicised through roots in Irish nationalism, while political dimensions were limited. In 1885 three Independent Crofter candidates were elected to Parliament, which listened to their pleas. The results included explicit security for the Scottish smallholders; the legal right to bequeath tenancies to descendants; and the creation of a Crofting Commission. The Crofters as a political movement faded away by 1892, and the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> gained their votes.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Highlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loch_Long.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Loch_Long.jpg/220px-Loch_Long.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Loch_Long.jpg/330px-Loch_Long.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Loch_Long.jpg/440px-Loch_Long.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loch_Long.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Loch_Long" title="Loch Long">Loch Long</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Scottish Reformation</a> achieved partial success in the Highlands. Roman Catholicism remained strong in some areas, owing to remote locations and the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> missionaries from Ireland, who regularly came to celebrate <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a>. Although the presence of Roman Catholicism has faded, there remain significant Catholic strongholds within the Highlands and Islands such as <a href="/wiki/Moidart" title="Moidart">Moidart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morar" title="Morar">Morar</a> on the mainland and <a href="/wiki/South_Uist" title="South Uist">South Uist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barra" title="Barra">Barra</a> in the southern Outer Hebrides. \nThe remoteness of the region and the lack of a Gaelic-speaking clergy undermined the missionary efforts of the established church. The later 18th century saw somewhat greater success, owing to the efforts of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_SPCK" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish SPCK">SSPCK</a> missionaries and to the disruption of traditional society after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Culloden" title="Battle of Culloden">Battle of Culloden</a> in 1746. In the 19th century, the evangelical Free Churches, which were more accepting of Gaelic language and culture, grew rapidly, appealing much more strongly than did the established church.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For the most part, however, the Highlands are considered predominantly Protestant, loyal to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a>. In contrast to the Catholic southern islands, the northern <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a> islands (Lewis, Harris and North Uist) have an exceptionally high proportion of their population belonging to the Protestant <a href="/wiki/Free_Church_of_Scotland_(post_1900)" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Church of Scotland (post 1900)">Free Church of Scotland</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Free_Presbyterian_Church_of_Scotland" title="Free Presbyterian Church of Scotland">Free Presbyterian Church of Scotland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a> have been described as the last bastion of <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> in Britain<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and the Sabbath remains widely observed. Inverness and the surrounding area has a majority Protestant population, with most locals belonging to either <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">The Kirk</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Free_Church_of_Scotland_(post_1900)" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Church of Scotland (post 1900)">Free Church of Scotland</a>. The church maintains a noticeable presence within the area, with church attendance notably higher than in other Scottish cities. Religion continues to play an important role in Highland culture, with Sabbath observance still widely practised, particularly in the Hebrides.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_geography">Historical geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span the administrative centre and traditional capital of the Highlands</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Allt_a%27_Mhuilinn.JPG" the Allt a\' Mhuilinn</div></div></div>\n<p>In traditional Scottish <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, the Highlands refers to that part of Scotland north-west of the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Boundary Fault</a>, which crosses mainland Scotland in a near-straight line from <a href="/wiki/Helensburgh" title="Helensburgh">Helensburgh</a> to <a href="/wiki/Stonehaven" title="Stonehaven">Stonehaven</a>. However the flat coastal lands that occupy parts of the counties of <a href="/wiki/Nairnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Nairnshire">Nairnshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morayshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Morayshire">Morayshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banffshire" title="Banffshire">Banffshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire_(historic)" title="Aberdeenshire (historic)">Aberdeenshire</a> are often excluded as they do not share the distinctive geographical and cultural features of the rest of the Highlands. The north-east of <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, are also often excluded from the Highlands, although the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> are usually included. The Highland area, as so defined, differed from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowlands</a> in language and tradition, having preserved <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> speech and customs centuries after the <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicisation</a> of the latter; this led to a growing perception of a divide, with the cultural distinction between Highlander and Lowlander first noted towards the end of the 14th century. In <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>, the boundary between the Highlands and the Lowlands is not well defined. There is a stone beside the <a href="/wiki/A93_road" title="A93 road">A93 road</a> near the village of <a href="/wiki/Dinnet" title="Dinnet">Dinnet</a> on <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Aberdeenshire" title="River Dee, Aberdeenshire">Royal Deeside</a> which states \'You are now in the Highlands\', although there are areas of Highland character to the east of this point.\n</p><p>A much wider definition of the Highlands is that used by the <a href="/wiki/Scotch_Whisky" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotch Whisky">Scotch Whisky</a> industry. <a href="/wiki/Highland_Single_Malts" class="mw-redirect" title="Highland Single Malts">Highland Single Malts</a> are produced at distilleries north of an imaginary line between <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> thus including all of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angus,_Scotland" title="Angus, Scotland">Angus</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> is traditionally regarded as the capital of the Highlands,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> although less so in the Highland parts of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angus,_Scotland" title="Angus, Scotland">Angus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perthshire" title="Perthshire">Perthshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stirlingshire" title="Stirlingshire">Stirlingshire</a> which look more to <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a> as their commercial centres. Under some of the wider definitions in use, <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> could be considered the largest city in the Highlands, although it does not share the recent Gaelic cultural history typical of the Highlands proper.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highland_Council_area">Highland Council area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Highlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Highland Council area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Highland_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Highland Council">Highland Council</a> area, created as one of the local government <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Scotland" title="Subdivisions of Scotland">regions of Scotland</a>, has been a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary council</a> area since 1996. The council area excludes a large area of the southern and eastern Highlands, and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>, but includes <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>. <i>Highlands</i> is sometimes used, however, as a name for the council area, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Highlands and Islands Fire and Rescue Service">Highlands and Islands Fire and Rescue Service</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Scotland" title="Northern Scotland">Northern</a></i>, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Constabulary" title="Northern Constabulary">Northern Constabulary</a></i>, is also used to refer to the area covered by the fire and rescue service. This area consists of the Highland council area and the <a href="/wiki/Island_council_areas_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Island council areas of Scotland">island council areas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> and the Western Isles.\n</p><p>Highland Council signs in the <a href="/wiki/Pass_of_Drumochter" title="Pass of Drumochter">Pass of Drumochter</a>, between <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glen_Garry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Glen Garry (page does not exist)">Glen Garry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dalwhinnie" title="Dalwhinnie">Dalwhinnie</a>, say "Welcome to the Highlands".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highlands_and_Islands">Highlands and Islands</span><span and Islands">Highlands and Islands</a> area. An <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_constituencies_and_regions" title="Scottish Parliament constituencies and regions">electoral region</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="Highlands and Islands (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">Highlands and Islands</a></i> is used in elections to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>: this area includes <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, as well as the Highland Council local government area, the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a> and most of the <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Bute" title="Argyll and Bute">Argyll and Bute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a> local government areas. <i>Highlands and Islands</i> has, however, different meanings in different contexts. It means Highland (the local government area), Orkney, Shetland, and the Western Isles in <i><a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Highlands and Islands Fire and Rescue Service">Highlands and Islands Fire and Rescue Service</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Scotland" title="Northern Scotland">Northern</a></i>, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Constabulary" title="Northern Constabulary">Northern Constabulary</a></i>, refers to the same area as that covered by the fire and rescue service.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_crossings">Historical crossings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Highlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historical crossings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There have been <a href="/wiki/Trackway" title="Trackway">trackways</a> from the Lowlands to the Highlands since <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> times. Many traverse the <a href="/wiki/Mounth" title="Mounth">Mounth</a>, a spur of mountainous land that extends from the higher inland range to the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> slightly north of <a href="/wiki/Stonehaven" title="Stonehaven">Stonehaven</a>. The most well-known and historically important trackways are the <a href="/wiki/Causey_Mounth" title="Causey Mounth">Causey Mounth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elsick_Mounth" title="Elsick Mounth">Elsick Mounth</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryne_Corse_Mounth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cryne Corse Mounth (page does not exist)">Cryne Corse Mounth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairnamounth" class="mw-redirect" title="Cairnamounth">Cairnamounth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Courier_delivery">Courier delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Highlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Courier delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although most of the Highlands is geographically on the British mainland, it is somewhat less accessible than the rest of Britain; thus most UK couriers categorise it separately, alongside <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and other offshore islands. They thus charge additional fees for delivery to the Highlands, or exclude the area entirely. Whilst the physical remoteness from the largest population centres inevitably leads to higher transit cost, there is confusion and consternation over the scale of the fees charged and the effectiveness of their communication,<sup id="cite_ref-CABcouriers_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CABcouriers-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and the use of the word Mainland in their justification. Since the charges are often based on postcode areas, many far less remote areas, including some which are traditionally considered part of the lowlands, are also subject to these charges.<sup id="cite_ref-CABcouriers_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CABcouriers-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Royal Mail</a> is the only delivery network bound by a Universal Service Obligation to charge a uniform tariff across the UK. This, however, applies only to mail items and not larger packages which are dealt with by its <a href="/wiki/Parcelforce" title="Parcelforce">Parcelforce</a> division.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Highlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg/220px-Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg/330px-Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg/440px-Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1692" data-file-height="1204" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Liathach_from_Beinn_Eighe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Liathach seen from <a href="/wiki/Beinn_Eighe" title="Beinn Eighe">Beinn Eighe</a>. With the Munro "Top" of Stuc a\' Choire Dhuibh Bhig 915 m (3,001 ft) in the foreground and the two Munro summits in the background.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg/220px-Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg/330px-Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg/440px-Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3006" data-file-height="2026" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Main_ridge_of_the_cuillin_in_skye_arp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The main ridge of the <a href="/wiki/Cuillin" title="Cuillin">Cuillin</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The Highlands lie to the north and west of the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Boundary Fault</a>, which runs from <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a> to <a href="/wiki/Stonehaven" title="Stonehaven">Stonehaven</a>. This part of Scotland is largely composed of ancient rocks from the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a> periods which were <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">uplifted</a> during the later <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Orogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Caledonian Orogeny">Caledonian Orogeny</a>. Smaller formations of <a href="/wiki/Lewisian_complex" title="Lewisian complex">Lewisian gneiss</a> in the northwest are up to 3 billion years old. The overlying rocks of the <a href="/wiki/Torridonian" title="Torridonian">Torridon Sandstone</a> form mountains in the <a href="/wiki/Torridon_Hills" title="Torridon Hills">Torridon Hills</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Liathach" title="Liathach">Liathach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beinn_Eighe" title="Beinn Eighe">Beinn Eighe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wester_Ross" title="Wester Ross">Wester Ross</a>.\n</p><p>These foundations are interspersed with many <a href="/wiki/Igneous" class="mw-redirect" title="Igneous">igneous</a> intrusions of a more recent age, the remnants of which have formed mountain <a href="/wiki/Massif" title="Massif">massifs</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Cairngorms" title="Cairngorms">Cairngorms</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cuillin" title="Cuillin">Cuillin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Skye" title="Skye">Skye</a>. A significant exception to the above are the fossil-bearing beds of <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a> found principally along the <a href="/wiki/Moray_Firth" title="Moray Firth">Moray Firth</a> coast and partially down the Highland Boundary Fault. The <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> beds found in isolated locations on <a href="/wiki/Skye" title="Skye">Skye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Applecross" title="Applecross">Applecross</a> reflect the complex underlying geology. They are the original source of much <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_oil" title="North Sea oil">North Sea oil</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Glen" title="Great Glen">Great Glen</a> is formed along a <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform fault</a> which divides the <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Mountains" title="Grampian Mountains">Grampian Mountains</a> to the southeast from the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Highlands" title="Northwest Highlands">Northwest Highlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Keay_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keay-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The entire region was covered by ice sheets during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> ice ages, save perhaps for a few <a href="/wiki/Nunatak" title="Nunatak">nunataks</a>. The complex <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> includes incised valleys and <a href="/wiki/Loch" title="Loch">lochs</a> carved by the action of mountain streams and ice, and a <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> of irregularly distributed mountains whose summits have similar heights above sea-level, but whose bases depend upon the amount of <a href="/wiki/Denudation" title="Denudation">denudation</a> to which the plateau has been subjected in various places.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_of_interest">Places of
