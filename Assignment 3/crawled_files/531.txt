class="mw-redirect" title="Member of the Order of Merit">OM</a></span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/\xc3\xa6/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">\xc3\xa6</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 9 May 1860&#160;&#8211;&#32; 19 June 1937) was a Scottish novelist and playwright, best remembered today as the creator of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan" title="Peter Pan">Peter Pan</a>. He was born and educated in Scotland and then moved to London, where he wrote a number of successful novels and plays. There he met the <a href="/wiki/Llewelyn_Davies_boys" title="Llewelyn Davies boys">Llewelyn Davies boys</a>, who inspired him to write about a baby boy who has magical adventures in <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gardens" title="Kensington Gardens">Kensington Gardens</a> (included in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_White_Bird" title="The Little White Bird">The Little White Bird</a></i>), then to write <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan,_or_The_Boy_Who_Wouldn%27t_Grow_Up" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Pan, or The Boy Who Wouldn&#39;t Grow Up">Peter Pan, or The Boy Who Wouldn\'t Grow Up</a></i>, a "fairy play" about an ageless boy and an ordinary girl named Wendy who have adventures in the fantasy setting of <a href="/wiki/Neverland" title="Neverland">Neverland</a>.\n</p><p>Although he continued to write successfully, <i>Peter Pan</i> overshadowed his other work, and is credited with popularising the name <a href="/wiki/Wendy" title="Wendy">Wendy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Barrie unofficially adopted the Davies boys following the deaths of their parents. Barrie was made a <a href="/wiki/Baronet" title="Baronet">baronet</a> by <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> on 14 June 1913,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit_(Commonwealth)" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Merit (Commonwealth)">Order of Merit</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1922_New_Year_Honours" title="1922 New Year Honours">1922 New Year Honours</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Before his death, he gave the rights to the Peter Pan works to <a href="/wiki/Great_Ormond_Street_Hospital_for_Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Ormond Street Hospital for Children">Great Ormond Street Hospital for Children</a> in London, which continues to benefit from them.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" and adolescence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._Barrie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood and adolescence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>James Matthew Barrie was born in <a href="/wiki/Kirriemuir" title="Kirriemuir">Kirriemuir</a>, Angus, to a conservative <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinist</a> family. His father David Barrie was a modestly successful weaver. His mother Margaret Ogilvy assumed her deceased mother\'s household responsibilities at the age of eight. Barrie was the ninth child of ten (two of whom died before he was born), all of whom were schooled in at least <a href="/wiki/The_three_Rs" title="The three Rs">the three Rs</a> in preparation for possible professional careers. His siblings were: Alexander (1842 \xe2\x80\x93 16 July 1914), Mary Ann (1845\xe2\x80\x931918), Jane (14 March 1847 \xe2\x80\x93 31 August 1895), Elizabeth (12 March 1849 \xe2\x80\x93 1 April 1851), Agnes (23 Dec 1850 \xe2\x80\x93 1851), David Ogilvy (30 January 1853 \xe2\x80\x93 29 January 1867), Sarah (3 June 1855  \xe2\x80\x931 November 1913), Isabella (4 January 1858 \xe2\x80\x93 1902) and Margaret (9 July 1863 \xe2\x80\x93 1936).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He was a small child and drew attention to himself with storytelling.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He only grew to 5&#160;ft 3&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> in. (161&#160;cm) according to his 1934 passport.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When he was 6 years old, Barrie\'s next-older brother David (his mother\'s favourite) died the day before his 14th birthday in an ice-skating accident.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This left his mother devastated, and Barrie tried to fill David\'s place in his mother\'s attentions, even wearing David\'s clothes and whistling in the manner that he did. One time, Barrie entered her room and heard her say, "Is that you?" "I thought it was the dead boy she was speaking to", wrote Barrie in his biographical account of his mother <i>Margaret Ogilvy</i> (1896) "and I said in a little lonely voice, \'No, it\'s no\' him, it\'s just me.<span style="padding-right:.2em;">\'</span>" Barrie\'s mother found comfort in the fact that her dead son would remain a boy forever, never to grow up and leave her.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkin_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Eventually, Barrie and his mother entertained each other with stories of her brief childhood and books such as <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe" title="Robinson Crusoe">Robinson Crusoe</a></i>, works by fellow Scotsman <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pilgrim%27s_Progress" title="The Pilgrim&#39;s Progress">The Pilgrim\'s Progress</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chaney_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaney-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the age of 8, Barrie was sent to <a href="/wiki/The_Glasgow_Academy" title="The Glasgow Academy">the Glasgow Academy</a> in the care of his eldest siblings Alexander and Mary Ann, who taught at the school. When he was 10, he returned home and continued his education at the <a href="/wiki/Forfar_Academy" title="Forfar Academy">Forfar Academy</a>. At 14, he left home for <a href="/wiki/Dumfries_Academy" title="Dumfries Academy">Dumfries Academy</a>, again under the watch of Alexander and Mary Ann. He became a voracious reader, and was fond of <a href="/wiki/Penny_Dreadful" class="mw-redirect" title="Penny Dreadful">Penny Dreadfuls</a> and the works of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Michael_Ballantyne" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Michael Ballantyne">Robert Michael Ballantyne</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Fenimore_Cooper" title="James Fenimore Cooper">James Fenimore Cooper</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Dumfries" title="Dumfries">Dumfries</a>, he and his friends spent time in the garden of <a href="/wiki/Moat_Brae" title="Moat Brae">Moat Brae</a> house, playing pirates "in a sort of Odyssey that was long afterwards to become the play of <i>Peter Pan</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-Speeches_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speeches-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They formed a drama club, producing his first play <i>Bandelero the Bandit</i>, which provoked a minor controversy following a scathing moral denunciation from a clergyman on the school\'s governing board.<sup id="cite_ref-Chaney_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaney-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_career">Literary career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._Barrie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg/220px-PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg" width="220" height="410" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg/330px-PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg/440px-PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg 2x" data-file-width="529" data-file-height="985" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PeterPan_Statue_Londres.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan_statue" title="Peter Pan statue">Peter Pan statue</a> by Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Frampton" title="George Frampton">George Frampton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gardens" title="Kensington Gardens">Kensington Gardens</a>, London</div></div></div>\n<p>Barrie knew that he wished to follow a career as an author. However, his family attempted to persuade him to choose a profession such as the ministry. With advice from Alexander, he was able to work out a compromise: he would attend a university, but would study literature.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Barrie enrolled at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> where he wrote drama reviews for the <i><a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Courant" title="Edinburgh Courant">Edinburgh Evening Courant</a></i>. He graduated and obtained an M.A. on 21 April 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following a job advertisement found by his sister in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scotsman" title="The Scotsman">The Scotsman</a></i>, he worked for a year and a half as a staff journalist on the <i><a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Journal" title="Nottingham Journal">Nottingham Journal</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He then returned to Kirriemuir. He submitted a piece to the <i>St. James\'s Gazette</i>, a London newspaper, using his mother\'s stories about the town where she grew up (renamed "Thrums"). The editor "liked that Scotch thing" so well that Barrie ended up writing a series of these stories.<sup id="cite_ref-Chaney_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaney-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They served as the basis for his first novels: <i>Auld Licht Idylls</i> (1888), <i>A Window in Thrums</i> (1890),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and <i>The Little Minister</i> (1891).\n</p><p>The stories depicted the "Auld Lichts", a strict religious sect to which his grandfather had once belonged.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Modern literary criticism of these early works has been unfavourable, tending to disparage them as sentimental and nostalgic depictions of a parochial Scotland, far from the realities of the industrialised nineteenth century, seen as characteristic of what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Kailyard_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Kailyard School">Kailyard School</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>. Despite, or perhaps because of, this, they were popular enough at the time to establish Barrie as a successful writer. Following that success, he published <i>Better Dead</i> (1888) privately and at his own expense, but it failed to sell.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> His two "Tommy" novels, <i>Sentimental Tommy</i> (1896) and <i>Tommy and Grizel</i> (1900), were about a boy and young man who clings to childish fantasy, with an unhappy ending.\n</p><p>Meanwhile, Barrie\'s attention turned increasingly to works for the theatre, beginning with a biography of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Savage_(poet)" title="Richard Savage (poet)">Richard Savage</a>, written by Barrie and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Brereton_Marriott_Watson" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Brereton Marriott Watson">H. B. Marriott Watson</a>; unfortunately, it was performed only once and critically panned.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He immediately followed this with <i>Ibsen\'s Ghost</i> (or <i>Toole Up-to-Date</i>)(1891),<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> of <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Henrik Ibsen</a>\'s dramas <i><a href="/wiki/Hedda_Gabler" title="Hedda Gabler">Hedda Gabler</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ghosts_(play)" title="Ghosts (play)">Ghosts</a></i>. <i>Ghosts</i> had been unlicensed in the UK until 1914,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but had created a sensation at the time from a single "club" performance.\n</p><p>The production of <i>Ibsen\'s Ghost</i> at <a href="/wiki/Toole%27s_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Toole&#39;s Theatre">Toole\'s Theatre</a> in London was seen by <a href="/wiki/William_Archer_(critic)" title="William Archer (critic)">William Archer</a>, the translator of Ibsen\'s works into English. Apparently comfortable with the parody, he enjoyed the humour of the play and recommended it to others. Barrie\'s third play <i>Walker, London</i> (1892) resulted in him being introduced to a young actress named Mary Ansell. He proposed to her and they were married on 9 July 1894. Barrie bought her a <a href="/wiki/Saint_Bernard_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Bernard (dog)">Saint Bernard</a> puppy, who played a part in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_White_Bird" title="The Little White Bird">The Little White Bird</a></i>. He used Ansell\'s given name for many characters in his novels.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Barrie also authored <i><a href="/wiki/Jane_Annie" title="Jane Annie">Jane Annie</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Comic_opera" title="Comic opera">comic opera</a> for <a href="/wiki/Richard_D%27Oyly_Carte" title="Richard D&#39;Oyly Carte">Richard D\'Oyly Carte</a> (1893), which unfortunately failed. He begged his friend <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a> to revise and finish it for him.\n</p><p>In 1901 and 1902, he had back-to-back successes; <i><a href="/wiki/Quality_Street_(play)" title="Quality Street (play)">Quality Street</a></i> was about a respectable, responsible <a href="/wiki/Old_maid" class="mw-redirect" title="Old maid">old maid</a> who poses as her own flirtatious niece to try to win the attention of a former suitor returned from the war. Following that, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Admirable_Crichton" title="The Admirable Crichton">The Admirable Crichton</a></i> was a critically acclaimed social commentary with elaborate staging, about an aristocratic family and their household servants shipwrecked on a desert island. Under these difficult circumstances, a male household member seems better suited to taking on the responsibilities of leadership than the lord of the manor.\n</p><p>The character of "Peter Pan" first appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_White_Bird" title="The Little White Bird">The Little White Bird</a></i>. The novel was published in the UK by <a href="/wiki/Hodder_%26_Stoughton" title="Hodder &amp; Stoughton">Hodder &amp; Stoughton</a> in 1902, and serialised in the US in the same year in <i><a href="/wiki/Scribner%27s_Magazine" title="Scribner&#39;s Magazine">Scribner\'s Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Barrie\'s more famous and enduring work <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan,_or_The_Boy_Who_Wouldn%27t_Grow_Up" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Pan, or The Boy Who Wouldn&#39;t Grow Up">Peter Pan, or The Boy Who Wouldn\'t Grow Up</a></i> had its first stage performance on 27 December 1904.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This play introduced audiences to the name <i>Wendy</i>; it was inspired by a young girl named <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Henley" title="Margaret Henley">Margaret Henley</a> who called Barrie "Friendy", but could not pronounce her <i>R</i>s very well. The <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury" title="Bloomsbury">Bloomsbury</a> scenes show the societal constraints of late Victorian and Edwardian middle class domestic reality, contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Neverland" title="Neverland">Neverland</a>, a world where morality is ambivalent. <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a> described the play as "ostensibly a holiday entertainment for children but really a play for grown-up people", suggesting deeper social metaphors at work in <i>Peter Pan</i>.\n</p><p>Barrie had a long string of successes on the stage after <i>Peter Pan</i>, many of which discuss social concerns, as Barrie continued to integrate his work and his beliefs. <i>The Twelve Pound Look</i> (1921) concerns a wife divorcing a peer and gaining an independent income. Other plays, such as <i>Mary Rose</i> (1920) and <i>Dear Brutus</i> (1917), revisit the idea of the ageless child and parallel worlds.\n</p><p>Barrie was involved in the 1909 and 1911 attempts to challenge the censorship of the theatre by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chamberlain" title="Lord Chamberlain">Lord Chamberlain</a>, along with a number of other playwrights.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1911, Barrie developed the <i>Peter Pan</i> play into the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_and_Wendy" title="Peter and Wendy">Peter and Wendy</a></i>. In April 1929, Barrie gave the <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> of the Peter Pan works to <a href="/wiki/Great_Ormond_Street_Hospital" title="Great Ormond Street Hospital">Great Ormond Street Hospital</a>, a leading children\'s hospital in London. The <a href="/wiki/Peter_and_Wendy#Copyright_status" title="Peter and Wendy">current status of the copyright</a> is somewhat complex.\n</p><p>His final play was <i>The Boy David</i> (1936), which dramatised the Biblical story of King <a href="/wiki/Saul" title="Saul">Saul</a> and the young <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a>. Like the role of Peter Pan, that of David was played by a woman, <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Bergner" title="Elisabeth Bergner">Elisabeth Bergner</a>, for whom Barrie wrote the play.<sup id="cite_ref-methuen_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-methuen-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_connections">Social connections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._Barrie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social connections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg/220px-Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg" width="220" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg/330px-Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg/440px-Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1125" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_James_M._Barrie.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sir James Barrie, around 1895.</div></div></div>\n<p>Barrie moved in literary circles and had many famous friends in addition to his professional collaborators. Novelist <a href="/wiki/George_Meredith" title="George Meredith">George Meredith</a> was an early social <a href="/wiki/Patronage" title="Patronage">patron</a>. He had a long correspondence with fellow Scot <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>, who lived in <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a> at the time, but the two never met in person. <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a> was his neighbour in London for several years, and once participated in a Western that Barrie scripted and filmed. <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a> was a friend of many years, and tried to intervene when Barrie\'s marriage fell apart. Barrie met <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a> through <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Clifford" title="Hugh Clifford">Hugh Clifford</a> while he was staying in London.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, Barrie sometimes stayed at <a href="/wiki/Stanway_House" title="Stanway House">Stanway House</a> near the village of <a href="/wiki/Stanway,_Gloucestershire" title="Stanway, Gloucestershire">Stanway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>. He paid for the pavilion at Stanway <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> ground. Barrie also founded an amateur cricket team for his friends. The people who played on the team at various times included such luminaries as <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/P._G._Wodehouse" title="P. G. Wodehouse">P. G. Wodehouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerome_K._Jerome" title="Jerome K. Jerome">Jerome K. Jerome</a>, <a href="/wiki/G._K._Chesterton" title="G. K. Chesterton">G. K. Chesterton</a>, <a href="/wiki/A._A._Milne" title="A. A. Milne">A. A. Milne</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._W._Hornung" title="E. W. Hornung">E. W. Hornung</a>, <a href="/wiki/A._E._W._Mason" title="A. E. W. Mason">A. E. W. Mason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Raleigh_(professor)" title="Walter Raleigh (professor)">Walter Raleigh</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._V._Lucas" title="E. V. Lucas">E. V. Lucas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Hewlett" title="Maurice Hewlett">Maurice Hewlett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owen_Seaman" title="Owen Seaman">Owen Seaman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Partridge" title="Bernard Partridge">Bernard Partridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augustine_Birrell" title="Augustine Birrell">Augustine Birrell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Du_Chaillu" title="Paul Du Chaillu">Paul Du Chaillu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Herbert_La_Thangue" title="Henry Herbert La Thangue">Henry Herbert La Thangue</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Cecil_Ives" title="George Cecil Ives">George Cecil Ives</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Llewelyn_Davies" title="George Llewelyn Davies">George Llewelyn Davies</a> (see below) and the son of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Tennyson" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfred Tennyson">Alfred Tennyson</a>. The team was called the <a href="/wiki/Allahakbarries" title="Allahakbarries">Allahakbarries</a>, under the mistaken belief that "Allah akbar" meant "Heaven help us" in Arabic (rather than "God is great").<sup id="cite_ref-Chaney_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaney-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Barrie befriended Africa explorer <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Thomson_(explorer)" title="Joseph Thomson (explorer)">Joseph Thomson</a> and Antarctica explorer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">Robert Falcon Scott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He was <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godfather</a> to Scott\'s son <a href="/wiki/Peter_Scott" title="Peter Scott">Peter</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Chaney_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaney-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and was one of the seven people to whom Scott wrote letters in the final hours of his life during his  expedition to the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>, asking Barrie to take care of his wife <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Scott" title="Kathleen Scott">Kathleen</a> and son <a href="/wiki/Peter_Scott_(conservationist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Scott (conservationist)">Peter</a>. Barrie was so proud of the letter that he carried it around for the rest of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1896, his agent Addison Bright persuaded him to meet with Broadway producer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Frohman" title="Charles Frohman">Charles Frohman</a>, who became his financial backer and a close friend, as well.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Frohman was responsible for producing the debut of <i>Peter Pan</i> in both England and the US, as well as other productions of Barrie\'s plays. He famously declined a lifeboat seat when the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Lusitania" title="RMS Lusitania">RMS <i>Lusitania</i></a> was sunk by a German <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> in the North Atlantic. Actress <a href="/wiki/Rita_Jolivet" title="Rita Jolivet">Rita Jolivet</a> stood with Frohman, George Vernon and Captain Alick Scott at the end of Lusitania\'s sinking, but she survived the sinking and recalled Frohman paraphrasing <i>Peter Pan</i>: \'Why fear death? It is the most beautiful adventure that life gives us.\'<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His secretary from 1917, <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Asquith" class="mw-redirect" title="Cynthia Asquith">Cynthia Asquith</a>, was the daughter-in-law of <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a>, British Prime Minister from 1908 to 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In the 1930s, Barrie met and told stories to the young daughters of the <a href="/wiki/George_VI_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George VI of the United Kingdom">Duke of York</a>, the future <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Queen Elizabeth II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Margaret" class="mw-redirect" title="Princess Margaret">Princess Margaret</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._Barrie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Barrie became acquainted with actress Mary Ansell in 1891, when he asked his friend <a href="/wiki/Jerome_K._Jerome" title="Jerome K. Jerome">Jerome K. Jerome</a> for a pretty actress to play a role in his play <i>Walker, London</i>. The two became friends, and she helped his family to care for him when he fell very ill in 1893 and 1894.<sup id="cite_ref-Chaney_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaney-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They married in Kirriemuir on 9 July 1894,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> shortly after Barrie recovered, and Mary retired from the stage; but the relationship was reportedly unconsummated,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and the couple had no children. The wedding was a small ceremony in his parents\' home, in the Scottish tradition.\n</p><p>In 1895, the Barries bought a house on Gloucester Road, in South Kensington.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Barrie would take long walks in nearby Kensington Gardens, and in 1900 the couple moved into a house directly overlooking the gardens at 100 Bayswater Road. Mary had a flair for interior design and set about transforming the downstairs, creating two large reception rooms with painted panelling and adding fashionable features, such as a conservatory.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In the same year, Mary found Black Lake Cottage, at <a href="/wiki/Farnham" title="Farnham">Farnham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, which became the couple\'s "bolt hole" where Barrie could entertain his cricketing friends and the Llewelyn Davies family.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Beginning in mid-1908, Mary had an affair with <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Cannan" title="Gilbert Cannan">Gilbert Cannan</a> (who was twenty years younger than her<sup id="cite_ref-holroyd_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holroyd-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and an associate of Barrie\'s in his anti-censorship activities), including a visit together to Black Lake Cottage, known only to the house staff. When Barrie learned of the affair in July 1909, he demanded that she end it, but she refused. To avoid the scandal of divorce, he offered a legal separation if she would agree not to see Cannan any more, but she still refused. Barrie sued for divorce on the grounds of infidelity; the divorce was granted in October 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkin_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Knowing how painful the divorce was for him, some of Barrie\'s friends wrote to a number of newspaper editors asking them not to publish the story. In the event, only three newspapers did.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Barrie continued to support Mary financially even after she married Cannan, by giving her an annual allowance, which was handed over at a private dinner held on her and Barrie\'s wedding anniversary.<sup id="cite_ref-holroyd_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holroyd-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Llewelyn_Davies_family">Llewelyn Davies family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._Barrie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Llewelyn Davies family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg/220px-J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg" width="220" height="315" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg/330px-J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg/440px-J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg 2x" data-file-width="559" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:J._M._Barrie_in_1902.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>J. M. Barrie by <a href="/wiki/George_Charles_Beresford" title="George Charles Beresford">George Charles Beresford</a>, 1902</div></div></div>\n<p>The Llewelyn Davies family played an important part in Barrie\'s literary and personal life, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Llewelyn_Davies" title="Arthur Llewelyn Davies">Arthur</a> (1863\xe2\x80\x931907), <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Llewelyn_Davies" title="Sylvia Llewelyn Davies">Sylvia</a> (1866\xe2\x80\x931910) (daughter of <a href="/wiki/George_du_Maurier" title="George du Maurier">George du Maurier</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Llewelyn_Davies_boys" title="Llewelyn Davies boys">their five sons</a>: <a href="/wiki/George_Llewelyn_Davies" title="George Llewelyn Davies">George</a> (1893\xe2\x80\x931915), <a href="/wiki/Jack_Llewelyn_Davies" title="Jack Llewelyn Davies">John</a> (Jack) (1894\xe2\x80\x931959), <a href="/wiki/Peter_Llewelyn_Davies" title="Peter Llewelyn Davies">Peter</a> (1897\xe2\x80\x931960), <a href="/wiki/Michael_Llewelyn_Davies" title="Michael Llewelyn Davies">Michael</a> (1900\xe2\x80\x931921) and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Llewelyn_Davies" title="Nicholas Llewelyn Davies">Nicholas</a> (Nico) (1903\xe2\x80\x931980).\n</p><p>Barrie became acquainted with the family in 1897, meeting George and Jack (and baby Peter) with their nurse (<a href="/wiki/Nanny" title="Nanny">nanny</a>) Mary Hodgson in London\'s <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gardens" title="Kensington Gardens">Kensington Gardens</a>. He lived nearby and often walked his Saint Bernard dog Porthos<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> in the park. He entertained the boys regularly with his ability to wiggle his ears and eyebrows, and with his stories. He did not meet Sylvia until a chance encounter at a dinner party in December. She told Barrie that Peter had been named after the title character in her father\'s novel, <i>Peter Ibbetson</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Barrie became a regular visitor at the Davies household and a common companion to Sylvia and her boys, despite the fact that both he and she were married to other people.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkin_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1901, he invited the Davies family to Black Lake Cottage, where he produced an album of captioned photographs of the boys acting out a pirate adventure, entitled <i>The Boy Castaways of Black Lake Island</i>. Barrie had two copies made, one of which he gave to Arthur, who misplaced it on a train.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The only surviving copy is held at the Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library at Yale University.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The character of Peter Pan was invented to entertain George and Jack. Barrie would say, to amuse them, that their little brother Peter could fly. He claimed that babies were birds before they were born; parents put bars on nursery windows to keep the little ones from flying away. This grew into a tale of a baby boy who did fly away.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Arthur Llewelyn Davies died in 1907, and "Uncle Jim" became even more involved with the Davies family, providing financial support to them. (His income from <i>Peter Pan</i> and other works was easily adequate to provide for their living expenses and education.) Following Sylvia\'s death in 1910, Barrie claimed that they had recently been engaged to be married.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkin_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Her will indicated nothing to that effect, but specified her wish for "J. M. B." to be trustee and guardian to the boys, along with her mother Emma, her brother <a href="/wiki/Guy_du_Maurier" title="Guy du Maurier">Guy du Maurier</a> and Arthur\'s brother Compton. It expressed her confidence in Barrie as the boys\' caretaker and her wish for "the boys to treat him (&amp; their uncles) with absolute confidence &amp; straightforwardness &amp; to talk to him about everything." When copying the will informally for Sylvia\'s family a few months later, Barrie inserted himself elsewhere: Sylvia had written that she would like Mary Hodgson, the boys\' nurse, to continue taking care of them, and for "Jenny" (referring to Hodgson\'s sister) to come and help her; Barrie instead wrote "Jimmy" (Sylvia\'s nickname for him). Barrie and Hodgson did not get along well, but served together as surrogate parents until the boys were grown.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkin_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Barrie also had friendships with other children, both before he met the Davies boys and after they had grown up, and there has since been speculation that Barrie was a <a href="/wiki/Pedophilia" title="Pedophilia">paedophile</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> One source for the speculation is a scene in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_White_Bird" title="The Little White Bird">The Little White Bird</a></i>, in which the protagonist helps a small boy undress for bed, and at the boy\'s request they sleep in the same bed.<sup id="cite_ref-White_1994_11-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, there is no evidence that Barrie had sexual contact with children, nor that he was suspected of it at the time. Nico, the youngest of the brothers, denied as an adult that Barrie ever behaved inappropriately.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkin_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> "I don\'t believe that Uncle Jim ever experienced what one might call \'a stirring in the undergrowth\' for anyone\xe2\x80\x94man, woman, or child", he stated. "He was an innocent\xe2\x80\x94which is why he could write Peter Pan."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> His relationships with the surviving Davies boys continued well beyond their childhood and adolescence.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan_statue" title="Peter Pan statue">Peter Pan statue</a> in Kensington Gardens, erected secretly overnight for <a href="/wiki/May_Morning" title="May Morning">May Morning</a> in 1912, was supposed to be modelled upon old photographs of Michael dressed as the character. However, the sculptor, <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Frampton" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir George Frampton">Sir George Frampton</a>, used a different child as a model, leaving Barrie disappointed with the result. "It doesn\'t show the devil in Peter", he said.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkin_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkin-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Barrie suffered bereavements with the boys, losing the two to whom he was closest in their early twenties. George was killed in action in 1915, in the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Michael, with whom Barrie corresponded daily while at boarding school and university, drowned in 1921, with his friend, Rupert Buxton,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> at a known danger spot at <a href="/wiki/Sandford_Lock" title="Sandford Lock">Sandford Lock</a> near <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, one month short of his 21st birthday. Some years after Barrie\'s death, Peter compiled his <i>Morgue</i> from family letters and papers, interpolated with his own informed comments on his family and their relationship with Barrie. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Llewelyn_Davies#Death" title="Peter Llewelyn Davies">Peter died</a> by throwing himself in front of a train shortly after completing the work.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._M._Barrie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Barrie died of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> at a nursing home in the West End of London on 19 June 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> He was buried at Kirriemuir next to his parents and two of his siblings.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> His birthplace at 4 Brechin Road is maintained as a museum by the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Scotland" title="National Trust for Scotland">National Trust for Scotland</a>.\n</p><p>He left the bulk of his estate to his secretary <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Asquith" class="mw-redirect" title="Cynthia Asquith">Cynthia Asquith</a>, but excluding the rights to all <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan" title="Peter Pan">Peter Pan</a> works (which included <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_White_Bird" title="The Little White Bird">The Little White Bird</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan_in_Kensington_Gardens" title="Peter Pan in Kensington Gardens">Peter Pan in Kensington Gardens</a></i>, the play <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan,_or_The_Boy_Who_Would_Not_Grow_Up" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Pan, or The Boy Who Would Not Grow Up">Peter Pan, or The Boy Who Would Not Grow Up</a></i> and the novel <i>Peter and Wendy</i>), whose copyright he had previously given to <a href="/wiki/Great_Ormond_Street_Hospital" title="Great Ormond Street Hospital">Great Ormond Street Hospital</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biographies">Biographies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
